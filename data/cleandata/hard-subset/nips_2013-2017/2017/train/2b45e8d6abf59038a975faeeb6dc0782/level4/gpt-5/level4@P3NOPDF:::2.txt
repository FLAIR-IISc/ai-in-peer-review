Summary
The paper proposes the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching, especially in training generative models. PMD is presented as a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance. The authors claim that PMD offers stronger gradients, works well with small mini-batches, and involves fewer hyperparameters than MMD.

Strengths
- The motivation is clear: MMD-based training often suffers from weak gradients and the need for large mini-batches. PMD directly targets these issues.
- The empirical results convincingly demonstrate that PMD can be effective with small mini-batch sizes, which is appealing for efficiency and stability in practice.
- The connection to the Wasserstein distance is conceptually appealing and provides a principled grounding for the method.
- The method appears to enable faster training due to reduced batch-size requirements, which is a practical advantage over large-batch MMD approaches.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evidence for the “stronger gradients” claim is not fully convincing. Since MMD’s behavior depends critically on the kernel bandwidth (sigma), any comparison of gradient strength should consider either (a) the best sigma per setting or (b) a reasonable range of sigmas. Without this, the comparison risks penalizing MMD for suboptimal hyperparameter choices rather than reflecting an intrinsic weakness.
- The claim of “fewer hyperparameters” is not well substantiated. PMD relies on the choice of a distance metric, which itself may introduce hyperparameters (e.g., feature scaling, learned embeddings, or metric parameters). In high-dimensional settings, defining a reliable metric is challenging; distance concentration and feature scaling can heavily influence outcomes. Thus, PMD may inherit the same sensitivity to metric choice that affects MMD’s kernel choice.
- The discussion does not adequately acknowledge that MMD benefits from standard, widely used heuristics for bandwidth selection (e.g., the median heuristic) or multi-kernel variants that mitigate sensitivity to a single bandwidth. If MMD is tuned using these standard practices, the gap in performance and gradient quality may narrow.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strength of gradients: Provide systematic comparisons against MMD with properly tuned bandwidths. Consider:
  - Selecting sigma via cross-validation or validation-set performance.
  - Using multi-kernel MMD or kernel mixtures to reduce bandwidth sensitivity.
  - Reporting gradient norms and their correlation with training progress across a range of sigmas, not just a single setting.
- Hyperparameter claims: Clarify what constitutes the hyperparameters for PMD. If PMD depends on a particular metric or feature space:
  - Report sensitivity analyses with respect to metric choices and any associated parameters.
  - Discuss practical guidelines for choosing the metric and whether there are standard heuristics analogous to MMD bandwidth selection.
- High-dimensional robustness: Provide experiments or analyses that probe performance in higher-dimensional data regimes where distances are known to be less informative. This would help assess whether PMD’s reliance on a metric meaningfully limits its applicability.
- Baselines: Include comparisons against strong MMD baselines with standard heuristics (e.g., median heuristic, multi-kernel MMD), and, where relevant, report results with broader mini-batch sizes to contextualize the benefits of small-batch PMD.
- Clarity on the Wasserstein connection: While PMD is described as a sampled approximation to the Wasserstein distance, it would be helpful to detail the approximation quality, biases, and sample complexity. Clarifying when PMD is expected to align closely with Wasserstein and when it may deviate would strengthen the theoretical narrative.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses a real and practical shortcoming of MMD-based training and provides a method that appears to work well with small mini-batches, enabling faster and potentially more stable training. The empirical support for the small mini-batch claim is solid. However, the evidence for stronger gradients relative to MMD is incomplete without fair bandwidth tuning or multi-scale baselines, and the claim of fewer hyperparameters is not convincingly supported given PMD’s dependence on a chosen distance metric—particularly problematic in high-dimensional settings.

With stronger experimental comparisons (especially against well-tuned MMD variants), clearer discussion of hyperparameters and metric sensitivity, and more thorough analysis in higher-dimensional regimes, the paper would make a compelling case. As it stands, PMD seems like a meaningful and practical improvement over large-batch MMD methods, but its reliance on a distance metric may limit its effectiveness on complex, high-dimensional data.
