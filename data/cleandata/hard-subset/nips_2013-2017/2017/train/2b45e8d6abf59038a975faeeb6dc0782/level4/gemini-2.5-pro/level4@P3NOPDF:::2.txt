This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based approximation of the Wasserstein metric, as an alternative to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. The authors posit that PMD offers several advantages over MMD, most notably providing stronger training gradients, enabling the use of smaller mini-batches, and requiring fewer hyperparameters. These properties are particularly relevant for training generative models, where MMD is known to suffer from challenges.

The paper effectively addresses known limitations of using MMD for training generative models, namely the weak gradients and the need for large mini-batches to get a stable estimate of the discrepancy. The proposed PMD method offers a practical and well-motivated solution to these issues. A key strength of the paper lies in its empirical validation of the small mini-batch claim. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that PMD can operate effectively with smaller batch sizes, a significant practical advantage that can lead to faster training and lower memory overhead compared to traditional large-batch MMD approaches.

Despite these strengths, some of the paper's central claims could be better substantiated.

First, the verification of the "stronger gradients" claim is not entirely convincing. The gradient magnitude and overall performance of MMD are critically dependent on the choice of the kernel bandwidth (e.g., the sigma parameter for an RBF kernel). To make a fair and robust comparison, the authors should evaluate PMD against MMD with a properly tuned bandwidth parameter or, at the very least, show results across a range of bandwidths. Without this, it is difficult to conclude that PMD inherently provides stronger gradients than a well-configured MMD.

Second, the claim of having fewer hyperparameters is debatable. While PMD removes the kernel bandwidth parameter, it introduces a dependency on a distance metric (e.g., L1 or L2 distance). The choice of this metric is itself a crucial hyperparameter. Moreover, in high-dimensional spaces, defining a meaningful and robust distance metric is a non-trivial problem, and PMD may suffer from the curse of dimensionality in a manner analogous to MMD. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this trade-off. Furthermore, there are several established heuristics for setting the MMD bandwidth (such as the median heuristic) that effectively make MMD a parameter-free method in practice. A comparison against MMD using such heuristics would provide a more balanced assessment.

In summary, the proposed method is a valuable contribution, offering a clear advantage in its ability to permit small mini-batch training. This makes it a valid and potentially more efficient improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, the claims regarding superior gradients and fewer hyperparameters require more rigorous support. The method's fundamental reliance on a predefined distance metric may also limit its effectiveness on high-dimensional and complex data distributions, a challenge it shares with its MMD counterpart. Overall, the paper presents a promising direction, but its claims should be tempered and contextualized more carefully.
