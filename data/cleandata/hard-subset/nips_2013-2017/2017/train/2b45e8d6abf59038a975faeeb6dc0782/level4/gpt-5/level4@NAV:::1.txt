Review- The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance computed between two minibatches drawn from the respective distributions. This Wasserstein distance is evaluated either exactly in O(N^3) or approximately in O(N^2).

Pros:
- The experimental study of this Wasserstein-based computation is interesting. The generated samples, however, are not on par with those from Wasserstein GAN.

Cons:
- Estimating the Wasserstein distance between complex, multimodal distributions likely requires large N. I expect issues to already appear when matching a Gaussian mixture (including learning variances).
- With insufficient N, the optimization may attain a global minimum different from the true distribution. For example, the learned model may have lower entropy. The SVHN samples in Figure 4 appear to have limited diversity; the digit 8 shows up frequently.

Update:
I have read the rebuttal. Thanks for the additional experiments. The authors should clarify the limitations of PMD and MMD. MMD works with batch_size=2. MMD can be trained with SGD if one uses the unbiased estimator from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper. Thus, MMD can converge to the correct distribution even with small minibatches. By contrast, PMD does not have a known unbiased gradient estimator.
