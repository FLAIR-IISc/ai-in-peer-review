- Introduces PMD as an alternative to MMD for estimating distances between probability distributions.
- Proves PMD is a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein (earth mover’s) metric.
- Claims PMD offers stronger gradients, allows smaller mini-batches, and reduces hyperparameter tuning compared to MMD.
- Strong motivation: MMD suffers from kernel bandwidth sensitivity, vanishing gradients, and large mini-batch requirements.
- Empirical results indicate faster convergence for PMD versus MMD.
- The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is questionable because PMD depends on a distance metric d(x,y), which can introduce its own hyperparameters.
- Reliance on a chosen distance metric is a potential limitation, especially in high-dimensional spaces where metric choice strongly affects performance.
- Verification of the stronger-gradient claim is insufficiently rigorous; MMD should be evaluated with optimized bandwidth (e.g., via validation) for fair comparison.
- Overall assessment: PMD is a valuable, theoretically grounded contribution with practical speed and stability benefits.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses known MMD weaknesses, is grounded in Wasserstein theory, shows improved convergence, and yields stronger gradients.
- Arguments against acceptance: hyperparameter reduction not fully substantiated, distance-metric sensitivity in high dimensions, and insufficiently rigorous gradient comparisons.
