This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimation of the Wasserstein metric between two distributions. The authors propose an algorithm to learn the parameters of the distributions using PMD as an objective. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.

The paper provides a clear and concise overview of the related work, including the limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), which is a popular method for estimating the distance between two distributions. The authors highlight the advantages of PMD over MMD, including fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes for gradient-based optimization.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The authors show that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed in both tasks. The generated images using PMD are also presented, which appear to be less noisy than those generated by MMD.

However, there are some limitations to the proposed method. The computation of PMD requires an exact O(N^3) or an approximate O(N^2) algorithm, which may not be efficient for large N. The authors acknowledge this limitation but argue that the time complexity is still acceptable compared to the cost of training neural networks.

One potential issue with the proposed method is that it may require large N to estimate the Wasserstein distance between two diverse multimodal distributions. If N is not large enough, the optimization may have a global minimum at a point different from the true distribution. For example, the learned distribution may have less entropy. The authors do not provide a clear solution to this problem, and it is unclear how PMD would perform in such scenarios.

In the rebuttal, the authors provide additional experiments and clarify some of the limitations of PMD and MMD. However, it is still unclear whether PMD has a known unbiased estimator of the gradient, which may affect its convergence properties.
