- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): Wasserstein distance between minibatches, computed with exact O(N^3) and approximate O(N^2) algorithms.  
- Experiments are informative but PMD fails to match state-of-the-art image generation quality (e.g., vs. WGAN).  
- Strength: novel approach to estimating/applying Wasserstein distance for distribution matching; empirical exploration is valuable.  
- Core weakness: PMD is highly sensitive to minibatch size and requires large N to estimate Wasserstein accurately, especially for complex/multimodal distributions.  
- With insufficient N, optimization can converge to local minima, producing reduced-entropy solutions and mode collapse.  
- SVHN results (Fig.4) show lack of diversity and over-representation of digit '8', supporting collapse concerns.  
- Original manuscript lacks a clear, thorough comparison of PMD’s limitations to established methods like MMD.  
- Rebuttal clarifies MMD’s advantage: availability of an unbiased gradient estimator allows effective training with very small batches (e.g., batch_size=2).  
- PMD currently lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator, making its optimization more susceptible under practical minibatch constraints.  
- Minor typo noted: "usally" → "usually".  
- Overall: interesting but limited approach; computational costs and batch-sensitivity outweigh benefits—recommend rejection.  
- Suggested next steps: research ways to mitigate minibatch sensitivity or derive an unbiased gradient estimator and more directly contrast PMD with alternatives like MMD.
