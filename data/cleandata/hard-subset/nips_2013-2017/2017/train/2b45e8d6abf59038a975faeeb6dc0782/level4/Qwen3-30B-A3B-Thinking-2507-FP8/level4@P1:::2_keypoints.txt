- Paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a new sample-based estimator of distance between distributions and an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD as objective.
- Authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Earth Mover’s Distance).
- PMD is positioned as an alternative to MMD, which suffers from kernel bandwidth sensitivity, weak gradients, and large mini-batch requirements.
- PMD requires no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, removing a critical tuning parameter present in MMD.
- PMD yields stronger, more consistent gradients across samples (Figure 2(b)) compared to MMD’s highly variable gradients.
- PMD allows much smaller mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100 vs 1000), enabling faster convergence (Figures 3(a) and 5).
- Theoretical foundation is solid: PMD is connected to optimal transport and supported by Propositions 3.1–3.3.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough: domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) show PMD outperforms MMD in final performance and convergence speed.
- Ablation studies (Section 5.3) substantiate the benefits of PMD’s smaller mini-batch sizes.
- Claim of “fewer hyperparameters” is weakened because PMD depends on a choice of distance metric (e.g., L1/L2), which can introduce its own hyperparameters.
- Paper lacks guidance on selecting appropriate distance metrics in high-dimensional spaces, limiting applicability to complex data like natural images.
- Using pixel-level L1 leads to blurry outputs on SVHN and LFW, indicating metric choice is critical for image tasks.
- Gradient-strength claim should be compared against MMD best practices (median heuristic, multiple kernels) to rule out bandwidth-tuning confounds.
- Overall assessment: promising, theoretically sound, and empirically validated method that improves training efficiency but needs clearer treatment of hyperparameter and metric-selection issues.
- Strengths highlighted: strong theory, better gradients and mini-batch efficiency, comprehensive empirical evaluation, and faster convergence.
- Weaknesses highlighted: unsupported “fewer hyperparameters” claim, limited discussion on distance metric selection for high-dimensional data, and incomplete MMD baseline tuning in gradient comparisons.
