This paper defines the difference in population size between two distributions.

The Wasserstein distance between two mini-batches from the distribution is considered as the difference in population size.

The Wasserstein distance is computed by an exact O(N^3) or approximate O(N^2) algorithm.

Advantages:
- The computation of the Wasserstein distance is quite interesting to experiment with.
- The resulting numbers are not as good as those obtained from Wasserstein GAN.

Disadvantages:
- A large N will be required for the proposed distance to estimate the Wasserstein distance between two different multimodal distributions.
- I suspect that if Gaussian mixtures (including variance learning) are attempted to be matched, the problems will become apparent.
- If N is not large enough, the global minimum of optimization may be at a point different from the actual distribution.
- For example, the learned distribution may have lower entropy.

The authors should clarify the limitations of PMD and MMD.
MMD is fine with batch_size=2.
MMD can be trained with SGD, provided an unbiased estimator of MMD is used from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper.
Therefore, MMD can be allowed to converge towards the correct distribution by using small mini-batches.
On the other hand, PMD does not have a known unbiased estimator of the gradient.
