The paper "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning" proposes a novel approach for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples, called Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD). The authors define PMD as the minimum weight matching between two random populations from the distributions and show that it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. They also propose a stochastic gradient descent algorithm to learn parameters of the distributions using PMD.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of PMD and its connections to other discrepancies, such as the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD). The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, and the results are promising.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a differentiable estimation of the distance between two distributions, which is important for many deep learning tasks. The authors also highlight the advantages of PMD over MMD, including fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes for gradient-based optimization.

However, there are some limitations to the proposed approach. One concern is that PMD may require large N to estimate the Wasserstein distance between two diverse multimodal distributions accurately. The authors should clarify this limitation and discuss potential solutions to address it. Additionally, the optimization process may have a global minimum at a point different from the true distribution if N is not large enough, which could result in a learned distribution with less entropy.

The experiments on generative modeling show promising results, but the generated images seem to have low diversity. For example, digit 8 appears frequently in the SVHN samples. The authors should investigate this issue and provide more diverse samples to demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in generative modeling.

Minor typos were found in the paper, including "usally" instead of "usually" on line 141.

In the rebuttal, the authors provided additional experiments and clarified some of the concerns raised. However, they should further clarify the limitations of PMD and MMD, particularly regarding the use of small mini-batches. As mentioned in the rebuttal, MMD can be trained with SGD using the unbiased estimator of MMD from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper, which allows it to converge to the right distribution even with small mini-batches. On the other hand, PMD does not have a known unbiased estimator of the gradient, which may limit its use in certain scenarios.

Overall, the paper presents a novel approach for estimating the distance between two distributions and demonstrates its effectiveness in various deep learning tasks. However, the authors should address the limitations and concerns raised to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach.
