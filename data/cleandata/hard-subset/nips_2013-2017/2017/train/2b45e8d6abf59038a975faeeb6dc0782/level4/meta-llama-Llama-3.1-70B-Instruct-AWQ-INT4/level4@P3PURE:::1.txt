The paper introduces the concept of Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) between two distributions, defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches drawn from these distributions. This distance is computed using either an exact O(N^3) algorithm or an approximate O(N^2) method.

The strengths of this work include the experimentation with Wasserstein distance computation, which yields some interesting results. However, the quality of the generated images falls short of those produced by Wasserstein GAN.

There are several limitations to the proposed approach. Firstly, accurately estimating the Wasserstein distance between two diverse, multimodal distributions would likely require a large value of N. This issue may already be apparent when attempting to match a mixture of Gaussians, including learning the variances. Secondly, if N is not sufficiently large, the optimization process may converge to a global minimum that does not correspond to the true distribution. For instance, the learned distribution may exhibit lower entropy. This concern is supported by the SVHN samples in Figure 4, which appear to have low diversity, with digit 8 occurring frequently.

After reading the rebuttal, I appreciate the additional experiments provided by the authors. However, I believe it is essential for the authors to clarify the limitations of both PMD and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Notably, MMD can be effectively trained with a batch size of 2 and can converge to the correct distribution when using small minibatches, thanks to the unbiased estimator of MMD introduced in the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper. In contrast, PMD lacks a known unbiased estimator of the gradient, which may hinder its performance.
