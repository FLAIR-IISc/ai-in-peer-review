- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator equal to minimum-weight matching cost between two sample sets.
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein (Earth Mover's) distance.
- Presents a differentiable PMD-based objective and algorithm enabling SGD training of deep networks using population matching with small mini-batches.
- Claims PMD addresses practical drawbacks of MMD: fewer hyperparameters, stronger gradients, and support for small mini-batches leading to faster convergence.
- Empirically evaluates PMD on unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing competitive or superior performance and faster convergence.
- Strength: principled theoretical foundation linking PMD to Wasserstein distance improves credibility of the method.
- Strength: practical algorithm for computing matching on a larger population then SGD on small mini-batches effectively reduces required batch sizes.
- Strength: thorough experiments across two tasks with clear empirical advantages (e.g., Figure 3(a) and 5(a)).
- Strength: paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and situates itself well relative to prior work.
- Weakness: “fewer hyperparameters” claim is weak because PMD requires choosing a distance metric d(·,·) (e.g., L1 vs L2), which is critical in high-dimensional/perceptual spaces.
- Weakness: “stronger gradients” claim lacks robust comparison to MMD across bandwidth choices; results may depend on suboptimal kernel bandwidth selection.
- Weakness: reliance on simple sample-space distances (e.g., pixel L1) limits performance on high-dimensional perceptual data; using feature spaces increases modeling complexity.
- Weakness: matching computation has O(N^2)/O(N^3) cost (mitigated by approximations), which could be a bottleneck for very large populations.
- Overall: PMD is a valuable, theoretically sound contribution that enables small-batch training and shows strong empirical results, but hyperparameter and gradient claims need stronger validation and computational trade-offs remain.
