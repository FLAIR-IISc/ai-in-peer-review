This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a compelling alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein metric (or Earth Mover’s Distance) and possesses several advantages over MMD, most notably stronger gradients and the ability to utilize smaller mini-batch sizes, alongside a reduction in the number of hyperparameters.

For training generative models, MMD’s well-known limitations of weak gradients and the necessity for large mini-batches are effectively addressed by this proposal, providing a valuable solution to both issues. The claim regarding smaller mini-batch sizes is convincingly supported by the empirical results. However, the verification of stronger gradients is less conclusive, as the performance of MMD is heavily dependent on the scale parameter σ. Therefore, any such claim necessitates consideration of either the optimal σ or a range of σ values.

Regarding the claim of fewer hyperparameters, I find the justification less persuasive. PMD relies on a distance metric, which may itself contain hyperparameters comparable to those found in MMD. Furthermore, obtaining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces is challenging, and PMD may therefore encounter similar difficulties as MMD in this regard. Conversely, existing heuristics offer guidance on selecting the bandwidth parameter for MMD; a comparison against these heuristics, treating MMD as a principled hyperparameter-tuned metric, would strengthen the analysis.

Overall, the proposed method exhibits the beneficial property of enabling small mini-batch sizes, thereby facilitating faster training. It appears to be a valid improvement over large-batch MMD approaches. However, its continued reliance on a distance metric introduces a potential limitation regarding its performance when modeling high-dimensional data.
