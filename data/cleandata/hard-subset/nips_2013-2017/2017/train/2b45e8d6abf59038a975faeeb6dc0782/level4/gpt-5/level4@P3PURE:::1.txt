Summary:
The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance computed between two minibatches sampled from the respective distributions. The Wasserstein distance is evaluated either via an exact O(N^3) algorithm or an approximate O(N^2) algorithm.

Pros:
- It is interesting to see experiments that directly compute the Wasserstein distance in this fashion.

Cons:
- The proposed discrepancy appears to require large minibatch sizes N to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between diverse, multimodal distributions. I suspect problems would already emerge when matching a mixture of Gaussians (including learning the variances).
- If N is not sufficiently large, the optimization may reach a global minimum that differs from the true distribution; for example, the learned distribution may have lower entropy.
- The SVHN samples in Figure 4 seem to exhibit low diversity (e.g., the digit 8 appears frequently).
- The generated images are not as good as those produced by Wasserstein GAN.

Minor typos:
- Line 141: “usally” → “usually”

Update after rebuttal:
I have read the rebuttal—thank you for the additional experiments. The paper should clearly articulate the limitations of both PMD and MMD.
- MMD is fine with batch_size = 2.
- MMD can be trained with SGD if one uses the unbiased estimator from the original “A Kernel Two-Sample Test” paper. Consequently, MMD can converge to the correct distribution even with small minibatches.
- By contrast, PMD does not have a known unbiased estimator of the gradient.
