- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a minimum-weight matching–based distribution distance estimator as a practical alternative to MMD.  
- Theoretical result: PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3; Propositions 3.1–3.2).  
- Practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger per-sample gradients, and compatibility with much smaller minibatches.  
- Empirical validation: PMD outperforms MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling in performance, convergence speed, and minibatch efficiency (Sections 5.1–5.3; Figures 2b, 3a, 5a).  
- Strength — theoretical rigor: solid grounding in optimal transport theory and explicit addressing of known MMD issues.  
- Strength — empirical evidence: comprehensive experiments showing minibatch advantage and gradient-strength benefits.  
- Strength — originality: method is distinct from MMD and WGAN, offering a parameter-free, direct matching approach.  
- Strength — clarity/reproducibility: well-structured exposition, clear definitions, pseudocode, and reproducible experimental setup.  
- Weakness — hyperparameter claim needs nuance: PMD removes kernel bandwidth but still requires choosing a distance metric (e.g., L1 vs L2) and scaling.  
- Weakness — limited generalizability: reliance on fixed distance metrics can hurt performance on high-dimensional natural images (blurry SVHN/LFW results).  
- Suggested future work: explore learned or adaptive metrics to address brittleness in complex data.  
- Recommendation: Accept, with revision to clarify the “reduced hyperparameter burden” claim and discuss metric-dependency limitations.
