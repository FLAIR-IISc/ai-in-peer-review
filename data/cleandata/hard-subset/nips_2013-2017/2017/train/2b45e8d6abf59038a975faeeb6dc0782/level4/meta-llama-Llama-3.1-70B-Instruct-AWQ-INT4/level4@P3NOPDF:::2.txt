This paper proposes the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a superior alternative to the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching applications. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein metric, or earth mover's distance, and highlight several advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes, as well as fewer hyperparameters.

For training generative models, the MMD metric is known to suffer from weak gradients and the requirement of large mini-batches. The proposals in this paper provide a solution to both of these issues. The empirical results verify the claim of smaller mini-batch sizes, and the authors demonstrate that PMD can be effective with smaller batch sizes. However, the verification of the stronger gradients claim is less convincing, as the MMD metric depends on the scale parameter sigma. To make a more robust claim, it would be essential to consider either the optimal sigma or a range of sigmas.

Regarding the claim of having fewer hyperparameters, I believe it is less well-supported. While PMD may have fewer hyperparameters than MMD, it relies on a distance metric, which can itself contain additional hyperparameters. Furthermore, obtaining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional space can be challenging, and PMD may suffer from the same issue of relying on a distance metric as MMD. In contrast, there are standard heuristics for choosing the bandwidth parameter in MMD, which could be used to make MMD a hyperparameter-free metric. A comparison with such heuristics would strengthen the authors' claim.

Overall, I think the proposed method has the desirable property of permitting small mini-batch sizes, which can lead to faster training. This appears to be a valid improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, the reliance on a distance metric may limit the success of PMD in modeling higher-dimensional data.
