

The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel approach for estimating the distance between probability distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, and the authors prove it's a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. This work addresses key limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), the most widely used sample-based distribution distance metric, which suffers from bandwidth sensitivity, weak gradients, and requires large mini-batch sizes for reliable estimation.

The paper makes a significant contribution by connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric, which has gained popularity in machine learning for its theoretical properties and practical utility. While the Wasserstein metric itself is computationally challenging to compute directly, PMD provides a practical, differentiable estimator that overcomes MMD's drawbacks. The authors clearly demonstrate that PMD has no bandwidth hyper-parameter, provides stronger gradients (as shown in Figure 2b), and can operate with standard mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100) rather than the large batch sizes required by MMD (typically 1000). The theoretical foundation is solid, with Proposition 3.3 establishing the strong consistency of PMD as a Wasserstein metric estimator through appropriate application of Varadarajan's theorem and stability of optimal transport.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The mathematical definitions are precise, and the connection to prior work (MMD, Wasserstein metric, WGAN) is well-established. The authors effectively distinguish PMD from existing approaches, particularly noting that unlike WGAN which requires a critic network and cannot provide absolute Wasserstein distances, PMD is parameter-free and computable in polynomial time. The paper also thoughtfully addresses computational limitations by proposing an approximate matching algorithm with O(N²) complexity, demonstrating through experiments that this approximation doesn't significantly degrade performance.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare PMD with MMD across two important deep learning tasks: domain adaptation on the Office benchmark (showing significant improvements on a→w and a→d tasks) and generative modeling on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW datasets. Their results demonstrate that PMD consistently outperforms MMD in both performance and convergence speed. The ablation studies (Figures 5a-c) thoroughly investigate PMD's sensitivity to population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithm choice, providing valuable practical insights. The empirical analysis of gradient magnitudes (Figure 2b) effectively supports the theoretical claim about stronger gradients.

The paper's strengths include its strong theoretical foundation, clear presentation, and comprehensive empirical validation. The authors have addressed key limitations of existing methods and provided a practical solution that could have significant impact on distribution distance estimation in deep learning. The applications to domain adaptation and generative modeling are well-chosen and demonstrate the method's practical utility.

Some minor weaknesses include: the generative modeling results on SVHN and LFW show blurry images, suggesting PMD may benefit from using a different distance metric (like L2) for natural images rather than L1. The authors might have explored this limitation more thoroughly. Additionally, while they mention PMD's potential for multimodal distributions, they don't provide specific experiments with challenging multimodal datasets where N might be smaller than the number of modes.

The paper's significance is substantial. By addressing practical limitations of MMD that have hindered its adoption in deep learning, PMD offers a more efficient and robust alternative for distribution distance estimation. This has direct implications for numerous applications including domain adaptation, generative modeling, and metric learning. The method's simplicity (no additional hyper-parameters beyond standard deep learning settings) and computational efficiency make it particularly attractive for practitioners.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in distribution distance estimation by providing a method that is theoretically grounded, practically efficient, and empirically superior to the current standard (MMD). The authors have thoroughly addressed the necessary theoretical and experimental validations, and their comparison with MMD is fair and comprehensive.

In summary, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field with strong theoretical foundations, clear presentation, and compelling empirical results. The advantages of PMD over MMD are well-demonstrated across multiple tasks, and the method has clear potential for broader adoption in deep learning applications. The minor limitations identified do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation connecting to Wasserstein metric, practical advantages over MMD (no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch), comprehensive empirical validation across multiple tasks, clear writing and organization.

Con: Limited exploration of PMD's performance with multimodal distributions where N < number of modes, generative results on natural images show some blurriness with L1 distance.

Given the paper's technical soundness, clear presentation, and significant practical contributions to a fundamental problem in machine learning, I recommend acceptance. The work represents a meaningful advance in distribution distance estimation that addresses key limitations of existing methods and has clear potential for impact across multiple deep learning applications.
