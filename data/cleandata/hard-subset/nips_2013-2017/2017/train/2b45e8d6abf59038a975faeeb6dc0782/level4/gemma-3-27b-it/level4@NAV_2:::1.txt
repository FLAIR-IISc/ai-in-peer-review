The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples, motivated by the limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), specifically its sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and large mini-batch requirements. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations, and the authors prove its strong consistency as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The proposed approach is applied to domain adaptation and generative modeling, demonstrating improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

This paper tackles an important problem in deep learning – estimating distributional divergence from samples – and offers a potentially valuable alternative to MMD. The connection to the Wasserstein metric provides a strong theoretical foundation, and the empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling are encouraging. The authors clearly articulate the drawbacks of MMD and position PMD as addressing these issues, particularly regarding gradient behavior and mini-batch size. The inclusion of a pseudocode illustration and discussion of computational complexity is helpful.

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical grounding and the demonstrated empirical improvements over MMD. The analysis relating PMD to the Wasserstein metric is a significant contribution, and the observation of stronger gradients is a compelling advantage. The experiments, while limited, showcase the potential of PMD in practical applications. The discussion of computational costs, including the trade-offs between exact and approximate matching, is also valuable.

However, there are also weaknesses. The paper does not fully address the sensitivity of PMD to the population size *N*. While the authors demonstrate empirical results with different *N* values, a more thorough theoretical analysis of how approximation error scales with *N* for diverse and multimodal distributions would be beneficial. The authors acknowledge that the L1 distance used in their experiments may not be optimal for natural images, and the resulting blurry images support this claim, suggesting a limitation in the direct applicability of PMD to complex data. Further, the claim of a stronger gradient needs more careful qualification; while the magnitude may be more consistent, this doesn't necessarily translate to more *informative* gradients for optimization, and the authors do not directly address this.

Arguments for acceptance:
* Provides a theoretically sound alternative to MMD with strong consistency guarantees.
* Demonstrates empirical improvements in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
* Addresses limitations of MMD related to gradient behavior and mini-batch size.
* Offers a clear explanation of the method and its computational complexity.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Limited analysis of the impact of population size *N* on approximation accuracy for complex distributions.
* Potential limitations of using L1 distance for complex data, leading to suboptimal results in image generation.
* Lack of detailed discussion regarding the informativeness of the observed stronger gradients. 
* The paper does not address existing unbiased estimators for MMD that can also enable training with small minibatches, potentially diminishing the advantage of PMD.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributional divergence estimation. While some aspects warrant further investigation, the theoretical soundness, empirical results, and clear presentation make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS conference.
