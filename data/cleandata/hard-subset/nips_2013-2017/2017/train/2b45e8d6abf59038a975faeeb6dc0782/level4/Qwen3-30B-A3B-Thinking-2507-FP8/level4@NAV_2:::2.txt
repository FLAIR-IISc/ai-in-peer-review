

This paper introduces population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a novel approach for estimating the distance between probability distributions based on samples, with applications in deep learning tasks such as domain adaptation and generative modeling. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, defined as the minimum weight matching cost between sample populations from two distributions. The paper claims that PMD overcomes key limitations of maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), including sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, weak gradients, and the requirement for large mini-batch sizes. The authors present theoretical analysis showing PMD's connection to the Wasserstein metric and empirical results demonstrating PMD's advantages over MMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

The strengths of this paper include a clear theoretical foundation connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric, which is a well-established statistical distance with theoretical guarantees. The empirical validation of PMD's ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100 instead of 1000) is well-supported by the experimental results, particularly in the domain adaptation task where PMD converges faster than MMD. The paper also provides a thorough comparison of PMD with MMD across multiple dimensions, including gradient strength and hyperparameter sensitivity.

However, the paper's claim that PMD has "fewer hyperparameters" requires more careful justification. While PMD eliminates the kernel bandwidth parameter of MMD, it relies on a distance metric (e.g., L1 or Euclidean) which may contain its own hyperparameters or require careful selection in high-dimensional spaces. The paper does not adequately address how to select an appropriate distance metric for different applications or compare against standard heuristics for choosing MMD's bandwidth parameter. The verification of stronger gradients for PMD is also somewhat limited, as the gradient comparison depends on the choice of MMD's bandwidth parameter, which was not systematically explored across a range of values.

The significance of this work is evident in its practical implications for deep learning training efficiency, as smaller mini-batch sizes directly translate to faster convergence. The paper makes a valid contribution to the field by providing an alternative distribution distance metric that addresses specific limitations of MMD in practice. However, the reliance on distance metric selection remains a potential limitation, particularly for high-dimensional data where distance metrics can suffer from the curse of dimensionality.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound approach with clear advantages over MMD in terms of training efficiency. The main weaknesses involve insufficient justification for the "fewer hyperparameters" claim and incomplete verification of the stronger gradient assertion. The work is original in its connection to the Wasserstein metric through minimum weight matching, though it builds upon well-established concepts in optimal transport. Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by offering a practical alternative to MMD that addresses specific training challenges.
