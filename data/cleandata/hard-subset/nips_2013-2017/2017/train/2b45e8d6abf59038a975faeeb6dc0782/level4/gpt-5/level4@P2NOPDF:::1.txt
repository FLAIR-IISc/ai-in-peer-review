This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance computed between two minibatches drawn from the respective distributions. The Wasserstein distance is evaluated either with an exact optimal transport solver at O(N^3) cost or with an approximate method at O(N^2).

Pros:
- The experimental exploration of minibatch-based Wasserstein computations is interesting and timely.
- The study provides empirical comparisons; however, the image quality lags behind results reported for Wasserstein GANs.

Cons:
- Accurately approximating the Wasserstein distance between complex, multimodal distributions appears to require large minibatch sizes N. I expect issues to arise even on relatively simple multimodal settings, such as mixtures of Gaussians (especially when variances must be learned).
- With small N, the optimization objective may have a global minimum that differs from the true data distribution. In practice, this can bias the learned model toward lower-entropy solutions. The SVHN samples in Figure 4 seem to have limited diversity; for instance, the digit “8” appears disproportionately often.

Update after rebuttal:
Thank you for the additional experiments. The manuscript would benefit from a clearer discussion of the limitations of both PMD and MMD.

- MMD can operate with very small batch sizes (even batch_size = 2).
- Using the unbiased MMD estimator from the original “A Kernel Two-Sample Test” paper allows straightforward SGD training. Consequently, MMD-based training can converge to the correct distribution even when using small minibatches.
- By contrast, for PMD there is no known unbiased estimator of the gradient. This limitation makes PMD more sensitive to batch size and raises concerns about convergence to the true target distribution under practical minibatch regimes.

Overall evaluation:
- Conceptually, framing PMD as minibatch Wasserstein distance is simple and appealing, and the experimental investigation is valuable. 
- However, the reliance on large N for reliable estimation, the lack of an unbiased gradient estimator, and the observed reduction in sample diversity weaken the practical impact compared to alternatives like MMD (which admits unbiased stochastic gradients) or WGAN variants (which achieve stronger image quality).
- Clarifying these trade-offs, especially when batch sizes are constrained, would substantially strengthen the paper.
