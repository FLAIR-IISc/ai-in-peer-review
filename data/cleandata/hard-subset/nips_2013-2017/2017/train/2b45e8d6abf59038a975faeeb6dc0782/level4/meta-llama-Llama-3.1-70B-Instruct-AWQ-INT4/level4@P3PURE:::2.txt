This paper proposes the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a superior alternative to the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching applications. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein metric, or earth mover's distance, and highlight several advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients, applicability to smaller mini-batch sizes, and fewer hyperparameters.

For training generative models, at least, the MMD metric is known to suffer from weak gradients and the requirement of large mini-batches, making the proposals in this paper a welcome solution to both issues. The empirical results provide strong evidence for the claim of smaller mini-batch sizes. However, the verification of the stronger gradients claim is less convincing, as the MMD metric's performance depends on the scale parameter sigma. Therefore, it is essential to consider either the optimal sigma or a range of sigmas when making such a claim.

The assertion that PMD has fewer hyperparameters is less well-supported, as PMD relies on a distance metric that may itself contain additional hyperparameters, similar to the MMD case. Furthermore, obtaining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces can be challenging, which may limit PMD's success in modeling higher-dimensional data. In contrast, there are established heuristics for choosing the bandwidth parameter in MMD, which could be used to make MMD a hyperparameter-free metric. A comparison with these heuristics would strengthen the authors' claim.

Overall, the proposed method has the desirable property of allowing for small mini-batch sizes, enabling faster training. This appears to be a valid improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, the reliance on a distance metric remains a limitation, potentially hindering its success in modeling high-dimensional data.
