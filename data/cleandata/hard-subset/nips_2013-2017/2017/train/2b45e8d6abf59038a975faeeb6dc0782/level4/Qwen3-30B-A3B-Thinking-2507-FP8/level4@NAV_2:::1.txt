

The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable estimator of distribution distance based on samples, defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations from each distribution. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, which is a valid statistical distance. They propose an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD as an objective, and apply it to domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The paper relates PMD to previous work on Wasserstein distance estimation (e.g., WGAN) and maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), noting that PMD avoids MMD's kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, provides stronger gradients, and allows smaller mini-batch sizes during training.

Strengths:
- The theoretical proof that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric is sound and contributes to the understanding of distribution distance estimation.
- The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages of PMD over MMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling, with improved performance and faster convergence.
- The proposed algorithm for parameter learning with PMD is well-motivated and correctly implements the minimum weight matching approach.
- The comparison with MMD is thorough, addressing key limitations of MMD including its sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, weak gradients, and requirement for large mini-batch sizes.

Weaknesses:
- The paper does not sufficiently analyze the limitations of PMD, particularly for multimodal distributions. The claim that PMD requires large N for diverse multimodal distributions is not empirically validated with experiments on challenging multimodal cases (e.g., mixture of Gaussians where learning variances is critical).
- The SVHN results in Figure 4 show low diversity (digit 8 appears frequently), suggesting PMD may produce distributions with reduced entropy or mode collapse when N is not sufficiently large.
- The paper does not adequately address whether PMD can converge to the true distribution with small mini-batch sizes, as the authors claim it can use smaller batches than MMD. The rebuttal mentions that MMD can be trained with small batches using an unbiased estimator, but the paper does not properly acknowledge this point or compare the convergence properties.
- While the authors claim PMD has no bandwidth hyperparameter, the choice of L1 distance as the sample space metric is treated as fixed, which may actually represent a hyperparameter in disguise.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation and comprehensive experiments. However, the lack of analysis on PMD's limitations for multimodal distributions and the incomplete comparison with MMD's small-batch capabilities weakens the quality assessment.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The pseudocode in Figure 1 is particularly helpful for understanding the algorithm.

Originality: The approach of using minimum weight matching as a differentiable estimator for distribution distance represents a novel contribution to the field. The connection to Wasserstein metric and comparison with MMD are well-motivated, though the core idea of using matching for distance estimation has some precedents in optimal transport literature.

Significance: The practical advantages of PMD (stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size) are significant for deep learning applications. However, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that PMD solves a critical problem that existing methods cannot address, particularly since the generative results are not as good as WGAN.

Arguments for acceptance: PMD offers a theoretically grounded alternative to MMD with clear practical advantages in training speed and parameter sensitivity. The empirical results across two important deep learning tasks demonstrate the method's effectiveness.

Arguments against acceptance: The lack of thorough analysis of PMD's limitations for multimodal distributions and the incomplete comparison with MMD's small-batch training capabilities are significant weaknesses that undermine the paper's claims. The generative results are inferior to state-of-the-art methods like WGAN, which limits the significance of the contribution.
