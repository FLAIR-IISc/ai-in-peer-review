- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator that addresses key limitations of MMD.  
- Defines PMD as the minimum-weight matching cost between sample populations and proves strong consistency to the 1-Wasserstein metric.  
- Provides a practical algorithm to use PMD as a training objective in deep networks, claiming theoretical and empirical advantages over MMD.  
- Situates the work well in literature, addressing MMD’s issues: kernel bandwidth sensitivity, vanishing gradients, and large mini-batch requirements.  
- PMD requires no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, yields stronger gradients, and works with standard mini-batch sizes (e.g., ~100).  
- Strong theoretical contributions: propositions linking PMD to optimal transport and a straightforward, rigorous proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3).  
- Empirical approximation error study (Figure 2a) gives useful practical insights into PMD’s behavior.  
- Domain adaptation experiments (Office) show PMD significantly outperforms MMD on some tasks and matches it on others.  
- Generative modeling experiments (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) show PMD produces less noisy samples, particularly in pixel space.  
- Some generated images (SVHN, LFW) appear blurry, indicating potential limitations for natural image generation.  
- Ablation studies demonstrate PMD’s advantages with respect to mini-batch size, population size, and computational efficiency.  
- Computational discussion acknowledges cubic complexity of exact matching but shows approximated matching (O(N²)) is practical.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized, with logical theoretical development and detailed experimental settings.  
- Main limitations: limited evaluation on highly multi-modal generative tasks and reliance on L1 distance for natural images.  
- Overall assessment: substantial, well-supported contribution that is practical and immediately applicable; recommended for acceptance.
