

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a distribution matching metric alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing it offers advantages including stronger gradients, compatibility with smaller minibatch sizes, and reduced hyperparameter sensitivity. The authors demonstrate PMD as a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein distance, which theoretically supports its utility. Empirical results convincingly validate PMD’s efficacy in enabling small-minibatch training—a significant practical advantage over MMD, which often necessitates large batches to avoid gradient vanishing. This contribution is valuable for generative modeling applications where computational efficiency is critical.

However, two key claims require stronger justification. First, the assertion of "stronger gradients" for PMD lacks sufficient empirical support. MMD’s performance is highly sensitive to the bandwidth parameter σ, and a fair comparison must evaluate MMD under optimal σ settings (e.g., via median heuristic or grid search), not just a single arbitrary value. The paper’s comparison appears to use a fixed σ for MMD while optimizing PMD’s parameters, which undermines the gradient strength claim. A more rigorous analysis would compare both metrics under their respective optimal configurations, as is standard practice for such metrics.

Second, the claim that PMD reduces hyperparameters is not adequately substantiated. While PMD avoids MMD’s σ hyperparameter, it inherits dependency on a distance metric (e.g., Euclidean, Mahalanobis), which itself may require tuning or heuristic specification. Crucially, the paper does not address whether this distance metric introduces comparable or additional hyperparameters. In contrast, MMD benefits from well-established heuristics for σ selection (e.g., median heuristic), which effectively renders MMD "hyperparameter-free" in practice. The authors should explicitly compare PMD against MMD with such heuristics to validate their hyperparameter reduction claim. Without this, the assertion remains unsubstantiated.

A deeper concern is PMD’s reliance on a distance metric, which poses a fundamental limitation for high-dimensional data. The paper acknowledges this but underestimates its impact. In high-dimensional spaces, distance metrics suffer from the curse of dimensionality—distances become increasingly uniform, rendering metrics like Euclidean ineffective for meaningful distribution comparison. This issue plagues both MMD (when using inappropriate kernels) and PMD (when using standard metrics), yet PMD does not propose solutions to this challenge. While the paper demonstrates success in low-to-moderate dimensional experiments (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR-10), the lack of analysis on high-dimensional scalability (e.g., ImageNet, high-dimensional embeddings) leaves a critical gap. The authors should address whether PMD’s distance dependency inherently restricts its applicability to complex, high-dimensional data—where MMD’s kernel-based flexibility might still hold an edge.

Overall, PMD presents a compelling solution for small-minibatch distribution matching, offering tangible computational benefits for training generative models. Its empirical validation of minibatch efficiency is robust and well-motivated. However, the theoretical advantages over MMD require more rigorous substantiation, particularly regarding gradient strength and hyperparameter claims. The paper’s oversight of distance metric limitations in high dimensions is a notable weakness that must be addressed for broader applicability. If the authors can refine their analysis to account for optimal MMD parameterization and provide high-dimensional experiments or theoretical insights into metric dependency, PMD would be a stronger contribution. As presented, it offers a useful but context-limited advance—valuable for specific small-batch scenarios but not a universal replacement for MMD. The core innovation (small-batch compatibility) is significant, yet the paper’s claims extend beyond its empirical evidence. A revised version should temper its assertions to align with the demonstrated results and explicitly acknowledge the distance metric’s role as a key constraint.
