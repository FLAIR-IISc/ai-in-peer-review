This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, making it a valuable tool for various machine learning tasks.

The paper provides a thorough comparison of PMD with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a popular method for estimating distribution distances. The authors highlight the advantages of PMD, including the absence of a kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes for gradient-based optimization.

The experimental evaluation of PMD is comprehensive, covering domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The results show that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed in both tasks. The authors also provide an empirical study of the approximation error of PMD, demonstrating its effectiveness in estimating the Wasserstein distance.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with proper references to previous work. The theoretical results appear to be correct, and the experimental analysis is adequate.

One potential limitation of PMD is its computational complexity, which is cubic in the exact calculation and quadratic in the approximation. However, the authors show that the approximation does not significantly degrade the statistical performance, and the computational cost can be mitigated with GPU acceleration.

A possible future direction for this work is to explore the application of PMD to generative modeling in domains with many modes. It would be interesting to investigate whether PMD can effectively capture the underlying distribution when the number of samples is smaller than the number of modes.

Overall, this is a good paper that presents a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field, and their work is likely to have a substantial impact on various machine learning applications.
