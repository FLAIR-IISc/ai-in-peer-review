This paper introduces the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to MMD for distribution matching. PMD is presented as an empirical approximation of the Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance. The paper claims several advantages over MMD: stronger gradients, the ability to use smaller mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters.

In the context of training generative models, MMD often suffers from weak gradients and requires large mini-batches, so the proposed approach offers a welcome solution to both issues. The empirical results convincingly support the small mini-batch claim. The evidence for stronger gradients is less compelling, however. Because MMD depends on the bandwidth parameter σ, any comparison should consider either the optimal σ or a range of σ values when assessing gradient strength.

The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is also less well supported. PMD relies on a distance metric, which may itself introduce hyperparameters, much like MMD. Moreover, in high-dimensional spaces, reliable distance metrics are difficult to obtain, so PMD may inherit the same challenges associated with dependence on a distance metric as MMD. In addition, there are standard heuristics for choosing the MMD bandwidth; it would be appropriate to compare against these and, for fairness, treat MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free as well.

Overall, the method’s ability to operate with small mini-batches enables faster training and appears to be a meaningful improvement over large-batch MMD approaches. Nevertheless, its reliance on a distance metric may limit its effectiveness on higher-dimensional data.
