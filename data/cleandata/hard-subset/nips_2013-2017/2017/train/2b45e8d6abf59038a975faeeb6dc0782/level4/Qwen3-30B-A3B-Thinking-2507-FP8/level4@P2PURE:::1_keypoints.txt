- Paper defines Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches drawn from the distributions.
- PMD computed via exact O(N^3) algorithm or approximate O(N^2) method.
- Pros: experiments using this Wasserstein computation are interesting.
- PMD likely requires large minibatch sizes N to accurately estimate distances for diverse, multimodal distributions.
- Limitation apparent when matching mixtures of Gaussians (including learning variances).
- With insufficient N, optimization may converge to a wrong global minimum (e.g., learned distributions with lower entropy).
- Empirical symptom: SVHN samples in Figure 4 show low diversity, with digit 8 overrepresented.
- Minor typo: Line 141 "s/usally/usually".
- Update/request: authors should clarify PMD limitations relative to MMD — MMD is valid with batch_size=2 and has an unbiased estimator enabling SGD convergence with small minibatches.
- PMD currently lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator.
