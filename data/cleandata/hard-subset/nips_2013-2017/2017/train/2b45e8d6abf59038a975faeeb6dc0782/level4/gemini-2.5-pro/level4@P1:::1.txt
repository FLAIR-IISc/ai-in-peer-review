This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two sets of samples (or mini-batches) drawn from the distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The proposed method is motivated as an alternative to the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), aiming to address MMD's sensitivity to kernel hyperparameters, issues with weak gradients, and its purported requirement for large mini-batches. The PMD objective is optimized via stochastic gradient descent, using either an exact O(N^3) or an approximate O(N^2) algorithm to solve the matching problem at each step. The authors demonstrate the utility of PMD on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposal to use a direct, sample-based approximation of the primal form of the Wasserstein-1 distance as a training objective is an interesting and novel contribution.
*   The theoretical analysis providing a proof of strong consistency for PMD as an estimator of the Wasserstein metric establishes a solid foundation for the method.
*   The paper presents empirical results on both generative modeling and domain adaptation, offering a direct comparison against MMD and demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed discrepancy measure.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Sample Complexity and Estimation Quality:** A primary concern is that a reliable estimation of the Wasserstein distance, especially for diverse and multimodal distributions, requires a very large sample size N. With an insufficient N, the matching problem may not accurately reflect the true underlying geometry of the distributions. I suspect that this limitation would become apparent even in moderately complex scenarios, such as learning a mixture of Gaussians with unknown variances.
*   **Biased Objective and Mode Collapse:** The use of a finite, and often small, N can introduce a systematic bias into the optimization objective. This may lead the model to converge to a global minimum that does not correspond to the true data distribution. For instance, the optimization may favor solutions with lower entropy to minimize the matching cost across a small sample. The generated SVHN samples in Figure 4 seem to support this concern, as they exhibit low diversity, with a noticeable over-representation of certain digits (e.g., '8').
*   **Comparison with MMD and Gradient Estimation:** The paper's claim that MMD requires large mini-batches for training is a central motivation, but this point is debatable. The original work on MMD ("A Kernel Two-Sample Test," Gretton et al.) introduced an unbiased estimator that can be effectively used with stochastic gradient descent and very small mini-batch sizes (as small as 2). Therefore, MMD can converge to the correct distribution using small mini-batches. In contrast, the gradient estimator proposed for PMD is biased, as the optimal matching M* is determined from the current mini-batch and then treated as a constant for the gradient calculation. The paper does not provide an unbiased estimator for the PMD gradient, which is a significant theoretical and practical drawback compared to MMD.
*   **Generative Quality:** While the primary comparison is with MMD, the quality of the generated images appears to be lower than that achieved by other contemporary methods based on the Wasserstein distance, such as Wasserstein GAN (WGAN). This raises questions about the practical advantages of this particular estimation approach for high-fidelity generative modeling.

**Post-rebuttal update:**

I have read the author's rebuttal and thank them for the additional experiments. However, my core concerns remain. The limitations regarding the biased gradient estimator for PMD compared to the unbiased estimator available for MMD are significant. The authors should clarify this distinction and the resulting implications for optimization and convergence, as it directly challenges one of the paper's main motivations for preferring PMD over MMD.
