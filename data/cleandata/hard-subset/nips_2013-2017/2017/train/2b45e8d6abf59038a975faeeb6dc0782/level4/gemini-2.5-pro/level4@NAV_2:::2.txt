This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, and proposes it as an alternative to the commonly used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for comparing probability distributions. The authors establish that PMD, defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples, is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric. The key advantages highlighted over MMD include the absence of a kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, the presence of stronger gradients during optimization, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes for stochastic gradient descent. The utility of PMD is demonstrated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper effectively addresses well-known practical limitations of MMD when used as a training objective in deep learning, namely its sensitivity to the kernel bandwidth, the potential for vanishing gradients, and the requirement of large mini-batches for a stable estimate, which can slow down training.
*   The proposed method is theoretically well-founded, connecting the sample-based matching problem directly to the Wasserstein metric. This provides a clear and interpretable alternative to other sample-based divergences.
*   The most significant practical contribution is PMD's compatibility with small mini-batches for SGD updates, as distinct from the larger population size used for matching. The empirical results convincingly validate this claim (Fig. 5a), demonstrating that this property can lead to faster convergence and better final performance compared to MMD.
*   The approach is an interesting alternative to recent Wasserstein-based methods like WGAN, as it approximates the primal form of the metric directly without requiring an auxiliary parameterized critic network.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The empirical validation for the "stronger gradients" claim could be more robust. The gradient of MMD is highly sensitive to the choice of the kernel bandwidth (σ). The comparison presented in Figure 2(b) would be more compelling if it benchmarked against MMD using an optimally chosen σ or a mixture of kernels with different bandwidths, as is common practice in the literature to avoid issues with kernel saturation.
*   The claim of having "fewer hyper-parameters" is not entirely convincing. PMD removes the kernel bandwidth but introduces the choice of a distance metric `d(·, ·)`. This choice is itself a critical, non-trivial hyperparameter. The paper uses the L1 distance, but in high-dimensional and structured spaces (like images), pixel-wise L1/L2 distances are often poor measures of perceptual similarity. The acknowledged blurriness of generated images might be a direct consequence of this choice. The paper would be stronger if it discussed the sensitivity of PMD to this metric choice.
*   Related to the point above, standard heuristics for setting the MMD bandwidth (e.g., the median heuristic) are often used to make MMD effectively "hyperparameter-free" in practice. A comparison against MMD using such heuristics would provide a fairer assessment of the practical benefits of PMD in this regard.
*   The computational cost of solving the matching problem, even with the O(N^2) approximation, could be a bottleneck compared to the O(N^2) closed-form MMD computation, especially as N grows. While the authors argue the cost is acceptable relative to backpropagation, it remains an additional complexity.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a valuable and well-motivated contribution by proposing PMD as a practical estimator for the Wasserstein distance. Its primary strength lies in enabling more efficient training via small mini-batches, offering a clear advantage over traditional MMD-based methods that require large batches. The method is a valid and interesting improvement in the space of sample-based distribution matching. However, the claims regarding stronger gradients and fewer hyperparameters could be substantiated more rigorously. The method's effectiveness appears to hinge on the choice of an appropriate distance metric, which may limit its out-of-the-box success on complex, high-dimensional data where designing such metrics is a significant challenge in itself.
