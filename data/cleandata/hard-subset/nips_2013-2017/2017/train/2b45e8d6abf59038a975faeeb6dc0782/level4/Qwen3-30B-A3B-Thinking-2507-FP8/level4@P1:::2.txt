

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a new method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. The authors argue that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Earth Mover's Distance), and propose an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD as the objective. The paper compares PMD to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), which has been widely adopted in deep learning tasks such as domain adaptation and generative modeling but suffers from sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, weak gradients, and large mini-batch requirements.

The paper makes several compelling claims about PMD's advantages over MMD. First, PMD has no bandwidth hyperparameter, eliminating the need for tuning a critical parameter that significantly affects MMD's performance. Second, PMD provides stronger gradients, as demonstrated in Figure 2(b), which shows PMD gradients maintain consistent magnitude across samples, while MMD gradients vary significantly, with many samples receiving weak gradients. Third, PMD allows for smaller mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100 instead of 1000), which leads to faster convergence as shown in Figure 3(a) for domain adaptation and Figure 5 for generative modeling. These claims are well-supported by the empirical results.

The theoretical foundation of PMD is solid. The authors prove PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, establishing a clear connection between PMD and optimal transport theory. They demonstrate that the minimum weight matching problem used in PMD corresponds to the optimal transport problem, which is well-motivated and supported by Propositions 3.1-3.3.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare PMD with MMD on two challenging tasks: domain adaptation (using the Office dataset) and generative modeling (using MNIST, SVHN, and LFW datasets). In both cases, PMD outperforms MMD in terms of final performance and convergence speed. The ablation studies in Section 5.3 provide strong evidence for the benefits of PMD's smaller mini-batch size, and the comparison of convergence speed in Figure 3(a) clearly demonstrates PMD's advantage.

However, there are important limitations to the claims made in the paper. The authors claim PMD has "fewer hyperparameters" than MMD, but this claim is weakened by the fact that PMD depends on a distance metric (e.g., L1 or L2), which may contain hyperparameters of its own. While L1 distance is used in the experiments, other distance metrics (e.g., weighted L1 or Lp distance) could introduce additional hyperparameters. The paper doesn't adequately address how to select an appropriate distance metric in high-dimensional spaces, which may limit PMD's applicability to complex data like natural images.

The claim about stronger gradients could be better supported by comparing against best practices for MMD bandwidth selection. The authors acknowledge MMD's performance is sensitive to the bandwidth parameter σ, but they don't compare against state-of-the-art bandwidth selection methods (e.g., the median heuristic or multiple kernel approaches). Without such a comparison, it's difficult to conclude that PMD's gradient strength is inherently better, as the observed advantage might be due to the specific bandwidth choice used for MMD in the experiments.

The paper also doesn't fully address the challenge of distance metric selection in high-dimensional spaces. While PMD uses L1 distance in the experiments, the authors acknowledge that "pixel-level L1 distance is not good for natural images" and note that PMD generates blurry images on SVHN and LFW datasets, suggesting that the distance metric choice is critical for success.

Overall, the paper presents a promising alternative to MMD that addresses several practical limitations. The ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes is a significant advantage for training efficiency, and the theoretical foundation is sound. The empirical results strongly support the claim that PMD leads to faster convergence than MMD. However, the "fewer hyperparameters" claim needs qualification, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of distance metric selection in high-dimensional spaces.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical foundation connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric
- Demonstrated advantages over MMD in terms of gradient strength and mini-batch size
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation on two important deep learning tasks
- Clear demonstration of faster convergence speed

Weaknesses:
- The "fewer hyperparameters" claim is not fully supported
- Limited discussion of distance metric selection in high-dimensional spaces
- The gradient strength comparison could be strengthened by comparing against best practices for MMD bandwidth selection

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, particularly for applications where training efficiency is important. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. With minor clarifications regarding hyperparameter claims and distance metric selection, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
