This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a metric defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches sampled from two distributions. The Wasserstein distance is computed using either an exact O(N^3) algorithm or an approximate O(N^2) algorithm, where N is the minibatch size.

**Strengths:**
*   The experimental approach of computing the Wasserstein distance on minibatches is interesting to see in practice. While the quality of the generated images does not match that of Wasserstein GANs, this direct application is a novel exploration.

**Weaknesses:**
*   A primary concern is that the proposed distance requires a large minibatch size (N) to accurately estimate the true Wasserstein distance between two diverse, multimodal distributions. I suspect this limitation would become apparent even in simpler synthetic settings, such as learning a mixture of Gaussians (including the variances).
*   If N is not sufficiently large, the optimization objective may possess a global minimum that does not correspond to the true data distribution. This can cause the learned distribution to have lower entropy or suffer from mode collapse. The SVHN samples presented in Figure 4 appear to support this concern, as they exhibit low diversity with a noticeable overrepresentation of the digit '8'.

**Update After Rebuttal:**
I have read the author's rebuttal and thank them for the additional experiments.

The authors should clarify the limitations of PMD, especially in comparison to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). MMD performs well even with very small batch sizes (e.g., a batch size of 2 is sufficient). Crucially, as shown in the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper, MMD has an unbiased estimator that allows for stable training with Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). Consequently, an MMD-based objective can converge to the correct distribution even when using small minibatches.

In contrast, the proposed PMD does not have a known unbiased estimator for its gradient. This is a significant theoretical drawback, as it implies that training with small minibatches may introduce bias and prevent convergence to the correct target distribution.
