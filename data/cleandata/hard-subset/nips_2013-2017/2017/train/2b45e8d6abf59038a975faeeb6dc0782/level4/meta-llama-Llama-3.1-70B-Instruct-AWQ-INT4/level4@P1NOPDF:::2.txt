This paper presents the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a superior alternative to the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching applications. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein metric, or earth mover's distance, and highlight several advantages over MMD, most notably stronger gradients and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes, as well as fewer hyperparameters.

For training generative models, at least, the MMD metric is known to suffer from weak gradients and the requirement of large mini-batches. The proposals in this paper offer a nice solution to both of these problems. The empirical results convincingly verify the claim of smaller mini-batch sizes. However, the verification of the stronger gradients claim is less satisfactory. Since the MMD metric depends on the scale parameter sigma, it is essential to consider either the best sigma or a range of sigmas when making such a claim to ensure a fair comparison.

Regarding the claim of having fewer hyperparameters, I feel that this is less well-supported. While PMD may not have the same hyperparameters as MMD, it does depend on a distance metric, which can itself contain additional hyperparameters. Moreover, obtaining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional space can be challenging, which may limit the success of PMD in modeling higher-dimensional data. In contrast, there are established heuristics for choosing the bandwidth parameter in MMD, which could be used to make MMD a hyperparameter-free metric. It would be beneficial to compare PMD against these heuristics.

Overall, I believe that the proposed method has the desirable property of permitting small mini-batch sizes, which enables faster training. This appears to be a valid improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, the reliance on a distance metric remains a potential limitation, particularly when modeling higher-dimensional data.
