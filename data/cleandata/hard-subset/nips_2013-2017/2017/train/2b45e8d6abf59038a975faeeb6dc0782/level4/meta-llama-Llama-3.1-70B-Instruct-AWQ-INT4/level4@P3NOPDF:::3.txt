The authors present PMD, a population-based divergence between probability distributions, and demonstrate its effectiveness as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance. The proposed estimator is conceptually simple and differentiable, allowing for seamless integration with neural network-based models. This is a significant advantage, as it enables the training of complex models that can leverage the strengths of PMD.

One of the notable strengths of the paper is the thorough comparison between PMD and MMD, the most prominent population-based divergence in machine learning. This comparison provides valuable insights into the relative merits of each approach and helps to establish PMD as a viable alternative. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their method, specifically the cubic complexity of exact calculation, and propose an approximation with quadratic complexity. They demonstrate through empirical results that this approximation does not significantly degrade statistical performance.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with proper citations to relevant previous work. The theoretical results appear to be correct, and the experimental analysis is adequate. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of PMD to MMD and other methods in the context of domain adaptation, as well as a comparison to MMD for generative modeling. These experiments help to validate the effectiveness of PMD in various scenarios.

One potential avenue for future exploration is the application of PMD to generative modeling in domains with multiple modes. It would be interesting to investigate whether PMD remains effective when the sample size is smaller than the number of modes. This could provide further insight into the robustness and versatility of the proposed method.

In conclusion, this is a well-written paper that presents a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. The authors have made a compelling case for the effectiveness of PMD, and their work is likely to be of interest to researchers in machine learning and related fields.
