The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) between two distributions, defined as the Wasserstein distance computed between two minibatches drawn from those distributions. The Wasserstein distance is evaluated either exactly in O(N^3) time or approximately in O(N^2) time.

Pros:
- The experimental exploration of this Wasserstein-based objective is interesting.
- However, the generated images are not as compelling as those produced by Wasserstein GANs.

Cons:
- Accurately estimating the Wasserstein distance between diverse, multimodal distributions likely requires large minibatch sizes N. I suspect issues would already appear when matching a mixture of Gaussians (including learning the variances).
- With insufficient N, the optimization may attain a global minimum that does not correspond to the true distribution; for instance, the learned distribution may have lower entropy. The SVHN samples in Figure 4 appear to have limited diversity; the digit 8 shows up disproportionately often.

Update:
I have read the rebuttal—thank you for the additional experiments. The authors should clarify the limitations of both PMD and MMD. MMD works even with batch_size=2 and can be trained via SGD when using the unbiased estimator from the original “A Kernel Two-Sample Test” paper. Thus, MMD can converge to the correct distribution with small minibatches. In contrast, PMD currently lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator.
