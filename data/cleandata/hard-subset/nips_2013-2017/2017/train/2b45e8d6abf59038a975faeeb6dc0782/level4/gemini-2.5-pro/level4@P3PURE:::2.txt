This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. The authors show that PMD is a sample-based approximation of the Wasserstein metric (or Earth Mover's Distance) and claim several advantages over MMD, most notably stronger training gradients, applicability with smaller mini-batch sizes, and fewer hyperparameters.

It is well-known that when training generative models, MMD can suffer from weak gradients and requires large mini-batches. The proposed method offers a potential solution to these two problems. The claim of effectiveness with small mini-batches is well-supported by the empirical results. However, the verification of the "stronger gradients" claim is less convincing. Since the MMD metric's performance is highly dependent on the kernel scale parameter (sigma), a fair comparison would require evaluating MMD with either an optimally tuned sigma or across a range of sigmas.

The claim of having fewer hyperparameters is also not fully substantiated. PMD relies on a chosen distance metric, which may itself introduce hyperparameters, similar to the kernel in MMD. Furthermore, defining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces is a known challenge, suggesting that PMD may face similar difficulties to MMD in this regard. Conversely, standard heuristics exist for setting the MMD bandwidth parameter, which can make it effectively hyperparameter-free in practice. It would strengthen the paper to compare PMD against an MMD baseline that employs such heuristics.

Overall, the proposed method's key advantage is its ability to operate with small mini-batch sizes, which can significantly speed up training. This makes it a promising improvement over MMD methods that require large batches. However, the method's fundamental reliance on a distance metric remains a concern, as this may limit its applicability and success when modeling complex, high-dimensional data.
