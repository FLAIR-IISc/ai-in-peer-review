Summary
The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based objective for matching distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum-weight matching between two sample sets and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance. The authors propose using PMD as a differentiable training objective optimized via SGD, and argue it offers practical advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD): stronger gradients, the ability to use smaller mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters. Empirically, PMD is evaluated on domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, where it outperforms MMD in both accuracy and convergence speed.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned as an alternative to MMD-based training, which is known to require careful bandwidth tuning and large batches in generative modeling. PMD closely relates to the empirical Wasserstein-1 distance via optimal transport. The proposed use as a training objective and the accompanying optimization scheme target known limitations of MMD.

Strengths
- Addresses concrete drawbacks of MMD in generative modeling: weak gradients and the need for large mini-batches.
- The empirical evidence for the small mini-batch claim is convincing and shows clear training speed benefits.
- The formulation is intuitive, grounded in optimal transport, and comes with a consistency result.
- Practical implementation details (exact/approximate matching) and timing analyses are useful.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- “Stronger gradient” claim: The current evidence is less persuasive because MMD’s gradients depend critically on the kernel bandwidth σ. To substantiate this claim, the comparison should include either the best σ or a reasonable range (e.g., median heuristic, mixtures), and show robustness across these choices.
- “Fewer hyperparameters” claim: While PMD removes kernel bandwidth tuning, it still depends on the choice of the ground distance, which can itself involve hyperparameters and design choices (e.g., L1 vs. L2, feature vs. pixel space). In high-dimensional spaces, distances can be unreliable; PMD may thus inherit sensitivity to the metric, analogous to MMD’s sensitivity to σ. Since standard heuristics exist for MMD bandwidth selection, treating MMD as hyperparameter-free under such heuristics would make for a fairer comparison.
- High-dimensional data: The reliance on a raw metric is a potential weakness for complex, high-dimensional data. The generative results suggest PMD performs better in code space than pixel space, which supports this concern and underscores the need to discuss or test metric choices more broadly.

Quality
The methodology is sound and the consistency result is appropriate for the proposed estimator. The training claims are supported for mini-batch size; the gradient-strength claim needs broader σ comparisons for MMD. It would also help to include sensitivity analyses for the ground metric (choice and any parameters) and, where possible, comparisons using standard bandwidth heuristics or mixtures for MMD.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear. It would benefit from:
- Explicit discussion of the ground metric choice, any associated hyperparameters, and sensitivity to this choice.
- A more thorough treatment of the MMD baseline, including standard bandwidth selection heuristics and mixtures.
- Clarification of when approximate vs. exact matching is used, and how much this affects performance across tasks.

Originality
PMD corresponds to the empirical Wasserstein-1 distance via minimum-weight matching. The novelty lies primarily in leveraging this estimator as a differentiable training objective with practical optimization strategies and demonstrating its empirical advantages over MMD.

Significance
The ability to train with smaller mini-batches is a meaningful practical contribution, particularly for deep generative modeling. However, the dependence on a ground metric may limit applicability on high-dimensional data unless suitable feature spaces are used or metric choices are carefully justified.

Pros
- Clear practical benefit: effective training with small mini-batches.
- Empirically competitive or superior to MMD on tested tasks.
- Conceptually simple and well-grounded in optimal transport.

Cons
- Evidence for stronger gradients vs. MMD is incomplete without bandwidth tuning baselines.
- Claims about fewer hyperparameters overlook the importance and difficulty of choosing a ground metric in high dimensions.
- Potential limitations on high-dimensional data where raw distances are unreliable.

Recommendation
A promising and practically useful contribution that improves over large-batch MMD methods by enabling faster training with smaller batches. To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
- Comparing against MMD with standard bandwidth heuristics (e.g., median trick, kernel mixtures) when evaluating gradient strength and overall performance.
- Adding sensitivity analyses and discussion of the ground metric choice for PMD, especially in high-dimensional settings.
