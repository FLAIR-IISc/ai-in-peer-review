This paper introduces the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to MMD for distribution matching. PMD is presented as a sampled approximation to the Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance, and is argued to have several advantages over MMD: stronger gradients, effective use with smaller mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters.

In the context of training generative models, the weaknesses of MMD—namely, weak gradients and the need for large mini-batches—are well known. The paper’s proposals address both issues, and the empirical results convincingly support the claim that PMD works well with small mini-batches.

However, the evidence for the “stronger gradients” claim is less compelling. Because MMD critically depends on the kernel bandwidth (sigma), any comparison should account for the best choice of sigma or report performance across a range of sigmas. Without such a sweep or a principled selection procedure, it is difficult to conclude that PMD consistently provides stronger gradients than a well-tuned MMD.

The claim of “fewer hyperparameters” is also not fully supported. PMD depends on a distance metric, which itself may introduce hyperparameters (e.g., scaling or learned metrics), much like MMD depends on a kernel and its bandwidth. Moreover, reliable distance estimation in high-dimensional spaces is challenging; as a result, PMD may inherit the same fundamental difficulties associated with distance-based methods in high dimensions. By contrast, for MMD there exist standard bandwidth heuristics; it would be fair to compare against those and, to some extent, treat MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free under these heuristics.

Overall, the method’s ability to enable small mini-batch training—and thus faster iterations—is a notable practical advantage, and the approach appears to offer a meaningful improvement over large-batch MMD-based methods. Nonetheless, its reliance on a distance metric may limit applicability to higher-dimensional data, where distance measures are notoriously fragile.

Suggestions:
- Substantiate the “stronger gradients” claim by evaluating MMD across a range of bandwidths or using best-of-sweep sigma selections.
- Compare against standard MMD bandwidth heuristics to provide a fair assessment of hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Discuss the choice of distance metric for PMD and its robustness in high-dimensional settings, including any associated hyperparameters.
