- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein distance via minimum weight matching.
- Applies PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, claiming advantages over MMD in hyperparameter sensitivity, gradient behavior, and mini-batch requirements.
- Theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is clear and a proposition proving strong consistency is provided.
- Paper is well-structured with logical presentation of method, theory, and experiments.
- Experimental comparisons to MMD on standard benchmarks are reasonable and show PMD avoiding MMD’s bandwidth tuning issue.
- Computational cost of the matching problem is acknowledged but practical implications for high-dimensional data and large sample sizes are insufficiently explored.
- Claim of “stronger gradients” is under-justified; Figure 2(b) is limited and more rigorous analysis of gradient magnitude and variance is needed.
- Discussion of PMD limitations for small N is incomplete and does not fully address potential convergence to suboptimal (e.g., lower-entropy) solutions.
- Empirical concern: limited diversity in generated SVHN samples (digit 8 overrepresented) suggests possible learning or diversity issues.
- Rebuttal addresses some points but leaves key concerns unresolved.
- Valid point that MMD can be trained with small mini-batches using an unbiased estimator, weakening PMD’s claimed mini-batch advantage.
- Lack of an unbiased estimator for the PMD gradient remains a notable drawback.
- Paper should more clearly compare limitations and convergence guarantees of both PMD and MMD, especially w.r.t. sample size sensitivity.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel OT-based estimator, theoretical guarantees, demonstrated empirical improvements, and no bandwidth tuning.
- Arguments against acceptance: high computational cost at scale, limited gradient/convergence analysis, sample diversity concerns, and weakened mini-batch advantage after rebuttal.
- Overall assessment: novel and theoretically sound with promising results, but outstanding concerns about scalability, gradient behavior, and convergence — reviewer leans weak accept pending revisions.
- Minor issue: typo on Line 141 — “usally” should be “usually.”
