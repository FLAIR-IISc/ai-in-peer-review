Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based discrepancy defined as the average cost of the minimum-weight bipartite matching between two equal-size sets of samples drawn from two distributions. With a fixed ground metric (e.g., L1), PMD coincides with the empirical optimal transport (OT) cost (earth mover’s distance) under uniform weights. The authors prove strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of the first Wasserstein distance and propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective by computing matchings for sampled “populations,” then performing several SGD updates while keeping the matching fixed. They compare PMD to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) on domain adaptation and generative modeling, arguing that PMD has fewer hyperparameters, yields stronger gradients, and allows smaller mini-batches.

The work is related to a rich literature on OT and Wasserstein metrics (Villani; Rubner et al.), as well as recent deep generative modeling with Wasserstein objectives (WGAN) and sample-based divergences (MMD/GMMN). A notable omission is entropically regularized OT (“Sinkhorn distances,” Cuturi, NIPS 2013), which provides fast, differentiable approximations to Wasserstein distances and has been widely used in deep learning contexts; a comparison there would be informative. The paper also connects to energy/Cramér distances (e.g., Bellemare et al., 2017).

Strengths
- Clear formulation: PMD is a straightforward, parameter-free empirical OT objective with a principled consistency guarantee.
- Practical algorithm: The decoupling of matching from gradient steps is simple to implement; approximate matching reduces cost from O(N^3) to O(N^2).
- Empirical findings: On Office domain adaptation, PMD outperforms or matches MMD on several shifts and converges faster. For generative modeling, PMD produces competitive samples without adversarial training and is comparatively stable.
- Insightful discussion of gradients: The paper provides an intuitive analysis and empirical evidence that PMD yields more uniformly strong sample-wise gradients than RBF-MMD.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Finite-sample behavior and sample complexity: While strong consistency is standard for empirical OT, the paper lacks finite-sample error rates or guidance on the population size N needed for multimodal, high-variance distributions. This matters because minimum-cost one-to-one matching can couple samples across modes when N is small, potentially biasing training toward low-entropy solutions.
- Limited stress tests: A simple multimodal toy (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians with varying variances and separated modes) would reveal PMD’s robustness to mode coverage and variance estimation under limited N. The SVHN samples show low diversity (e.g., many 8’s), suggesting mode under-coverage.
- Comparisons and missing baselines: No comparison to Sinkhorn OT, which is fast, differentiable, and scalable on GPUs. WGAN is briefly discussed, but stronger image quality baselines (e.g., WGAN-GP) are not quantitatively compared. For MMD, the paper focuses on biased estimators with large batches; unbiased (U-statistic or linear-time) MMD estimators permit SGD with small batches and should be included to support the “smaller mini-batch” claim for PMD.
- Differentiability and gradient estimation: The paper states the gradient does not propagate through the discrete matching. While subgradients exist almost everywhere, the unbiasedness of the stochastic gradient estimator deserves a more careful justification (non-smooth objective; matching can change with θ). Clarifying conditions under which the estimator is unbiased (or demonstrating empirically that it behaves well) would strengthen the claims.
- Evaluation metrics: Generative results are presented visually. Including quantitative metrics (e.g., class-conditional diversity on SVHN, precision/recall for generative models) would substantiate the mode-coverage claim. For domain adaptation, results improve on 2/6 shifts and are comparable otherwise; ablations and significance testing would help.

Quality
- Theory: Correct and clearly presented consistency argument for PMD as empirical OT; however, this result is well known in OT literature. No finite-sample guarantees or complexity/error trade-offs.
- Algorithms: Exact and approximate matching are standard; empirical timing suggests feasibility at modest N. The proposed inner-loop SGD with fixed matching is reasonable and effective in practice.
- Experiments: Mixed but promising. Convergence speed results are clear; sample quality is acceptable but not state-of-the-art. Claims about gradient strength are supported by a useful diagnostic.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with helpful pseudocode and figures. Distinguishing “population size” from “mini-batch size” earlier would reduce confusion. Provide explicit wall-clock comparisons including GPU costs where possible. Minor typo: “usally” → “usually” (line 141).

Originality
- Using empirical OT (minimum matching) as a training objective is natural and has appeared in related forms; the main novelty is the “population matching” framing and the practical inner-loop optimization strategy. Missing discussion of Sinkhorn distances is a significant gap.

Significance
- Moderate. PMD is a useful alternative to MMD/adversarial objectives with practical benefits (no kernel bandwidth, stable gradients). However, broader impact is limited by scalability/sample-complexity concerns and the absence of comparisons to established OT solvers used in deep learning.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, principled objective with solid consistency footing.
- Practical training recipe with approximate matching and faster convergence than MMD in reported tasks.
- Parameter-free and stable gradients; useful for practitioners wary of adversarial training.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete evaluation and missing key baselines (unbiased MMD with small batches; Sinkhorn OT; stronger Wasserstein GANs).
- Limited analysis of finite-sample behavior and potential mode-collapse/entropy reduction with small N.
- Generative results are not competitive with state-of-the-art and lack quantitative metrics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add controlled multimodal experiments (mixture of Gaussians with varying separation/variance) to study sample complexity, mode coverage, and entropy.
- Compare against Sinkhorn-regularized OT, and include unbiased/linear-time MMD baselines using small batches.
- Provide quantitative diversity metrics for generative models (e.g., label distribution on SVHN, precision/recall curves).
- Clarify the stochastic gradient estimator’s properties for the non-smooth objective and discuss when fixing the matching in the inner loop is safe.
- Discuss limitations explicitly (e.g., need for larger N on complex, multimodal distributions; potential low-entropy bias; sensitivity to ground metric; benefits of computing PMD in feature space).

Update after rebuttal
Thank you for the additional experiments. The paper would benefit from explicitly clarifying limitations of both PMD and MMD. In particular: (1) unbiased or linear-time estimators allow MMD to train with small mini-batches (even size 2) via SGD, mitigating the “large-batch” criticism; (2) please clarify whether PMD admits an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator and under what assumptions. Incorporating these clarifications and the suggested baselines would substantially strengthen the paper.
