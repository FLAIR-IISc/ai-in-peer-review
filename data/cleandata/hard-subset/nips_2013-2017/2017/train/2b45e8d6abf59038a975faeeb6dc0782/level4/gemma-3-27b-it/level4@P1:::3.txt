The authors present Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a population-based divergence measure between probability distributions, and demonstrate that it is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance.

The estimator is conceptually simple and differentiable, which allows for training neural network-based models. The authors thoroughly compare PMD with MMD, the most prominent population-based divergence in machine learning. They acknowledge the computational cost of their method – cubic complexity for exact calculation – and propose an approximation with quadratic complexity, demonstrating empirically that this does not significantly degrade performance.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate references to related work. The theoretical results appear sound. The experimental analysis is adequate, comparing PMD to MMD in domain adaptation and to MMD in generative modeling. 

I would have liked to see the method evaluated on generative modeling tasks with multi-modal distributions. It’s worth considering whether PMD performs well when the population size *N* is smaller than the number of modes in the data. 

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** PMD provides a new approach to estimating the Wasserstein distance, offering advantages over existing methods like MMD.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper establishes a strong theoretical foundation, proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric.
*   **Practical Advantages:** PMD addresses limitations of MMD, such as sensitivity to bandwidth selection and potentially requiring larger mini-batch sizes.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments demonstrate that PMD performs competitively with, and in some cases outperforms, MMD on relevant tasks (domain adaptation and generative modeling).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and explains the concepts in a clear and accessible manner.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While an approximation is proposed, the computational complexity remains a potential concern for very large datasets.
*   **Limited Generative Modeling Evaluation:** The generative modeling experiments could be strengthened by including more challenging datasets with multi-modal distributions.
