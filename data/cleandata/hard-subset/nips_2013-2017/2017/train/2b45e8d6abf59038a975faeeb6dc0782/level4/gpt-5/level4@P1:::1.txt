Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of distributional distance defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets. This is exactly the 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between the empirical distributions of the two minibatches. The authors show PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the underlying Wasserstein-1 distance and present a stochastic optimization procedure that alternates computing a matching and performing several SGD steps while holding the matching fixed. Exact matching uses the Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)); an O(N^2) approximate algorithm is also considered. The work is positioned against maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), arguing PMD avoids kernel bandwidth tuning, yields stronger gradients, and permits smaller SGD minibatches. Applications are shown in domain adaptation (Office dataset) and deep generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW). The paper also discusses connections to dual OT approximations and WGAN.

Strengths:
- Clear formulation: PMD as empirical OT between minibatches, with a straightforward consistency argument via Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of OT.
- Practical algorithmic details: exact and approximate matchers; “frozen matching” inner-loop SGD; empirical study of gradient magnitudes and batch-size effects.
- Domain adaptation results against MMD are encouraging on several transfer directions, with faster convergence.
- It is useful to see a systematic empirical comparison between PMD and MMD under matched settings.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Sample complexity for multimodal distributions: Estimating Wasserstein accurately between complex, diverse, multimodal distributions typically requires large N. I suspect issues would appear already on mixtures of Gaussians (including learning variances). This affects both estimation and optimization when PMD is used as a training objective.
- Optimization bias at small N: If the minibatch population is not large enough, the PMD-based objective may have a global minimum different from the true distribution, favoring lower-entropy solutions. In the SVHN samples (Figure 4), diversity appears limited (e.g., digit 8 occurs disproportionately often), consistent with reduced entropy or mode undercoverage.
- Comparative generative quality: The generated images are not as good as those produced by Wasserstein GANs in the literature, limiting the significance of PMD for generative modeling as currently instantiated (especially in pixel space with L1).
- Claims about minibatch sizes: The paper emphasizes that MMD “requires large minibatches,” whereas PMD can use small minibatches. However, MMD can be trained with SGD using the unbiased U-statistic estimator from “A Kernel Two-Sample Test” (Gretton et al.), and in practice can work with very small batch sizes (even batch_size = 2). In contrast, PMD does not have a known unbiased estimator of the gradient, so its minibatch advantage should be qualified.
- Compute and scalability: Even with O(N^2) approximate matching, the matching step is a nontrivial overhead unless GPU-accelerated; the current implementation is single-threaded CPU. The paper notes GPU acceleration is possible, but actual end-to-end GPU results would strengthen the case.

Quality:
- Theoretical claims are correct as stated and rely on standard results; however, finite-sample error bounds are not provided, and the empirical scaling of error with N is modest (roughly N^-0.23).
- Experimental methodology is mostly solid, but the generative comparisons do not include WGAN baselines, and evidence of diversity/mode coverage is limited.

Clarity:
- Generally well-organized. Please clarify the “population” vs “minibatch” terminology to avoid confusion with MMD literature, and explicitly state that gradients do not backpropagate through the discrete matching.
- Minor typo: Line 141, “usally” → “usually.”
- It would help to explicitly discuss the limitations of both PMD and MMD under small minibatches and multimodality.

Originality:
- Using empirical OT (minimum matching) as a training loss is known in OT literature, but the paper’s combination of a consistency proof, practical approximations, and empirical analysis against MMD in deep settings is a useful contribution.

Significance:
- For domain adaptation, PMD is competitive and sometimes superior to MMD with faster convergence; this is practically interesting. For generative modeling, results are weaker than WGAN-quality images, suggesting limited impact in that area unless combined with better perceptual metrics or feature spaces.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and principled formulation with a consistency guarantee.
- Practical algorithm with empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence than MMD in domain adaptation.
- Useful empirical insights into PMD vs MMD behavior under varying N and batch sizes.

Cons for acceptance:
- Potential need for large N in multimodal settings; risk of low-entropy minima at small N.
- Generative results lag behind WGAN; limited diversity observed on SVHN.
- Overstated claim about MMD requiring large minibatches; MMD admits an unbiased estimator usable with very small batches, whereas PMD lacks an unbiased gradient estimator.
- Computational overhead of matching without demonstrated GPU acceleration.

Suggestions:
- Add experiments on synthetic mixtures of Gaussians (including variance learning) to probe sample complexity and optimization behavior.
- Quantify diversity/mode coverage (e.g., class-conditional coverage on SVHN) and report metrics beyond visual inspection.
- Clarify and temper claims about minibatch size advantages relative to MMD’s unbiased estimator.
- Discuss practical guidance on choosing N and matching approximation, and the trade-offs between accuracy and speed.

I appreciate the additional experiments. Please explicitly clarify the limitations of both PMD and MMD. In particular, MMD can be trained with SGD using the unbiased estimator from “A Kernel Two-Sample Test,” allowing very small minibatches (e.g., batch_size = 2) while converging to the correct distribution.
