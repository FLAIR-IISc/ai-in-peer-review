- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based distance between distributions defined via minimum weight matching.  
- Shows PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance.  
- Introduces a stochastic gradient descent algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD.  
- Paper is well-written with thorough theoretical analysis and connections to other discrepancies such as MMD.  
- Experiments demonstrate PMD’s effectiveness in domain adaptation and generative modeling with promising results.  
- PMD provides a differentiable distance estimator beneficial for deep learning tasks.  
- Claimed advantages over MMD: fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes for optimization.  
- Limitation: PMD may require large sample size N to accurately estimate Wasserstein distance for diverse multimodal distributions.  
- Optimization concern: with small N the global minimum for training may diverge from the true distribution, yielding lower-entropy learned distributions.  
- Generative-modeling results show limited sample diversity (e.g., overrepresented digit 8 in SVHN); authors should investigate and show more diverse outputs.  
- Minor typo noted: “usally” instead of “usually” on line 141.  
- Rebuttal added experiments and clarifications but did not fully resolve limitations around small mini-batches and PMD’s gradient estimation.  
- Important point: MMD has an unbiased SGD-compatible estimator that can work with small mini-batches, whereas PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, solid theoretical analysis, and effective experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: need for large N, potential wrong global minima with small N, and low diversity in generated samples.  
- Overall rating 7/10 with recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address limitations on mini-batches and sample diversity.
