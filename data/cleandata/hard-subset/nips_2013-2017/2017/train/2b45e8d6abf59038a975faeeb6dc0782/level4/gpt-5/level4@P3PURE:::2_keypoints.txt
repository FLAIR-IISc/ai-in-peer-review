- Proposes PMD as a sampled approximation to the Wasserstein distance and an alternative to MMD with purported benefits (stronger gradients, small-batch effectiveness, fewer hyperparameters).
- Addresses known MMD weaknesses (weak gradients, need for large mini-batches) and shows empirical evidence that PMD works well with small mini-batches.
- Evidence for the “stronger gradients” claim is weak because MMD’s performance depends critically on kernel bandwidth and needs bandwidth sweeps or principled selection for fair comparison.
- The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is not fully supported since PMD depends on a distance metric that can introduce its own hyperparameters (scaling, learned metrics).
- Reliable distance estimation in high-dimensional spaces is challenging, so PMD may inherit the fragility of distance-based methods and have limited applicability in high dimensions.
- MMD has standard bandwidth heuristics; comparisons should include these to fairly assess hyperparameter sensitivity and possibly treat MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free under those heuristics.
- Overall, PMD’s ability to enable small mini-batch training and faster iterations is a notable practical advantage and a meaningful improvement over large-batch MMD methods.
- Suggestions: evaluate MMD across a range of bandwidths or best-of-sweep sigmas, compare against standard MMD bandwidth heuristics, and discuss PMD’s distance-metric choice and robustness in high-dimensional settings (including its hyperparameters).
