This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based metric for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two sets of samples (or populations) drawn from their respective distributions. The authors provide a key theoretical result, proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. This work positions PMD as an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors claim several advantages over MMD: PMD is free of the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, provides stronger gradients for optimization, and allows for more efficient training with smaller mini-batches. To demonstrate its practical utility, PMD is applied as a training objective in two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling. In these applications, the empirical results suggest that PMD can achieve better performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.

### Strengths

*   **Novelty and Theoretical Grounding:** The paper proposes a novel and intuitive method for comparing distributions. The formal connection establishing PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approach, linking it to the well-studied theory of optimal transport.
*   **Simplicity and Practicality:** A key advantage of PMD is that it is parameter-free, removing the need to tune a kernel and its associated hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidth), which is a common challenge when using MMD. It is also directly computable from samples without requiring an auxiliary parameterized model, distinguishing it from adversarial methods like Wasserstein GANs.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are valuable, demonstrating that this direct computation of the Wasserstein distance between mini-batches is a viable training objective. The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling show promising results, indicating that PMD is a competitive alternative to MMD.

### Weaknesses

*   **Finite-Sample Estimation Issues:** The primary concern with PMD lies in its reliance on a finite-sample approximation of the true Wasserstein metric. For complex, high-dimensional, or multimodal distributions, the population size (N) required for an accurate estimate might be prohibitively large. With an insufficient N, the optimization objective could be biased, potentially guiding the model towards a solution that does not fully capture the target distribution. This could manifest as mode collapse or a learned distribution with lower entropy. This concern seems to be supported by the generative modeling results in Figure 4 for SVHN, where the generated samples appear to lack diversity (e.g., a high frequency of the digit '8'). The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this limitation and the sample complexity of the estimator.
*   **Potentially Misleading Comparison to MMD:** The paper’s claims regarding the advantages of PMD over MMD in the context of training may be overstated. The argument that MMD requires a large mini-batch size is not entirely accurate. The original work on MMD ("A Kernel Two-Sample Test") introduced an unbiased stochastic estimator that allows for training with mini-batch sizes as small as two. Consequently, MMD-based models can be trained effectively with standard SGD, which challenges PMD's claimed superiority in training efficiency based on its use of small update batches.
*   **Biased Gradient Estimator:** A significant theoretical concern is that the gradient estimator for PMD appears to be biased. The derivation in Eq. (3) relies on the assumption that the optimal matching `M*` is locally constant, meaning the gradient is not propagated through the `argmin` operation. While this makes the computation tractable, it results in a biased estimator of the gradient of the true expected PMD objective (Eq. 2). In contrast, MMD possesses a known unbiased gradient estimator. The authors should acknowledge this and discuss the potential implications of using a biased gradient on the optimization process.
*   **Quality of Generated Samples:** While the experiments demonstrate the principle, the quality of the generated images appears lower than state-of-the-art generative models that also leverage the Wasserstein distance, such as WGAN.

### Overall Assessment

The paper introduces an interesting and well-motivated approach for comparing distributions. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is elegant and the parameter-free nature is appealing. However, the work has several significant weaknesses that should be addressed. The reliance on finite-sample estimation without a clear understanding of its biases is a major concern, particularly for complex distributions. Furthermore, the claims of superiority over MMD should be revised to provide a more balanced comparison that acknowledges the existence of unbiased, small-batch estimators for MMD. Finally, the use of a biased gradient estimator is a crucial point that warrants discussion. While the ideas are promising, these issues temper the significance of the contribution in its current form.
