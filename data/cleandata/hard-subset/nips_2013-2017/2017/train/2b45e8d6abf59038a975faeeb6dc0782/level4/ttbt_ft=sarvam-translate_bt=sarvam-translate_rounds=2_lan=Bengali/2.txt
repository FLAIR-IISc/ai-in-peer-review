This paper presents Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a promising alternative to MMD for distribution matching applications. It demonstrates that PMD is a sample version of the Wasserstein metric or Earth Movers distance, and has some advantages over MMD, including the applicability of strong gradients and small mini-batch sizes, and a smaller number of hyperparameters.

At least for training generative models, the MMD metric suffers from the need for weak gradients and large mini-batches. Therefore, this paper proposes a solution that provides an excellent solution to both of these problems. The claim of small mini-batches has been well-verified in empirical results. The verification of the strong gradient claim is less satisfactory, as the MMD metric depends on the scale parameter sigma. When making such a claim, it is essential to consider the best sigma or range of sigma.

Regarding the small number of hyperparameters, I believe this claim is less justified, as PMD depends on a distance metric, and this distance metric may have additional hyperparameters, as is also seen in the case of MMD. Moreover, it is difficult to find a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces, so PMD may suffer from the same problem of relying on a distance metric like MMD. On the other hand, there are some standard heuristics for choosing bandwidth parameters for MMD. Compared to such heuristics, it is better to consider MMD as a hyperparameter-free metric.

Overall, I believe the proposed method has excellent features that allow for small mini-batch sizes, which helps in faster training. This seems to be a valid improvement over the large-batch MMD method. However, there is still the problem of relying on a distance metric, which may limit its success in modeling high-dimensional data.
