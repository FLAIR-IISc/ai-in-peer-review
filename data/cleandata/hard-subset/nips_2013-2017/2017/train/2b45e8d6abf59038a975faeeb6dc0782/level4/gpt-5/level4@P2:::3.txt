Summary
This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based measure of discrepancy between probability distributions. PMD is defined via minimum-weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance. The objective is differentiable, enabling gradient-based training of neural network models without requiring an auxiliary critic. The authors provide an algorithm for parameter learning with PMD and apply it to domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. Empirically, PMD compares favorably to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a widely used sample-based divergence, in both performance and convergence speed.

Relation to prior work
PMD is positioned as a practical, differentiable estimator of the Wasserstein distance that avoids the kernel bandwidth tuning of MMD and the critic training required in adversarial estimators (e.g., WGAN). The paper offers direct comparisons to MMD across tasks, which is appropriate given MMD’s prominence in domain adaptation and generative modeling.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and practicality: PMD has a simple definition (minimum-weight matching) and yields a differentiable training objective, enabling straightforward integration into deep learning pipelines.
- Theoretical support: The authors prove strong consistency with the Wasserstein metric. The theoretical framing appears sound.
- Careful comparison to MMD: The paper discusses computational trade-offs (exact cubic versus approximate quadratic matching) and demonstrates empirically that approximate matching does not substantially degrade results.
- Empirical evaluation: Results on domain adaptation and generative modeling are solid. Comparisons to MMD are fair and demonstrate improved performance and faster convergence in several settings.
- Presentation: The paper is well organized, clearly written, and appropriately references relevant literature.

Weaknesses and questions
- Coverage of multimodal generative settings: It would strengthen the paper to include experiments on datasets with many modes (e.g., more challenging synthetic or real data) to assess PMD’s behavior when the number of modes exceeds the population size N. Does PMD degrade or bias matching in such cases?
- Computational considerations: While the quadratic approximation is effective, a more detailed analysis of its approximation error and impact on gradients during training (beyond wall-clock metrics) would be useful.
- Sensitivity analyses: Additional ablations on choice of base distance (e.g., L1 vs L2 or learned feature distances) for PMD in image domains could clarify when PMD benefits from feature-space training.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The methods and claims are well supported by theory and experiments. The consistency result is appropriate and clearly stated. The empirical methodology appears sound.
- Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well structured, with adequate detail to understand and reproduce the main results.
- Originality: While grounded in well-known optimal transport ideas, the specific formulation and use of PMD as a differentiable training objective, plus the practical training strategy, provide a useful contribution relative to MMD-based training and adversarial approaches.
- Significance: The approach addresses known limitations of MMD (kernel bandwidth sensitivity, weak gradients, large batch requirements) and provides a practical alternative for distribution matching tasks. The potential impact on domain adaptation and generative modeling is meaningful.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, principled, and differentiable estimator of Wasserstein distance.
- Strong theoretical guarantee (consistency).
- Demonstrated empirical gains over MMD in accuracy and convergence.
- Reduced hyperparameter burden compared to MMD (no kernel bandwidth).
- Practical training recipe with approximate matching and smaller minibatches.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited evidence on highly multimodal generative settings and behavior when N is smaller than the number of modes.
- Limited analysis of how approximate matching impacts optimization dynamics beyond performance and timing.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed paper proposing a practical and theoretically grounded alternative to MMD for sample-based distribution matching. The method is simple, differentiable, and effective across two important applications. While broader evaluation on multimodal generative tasks would further strengthen the work, the current results and analysis are compelling. I view this as a solid and potentially impactful contribution.
