- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of W1 using minimum-weight bipartite matching and a practical learning algorithm.  
- Shows PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance.  
- Uses a “frozen” matching to compute gradients (no gradients through discrete matching) to enable SGD.  
- Argues advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger non-saturating gradients, and smaller SGD mini-batches.  
- Empirical improvements over MMD demonstrated in domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW).  
- Theoretical connection to empirical optimal transport is sound; consistency follows from OT stability and Varadarajan’s theorem.  
- Algorithmic details include exact Hungarian (O(N^3)) and an O(N^2) approximation; practical choices and trade-offs are explained.  
- The informal justification for ignoring gradients through matching should be made more formal (subgradient or a.e. differentiability).  
- Empirical results show faster convergence and reduced hyperparameter sensitivity versus MMD, but some pixel-space samples are blurry.  
- Paper is well written and clear; plots explain algorithmic choices (population vs batch size, matching approximations).  
- Should acknowledge and compare to entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) and differentiable OT work (Genevay et al.).  
- Should compare to stronger MMD baselines (linear-time estimators, multi-kernel/bandwidth strategies).  
- Significance is promising, but broader impact needs comparisons to recent Wasserstein methods (WGAN, Sinkhorn) with standard metrics (Inception, FID).  
- Need discussion of sample complexity in high dimensions and practical mitigations (feature learning, regularization).  
- Pros: simple, theoretically grounded objective; practical algorithm; empirical advantages over MMD; clear presentation.  
- Cons: missing comparisons to scalable OT and stronger MMD baselines; informal gradient treatment; potential O(N^2) memory/time issues; limited quantitative generative evaluation; domain-adaptation tuning uses labeled target data.  
- Suggestions: add Sinkhorn and WGAN comparisons with standard metrics, evaluate multi-modal synthetic cases, provide formal subgradient treatment, strengthen MMD baselines, and report memory/runtime scaling (GPU implementation desirable).  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-presented, practically useful contribution with acceptance-leaning recommendation pending stronger positioning and quantitative evaluations.
