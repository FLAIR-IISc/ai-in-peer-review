This paper proposes population matching discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and has several advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch sizes, and fewer hyperparameters.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of MMD, particularly in the context of generative modeling. The proposed PMD method is well-defined, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of its properties, including its relationship to the Wasserstein metric.

The empirical results are impressive, demonstrating the effectiveness of PMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The authors also provide a thorough comparison with MMD, highlighting the advantages of PMD in terms of convergence speed and performance.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. Firstly, the claim that PMD has fewer hyperparameters is not entirely convincing, as the choice of distance metric may introduce additional hyperparameters. Furthermore, the authors do not provide a thorough comparison with standard heuristics for choosing the bandwidth parameter in MMD, which could be used to make MMD a hyperparameter-free metric.

Additionally, the verification of the stronger gradients claim could be improved by considering a range of sigma values for MMD. Nevertheless, the paper provides a valuable contribution to the field, and the proposed PMD method has the potential to improve the performance of generative models and other applications that rely on distribution matching.

Strengths:

* Well-motivated and clearly explained
* Thorough analysis of PMD's properties
* Impressive empirical results
* Potential to improve performance of generative models and other applications

Weaknesses:

* Claim of fewer hyperparameters is not entirely convincing
* Limited comparison with standard heuristics for MMD
* Verification of stronger gradients claim could be improved

Acceptance:

* Yes, the paper provides a valuable contribution to the field and has the potential to improve the performance of generative models and other applications.
