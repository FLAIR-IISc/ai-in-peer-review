- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based metric proposed as an alternative to MMD for comparing distributions.
- Shows PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein (Earth Mover's) distance via minimum weight matching between samples.
- Authors claim PMD avoids kernel bandwidth tuning, yields stronger training gradients, and works with smaller mini-batches than MMD.
- Empirically, PMD outperforms MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
- Strength: directly addresses MMD’s weak/vanishing gradients and large-batch requirements for deep generative model training.
- Strength: convincingly demonstrates effective optimization with small mini-batches, supported by empirical results (e.g., Fig. 5a).
- Strength: establishes a clear theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric.
- Weakness: the “stronger gradients” claim is unconvincing because MMD gradient strength depends on kernel bandwidth; comparisons lack tuning across σ or optimal σ baselines.
- Weakness: “fewer hyperparameters” is debatable—PMD replaces kernel choice with a base distance metric choice, which can be equally critical; MMD bandwidth heuristics (e.g., median) reduce its practical hyperparameter burden.
- Weakness: PMD’s reliance on a chosen distance metric may limit applicability in high-dimensional or structured data where simple metrics are ineffective (curse of dimensionality).
- Overall: valuable and practical contribution (notably for faster training with small batches), but some claims are overstated and PMD trades MMD’s kernel dependency for a potentially limiting metric dependency.
