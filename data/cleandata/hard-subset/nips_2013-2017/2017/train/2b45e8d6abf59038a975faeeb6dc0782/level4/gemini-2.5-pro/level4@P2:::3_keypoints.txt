- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based divergence defined via minimum weight matching between two samples, and proves it strongly consistently estimates the first Wasserstein metric.
- Positions PMD as a practical alternative to MMD, arguing it overcomes key MMD limitations in deep learning.
- Eliminates kernel bandwidth tuning required by MMD, reducing sensitive hyperparameter dependence.
- Provides stronger and more stable gradients (e.g., with L1 ground distance), mitigating RBF kernel saturation/vanishing issues.
- Enables use of smaller mini-batch sizes during SGD, improving training convergence speed.
- Paper is well-written, clearly structured, and includes helpful pseudocode and illustrations.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough, showing PMD outperforms MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling, with ablations on population/mini-batch size and matching algorithms.
- Authors transparently acknowledge computational cost (exact O(N³)) and evaluate a faster O(N²) approximation that works well in practice.
- Concern about PMD's behavior on highly multi-modal distributions when sample size N is smaller than number of modes; needs empirical study or discussion.
- Computational scalability remains a potential bottleneck for very large N compared to MMD; trade-offs between accuracy and cost should be discussed further.
- Experiments only use L1 as the ground metric; discussion or evaluation of other metrics (e.g., L2) and their implications is missing.
- Overall assessment: strong, well-executed paper with solid theory and empirical results; recommendation to accept.
