

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel approach to estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. The authors define PMD as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from two distributions, proving it to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD as an objective, demonstrating advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in terms of hyperparameter requirements, gradient strength, and mini-batch size. The authors apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing improved performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.

The work relates well to previous NIPS contributions on distribution distance estimation, particularly MMD [17] and Wasserstein GAN [3]. The authors correctly position their work as addressing MMD's limitations: the need for bandwidth tuning, vanishing gradients, and large mini-batch requirements. The connection to Wasserstein metric is well-established through the theoretical analysis, and the paper makes a clear distinction from WGAN by avoiding the need for a separate critic network.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical contribution is sound - the proof of PMD as a consistent estimator of Wasserstein metric is rigorous and well-motivated. The connection to optimal transport theory is clearly articulated.

2. The paper makes a compelling case for PMD's practical advantages over MMD. The empirical results consistently show PMD outperforming MMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, with faster convergence. The gradient analysis (Fig. 2b) convincingly demonstrates PMD's stronger gradients.

3. The implementation details are thorough, including the practical considerations of using exact versus approximate matching algorithms. The authors provide valuable insights about the trade-offs between computational cost and performance.

4. The paper is well-organized with clear motivation, solid theoretical foundation, and convincing experimental results.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not sufficiently address the fundamental limitation of PMD: its dependence on large population sizes N for accurate estimation. The authors claim PMD can use smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs MMD's 1000), but fail to demonstrate this advantage when estimating the Wasserstein distance between complex, multimodal distributions. For example, the experiments on SVHN (Fig. 4) show generated images with low diversity (frequent digit 8), suggesting PMD struggles with multimodal data. The paper should address whether PMD can effectively capture complex distributions without very large N.

2. The claim that "MMD can only use mini-batch size equal to population size" is inaccurate. The original MMD paper [17] provides an unbiased estimator that can be computed with smaller mini-batches (batch_size=2), though it may require more iterations to converge. The authors should clarify this distinction between the unbiased estimator and the standard MMD implementation.

3. The paper doesn't adequately address the computational complexity trade-offs. While PMD's O(N^3) Hungarian algorithm is acceptable for small N (as the authors note), the authors don't provide sufficient evidence that PMD's computational requirements are actually lower than MMD's when considering the full training pipeline. The time breakdown in Fig. 5(c) shows that approximated matching is comparable to SGD, but the authors don't demonstrate whether this is a net gain for the overall training process.

4. The generative modeling results are somewhat underwhelming. The PMD-generated images on SVHN and LFW are blurry compared to state-of-the-art methods, and the authors don't explain why PMD struggles with natural images. The paper claims PMD "outperforms MMD" but doesn't establish a clear comparison with other generative models (e.g., GANs, VAEs).

Significance:
The paper addresses a real problem in deep learning - the limitations of MMD as a training objective. PMD offers a practical alternative with demonstrable advantages in specific settings. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the paper's failure to show PMD's effectiveness on complex, multimodal distributions and the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art generative models. The authors acknowledge this in their conclusion, noting they plan to derive finite-sample error bounds in future work.

Clarity:
The paper is well-written, with clear mathematical formulations and good organization. The theoretical results are presented logically, and the experimental setup is well-described. The figures (particularly Figs. 2 and 5) effectively support the key claims.

Quality:
The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are generally convincing. However, the paper would benefit from more rigorous experiments demonstrating PMD's performance on complex, multimodal distributions and a more thorough comparison with the unbiased MMD estimator.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting alternative to MMD with clear theoretical foundations and practical advantages. The main limitations concern the paper's failure to adequately address PMD's performance on complex multimodal distributions and the inaccurate characterization of MMD's mini-batch requirements. With these limitations addressed, the paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

Recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions addressing the limitations noted above). The paper has significant potential to impact deep learning applications that require distribution distance estimation, particularly in scenarios where MMD's limitations are problematic. The authors should clarify the relationship between PMD and MMD's unbiased estimator, provide more thorough experiments on multimodal distributions, and better address the computational trade-offs.
