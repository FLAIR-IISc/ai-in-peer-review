### Summary

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel divergence measure for training generative models. PMD is defined as the Wasserstein distance computed directly between two minibatches, one from the model distribution and one from the target data distribution. The authors employ an exact O(N^3) algorithm or an O(N^2) approximation to calculate this distance on minibatches of size N, and use the resulting value as the training loss for the generator.

### Strengths

The primary strength of the paper is its exploration of a direct computational approach to the Wasserstein distance for generative modeling. While methods like Wasserstein GAN rely on the dual formulation, this work provides interesting experimental results based on the primal form, calculated on minibatches. This offers a different perspective and contributes a valuable data point to the ongoing research in distance-based generative models.

### Weaknesses

The main weakness of the proposed method is that PMD is a biased estimator of the true Wasserstein distance between the underlying population distributions. The practical and theoretical implications of this bias are significant:

1.  **Dependence on Large Minibatch Size (N):** To obtain a reasonably accurate estimate of the Wasserstein distance between two complex, multimodal distributions, the minibatch size N would need to be extremely large. For typical batch sizes used in deep learning, the PMD is likely a poor approximation of the true distance. I suspect this limitation would be clearly visible even in simpler synthetic experiments, such as learning a mixture of Gaussians with learnable variances.

2.  **Incorrect Optimization Objective:** More critically, when N is not sufficiently large, the optimization objective itself is flawed. The global minimum of the minibatch-based PMD may not correspond to the point where the model distribution perfectly matches the true data distribution. This can systematically bias the generator towards learning a distribution with lower entropy or variance than the target, leading to mode collapse.

3.  **Empirical Evidence of Low Diversity:** This concern about mode collapse appears to be supported by the qualitative results presented in the paper. For example, the SVHN samples shown in Figure 4 seem to have low diversity, with a noticeable overrepresentation of the digit "8". This suggests the model has converged to a solution that covers only a subset of the data modes.

4.  **Comparison with MMD and Gradient Bias:** The authors' rebuttal and comparison to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) further highlight a fundamental theoretical issue. MMD, when trained using the unbiased V-statistic estimator from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper (Gretton et al.), is well-suited for stochastic gradient descent (SGD). This unbiased estimator allows MMD-based models to converge to the correct target distribution even when trained with very small minibatches (e.g., a batch size of 2). In contrast, PMD does not have a known unbiased estimator for its gradient with respect to the generator's parameters. The gradient of the Wasserstein distance computed on a small minibatch is inherently a biased estimate of the true gradient. Therefore, training with SGD is not guaranteed to converge to the correct solution and will be heavily influenced by the choice of N. The authors should clarify this critical limitation in the paper.

### Clarity and Quality

The paper is generally well-written and the proposed method is described clearly.

### Post-Rebuttal Comments

I thank the authors for their response and the additional experiments. However, my primary concerns about the biased nature of the PMD estimator and its negative implications for training via SGD remain. The comparison to MMD, which benefits from a known unbiased estimator that works well with small batches, underscores the theoretical shortcomings of the proposed approach. The authors should explicitly acknowledge and discuss the limitation that PMD gradients are biased and explain how this affects convergence guarantees.
