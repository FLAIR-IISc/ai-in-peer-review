This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel metric for comparing probability distributions based on samples. PMD is positioned as an alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (also known as the Earth Mover's Distance), which is calculated as the minimum weight matching between two sets of samples. The key advantages of PMD over MMD, as argued by the authors, include the absence of a sensitive kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, the presence of stronger training gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes for stochastic optimization. The method is evaluated on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, where it is shown to outperform MMD.

**Strengths:**

*   The work directly addresses well-known limitations of MMD in training deep generative models, namely its tendency to produce weak or vanishing gradients and its requirement for large mini-batch sizes to obtain reliable estimates. The proposed PMD offers a practical solution to these issues.
*   A significant strength of the paper is the ability of PMD to be optimized effectively with small mini-batches. This claim is well-supported by the empirical results (e.g., Figure 5a) and represents a key practical advantage, potentially leading to faster convergence and more efficient training compared to MMD-based methods that require large batches.
*   The connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is clearly established, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed discrepancy measure.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The claim of "stronger gradients" is not entirely convincing. The gradient strength of MMD is highly dependent on the choice of kernel bandwidth (σ). The comparison in Figure 2b would be more compelling if it benchmarked PMD against MMD with an optimally tuned σ, or across a range of σ values. Without this, it is unclear if the observed weak gradients for MMD are an inherent flaw or simply a result of a suboptimal hyperparameter choice.
*   The argument that PMD has "fewer hyperparameters" than MMD is debatable. While PMD eliminates the kernel bandwidth, it introduces a dependency on the choice of a base distance metric (e.g., L1, L2). This choice is critical and can be just as impactful as the kernel choice in MMD. For high-dimensional or structured data, simple metrics like L1 may not be suitable, and more complex, parameterized metrics might be required. Furthermore, established heuristics exist for setting the MMD bandwidth (e.g., the median heuristic), which can render MMD effectively hyperparameter-free in practice. A comparison against MMD using such heuristics would have provided a fairer assessment of this claim.
*   The method's performance is fundamentally tied to the effectiveness of the chosen distance metric. In high-dimensional spaces, standard distance metrics like Euclidean or L1 distance often become less meaningful (a phenomenon related to the curse of dimensionality). This could limit PMD's applicability for complex, high-dimensional data distributions, such as natural images, where the choice of a suitable metric is a non-trivial research problem in itself. This dependency is analogous to MMD's reliance on a suitable kernel.

**Overall Assessment:**

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution. The proposed PMD method's ability to operate with small mini-batches makes it a valid and potentially more efficient improvement over MMD-based approaches, particularly for large-scale generative modeling. However, the claims regarding stronger gradients and fewer hyperparameters seem overstated. The method effectively trades MMD's dependency on a kernel for a dependency on a distance metric, which may limit its success on higher-dimensional data where defining a good metric is challenging. Despite these concerns, the practical benefits of faster training make this an interesting and worthwhile direction for research.
