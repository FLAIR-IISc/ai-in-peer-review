- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator using minimum-weight bipartite matching to measure distance between distributions.
- Shows PMD equals empirical optimal transport and is strongly consistent for the first Wasserstein distance (W1).
- Uses PMD as a training objective by alternately computing matchings and updating parameters via SGD, arguing matching is locally stable so gradients need not flow through it.
- Implements exact Hungarian matching (O(N^3)) and an approximate solver (O(N^2)), finding the approximation adequate in practice.
- Contrasts PMD with MMD: no kernel bandwidth, claimed stronger gradients, and ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches than population size.
- Contrasts PMD with WGAN and Sinkhorn OT: avoids a critic and entropic smoothing but introduces discrete matching.
- Connection to prior work: operationalizes primal OT/EMD; conceptually similar to GMMN and recent OT-based learning, with focus on practical solvers and consistency result.
- Strength: clear OT connection and proof of strong consistency for W1.
- Strength: practical training recipe leveraging matching stability and effective approximate matching.
- Strength: empirical advantages over MMD in convergence speed, hyperparameter robustness, and mini-batch usage.
- Strength: informative empirical analyses (gradient distributions, approximation error vs N, timing).
- Applications: tested on domain adaptation and generative modeling with competitive results against MMD.
- Weakness: sample complexity concerns for multimodal distributions; large N may be needed to capture modes and avoid mode collapse.
- Weakness: computational cost is high (O(N^2)–O(N^3)), and no GPU-accelerated matching implementation provided.
- Weakness: comparison with MMD is incomplete—unbiased MMD estimators enable very small-batch SGD and should be included as baseline.
- Weakness: PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator; fixed-match gradient estimation may be biased and discontinuous.
- Weakness: generative image quality trails WGAN; L1 pixel-space distance causes blur unless using better feature spaces.
- Weakness: theoretical results limited to asymptotic consistency—no finite-sample bounds, stability analysis, or mode-coverage guarantees.
- Quality: technically sound and reproducible in principle, but would benefit from deeper analysis of gradient bias and broader baselines.
- Clarity: well written and organized; needs clearer assumptions about matching stability and implications for gradient bias.
- Originality: moderate—idea of empirical OT matching is known; contribution is packaging with consistency proof, solvers, and empirical evaluation.
- Significance: provides a practical, parameter-free alternative to MMD with some advantages, but not superior to WGAN for generative tasks.
- Pros: simple, parameter-free estimator of W1; strong gradients and faster convergence vs MMD in some tasks; approximate matching works and allows smaller mini-batches.
- Cons: may require large N for multimodal coverage; high computational cost; missing fair unbiased-MMD baseline; no unbiased gradient estimator or finite-sample guarantees.
- Suggestions: experiment on Gaussian mixtures for mode coverage, compare to unbiased MMD/Sinkhorn/WGAN, analyze gradient bias/variance, provide finite-sample/stability bounds, use feature-space distances and diversity metrics, and consider GPU matching implementations.
- Minor typo: Line 141 “usally” → “usually.”
- Post-rebuttal: emphasize limitations of both PMD and MMD—MMD admits unbiased small-batch SGD while PMD lacks an unbiased gradient estimator.
- Overall recommendation: Borderline — promising practical formulation with asymptotic grounding and empirical benefits, but needs stronger comparisons, theoretical and empirical analysis of gradient bias and small-N behavior.
