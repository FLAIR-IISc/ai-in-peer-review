This work introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance computed between two minibatches sampled from their respective distributions. The calculation of this distance relies on either an exact algorithm with O(N^3) complexity or an approximate O(N^2) alternative.

**Strengths:**
- The experimental investigation into this specific method for computing the Wasserstein distance is a noteworthy aspect of the paper. However, the quality of the generated images does not appear to match that of standard Wasserstein GANs.

**Weaknesses:**
- A significant concern is that the proposed discrepancy measure likely requires a large minibatch size (N) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance, particularly for diverse and multimodal distributions. I suspect this limitation would manifest even in simpler tasks, such as matching a mixture of Gaussians, especially when the model is also required to learn the variances.
- When N is insufficient, the optimization's global minimum may shift away from the true data-generating distribution. This could lead to a learned distribution with lower entropy. The SVHN samples in Figure 4, for instance, appear to exhibit low diversity, with the digit '8' being overrepresented.

**Post-Rebuttal Update:**
I have reviewed the author rebuttal and thank the authors for conducting additional experiments.

The authors should provide a clearer discussion on the limitations of PMD, especially when contrasted with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). MMD functions effectively even with extremely small batch sizes (e.g., a batch size of 2). Furthermore, MMD is well-suited for training with SGD, as an unbiased estimator is available, as shown in the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper. Consequently, MMD can converge to the correct target distribution using small minibatches. In contrast, PMD does not have a known unbiased estimator for its gradient, which is a considerable drawback.
