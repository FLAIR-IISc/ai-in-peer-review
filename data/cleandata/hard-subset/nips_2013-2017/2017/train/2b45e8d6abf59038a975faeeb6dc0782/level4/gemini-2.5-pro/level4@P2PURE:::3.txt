The authors introduce PMD, a population-based divergence between probability distributions, and demonstrate that it is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance. The proposed estimator is conceptually simple and differentiable, a key property that allows its use in training neural network-based models. The authors provide a thorough comparison of PMD to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a prominent population-based divergence in machine learning.

The authors acknowledge a primary drawback of their method: the exact calculation has cubic complexity. To address this, they propose an approximation with quadratic complexity and show empirically that this relaxation does not lead to a significant degradation in statistical performance.

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and includes appropriate references to prior work. The theoretical results appear sound, and the experimental analysis is adequate. The experiments include comparisons against MMD and other methods for domain adaptation, as well as against MMD for generative modeling.

A potential area for improvement would be the application of the method to generative modeling in domains with a large number of modes. A remaining question is whether PMD performs well when the sample size, N, is smaller than the number of modes in the target distribution.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a potentially very useful method for comparing probability distributions.
