Summary:
The paper introduces PMD, a population-based divergence between probability distributions, and shows it is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance. The estimator is conceptually simple and fully differentiable, making it suitable for training neural network–based models. The authors provide a thorough comparison with MMD, the most widely used population-based divergence in machine learning. They also discuss computational complexity: exact PMD computation is cubic, but they propose a quadratic-time approximation and demonstrate empirically that it maintains strong statistical performance.

Strengths:
- Clear contribution: PMD offers a differentiable, conceptually simple divergence that is theoretically grounded through consistency with the Wasserstein distance.
- Theoretical soundness: The results appear correct and are presented clearly.
- Practical relevance: Differentiability is important for integrating divergences into end-to-end neural training.
- Empirical rigor: The comparison to MMD is careful and comprehensive. The experiments cover domain adaptation and generative modeling, aligning with common use cases of population-based divergences.
- Thoughtful treatment of computation: The authors acknowledge the cubic complexity of exact PMD and provide a quadratic approximation that seems effective in practice.
- Presentation quality: The paper is well structured, well written, and properly references related work.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Computational cost at scale: Even with the quadratic approximation, the method may be costly for very large datasets or high-throughput training. More clarity on practical limits (e.g., wall-clock time vs. MMD baselines, memory use) would strengthen the case for adoption.
- Evaluation on highly multimodal data: The generative modeling experiments are informative, but I would have liked to see results on datasets with many modes (e.g., synthetic mixtures with controlled mode counts or challenging real data prone to mode collapse). This would help assess whether PMD encourages better mode coverage than MMD or adversarial objectives.
- Sample size vs. mode count: How does PMD behave when the sample size N is smaller than the number of modes in the target distribution? Is there evidence that PMD remains effective or degrades gracefully in this regime, and how does it compare to MMD and Wasserstein estimators under the same constraints?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide additional empirical results on multimodal generative tasks, ideally with diagnostics for mode coverage (e.g., number of recovered modes, precision/recall for generative models).
- Include more detailed runtime and scalability analyses comparing exact PMD, the quadratic approximation, and MMD across a range of dataset sizes and dimensions. If possible, report batch-level costs during neural training.
- Clarify guidance for practitioners on when to use the approximation, how to select its parameters, and how approximation error may affect gradients and training stability.

Overall assessment:
This is a solid paper that introduces a promising and potentially impactful divergence for comparing distributions. The theoretical results are sound, the method is practical for neural training due to differentiability, and the empirical evaluation is generally adequate with a strong focus on comparisons to MMD. The main remaining questions concern computational scalability at very large scales and performance on highly multimodal generative tasks, especially when the sample size may be smaller than the number of modes.

All things considered, I view the paper favorably and believe PMD could become a useful tool in the distributional comparison toolbox. I recommend acceptance.
