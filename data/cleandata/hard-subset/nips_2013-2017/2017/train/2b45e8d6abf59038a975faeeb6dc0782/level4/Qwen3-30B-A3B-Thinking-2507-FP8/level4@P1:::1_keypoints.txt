- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein distance defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two samples.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Proposes an optimization algorithm using PMD as a training objective and evaluates it on domain adaptation and generative modeling.  
- Claims empirical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients per sample, and viability with smaller mini-batches.  
- Builds on Wasserstein-based generative modeling literature but could better situate itself relative to other approximations like WGAN and variants.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric.  
- Strength: empirical results show faster convergence and advantages over MMD across tasks.  
- Strength: thorough experimental validation including ablations on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithm choice.  
- Strength: detailed implementation comparison of exact vs approximate matching algorithms.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to population size N, especially for complex, multimodal distributions requiring large N to capture all modes.  
- Weakness: limited discussion/justification of the choice of distance metric (L1 vs L2), despite claiming no bandwidth hyperparameter.  
- Weakness: generated images (SVHN, LFW) show blurriness, indicating PMD may be suboptimal for pixel-level generation.  
- Rebuttal note: MMD can be trained with small batches using the unbiased estimator, but authors should more clearly quantify PMD’s stronger gradients per sample.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions—clarify limits of PMD (when it outperforms MMD), address population-size impacts, justify metric choice, and discuss image-quality limitations.
