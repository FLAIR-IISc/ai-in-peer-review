- Proposal: PMD is a sample-based estimator equal to the 1-Wasserstein distance between two minibatch empirical distributions, computed via minimum-weight bipartite matching.  
- The paper proves strong consistency of PMD using Varadarajan’s theorem and OT stability.  
- Algorithm: alternating frozen-match inner-loop SGD with exact (Hungarian O(N^3)) and approximate (O(N^2)) matchers.  
- Positioned against MMD: claims PMD avoids kernel tuning, gives stronger gradients, and allows smaller minibatches.  
- Empirical applications: domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW).  
- Strength: Clear formulation and straightforward theoretical justification.  
- Strength: Practical algorithmic details and empirical study of gradient magnitudes and batch-size effects.  
- Strength: Encouraging domain adaptation results and faster convergence versus MMD in several transfers.  
- Strength: Systematic empirical comparison between PMD and MMD under matched settings.  
- Weakness: Sample complexity concerns for multimodal distributions—Wasserstein estimation may require large N (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians).  
- Weakness: Optimization bias at small N may favor low-entropy solutions and reduce diversity/mode coverage (e.g., SVHN).  
- Weakness: Generated images are lower quality than WGANs, limiting PMD’s generative modeling impact in pixel/L1 space.  
- Weakness: Claim that MMD needs large minibatches is overstated—MMD has an unbiased U-statistic estimator usable with very small batches.  
- Weakness: PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator, so minibatch-size advantages should be qualified.  
- Weakness: Matching step adds nontrivial compute overhead; current implementation is single-threaded CPU without demonstrated GPU acceleration.  
- Quality: Theoretical claims are correct but lack finite-sample error bounds; empirical error scales modestly (~N^-0.23).  
- Quality: Experiments largely solid but missing WGAN baselines and robust diversity/mode-coverage metrics.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-organized but should clarify “population” vs “minibatch” terminology and state that gradients do not backpropagate through discrete matching.  
- Originality: Using empirical OT as a training loss is known, but the combination of consistency proof, practical approximations, and PMD-vs-MMD empirical analysis is a useful contribution.  
- Significance: Practically interesting for domain adaptation (competitive/faster than MMD); limited impact for generative modeling without better metrics or feature spaces.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled formulation with consistency guarantee, practical algorithm, and empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence in domain adaptation.  
- Cons for acceptance: need for large N in multimodal cases, low-entropy minima risks, weaker generative results versus WGAN, overstated minibatch claims, and compute overhead.  
- Suggestions: add synthetic mixtures-of-Gaussians experiments, quantify diversity/mode coverage, temper minibatch-size claims, and provide guidance on choosing N and matching approximations.  
- Rebuttal update: explicitly clarify that MMD admits an unbiased SGD estimator for tiny batches, whereas PMD currently lacks an unbiased gradient estimator; frame minibatch trade-offs with these nuances.
