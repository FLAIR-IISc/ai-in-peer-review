This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches sampled from their respective distributions. The Wasserstein distance is computed using either an exact O(N^3) algorithm or an approximate O(N^2) algorithm.

**Pros:**
- The experiments demonstrating this particular method for computing the Wasserstein distance are interesting. However, the quality of the generated images appears inferior to those from a Wasserstein GAN.

**Cons:**
- The proposed distance likely requires a large minibatch size (N) to accurately estimate the true Wasserstein distance between complex, multimodal distributions. I suspect that limitations would become apparent even in simpler settings, such as matching a mixture of Gaussians, especially when the variances also need to be learned.
- When N is insufficient, the optimization objective may possess a global minimum that does not correspond to the true data distribution. This could cause the learned distribution to exhibit lower entropy. For instance, the SVHN samples shown in Figure 4 appear to lack diversity, with a notable overrepresentation of the digit '8'.

**Update:**
I have read the author's rebuttal. I thank the authors for the additional experiments.

The authors should clarify the limitations of PMD, particularly in comparison to MMD. MMD can be effectively trained with SGD using small minibatches (e.g., `batch_size=2`) by leveraging the unbiased estimator from the "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper. Consequently, MMD-based training can converge to the target distribution. In contrast, it is not clear if an unbiased estimator for the PMD gradient exists, which is a significant disadvantage for stochastic optimization.
