This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel, sample-based method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between samples drawn from the two distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). This work positions PMD as a practical alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), aiming to address several of MMD's limitations in deep learning applications. The paper also contrasts PMD with recent work on Wasserstein GANs, noting that PMD provides a direct, parameter-free estimate of the W1 distance without requiring an auxiliary critic network. The utility of PMD is demonstrated in two key deep learning applications: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Conceptual Simplicity and Practicality:** The proposed estimator is conceptually simple and, crucially, differentiable with respect to the sample locations. This allows it to be used directly as a loss function for training deep neural networks via gradient-based optimization.

2.  **Principled Alternative to MMD:** The paper provides a thorough and insightful comparison to MMD, the most established competitor in this space. The authors convincingly argue for several advantages of PMD:
    *   It has no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, removing a common tuning burden.
    *   It empirically provides stronger and more stable gradients, which can mitigate the vanishing gradient problem MMD sometimes suffers from, especially when samples are far apart or very close.
    *   It allows for the use of smaller mini-batch sizes for SGD updates than the full population size, potentially leading to faster convergence.

3.  **Computational Honesty:** The authors are transparent about the computational complexity of PMD (O(N³) for the exact solution using the Hungarian algorithm). They proactively address this by employing and evaluating an O(N²) approximation, and their empirical results suggest this approximation works well in practice without significant performance degradation.

4.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both domain adaptation on the Office dataset and generative modeling on standard image benchmarks (MNIST, SVHN, LFW). The results generally support the claims that PMD outperforms or is competitive with MMD, particularly in terms of convergence speed and final performance on several tasks.

**Weaknesses / Questions for Authors:**

My main question concerns the behavior of PMD in highly multi-modal settings, which are common in generative modeling. Specifically, the matching is performed between two sample populations of size N. If the true distributions have a number of modes significantly larger than N, it is not immediately clear how well the matching would capture the true underlying distance. For example, if one distribution has K modes and another has K' modes, with K, K' >> N, would the PMD still provide a useful training signal, or would it be dominated by matching samples within the few modes that happen to be sampled? A small experiment or discussion on this point would strengthen the paper's claims for generative modeling.

A minor point: while the L1 distance is used in the experiments, a brief discussion on the choice of the ground distance `d(·,·)` and its potential impact on performance in different domains (e.g., L2 for images, or other learned metrics) could be beneficial for practitioners looking to apply this method.

**Evaluation based on NIPS criteria:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high quality. The theoretical claim of strong consistency is well-supported by connecting existing results in optimal transport theory. The experimental methodology is sound, and the authors are honest about the computational trade-offs of their method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the definition of PMD is precise, and its connection to the Wasserstein metric is explained logically. The algorithm for parameter learning is presented with helpful pseudocode and illustrations, which aids in understanding.
*   **Originality:** The paper's contribution is original. While the Wasserstein metric and bipartite matching are well-known, their combination into a direct, primal, sample-based estimator for training deep models in this manner is novel. The formulation of PMD and its associated learning algorithm represents a new and distinct approach compared to existing MMD-based or GAN-based methods for comparing distributions.
*   **Significance:** The work is significant. It provides a practical and principled alternative to MMD, a workhorse method in machine learning. Given the importance of comparing distributions in areas like domain adaptation, generative modeling, and model criticism, PMD is likely to be used and built upon by other researchers. Its simplicity and lack of a kernel bandwidth parameter make it an attractive tool for practitioners.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and potentially highly useful method for comparing distributions in deep learning contexts. Its theoretical grounding as a consistent estimator for the Wasserstein metric, its clear practical advantages over MMD, and its solid empirical validation make a compelling case for its acceptance. The question regarding performance in highly multi-modal settings is a point for potential improvement but does not detract from the overall strength and contribution of the work.

I recommend acceptance.
