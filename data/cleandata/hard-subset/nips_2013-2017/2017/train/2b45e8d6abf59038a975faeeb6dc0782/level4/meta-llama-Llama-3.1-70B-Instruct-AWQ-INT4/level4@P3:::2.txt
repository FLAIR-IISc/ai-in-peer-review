This paper presents the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a better alternative to MMD for distribution matching applications. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein metric or earth mover's distance and highlight its advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes, as well as fewer hyperparameters.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of MMD, particularly in the context of training generative models. The proposal to use PMD as a solution to these problems is well-reasoned, and the empirical results provide strong evidence for the benefits of PMD, especially in terms of its ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes.

However, the verification of the stronger gradients claim is less satisfactory, as the MMD metric depends on the scale parameter sigma. It would be essential to consider either the best sigma or a range of sigmas when making such a claim. Additionally, the claim that PMD has fewer hyperparameters is not entirely convincing, as PMD depends on a distance metric, which may contain extra hyperparameters. Moreover, selecting a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces can be challenging, and PMD may suffer from the same issue as MMD.

The authors could strengthen their argument by comparing their method against standard heuristics for choosing the bandwidth parameter in MMD, which could potentially render MMD a hyperparameter-free metric. Nevertheless, the proposed method has the desirable property of permitting small mini-batch sizes, leading to faster training. This appears to be a valid improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, the reliance on a distance metric may limit its success in modeling higher-dimensional data.

Strengths:

* The paper provides a clear explanation of the limitations of MMD and proposes a well-reasoned solution using PMD.
* The empirical results demonstrate the benefits of PMD, particularly in terms of its ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the relationship between PMD and the Wasserstein metric.

Weaknesses:

* The verification of the stronger gradients claim is less satisfactory due to the dependence on the scale parameter sigma.
* The claim that PMD has fewer hyperparameters is not entirely convincing, as PMD depends on a distance metric.
* The authors could strengthen their argument by comparing their method against standard heuristics for choosing the bandwidth parameter in MMD.
