The paper introduces the concept of Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) between two distributions, defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches drawn from these distributions. This distance is calculated using either an exact algorithm with a time complexity of O(N^3) or an approximate algorithm with a time complexity of O(N^2).

The strengths of this paper include:

* The experimental results obtained using this Wasserstein distance computation are intriguing, despite not being as impressive as those achieved by Wasserstein GAN in terms of generated image quality.

However, there are several limitations and concerns:

* To accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between two diverse multimodal distributions, the proposed distance would likely require a large value of N. This issue could become apparent even when attempting to match a mixture of Gaussians, including learning the variances.
* If N is insufficiently large, the optimization process may converge to a global minimum that does not correspond to the true distribution. For instance, the learned distribution might exhibit lower entropy. This concern is supported by the SVHN samples in Figure 4, which appear to have low diversity, with digit 8 occurring frequently.
* Minor corrections: Line 141 should be revised to replace "usally" with "usually".

Upon reviewing the rebuttal, I appreciate the additional experiments provided by the authors. However, I suggest that the authors clarify the limitations of both PMD and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Notably, MMD can be effectively used with a batch size of 2 and can be trained using Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) when employing the unbiased estimator of MMD from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper. This allows MMD to converge to the correct distribution even with small minibatches. In contrast, PMD lacks a known unbiased estimator of the gradient, which is a significant limitation.
