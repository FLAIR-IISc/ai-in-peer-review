- Paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): Wasserstein distance between two minibatches, computed via exact O(N³) or approximate O(N²) algorithms.  
- Pros: Experimental evaluation of the minibatch Wasserstein computation is noteworthy.  
- PMD likely requires large minibatch sizes N to accurately estimate Wasserstein for diverse, multimodal distributions, limiting its use on complex targets (e.g., mixture of Gaussians, variance learning).  
- With insufficient N, optimization can converge to suboptimal minima that reduce entropy instead of matching the target distribution (SVHN samples show low diversity; digit 8 overrepresented).  
- Minor typo: Line 141 should read "usually" (s/usally/usually).  
- Update/request: Authors should clarify PMD's limitations relative to MMD—MMD has an unbiased estimator that enables SGD training and convergence with very small minibatches (e.g., batch_size=2); PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator and thus lacks comparable convergence guarantees.
