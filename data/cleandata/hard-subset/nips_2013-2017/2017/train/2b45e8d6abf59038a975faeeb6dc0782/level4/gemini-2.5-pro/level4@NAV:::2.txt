This work introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a superior alternative to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sample-based approximation of the Wasserstein metric (or Earth Mover's Distance) and is argued to possess several advantages over MMD, notably stronger gradient signals, compatibility with smaller mini-batch sizes, and fewer hyperparameters.

For the application of training generative models, MMD is known to suffer from weak gradients and a dependency on large mini-batches. The method proposed in this paper offers a compelling solution to both of these challenges. The claim regarding small mini-batch performance is convincingly supported by the empirical results. However, the validation for the stronger gradients claim is less persuasive. Since MMD's performance is highly sensitive to the kernel scale parameter (sigma), a fair comparison must involve either an optimally chosen sigma or a sweep across a range of sigmas.

The claim of having fewer hyperparameters is also not fully substantiated. PMD's formulation relies on a distance metric, which can introduce its own set of hyperparameters, similar to the MMD case. Moreover, defining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces is a known challenge, suggesting PMD may face the same fundamental limitation as MMD in this regard. It would strengthen the paper's claims to compare against MMD variants that employ standard heuristics for bandwidth selection, thereby treating MMD as a similarly hyperparameter-free method for a more direct comparison.

In conclusion, the proposed method's most significant advantage is its ability to permit small mini-batch sizes, facilitating faster training cycles. This represents a valid improvement over MMD methods that require large batches. However, the method's core reliance on a chosen distance metric remains a potential drawback that could limit its effectiveness when modeling data in higher dimensions.
