Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the distance between two distributions. PMD computes the minimum-weight bipartite matching between two size-N samples (populations) drawn from the distributions, using a ground distance (e.g., L1). The authors show that PMD coincides with the empirical optimal transport (OT) problem and is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance (W1). They use PMD as a differentiable training objective by alternately computing the matching and updating parameters via SGD, arguing that the matching itself is locally stable and does not require gradient propagation. They further propose using either exact Hungarian matching in O(N^3) or an approximate O(N^2) algorithm, and empirically find the approximation adequate.

PMD is contrasted with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD): PMD has no kernel bandwidth, is claimed to produce stronger gradients, and can use smaller SGD mini-batches than the population size. Applications include unsupervised domain adaptation (as a discrepancy between source and target feature distributions) and sample-based generative modeling, where PMD is used in place of MMD/GMMN or adversarial critics (GANs/WGAN). Experiments show improved convergence and performance over MMD on several domain adaptation tasks and visually less noisy samples for some generative settings, though quality trails WGAN.

Relation to prior work
- PMD operationalizes the primal OT/Wasserstein distance (Villani, EMD/earth mover’s distance) between empirical measures, aligning with classic minimum-cost matching. This connection is known, but using it as a stochastic objective for deep models with practical matching solvers is the focus here.
- Compared to MMD (Gretton et al.), PMD avoids kernel bandwidth tuning; however, MMD admits unbiased estimators enabling small-batch SGD (see comments below).
- Compared to WGAN, PMD avoids a critic but introduces discrete matching; unlike Sinkhorn-regularized OT, PMD does not use entropic smoothing.
- The work is close in spirit to GMMN (MMD-based) and recent OT-based learning; the consistency result and practical training scheme are the main contributions.

Strengths
- Clear connection to OT with a straightforward definition and proof of strong consistency for W1.
- Practical training recipe leveraging matching stability; approximate matching reduces cost while retaining performance.
- Empirical advantages over MMD: faster convergence in some settings, reduced sensitivity to hyperparameters, and ability to use smaller mini-batches than the population size.
- Useful empirical analyses (gradient magnitude distribution; approximation error vs N; timing breakdown).
- Applications to two tasks (domain adaptation and generative modeling) with competitive or improved results over MMD in several cases.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Sample complexity for multimodal distributions: Estimating W1 via finite matchings can require large N to correctly capture diverse modes and scales (e.g., mixtures with varying variances). This may lead to biased optimization when N is small, potentially reducing entropy and diversity. The SVHN samples appear to exhibit limited diversity (e.g., frequent 8s).
- Computational cost: Even with approximate matching, O(N^2) cost per population and all-pairs distance computation can be heavy; the exact O(N^3) solver is impractical for large N. GPU-accelerated matching is suggested but not implemented.
- Comparisons with MMD: The claim that MMD requires large batches is overstated. The unbiased MMD estimator (as in “A Kernel Two-Sample Test”) enables small-batch SGD (even batch size 2), which can converge to the correct distribution in expectation. This baseline should be included for a fair comparison of convergence and batch-size sensitivity.
- Gradient unbiasedness: PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator; gradients are taken through a fixed matching, which changes discontinuously. While the local stability argument is plausible, a discussion of bias/variance and convergence guarantees would strengthen the work.
- Image quality: Generative results are weaker than WGAN on natural images; L1 in pixel space induces blur. While code-space PMD helps, this limits practical significance without stronger features or perceptual metrics.
- Theory: Only asymptotic consistency is provided; no finite-sample error bounds, stability analysis for stochastic optimization, or mode-coverage guarantees.

Quality
- Technically sound connection to empirical OT and consistency; the training heuristic is reasonable but would benefit from a more formal treatment of gradient estimation and matching stability.
- Experiments are informative but comparisons could be broader (e.g., WGAN, Sinkhorn OT, unbiased MMD, kernel mixtures tuned via validation).
- Reproducibility looks feasible given implementation details; more clarity on hyperparameters and stopping criteria would help.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the OT connection and algorithm are clear. Minor language issues remain (see typo below). Clarify the assumptions under which the matching remains stable across gradient steps and the implications for gradient bias.

Originality
- Using empirical OT via bipartite matching as a training objective is a known idea; the contribution is packaging it as PMD with consistency, practical solvers, and empirical evaluation against MMD. Moderate novelty.

Significance
- Provides a practical alternative to MMD with some advantages; however, for generative modeling, it does not surpass WGAN. Potentially useful for tasks where kernel choice is problematic and moderate N is feasible.

Pros
- Simple, parameter-free (no kernel bandwidth), and consistent estimator of W1.
- Stronger and more uniform gradients in practice; faster convergence than MMD in some tasks.
- Approximate matching works well; ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches.

Cons
- Potentially large N needed for multimodal distributions; risk of entropy reduction and mode under-coverage with small N.
- Computationally costly (O(N^2)–O(N^3)) without GPU-accelerated matching.
- Missing fair baselines with unbiased MMD small-batch training; generative quality below WGAN.
- No unbiased gradient estimator or finite-sample guarantees.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include experiments on mixtures of Gaussians (learning means and variances) to probe mode coverage and sample complexity.
- Compare against unbiased MMD with small batches, Sinkhorn-regularized OT, and WGAN under matched architectures and training budgets.
- Analyze gradient bias/variance due to fixed matchings, and provide finite-sample error or stability bounds if possible.
- Use feature-space distances (e.g., learned perceptual embeddings) to mitigate blur in image generation, and quantify diversity (e.g., precision/recall for GANs or coverage metrics).
- Clarify when PMD is preferable to MMD (e.g., bandwidth sensitivity) and to adversarial methods (e.g., stability trade-offs).
- Consider and discuss GPU implementations of matching to reduce wall-clock cost.

Minor typos
- Line 141: usally → usually.

Post-rebuttal comments
Thank you for the additional experiments. Please explicitly discuss limitations of both PMD and MMD. Notably, MMD can be trained with SGD using the unbiased estimator from “A Kernel Two-Sample Test,” allowing very small mini-batches (even batch_size=2) and convergence to the correct distribution in expectation. By contrast, PMD currently lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator. Making these trade-offs clear will help readers choose appropriately.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. The paper offers a clear, practical formulation with solid asymptotic grounding and some empirical benefits over MMD, but falls short in sample complexity analysis, computational efficiency, and generative quality relative to WGAN. Strengthening the comparisons and theoretical/empirical analysis of gradient bias and small-N behavior would significantly improve the contribution.
