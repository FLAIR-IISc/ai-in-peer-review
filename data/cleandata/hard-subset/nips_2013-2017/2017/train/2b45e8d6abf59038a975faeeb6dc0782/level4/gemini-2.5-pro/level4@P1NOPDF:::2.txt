This paper proposes the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a promising alternative to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for tasks involving distribution matching. The authors show that PMD is a sample-based approximation of the Wasserstein metric (or Earth Mover's Distance) and that it offers several advantages over MMD, most notably stronger training gradients, compatibility with small mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters.

In the context of training generative models, MMD is indeed known to suffer from weak gradients and the need for large mini-batches. The method proposed in this paper therefore offers a compelling solution to both of these significant problems. The claim regarding small mini-batches is convincingly demonstrated in the empirical results. However, the verification of the stronger gradients claim is less satisfactory. Since the performance of the MMD metric is highly dependent on its kernel bandwidth parameter (sigma), a fair comparison would require evaluating MMD with either an optimal sigma or across a range of sigmas.

The claim of requiring fewer hyperparameters is also questionable. While PMD removes the kernel bandwidth parameter, it introduces a dependency on a distance metric, which may itself have hyperparameters. More importantly, defining a meaningful distance metric in high-dimensional spaces is a well-known challenge. Consequently, PMD may suffer from the same fundamental difficulty as MMD, which relies on the choice of a suitable kernel. Furthermore, standard heuristics exist for selecting the MMD bandwidth parameter. A more thorough comparison would involve benchmarking against an MMD implementation that uses such heuristics, thereby treating both methods as effectively hyperparameter-free.

Overall, the proposed method has the valuable property of enabling the use of small mini-batches, which can significantly accelerate training. It appears to be a valid and practical improvement over MMD-based methods that require large batches. However, its reliance on a user-defined distance metric remains a concern, as this may limit its effectiveness when modeling high-dimensional data—a challenge it ultimately shares with kernel-based MMD.
