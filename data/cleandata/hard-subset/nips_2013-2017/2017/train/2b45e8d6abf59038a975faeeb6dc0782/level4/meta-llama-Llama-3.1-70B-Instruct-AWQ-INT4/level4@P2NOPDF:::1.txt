The paper introduces the concept of Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) between two distributions, defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches from the respective distributions. This distance is computed using either an exact O(N^3) algorithm or an approximate O(N^2) method.

The paper's experimental results, particularly those involving the computation of the Wasserstein distance, are noteworthy. However, the quality of the generated images is not comparable to those produced by the Wasserstein GAN.

There are several limitations to the proposed distance metric. Firstly, accurately estimating the Wasserstein distance between two diverse, multimodal distributions would likely require a large N. This could potentially lead to issues, such as difficulties in matching a mixture of Gaussians, including learning the variances.

Furthermore, if N is not sufficiently large, the optimization process may converge to a global minimum that does not accurately represent the true distribution. For instance, the learned distribution may exhibit lower entropy. This is evident in the SVHN samples presented in Figure 4, which appear to have low diversity, with digit 8 being overrepresented.

After reading the rebuttal, I would like to thank the authors for providing additional experimental results. However, I believe it is essential for the authors to clarify the limitations of both PMD and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Notably, MMD can be effectively trained using a batch size of 2 and can converge to the correct distribution when utilizing small minibatches, thanks to the unbiased estimator of MMD introduced in the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper. In contrast, PMD lacks a known unbiased estimator of the gradient, which may hinder its performance.
