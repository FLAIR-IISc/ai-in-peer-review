The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the Wasserstein distance computed between minibatches drawn from two distributions. The Wasserstein distance is calculated using either an exact O(N^3) or an approximate O(N^2) algorithm.

Pros:
- The experimental evaluation of this Wasserstein distance computation is noteworthy. However, the quality of the generated images does not reach the level of those produced by Wasserstein GANs.

Cons:
- Estimating the Wasserstein distance between two significantly different, multimodal distributions would likely require very large minibatches (N). I anticipate difficulties even when attempting to match mixtures of Gaussians, particularly if learning the variances of those Gaussians. 
- Insufficiently large N can lead the optimization process to converge to a suboptimal solution, differing from the true underlying distribution. For instance, the learned distribution may exhibit reduced entropy. The SVHN samples presented in Figure 4 appear to lack diversity, with the digit 8 being noticeably overrepresented.

Update:
I have reviewed the rebuttal and appreciate the additional experiments provided.
The authors should more clearly delineate the limitations of PMD in comparison to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Specifically, MMD can be effectively used with small batch sizes (e.g., batch_size=2), and can be trained with Stochastic Gradient Descent when employing the unbiased estimator of MMD from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper, allowing for convergence to the correct distribution even with small minibatches. In contrast, PMD currently lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator.
