

The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches drawn from the target and generated distributions. While the Wasserstein distance can be computed exactly in O(N³) or approximately in O(N²), the proposed approach has significant limitations.  

**Strengths:**  
The experimental results are noteworthy, particularly the demonstration of applying Wasserstein-based discrepancy in training.  

**Limitations:**  
1. **Scalability to multimodal distributions:** PMD requires large minibatch sizes (N) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between diverse, multimodal distributions. This becomes problematic when modeling complex structures, such as mixtures of Gaussians where variance learning is essential. Problems would likely manifest early—e.g., during mixture-of-Gaussians experiments—where insufficient N leads to poor approximation.  
2. **Optimization instability:** With small N, the optimization may converge to a local minimum that diverges from the true distribution. Specifically, the learned distribution may exhibit reduced entropy (e.g., over-concentrated modes). This is evident in Figure 4, where SVHN samples show low diversity (e.g., excessive digit-8 repetitions).  
3. **Comparison to MMD:** The paper understates PMD’s key limitation relative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). MMD is robust to small minibatches (e.g., batch_size=2) and converges to the correct distribution when using the unbiased gradient estimator from Gretton et al.’s original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" (2012). Crucially, MMD supports SGD training via this estimator. In contrast, PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator, making it inherently less suitable for small-batch optimization. The authors should explicitly clarify this distinction in the revision.  

**Minor issues:**

**Rebuttal update:**  
Following the authors’ response and additional experiments, the core critique regarding PMD’s limitations remains valid. The rebuttal does not sufficiently address why PMD cannot leverage the unbiased gradient framework that enables MMD’s small-batch convergence. The authors should clarify this gap in the final version.  

**Summary:**  
While PMD offers an interesting theoretical angle, its practical utility is constrained by computational demands and instability with small N. The comparison to MMD is critical: MMD’s compatibility with small minibatches and SGD training renders it far more practical for generative modeling. The authors must explicitly acknowledge PMD’s lack of an unbiased gradient estimator and its dependence on large N to avoid misleading readers about its applicability.
