- Introduces PMD: the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches, computed exactly in O(N^3) or approximately in O(N^2).
- Novelty/strength: applying Wasserstein distance on minibatches is an interesting, novel experimental exploration, though generated image quality is worse than Wasserstein GANs.
- Key weakness: PMD requires large minibatch size N to accurately estimate Wasserstein for diverse, multimodal distributions; this limitation likely shows on simple synthetic tasks (e.g., Gaussian mixtures).
- Optimization risk: with small N the objective may have global minima that do not match the true distribution, leading to reduced entropy or mode collapse.
- Empirical concern: SVHN samples suggest low diversity and overrepresentation of digit '8', supporting the mode-collapse concern.
- Minor typo: Line 141 "usally" should be "usually".
- Rebuttal note: reviewer read and thanks authors for additional experiments.
- Major theoretical drawback: PMD lacks a known unbiased estimator for its gradient, so small-minibatch training may be biased and fail to converge.
- Comparison to MMD: unlike PMD, MMD works well with very small batches and has an unbiased estimator that enables stable SGD and convergence with small minibatches; authors should clarify PMD’s limitations relative to MMD.
