The authors present a novel method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples, called population matching discrepancy (PMD). PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors propose a stochastic gradient descent algorithm to learn the parameters of the distributions using PMD as an objective.

The paper thoroughly compares PMD to the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), a popular method for estimating the distance between two distributions. The authors highlight the advantages of PMD over MMD, including fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes for gradient-based optimization.

The experimental analysis is adequate, with comparisons between PMD and MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The results show that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed in both tasks.

The paper is well-structured and written, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical properties. The authors provide references to previous work where due, demonstrating a good understanding of the relevant literature.

One potential drawback of the method is the cubic complexity of exact calculation, but the authors propose an approximation with quadratic complexity, which they show does not degrade statistical performance significantly.

One potential area for future work is to investigate the performance of PMD in generative modeling tasks with many modes, particularly when the sample size N is smaller than the number of modes.

Overall, this is a good paper that presents a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through thorough experimental analysis and provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their work.

Strengths:

* Novel method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions
* Strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric
* Fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes compared to MMD
* Adequate experimental analysis, including comparisons to MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks

Weaknesses:

* Cubic complexity of exact calculation, although an approximation with quadratic complexity is proposed
* Limited investigation of performance in generative modeling tasks with many modes

Acceptance:

* Yes, I recommend accepting this paper. The authors present a novel and potentially very useful method for comparing distributions, and their experimental analysis is adequate. The paper is well-structured and written, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature.
