This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two sets of samples, and the authors prove that it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper positions PMD as an alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD addresses several of MMD's key drawbacks. Specifically, the authors claim that PMD has fewer hyperparameters, provides stronger gradients for optimization, and, most notably, allows for the use of small mini-batches during training. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Addresses a Practical Problem:** The paper tackles well-known limitations of MMD-based training, particularly the need for large batch sizes to obtain a reliable estimate of the discrepancy, which can significantly slow down training. The proposed method provides an intuitive and theoretically grounded solution to this issue.

2.  **Enables Faster Training:** The most significant contribution is the ability of PMD to be optimized with small mini-batches, decoupled from the larger population size used for matching. This allows for more frequent gradient updates and can lead to faster convergence. The empirical results in Figure 5(a) provide compelling evidence for this claim, showing that smaller mini-batch sizes (`|B|`) lead to better final performance for a fixed number of epochs. This is a strong practical advantage over MMD, where the batch size for SGD is tied to the population size.

3.  **Theoretical Grounding:** The connection of PMD to the first Wasserstein metric is clearly established. Proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method. This situates the work within the broader context of optimal transport and integral probability metrics.

4.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that PMD can outperform MMD in both domain adaptation (Table 1) and generative modeling (Figure 4). The generated images, particularly on the pixel space, appear less noisy than those from MMD-based models, and the method achieves faster convergence on the domain adaptation task (Figure 3a).

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **The "Fewer Hyperparameters" Claim is Overstated:** The paper argues that PMD is superior to MMD because it does not have a kernel bandwidth parameter (`sigma`). However, PMD requires the selection of a distance metric `d(·, ·)`, which is itself a critical hyperparameter. The choice between L1, L2, or other metrics can significantly impact performance, especially in high-dimensional spaces. This choice is analogous to the choice of a kernel and its parameters in MMD. The comparison would be fairer if the authors acknowledged this and discussed the sensitivity of PMD to the choice of `d(·, ·)`.

2.  **Incomplete Comparison with MMD Heuristics:** The critique of MMD's sensitivity to `sigma` is valid, but the comparison could be stronger. In practice, MMD is often used with a median heuristic to automatically set the bandwidth, or with a mixture of kernels at different scales. These approaches make MMD much less sensitive to manual tuning and are standard practice in many applications. To make a more compelling case, the authors should compare PMD against MMD equipped with these common, more robust heuristics.

3.  **The "Stronger Gradients" Claim Needs More Nuanced Analysis:** The analysis in Figure 2(b) shows that PMD gradients are more uniformly distributed than MMD gradients for a converged model. However, the magnitude and behavior of MMD's gradients are highly dependent on the kernel bandwidth `sigma`. An outlier sample might receive weak gradients with one `sigma` but strong gradients with another. A more thorough analysis would involve studying the gradient distributions for MMD across a range of reasonable `sigma` values, including those selected by the median heuristic. While the authors' claim is plausible, the current evidence is based on a single setting and may not be universally true.

4.  **Challenges in High-Dimensional Spaces:** Like MMD, PMD relies on a distance metric computed directly on the samples (or features). It is well-known that distance metrics like L1 or L2 become less meaningful in very high-dimensional spaces (the "curse of dimensionality"). This may explain why the generated images in Figure 4 are somewhat blurry and why the best results are obtained on the lower-dimensional autoencoder latent space (`ae` architecture). This is a fundamental limitation shared by both methods, and the paper would be stronger if the authors discussed this issue more explicitly.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable and well-motivated contribution. By connecting minimum weight matching to distribution comparison, it offers a practical estimator for the Wasserstein metric that effectively solves the large mini-batch problem inherent in MMD-based training. The resulting improvement in training speed is a significant practical achievement.

However, the claims of superiority regarding hyperparameters and gradient strength are not fully substantiated and rely on a somewhat simplified view of how MMD is used in practice. Despite these points, the core idea is sound and the proposed method demonstrates clear advantages in terms of training efficiency. It is a valid and interesting improvement over standard large-batch MMD methods and is likely to be of interest to researchers working on generative models and domain adaptation.
