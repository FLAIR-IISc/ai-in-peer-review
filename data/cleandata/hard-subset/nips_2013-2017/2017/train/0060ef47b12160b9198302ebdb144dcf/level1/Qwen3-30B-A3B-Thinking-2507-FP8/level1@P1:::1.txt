

# Peer Review

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach for fast, real-time image saliency detection applicable to any differentiable image classifier. The authors propose training a masking model (based on U-Net architecture with ResNet-50 as encoder) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, avoiding the time-consuming iterative methods used in prior work. The key contributions include a new objective function combining smoothness, small mask size, and classification performance, and a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept. The method outperforms existing weakly supervised approaches on ImageNet object localization and produces sharp, interpretable saliency maps. The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on saliency detection (e.g., [11, 16, 17]) and recent methods like [2], but distinguishes itself by being model-agnostic, fast, and producing higher quality saliency maps.

## Quality
The paper presents technically sound work with a well-defined problem and clear methodology. The authors carefully address the challenge of introduced evidence when manipulating images (Section 3.1) and propose a robust solution through mask smoothness and random evidence removal. The experimental design is comprehensive, including evaluations on both ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets. The new saliency metric (Section 3.2) is well-motivated and provides a better assessment of saliency map quality than standard localization accuracy. The authors demonstrate that their method achieves 36.7% localization error on ImageNet (comparable to fully supervised methods at 34.3%), outperforming other weakly supervised approaches. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need for a pre-trained ResNet encoder (though they show it can be trained from scratch), and provides thorough ablation studies.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem of saliency detection, and the related work section properly contextualizes the contribution. Technical details of the masking model (Section 4) and objective function (Section 3.3) are explained with sufficient mathematical precision. The figures (particularly Figures 1, 2, 5, and 6) are helpful for visualizing the approach and results. One minor improvement could be clarifying the relationship between the new saliency metric and the objective function, as the metric is defined in Section 3.2 but the objective function is defined in Section 3.3. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to reproduce the results, though some implementation details (e.g., exact hyperparameter values for training) could be made more explicit.

## Originality
The paper makes several significant original contributions. The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass (rather than optimizing iteratively for each image) is novel and addresses a key limitation of existing methods. The new metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) is particularly valuable, as it better captures the interpretability of saliency maps than standard localization accuracy. The authors properly distinguish their work from closely related concurrent work [2] (which also uses optimization in image space but requires iterations) and from other gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16]. The masking model architecture with class embeddings (Section 4.1) represents a novel approach to learning saliency without direct access to the classifier's internals.

## Significance
This work has significant practical importance. The ability to produce high-quality saliency maps in real-time (100+ masks/second) enables applications like video saliency for autonomous vehicles that were previously impractical with iterative methods. The method outperforms existing weakly supervised approaches on ImageNet object localization, a significant result given that it requires no localization training data. The new metric provides researchers with a better tool to evaluate saliency methods. The paper's focus on interpretability of black-box classifiers addresses a critical need in applied machine learning, especially for safety-critical applications.

## Strengths
- Real-time performance (100+ masks/second) enables new applications
- High-quality saliency maps that outperform iterative methods
- New saliency metric that better assesses interpretability
- Thorough evaluation on both ImageNet and CIFAR-10
- Clear distinction from related work (including concurrent work [2])
- Proper handling of introduced evidence during mask application

## Weaknesses
- The need for a pre-trained ResNet encoder might limit applicability for some classifiers (though the authors show it can be trained from scratch)
- The paper doesn't fully explore the relationship between the masking model's biases and the saliency maps it produces
- The new metric is evaluated only on ImageNet; testing on other datasets would strengthen claims
- Limited discussion of computational requirements beyond speed (e.g., memory usage, scalability)

## Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel approach to fast saliency detection that addresses a significant limitation in the field
- Strong empirical results outperforming existing methods
- New metric provides better evaluation of saliency quality
- Real-world applicability demonstrated through potential use cases
- Well-written and clearly structured

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The need for a pre-trained ResNet might be seen as a limitation for some applications
- The paper doesn't fully address potential biases in the masking model itself
- Some technical details could be more explicit for reproducibility

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality paper with significant contributions to the field of model interpretability. The method is novel, well-motivated, and produces compelling results that outperform existing approaches. The new saliency metric is particularly valuable for the field. The paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the overall contribution, which is well-suited for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
