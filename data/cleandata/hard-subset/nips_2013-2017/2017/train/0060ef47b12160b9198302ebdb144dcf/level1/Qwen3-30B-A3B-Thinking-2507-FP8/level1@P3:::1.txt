

# Peer Review: "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper introduces a novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for black box classifiers. The authors propose training a masking model (based on U-Net architecture with ResNet-50 backbone) to predict saliency masks in a single forward pass, avoiding the iterative optimization required by previous methods. The key contributions include a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, a method to avoid adversarial artifacts through mask smoothing and randomized evidence removal, and demonstrated performance improvements over existing weakly supervised methods.

The work builds upon recent advances in saliency detection, particularly addressing limitations of gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] and iterative approaches like those by Zhou et al. [17] and Cao et al. [1]. The authors acknowledge parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] which also proposes an optimization-based method for saliency detection, but emphasizes their key difference: their masking model avoids iterative optimization by learning to predict saliency maps directly.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound method with a well-defined objective function (equation 4) that balances mask smoothness, region size, and classification performance. The training procedure includes important details to prevent overfitting (30% fake class labels, randomized evidence removal), and the experiments are thorough across ImageNet and CIFAR-10. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the metric's assumption of classifier invariance to scale/aspect ratio, and the requirement for differentiable classifiers (not truly "black box" as claimed in the title). The method's performance is compelling: 36.7% localization error on ImageNet, outperforming other weakly supervised methods and approaching fully supervised VGG (34.3%). The new saliency metric (equation 3) provides a meaningful evaluation beyond standard localization accuracy.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures (especially Figures 1, 2, and 5) that effectively illustrate the approach and results. The methodology section (Section 4) provides sufficient technical detail for reproduction, though some implementation specifics (e.g., exact hardware for the 100 masks/second claim) would benefit from more detail. The new metric is clearly defined and justified through the SSR concept, making it easy to understand the evaluation criteria.

**Originality:** The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass rather than using iterative optimization is novel for real-time applications. The masking model architecture (U-Net with ResNet-50 backbone) is well-suited to the task, and the authors provide a thoughtful analysis of how to avoid adversarial artifacts in the masks. The paper differentiates itself from Fong & Vedaldi [2] by explicitly incorporating the SSR objective (the third term in their objective function), which is a significant technical contribution. The new saliency metric is also a valuable addition to the field.

**Significance:** The method enables real-time saliency detection (100+ masks/sec), opening new applications like video-saliency for autonomous systems. The performance gains over existing weakly supervised methods are significant (Table 2), and the model's ability to produce masks nearly as interpretable as ground truth bounding boxes (Table 3) demonstrates meaningful progress. The paper's future work on video-saliency and weakly supervised segmentation suggests potential for broader impact beyond the immediate task.

**Weaknesses:** 
1. The title claims "black box" applicability, but the method requires differentiable classifiers, which is a limitation that should be more clearly acknowledged (the paper states this only in Section 4.1).
2. The new saliency metric (equation 3) has a potential instability when p approaches 0 (log(p) → -∞), though the authors mention thresholding the area at 0.05, they don't address this p-related instability.
3. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the masking model generalizes to classes not seen during training (only ImageNet classes were used for training).
4. The comparison to Fong & Vedaldi [2] is somewhat superficial; it would strengthen the paper to explicitly state how their objective function differs and why the additional terms are beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a technically sound, novel approach to real-time saliency detection that outperforms existing methods on standard benchmarks. The new metric is a valuable contribution, and the method's real-time capability opens new applications. The experiments are thorough and well-designed, with appropriate comparisons to relevant baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The "black box" claim is misleading (requires differentiability), and the paper could better address generalization to unseen classes. The metric's potential instability with low probabilities isn't fully resolved. The novelty is somewhat incremental compared to the parallel work by Fong & Vedaldi.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of model interpretability. The real-time capability is particularly valuable for practical applications, and the method's performance is impressive. The minor limitations noted do not detract significantly from the paper's main contributions. The work is well-positioned to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners in model interpretability.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of model interpretability. The method is novel, well-motivated, and outperforms existing approaches on relevant benchmarks. The new metric provides a valuable addition to the evaluation toolkit. The minor limitations noted are easily addressable in the camera-ready version.
