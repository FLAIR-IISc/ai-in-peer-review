### Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel approach for generating saliency maps for image classifiers. Instead of iteratively optimizing a mask for each specific input image, the authors propose training a separate, feed-forward "masking model" to generate these maps in a single pass. This masking model, based on a U-Net architecture, is trained to produce masks that manipulate the output of a given "black box" classifier in a desirable way. The training objective is thoughtfully designed to encourage masks that are small and smooth, while simultaneously preserving the classifier's confidence for the target class within the masked region (a "smallest sufficient region" objective) and diminishing its confidence when the masked region is removed (a "smallest destroying region" objective). The authors demonstrate that this approach is orders of magnitude faster than iterative methods, enabling real-time applications. They validate their method on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, showing state-of-the-art performance on the weakly supervised object localization task and introducing a new metric to evaluate the quality of saliency-based explanations.

The work builds upon the lineage of perturbation-based saliency methods (e.g., Zhou et al., 2014; Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), which find salient regions by altering parts of the image and observing the effect on the classifier's output. The key innovation here is to *amortize* the costly iterative optimization process of prior work by learning a generative model that directly predicts the optimal perturbation mask. This shifts the computational burden from inference time to a one-off training phase, which is a significant conceptual and practical contribution.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of training a dedicated neural network to explain another is highly novel and significant. It reframes saliency detection from an iterative optimization problem to a conditional image generation task. The resulting speed-up (over 100 images/sec) is a major practical advantage, opening up new possibilities for real-time video saliency and interactive model debugging that are infeasible with previous optimization-based methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The proposed objective function (Eq. 4) is well-motivated, elegantly combining principles of sufficiency (SSR), necessity (SDR), and regularization (sparsity, smoothness). The discussion on "introduced evidence" and the proposed solution of using randomized masking schemes during training is insightful and demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem's subtleties. The U-Net based architecture is a suitable choice for generating high-resolution, precise masks.

3.  **Experimental Results:** The quantitative results are impressive. Achieving a new state-of-the-art on the ImageNet weakly supervised object localization task (36.7% error) is a powerful demonstration of the quality of the generated masks, especially when outperforming other dedicated weakly supervised methods. The qualitative results (Fig. 5) are also compelling, showing masks that are significantly sharper and more focused than those from competing methods.

4.  **New Saliency Metric:** The introduction of a new saliency metric based on information concentration is a valuable contribution to the field of interpretability, which often struggles with standardized evaluation. While it has its own assumptions, it moves beyond localization accuracy to better capture the notion of a compact, sufficient explanation.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Missing Baselines:** A significant omission in the experimental comparison is the family of Class Activation Mapping methods, particularly Grad-CAM. Grad-CAM is a widely used, single-pass method that is also applicable to a wide range of CNN architectures. It would serve as a crucial baseline for both speed and localization performance. Including Grad-CAM in Tables 1, 2, and 3 would provide a more complete picture of where the proposed method stands relative to the current state-of-the-art in fast saliency methods.

2.  **Clarification of "Black Box":** The term "black box" may be slightly miscalibrated. The training process requires gradients to flow from the classifier's output back to the masking model's parameters to minimize the objective function. This implies that the classifier must be differentiable (a "white box" during training). While the *trained masking model* can then be used without access to the classifier's internals, the training dependency should be stated more clearly. This is an important distinction from methods that can use zeroth-order optimization on truly opaque models.

3.  **Training Cost as a Limitation:** The paper's primary advantage is inference speed, achieved by shifting computation to a heavy, one-time training process (on 250,000 ImageNet images). This is a reasonable trade-off, but it is a limitation. A new masking model must be trained from scratch for every classifier one wishes to explain. A brief discussion of this trade-off and the associated computational cost of training would make the presentation more balanced.

4.  **Saliency Metric Design Choices:** The new saliency metric `s(a, p)` is promising, but its design involves choices that could benefit from further justification. Forcing a tight rectangular crop may penalize methods that correctly identify non-rectangular objects. Furthermore, resizing this arbitrary crop to a fixed square input for the classifier can introduce significant aspect ratio distortions, which may unfairly impact the classifier's confidence `p` and thus the metric's value. A discussion of the potential impact of these choices would strengthen this contribution.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper presenting a novel and powerful paradigm for saliency map generation. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated with state-of-the-art results. The shift from iterative optimization to a learned, amortized prediction model is a significant contribution that pushes the boundaries of real-time model interpretability. Despite some minor weaknesses, primarily the omission of key baselines like Grad-CAM and a slight ambiguity in the use of "black box", the strengths of this work in terms of originality, performance, and potential impact are substantial. The paper is very clearly written and provides a solid foundation for future research in learned explanation models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
