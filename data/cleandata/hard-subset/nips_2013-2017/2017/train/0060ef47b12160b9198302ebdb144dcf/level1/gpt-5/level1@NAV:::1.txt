This paper proposes a learned, feed-forward saliency generator for differentiable image classifiers. Instead of optimizing a per-image perturbation mask (as in meaningful perturbations, Fong & Vedaldi 2017), the authors train a U-Net–style “masking model” (ResNet-50 encoder) to output a class-conditional mask in a single pass by minimizing a composite objective that balances smoothness (TV), area, sufficiency (retain-class term, SSR), and destroying evidence (remove-class term, SDR). To mitigate introduced evidence, the method applies masks against randomly sampled “alternative images” (heavy blur or noisy constant), and regularizes for smoothness. The paper also proposes a new saliency metric based on the tightest box enclosing the mask: s = log(ã) − log(p), trading off crop area and retained class probability. Experiments on ImageNet (with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 black boxes) and CIFAR-10 show sharper, artifact-resistant masks, improved weakly supervised localization relative to prior saliency/weak-supervision baselines, and real-time inference rates.

Relation to prior work: The approach bridges perturbation-based explanations (Fong & Vedaldi; Ribeiro et al. LIME) and backprop/activation methods (Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop; CAM/Grad-CAM). Its novelty lies in amortizing perturbation optimization and jointly enforcing SSR and SDR criteria.

Quality: Technically sound with a clear objective and practical tricks (fake-label training, randomized A) that address known pitfalls (adversarial artifacts). However, more ablations are needed: contribution of each loss term, sensitivity to λi, and alternative-image choices. The “black-box” claim requires gradients through the classifier during training, so the assumption is closer to “differentiable gray-box.” The proposed metric is sensible but dependent on classifier calibration and resize distortions; complementary insertion/deletion curves (as in Fong & Vedaldi) would strengthen claims.

Clarity: Generally well written. Training protocol and architecture are described in useful detail; adding a hyperparameter sensitivity table and explicit comparison to Grad-CAM/Integrated Gradients would aid readers.

Originality: High in amortizing saliency optimization and combining SSR/SDR with stochastic evidence removal. The metric is a thoughtful addition.

Significance: Real-time, sharper masks and improved weakly supervised localization are valuable; applicability to video is compelling. Training is per-classifier, which may limit immediate adoption.

Pros for acceptance:
- Amortized, real-time saliency with competitive/better localization versus strong baselines
- Principled objective unifying SSR and SDR with effective regularization
- New, interpretable metric; thorough experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses
- “Black-box” assumption requires gradients; scope clarification needed
- Metric dependence on calibration; missing insertion/deletion evaluations and comparisons to Grad-CAM/Integrated Gradients
