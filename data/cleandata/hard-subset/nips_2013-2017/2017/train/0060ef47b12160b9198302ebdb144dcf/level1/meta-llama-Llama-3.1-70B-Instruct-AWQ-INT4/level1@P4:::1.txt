The paper "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers" proposes a fast and accurate model-based saliency detection method that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The method trains a masking model to manipulate the scores of the classifier by masking salient parts of the input image, requiring only a single forward pass to perform saliency detection. The authors test their approach on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet datasets, achieving results that outperform other weakly supervised methods.

The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors provide a thorough review of related work, highlighting the limitations of existing saliency detection methods and the advantages of their proposed approach. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and the motivation for the work, and the methodology is clearly explained.

The technical soundness of the paper is strong. The authors provide a detailed description of their approach, including the architecture of the masking model and the training process. The use of a U-Net architecture and a pre-trained ResNet encoder is well-justified, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how the model is trained to minimize the objective function.

The paper also makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a new saliency metric, which is used to evaluate the quality of the produced saliency maps. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms other saliency detection techniques on this metric, and that it is on par with ground truth bounding boxes.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained ResNet encoder. While the authors demonstrate that the masking model can be trained from scratch, it is not clear how well the method would perform with a different encoder or without the benefit of pre-training.

Another potential area for improvement is the evaluation of the method. While the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method on the ImageNet dataset, it would be beneficial to see evaluations on other datasets as well. Additionally, the authors could provide more analysis of the failure cases, where the method does not perform well.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the technical soundness is strong. The contribution to the field is significant, and the paper has the potential to be a valuable resource for researchers working on saliency detection and explainability.
