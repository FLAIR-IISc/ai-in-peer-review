This paper presents a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method by training a masking model to predict saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass. This addresses a key limitation of iterative saliency methods, which are computationally expensive. The core idea – learning to predict saliency rather than computing it iteratively – is a reasonable and potentially impactful approach. The paper introduces a new saliency metric (based on SSR) and demonstrates competitive results on ImageNet localization and CIFAR-10, outperforming other weakly supervised methods.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is clearly defined, and the experiments appear well-designed and executed. The inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., CIFAR-10 experiments) strengthens the analysis. The introduction of the new saliency metric is a valuable contribution, though its theoretical grounding could be more thoroughly explored. The training process for the masking model is complex, and the authors adequately address potential issues like overfitting and introducing artifacts. However, the explanation of the loss function (Equation 4) is a bit dense and could benefit from a clearer breakdown of each term’s purpose. The empirical results support the claims made. It's a complete piece of work, not just a position paper, providing a full pipeline from model training to evaluation. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as potential biases in the masking model itself, demonstrating honesty in their evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though some sections are more accessible than others. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the proposed solution. The related work section provides a good overview of existing saliency detection methods. However, the detailed explanation of the objective function (Section 3.3) and the masking model architecture (Section 4) could be simplified for better readability. Visual aids, like diagrams illustrating the masking process and the U-Net architecture, are helpful. The notation is consistent, and the experiments are described clearly.

**Originality:** The core idea of learning a saliency map predictor is novel. While iterative methods exist (and are well-referenced), this approach shifts the paradigm to a learned, feed-forward process. The combination of the masking model with the specific objective function and the new saliency metric adds to the originality. The authors properly situate their work within the existing literature, acknowledging related efforts like Fong and Vedaldi's (2017) work on minimizing image changes to reduce class scores. The references are comprehensive and include relevant NIPS publications.

**Significance:** The proposed method addresses a practical limitation of existing saliency techniques – computational cost. The ability to perform real-time saliency detection opens up possibilities for applications like video analysis and interactive visual explanations. The improved performance on ImageNet localization and the introduction of a new, theoretically motivated saliency metric are significant contributions. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in weakly supervised object localization and offers a promising direction for future research in interpretable machine learning. The work could have a broad impact, potentially influencing how we understand and debug deep learning models.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The learned saliency map predictor is a novel and promising approach.
*   **Performance:** The method achieves competitive results on standard benchmarks.
*   **Practicality:** The real-time capability is a significant advantage.
*   **New Metric:** The introduced saliency metric provides a more nuanced evaluation of saliency maps.
*   **Sound Methodology:**  The experiments are well-designed and the results are well-supported.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The objective function and training process are somewhat complex and could be simplified.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the saliency metric is motivated, its theoretical underpinnings could be explored further.
*   **Limited Analysis of Biases:** The acknowledgment of potential biases in the masking model is good, but a more in-depth analysis would be beneficial.




**Overall Assessment:** I recommend accepting this paper. The work is technically sound, novel, and has the potential to make a significant impact on the field of interpretable machine learning. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper meets the NIPS criteria for acceptance. While further investigation into the theoretical aspects of the saliency metric and potential biases would be valuable, these are not critical flaws that would justify rejection.
