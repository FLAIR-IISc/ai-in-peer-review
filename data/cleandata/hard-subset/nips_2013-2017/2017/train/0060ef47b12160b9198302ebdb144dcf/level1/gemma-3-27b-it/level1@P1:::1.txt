This paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method by training a masking model to predict salient regions for a given classifier. The core idea is to learn a mask that, when applied to the input, reveals the regions most crucial for the classifier’s decision. This contrasts with iterative methods which are computationally expensive. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept and demonstrate superior performance on ImageNet object localization compared to other weakly supervised approaches.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in saliency detection and interpretability. Gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] offer speed but lack quality, while iterative patch removal [17] and optimization-based approaches [1, 2] are more accurate but slow. The proposed method aims to bridge this gap by learning a fast saliency predictor. The connection to Fong & Vedaldi [2] is explicitly acknowledged, positioning this work as a learned alternative to their iterative optimization approach.  The introduction of a new saliency metric, while rooted in SSR/SDR concepts [2], aims to provide a more informative evaluation than standard localization accuracy.  Related work in U-Net architectures [8] and ResNet encoders [3] are also appropriately cited.

**Strengths:**

* **Speed:** The primary strength is the significant speed improvement over iterative saliency methods. The authors claim real-time performance, making it applicable to dynamic applications like video analysis.
* **Accuracy:** Empirical results on ImageNet localization demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques. The introduced saliency metric also supports this claim, showing better alignment with ground truth saliency.
* **Novelty:** The learned masking model is a novel approach to saliency detection, offering a compelling alternative to existing methods.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The motivation for the work is clear, and the proposed method is explained in detail.  The inclusion of diagrams (though needing slight improvement, see Weaknesses) aids understanding.
* **Completeness:** The authors address potential issues like introduced evidence (adversarial artifacts) and propose mitigation strategies.  They also demonstrate the method’s applicability to a different dataset (CIFAR-10).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Equation 4) contains several terms and hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4). While justified, the sensitivity of the results to these parameters is not thoroughly explored. Ablation studies regarding these hyperparameters would be valuable.
* **Masking Model Architecture Details:** While the U-Net architecture is mentioned, the specific configuration (number of channels, filter sizes, etc.) isn’t entirely clear. More detailed architectural information is needed for reproducibility.
* **Visualization Clarity:**  Figures 1, 3, and 5 are somewhat difficult to interpret due to their size and resolution. Higher-resolution versions of these figures, with clearer labeling, would significantly improve comprehension. Figure 2 could be expanded to explain the SSR and SDR concepts with more illustrative examples.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the authors compare to Fong & Vedaldi [2] and other methods, a more comprehensive comparison with recent advancements in saliency detection (e.g., methods leveraging attention mechanisms) would strengthen the paper.
* **Implicit Assumptions:** The choice of blur as an alternative image 'A' in the evidence removal function relies on the assumption that blurring introduces less evidence than other methods. A more rigorous justification or exploration of alternative 'A' options would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in deep learning interpretability—efficiently identifying salient regions in images for black-box classifiers.
* The proposed method offers a compelling trade-off between speed and accuracy, outperforming existing weakly supervised approaches.
* The introduction of a new saliency metric provides a more nuanced evaluation of saliency maps.
* The experimental results on both ImageNet and CIFAR-10 are convincing and demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The objective function’s complexity and lack of extensive hyperparameter tuning raise concerns about the robustness of the results.
* The lack of a comprehensive comparison to the latest advancements in saliency detection limits the paper’s impact.
* The limited details regarding the masking model architecture and visualization issues hinder reproducibility and understanding.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution to the field of interpretability. The proposed method offers a significant improvement in speed without sacrificing accuracy. While some weaknesses remain, particularly regarding the objective function and architectural details, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the clarity of figures, providing more architectural details, and conducting ablation studies to assess the sensitivity of results to hyperparameters.  A slightly broader comparison to the current literature would also be valuable.
