- Proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency method that trains a masking model to predict saliency maps for any differentiable classifier in a single forward pass.  
- Addresses the high computational cost of iterative saliency methods by replacing them with a learned, feed-forward predictor.  
- Introduces a new saliency metric (based on SSR) to evaluate saliency maps.  
- Demonstrates competitive results on ImageNet localization and CIFAR-10, outperforming other weakly supervised methods.  
- Method is technically sound, clearly defined, and accompanied by well-designed experiments and ablation studies.  
- Training procedure is complex; authors acknowledge and address risks like overfitting and artifact introduction.  
- Explanation of the loss function (Equation 4) is dense and would benefit from clearer breakdown of each term.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, with helpful visual aids, but Section 3.3 (objective) and Section 4 (masking architecture) could be simplified.  
- Core idea of learning saliency maps (instead of iterative computation) is novel and well-situated in related work.  
- Combination of masking model, objective function, and new metric adds to the paper’s originality.  
- Practical significance: real-time saliency enables applications like video analysis and interactive explanations.  
- New metric and improved localization performance advance weakly supervised object localization and interpretability research.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, strong performance, practicality, new metric, and sound methodology.  
- Arguments against acceptance: training/objective complexity, limited theoretical grounding for the metric, and insufficient analysis of masking-model biases.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — strengths and impact outweigh weaknesses, though further theoretical and bias analyses are desirable.
