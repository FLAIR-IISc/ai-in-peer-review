Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a fast, model-conditioned saliency method that trains a masking network to produce per-class saliency maps for a fixed, differentiable image classifier. The key objective combines: (i) smoothness (TV penalty), (ii) area sparsity, (iii) sufficiency (preserving region yields high class score), and (iv) destruction (removing region yields low score), thereby unifying “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) and “smallest destroying region” (SDR). To mitigate artifacts from masking, the edited image blends the original with a randomly sampled alternative (either heavy blur or noisy constant color), and the mask output is constrained to be smooth and low-frequency. The masking model uses a U-Net with class conditioning via a learned class embedding and a multiplicative feature filter on high-level features, followed by upsampling to a mask. Training uses the black-box classifier’s outputs (no change to the classifier), with a trick of injecting fake labels 30% of the time so the network must attend to the class selector.

The paper also proposes a saliency metric s(a, p) = log(max(a, 0.05)) − log(p) that measures how small a rectangular crop enclosing the salient region is while retaining high class confidence. Empirically, the method is evaluated on ImageNet (with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50 as “black boxes”) and on CIFAR-10, showing sharp, interpretable masks, competitive weakly supervised localization (WSOL) error, and strong scores on the proposed metric. Compared to iterative perturbation methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, developed in parallel) and gradient-based methods, this approach claims better quality and orders-of-magnitude speed (single forward pass), enabling real-time use.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing of SSR/SDR and explicit objective that balances both; addresses artifacts through thoughtful design (TV, down/up-sampling, random A).
- Practical contribution: a feed-forward saliency predictor that generalizes across images/classes and runs in real time.
- Sensible architecture: class-conditioned U-Net with a simple, interpretable feature filter; training trick with fake labels avoids ignoring the class selector.
- Empirical breadth: three different ImageNet classifiers and a distinct dataset (CIFAR-10). WSOL results appear strong for a weakly supervised method.
- Proposed saliency metric aligns with SSR intuition and reduces susceptibility to adversarial masking by validating recognition on crops rather than masked composites.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “Black box” terminology is potentially misleading: training requires backpropagation through the classifier (any “differentiable” classifier), which is not black-box in the strict sense of no gradient access. Clarify the assumption and scope.
- Limited ablations: the paper motivates several design choices (TV, mask resolution/upsampling, random A schedule, fake-label probability, objective weights). It would strengthen the claims to quantify their individual contributions and the SSR vs SDR terms’ trade-off.
- The proposed metric depends on resizing cropped regions without preserving aspect ratio; this can interact with classifier invariances and may favor certain methods. Additional validation with alternative perturbation/measurement protocols would increase confidence.
- Comparisons: while qualitative results are convincing, the quantitative comparisons to iterative baselines (e.g., meaningful perturbations) and CAM-style WSOL are not deeply dissected. For fairness, reporting computation budgets, threshold sensitivity, and whether methods were tuned for the evaluation protocol would help.
- Generalization beyond the training data distribution is not probed (domain shift, different datasets or classes unseen in classifier training).
- CIFAR-10 evaluation is qualitative; a quantitative proxy (e.g., the proposed metric, localization to known object extents) would be helpful.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Generally strong. The objective is well-motivated; architecture/training are plausible and described with key hyperparameters. Empirical evidence shows improved WSOL and the new metric. However, missing ablations and deeper fairness checks modestly weaken the strength of evidence.
- Clarity: Good. The paper explains SSR/SDR, artifact issues, and training heuristics clearly. The architecture is described in enough detail to reimplement; hyperparameters are given. Minor points: be explicit about optimizer, learning rates, training epochs per model, and dataset splits; clarify “black box” vs “fixed differentiable classifier.”
- Originality: Moderate-to-strong. The idea of optimizing image perturbations for saliency is known; the main novelty is training a feed-forward, class-conditioned predictor to approximate the optimization, plus the combined SSR/SDR objective and the proposed crop-based metric. The approach is a meaningful combination with practical impact.
- Significance: High practical significance due to speed and improved interpretability, likely to influence subsequent work on real-time explanations and WSOL. Scientific significance is solid but would be higher with stronger ablations and broader comparisons.

Questions for the authors
- Do you require gradient access to the classifier during training? If yes, please rephrase “black box” and discuss applicability when gradients are unavailable.
- How sensitive are results to the random A schedule, TV weight, and fake-label probability? Can you provide ablations?
- Do results change when the class selector is the model’s predicted class vs. the ground-truth label?
- Have you evaluated with aspect-ratio-preserving crops or with multiple crop shapes, and how does that affect the metric and rankings?

Pros
- Fast, single-pass saliency with sharp, interpretable maps
- Well-posed objective bridging SSR/SDR; sound handling of artifacts
- Strong WSOL performance; new metric aligns with human interpretability
- Clear architecture and practical training tricks

Cons
- Requires differentiable access to the classifier; “black box” framing is ambiguous
- Limited ablations and fairness analysis in comparisons
- Metric has design choices (resizing, rectangular crops) that could bias results
- CIFAR-10 lacks quantitative evaluation

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution that meets NIPS criteria well on clarity and practical significance, with good technical quality and moderate originality. With additional ablations and clearer scope regarding classifier access, it would be an easy accept. As is, I lean to accept based on demonstrated effectiveness, speed, and clear exposition.
