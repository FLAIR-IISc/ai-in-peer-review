- Proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency method: a trainable U-Net-style masking network (ResNet-50 encoder) producing class-conditioned saliency masks in one forward pass.  
- Training objective combines total variation (smoothness), area penalty (compactness), preserve (SSR) and destroy (SDR) terms to keep/reduce class probability as appropriate.  
- Mitigates masking artifacts by randomly replacing masked pixels with either heavy blur or random color+noise during training.  
- Uses class-conditioning via feature gating and a fake-class selector 30% of the time to enforce conditioning.  
- Introduces a saliency metric based on area of the tight rectangular crop around the mask and classifier confidence; formula in paper is ambiguous.  
- Experiments train separate masking models for AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 on ImageNet and report improved weakly-supervised localization and the proposed metric.  
- Shows qualitative CIFAR-10 results with a model trained from scratch but no quantitative CIFAR metrics reported.  
- Claims runtime >100 FPS at 224x224, which is plausible given architecture but hardware/details are unspecified.  
- Objective is technically coherent and addresses common pitfalls like artifacts, smoothness, and need for SSR+SDR.  
- Class-conditioning and fake-label training strategy are plausible and reasonable design choices.  
- Empirically yields sharper masks and improved localization compared to several gradient-based and iterative baselines.  
- Missing ablations: no reported impact of SSR/SDR loss terms, TV weight, A choices, fake-label rate, or mask resolution.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity, optimizer/training schedules, and hardware for runtime claims are not specified, hurting reproducibility.  
- The “black-box” claim is limited: training requires gradients through the classifier, so true black-box models without gradient access are not supported.  
- Saliency metric definition is ambiguous (unclear whether logs are summed, differed, or multiplied) and needs clarification and analysis.  
- Evaluation protocol lacks clarity for baselines (e.g., GT vs predicted class for localization); apples-to-apples comparisons are needed.  
- Original contribution: a feed-forward, class-conditioned masking model approximating perturbation-based optimization, offering practical speedups over iterative methods.  
- Artifact-mitigation strategy (random A + TV) is sensible and well integrated.  
- Significance: enables real-time, interpretable saliency maps useful for interactive and video settings, and a metric that could improve evaluation practices.  
- Limitations: requires per-classifier training, needs classifier differentiability/gradient access, and the metric is classifier-dependent (may bias results).  
- Strengths: single-pass real-time saliency, joint SSR/SDR objective, clear architecture with gating, strong ImageNet localization results, and a concrete saliency metric.  
- Weaknesses: ambiguous metric, limited ablations/sensitivity analyses, incomplete comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM family, and sparse reproducibility details.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify metric formula and biases, add comprehensive ablations, compare to CAM/Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++, report CIFAR-10 quantitative results, test cross-model transfer, and provide full training/runtime details.  
- Arguments for acceptance: practical, real-time, artifact-robust saliency with strong ImageNet results and a well-motivated SSR+SDR objective.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete comparative evaluation (notably CAM/Grad-CAM), limited ablations, ambiguous metric, and constrained “black-box” claims due to training gradient requirements.
