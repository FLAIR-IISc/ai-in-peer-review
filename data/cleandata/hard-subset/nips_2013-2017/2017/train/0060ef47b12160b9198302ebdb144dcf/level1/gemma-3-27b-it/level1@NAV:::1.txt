## Review of “Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers”

This paper presents a novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for any differentiable image classifier. The authors address the limitations of existing iterative methods, which are computationally expensive, by training a masking model to directly predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. This model aims to identify image regions crucial for the classifier's decision, offering interpretability and potential applications like video saliency. The paper introduces a new saliency metric based on the “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) concept and demonstrates state-of-the-art results on ImageNet object localisation, outperforming other weakly supervised methods.

The work builds upon a significant body of research in saliency detection, referencing gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative removal approaches [17, 1, 2], and recent work leveraging optimization [2]. The authors clearly position their contribution as a move away from slow, iterative processes toward a learned, efficient model. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, highlighting the need to understand classifier behavior beyond accuracy [6, 13].

**Strengths:** The primary strength lies in the speed and potential for real-time applications. This is a significant advancement over existing methods. The proposed masking model architecture (U-Net with ResNet backbone) appears well-suited to the task. The introduction of a new saliency metric, tied to the SSR concept, is a valuable contribution, and the experimental results on ImageNet localization are compelling, demonstrating performance competitive with fully supervised techniques. The inclusion of CIFAR-10 experiments shows generalizability. 

**Weaknesses:** While the paper is generally well-written, the detailed explanation of the objective function (equation 4) is dense and could benefit from a more intuitive breakdown. The justification for certain design choices (e.g., the two-channel output of the U-Net and the specific sigmoid nonlinearity) feels somewhat lacking. While the authors mention potential biases in their model, a deeper discussion of how these biases might impact interpretation would be beneficial. The ablation studies are limited – it would be valuable to demonstrate the impact of each component of the loss function. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Addresses a key limitation of existing saliency methods (speed).
* Achieves strong results on ImageNet localisation, surpassing other weakly supervised approaches.
* Introduces a novel and theoretically grounded saliency metric.
* Demonstrates generalizability to a different dataset (CIFAR-10).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* The objective function is complex and could benefit from further clarification.
* Limited ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of individual components.
* Discussion of potential model biases could be more thorough.

**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and delivers strong empirical results. The weaknesses are addressable and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and provide more comprehensive analysis.
