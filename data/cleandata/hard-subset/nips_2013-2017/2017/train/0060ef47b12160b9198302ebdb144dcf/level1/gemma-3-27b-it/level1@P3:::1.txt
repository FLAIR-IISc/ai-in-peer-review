This paper presents a novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, moving away from iterative methods that are computationally expensive. The core idea is to train a masking model to predict saliency maps directly, enabling single-pass detection. The paper is well-motivated, addressing the need for interpretable explanations of classifier behavior and offering a solution that balances accuracy with speed. It builds upon the recent surge of interest in understanding and visualizing deep neural networks (e.g., [13, 11, 12, 16]), while attempting to overcome the limitations of iterative approaches [17, 1, 2]. The introduction of a new saliency metric, based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, is a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed trainable masking model is a significant departure from previous iterative methods for saliency detection, offering a substantial speed improvement. This single-pass approach is genuinely innovative and addresses a practical limitation of existing techniques.
* **Performance:** The experimental results on ImageNet demonstrate competitive performance against other weakly supervised methods, including those based on iterative optimization [1, 2]. Achieving better localization accuracy is a strong indicator of the approach's effectiveness.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The inclusion of diagrams (e.g., Figure 1, Figure 4) is helpful in understanding the architecture and process. The discussion of "fighting introduced evidence" (section 3.1) is particularly insightful.
* **New Metric:** The introduced saliency metric based on SSR/SDR is a thoughtful attempt to move beyond simple localization accuracy and capture a more nuanced understanding of saliency.  The theoretical grounding and justification of the metric are strong.
* **Generalizability:** The successful application to the CIFAR-10 dataset demonstrates that the method is not limited to large-scale datasets like ImageNet.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Equation 4) includes several terms and hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4). While the authors justify each term, the sensitivity of the method to these hyperparameters is not thoroughly investigated. A more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of each term would strengthen the paper.
* **U-Net Architecture Justification:** While using a U-Net architecture [8] is sensible, the specific reasons for choosing this architecture over others aren't fully explained. Further discussion of alternatives and why U-Net is particularly well-suited would be beneficial.
* **Adversarial Artifacts:** While the paper addresses the issue of adversarial artifacts, the effectiveness of the proposed solutions (e.g., equation 2, random A) isn’t rigorously evaluated. Demonstrating that these strategies truly mitigate the problem, perhaps with quantitative results, would enhance the credibility.
* **Limited Comparison to Gradient-Based Methods:** While the paper mentions gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] as being faster but of lower quality, a direct comparison of runtime and saliency map quality (using the proposed metric) against a representative gradient-based method would provide valuable context.
* **ResNet Encoder Dependency:** The reliance on a pre-trained ResNet encoder could introduce biases. While the CIFAR-10 experiment shows the masking model can work without pre-training, exploring the performance when the entire model is trained from scratch on ImageNet would be insightful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel and practical approach to saliency detection.
* The experimental results are promising and demonstrate competitive performance.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology.
* The proposed saliency metric is a valuable contribution to the field.
* The problem addressed is important and relevant to the NIPS audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the objective function and the lack of detailed ablation study.
* The somewhat limited investigation of the adversarial artifact mitigation strategies.
* The potential bias introduced by the pre-trained ResNet encoder.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning. The proposed method offers a significant improvement in speed and practicality compared to existing techniques, while maintaining competitive performance. The introduction of the new saliency metric is a valuable addition, and the paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Conduct a more thorough ablation study of the objective function hyperparameters.
2.  Provide a more detailed evaluation of the adversarial artifact mitigation strategies.
3.  Consider including a comparison to a representative gradient-based method for saliency detection.
4. Explore training the masking model from scratch with ImageNet data.
