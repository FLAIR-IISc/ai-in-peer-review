

This paper introduces a novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, proposing a masking model that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass rather than using iterative methods. The authors build upon previous work by Zhou et al. (SSR/SDR methods) and Fong & Vedaldi (optimization-based approaches), addressing their key limitation of computational inefficiency. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region concept, which better evaluates interpretability than standard localization accuracy.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with comprehensive experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, showing that their method outperforms existing weakly supervised approaches on object localization tasks while achieving real-time performance (100 saliency maps per second). The masking model architecture (based on U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) is well-suited for the task, and the authors provide a thoughtful analysis of artifacts and evidence removal. The new saliency metric is well-motivated and demonstrates that their method's explanations are comparable to ground truth bounding boxes.

Strengths include: (1) significant practical contribution with real-time capability enabling new applications like video saliency; (2) thorough evaluation with multiple baselines including iterative methods and central/crop baselines; (3) clear demonstration of the method's generalizability across different classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50); and (4) thoughtful analysis of artifacts and evidence introduction.

Weaknesses: The paper could better address potential limitations regarding the masking model's generalizability across radically different classifier architectures. While they test on three ImageNet classifiers, they don't explore more fundamental architectural differences. Additionally, the evaluation of their new metric would be strengthened by human evaluation of saliency map quality.

The work advances the state of the art in model interpretability with a practical, efficient solution that produces high-quality, sharp saliency maps. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear significance for both practitioners and researchers. The paper is clearly written with appropriate technical depth, making it suitable for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
