This paper proposes a fast and accurate model-based saliency detection method that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The method trains a masking model to manipulate the scores of the classifier by masking salient parts of the input image. The model generalises well to unseen images and requires a single forward pass to perform saliency detection, making it suitable for real-time systems.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the CIFAR-10 and ImageNet datasets, showing that the produced saliency maps are easily interpretable, sharp, and free of artifacts. They also introduce a new metric for saliency and test their method on the ImageNet object localisation task, achieving results that outperform other weakly supervised methods.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and its advantages over existing approaches. The experimental results are thorough and well-presented, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of their method's performance.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address the problem of saliency detection in a model-agnostic way. The authors' approach can be applied to any differentiable image classifier, making it a versatile tool for a wide range of applications.

The paper also makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a new metric for saliency. The proposed metric is based on the concept of smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR), and it provides a more nuanced evaluation of saliency detection methods.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. One potential limitation of the method is its reliance on a pre-trained ResNet encoder. While the authors demonstrate that their method can be trained from scratch, it is not clear how well the method would perform with other architectures or training protocols.

Another potential limitation is the method's sensitivity to hyperparameters. The authors report that they had to perform a grid search over a range of hyperparameters to achieve good results, which may not be practical for all users.

Finally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the method's computational complexity. While the authors report that their method can process over 100 images per second on a standard GPU, it is not clear how this compares to other saliency detection methods.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of saliency detection and provides a versatile and effective tool for a wide range of applications.
