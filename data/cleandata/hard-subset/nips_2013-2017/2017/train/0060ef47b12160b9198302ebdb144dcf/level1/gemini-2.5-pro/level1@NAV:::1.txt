This paper introduces a novel, model-based approach for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers in real-time. The core idea is to train a separate U-Net-based masking model to produce a saliency mask in a single forward pass, effectively amortizing the optimization process across the dataset. This contrasts with prior work like Fong and Vedaldi (2017), which performs a slow, iterative optimization for each input image, and with faster but lower-quality gradient-based methods. The authors propose a comprehensive objective function incorporating mask smoothness, area, and criteria analogous to Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and Smallest Destroying Region (SDR), alongside a new information-theoretic saliency metric for evaluation.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The concept of training a generative model to produce explanations is a compelling and novel shift from per-instance optimization. This enables real-time applications, such as video saliency, previously out of reach for high-quality perturbation-based methods, representing a significant practical advance.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. Claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, using multiple modern architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) as black boxes. The results, particularly the state-of-the-art performance on the weakly-supervised ImageNet localization task, are impressive and demonstrate the method's effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the proposed objective function and training procedure are explained in sufficient detail for reproduction. The introduction of a new saliency metric is a thoughtful contribution to the difficult problem of evaluating explainability.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance / For Improvement):**
*   **Conceptual Depth:** The primary weakness is the use of one complex neural network to explain another. While the authors acknowledge that their masking model might have its own biases, this is a fundamental concern that warrants more than a brief mention in the conclusion. The risk is that we are not seeing a true explanation of the black-box model, but rather an interpretation filtered through the biases of the masking model.
*   **Technical Justification:** The clarity could be slightly improved with a more thorough justification for certain design choices, such as the nonstandard nonlinearity used to generate the final mask (Eq. 8), beyond noting that it “greatly improved training.”
*   **Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper presents strong positive results but would benefit from an analysis of failure cases. When does the model produce poor or misleading saliency maps, and what does this reveal about the limitations of the approach or the behavior of the underlying black-box classifier?

Overall, the novelty of the approach and the strong empirical results outweigh the conceptual concerns. This work presents a significant step forward in making high-quality saliency analysis practical for real-time systems.
