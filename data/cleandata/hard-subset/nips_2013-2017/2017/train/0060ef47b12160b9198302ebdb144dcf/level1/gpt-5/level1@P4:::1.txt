Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method for differentiable image classifiers. Instead of per-image iterative optimization, a trainable masking network (U-Net style with a ResNet-50 encoder and class-conditioned gating) produces a saliency mask in a single forward pass for a given image and target class. The training objective combines four terms: total variation for smoothness, area penalty for compactness, a “preserve” term to keep the class probability high when the salient region is retained (SSR), and a “destroy” term to suppress the class probability when the salient region is removed (SDR). To mitigate artifacts arising from masking, the edited image replaces masked pixels with either a heavily blurred version or random color plus noise (chosen randomly each call). A training trick supplies fake class selectors 30% of the time to force conditioning on class. The authors also propose a saliency metric based on the area of the (tightest) rectangular crop around the mask and the classifier’s confidence on that crop, intended to quantify “smallest sufficient region.”

Experiments on ImageNet train separate masking models for AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 (treated as black boxes during inference) and report weakly-supervised localization accuracy, along with the proposed saliency metric, comparing against baselines including gradient-based methods and iterative perturbation methods. They also demonstrate qualitative results on CIFAR-10 with a masking model trained from scratch. The approach is conceptually close to iterative “meaningful perturbations” (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), but here the mask is predicted by a feed-forward network trained to jointly capture SSR and SDR; it contrasts with gradient-based saliency (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop) and CAM/weakly supervised localization (Zhou et al.).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The objective is coherent and addresses common pitfalls (adversarial artifacts, lack of smoothness, need for both SSR and SDR). The class-conditioning via feature gating is plausible, and the fake-label training strategy is a reasonable way to enforce conditioning.
- Empirical support: On ImageNet, the method yields sharper masks and improved weakly-supervised localization error compared to several baselines, approaching fully supervised localization performance. The proposed metric also favors the method. Runtime claims (>100 FPS at 224x224) are plausible given architecture.
- Missing analyses: Ablations are not reported (e.g., impact of the 3rd/4th loss terms, TV weight, the alternative image A choices, fake-label rate, mask resolution). Sensitivity to hyperparameters is unknown. There is no quantitative evaluation on CIFAR-10. Details such as optimizer, learning rates, training schedules, and hardware for runtime claims are not specified in the provided text. The “black-box” claim is limited during training (gradients through the classifier are needed).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized and clearly motivates SSR vs SDR, the artifact issue, and the architecture/training.
- The saliency metric definition is ambiguous as written: s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p) with “lower is better.” It is unclear whether this is a sum, difference, or product of logs. The text suggests a difference (log(ã) − log(p)) would match the stated behavior; please clarify the exact formula and sign conventions.
- Evaluation protocol details (e.g., whether localization uses ground-truth class vs predicted class, and how baselines are configured) are stated for the proposed method but less clear for competitors; ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.

Originality:
- Novelty lies in training a feed-forward, class-conditioned masking model to approximate the outcome of perturbation-based optimization, jointly enforcing SSR and SDR, and in the proposed saliency metric. This is a substantive step beyond iterative methods (Fong & Vedaldi) and offers a practical speedup. The artifact-mitigation design (random A and TV) is sensible and well-integrated.
- Related work coverage is reasonable but could better situate against CAM/Grad-CAM-style localization; the distinction versus CAM-based methods (architectural changes vs post hoc analysis) and quantitative comparisons would strengthen the claim.

Significance:
- The ability to produce real-time, artifact-robust saliency maps is practically impactful, enabling interactive and video settings. Improved weakly supervised localization suggests potential broader utility. The metric could stimulate more principled evaluation of saliency.
- However, the method requires training a separate saliency model per classifier and depends on differentiability and gradient access during training, which may limit immediate deployment for fully black-box (no gradients) third-party models. The metric is classifier-dependent and may favor methods optimized to that classifier.

Strengths:
- Single-pass, real-time saliency with sharp, interpretable masks.
- Joint SSR/SDR objective, with explicit artifact mitigation and smoothness enforcement.
- Clear architectural design with class-conditioned gating and practical training heuristics.
- Strong empirical results on weakly supervised localization; compelling qualitative results.
- Introduction of a concrete, operational saliency metric.

Weaknesses:
- Ambiguity in the metric definition; limited discussion of metric biases (e.g., classifier dependence, rectangular crops, aspect-ratio distortions).
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses; lack of quantitative CIFAR results.
- Comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM and other contemporary weakly supervised localization methods are not fully reported; fairness of using ground-truth class at test time should be discussed and, ideally, alternatives evaluated (e.g., using the model’s top-1 prediction).
- “Black-box” is somewhat overstated; gradients through the classifier are needed during training. Generalization across unseen classifiers is not evaluated.
- Reproducibility details (training hyperparameters, optimizer, runtime settings) are sparse.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the exact saliency metric formula, provide intuition, and analyze its sensitivity to resizing and to classifier calibration. Consider reporting normalized variants and human-judgment consistency.
- Add ablations: loss-term weights, choice of A, mask resolution, fake-label probability, training with/without TV, and the benefit of the class-gating module.
- Include comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM (and if possible Grad-CAM++) and Excitation Backprop using the same evaluation protocol; report both GT-label and predicted-label localization settings.
- Report quantitative CIFAR-10 metrics (localization error, proposed metric) and provide failure-case analyses.
- Evaluate cross-model generalization: train a masking model on one classifier and test on another to gauge transfer.
- Provide full training details (optimizer, learning rate, batch size, epochs, data splits) and precise runtime benchmarks on specified hardware.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong practical contribution: real-time, high-quality saliency with demonstrated gains over iterative and gradient-based baselines.
- Well-motivated objective addressing SSR and SDR together, with thoughtful artifact mitigation.
- Promising results on a standard large-scale dataset and a useful evaluation metric.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incomplete comparative evaluation (notably against CAM/Grad-CAM), limited ablations, and ambiguity in the metric definition.
- Dependence on classifier gradients during training and per-classifier training reduces generality of the “black-box” framing.
- Limited quantitative evidence beyond ImageNet localization and the proposed (classifier-dependent) metric.
