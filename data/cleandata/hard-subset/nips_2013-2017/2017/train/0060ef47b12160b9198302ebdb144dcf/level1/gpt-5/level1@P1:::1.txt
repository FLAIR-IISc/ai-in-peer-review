Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a trainable masking model that produces per-class image saliency maps for a fixed classifier in a single forward pass. The model is a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and a class-conditional gating (“feature filter”) at the top feature map; it outputs a soft mask used to perturb the input. Training minimizes a composite loss that (i) enforces smoothness (TV), (ii) penalizes mask area, (iii) preserves the target class score when the salient region is kept (SSR), and (iv) suppresses the class score when the salient region is removed (SDR). To reduce artifacts from masking, the method uses a randomized “alternative image” (heavy blur or noisy color) in the compositing function and TV regularization. To prevent the model from ignoring the class selector, fake labels are sampled 30% of the time.

The paper also proposes a new saliency metric inspired by SSR: find the tightest rectangle enclosing the predicted salient region, crop and resize it to the classifier’s input size, and compute s(a, p) = log(max(a, 0.05)) / log(p), where a is crop area fraction and p is the classifier’s probability. Lower is better.

The authors evaluate on ImageNet (using AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50 as black-boxes) and CIFAR-10. On ImageNet, their weakly supervised localization error is competitive with fully supervised VGG and better than other weakly supervised/perturbation-based approaches; saliency maps are visually sharp. On CIFAR-10 (with a FitNet classifier), they show the approach works without a pretrained encoder.

Relative to prior work: gradient-based saliency (Simonyan et al., Springenberg et al., Guided Backprop), CAM/Excitation Backprop (Zhou et al., Zhang et al.), and perturbation-based iterative methods (Fong & Vedaldi, concurrent to this paper; also Cao et al.) are discussed. The key difference is replacing per-image iterative optimization (as in Fong & Vedaldi) with a learned generator optimized to the perturbation objective, yielding real-time inference.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling objective that unifies SSR and SDR, with thoughtful regularization to reduce introduced evidence and artifacts.
- Practical training tricks (randomized alternative image, fake labels, class embedding gating) that appear effective and reproducible.
- Real-time inference with sharp, high-resolution masks; plausible for video applications.
- Strong results on weakly supervised ImageNet localization; saliency metric shows favorable performance and correlates with interpretability.
- Demonstrates applicability across different classifiers and datasets; CIFAR-10 variant trained from scratch.

Weaknesses
- “Black box” terminology is misleading: training requires backpropagation through the fixed classifier. The method is agnostic to the classifier’s architecture but not truly black-box at train time; differentiability and gradient access are required.
- The proposed saliency metric depends on the same classifier used during training, on its calibration, and on resizing artifacts. The ad hoc clipping at 0.05 and aspect ratio disregard may bias the metric; cross-classifier evaluation or human studies would strengthen claims.
- Limited ablations: no quantitative breakdown of the contributions of the SSR vs SDR terms, TV strength, randomized A choices, or class gating. No analysis of sensitivity to the 0.5 threshold used to binarize masks for localization.
- Comparisons appear incomplete: CAM/Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017), Guided Backprop, and Excitation Backprop should be compared under identical settings; details (numbers, variance) are missing in the text.
- A separate masking model must be trained per classifier; generalization across classifiers or to unseen classes is not evaluated.
- Failure cases (multi-object scenes, clutter, small objects, non-top-1 class conditioning) are not systematically analyzed.

Quality
The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The compositional loss and artifact mitigation are sensible, and the architecture choice (U-Net with multi-scale skip connections) is appropriate for sharp masks. However, the evaluation would benefit from:
- Rigorous ablations (loss components, TV, “A” variants, fake label rate).
- Fair, strong baselines (CAM/Grad-CAM, Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop, Fong & Vedaldi) on the same splits and classifiers with standardized metrics; report variance.
- Cross-classifier generalization (train with classifier A, evaluate masks with classifier B).
- Additional metrics (e.g., deletion/insertion curves, occlusion sensitivity) and, ideally, a small user study to corroborate interpretability.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The introduced-evidence discussion is thoughtful. Notation is mostly consistent. Some missing details limit reproducibility: optimizer, learning rates, schedule, data splits, mask threshold choice rationale, and exact baselines used in tables. Clarify whether gradients flow through the black-box during training (they must, given Eq. 4), reinforcing the “fixed white-box” nature.

Originality
Moderate-to-high. The central idea—learning a conditional mask generator to approximate the perturbation-based saliency objective—appears novel relative to prior iterative optimization methods. The unification of SSR and SDR in the loss and the practical training scheme are useful. The metric is a reasonable SSR-inspired proxy, though not conceptually groundbreaking.

Significance
High practical significance if claims hold: real-time, sharp, per-class saliency is valuable for debugging, weakly supervised localization, and video. The approach could catalyze a line of learned-explainer models tuned to target networks. The per-classifier training requirement and metric dependence temper generality but are acceptable for 2017 standards.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide comprehensive ablations and sensitivity analyses.
- Add standardized comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM, Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop, and Fong & Vedaldi on the same classifiers/datasets.
- Evaluate cross-classifier transfer and report failure cases.
- Assess the proposed metric’s robustness: use different classifiers for metric computation, preserve aspect ratio, report correlation with localization accuracy and deletion/insertion metrics.
- Clarify the “black box” claim and differentiability requirement; discuss cost of training a separate explainer per classifier.
- Release code and pretrained models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and practical approach yielding real-time, high-quality saliency with strong weakly supervised localization.
- Well-motivated objective and training design addressing introduced evidence and artifacts.
- Potentially impactful for interpretability and downstream applications (video, debugging).

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete empirical analysis and missing strong baselines/ablations weaken the strength of the claims.
- Proposed metric may favor the method and lacks robustness studies.
- “Black box” framing is inaccurate; per-classifier training limits generality.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The idea of learning a fast, generalizable masking model for perturbation-based saliency is timely and significant, and the empirical results are promising. Strengthening the experimental comparisons and clarifying limitations would make this a strong NeurIPS contribution.
