Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a trainable masking model that produces image-level saliency maps in a single forward pass for any given class and image, with the goal of explaining the decisions of an underlying differentiable classifier (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50). The key idea is to train a U-Net-style network (with a fixed ResNet-50 encoder for ImageNet, a smaller custom encoder for CIFAR-10) to output a soft mask M that simultaneously satisfies two desiderata: smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR). The training objective combines (i) a total-variation smoothness prior and (ii) area regularization with (iii) a “preserve” term that encourages high fc(φ(X,M)) and (iv) a “destroy” term that encourages low fc(φ(X,1−M)), where φ applies the mask by mixing the original image and a randomized “alternative” image A (blurred X or random color + noise) to fight adversarial artifacts. The paper also proposes a new evaluation metric s(a,p) based on the log-area of the tight crop around the salient region and the log-probability of the class on the cropped region, intended to capture SSR qualities while discouraging adversarial artifacts.

Relative to gradient/backprop-based attribution (Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), the method aims for better interpretability and localization by optimizing for causal perturbations rather than relying on sensitivity. Relative to iterative perturbation methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi’s Meaningful Perturbations developed in parallel; Cao et al. activation sparsification; Zhou et al.’s iterative preservation), the novelty is to amortize the optimization via a learned masking network, yielding real-time inference and, the authors argue, sharper, more faithful masks. The method is weakly supervised (labels only) and evaluated on ImageNet localization and via the proposed saliency metric; it also demonstrates transfer to CIFAR-10 with a scratch-trained masking network.

Strengths:
- Clear problem framing (SSR/SDR) and thoughtful discussion of “introduced evidence,” including practical countermeasures (randomized A, TV regularization, low-res mask upsampling).
- Amortized approach is compelling: one forward pass per image/class, with competitive or better localization performance than weakly supervised baselines and substantial speed advantage over iterative perturbation.
- Architecture/training details are pragmatic and novel: class-conditional gating via a learned embedding at the coarsest ResNet scale; two-channel output nonlinearity for masks; “fake class” sampling to enforce class conditioning.
- The proposed metric targets faithfulness to SSR and attempts to reduce adversarial confounds by cropping rather than masking.
- CIFAR-10 results show the approach is not dependent on a pre-trained encoder and can work at low resolutions.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Claims of “black-box” are overstated. Training requires a differentiable classifier and end-to-end gradients (the objective depends on fc(φ(X,M))). This is model-agnostic across differentiable architectures but not black-box in the strict sense used in the explanations literature.
- Objective clarity: Eq. (4) appears to have sign inconsistencies with the textual description; minimizing L should include a −log fc(φ(X,M)) preserve term. Please correct signs and specify λ4’s role formally (is the destroy term fc(·) raised to a power?).
- Proposed metric s(a,p): the formula as written (log(ã) log(p)) is ambiguous. If it is a product, both logs are negative for p<1, ã<1, making interpretation and comparisons tricky. The text suggests “lower is better” and an information-theoretic interpretation; more precise definition, normalization, and sensitivity analyses (e.g., to aspect ratio distortion on resize) are needed. The metric’s dependence on classifier invariances (scale/aspect ratio) could bias comparisons across methods and classes.
- Evaluation completeness: comparisons omit strong contemporaneous methods such as CAM/Grad-CAM (Zhou et al. 2016; Selvaraju et al. 2017), SmoothGrad (Smilkov et al. 2017), and Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al. 2017). Localization metrics should include pointing-game or pixel-level overlap where available, and threshold sensitivity (the 0.5 mask threshold choice seems arbitrary).
- Potential metric–training alignment: the loss encourages SSR-like behavior, and the proposed metric also rewards SSR, which may advantage the proposed method relative to methods optimized for different notions of saliency. It would help to evaluate on metrics that do not mirror the training objective (e.g., deletion/insertion curves).
- Generalization and scope: a separate masking model is trained per classifier. This limits practicality in settings with many downstream models. Some analysis of cross-classifier transfer (train on one classifier, test on another) would strengthen the “model-agnostic” claim.
- Robustness to adversarial/color-shift artifacts: while randomizing A helps, it does not eliminate artifacts. Qualitative failure cases, quantitative artifact tests (e.g., boundary artifacts, background leakage), and ablations (blur-only vs. noise-only A; TV strength; resolution of mask) are missing.
- Reproducibility: optimizer, learning rates, schedules, batch sizes, augmentations, and exact dataset splits are not fully specified. Public code would be valuable.

Quality:
Methodologically sound and well-motivated. The adversarial artifact discussion is careful, and the architecture/training tricks are sensible. However, the paper needs clearer equations, stronger baselines, and ablations to fully substantiate claims.

Clarity:
Generally well written with helpful figures, but key definitions (objective signs, metric formula) need correction/clarification. Provide full training details and evaluation protocols (datasets, splits, box derivation, threshold selection).

Originality:
Amortizing perturbation-based saliency with a trainable masking net is novel relative to contemporaneous iterative methods. The random-A scheme and class-gated U-Net are practical contributions. The metric idea is interesting, though related to deletion/insertion and cropping-based tests; its exact formulation needs refinement.

Significance:
If validated with stronger baselines and ablations, the approach is impactful: real-time, causal saliency maps are useful for interactive analysis and video. The weakly-supervised localization results are promising.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix Eq. (4) signs and precisely define s(a,p), including rationale and behavior across the (a,p) range; report deletion/insertion AUC and pointing-game to triangulate faithfulness.
- Compare against Grad-CAM, SmoothGrad, Integrated Gradients, and CAM-based WSOL methods under identical settings.
- Provide ablations: effect of random-A choices, TV/area weights, mask resolution, two-channel nonlinearity, fake-class sampling rate, and freezing vs. finetuning the encoder.
- Analyze failure modes and robustness (background context reliance, border artifacts).
- Explore cross-classifier transfer and training a single masking model for multiple classifiers.
- Release code and pretrained models; document all training hyperparameters.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Compelling and practical idea: amortized perturbation-based saliency enabling real-time use.
- Solid qualitative and quantitative indications of sharper, more faithful masks and better WSOL.
- Careful treatment of artifact avoidance and class conditioning.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Ambiguities in core equations/metric and incomplete comparisons to strong contemporaneous baselines.
- Limited analysis of robustness, ablations, and cross-classifier generalization.
- The “black-box” claim is misleading; differentiability and gradients are required during training.

Overall, I find the approach promising and potentially impactful, but I recommend acceptance contingent on clarifying the objective/metric and strengthening the experimental comparisons and analyses.
