- Proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method that trains a masking model to predict regions crucial for a classifier, avoiding iterative optimization.  
- Introduces a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) to provide more informative evaluation than standard localization accuracy.  
- Demonstrates superior ImageNet object localization performance compared to other weakly supervised approaches and shows applicability to CIFAR-10.  
- Positions the method as a learned, faster alternative to Fong & Vedaldi’s iterative optimization and situates work within gradient-based and optimization-based saliency literature.  
- Strength — Speed: claims significant, real-time speed improvement over iterative saliency methods, enabling dynamic applications like video.  
- Strength — Accuracy: empirical results and the new metric indicate better alignment with ground-truth saliency than comparable weakly supervised methods.  
- Strength — Novelty: the learned masking model offers a novel approach to saliency detection.  
- Strength — Clarity: paper is generally well-written with helpful diagrams that aid understanding.  
- Strength — Completeness: addresses introduced evidence/adversarial artifacts, proposes mitigations, and tests on another dataset.  
- Weakness — Objective complexity: the loss (Eq. 4) has many terms and hyperparameters (λ1–λ4) with insufficient sensitivity analysis or ablation studies.  
- Weakness — Architectural detail gaps: U-Net mention lacks full configuration (channels, filter sizes, etc.), hindering reproducibility.  
- Weakness — Visualization issues: figures are low resolution or poorly labeled and Figure 2 needs clearer SSR/SDR illustrations.  
- Weakness — Limited comparisons: lacks comprehensive evaluation against recent saliency advances (e.g., attention-based methods).  
- Weakness — Implicit assumptions: using blur as the alternative image 'A' is assumed to be appropriate without rigorous justification or exploration of alternatives.  
- Argument for acceptance: addresses a significant interpretability challenge, offers a strong speed/accuracy trade-off, introduces a useful metric, and shows convincing experiments.  
- Argument against acceptance: robustness concerns from complex loss/hyperparameters, limited comparisons, and missing architectural/visualization details reduce impact and reproducibility.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel contribution recommending acceptance with minor revisions to improve figure clarity, provide architectural details, add hyperparameter ablations, and broaden comparisons.
