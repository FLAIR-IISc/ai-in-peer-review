This paper introduces a fast saliency detection method applicable to any differentiable image classifier, achieved by training a masking model to predict salient regions. The core idea is to move away from iterative methods and instead learn a mapping from images to saliency maps directly. The authors propose a new saliency metric and demonstrate improvements over existing weakly supervised methods on object localization.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the need for faster and more interpretable saliency maps, especially given the unintuitive behaviors often exhibited by deep classifiers. It’s clearly positioned within the existing literature on saliency detection, correctly referencing gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative removal techniques [17, 1, 2], and acknowledges parallel work [2]. The introduction effectively highlights the limitations of current approaches and establishes the paper's contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of learning a saliency map directly via a masking model represents a shift from iterative methods, offering a potential speed advantage. The proposed saliency metric is also a novel contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method, incorporating a U-Net architecture with a ResNet encoder, is technically reasonable. The detailed explanation of the objective function (Eq. 4) and the loss terms demonstrates careful consideration of potential issues like adversarial artifacts. The modifications made for CIFAR-10 suggest adaptability.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations of concepts like SSR and SDR are clear, and the descriptions of the masking model and training process are sufficiently detailed.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The results on ImageNet object localization demonstrate a clear improvement over other weakly supervised methods, and the comparison with baselines (Table 2) is convincing. The CIFAR-10 experiments further validate the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Saliency Metric:** The proposed metric (Eq. 3), tying saliency to the smallest sufficient region concept, is a worthwhile contribution and provides a useful evaluation tool.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overly Detailed Objective Function:** While thorough, the objective function (Eq. 4) with multiple terms and hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4) feels somewhat complex and potentially brittle. The justification for the specific values chosen isn't fully explained, and sensitivity analysis to these parameters is missing.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study. It’s not clear how much each component of the objective function contributes to the final performance.  Specifically, the impact of the adversarial artifact mitigation techniques and the total variation penalty could be better quantified.
*   **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to weakly supervised methods is good, the paper doesn't compare against more sophisticated, fully supervised saliency detection methods (even if requiring more annotation). This would strengthen the claim of superiority.
*   **Implicit Assumptions:** The paper assumes differentiability of the black-box classifier. While common, this is not always the case, and acknowledging this limitation would be beneficial.
*   **Minor clarity issue:** The explanation of equation (8) regarding the non-standard non-linearity feels abrupt. Further explanation would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem (interpretable AI) with a novel and technically sound approach.
*   The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over existing weakly supervised methods.
*   The proposed saliency metric is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its ideas.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The complexity of the objective function and lack of ablation studies raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the method.
*   The comparison to stronger baselines is limited.
*   The potential for the method to be sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters is not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of saliency detection. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and experimentally validated. While some weaknesses exist, particularly regarding the complexity of the objective function and the lack of extensive ablation studies, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.  I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised in the review, specifically by providing a more detailed analysis of the objective function and its components.

**Confidence:** 7/10
