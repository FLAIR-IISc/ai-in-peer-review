## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper presents a novel approach to generating saliency maps for image classifiers, aiming for speed and accuracy. The core idea is to train a masking model – a U-Net architecture – to directly predict saliency maps for any differentiable classifier in a single forward pass, circumventing the iterative methods common in the field. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) concept and demonstrate strong performance on ImageNet object localization, outperforming other weakly supervised methods. 

The work builds upon existing saliency detection techniques like gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] and iterative patch removal [17], addressing their limitations in speed and quality. It also acknowledges concurrent work by Fong & Vedaldi [2] but distinguishes itself by learning a dedicated masking model rather than relying solely on iterative optimization. The paper appropriately references relevant literature and positions its contribution within the broader context of explainable AI.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed approach of learning a saliency detector is a significant departure from iterative methods, offering a compelling trade-off between speed and accuracy.
* **Performance:** The results on ImageNet localization are promising, demonstrating competitive performance against other weakly supervised techniques. The new saliency metric appears well-motivated and provides a useful evaluation tool.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations of the objective function and masking model architecture are clear.
* **Completeness:** The authors address potential issues like adversarial artifacts and introduce techniques to mitigate them, demonstrating careful consideration of potential weaknesses. The CIFAR-10 experiment further validates the generalizability of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Equation 4) contains several terms and hyperparameters. While explained, the sensitivity of the results to these parameters isn’t thoroughly investigated.
* **Ablation Studies:** While the authors mention modifications for CIFAR-10, more detailed ablation studies exploring the impact of different architectural choices (e.g., the U-Net structure, pre-training) would strengthen the analysis.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other saliency methods is somewhat limited. A more comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art techniques, including recent gradient-based improvements, would be beneficial.
* **Metric Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of the proposed saliency metric, particularly its reliance on scale and aspect ratio invariance of the classifier. Further discussion of these limitations and potential alternatives would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and important problem in explainable AI.
* Presents a novel and technically sound approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Objective function complexity and limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Relatively limited comparison to other state-of-the-art methods.



**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution to the field of saliency detection. The proposed method offers a compelling solution to the speed limitations of existing techniques while maintaining competitive accuracy. While some areas could benefit from further investigation, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
