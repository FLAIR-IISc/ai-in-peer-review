Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a trainable, model-agnostic masking network that produces class-conditional image saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass. The key idea is to replace iterative “meaningful perturbation”–style optimization with a U-Net–like feed-forward model trained to minimize a composite objective that simultaneously encourages (i) mask smoothness (TV), (ii) small preserved region (area penalty), (iii) “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) by preserving class probability under masking, and (iv) “smallest destroying region” (SDR) by suppressing the class probability when masking the complement. To reduce artifact-driven explanations, the paper mixes two evidence-removal strategies during training (strong blur of X vs. random color + noise as A in X⊙M + A⊙(1−M)) and uses a TV penalty and low-res masks upsampled bilinearly. A simple saliency metric is introduced based on the log area of the tight rectangular crop of the salient region and the log class probability on that crop, intended to reflect the SSR trade-off. Experiments on ImageNet (with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 as fixed “black-box” classifiers; a separate masking model per classifier) show improved weakly supervised localization (top-1 box accuracy) over several baselines and prior methods, and favorable saliency metric values; a qualitative CIFAR-10 study indicates the method also works without a pretrained encoder.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against gradient/backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), erasure-based iterative saliency (e.g., Object Detectors Emerge; meaningful perturbations by Fong & Vedaldi), and activation sparsification (Cao et al.). The approach is closest in spirit to Fong & Vedaldi’s optimization in image space, but differs by (a) training a predictor to amortize saliency over images and classes, (b) combining SSR and SDR terms in the objective, and (c) explicit countermeasures against artifact reliance via randomized A. It also relates to CAM/Excitation Backprop as weakly supervised localization baselines. A few very relevant contemporaneous saliency/localization works (e.g., Grad-CAM; SmoothGrad) are not discussed and would strengthen context and comparisons.

Strengths
- Practical and fast: A single forward pass per image/class yields sharp masks at >100 fps on 224×224, enabling real-time applications.
- Sound objective: Combining SSR and SDR with smoothness/area penalties and randomized evidence removal is well reasoned and addresses known pitfalls (artifacts, overfitting to occlusion heuristics).
- Clear architectural choice: A U-Net with a class-embedding–guided feature filter is appropriate for sharp, class-conditional masks; the “fake-label” training trick is a simple, effective way to enforce class selectivity.
- Empirical gains: Competitive or better weakly supervised localization on ImageNet and improved values under the proposed saliency metric; qualitative results are compelling and noticeably crisper than iterative baselines.
- Generality: Works across multiple external classifiers; CIFAR-10 experiment suggests the approach does not require a pretrained encoder.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “Black-box” terminology: Training requires backpropagation through the classifier’s softmax (fc), so the classifier cannot be a true black box in the query-only sense; this point should be clarified (e.g., “fixed external differentiable classifier”).
- Proposed metric: s(a,p)=log(ã)·log(p) is ad hoc, sensitive to classifier calibration and resizing distortions, and not normalized across classes/models. While useful as a proxy for SSR, it could be gamed by overconfident probabilities. No analysis of calibration sensitivity, robustness to crop aspect-ratio changes, or correlation with human judgments is provided.
- Comparisons and ablations: 
  - Missing comparisons to strong 2016–2017 baselines under identical backbones, especially CAM/Grad-CAM and Excitation Backprop, and to Fong & Vedaldi’s optimization using the same perturbation scheme. 
  - No ablation of the removal choices (blur vs. noise), TV strength, mask resolution, or the two-channel mask nonlinearity; the contribution of each to sharpness and artifact resistance is unclear.
  - Iterative baseline is described qualitatively; quantitative comparisons on localization and the proposed metric with equal computational budget are limited.
- Reproducibility details: Important training details are underspecified (optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, data splits, convergence criteria). The “ensure max(X_ij^T C_s) is high for correct label” trick is mentioned but not formalized in the loss.
- Localization protocol: It is implied that ground-truth class labels are provided at test time (as in standard weakly supervised localization), but this should be stated explicitly along with the exact split (val/test), single-crop vs. multi-crop evaluation, and whether classifier accuracy differences confound localization results.
- Generalization scope: CIFAR-10 results are qualitative only; quantitative metrics (e.g., localization proxy, deletion/insertion curves) would strengthen claims. No user study or human interpretability assessment.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a coherent objective and reasonable engineering choices. The experimental evidence supports the main claims (speed and sharper maps), but stronger baselines, calibration-aware analysis of the metric, and ablations are needed to fully substantiate superiority.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with intuitive motivation (SSR vs. SDR). Some notation and objective details are ambiguous (e.g., exponent “4” on the last term in Eq. 4; exact thresholding to derive the crop; optimizer/training hyperparameters). Clarify the meaning of “black box,” the training split, and evaluation protocol.

Originality
Amortizing perturbation-based saliency via a learned masking model is a novel and useful direction in 2017. The combination of SSR+SDR terms and randomized evidence removal is a thoughtful refinement of prior iterative methods. The metric is new but somewhat heuristic.

Significance
If confirmed by stronger comparisons, this could have meaningful impact by turning high-quality perturbation-based saliency into a real-time tool. The architecture/recipe is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., to segmentation or video).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify “black-box” assumptions and provide end-to-end training details for reproducibility.
- Add strong baselines: CAM and Grad-CAM on the same external classifiers; Fong & Vedaldi with identical perturbation A and TV; report both localization and the proposed saliency metric.
- Analyze the saliency metric: effect of temperature scaling, using logits or margins instead of softmax p, robustness to aspect ratio/resizing; report correlation with deletion/insertion curves or human judgments.
- Provide ablations: effect of TV weight, A choices, mask resolution, the two-channel mask normalization, and the fake-label strategy on sharpness, metric, and localization.
- Report failure cases and class-conditional qualitative results (including non-true classes).
- Release code/models to facilitate adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple idea that delivers large speedups and sharper maps.
- Well-motivated objective addressing known pitfalls.
- Promising empirical results on a major benchmark; practical relevance.

Cons for acceptance
- Comparisons to strongest baselines are incomplete.
- Metric is heuristic and calibration-sensitive; limited validation.
- Missing ablations and some clarity/reproducibility gaps.

Overall: A strong, practical contribution with clear potential; I lean toward acceptance if the authors can strengthen baselines and clarify evaluation details.
