

This paper introduces a novel approach to real-time saliency detection for black-box classifiers by training a masking model that produces saliency maps in a single forward pass. The authors address the limitations of iterative methods (e.g., [1,2]) by training a model to predict saliency masks directly, which they claim generalizes well to unseen images and achieves superior performance compared to existing weakly supervised methods.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it proposes a new saliency metric (equation 3) that directly evaluates the smallest sufficient region concept, providing a more interpretable measure than standard localization accuracy. Second, the masking model architecture (U-Net with ResNet backbone) is well-motivated and achieves remarkable speed (100+ images/sec on GPU), making real-time applications feasible. Third, the experiments on ImageNet show clear advantages over baselines, with the ResNet-trained model achieving 36.7% localization error compared to 34.3% for supervised VGG, a significant result given the weakly supervised nature of their approach.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with comprehensive experiments across ImageNet and CIFAR-10, and the architecture design effectively addresses the problem of adversarial artifacts through careful evidence removal techniques. The authors properly contextualize their work against recent advances like Fong and Vedaldi [2] and provide a thorough discussion of limitations in their future work section.

However, the paper could strengthen its claim of "generalization to unseen images" by including additional validation on datasets outside ImageNet/CIFAR-10. The paper also doesn't fully explore how the new metric might behave on edge cases or specific object classes. While the masking model's complexity (U-Net with ResNet backbone) is justified, the paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why this specific architecture was chosen over simpler alternatives.

The significance of this work is high, as real-time saliency detection enables applications in safety-critical systems like autonomous vehicles. The paper's clarity is excellent, with well-organized sections and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The novelty lies in the model-agnostic, single-pass approach rather than the metric itself, representing a meaningful advancement in the field.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a meaningful problem with well-supported claims. With minor clarifications regarding generalization and metric limitations, this paper would be an excellent addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
