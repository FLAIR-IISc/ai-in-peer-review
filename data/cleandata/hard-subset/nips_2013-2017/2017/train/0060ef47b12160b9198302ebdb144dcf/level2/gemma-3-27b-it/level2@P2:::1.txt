This paper proposes a novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for black-box classifiers. The core idea is to train a masking model to predict saliency maps directly, rather than relying on iterative methods like gradient-based approaches or patch removal. The authors claim this allows for significantly faster saliency detection with comparable or superior quality, and they demonstrate this on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, introducing a new saliency metric along the way.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in image saliency and interpretability, correctly citing relevant papers like Simonyan et al. (2013), Zeiler & Fergus (2014), and Zhou et al. (2016, 2015). The authors also acknowledge recent concurrent work by Fong & Vedaldi (2017) and Cao et al. (2015), placing their method in context. They effectively highlight the limitations of previous methods, namely their computational cost or lack of quality, providing a strong motivation for their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of training a dedicated model to predict saliency maps is a significant departure from iterative methods and offers a compelling solution to the speed-accuracy trade-off.
* **Speed:** The claim of real-time performance is a key strength, opening up possibilities for applications like video saliency analysis.
* **Empirical Results:** The results on ImageNet, especially the performance on the object localisation task, are promising and demonstrate that the proposed method can outperform other weakly supervised approaches.  The comparison to Fong & Vedaldi (2017) is well-handled.
* **New Saliency Metric:** The SSR/SDR-inspired metric (s(a,p)) is a thoughtful addition, providing a more nuanced evaluation of saliency maps than simple localisation accuracy. The theoretical justification based on information theory is a plus.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors address potential issues like adversarial artifacts and introduce techniques to mitigate them, showing a careful consideration of potential pitfalls. The CIFAR-10 experiment provides further validation across datasets.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup. The diagrams (Figures 1, 4) are helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Equation 4) contains several terms and hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4). The justification for the specific values chosen isn’t fully explored. A more detailed ablation study on the impact of these parameters would strengthen the results.
* **Masking Model Architecture:** While the U-Net architecture is a reasonable choice, the rationale for some design choices (e.g., the two-channel output and the non-standard nonlinearity in Equation 8) isn’t sufficiently explained.  Why did standard activation functions fail?
* **Generalization to Different Classifiers:** While the experiments demonstrate good results with AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50, the extent to which the masking model generalizes to drastically different classifier architectures isn't fully clear. Further investigation on more diverse classifiers would be beneficial.
* **Ablation of Evidence Removal:** The discussion on fighting introduced evidence (Section 3.1) presents several methods (blurring, noise). However, the experiments don't explicitly compare the performance of the model with and without these techniques. It’s unclear which evidence removal method contributes most to the results.
* **Saliency Metric Interpretation:** While the new saliency metric is well-motivated, its correlation with human perception isn't explicitly established. Demonstrating a strong correlation with human-annotated saliency maps would further validate its usefulness.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The introduction of a new saliency metric is a valuable contribution.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the method and results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The objective function contains several hyperparameters with limited justification.
* The generalization ability to diverse classifier architectures is not fully explored.
* The ablation studies could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution and promising results. The proposed method addresses a real need for fast and accurate saliency detection, and the experimental evaluation is thorough. While some aspects of the method could be further refined and explored, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly concerning the objective function hyperparameters and the ablation studies. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter selection process, a comparison of different evidence removal techniques, and a more comprehensive ablation study would strengthen the paper significantly.
