- Summary: Proposes a trained "masking model" (U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) that produces saliency maps in one feed-forward pass using SSR and SDR objectives, evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet with a new information-concentration metric.

- Novelty/significance: Amortizes expensive per-image optimization into a single trained explainer, enabling real-time saliency (e.g., video) and a new paradigm of training one network to explain another.

- Strong empirical results: Achieves state-of-the-art ImageNet weakly supervised localization (36.7% localization error), showing masks are useful for downstream tasks.

- High-quality saliency maps: Qualitative results show sharp, well-localized, low-noise masks that outperform gradient-based and iterative methods.

- Well-motivated objective and metric: Combines SSR/SDR with TV and area regularization to avoid artifacts and introduces a new metric for information concentration.

- "Black box" terminology concern: Training requires backprop through the classifier, so the classifier is white-box during training despite claims of black-box explanation.

- Generality and training overhead: A new masking model must be trained per classifier, limiting practicality for comparing or explaining many models.

- Explainer model bias: The masking model may impose dataset priors and not faithfully reflect the classifier's true reasoning; risk of "explaining the explainer."

- Sensitivity to hyperparameters: Several specific training choices (fake-label probability, evidence-removal scheme, multiple λs) suggest potential fragility and reproducibility issues.

- Metric implementation issue: Resizing crops while disregarding aspect ratio may distort inputs and confound the proposed saliency metric; alternatives like padding should be considered.

- Overall assessment: High-quality, impactful, and well-presented work that advances interpretability despite practical limitations that warrant clarification and further analysis.
