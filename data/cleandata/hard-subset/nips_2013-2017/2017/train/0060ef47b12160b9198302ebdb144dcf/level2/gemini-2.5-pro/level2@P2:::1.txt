This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate "masking model" to produce a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This is in contrast to prior methods that iteratively optimize a mask for each specific image, a process that is often slow. The masking model, based on a U-Net architecture, is trained to minimize a well-designed objective function that encourages masks to be small and smooth, while preserving the classifier's score for the selected region (Smallest Sufficient Region) and simultaneously destroying the score for the removed region (Smallest Destroying Region). The authors treat the target classifier as a "black box" during inference, though it must be differentiable for training the masking model. The paper also introduces a new metric for evaluating saliency based on the information concentration in a cropped region. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, where it achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task and produces visually impressive, sharp saliency maps in real-time.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution of this work is reframing saliency detection from a per-instance optimization problem to a learning problem. Training a dedicated model to predict saliency masks is a novel and powerful idea. The main benefit is a dramatic increase in speed, enabling real-time applications like video saliency, which is a significant practical advance over slow iterative methods.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method demonstrates excellent results. It outperforms other weakly supervised methods on the challenging ImageNet object localization task, even approaching the performance of some fully supervised methods. This is a strong validation of the approach.

3.  **High-Quality Saliency Maps:** The qualitative results shown in Figures 1, 5, and 6 are very compelling. The generated masks are sharp, well-localized, and free from the noisy artifacts that often plague gradient-based methods. They appear much more precise than the masks produced by the iterative optimization baseline shown.

4.  **Well-Motivated Objective and New Metric:** The objective function in Equation 4 is thoughtfully constructed, combining principles of both Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and Smallest Destroying Region (SDR) along with a total variation penalty for smoothness. This is a principled way to define what a "good" saliency mask should be. Furthermore, the introduction of a new saliency metric (Equation 3) is a valuable contribution. It moves beyond using localization as a proxy for saliency and attempts to directly measure the quality of the explanation, which is a key challenge in the field of interpretability.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity of "Black Box" Claim:** The paper repeatedly refers to the target classifier as a "black box". While this is true at inference time, training the masking model requires backpropagating gradients through the target classifier. This means the method is applicable to any *differentiable* classifier for which gradients are accessible, not truly any black box system (e.g., one accessed only via an API). The authors should be more precise with this terminology throughout the paper to avoid confusion. For instance, phrasing like "white-box training for black-box inference" might be more accurate.

2.  **Discussion of Training Overhead:** The paper rightly emphasizes the real-time inference speed. However, it omits any discussion of the training cost. A separate, complex masking model (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) must be trained for *each* classifier one wishes to explain. This represents a significant computational and storage overhead. A discussion of this trade-off—heavy offline training for fast online inference—would provide a more balanced view of the method's practicality. Adding details about the training time would be helpful.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The objective function (Equation 4) includes four weighting parameters (λ1 to λ4). The paper provides the values used in the experiments but offers no justification for these choices or analysis of the model's sensitivity to them. Given the complexity of the objective, a brief discussion on how these were tuned or an ablation study on their effects would strengthen the paper's technical depth and reproducibility.

4.  **Details on the New Saliency Metric:** The proposed metric is an interesting idea, but its formulation could be better justified. The information-theoretic motivation for the log-log form feels somewhat ad-hoc. Additionally, the need to resize the cropped region to fit the classifier's input size could be a significant confounder, as resizing can drastically alter the features available to the classifier. Acknowledging and discussing this potential issue more deeply would be beneficial. The choice of the 0.05 threshold for the area also seems arbitrary and should be briefly justified.

5.  **Quantitative Comparison to Concurrent Work:** The paper cites Fong and Vedaldi [2] as a parallel work that uses iterative optimization. While a qualitative comparison is provided in Figure 5, a direct quantitative comparison on the localization task and the new saliency metric is missing. Since the authors claim their method avoids overfitting and produces superior masks compared to iterative approaches, a direct quantitative comparison would provide much stronger evidence for this claim.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and effective method for real-time saliency detection. The core idea of training a predictive model for saliency is a significant contribution to the field of interpretability. The impressive empirical results, both quantitative (localization) and qualitative (mask sharpness), strongly support the authors' claims. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to a lack of detail or nuance in certain areas, rather than fundamental flaws in the approach, and can be addressed in a revision. The paper is well-written, the contributions are clear, and the work is likely to be highly influential. I recommend acceptance.
