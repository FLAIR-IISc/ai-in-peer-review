**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel method for generating image saliency maps for differentiable classifiers. The key idea is to train a deep generative network (a U-Net with a ResNet encoder) to produce a saliency mask for any given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This stands in contrast to prior work, which is either fast but yields low-quality explanations (e.g., gradient-based methods) or produces high-quality maps but requires slow, iterative optimization for each new image. The proposed "masking model" is trained using a composite objective function designed to produce masks that are smooth, small, and identify regions that are both sufficient for classification (Smallest Sufficient Region) and necessary for it (Smallest Destroying Region). The work is evaluated extensively on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, demonstrating that the generated maps lead to state-of-the-art performance on the weakly supervised object localization task. A new metric for evaluating the quality of saliency explanations is also proposed.

**Quality**

The paper is of high quality. The methodology is technically sound, and the motivation for training a generative model to amortize the cost of saliency map creation is compelling. The objective function (Eq. 4) is well-designed, thoughtfully combining four distinct and desirable properties for a saliency mask. The experimental validation is strong, using multiple standard black-box architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10) to demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The reported results, particularly the new state-of-the-art on weakly supervised object localization, provide convincing evidence of the method's superiority over existing techniques. The inclusion of an experiment on CIFAR-10 with a masking model trained from scratch is a good addition, showing that the approach does not strictly depend on a pre-trained encoder.

To further improve the work, the following points could be considered:
-   The objective function in Equation 4 contains four hyperparameters (λ₁ to λ₄) that balance the different terms. The paper presents the final values used but lacks a discussion on how these were chosen or an analysis of the model's sensitivity to them. An ablation study showing the effect of each term and the sensitivity to its weight would strengthen the empirical results.
-   The training process for the masking model depends on backpropagating gradients through the target classifier to compute the loss. The paper describes this classifier as a "black box," which could be misleading. While the trained masking model operates without internal access to the classifier at *inference time*, the training phase requires full, "white-box" access. Clarifying this distinction would improve the paper's precision. It is a significant practical point that a new masking model must be trained from scratch for each new target classifier.

**Clarity**

The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation is established effectively in the introduction, and the concepts of Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and Smallest Destroying Region (SDR) are explained well. The figures are illustrative and support the text effectively, especially Figure 5, which provides a strong qualitative comparison against other methods.

A few minor points could enhance clarity:
-   The proposed saliency metric in Equation 3 is an interesting idea. The information-theoretic intuition is helpful, but the justification for the specific form and the hard threshold at 0.05 could be explained in slightly more detail.
-   Equation 8 presents a non-standard nonlinearity for generating the mask. The paper notes that sigmoid/tanh functions did not perform well. A brief sentence elaborating on *why* this might be (e.g., to avoid gradient saturation or provide more flexibility) would be a valuable piece of information for readers.

**Originality**

The work is highly original. Its main contribution—framing saliency detection as a learning problem and training a network to amortize the expensive optimization process—is a novel and powerful concept in the interpretability space. While it builds on ideas from perturbation-based methods (like the concurrent work of Fong and Vedaldi [2]), it fundamentally changes the approach from a per-instance search problem to a single-shot prediction. This is a significant conceptual leap. The design of the objective function and the introduction of a new saliency metric are also novel contributions. The work is well-situated within existing literature, and its unique advantages are clearly articulated.

**Significance**

The significance of this work is high. It addresses a critical bottleneck in deploying high-quality saliency methods: computational cost. By enabling the generation of detailed saliency maps in real-time (over 100 FPS), this method opens the door to new applications that were previously infeasible, such as real-time video saliency or interactive model-debugging tools. The demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on a practical downstream task (weakly supervised localization) shows that the benefits are not purely academic. The ideas presented here are likely to be influential and could inspire further research into learned, amortized approaches for other explanation and model interpretation tasks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Presents a highly novel and impactful approach that reframes saliency generation as a learning problem, enabling real-time performance.
2.  Achieves state-of-the-art results on the challenging task of weakly supervised object localization on ImageNet.
3.  The proposed method is general and can be applied to any differentiable classifier.
4.  The paper is technically solid, well-written, and supported by extensive and convincing experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The term "black box" is used imprecisely, as the training method requires white-box (gradient) access to the target classifier.
2.  The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis for the hyperparameters in the objective function.

In conclusion, this is a strong submission with significant contributions in originality, quality, and potential impact. The weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to clarity, which can be addressed in a final version. The work represents a clear and important advancement for the field of machine learning interpretability.
