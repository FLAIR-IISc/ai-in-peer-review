## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method based on training a masking model to predict salient regions for a given classifier. The core idea is to move away from iterative methods, which are computationally expensive, and instead learn a direct mapping from image to saliency map. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the “smallest sufficient region” concept and demonstrate improved performance on ImageNet object localization compared to other weakly supervised approaches.

The paper addresses a relevant problem in the interpretability of deep learning models. The motivation for fast saliency detection is well-articulated, and the proposed approach is a logical step towards real-time applications like video saliency. The introduction effectively highlights the limitations of current classifiers and the need for methods to understand *why* they make certain predictions. The proposed objective function, combining smoothness, region size, and classification score preservation, appears well-reasoned and the use of a U-Net architecture is appropriate. 

A key strength is the empirical evaluation. The authors test their method on both CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, demonstrating generalization across datasets. The comparison to existing methods, including iterative approaches and other weakly supervised techniques, provides a solid benchmark. The new saliency metric is a valuable contribution, and the results suggest it correlates well with human interpretability. The ablation study on CIFAR-10, showing the model can be trained from scratch, is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the architecture is described, the details of hyperparameter tuning and the specific implementation choices (e.g., the choice of blur for evidence removal) could be more thoroughly explained for reproducibility. The discussion of adversarial artifacts, while acknowledged, feels somewhat superficial. A more in-depth analysis of the types of artifacts generated and their impact on the saliency maps would be beneficial. Furthermore, the connection between the proposed saliency metric and existing saliency measures could be further explored. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to fast saliency detection.
*   Strong empirical results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.
*   Introduction of a new, informative saliency metric.
*   Potential for real-time applications.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited detail regarding implementation and hyperparameter tuning.
*   Superficial discussion of adversarial artifacts.
*   Lack of deeper connection to existing saliency measures.

**Overall:** This is a solid contribution with a clear and well-motivated approach. Despite some minor weaknesses, the novelty, empirical results, and potential impact of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above regarding implementation details and the discussion of adversarial artifacts.
