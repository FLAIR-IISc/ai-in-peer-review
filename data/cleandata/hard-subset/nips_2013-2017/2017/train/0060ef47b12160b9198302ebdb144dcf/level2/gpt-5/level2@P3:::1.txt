Summary
The paper proposes a fast, model-conditioned saliency detection approach for differentiable image classifiers. Instead of per-image iterative optimization, a trainable masking network (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) predicts a saliency mask in a single forward pass. The mask is trained to satisfy both “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) and “smallest destroying region” (SDR) objectives, while discouraging adversarial artifacts via total variation regularization, low-resolution upsampling, and randomized evidence-removal schemes (blurred or noisy/color alternatives). A class-embedding “feature filter” encourages conditioning on the queried class; training also uses “fake” class selectors to prevent class-agnostic masks. The work introduces a new saliency metric that summarizes how concentrated and sufficient the evidence is by cropping to the tight bounding rectangle of the mask and measuring the class probability vs. crop area. Experiments on ImageNet (with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 black boxes) show improved weakly supervised localization error over prior techniques and better scores on the new metric; qualitative results on CIFAR-10 show plausible masks without pretraining.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned against backpropagation-based saliency (e.g., Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), and optimization-based perturbation approaches (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi’s Meaningful Perturbations; Cao et al.’s activation-preservation). The novelty lies in learning a masking model that amortizes per-image optimization and jointly targets SSR and SDR, along with practical regularization to reduce artifact-driven explanations. Compared to iterative meaningful perturbations (Fong & Vedaldi, arXiv:1704.03296), this work trades iteration for learning, yielding real-time performance and sharper masks.

Quality
- Methodology: The objective combines smoothness, sparsity, SSR-preservation, and SDR-removal; the randomized alternative-image A and TV penalty are reasonable defenses against mask-induced artifacts. The class embedding with gating at the deepest feature scale is a sensible conditioning mechanism. Training with fake class selectors addresses a common pitfall in weakly supervised attention.
- Evaluation: Weakly supervised localization on ImageNet follows established protocols and compares to reasonable baselines. The proposed metric, based on cropping and classification confidence, is clearly motivated by SSR. However, several evaluation aspects would benefit from clarification:
  - The saliency metric formula reads as a product of logs; based on the text, a difference (log area minus log probability) may have been intended. Disambiguation and sensitivity analysis would improve interpretability.
  - Because the masking model is trained against a specific classifier, evaluating the metric using that same classifier risks circularity. Cross-model evaluation (e.g., apply masks learned for classifier A but scored by classifier B) would strengthen claims that masks capture image evidence rather than model idiosyncrasies.
  - CIFAR-10 results are qualitative only; reporting the proposed metric and a localization proxy (where feasible) would improve completeness.
- Reproducibility: The paper provides many details (architecture, training tricks, loss weights) but lacks some training hyperparameters (optimizer, learning rates, batch size) and does not mention code release.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written with clear motivation and figures that illustrate SSR/SDR and mask behavior. Areas to clarify:
- “Black box” vs. “differentiable classifier”: training appears to require backpropagation through the classifier. It would be clearer to state that the approach is black-box at inference but requires gradient access during training (i.e., gray-box).
- Exact definition of the saliency metric and its intended directionality (lower is better), with rationale for the 0.05 area floor and analysis of aspect-ratio distortion from resizing crops.
- Precise evidence-removal distributions (noise parameters), and ablations for A choices and the loss weights (λ1–λ4).
- Minor reference formatting issues (e.g., Simonyan et al. “maps. CoRR”) and equation formatting.

Originality
Learning an amortized, class-conditioned masking model that jointly enforces SSR and SDR appears novel relative to contemporaneous work. The defensive training against introduced evidence, fake-class conditioning, and the specific U-Net-with-gated-deep-feature design are interesting contributions. The new metric is a useful, if simple, addition to the evaluation toolkit.

Significance
The approach achieves real-time saliency with improved weakly supervised localization and better scores on the proposed metric, which is valuable for interactive and video settings. If corroborated with stronger robustness and transfer evaluations, the method could become a practical alternative to iterative perturbation saliency. The idea of amortizing saliency optimization via a learned explainer network is broadly applicable.

Strengths
- Real-time saliency via a learned masking model; sharp, interpretable masks.
- Joint SSR/SDR objective with practical countermeasures against adversarial artifacts.
- Sensible conditioning on class queries; training tricks to avoid collapse to dominant object.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization on ImageNet; improved performance over iterative baselines.
- Simple, intuitive saliency metric aligned with SSR.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Metric clarity and validation:
  - Clarify the exact formula and sign; report metric sensitivity to area floor and resizing distortions.
  - Evaluate cross-model transfer of masks and metric to reduce circularity concerns.
- Robustness and ablations:
  - Provide ablations on λ1–λ4, alternative-image schemes (blur vs. noise), TV strength, and the SSR term’s contribution relative to SDR-only objectives.
  - Quantitative CIFAR-10 evaluation beyond qualitative figures.
- “Black-box” claim:
  - State explicitly the training-time requirement for gradients and discuss applicability to truly non-differentiable or inaccessible models.
- Comparison fairness:
  - Ensure that competing methods use comparable evidence-removal baselines and regularization; report runtime and quality trade-offs under matched settings.
- Reproducibility:
  - Add optimizer, LR schedule, batch size, training time, and implementation details; consider releasing code.

Pro-accept
- Practical and fast saliency with improved quantitative performance.
- Clear methodological contribution with plausible design choices.
- Potential impact on interactive and video explanation scenarios.

Con-accept
- Some evaluation choices risk circularity; limited robustness/transfer analysis.
- Metric definition needs clarification; minor presentation issues.
- Limited quantitative results on CIFAR-10 and lack of thorough ablations.

Overall
A solid, timely contribution that advances saliency from iterative optimization to real-time, model-conditioned masks with competitive empirical performance. With clarifications to the metric, strengthened transfer/ablation studies, and improved presentation, this work would make a valuable addition to NIPS.
