This paper presents a novel method for generating real-time saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier. The core contribution is to train a separate generative model, specifically a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder, to produce a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This approach contrasts with prior work, which typically falls into two categories: fast but lower-quality gradient-based methods, or high-quality but computationally expensive iterative optimization methods. The authors frame their work as a practical solution that achieves both high speed and high-quality results. The paper also introduces a comprehensive objective function for training the masking model and a new saliency metric to evaluate the interpretability of the resulting maps.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The primary contribution—amortizing the cost of saliency generation by training a dedicated model—is highly original and significant. It effectively addresses a major bottleneck for the practical application of perturbation-based explanation methods. The claims are strongly supported by extensive experiments on both ImageNet and CIFAR-10. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task, outperforming previous methods and demonstrating its effectiveness. The paper is very well-written and clear, with a thoughtful discussion of potential pitfalls like "introduced evidence" from masking.

The main weakness lies in the practical overhead of the approach. It requires training and storing a separate, complex masking model for each black-box classifier one wishes to explain. While this is a one-time cost, it could be prohibitive in scenarios with many target models. The authors could strengthen the paper by discussing the potential for a single masking model to generalize across multiple, similar black-box architectures. Additionally, while the new saliency metric is interesting, it is biased towards classifiers that are robust to aspect ratio distortion, a limitation that should be explicitly acknowledged.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and impactful approach for real-time saliency map generation.
*   State-of-the-art empirical results on a standard benchmark (weakly supervised localization).
*   Thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets and target models.
*   Well-written, clear, and demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem space.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The approach has a significant computational and storage overhead (one masking model per target classifier).
*   The proposed new metric has unaddressed biases.

Overall, the novelty of the method and the strength of the empirical results significantly outweigh the weaknesses. This work introduces a valuable new direction for model interpretability and is a strong candidate for acceptance.
