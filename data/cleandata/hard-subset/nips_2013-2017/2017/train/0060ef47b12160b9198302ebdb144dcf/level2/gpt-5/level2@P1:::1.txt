Summary
This paper proposes a trainable masking network that produces class-conditional saliency maps for a given differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass. The key idea is to learn a masking model with a U-Net architecture (ResNet-50 encoder, class-conditional gating) that optimizes a loss designed to (i) produce smooth, compact masks, (ii) preserve the classifier’s confidence when keeping only the salient region (SSR), and (iii) suppress the classifier’s confidence when the salient region is removed (SDR). To reduce artifacts and mitigate “introduced evidence,” the masking operation blends the original image with either a blurred version or a randomized color/noise background, and total variation regularization is used for smoothness. The authors also introduce a saliency metric based on class probability from the tightest crop containing the salient region, normalized by crop area (intended to operationalize SSR). The method is evaluated on ImageNet (weakly supervised localization) and CIFAR-10; it reportedly outperforms other weakly supervised methods and is substantially faster than iterative perturbation-based methods.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned among gradient/backprop-based explanations (Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), iterative deletion/preservation methods (e.g., Zhou et al.; CAM-style works), and the optimization-based perturbation approach of Fong & Vedaldi (2017), to which it is closest. The main novelty versus Fong & Vedaldi is to learn a reusable saliency predictor (single pass at test time) rather than optimizing a mask per image; the loss also targets both SSR and SDR simultaneously. The paper could additionally discuss class activation mapping variants (CAM, and especially Grad-CAM, Selvaraju et al., 2017) which were contemporaneous and directly relevant baselines for localization-quality saliency.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Training a dedicated saliency predictor against a fixed classifier yields real-time explanations with sharper masks than iterative optimization. This is a useful and novel design choice that others can build on.
- Thoughtful treatment of artifacts: The analysis of “introduced evidence” and the use of randomized alternative images plus TV regularization are well-motivated and practically important.
- Architecture and training: The U-Net with class embedding gating is simple and effective; the “fake class” strategy to force attention to the class selector is clever and addresses a real failure mode.
- Metric: The proposed SSR-inspired crop-probability/area metric provides a complementary lens to standard localization metrics.
- Empirical results: Qualitative maps look sharp and focused. Reported weakly supervised localization performance is competitive with (and sometimes better than) prior weakly supervised approaches. The CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates the approach does not require a pre-trained encoder.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Objective signs/notation: In Eq. (4), the third term is written as +λ3 log fc(Φ(X, M)), yet the text states it should encourage recognition from the preserved region; minimizing the loss would then push fc down, not up. This appears to be a sign error (likely −λ3 log fc). Please clarify. Similarly, in Eq. (3) for the saliency metric, “s(a,p) = log(ã) log(p)” is ambiguous (ratio or product?); the discussion suggests a ratio log(ã)/log(p). Precise definitions are important for reproducibility and interpretation.
- “Black box” characterization: Training the masking model requires backpropagation through the classifier; thus the method is not black-box in the strict sense (query-only). Clarify the assumptions (fixed, differentiable, gradient-accessible classifier during training; no access required at test time).
- Evaluation details: The paper reports improvements but omits some necessary specifics.
  - Localization: Thresholds used to binarize saliency for your method and for baselines (and whether class-agnostic thresholds are used across methods). Sensitivity to threshold choice.
  - Metric stability: Behavior of the proposed metric across different crop aspect ratios and resizing; confidence intervals or variance across images/classes; ablation on the area floor (0.05).
  - Ablations: Effects of λ1–λ4, exponent 4, and the two choices of the alternative image A; TV weight vs. mask sharpness vs. artifacts; with/without the fake-class training trick.
- Generalization and scope: A separate masking model is trained per classifier; it would be useful to show (i) cross-classifier generalization (train on one classifier, test explanations on another), and/or (ii) ability to adapt to a new classifier with limited additional training.
- Robustness: Since the method explicitly aims to avoid adversarial artifacts, a quantitative artifact analysis would help (e.g., measure spectral energy, adversarial transfer, or randomized smoothing tests). Also, reporting failure cases (e.g., small objects, heavy context dependence) would be informative.
- Baselines: Including Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ and CAM-style methods would strengthen comparisons, given their strong performance on weakly supervised localization.
- Claims on video: The “real-time” claim is compelling; however, no video saliency experiments are shown. Even a small demonstration or latency analysis under streaming conditions would substantiate the claim.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a well-motivated loss, reasonable architectural choices, and solid training heuristics. The main technical concerns are (i) clarity and correctness of equations (objective sign; metric definition), and (ii) missing ablations and statistical reporting that would bolster the empirical claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Figures are informative. Please fix the noted notational/sign ambiguities, specify all normalization/thresholding steps for evaluation, and provide more implementation details (learning rates, training time, data splits). A short algorithm box describing the training loop would aid reproducibility.

Originality
High. Training a separate, reusable saliency predictor with a combined SSR/SDR objective and explicit anti-artifact design is a substantial step beyond per-image optimization and typical backprop-based explanations.

Significance
High practical impact due to real-time saliency for large-scale classifiers and potential for video applications. The proposed metric and the artifact-aware masking policy are likely to influence subsequent work. The need to train per classifier is a limitation but acceptable for many deployments.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, practical approach enabling real-time, high-quality saliency maps.
- Careful treatment of introduced evidence and training pathologies.
- Competitive or superior weakly supervised localization results.
- Useful new evaluation metric and solid qualitative results.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguities/errors in key equations; missing ablations and statistical rigor.
- “Black box” terminology mismatch with gradient requirements.
- Incomplete baseline coverage (e.g., Grad-CAM).
- Limited analysis of robustness and generalization across classifiers.

Questions for authors
1) Please clarify the sign in Eq. (4) and the exact definition of the metric in Eq. (3).  
2) What thresholds are used for binarization across methods? How sensitive are results to this choice?  
3) Can you report ablations on λ’s, A choices, and the exponent in the last term?  
4) How much training time/data are needed per classifier? Does a model trained on ResNet explain GoogLeNet without retraining?  
5) Can you include Grad-CAM results and/or discuss differences?  
6) Any quantitative analysis of artifact suppression (e.g., frequency-domain measures)?

Overall recommendation
Accept (clear contribution, strong empirical promise, and practical relevance), contingent on fixing the equation issues and strengthening the evaluation with the suggested ablations and additional baselines.

Confidence
High for the assessment of novelty and positioning; medium for quantitative claims due to missing details in the provided text.
