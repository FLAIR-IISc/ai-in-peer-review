

This paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The authors train a masking model to predict saliency maps for any input image in a single forward pass, rather than using iterative methods that require hundreds of iterations. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region concept and demonstrate their approach on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet datasets.

**Strengths:**
1. The method is significantly faster than existing iterative approaches, achieving 100 saliency masks per second, which is suitable for real-time applications like video saliency.
2. The proposed saliency metric (s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p)) provides a novel way to evaluate saliency maps by focusing on the smallest sufficient region rather than just localization accuracy.
3. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, with a 36.7% error rate (compared to 34.3% for a fully supervised approach).
4. The approach generalizes well across different black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and even works on CIFAR-10 without requiring pre-trained models.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper does not adequately differentiate its approach from the parallel work by Fong & Vedaldi (2017), which also proposes a model-agnostic saliency method using iterative optimization in image space. The authors mention [2] but fail to clearly articulate how their masking model approach differs methodologically and in performance. This is a critical issue for originality assessment.

2. The justification for the new saliency metric is insufficient. While the metric is introduced as "highly tuned" for interpretability, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why it's superior to existing metrics beyond showing better numbers. The theoretical foundation for this metric is weak, and the paper doesn't compare how this metric correlates with human judgments of saliency.

3. The paper claims the method "generalises well to unseen images" but provides limited evidence beyond the ImageNet and CIFAR-10 results. More analysis of generalization across different image classes, object types, and classifier architectures would strengthen this claim.

4. The training process details are insufficient for reproducibility. The paper mentions using a 30% probability of fake class labels but doesn't explain how this was determined or why it's optimal. The class embedding training procedure is also under-described.

5. The paper misses several important comparisons with recent saliency methods. For example, it doesn't compare with Grad-CAM (which is widely used) or other fast saliency methods like Guided Backpropagation. This limits the significance of the claimed performance improvements.

**Constructive Suggestions:**
1. Clearly differentiate the approach from Fong & Vedaldi [2] in both methodology and results. Provide a table comparing the two approaches side-by-side, including computational efficiency, saliency quality, and required resources.

2. Strengthen the justification for the new metric by including human evaluation studies. Show how the metric correlates with human judgments of saliency quality, which would provide much stronger evidence for its superiority.

3. Include more detailed ablation studies on the impact of different components of the masking model architecture and the objective function.

4. Provide more thorough analysis of the model's limitations, particularly for images with multiple objects or ambiguous classes.

5. Add a comparison with Grad-CAM and other popular saliency methods to better establish the relative performance gains.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper addresses an important problem (real-time saliency detection) and makes a technically sound contribution with significant speed improvements over existing methods. The new saliency metric is a valuable addition to the field. However, the failure to adequately differentiate from parallel work and the insufficient justification for the new metric significantly impact the paper's originality and significance. While the results are promising, the paper would benefit from more rigorous comparisons with existing methods and a clearer articulation of its unique contributions.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the lack of differentiation from Fong & Vedaldi [2] is a major concern. If the authors can provide a clear comparison and justification for how their approach differs and improves upon the parallel work, this would substantially strengthen the paper. As it stands, the paper's contribution is somewhat diluted by the overlap with recent work. The speed improvements are valuable, but the significance is limited by the lack of clear differentiation from the most relevant prior work. I recommend rejection due to insufficient differentiation from existing work, but with the understanding that this could be addressed in a revision.
