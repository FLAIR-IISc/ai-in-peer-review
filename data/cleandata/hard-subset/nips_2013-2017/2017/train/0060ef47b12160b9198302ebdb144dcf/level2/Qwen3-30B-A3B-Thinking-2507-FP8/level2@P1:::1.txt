

This paper presents a novel, fast saliency detection method for differentiable image classifiers, trained to generate high-quality saliency masks in a single forward pass. The authors address a key limitation of existing methods (e.g., Zhou et al. 2014, Fong & Vedaldi 2017), which rely on iterative optimization and are too slow for real-time use. Their masking model, built on a U-Net architecture with ResNet-50 features, produces sharp, artifact-free saliency maps while introducing a new metric (s(a,p)) that better captures interpretability than standard localization accuracy. The approach is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, showing strong results: the ResNet-50-trained model achieves 36.7% localization error (vs. VGG’s 34.3% in weakly supervised settings), and the new metric demonstrates that their saliency maps approach ground-truth interpretability. The work aligns with NIPS trends in model interpretability (e.g., Ribeiro et al. 2016, Zhou et al. 2014) but offers a significant speed advantage over prior iterative methods.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical soundness and novelty:** The masking model framework is well-motivated and effectively solves the real-time saliency problem. The artifact-resistant masking strategy (Eq. 2) and TV penalty are practical solutions to a critical issue in saliency literature. The new metric (s(a,p)) is a thoughtful contribution that better aligns with the SSR/SDR definitions, and Table 3 convincingly shows their method outperforms baselines (e.g., 0.62 vs. 0.91 for "Max Box").  
2. **Experimental rigor:** The CIFAR-10 ablation (Section 5.3) proves the method generalizes beyond high-resolution images without pretraining, addressing a potential scalability concern. The ImageNet results are comprehensive, with ablation studies on classifier choice (AlexNet vs. ResNet) and thorough comparison to weakly supervised baselines (Table 2).  
3. **Practical significance:** The 100+ FPS speed enables real-time applications (e.g., video saliency for autonomous systems), a clear advancement over iterative methods. The work directly addresses a gap in the field: "real-time explainability" for deployed classifiers.  

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**  
1. **"Black box" claim needs refinement:** The paper states the method works for "any differentiable classifier," but the masking model requires access to the classifier’s gradients during training (e.g., Eq. 7 uses class embeddings). Clarify that it is *not* truly black-box (like Fong & Vedaldi 2017), but *model-agnostic* for differentiable classifiers. This nuance is important for readers assessing applicability.  
2. **Metric limitations:** The s(a,p) metric assumes classifier scale/aspect-ratio invariance (Section 3.2). For non-invariant classifiers (e.g., those trained on fixed-size crops), the metric may not generalize well. The authors should discuss this limitation and validate on a diverse set of classifiers (e.g., MobileNet).  
3. **Comparison to recent work:** The related work cites Fong & Vedaldi (2017) but omits direct comparison to Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al. 2017), a dominant gradient-based method. Adding a brief comparison (e.g., "Our method achieves 36.7% error vs. Grad-CAM’s 42.1% on ImageNet localization") would strengthen the significance claim.  
4. **Clarity improvements:** Section 3.1’s discussion of adversarial artifacts could be more concise. The "fighting introduced evidence" section (3.1) is technically sound but slightly redundant with Eq. 2. Streamlining this would improve readability.  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel speed/accuracy trade-off, strong metric innovation, real-world applicability, thorough experiments.  
*Con:* Overstated "black box" claim, limited metric validation scope, missing comparison to Grad-CAM.  

**Overall:** This is a strong contribution to the growing literature on model interpretability. The method is technically sound, well-evaluated, and addresses a meaningful practical problem. The minor weaknesses (primarily clarifications needed in the "black box" framing and metric scope) are easily addressable in revision. The work advances the state of the art in saliency detection by enabling real-time analysis without sacrificing quality, making it highly relevant to NIPS audiences focused on practical AI systems. I recommend acceptance.
