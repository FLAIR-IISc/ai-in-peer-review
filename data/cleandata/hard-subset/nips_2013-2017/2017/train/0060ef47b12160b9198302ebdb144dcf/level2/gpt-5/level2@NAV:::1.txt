This paper proposes a trainable masking network that produces per-class saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass. The key claims are: (1) a fast, model-agnostic (w.r.t. architectures) method that learns to generate sharp, artifact-resistant saliency masks suitable for real-time use; (2) a unified objective combining “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) and “smallest destroying region” (SDR) with TV and area penalties to discourage adversarial artifacts; (3) a new saliency metric based on classification confidence vs. crop area; and (4) improved weakly supervised localization on ImageNet, with additional results on CIFAR-10. Relative to prior NIPS/external work, it moves beyond gradient-based methods (Simonyan et al., GuidedBP, Excitation Backprop) and iterative perturbation approaches (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi’s Meaningful Perturbations, CAM-like methods) by amortizing the optimization into a U-Net-style predictor conditioned on class embeddings.

Quality: The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The training tricks (randomized evidence removal A, TV/area regularization, fake-label conditioning) are thoughtful and address common pitfalls (mask artifacts, class-agnostic behavior). Empirical support includes qualitative masks across AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet, ImageNet localization error competitive with fully supervised VGG and better than weakly supervised baselines, and a CIFAR-10 study from scratch. However, quantitative evidence lacks error bars, statistical tests, and ablations (loss term weights, removal schemes, TV strength). The “real-time” claim would benefit from precise hardware/runtime profiling.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written with many implementation details (architecture, hyperparameters). The black-box/differentiable phrasing is slightly inconsistent: training requires backprop through the classifier, so the approach is not black-box in the strict sense of score-only access.

Originality: Training a saliency predictor to amortize SDR/SSR optimization is novel and practically impactful. The metric s(a,p) is interesting but somewhat ad hoc; its dependence on aspect-ratio-insensitive resizing and on the classifier’s scale invariance should be discussed more deeply.

Significance: The approach is useful for interactive/real-time scenarios and weakly supervised localization, and could catalyze video saliency. Adoption would be strengthened by code release and broader comparisons (e.g., Grad-CAM).

Constructive suggestions:
- Provide ablations (loss components, A choices, mask resolution, TV strength) and report variance across seeds.
- Calibrate the new metric against human judgments; compare with deletion/insertion-style faithfulness curves.
- Report precise throughput/latency on standard hardware; analyze failure cases.
- Evaluate cross-class and cross-model generalization (single masking model across multiple classifiers).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Practical, fast, and accurate saliency with clear architectural and training insights.
- Competitive to strong baselines; compelling qualitative results.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited quantitative rigor (no ablations/statistics), metric justification incomplete.
- “Black-box” claim overstated; requires gradients to the classifier.
