This paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method for differentiable image classifiers. The core idea is to train a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, rather than relying on iterative methods. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) concept and demonstrate strong performance on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, outperforming other weakly supervised methods on object localization.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial aspect of understanding and trusting deep learning models – interpretability. The problem of slow, iterative saliency methods is clearly articulated. The proposed approach is a significant step towards real-time saliency detection, opening up possibilities for applications in video analysis and other dynamic scenarios. The introduction of a new saliency metric, grounded in the SSR idea, is a valuable contribution, and the experimental results are compelling, particularly the performance on ImageNet localization. The extensive related work section demonstrates awareness of the existing literature, referencing key papers in saliency detection and interpretability. The inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., CIFAR-10 experiments with/without pre-training) strengthens the conclusions.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the potential for introducing artifacts during mask application and attempt to mitigate this with techniques like blurring and noise addition, the discussion could be more in-depth. A more rigorous analysis of the types of artifacts generated and their impact on the saliency maps would be beneficial. The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, primarily focuses on localization accuracy. Exploring the correlation between the proposed saliency maps and human eye-fixation data could provide further validation and insight into the quality of the explanations.  The notation is sometimes a little dense, and equation (8) in particular feels somewhat ad-hoc and could use more justification. Furthermore, the term "evidence" is used frequently but isn’t always clearly defined. What constitutes “evidence” in the context of a deep neural network and how it’s different from features is not fully elaborated. While the authors state they avoid overfitting via the masking model, a more explicit discussion of regularization strategies and their impact would be useful. Finally, the supplementary material is mentioned but not directly included, limiting the ability to thoroughly assess the additional examples.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of learning a saliency map prediction model is a significant departure from iterative methods.
*   **Speed:** The single-pass nature of the model enables real-time saliency detection.
*   **Performance:** Strong results on ImageNet localization demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   **New Metric:** The SSR-based saliency metric provides a valuable tool for evaluating saliency maps.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** Demonstrates a strong understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Artifact Analysis:**  Limited discussion of artifact generation and its impact.
*   **Evaluation Focus:** Heavily focused on localization, lacking human-centric evaluation (e.g. eye tracking).
*   **Notational Clarity**: Some equations and terms (e.g. 'evidence') require further explanation.
*   **Regularization:** Insufficient discussion on regularization techniques used in the masking model.
*   **Missing Supplementary Material:** Limits full assessment of results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem (interpretability of deep learning) with a novel and effective solution.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The proposed approach has the potential to enable new applications in real-time systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The discussion of artifacts and potential biases could be more in-depth.
*   The evaluation could be broadened to include more human-centric metrics.
*   The paper’s clarity suffers from some dense notation and undefined terms.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of interpretability. While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty, speed, and performance of the proposed method are significant.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above (specifically, expanding on the artifact analysis and clarifying the notation). I encourage the authors to include the supplementary material with the final submission.
