- Proposes a trainable, model-conditioned U-Net masker that predicts saliency in one forward pass instead of per-image optimization.  
- Trains masks to satisfy both smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR) objectives with TV regularization and randomized evidence-removal to avoid artifacts.  
- Uses a class-embedding feature filter and fake-class selectors to condition masks on queried classes and avoid class-agnostic collapse.  
- Introduces a new saliency metric measuring class probability vs. crop area after tight bounding-rectangle cropping of the mask.  
- Experiments on ImageNet with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 show improved weakly supervised localization and better scores on the new metric.  
- Qualitative CIFAR-10 results show plausible masks without pretraining, but quantitative CIFAR-10 evaluation is missing.  
- Novelty lies in amortizing per-image optimization into a learned explainer and jointly optimizing SSR and SDR with practical regularization.  
- Methodology combines smoothness, sparsity, SSR preservation, and SDR removal; randomized A and TV penalty are reasonable defenses against artifact-driven explanations.  
- Evaluation follows established localization protocols but the saliency metric formula is ambiguous and needs disambiguation and sensitivity analysis.  
- Risk of circularity: training masks against a specific classifier and evaluating with the same classifier could conflate mask quality with model idiosyncrasies; cross-model evaluation is recommended.  
- Reproducibility: many architectural/training details provided, but key hyperparameters (optimizer, learning rates, batch size) and code release are missing.  
- Clarity issues: clarify “black-box” vs. differentiable classifier (requires gradients during training), metric directionality and area floor rationale, and evidence-removal distributions.  
- Suggests ablations on loss weights (λ1–λ4), alternative-image schemes, TV strength, and SSR vs. SDR contributions to strengthen claims.  
- Comparison fairness: ensure competing methods use comparable evidence-removal and report runtime/quality trade-offs under matched settings.  
- Significance: enables real-time saliency with potential for interactive/video use and could be a practical alternative to iterative perturbation methods.  
- Strengths: real-time, sharp interpretable masks; joint SSR/SDR objective; sensible class conditioning; competitive ImageNet localization; intuitive metric.  
- Weaknesses: metric ambiguity and potential circularity, limited quantitative CIFAR-10 results, incomplete ablations, unclear training-time gradient requirements, and missing reproducibility details.  
- Recommendation: pro-accept for practical, timely contribution but requests clarifications, stronger transfer/ablation studies, and improved presentation; overall a solid paper with needed refinements.
