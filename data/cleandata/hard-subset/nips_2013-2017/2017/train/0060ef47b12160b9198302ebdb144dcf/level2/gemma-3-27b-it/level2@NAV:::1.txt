## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a novel approach to generating image saliency maps for differentiable classifiers, moving away from iterative methods towards a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea—training a masking model to directly predict salient regions—is well-motivated by the limitations of existing techniques, namely their computational expense. The authors correctly identify the need for speed in applications like video-saliency and provide a compelling argument for a model-based approach.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear problem definition, thoughtful design of the masking model (utilizing a U-Net architecture), and the introduction of a new saliency metric grounded in the concept of Smallest Sufficient/Destroying Regions (SSR/SDR). The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet are promising, demonstrating competitive performance on object localization compared to other weakly supervised methods. The ablation study and discussion of adversarial artifacts (and mitigation strategies) add depth to the analysis. The inclusion of a new metric is also a valuable contribution, offering a more nuanced evaluation than standard localization accuracy. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the explanation of the masking model is fairly detailed, the rationale behind certain architectural choices (e.g., the two-channel output and specific nonlinearity) isn't fully explored. The connection between the chosen regularization terms (TV penalty, averaging) and the prevention of adversarial artifacts could be more clearly articulated. Furthermore, the impact of different black-box classifiers on the masking model’s performance is discussed, but a deeper analysis of *why* certain classifiers lead to better masks would be beneficial. The paper also assumes differentiability of the classifier, a limitation that should be more explicitly stated and discussed concerning real-world scenarios. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and efficient approach to saliency detection.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating competitive performance.
*   Introduction of a new, theoretically grounded saliency metric.
*   Clear writing and well-defined problem statement.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Justification for some architectural choices is lacking.
*   Limited discussion of the differentiability assumption.
*   Analysis of performance differences based on the underlying classifier could be improved.

**Overall:**

Despite these minor shortcomings, this is a solid contribution to the field of explainable AI. The proposed method offers a significant improvement in speed and efficiency compared to existing approaches. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above. The authors have presented a valuable tool with the potential for broad impact, particularly in real-time applications.
