**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps to explain the predictions of image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate neural network, termed a "masking model," to produce a saliency mask for a given image and class in a single feed-forward pass. This approach stands in contrast to existing methods that are either fast but produce low-quality, noisy maps (e.g., gradient-based methods) or produce high-quality maps but are computationally expensive due to iterative optimization for each new image (e.g., perturbation-based methods). The masking model, which uses a U-Net architecture with a ResNet-50 encoder, is trained to optimize a carefully designed objective function that encourages the resulting mask to be small and smooth, while preserving the classifier's score on the salient region (a Smallest Sufficient Region or SSR objective) and diminishing the score when that region is removed (a Smallest Destroying Region or SDR objective). The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, achieving state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task. They also introduce a new metric for evaluating saliency based on the information concentration within a cropped region.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution—amortizing the expensive optimization process of saliency map generation into a one-time training phase of a separate model—is a significant and novel idea. This enables real-time saliency detection, which opens up new applications like video analysis, a clear advantage over the slow iterative methods that are its main competitors in terms of quality. The concept of training one network to explain another is powerful and could inspire further research in this direction.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides compelling quantitative evidence of its method's effectiveness. Achieving a 36.7% localization error on the ImageNet weakly supervised localization task is a state-of-the-art result, outperforming previous methods by a notable margin. This demonstrates that the high-quality masks are not just visually appealing but also concretely useful for downstream tasks.

3.  **High-Quality Saliency Maps:** The qualitative results presented in the figures (e.g., Figure 5) are very impressive. The masks are sharp, well-localized, and free of the noise and artifacts common in gradient-based methods. They appear much more precise and interpretable than those from the iterative optimization method shown for comparison.

4.  **Well-Motivated Objective and New Metric:** The proposed objective function (Eq. 4) is thoughtfully constructed, combining principles of both SSR and SDR along with crucial regularization (Total Variation and area penalty) to prevent adversarial artifacts. Furthermore, the introduction of a new saliency metric (Eq. 3) is a valuable contribution, as it attempts to measure the "information concentration" of a saliency region, moving beyond the standard proxy of localization accuracy.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **"Black Box" Terminology:** The paper repeatedly refers to the classifier being explained as a "black box." However, the training process for the masking model requires backpropagating gradients from the classifier's output through the masking operation. This implies full access to the classifier's architecture and weights, making it a "white box" during the training phase. While the classifier is treated as a black box during *inference* time of the trained masking model, the initial claim is potentially misleading. Could the authors clarify this distinction?

2.  **Generality and Training Overhead:** A significant limitation is that a new, computationally expensive masking model must be trained from scratch for *each* black-box classifier one wishes to explain. This undermines the method's practicality in scenarios where one might want to compare explanations across many different models or explain a newly developed model. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this overhead and the method's applicability in such contexts.

3.  **Potential for Explainer Model Bias:** The explanation itself is generated by a complex model (a ResNet-based U-Net). This introduces a new layer of abstraction and potential for bias. How can we be certain that the masking model has truly learned the "behavior" of the black-box classifier, rather than learning its own priors about what constitutes a salient object from the ImageNet training data? For instance, if the masking model is trained on a dataset where "boats" are always in "water," would it be able to produce a mask for just a boat if the black-box classifier was shown a picture of a boat in a museum? A discussion on this meta-problem of "explaining the explainer" would be valuable.

4.  **Sensitivity to Training Hyperparameters:** The training process involves several specific choices: a 30% probability of using a "fake" label, a randomized scheme for evidence removal (blur vs. noise), and a set of four λ hyperparameters for the loss function. This suggests that training may be complex and sensitive to these settings. Some analysis of this sensitivity would improve the paper's contribution to reproducibility and practical adoption.

5.  **New Saliency Metric Details:** The proposed metric resizes the cropped salient region to the classifier's required input size, "disregarding aspect ratio." This heavy distortion could significantly impact a classifier's prediction, potentially confounding the metric. It would be useful to know how robust the metric is to this choice versus, for example, padding the crop to maintain the aspect ratio.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and effective method for generating saliency maps. The core idea of using a trained network for amortized inference of saliency is clever and impactful, and the strong performance on the challenging ImageNet localization benchmark provides solid validation. The work is well-presented and the results are compelling. The weaknesses are primarily related to practical limitations and points that could be clarified or discussed further, but they do not undermine the core contribution. This work is a clear step forward for interpretable machine learning.
