## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of generating saliency maps for image classifiers in a computationally efficient manner. Current methods often rely on iterative approaches which are slow. The authors propose a learned model (U-Net based) that predicts saliency maps directly, achieving real-time performance. They also introduce a novel saliency metric based on the "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) concept, aiming for more interpretable explanations. The core idea is compelling - learn to predict saliency instead of compute it – and the experimental results demonstrate promising performance on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a practical problem:** Generating saliency maps is crucial for understanding and debugging deep learning models, and speed is a significant bottleneck for many applications (e.g., video analysis).
* **Novel Approach:**  Learning a saliency detector directly is a significant departure from iterative methods and offers a clear path to real-time performance.
* **Strong Empirical Results:**  The paper demonstrates superior performance on object localisation compared to existing weakly supervised methods and a reasonable baseline. The CIFAR-10 experiments further support the generalizability of the approach.
* **New Saliency Metric:** The SSR-based metric appears well-motivated and offers a more nuanced evaluation of saliency than simple localization accuracy. The connection to information theory is insightful.
* **Detailed Implementation:** The authors provide thorough details about the model architecture, training procedure, and objective function, fostering reproducibility.
* **Addressing Introduced Evidence:** Recognizing and mitigating the issue of introduced evidence during masking is a thoughtful consideration.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Saliency Methods:** While comparisons to weakly supervised localization methods are present, the paper lacks a more comprehensive comparison to dedicated, *existing* saliency detection techniques (e.g., Gradient-CAM, Guided Backpropagation, etc.) in terms of both speed *and* qualitative/quantitative results beyond localization. Simply stating these methods produce "limited quality" isn't sufficient.
* **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function has several terms and hyperparameters. The rationale behind the specific weighting of these terms (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 4 in Equation 4) isn’t thoroughly explained and could benefit from an ablation study.
* **Mask Smoothness Regularization:**  The use of TV penalty for mask smoothness feels somewhat ad-hoc.  A more rigorous justification or comparison to alternative smoothness constraints would be beneficial.
* **Dependence on Black-Box Classifier:** The performance of the learned saliency detector is inherently tied to the quality of the black-box classifier. This dependency isn’t sufficiently discussed, and the potential for the saliency map to simply reflect biases *within* the classifier is a valid concern.
* **Potential for Adversarial Masks:** Despite efforts to mitigate adversarial artifacts, the possibility remains that the learned model could exploit subtle vulnerabilities in the black-box classifier to generate misleading saliency maps. More robust evaluation techniques to detect this would be beneficial.

**Quality:** 8/10 – The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The experimental results are convincing, and the theoretical motivation is reasonable.

**Clarity:** 8/10 – The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, some sections (e.g., the explanation of the objective function) could be simplified for broader accessibility.

**Originality:** 8/10 –  The approach of learning a saliency detector is novel and represents a significant departure from traditional methods. The SSR-based metric is also a valuable contribution.

**Significance:** 8/10 – The potential for real-time saliency detection opens up new possibilities for interpreting and debugging deep learning models, particularly in dynamic applications like video analysis. The new metric provides a better measure of interpretability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and promising approach to a practical problem.
* Strong empirical results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.
* Well-defined problem and clear presentation of the proposed solution.
* New saliency metric offers a valuable perspective on interpretability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited comparison to existing saliency detection techniques.
* Complexity of the objective function and limited justification for hyperparameter choices.
* Potential for the saliency map to reflect biases within the black-box classifier.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI. Addressing the weaknesses related to comparisons with existing methods and a more thorough justification of the objective function would further strengthen the work.  The potential benefits of real-time saliency detection, coupled with the novel approach and strong experimental results, make this a worthwhile contribution to NIPS.
