**Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"**

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel, model-based approach for generating saliency maps for pre-trained, black-box image classifiers. Instead of using iterative optimization or gradient-based methods for each input image, the authors train a separate deep neural network, specifically a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder, to directly predict a saliency mask in a single forward pass. The training objective is a composite loss function designed to produce masks that are smooth, small, and effective under both the Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and Smallest Destroying Region (SDR) criteria. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric to evaluate the trade-off between the size of a preserved image region and the classifier's confidence. The method is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, demonstrating state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization and showing qualitative improvements over existing techniques. A key advantage of the proposed method is its speed, enabling real-time saliency map generation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of training a dedicated "explainer" model to generate saliency maps for a black-box classifier is a significant departure from prevalent iterative or backpropagation-based methods. This model-based approach is elegant and has the potential to amortize the cost of explanation generation over many examples.

2.  **Performance and Speed:** The reported results are strong. The ability to generate saliency maps at over 100 images per second is a substantial practical advantage, opening up new applications such as real-time video saliency analysis. The weakly supervised object localization results are particularly impressive, outperforming previous methods and approaching the performance of some fully supervised techniques.

3.  **New Metric:** The proposal of a new saliency metric `s(a, p)` is a thoughtful contribution. It attempts to formalize the intuitive goal of preserving maximal class probability within a minimal image area, which is a useful concept for evaluating saliency methods beyond localization accuracy.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core idea and results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of methodological ambiguities, presentation flaws, and a general lack of rigor that make it unsuitable for publication at NIPS in its current form.

1.  **Methodological Opacity and Justification:**
    *   **Objective Function:** The objective in Equation (4) is a weighted sum of four distinct terms. The weights (`λ1` to `λ4`) are critical hyperparameters, yet their chosen values are presented without any justification, sensitivity analysis, or ablation study. The performance of the entire method likely hinges on this tuning, but the reader is given no insight into how these values were chosen or how robust the method is to their variation.
    *   **Ad-hoc Architectural Choices:** The paper contains several unprincipled design choices. The feature filter in Equation (7) is a simple scalar attention mechanism, but its justification is weak. The final nonlinearity in Equation (8) is non-standard, and the explanation that "sigmoid and tanh nonlinearities did not optimise properly" is insufficient. A proper analysis of the optimization difficulties or a more principled motivation for the proposed function is required.
    *   **Vague Training Details:** Section 4.1 describes crucial training "optimisations" with a concerning lack of detail. The use of "fake labels" is central to the model's ability to use the class selector, but the process is not reproducible from the description. How are fake classes chosen? Was an additional loss term used to train the embedding, as hinted at? This ambiguity is a major flaw.

2.  **Evaluation Concerns:**
    *   **New Metric `s(a, p)`:** While the idea is interesting, the information-theoretic interpretation is hand-wavy and not formally derived. The threshold `ã = max(a, 0.05)` is an arbitrary constant that could significantly affect the metric's values and rankings. More critically, the evaluation protocol involves resizing the cropped region to the classifier's required input size. This resizing operation is a major confounding variable that can introduce significant artifacts or alter object scales and aspect ratios, potentially helping or harming classification in ways unrelated to the saliency map's quality. This weakness is mentioned but its impact is not quantified.
    *   **Vague Comparisons:** In Table 3, the method is compared to generic "gradient-based" and "iterative" methods without specifying which ones. Given the parallel work of Fong and Vedaldi [2] is mentioned as an iterative method, it is unclear if this is the benchmark used. Specificity is required for meaningful comparison.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from a striking lack of polish, which obstructs understanding and reflects poorly on the work's carefulness.
    *   **Equation Numbering:** Equation (1) is used to define two different equations in Section 3.1. This is a fundamental error.
    *   **Notation:** In Section 3.3, the hyperparameters `λi` are referred to as `i`, which is confusing. The typo `λ2 = 10 3` presumably means `10^-3`.
    *   **Missing Figure Captions:** The provided text refers to six figures, but no captions are included. It is impossible to fully evaluate the qualitative claims or understand the model architecture (Figure 4) without them.
    *   **Unprofessional Referencing:** The reference list is in a state of extreme disarray. It lacks any consistent formatting, with entries being incomplete (e.g., [11], [12]), containing extraneous text, and mixing different citation styles. Furthermore, a footnote referring to the NIPS conference itself is strangely placed mid-sentence on the first page. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable.

**Decision**

While the paper presents a novel and potentially impactful direction for saliency research, the execution and presentation are deeply flawed. The combination of vague methodological descriptions, unmotivated hyperparameter choices, potential evaluation confounders, and a multitude of severe formatting and presentation errors leads me to recommend **rejection**. The core ideas are strong enough to warrant a resubmission after a thorough revision that addresses the critical issues of reproducibility, methodological rigor, and professional presentation.
