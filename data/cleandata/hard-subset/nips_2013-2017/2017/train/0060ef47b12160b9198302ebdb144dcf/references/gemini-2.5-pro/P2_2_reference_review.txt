### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel, model-agnostic method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers in real-time. The core idea is to reframe saliency detection from a slow, iterative optimization problem (performed per image) into a fast, amortized inference problem. The authors propose training a separate, sophisticated neural network (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) to directly predict a saliency mask for a given image and class pair in a single feed-forward pass. To train this "masking model," they devise a comprehensive objective function that encourages the generated masks to identify regions that are both sufficient for classification (Smallest Sufficient Region - SSR) and necessary for it (Smallest Destroying Region - SDR), while also promoting smoothness and small area. The paper also introduces a new metric to evaluate saliency based on the information concentration within a cropped region. The method is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on weakly supervised object localization and outperforming existing methods on their proposed saliency metric.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Effective Framing of the Problem:** The key contribution—amortizing the cost of saliency map generation by training a dedicated predictor model—is a significant conceptual shift from prior iterative approaches (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017). This successfully trades a slow, per-instance optimization for a one-time, upfront training cost, enabling real-time performance. This is a powerful idea with practical implications.

2.  **High-Quality Results and Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental results are very strong. The method achieves a new state-of-the-art in weakly supervised object localization on ImageNet, outperforming previous methods by a considerable margin. The qualitative examples in Figure 5 are visually compelling, showing sharp and accurate masks that are clearly superior to the compared iterative method. The extension to CIFAR-10, particularly without a pre-trained encoder, demonstrates the generalizability and robustness of the approach.

3.  **Thoughtful Objective Function and New Metric:** The paper shows a deep understanding of the subtleties of saliency. The discussion of "introduced evidence" and the formulation of an objective function (Eq. 4) that combines SSR, SDR, smoothness (TV), and area (AV) penalties is comprehensive and well-motivated. The new saliency metric (Eq. 3) is also a valuable contribution, providing a more direct measure of a mask's explanatory power than the proxy task of localization, as it explicitly balances the preserved area against the resulting classification confidence.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out logically, the related work is appropriately contextualized, the proposed method is described in sufficient detail, and the experiments are well-designed to support the claims.

#### **Weaknesses**

1.  **High Upfront Training Cost and Complexity:** The primary drawback of this approach is its setup cost. For every black-box model one wishes to explain, a separate, complex masking model (a ResNet-50 based U-Net) must be trained. The paper notes training on 250,000 images, which is a significant computational investment. This contrasts with iterative methods that, while slow at inference, require no specific training. This trade-off should be discussed more explicitly. How long does it take to train one masking model? For practitioners wanting to quickly probe a single model, this method may be prohibitively expensive.

2.  **Potential for Lack of Faithfulness:** The method introduces a second model to explain the first. While empirically successful, this raises a fundamental question of faithfulness. Is the masking model truly learning to replicate the black-box classifier's "reasoning," or is it learning a highly effective object segmentation prior that merely correlates well with the classifier's decisions? The paper alludes to this by mentioning the model's own biases in the conclusion, but this is a critical point. For example, if the black-box classifier has a known flaw (e.g., relying on snow to classify polar bears), it is not guaranteed that the masking model will faithfully highlight the snow. It might instead learn from its own ImageNet pre-training to highlight the bear, producing a plausible but unfaithful explanation. This gap between plausibility and faithfulness is a key challenge in XAI that this method inherits.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The objective function in Equation 4 contains four weighting parameters (λ1 to λ4). The paper provides the values used in the experiments but offers no analysis of their sensitivity or a principled way to set them. The performance and nature of the generated masks could be highly dependent on the tuning of these parameters, which affects the method's robustness and ease of use.

### **Detailed Comments and Justification**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed architecture is sound, the training procedure includes clever techniques (e.g., using fake labels to enforce class-conditioning), and the experimental validation is rigorous. The claims of superior performance and speed are well-supported by the evidence provided.

*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The manuscript is a model of clarity. Figures are informative, and the progression from motivation to method to results is logical. The formulation of the objective function and the new metric is explained with precision.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on concepts like SSR/SDR from prior work (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), the central idea of training a predictive model for saliency maps is novel and distinguishes it clearly from previous gradient-based or iterative optimization-based methods. The combination of ideas into a single, effective system is a significant contribution.

*   **Significance:** The significance is high. By enabling real-time saliency generation, this work opens up new application areas, such as video analysis and interactive model debugging, that were infeasible with slower methods. The demonstrated performance on weakly supervised localization also pushes the state of the art on that task. Researchers in explainable AI and computer vision are very likely to build upon these ideas, either by refining the masking model architecture, the objective function, or by applying the core concept to other domains.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and powerful paradigm shift for saliency detection (amortized inference vs. per-instance optimization).
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard, competitive benchmark (weakly supervised localization).
*   The method is fast, practical for real-time applications, and produces visually impressive, high-quality saliency maps.
*   Introduces a well-motivated new metric for evaluating saliency.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   The high training cost per black-box model is a significant practical limitation that is not fully addressed.
*   The method introduces an "explainer" model, which raises fundamental questions about faithfulness versus plausibility that are not experimentally explored.

### **Recommendation**

This is a strong paper with a novel idea, excellent execution, and impressive results. The weaknesses, while important to acknowledge, are primarily related to practical trade-offs and deeper philosophical questions in XAI rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed method. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. The work is a clear and significant advance for the field of model interpretability.

**Decision: Accept**
