

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection for black box classifiers that significantly improves upon existing methods in terms of speed and quality. The authors address the limitation of current saliency methods (e.g., iterative patch removal [17], gradient-based approaches [11, 12]) which are either slow or produce low-quality explanations. Their key insight is to train a model (a U-Net based masking network) that can predict saliency maps in a single forward pass rather than iteratively optimizing for each image. This approach is both model-agnostic and orders-of-magnitude faster than existing methods, enabling real-time applications. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric (s(a,p)) based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which better evaluates the interpretability of saliency maps compared to traditional localization accuracy metrics.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. It builds upon the SSR/SDR concepts introduced in [2, 17] but addresses their limitations through a model-based approach. The authors correctly position their work relative to parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2], acknowledging that while both approaches aim for model-agnostic saliency, their method is fundamentally different in being a trainable model rather than an iterative optimization process. They also properly cite relevant gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] and patch removal techniques [17].

**Strengths:**
1. Technical soundness: The objective function (equation 4) effectively combines smoothness (TV penalty), region size, classification confidence, and destruction of class probability. The authors properly address the critical issue of adversarial artifacts through mask smoothing and randomized evidence removal, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. Novelty: The core contribution of training a model to predict saliency maps in real-time is novel and well-motivated. The introduction of the s(a,p) metric is particularly valuable as it directly measures how well the saliency region preserves classification confidence (SSR) while being small, which is more aligned with the conceptual goal of saliency than localization accuracy.

3. Experimental results: The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, showing that their method outperforms existing weakly supervised techniques on ImageNet object localization (36.7% error rate vs. 34.3% for fully supervised VGG [10]). The CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates generalizability to different datasets and shows that the masking model doesn't require pre-trained weights, which is an important practical advantage.

4. Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement, methodology, and results are presented logically with clear figures (e.g., Figure 1 showing real-time saliency, Figure 2 illustrating SSR vs SDR). The architecture diagram (Figure 4) is particularly helpful for understanding the proposed masking model.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of the method's limitations. For example, it would be valuable to know if the method works equally well for all classes (e.g., does it perform better on animals than on textures?), or if there are specific image types where the saliency maps are less accurate.

2. The comparison with [2] is somewhat limited. While the authors acknowledge that [2] was developed in parallel, they don't provide a detailed comparison of the two approaches (e.g., speed differences, quality metrics, or computational requirements for training vs. inference).

3. The paper doesn't fully explore the trade-offs of their method. For instance, how does the training time of the masking model compare to the time savings during inference? The paper states the masking model can process "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second," but doesn't compare this to the training time required to develop the model.

4. The new metric (s(a,p)) is well-motivated, but the paper could better justify why it's superior to existing metrics beyond the examples provided. A more detailed theoretical analysis of how s(a,p) correlates with human interpretability would strengthen this contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of model interpretability. The proposed method addresses a critical limitation of existing saliency techniques (speed) while improving quality, and the new metric provides a more meaningful way to evaluate saliency explanations. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling. The paper is well-written and makes a clear, novel contribution that advances the state of the art in real-time saliency detection.

The main weaknesses are the limited discussion of method limitations and the somewhat superficial comparison with the parallel work [2]. However, these do not detract from the overall quality of the contribution. The paper meets all the NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound, clearly written, novel, and significant.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Proposes a fundamentally new approach to real-time saliency detection (model-based rather than iterative)
- Introduces a new saliency metric that better aligns with the conceptual goal of saliency
- Demonstrates superior performance on standard benchmarks
- Provides clear, well-organized presentation of methods and results
- Enables new applications (video-saliency) not possible with existing methods

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited analysis of method limitations
- Comparison with parallel work [2] could be more detailed
- Does not fully explore trade-offs between training and inference time
