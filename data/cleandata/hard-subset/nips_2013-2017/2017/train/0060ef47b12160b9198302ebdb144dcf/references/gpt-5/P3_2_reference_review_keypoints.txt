- Proposes a learned, real-time saliency method that predicts soft masks via a U-Net–style model to approximate SSR and SDR in one forward pass.  
- Trains the masking model amortizing per-image perturbation optimization using a composite objective instead of per-image optimization.  
- Training objective combines TV smoothness, an area penalty for compactness, preservation of class probability on retained region (SSR), and reduction of class probability when salient region removed (SDR).  
- Addresses introduced evidence by applying masks with randomized alternatives (blur/noise) and enforcing smooth masks to reduce adversarial artifacts.  
- Introduces a new saliency metric based on class probability on the tightest crop and crop area; formulation is ambiguous in the paper.  
- Experiments on ImageNet with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 show improved weakly-supervised localization accuracy and substantial runtime speedups vs. optimization-based methods.  
- Demonstrates qualitative CIFAR-10 results indicating the approach can be trained from scratch on low-resolution data.  
- Situates work relative to gradient/backprop saliency, patch-removal/CAM methods, and optimization-based meaningful perturbations; main novelty is amortizing perturbation optimization.  
- Missing comparisons to widely used baselines Grad-CAM and SmoothGrad, weakening empirical claims.  
- Methodology and architecture (U-Net with class-conditional feature filter) are well-motivated; the fake-label training trick is a useful practical detail.  
- Randomization of alternative images A and TV penalty effectively target introduced-evidence and mask sharpness issues.  
- Claim of “model-agnostic black box” is misleading: training requires a differentiable classifier and backprop through it.  
- Empirical support is promising but incomplete: baseline omissions, ambiguous metric, and lack of standard perturbation-based faithfulness tests.  
- The new saliency metric s(a,p)=log(ã) log(p) is under-specified, ambiguous, and needs analysis for monotonicity and calibration.  
- CIFAR-10 results lack quantitative metrics and are only qualitative.  
- Key ablations are missing: TV weight, area penalty, SDR term, choice of A, mask resolution/upsampling, and fake-label probability.  
- Unclear whether explanations are generated for predicted vs. ground-truth classes at test time; both should be evaluated.  
- Reproducibility details are incomplete: optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, exact preprocessing, and code availability not specified.  
- Paper is generally readable but has typos, formatting issues, ambiguous notation, and inconsistent references.  
- Core idea of amortizing perturbation-based saliency into a feed-forward predictor is novel and practically useful.  
- The randomization over A and fake-label heuristic are practical, original contributions; the saliency metric is novel but heuristic.  
- Significance: potential for real-time, sharp, faithful saliency useful in deployment, but per-classifier retraining limits plug-and-play applicability.  
- Pros: clear motivation, well-motivated objective, elegant architecture, training tricks, runtime benefits, and competitive localization performance.  
- Cons: overstated black-box claim, missing key baselines, under-specified metric, limited ablations, unclear multi-object/failure-mode analysis, per-classifier training requirement, clarity and reproducibility issues.  
- Questions for authors include clarifying backprop requirements, comparison to Grad-CAM/SmoothGrad and insertion/deletion curves, disambiguating and validating the metric, providing ablations, testing predicted vs. GT class explanations, and cross-model generalization.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept pending stronger baselines, clearer metric definition, thorough ablations, and clarification of black-box claims.
