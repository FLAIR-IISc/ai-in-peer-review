### **Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"**

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel method for generating saliency maps for image classifiers. The core problem addressed is the trade-off between speed and quality in existing saliency methods: gradient-based methods are fast but often noisy and uninterpretable, while perturbation/optimization-based methods produce higher-quality maps but are prohibitively slow due to their iterative nature. The authors propose to overcome this by training a deep neural network (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) to *predict* a saliency mask in a single forward pass. This "masking model" is trained to optimize a carefully designed objective function that encourages the mask to identify the Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and the Smallest Destroying Region (SDR) for a given class, while also promoting mask smoothness and small area. The training process treats the target classifier as a "black box" in the sense that its architecture is not modified, though its gradients are used during the training of the masking model. The authors demonstrate that their approach is orders of magnitude faster than iterative methods, achieves state-of-the-art results on weakly supervised object localization, and produces sharp, high-quality masks. They also introduce a new metric to evaluate saliency based on classification confidence within a cropped salient region.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The primary contribution—amortizing the expensive test-time optimization of saliency masks into a single forward pass of a trained model—is highly novel and significant. It directly addresses a major bottleneck for the practical application of high-quality interpretability methods, enabling real-time use cases like video saliency analysis, which were previously infeasible with methods like those of Fong & Vedaldi [2].

2.  **Principled Objective Function:** The proposed loss function (Eq. 4) is a major strength. It is well-motivated and elegantly combines multiple desirable properties for a saliency map:
    *   It jointly optimizes for both SSR (term 3) and SDR (term 4), which are complementary and important definitions of saliency.
    *   It includes explicit regularization for smoothness (TV penalty) and size (AV penalty), which helps prevent the generation of adversarial, non-interpretable masks—a subtle but critical issue the authors rightly identify.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on the challenging task of weakly supervised object localization on ImageNet, outperforming previous saliency and weakly supervised methods (Table 2). The performance being close to a fully supervised VGG model [10] is particularly impressive. The qualitative results (Fig. 5) clearly show that the generated masks are sharp, well-localized, and superior to those from competing methods.

4.  **Demonstrated Generality:** The approach is validated across three different "black-box" architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50), demonstrating that the framework is not tied to a specific classifier type. Furthermore, the successful application to CIFAR-10 with a model trained from scratch (Sec 5.3) shows that the method is not dependent on large-scale pre-training for the masking network and works on different data domains.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **"Black Box" Terminology:** The use of the term "black box" is slightly imprecise and potentially misleading. While the target classifier's weights are frozen and its architecture is not modified, the training of the masking model requires backpropagating through the classifier to compute the loss terms `fc(·)`. This is more accurately a "white-box during training, black-box during inference" approach. True black-box methods (e.g., LIME [6]) would only query the classifier's output probabilities. It would be beneficial for the authors to clarify this distinction, perhaps by referring to the target model as a "non-invasive" or "fixed-function" model.

2.  **Complexity of the Explainer:** The masking model itself is a complex ResNet-50 based U-Net. This raises the question of "who explains the explainer?" While the output masks are interpretable, the generation process is complex. The authors acknowledge this as a potential avenue for future work, but a brief discussion on the trade-offs would be valuable. For instance, how does the performance degrade if a much simpler encoder is used for the masking model? The complexity also implies a significant computational cost and data requirement for training the masking model for each new target classifier.

3.  **Validation of the New Metric:** The new saliency metric `s(a, p)` is an interesting idea motivated by the shortcomings of using localization as a proxy for saliency. However, its formulation feels somewhat ad-hoc. The information-theoretic interpretation is tenuous, and the metric's behaviour is sensitive to the `a_tilde = max(a, 0.05)` threshold. A more in-depth analysis or ablation study on this metric would strengthen its introduction. Additionally, resizing the arbitrary crop to a fixed square input for the classifier could disproportionately affect models that are not invariant to aspect ratio changes, a confound the authors note but do not fully explore.

4.  **Comparison to Iterative Optimization of the Same Objective:** The paper claims that iterative optimization (as in [2]) can overfit and produce blurry masks. While plausible, this claim would be much stronger if the authors had included a baseline where they directly optimize their own objective function (Eq. 4) iteratively for each test image. This would provide a direct, apples-to-apples comparison of the *amortized* vs. *iterative* approach on the *same* objective, isolating the effect of their core contribution.

**Clarity, Quality, Originality, and Significance**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the methodology is described in detail (including crucial training tricks like using "fake" labels), and the experiments are easy to follow.
*   **Quality:** The work is of high technical quality. The proposed model architecture and training procedure are sound and well-justified. The experiments are comprehensive and support the claims made.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of training a network to generate saliency maps for another fixed network is highly original. While it builds on concepts from U-Nets and perturbation-based saliency, the synthesis is novel and creates a new class of saliency detectors.
*   **Significance:** The significance is very high. By enabling real-time generation of high-quality saliency maps, this work opens up new applications in video analysis, interactive debugging of models, and safety-critical systems (e.g., autonomous driving). It represents a substantial advance in the field of model interpretability.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This is an excellent paper presenting a novel, well-executed, and significant contribution to the field of machine learning interpretability. It solves a key practical problem with existing methods and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance. Despite minor weaknesses regarding the "black box" terminology and the need for a more direct baseline comparison, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This work is likely to be highly influential and to inspire follow-up research.
