

# Peer Review: "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a novel approach to generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers that operates in real-time, contrasting with existing iterative methods that require hundreds of passes. The authors propose a masking model based on a U-Net architecture that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which they argue better captures the interpretability of saliency maps than traditional localization metrics. The method is evaluated on ImageNet using three different black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and extended to CIFAR-10. The authors claim their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than iterative methods while achieving better localization accuracy and producing higher quality saliency masks.

The paper builds upon the recent trend of model interpretation methods, particularly those addressing the limitations of gradient-based approaches (e.g., Guided Backpropagation) and iterative methods like those proposed by Zhou et al. [17] and Fong and Vedaldi [2]. The authors position their work as a significant advancement by addressing the computational bottleneck of existing methods while maintaining or improving interpretability.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Approach**: The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than using iterative optimization is genuinely novel and addresses a critical bottleneck in the field. The paper successfully demonstrates that this approach can achieve real-time performance (100 masks/second) while maintaining or improving accuracy.

2. **Well-Motivated Saliency Metric**: The introduction of the new metric (s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p)) is well-justified and addresses a critical limitation of existing evaluation methods. The metric directly relates to the SSR concept and provides a more meaningful assessment of saliency map quality than traditional localization accuracy.

3. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The paper provides thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets (ImageNet and CIFAR-10) and different black-box classifiers. The comparison with multiple baselines, including iterative methods and weakly supervised approaches, is robust.

4. **Technical Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The architecture of the masking model (Figure 4) is clearly described, and the training process is detailed with appropriate technical depth.

5. **Practical Significance**: The real-time capability opens up new application areas (e.g., video saliency for autonomous vehicles), which is a significant practical contribution beyond just academic interest.

## Weaknesses and Criticisms

1. **Contradiction in "Model Agnostic" Claim**: The paper repeatedly claims their method is "model agnostic," yet the masking model relies heavily on a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder (Section 4). This creates a contradiction: the masking model is not truly agnostic to the black-box classifier's internal representations. The authors should clarify this limitation and potentially explore how to make the masking model more universally applicable.

2. **Incomplete Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: While the paper references Fong and Vedaldi [2] (which was developed in parallel), it doesn't provide a detailed comparison with their method. The paper claims superiority but lacks direct comparison on the same metrics. For a paper claiming to advance the state of the art, a more thorough comparison with the most recent methods would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Lack of Analysis of Mask Quality**: The paper shows examples of saliency maps (Figure 5) but doesn't provide quantitative analysis of mask quality beyond the new metric. For instance, how do the masks compare to ground truth segmentation masks? How do they perform on images with multiple objects?

4. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations**: The paper mentions that the masking model may have its own biases (Section 6), but it doesn't explore this in depth. Given the importance of bias in explainable AI, this is a significant gap. The authors should discuss potential sources of bias in their method and how they might affect interpretation.

5. **Ambiguity in Evidence Removal**: The paper proposes several methods to avoid introducing artifacts (Section 3.1), but the effectiveness of these methods is not rigorously validated. For example, how does the random choice between blurred and noise images affect the quality of the saliency maps? The paper should provide more empirical evidence for this design choice.

6. **Limited Generalization Analysis**: The paper demonstrates success on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, but it doesn't test the method on more challenging datasets or with different types of classifiers (e.g., non-CNN classifiers). The paper claims to work with "any differentiable image classifier," but this isn't thoroughly validated.

7. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't include ablation studies to analyze the contribution of each component of their objective function (Section 4.1). For example, how much does the TV penalty contribute to the final result? How sensitive is the model to the hyperparameters?

## Specific Technical Comments

1. **Masking Model Architecture**: The architecture (Figure 4) is well-described, but the authors should clarify why they chose ResNet-50 specifically for the encoder. Would other backbone networks (e.g., EfficientNet, Vision Transformer) work equally well? The paper claims the complexity is comparable to ResNet-50, but without comparison to other architectures, this claim is hard to verify.

2. **Training Process**: The training process with "fake labels" (Section 4.1) is an interesting technique to prevent the model from ignoring the class selector, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that this actually improves performance. The authors should include a comparison showing the difference in performance with and without this technique.

3. **Saliency Metric**: While the new metric is well-motivated, the paper doesn't address how it handles cases where the classifier is uncertain (p close to 0.5). The threshold of 0.05 for the area (ã = max(a, 0.05)) is arbitrary and should be better justified.

4. **CIFAR-10 Experiment**: The CIFAR-10 experiment (Section 5.3) is promising but lacks context. The authors should compare their results to existing weakly supervised methods for CIFAR-10, not just baselines like "center" and "max box."

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with a novel approach to real-time saliency detection. The core contribution of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than using iterative optimization is significant and addresses a real bottleneck in the field. The new saliency metric is well-justified and provides a more meaningful way to evaluate saliency maps.

However, the paper has several important weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be considered for publication at NeurIPS. The contradiction in the "model agnostic" claim, the incomplete comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and the insufficient discussion of limitations and potential biases are particularly concerning. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of the method's limitations, a more comprehensive comparison with recent approaches, and additional experiments to validate the claims about model agnosticism and generalization.

## Recommendation

**Accept with Minor Revisions**

The paper presents a genuinely novel approach with significant practical value. The main weaknesses are related to incomplete comparisons with the state of the art and insufficient discussion of limitations, rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology. With the suggested revisions, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI.

## Specific Recommendations for Revision

1. **Clarify the "Model Agnostic" Claim**: Either modify the claim to reflect that the masking model is not truly agnostic (it relies on a pre-trained ResNet), or provide evidence that the masking model can be trained without this dependency.

2. **Expand Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: Include a direct comparison with Fong and Vedaldi [2] on the same metrics, and compare with more recent methods (e.g., the recent work by Bach et al. on gradient-based methods).

3. **Add Ablation Studies**: Include ablation studies to analyze the contribution of each component of the objective function.

4. **Expand Discussion of Limitations**: Add a dedicated section discussing the potential biases in the masking model and how they might affect the saliency maps.

5. **Provide More Context for CIFAR-10 Results**: Compare the CIFAR-10 results with existing weakly supervised methods for that dataset.

6. **Justify Thresholds**: Better justify the threshold of 0.05 for the area in the saliency metric.

7. **Include More Quantitative Analysis of Mask Quality**: Provide metrics beyond the new saliency metric to analyze the quality of the saliency maps (e.g., comparison to ground truth segmentation masks).

With these revisions, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the NeurIPS community and significantly advance the state of the art in real-time saliency detection.
