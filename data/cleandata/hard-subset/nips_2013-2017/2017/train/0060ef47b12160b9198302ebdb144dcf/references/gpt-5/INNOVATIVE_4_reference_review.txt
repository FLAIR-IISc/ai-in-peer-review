Summary
The paper proposes an amortized, model-conditioned saliency method that replaces slow per-image iterative optimization with a feed-forward “masking model” that predicts a class-conditional saliency mask in one pass. The mask is trained to minimize an objective that balances smoothness (TV), small area, “smallest sufficient region” (high class score on preserved region), and “smallest destroying region” (low class score when the salient region is removed). To mitigate artifact-driven explanations, the authors randomize the evidence-removal baseline A (strong blur or random color+noise) and enforce smoothness/low resolution upsampling. They also introduce a new saliency metric based on the area-probability trade-off and evaluate on ImageNet weakly supervised localization, reporting competitive error and large speedups over iterative methods; they also show qualitative results on CIFAR-10 with a scratch-trained masking model.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: amortizing saliency via a learned U-Net-style masking network trained against a fixed classifier is a bold, useful idea. It reframes “explain-by-optimization” into a learnable module that generalizes across images and achieves real-time performance.
- Objective design: combining SSR and SDR in one loss is well-motivated; the discussion and practical steps to reduce adversarial artifacts (randomized A, TV, low-to-high resolution path) are thoughtful and likely to matter in practice.
- Practical significance: producing 100+ 224×224 maps/s enables video-time saliency and interactive diagnostics; training per black-box and not needing the classifier at inference is appealing for deployment.
- Empirics: competitive ImageNet localization error without any localization supervision; improved over several baselines and concurrent iterative perturbation methods; sensible training tricks (fake labels to enforce class-conditioning).
- Scope: works across architectures (AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet) and datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10), and qualitatively captures differences in “how” different classifiers look at images.

Weaknesses and questions
- Saliency metric appears inconsistent with the stated intent. s(a,p) = log(ã)/log(p) yields smaller scores when p decreases (since log(p)→−∞), which incentivizes making the classifier less confident rather than preserving confidence in a small region (SSR). A method could drive p down and “look good” under this metric. Please clarify the definition/sign or provide a corrected formulation (e.g., −log(p)/−log(ã), or a product/weighted sum) and re-evaluate.
- “Black box” vs differentiable access. Training requires backprop through fc to optimize M; hence you need white-box gradients (or at least differentiability). The paper at times says “model agnostic” and “black box,” but later restricts to “any differentiable classifier.” Please clarify this requirement and the extent to which gradients (vs only scores) are needed.
- Comparisons and ablations. Quantitative comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM/Guided Backprop/Excitation Backprop are limited or absent, and the evaluation emphasizes localization rather than standard explanation quality metrics. Ablations isolating the contribution of each loss term (especially the SDR term and the randomized A), the mask resolution, and the TV penalty would strengthen claims about design choices.
- Evaluation details. The ImageNet localization protocol details (split, multi-instance handling, threshold choice robustness) are under-specified; reported numbers should be situated against strong contemporary baselines (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM) with consistent preprocessing and runtime. Include run-time comparisons to iterative meaningful perturbations (number of steps × ms/step) to substantiate “orders-of-magnitude faster.”
- Generalization across classifiers. The masking model is trained per target classifier; it would be informative to test cross-classifier transfer (train on ResNet, apply to GoogLeNet) to gauge whether the model learns general visual priors vs idiosyncrasies of the target classifier.
- Robustness to artifact priors. While randomized A helps, additional sanity checks would increase confidence: (i) varying blur/noise strengths, (ii) training with a fixed A to show degradation, (iii) evaluating with “deletion/insertion” curves (remove/insert salient pixels and track the score trajectory) to corroborate SSR/SDR claims.
- Clarity and citations. Some related work is missing or under-discussed (e.g., Grad-CAM). Notational clarity could be improved (e.g., define all variables at first use; ensure consistent use of “masking model” vs “mask M”).

Quality
The core technical idea is sound and implemented with reasonable engineering choices. The experimental section shows promise but would benefit from stronger baselines, clearer protocols, and ablations. The saliency metric issue is substantial and should be corrected.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, with helpful figures and motivation. Please clarify the black-box assumption, fix the metric, and add missing evaluation details.

Originality
Amortizing iterative perturbation into a trainable, class-conditional masking network that optimizes both SSR and SDR is a novel and timely contribution relative to gradient/backprop saliency and iterative meaningful perturbations.

Significance
High practical impact if validated: real-time, deployment-friendly saliency with better localization and sharper masks is valuable for interpretability and weakly supervised localization/segmentation. The approach could spur further work on learned explanation modules.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel direction (learned, amortized saliency) with compelling speed/quality benefits.
- Thoughtful handling of artifact issues and dual SSR/SDR objective.
- Strong qualitative results and competitive weakly supervised localization.

Cons for acceptance
- Saliency metric appears flawed as written; needs correction and re-evaluation.
- Limited quantitative comparisons/ablations; black-box claims need tightening.
- Some evaluation details and references are missing.

Suggestions
- Fix and justify the saliency metric; report sensitivity and rank correlations with localization/insertion-deletion metrics.
- Add comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM and report runtimes.
- Provide ablations for loss terms, A choices, mask resolution/TV, and transfer across classifiers.
- Clarify the requirement of differentiability and gradient access.

Recommendation
Accept (weak). The core idea is novel and promising, with clear practical upside. With the metric corrected and evaluations strengthened, this work can set a new direction for real-time, model-conditioned saliency.

Confidence
High on the conceptual assessment; moderate on empirical conclusions pending metric correction and additional baselines.
