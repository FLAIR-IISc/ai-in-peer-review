This paper proposes a learned, feed-forward saliency detection method as an alternative to iterative approaches. The core idea – training a model to predict saliency maps directly – is conceptually sound and addresses a significant bottleneck in existing saliency techniques. However, the paper suffers from a surprising number of shortcomings, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies and a lack of rigorous experimental control to a somewhat overstated claim of novelty and insufficient discussion of limitations. While the core idea holds promise, the execution and presentation fall short of NIPS standards.

**Summary:** The authors address the problem of generating saliency maps for image classifiers, aiming for real-time performance. They argue that iterative methods are too slow and propose a U-Net architecture trained to predict saliency masks directly. A novel saliency metric (based on smallest sufficient region principles) is introduced, and experiments are conducted on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addressing a practical problem:** Real-time saliency detection *is* valuable, and the proposed approach offers a speed advantage.
*   **Novel metric:** The introduced saliency metric, based on SSR/SDR concepts, is a reasonable attempt to formalize saliency evaluation beyond simple localization accuracy.
*   **Comprehensive experiments:** Experiments are performed on multiple datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10) and with different black-box classifiers.
*   **U-Net architecture:** Utilizing a U-Net is a sensible choice for generating high-resolution saliency maps.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational inconsistencies:** Equation (1) defines *E* as X\*M, implying element-wise multiplication, yet the subsequent description speaks of “setting regions to a constant colour”. This is ambiguous. Similarly, equation (6) redefines (X,M) without clearly establishing it's role in equation (4).
*   **Insufficient ablation studies:** While the authors mention randomizing the alternative image *A* in the evidence removal function, there’s no discussion of the sensitivity of results to the choice of blur strength or noise level. How does performance vary with different settings?
*   **Overstated novelty:** The authors repeatedly emphasize the novelty of their approach. However, the concept of learning to approximate iterative methods is not entirely new, and the connection to related work (e.g., [2] which *also* optimizes in image space) is downplayed. The phrasing frequently implies a significant leap over existing methods without fully justifying it.
*   **Ambiguous regularization:** The explanation of the training process (section 4.1) regarding the fake label and the purpose of the scaling of the loss function is poorly articulated. It's unclear *why* these techniques are employed and how they contribute to the learning process.
*   **Lack of statistical significance:** Tables 1, 2, and 3 present numerical results without any indication of statistical significance. Differences in error rates or metric values could be due to chance. Standard deviation or confidence intervals are missing.
*   **Subjective evaluation:** The claim that masks are "clearly interpretable" (section 5.3) is subjective. A more rigorous evaluation, potentially involving human subjects, would strengthen this assertion.
*   **The choice of 4 in equation 4:** The choice of 4 = 0.3 is arbitrary and lacks justification. Why this value and not another?
*   **Figure quality and clarity:** Several figures (e.g., Fig. 3) are low resolution and difficult to interpret. The text references specific details *within* the figures that are hard to discern. Fig. 2 lacks clear labeling for "SSR" and "SDR".

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The idea of a learned saliency detector is promising and could be valuable to the research community. The authors demonstrate a working system and offer a novel metric.

**Con:** The paper’s weaknesses – notational imprecision, lack of rigorous analysis, overstated novelty, and insufficient clarity – are substantial. The experimental validation is weakened by the absence of statistical significance. The presentation feels rushed and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the core idea has merit, the numerous flaws, both major and minor, detract significantly from the paper’s quality. The lack of rigor in the experimental evaluation and the overstated claims of novelty raise concerns about the validity and generalizability of the results. The authors would benefit from addressing these issues through a more thorough analysis, clearer presentation, and a more honest assessment of their contribution relative to existing work. A major revision addressing these points might be reconsidered, but in its current state, this paper does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS.
