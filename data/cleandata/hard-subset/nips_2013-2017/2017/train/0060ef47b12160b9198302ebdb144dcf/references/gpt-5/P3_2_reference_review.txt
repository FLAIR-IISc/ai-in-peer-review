Summary
The paper proposes a learned, real-time saliency method for image classifiers that approximates both smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR) in a single forward pass. Instead of per-image optimization, a U-Net–style “masking model” is trained to predict a soft mask given an image and a class selector. The training objective combines: (i) total-variation regularization for smoothness, (ii) area penalty to encourage compactness, (iii) preservation of class probability on the retained region (SSR), and (iv) reduction of class probability when the salient region is removed (SDR). The authors address “introduced evidence” by applying masks via randomized alternatives (blur or noise) and enforcing smooth masks. They also introduce a saliency metric based on the probability of the class on the tightest crop containing the saliency region and the crop’s area. Experiments on ImageNet with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 black boxes show improved weakly-supervised localization accuracy over baselines and optimization-based methods, and a qualitative CIFAR-10 study indicates the approach can be trained from scratch on low-resolution images.

Relation to prior work
The paper nicely situates itself with gradient/backprop-based explanations (saliency, guided backprop, Excitation Backprop), patch removal and CAM-style localization, and optimization-based meaningful perturbations (Fong & Vedaldi). The main novelty is amortizing the perturbation-based optimization into a learned predictor trained against a composite SSR/SDR objective and explicitly addressing introduced evidence. Notably absent from the discussion are Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) and SmoothGrad (Smilkov et al., 2017), which are contemporary and widely used baselines; a direct comparison would strengthen the claims.

Quality
- Methodology: The objective is well-motivated, and the architectural choices (U-Net with a class-conditional “feature filter”) are reasonable. The “fake label” training trick to force attention to the class selector is a useful detail. The randomization of the alternative image A and the TV penalty directly target adversarial artifacts and mask sharpness.
- Soundness: The approach requires a differentiable classifier during training (to optimize mask parameters through fc((X, M))), so the “model-agnostic black box” claim is only partially accurate; it is architecture-agnostic but not gradient-free. This should be clarified.
- Empirical support: The weakly supervised localization results are promising, and the speed advantage is a clear practical benefit. However:
  - Baselines miss Grad-CAM/SmoothGrad and more recent weakly supervised localization baselines. Comparison to Fong & Vedaldi is discussed but quantitative gaps are not fully elaborated.
  - The new saliency metric is interesting but ad hoc. s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p) is ambiguous (is it a product or a ratio?). Its properties (monotonicity, calibration across classes, sensitivity to resizing without aspect-ratio preservation) need validation. Deletion/insertion curves or perturbation tests would give a more robust faithfulness assessment.
  - CIFAR-10 results are purely qualitative; quantitative metrics would help.
  - Ablations are missing: effects of TV weight, area penalty, the SDR term, choice of A, mask resolution/upsampling, and the fake-label probability.
  - It is unclear whether explanations are generated for the predicted vs. ground-truth class at test time; both should be assessed.
- Reproducibility: Some details are provided (loss weights, training set size), but optimization details (optimizer, LR schedule, batch size), precise preprocessing, and code availability are not specified.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-structured, with a thoughtful discussion of introduced evidence. There are a number of typos and formatting issues (e.g., “s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p)” is unclear, reference formatting is inconsistent, notational conventions for masking/cropping could be cleaner). Figures described are informative but not shown here; ensuring high-resolution, side-by-side comparisons with clear legends would help.

Originality
The core idea—amortizing perturbation-based saliency into a feed-forward predictor trained with a composite SSR/SDR objective—is novel relative to earlier per-image optimization methods and gradient-based explanations. The “fake label” training heuristic and the randomization over A are practical contributions. The proposed saliency metric is new, albeit somewhat heuristic.

Significance
If validated with stronger baselines and more thorough evaluations, a method that yields sharper, faithful, and real-time saliency could be useful for deployment (e.g., video, interactive analysis). The ability to train per black box and produce single-pass masks is compelling. However, requiring retraining a masking model per classifier reduces plug-and-play applicability, and the current evaluation leaves open questions about faithfulness vs. localization quality and robustness to choice of A.

Pros
- Clear goal and motivation: fast, sharp, faithful saliency maps.
- Well-motivated objective combining SSR and SDR, with explicit anti-artifact measures.
- Elegant architecture leveraging U-Net and class embeddings; practical training tricks.
- Strong runtime benefits and competitive weakly supervised localization performance.
- Interesting attempt at a saliency metric with reduced vulnerability to introduced evidence.

Cons
- “Model-agnostic/black-box” claim is overstated; training requires gradients of the classifier.
- Missing key baselines (Grad-CAM, SmoothGrad) and more comprehensive comparisons to optimization-based methods.
- Saliency metric is under-specified and not validated via standard perturbation-based faithfulness tests; equation ambiguous.
- Limited ablations; lack of analysis on failure modes, multi-object scenes, and absent-class selectors.
- Per-classifier training requirement; unclear cross-model generalization or transfer.
- Clarity and reference formatting issues; some reproducibility details missing.

Questions for authors
- Does training require backprop through the classifier? If so, please clarify the extent of “black box” assumptions.
- How does your method compare quantitatively to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ and SmoothGrad on localization and perturbation-based faithfulness (insertion/deletion curves)?
- Please disambiguate and analyze the proposed metric (mathematical form, sensitivity, correlation with human judgments).
- Provide ablations on TV/area/SDR terms, alternative A choices, mask resolution, and the fake-label probability.
- Do you generate explanations for predicted labels? How does performance differ from using ground truth?
- Can a single masking model handle multiple classifiers? How does it generalize across architectures?

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea of an amortized, perturbation-inspired saliency predictor with an SSR/SDR objective is promising and practically impactful. Strengthening the empirical evaluation with missing baselines, clearer metric definition, and ablations, and clarifying the black-box claim would solidify the contribution.
