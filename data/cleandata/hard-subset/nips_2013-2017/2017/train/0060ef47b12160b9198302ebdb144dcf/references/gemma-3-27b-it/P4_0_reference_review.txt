This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, moving away from iterative methods towards a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea—training a model to directly predict saliency maps—is a significant shift, addressing the computational bottleneck of existing techniques like occlusion or optimization-based methods [1, 2, 17]. The authors also propose a new saliency metric based on the Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) concept, aiming for a more informative evaluation than simple localization accuracy. 

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in saliency detection and explainable AI [5, 6, 11, 12, 16].  The iterative approaches of Zhou et al. [17] and Cao et al. [1] are clearly positioned as the motivation for the faster, learned approach. The recent work of Fong and Vedaldi [2], which shares the black-box and optimization focus, is appropriately acknowledged and contrasted. The proposed metric also acknowledges related concepts presented in [2].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The learned saliency model represents a distinct departure from existing iterative techniques, offering a potentially significant speed advantage.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The concepts are explained clearly, and the motivations for each design choice are presented.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed objective function (Eq. 4) appears well-formulated, incorporating smoothness constraints and attempting to address the issue of introduced evidence. The U-Net architecture (Fig. 4) is a reasonable choice.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on ImageNet demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, with competitive localization accuracy (Table 1) and favorable results on the proposed saliency metric (Table 3). The CIFAR-10 experiment further demonstrates generalizability.
* **New Saliency Metric:** The introduction of the SSR-based saliency metric is a valuable contribution, providing a more nuanced evaluation than standard localization metrics.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Objective Function Regularization:** While the introduction of the 'A' term in the evidence removal function (Eq. 6) is a clever attempt to mitigate adversarial artifacts, the random selection of A (blurred image or random noise) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of A’s impact, or perhaps a learned A, could strengthen the argument.
* **Masking Model Architecture Detail:** The rationale for specific design choices within the U-Net architecture (e.g., the two-channel output and the non-standard nonlinearity in Eq. 8) could be elaborated upon.  Why did standard nonlinearities fail?  
* **Comparison with Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons are made to existing saliency methods and simple baselines, the performance gains, though demonstrated, don’t always appear massive. A comparison to more recent and advanced saliency methods (beyond those cited) could offer a stronger evaluation.
* **Ablation Studies:**  Ablation studies to examine the contribution of each term in the objective function (Eq. 4) are missing. This would provide valuable insight into the importance of the TV penalty, the SSR/SDR terms, and the evidence removal component.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper presents mainly positive results. A more thorough discussion of failure cases and limitations of the method would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and promising approach to a challenging problem in explainable AI.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The new saliency metric provides a valuable tool for evaluating saliency maps.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Some design choices lack sufficient justification (e.g., the selection of A in the evidence removal function).
* The lack of ablation studies limits understanding of the contribution of different components of the objective function.
* Comparison to a wider range of state-of-the-art saliency methods would strengthen the evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of explainable AI.  The proposed method offers a significant improvement in speed over existing saliency detection techniques while maintaining competitive accuracy.  The introduction of the new saliency metric is a valuable addition. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should consider adding an ablation study and a more detailed discussion of the choices made in the objective function and masking model architecture.
