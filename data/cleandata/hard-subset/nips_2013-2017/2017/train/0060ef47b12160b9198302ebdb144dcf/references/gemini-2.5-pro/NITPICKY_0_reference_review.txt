### Summary

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers in real-time. The core idea is to replace slow, iterative optimization-based approaches with a trainable model (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) that learns to predict a high-quality saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. The masking model is trained to optimize a composite objective function that encourages masks to be small (SSR), effective at destroying the prediction when inverted (SDR), and smooth, while being robust to different methods of evidence removal. The authors also introduce a new metric to evaluate saliency based on the information concentration within a cropped region. Experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 show that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster than iterative techniques and achieves state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution—framing saliency map generation as a prediction task for a trained network rather than an iterative optimization problem—is a significant and practical idea. It directly addresses the critical speed-quality trade-off that plagues existing saliency methods, enabling potential real-time applications like video saliency, which is a compelling direction.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates impressive performance on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, outperforming previous methods and even approaching the performance of some fully supervised techniques. This provides strong evidence for the quality of the generated masks.
3.  **Comprehensive Objective Function:** The proposed objective function (Eq. 4) is well-conceived, thoughtfully combining principles of Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR), Smallest Destroying Region (SDR), mask area, and smoothness (Total Variation). This formulation is more holistic than many prior works that focus only on one aspect (e.g., SDR).
4.  **A Novel Evaluation Metric:** The introduction of a new saliency metric (Section 3.2) is a valuable contribution. While localization accuracy is a common proxy, it is not the true goal of saliency. The proposed metric, which attempts to measure the concentration of class-relevant information in a small area, is a step towards a more principled evaluation of saliency methods.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core idea and results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of technical inaccuracies, unclear justifications, and presentation flaws that undermine its quality and reproducibility.

**Major Technical Issues:**

1.  **Incorrect Objective Function Formulation:** The third term in the objective function in Equation 4, `λ3 * log(fc(φ(X,M)))`, is intended to maximize the class probability of the preserved region. However, since the overall objective `L(M)` is being minimized, this term should be formulated as `-λ3 * log(fc(φ(X,M)))`. As written, the objective encourages *minimizing* the log-probability of the target class, which is contrary to the stated goal of finding a sufficient region. This appears to be a critical error in the paper's central formulation.
2.  **Flawed Saliency Metric Interpretation:** The paper's interpretation of its new saliency metric `s(a, p) = log(ã) / log(p)` (Eq. 3) is incorrect. A "good" saliency map should have a small area `a` (making `log(ã)` a large negative number) and a high probability `p` (making `log(p)` a small negative number). This combination results in a *large* positive value for `s`. The paper states the opposite: "a low value for the saliency metric is a characteristic of good saliency detectors." This fundamental misunderstanding of their own metric calls into question the results presented in Table 3 and the conclusions drawn from them.
3.  **Ambiguous Use of "Model-Agnostic":** The paper repeatedly claims the method is "model agnostic." This is misleading. While the trained masking model does not require access to the black-box classifier's internals *at inference time*, it is trained specifically for one particular classifier. A separate masking model must be trained for each new black-box model (as done in the experiments for AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50). The method is not agnostic in the sense that a single trained saliency model can explain any arbitrary classifier. This distinction is crucial and should be clarified.

**Clarity and Methodological Justification:**

1.  **Ad-hoc Architectural Choices:** The paper introduces several non-standard components with weak justification. The feature filter in Eq. 7 is presented without much intuition. More critically, the output nonlinearity in Eq. 8 (`Ms = abs(C0) / (abs(C0) + abs(C1))`) is highly unusual. The justification that "sigmoid and tanh nonlinearities did not optimise properly" is insufficient for a top-tier publication. Why was this specific form chosen over others, such as a 1x1 convolution followed by a single sigmoid, or a softmax? This feels like an arbitrary engineering choice that lacks principled reasoning.
2.  **Missing Hyperparameter Details and Analysis:** The objective function relies on four crucial hyperparameters (`λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4`). Their values are simply stated without any justification, ablation study, or sensitivity analysis. The performance of the model is likely highly dependent on the balance between these terms, and this is left entirely unexplored.
3.  **Insufficient Detail for Reproducibility:** Several key details of the training process are vague. For the evidence removal function `φ`, the parameters for the "random colour image with the addition of a Gaussian noise" are not specified. It is also unclear how the class embedding `C` in the feature filter is initialized and optimized.

**Presentation and Formatting:**

1.  **Sloppy Referencing:** The bibliography is inconsistent and contains numerous formatting errors (e.g., dangling commas, incomplete entries). This suggests a lack of attention to detail in the manuscript's preparation.
2.  **Submission Formatting:** The presence of a conference header ("st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)...") on the first page is inappropriate for an anonymized submission and is another sign of carelessness.

### Decision

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea of training a predictive model for saliency maps is novel and addresses a significant limitation (speed) of high-quality saliency methods.
*   The reported results on weakly supervised localization are state-of-the-art, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The paper has the potential to open up new applications in real-time interpretability.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   There appear to be fundamental errors in the mathematical formulation of both the objective function and the new saliency metric, which severely undermines the technical soundness of the paper.
*   The justification for key methodological and architectural choices is weak or absent, making the work feel more like an engineering report than a scientific paper.
*   Crucial details required for reproducibility are missing.
*   The repeated misleading use of the term "model-agnostic" and the overall sloppiness in presentation detract from the paper's quality.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**

While the paper is based on a strong and promising idea, the execution is critically flawed. The errors in the core objective function and the evaluation metric are too significant to overlook. The work requires a major revision to correct these technical inaccuracies, provide proper justification for its design choices, and improve its clarity and presentation before it can be considered for publication.
