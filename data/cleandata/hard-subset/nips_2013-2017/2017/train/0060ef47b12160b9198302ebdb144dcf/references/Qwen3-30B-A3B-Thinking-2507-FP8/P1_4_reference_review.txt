

# Peer Review: Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection for black box classifiers that achieves real-time performance through a trainable masking model. Instead of using iterative methods that require hundreds of forward passes to generate a saliency map, the authors train a U-Net-based model to predict saliency masks in a single forward pass. The key contributions include: (1) a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept that better evaluates interpretability than traditional localization accuracy; (2) a careful treatment of "introduced evidence" when manipulating images for saliency detection; and (3) a model-agnostic approach that works with any black box classifier. The paper demonstrates that their method achieves state-of-the-art results on ImageNet for weakly supervised object localization while being orders of magnitude faster than existing methods.

The work builds on recent advances in saliency detection (e.g., [2, 1, 11, 17]) but makes a significant departure from the iterative approaches by proposing a model-based solution. It relates most closely to the concurrent work by Fong and Vedaldi (2017) which also proposes a black-box saliency method, though the authors claim their approach is faster and produces higher quality saliency maps.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Impact**: The paper addresses a critical bottleneck in interpretability research—real-time saliency detection. The ability to generate saliency maps at 100+ per second (vs. hundreds of iterations per map for iterative methods) opens up new applications like video saliency and real-time model debugging.

2. **Novel Saliency Metric**: The proposed saliency metric s(a,p) is a meaningful contribution that better aligns with the theoretical definition of saliency as the smallest sufficient region. It provides a more interpretable evaluation method than standard localization accuracy, as demonstrated by the paper's results showing their method performs nearly as well as ground truth bounding boxes.

3. **Rigorous Treatment of Artifacts**: The paper's thoughtful discussion of "introduced evidence" when manipulating images for saliency detection is a significant strength. The authors propose multiple techniques (randomized evidence removal, TV penalty) to prevent adversarial artifacts, which is a critical issue often overlooked in prior work.

4. **Strong Experimental Results**: The method achieves competitive results on weakly supervised object localization (36.7% error rate on ImageNet, comparable to fully supervised approaches) and outperforms baselines on the new saliency metric. The CIFAR-10 experiments demonstrate the approach generalizes to different datasets and doesn't require pre-trained models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Recent Work**: The paper acknowledges Fong and Vedaldi's concurrent work but doesn't sufficiently contrast their approach. Both methods are optimization-based and black-box, with the main differences being the authors' use of a trained model versus iterative optimization. The paper should more clearly articulate why their approach is superior (beyond just speed) and provide a direct comparison on the same metrics.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks theoretical analysis to explain why the masking model approach works better than iterative methods. Why does training a model produce better saliency maps than direct optimization? This is a significant gap in the paper.

3. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is limited to ImageNet and CIFAR-10. More diverse datasets (e.g., with different object classes, image complexities) would strengthen the claims. Additionally, the paper doesn't provide qualitative analysis of failure cases or limitations of the method.

4. **Implementation Details**: The paper could provide more details about the training procedure, hyperparameters, and computational resources used. While the paper mentions "100+ masks per second," it doesn't specify the exact hardware configuration, making it difficult to assess the claimed speed.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of model interpretability by enabling real-time saliency detection. The new saliency metric is particularly valuable for evaluating the quality of explanations, and the paper's careful treatment of introduced evidence is a notable strength.

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Provides a practical solution to a real bottleneck in model interpretability
- Introduces a meaningful new metric for evaluating saliency maps
- Demonstrates strong empirical results across two datasets
- Addresses a critical issue (introduced evidence) often overlooked in saliency work
- Enables new applications like video saliency

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- Insufficient differentiation from concurrent work (Fong & Vedaldi 2017)
- Lacks theoretical justification for the approach
- Limited evaluation scope
- Some implementation details are missing

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of model interpretability with clear practical implications. While the differentiation from concurrent work needs strengthening, the experimental results are compelling and the new saliency metric is a significant contribution. The authors should address the following points before final acceptance:

1. More clearly differentiate their approach from Fong and Vedaldi (2017) in terms of both methodology and results.
2. Provide more theoretical justification for why the masking model approach produces better saliency maps.
3. Include a brief discussion of limitations and failure cases.
4. Add more implementation details about training procedure and computational resources.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in machine learning interpretability. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way through both empirical results and a new evaluation metric. The real-time capability is particularly noteworthy for practical applications.
