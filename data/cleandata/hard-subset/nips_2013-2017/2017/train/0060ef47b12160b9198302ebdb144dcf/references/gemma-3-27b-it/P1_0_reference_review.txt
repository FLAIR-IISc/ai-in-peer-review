## NIPS 2017 Review: Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers. Unlike iterative methods which are computationally expensive, the authors propose training a model (a U-Net architecture with a ResNet backbone) to directly predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. This allows for real-time saliency generation. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric, based on the Smallest Sufficient/Destroying Region (SSR/SDR) concept, designed to better evaluate the interpretability of saliency maps.  Experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 demonstrate the speed and accuracy of the proposed method, outperforming existing techniques in both localization accuracy and the newly proposed saliency metric.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in image saliency and explainable AI. It acknowledges and contrasts itself with earlier gradient-based methods (e.g., [11, 12, 16]) which, while fast, suffer from quality issues. It differentiates itself from iterative patch removal techniques [17, 1, 2] by focusing on a learned, non-iterative solution for speed. The work is most closely related to [2] (Fong & Vedaldi) which also adopts a black-box approach, but the authors improve upon this by proposing a learned model instead of iterative optimization and introducing a more refined saliency metric.  The use of a U-Net architecture for this task is a sensible choice given its established success in image segmentation.

**Strengths:**

* **Speed:** The primary strength is the significant speed improvement over iterative methods. Achieving real-time saliency detection opens up possibilities for applications like video analysis.
* **Accuracy & Quality:** The proposed method achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, performance on established benchmarks (ImageNet localization) and the newly proposed saliency metric. The visualizations in Figure 5 demonstrate visually compelling and seemingly accurate saliency maps.
* **Novel Saliency Metric:** The SSR/SDR-based metric (Equation 3) is a valuable contribution.  It addresses the limitations of purely localization-based metrics by considering the *informativeness* of the salient region, not just its spatial extent.
* **Comprehensive Approach:** The paper is thorough in addressing potential issues, such as the introduction of adversarial artifacts during evidence removal (Section 3.1), and proposes mitigation strategies.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the methodology and results. The inclusion of an appendix with further examples is helpful.
* **Demonstrated Generalization:** The successful application of the method to CIFAR-10, even when training the model from scratch, demonstrates its potential for generalization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions several parameters (e.g., in the objective function) but lacks a detailed analysis of their sensitivity.  A more robust investigation into the impact of these parameters would strengthen the results.
* **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Equation 4) is somewhat complex with multiple terms and regularizers. The justification for the specific weights assigned to each term (1, 2, 3, 4) could be further elaborated.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While the CIFAR-10 experiment shows training from scratch is possible, a more detailed ablation study, perhaps on ImageNet, investigating the impact of the pre-trained ResNet encoder versus training from scratch, would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost of Training:**  Although inference is fast, the training process itself is likely to be computationally intensive. The paper doesn’t provide details on training time or resource requirements.
* **Metric Limitations:** The saliency metric, while an improvement, relies on resizing the cropped region to a fixed size, potentially distorting aspect ratio.  The authors acknowledge this but could explore alternative approaches to address this.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem (explainability of deep learning models) with a novel and effective approach.
* The proposed method significantly improves the speed of saliency detection without sacrificing accuracy.
* The introduced saliency metric provides a more nuanced evaluation of saliency maps.
* The experiments are well-conducted and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on multiple datasets.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The hyperparameter sensitivity and objective function complexity require further investigation.
* A more comprehensive ablation study would strengthen the conclusions.
* The training cost is not explicitly addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of explainable AI. The proposed method offers a significant improvement in the speed of saliency detection while maintaining competitive accuracy. The novel saliency metric is a valuable addition to the toolkit for evaluating saliency maps.  While there are some minor weaknesses regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and ablation studies, these do not fundamentally detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** I believe this paper represents a significant advance in the field and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.  I would encourage the authors to address the minor weaknesses identified in the review through further experimentation and analysis if possible, but I do not believe these are critical for acceptance.
