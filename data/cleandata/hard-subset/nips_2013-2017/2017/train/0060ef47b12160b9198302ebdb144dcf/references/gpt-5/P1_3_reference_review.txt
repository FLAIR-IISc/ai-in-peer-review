Summary
The paper proposes a trainable, model-based saliency detector that produces class-conditional saliency maps for images in a single forward pass. Instead of iterative perturbation per image, the authors train a U-Net-like “masking model” to output a mask M that simultaneously (i) preserves the target class prediction when applied to the image (SSR-like objective) and (ii) destroys the prediction when applied complementarily (SDR-like objective), while encouraging smoothness and small area. To mitigate “introduced evidence” when masking, they replace masked pixels with randomized alternatives (strong blur or noisy constant color) and add TV regularization to avoid adversarial artifacts. They also propose a new saliency metric intended to capture the smallest sufficient region (SSR): s(a, p) = log(ã)·log(p) where a is the area of the tightest rectangular crop covering the salient region and p is the classifier’s class probability on the resized crop; lower is better. The masking network uses a ResNet-50 encoder (frozen) and a U-Net decoder, with a class-embedding “feature filter” at the coarsest scale. The model is trained per black-box classifier (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) on ImageNet, and qualitatively produces sharper maps than gradient-based and iterative methods. Quantitatively, it improves weakly-supervised localization error and outperforms baselines under the new SSR metric. A CIFAR-10 experiment (no pretrained encoder) yields interpretable maps, suggesting the approach does not rely on a pretrained encoder.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against: gradient-based saliency (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), which are fast but often noisy and hard to interpret; iterative perturbation methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, Meaningful Perturbations; Cao et al.), which optimize masks per image but are slow and can introduce artifacts. The paper’s main novelty is to amortize the per-image optimization into a learned model that predicts masks in real time, while combining both SSR and SDR objectives and randomizing the replacement content to reduce overfitting to a particular masking scheme. The proposed objective and training heuristics (fake labels to enforce class conditioning, random A) are sensible and align with observations in recent interpretability work.

Strengths
- Conceptual: Clear motivation to combine SSR and SDR goals in a single objective; thoughtful discussion and mitigation of introduced-evidence artifacts.
- Method: A practical architecture that incorporates class conditioning via a class-embedding gate at the coarsest scale and multi-scale refinement via U-Net. Training tricks (fake labels, random A) address common failure modes.
- Efficiency: Orders-of-magnitude faster inference than iterative methods; real-time saliency enables new applications (e.g., video).
- Empirics: Improved weakly supervised localization error on ImageNet versus several baselines; a simple new metric aligned with SSR intuition shows favorable results; qualitative maps look sharp and class-specific. CIFAR-10 experiment suggests the approach does not require a pretrained encoder.
- Clarity of motivation and approach, with practical details reported (hyperparameters, speed).

Weaknesses
- “Black-box” claim: The approach requires backprop through the classifier during training (at least to the input), so it is not black-box in the strict, gradient-free sense. The paper should clarify the assumption (any differentiable, frozen classifier).
- Objective notation/signs: Eq. (4) appears inconsistent with the stated intent (encouraging recognition on preserved region). As written with a positive log term, minimizing would decrease the class probability; likely the correct term is −λ3 log fc(Φ(X,M)). Please fix signs and exponents for reproducibility.
- Evaluation details: The localization results are promising, but tables/values are not fully specified here (protocol, splits, variance). Fairness of comparisons (e.g., “fully supervised VGG” vs weakly supervised here) should be discussed more carefully.
- New saliency metric: While intuitive, it relies on classifier invariance to scale/aspect and uses rectangular crops that can include spurious context. Sensitivity analyses (e.g., to aspect ratio, alternative cropping, per-class behavior) would strengthen the claim.
- Ablations missing: No quantitative ablations of loss terms (SSR vs SDR vs TV vs area), fake-label probability, random A choices, freezing vs training the encoder, mask resolution/upsampling. These would help validate design choices and robustness.
- Scope/generalization: The masking network is trained separately per classifier; no study of cross-classifier transfer or multi-classifier training. It would be useful to know whether one masking model can generalize across architectures, or the degree to which it captures classifier-specific biases.
- Comparisons to concurrent [Fong & Vedaldi] are mostly qualitative; a more controlled quantitative comparison (with matched replacement schemes and budgets) would clarify advantages beyond speed.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with thoughtful mitigations for known pitfalls. The core loss and architecture are reasonable, and the training heuristics are well-motivated. However, the sign/notation issues in the loss should be corrected, and the evaluation would benefit from ablations and more rigorous, fully specified comparisons.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The discussion of introduced evidence is particularly helpful. Please clarify the “black-box” assumption, correct Eq. (4), and provide more precise evaluation protocol details (datasets/splits, thresholds, metrics, CIs).

Originality
Amortizing perturbation-based saliency into a learned predictor with combined SSR/SDR objectives and randomized replacement is novel in this space (as of 2017). The class-embedding gating and training tricks are interesting and likely to be adopted.

Significance
High practical significance: real-time, class-conditional saliency for standard classifiers, with improved localization and a useful metric. This is likely to be used and extended (e.g., to video, segmentation), and addresses a known limitation of iterative methods.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify whether gradients through the classifier are required during training, and whether the method works with non-differentiable or API-only models.
- Provide ablations: remove SSR/SDR terms, vary λs, TV, random A, fake-label probability, encoder freezing. How do these affect localization and the SSR metric?
- Can a single masking model be trained to work across multiple classifiers? How well does it transfer?
- How sensitive is the SSR metric to the crop-resize policy and classifier calibration?
- What is total training time, and how does the method scale with image size and number of classes?

Pros
- Fast, accurate, and practical saliency with strong qualitative and quantitative results.
- Well-motivated objective combining SSR and SDR; good handling of introduced evidence.
- Useful new metric for SSR; real-time applicability; clear path to extensions.

Cons
- Not truly black-box; clarity issues around objective signs.
- Limited ablations and incomplete evaluation details; metric sensitivity not explored.
- Per-classifier training required; limited analysis of generalization/transfer.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a promising and practically important approach with solid ideas and encouraging results. With clarifications and a few additional experiments/ablations, it would be a strong contribution.

Confidence
High on methodology assessment; moderate on empirical claims pending full tables/protocol details.
