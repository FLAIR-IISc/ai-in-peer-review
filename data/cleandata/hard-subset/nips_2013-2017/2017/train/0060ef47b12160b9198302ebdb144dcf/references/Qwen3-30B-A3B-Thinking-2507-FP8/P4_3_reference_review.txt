

# Review: Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection that operates in real-time for black box classifiers. The authors address the limitation of iterative saliency methods (e.g., [1, 2, 17]) which require hundreds of iterations per image and are too slow for practical applications. Instead of optimizing saliency maps iteratively, they train a masking model (based on U-Net architecture) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. The paper introduces a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which better captures the interpretability of saliency maps compared to standard localization accuracy. The authors also address the problem of introduced evidence during saliency computation by proposing a randomized evidence removal scheme with smoothness constraints.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on saliency detection, including gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative patch removal [17], and recent optimization-based approaches [2]. The authors position their work as distinct from [2] by training a model to predict saliency maps rather than optimizing them iteratively for each image, enabling real-time operation.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-defined objective function (equation 4) that addresses multiple aspects of saliency quality: mask smoothness (TV penalty), small region size (area penalty), classification confidence (SSR), and destruction of class probability (SDR). The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem of introduced evidence during saliency computation and propose a practical solution with randomized evidence removal.

2. **Novelty**: The core contribution of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than optimizing them iteratively for each image is novel and well-motivated. The new saliency metric (equation 3) that evaluates the smallest sufficient region is also a significant contribution, as it better aligns with the interpretability goals of saliency maps than standard localization metrics.

3. **Significance**: The paper demonstrates a 100x speedup over iterative methods (100 saliency masks per second) while achieving better localization accuracy (36.7% error on ImageNet) than previous weakly supervised approaches. This enables new applications like video-saliency, which is particularly relevant for real-world systems like autonomous vehicles.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized with clear explanations of the motivation, methodology, and evaluation. The masking model architecture is described in sufficient detail, and the new saliency metric is well-justified with theoretical grounding.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Parallel Work**: The authors mention that [2] was developed in parallel to their work, but the paper lacks a direct comparison with this method. Since [2] also proposes a model-agnostic optimization-based approach, a thorough comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of Bias Analysis**: The paper acknowledges potential biases in the masking model but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of these biases. The claim that "the model does not seem to influence the model performance in finding biases in other black boxes" lacks empirical support.

3. **Implementation Details**: While the masking model architecture is described, the paper doesn't provide sufficient detail on training hyperparameters, computational resources, or training time. This makes it difficult to assess the practical feasibility of the approach.

4. **Limited Evaluation of Metric**: The paper introduces a new metric but doesn't thoroughly analyze how it correlates with human perception of saliency. A qualitative analysis with human subjects would strengthen the claim that the metric better captures interpretability.

## Pro/Con for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel approach to real-time saliency detection with clear advantages over iterative methods
- Well-motivated and theoretically grounded new saliency metric
- Strong empirical results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 with competitive localization accuracy
- Practical significance for real-world applications requiring real-time explanations

**Con:**
- Limited comparison with the parallel work [2] that addresses the same problem
- Insufficient analysis of potential biases introduced by the masking model
- Lack of detailed implementation and training information
- Limited evaluation of the new metric beyond quantitative results

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of model interpretability by presenting a novel, real-time approach to saliency detection for black box classifiers. The technical approach is sound, with a well-justified objective function and a clear demonstration of the method's advantages over existing techniques. The new saliency metric is a valuable addition to the evaluation toolkit for saliency methods.

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance. While there are some weaknesses in the comparison with related work and analysis of potential biases, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper's novelty lies in its model-based approach to saliency detection, which enables real-time operation while maintaining high quality.

I recommend acceptance of this paper as it makes a meaningful contribution to the field of model interpretability with clear practical implications. The work advances the state-of-the-art in saliency detection by addressing the critical need for real-time explanations in practical applications.
