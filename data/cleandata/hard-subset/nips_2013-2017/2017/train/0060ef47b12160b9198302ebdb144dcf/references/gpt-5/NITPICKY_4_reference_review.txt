Summary
The paper proposes a trainable, class-conditional masking network that produces saliency maps for a fixed image classifier in a single forward pass. The core idea is to amortize perturbation-based saliency optimization: instead of iteratively solving for a mask per image (as in Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), a U-Net–style model with a ResNet-50 encoder is trained to minimize a composite loss combining (i) total variation for smoothness, (ii) area penalty, (iii) preservation of the target class score on the preserved region (SSR-like), and (iv) suppression of the score when the salient region is removed (SDR-like). To mitigate artifacts, the masked-out content is replaced with a randomized “alternative image” (strong blur or random color + noise), and the loss occasionally uses fake labels to enforce conditioning on the class embedding. The authors evaluate on ImageNet using AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 as black boxes, claim better weakly supervised localization accuracy and introduce a new “saliency metric” based on the product of log area and log class probability on a tight crop. They also show a small CIFAR-10 experiment with a randomly initialized encoder.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on perturbation-based explanations (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), excision/occlusion methods, and backprop/activation mapping (Zeiler & Fergus, Simonyan et al., Grad/Guided Backprop, CAM/Excitation Backprop). The main novelty is the amortized predictor that learns to output high-quality masks quickly, and a composite objective attempting to unify SSR and SDR in one training signal.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: amortizing perturbation-based saliency into a learned predictor is appealing, enabling real-time explanations and video use-cases.
- Thoughtful handling of “introduced evidence”: randomized replacement A, smoothness via TV, and downsample/upsample design.
- Class-conditional mechanism via a learned embedding and a simple feature filter; practical trick of using fake labels to force conditioning.
- Broad evaluation across three standard classifiers; real-time throughput and a CIFAR-10 variant suggest some generality.
- Attempt to formalize a saliency metric tied to SSR and to avoid masking artifacts by cropping.

Weaknesses (technical and presentation; many are small but numerous and consequential)
- Objective function is ambiguously and inconsistently specified. Equation (4) contains sign/notation errors: “+ log(fc(·))” would increase the loss for higher probabilities, contradicting the stated aim; the last term “3fc(·) 4” is syntactically unclear (is 4 a coefficient or an exponent? The text suggests an exponent < 1, which is counterintuitive for penalizing nonzero scores). The operator/function symbol used in fc((X,M)) is not defined (presumably φ(X,M)). These issues prevent reproduction.
- Masking equations (1), (2), and (6) omit the elementwise product symbol; parentheses and spacing are inconsistent, compounding ambiguity.
- “Model-agnostic/black-box” claim is misleading. Training requires backpropagating through the classifier (differentiable access), so the method is not black-box in the standard sense (query-only). Please clarify the scope (“architecture-agnostic; classifier frozen but differentiable”).
- New saliency metric s(a,p)=log(ã)·log(p) is ad hoc, not clearly justified by information theory, and numerically fragile as p→0. The rectangular tight crop with aspect-ratio-agnostic resizing biases the metric toward scale/aspect-invariant classifiers; sensitivity analysis is missing.
- Mismatch between training loss (masking with randomized A) and metric (cropping) is unaddressed; ablations quantifying the effect of replacement strategies A on final masks and on the metric are absent.
- Empirical evidence is not sufficiently documented in the main text. Claims of outperforming prior work rely on tables/appendix not visible here; crucial details (exact numbers, dataset splits, statistical variability) are lacking.
- Important training details are missing: optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, number of steps/epochs for ImageNet models, data augmentation, early stopping. No ablations on λ weights, fake-label probability, blur σ, mask resolution, threshold choice for 0.5, or the necessity of freezing the encoder.
- The “feature filter” (Eq. 7) and class-embedding training are underspecified; the suggested auxiliary objective to push dot products high/low is not formalized.
- The mask is produced at 1/4 resolution and bilinearly upsampled; no analysis of the effect on sharpness or localization. Threshold 0.5 is arbitrary; no calibration or sensitivity study.
- CIFAR-10 results are qualitative only; no quantitative localization or metric reported.
- References are severely inconsistent and incomplete (misnumbering, missing venues/years, incorrect mapping of methods to citations; e.g., Guided Backprop is misreferenced; “transposed convolution [15]” points to a different paper). This undermines clarity and scholarship.
- Formatting and proofreading issues: “st Conference…” artifact, missing symbols, inconsistent equation numbering/spacing, undefined notation.

Quality
While the high-level idea is sound and potentially useful, the paper’s technical presentation has significant ambiguities that hinder verification. The experimental section, as presented, lacks enough quantitative detail for a rigorous assessment.

Clarity
The exposition is generally readable but marred by numerous notation, citation, and formatting errors, and by missing training details and ablations.

Originality
Moderately original: amortized prediction of perturbation-style masks is a reasonable extension beyond iterative optimization, but conceptually close to existing perturbation-based and weakly supervised localization lines.

Significance
Potentially impactful if made rigorous and reproducible; real-time saliency is valuable. As written, the contribution is weakened by unclear objectives and incomplete evaluation.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical and timely idea with plausible speed and quality advantages.
- Simple architecture and training tricks likely to be adopted if properly specified.
- Attempts to unify SSR and SDR in a single objective.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguous/incorrect loss specification; non-reproducible.
- Overstated black-box claims.
- Weakly justified metric; limited quantitative evidence and missing ablations.
- Poor citation hygiene and multiple presentation errors.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and precisely define the objective (full formula with signs, coefficients/exponents, and φ), and provide ablations of λ’s and A choices.
- Clarify the access model to the classifier and temper “black-box” language.
- Provide complete experimental details and release code.
- Compare against CAM/Grad-CAM/Guided Backprop/Excitation Backprop and perturbation baselines with standardized metrics; include sensitivity to mask threshold and resolution.
- Rigorously analyze the proposed metric (stability, sensitivity, correlation with human judgment).
- Fix references and notation thoroughly.

Recommendation
Reject (as is). The idea is promising, but the accumulation of technical ambiguities and presentation issues prevents a confident, reproducible assessment. With a cleaned-up objective, stronger quantitative evaluation, and corrected scholarship, this work could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
