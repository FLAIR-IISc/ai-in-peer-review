

# Review: Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection that can generate high-quality saliency maps in real-time (100+ maps per second) for black-box image classifiers. The authors train a masking model (based on U-Net architecture) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, rather than using iterative methods that require hundreds of operations per image. The key contributions include a new objective function that balances smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR) concepts, a new saliency metric based on the tightest crop that maintains classification confidence, and thorough experimental validation on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets. The work builds on but significantly extends prior saliency methods like the iterative patch removal approach of Zhou et al. [17] and the black-box optimization method of Fong and Vedaldi [2], which were developed in parallel to this work.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their objective function (equation 4) and introduce a new metric (equation 3) that addresses limitations of existing saliency evaluation methods. The experiments are comprehensive, showing clear improvements over baselines on both localisation accuracy (36.7% error rate for ResNet-50 trained model) and their new saliency metric. The implementation details are sufficient for reproduction, including model architecture, training parameters, and evaluation protocols. However, the paper would benefit from more specific details about the training process (e.g., learning rate, batch size, number of epochs) and a more thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art saliency methods beyond those mentioned.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The motivation for the problem is compelling, and the explanations of the SSR/SDR concepts and new metric are particularly clear. The figures effectively illustrate the concepts (e.g., Figure 1 showing saliency maps, Figure 5 comparing methods). The writing is precise, and the contributions are well-defined. One minor weakness is that the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate from the concurrent work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] - it mentions this work but doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs or why their training-based method is superior to the iterative optimization approach.

## Originality

The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than using iterative optimization is novel and represents a significant step beyond current state-of-the-art methods. The paper effectively distinguishes its approach from gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] and iterative patch removal [17]. The new metric is also a meaningful contribution to the field, as it better captures the interpretability of saliency maps than existing localisation accuracy metrics. The paper's focus on real-time application is particularly valuable for practical deployment. The work is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on saliency, such as those by Zhou et al. [17] and Simonyan et al. [10], which relied on iterative approaches.

## Significance

This work addresses a critical need in explainable AI - understanding what classifiers are actually looking at, rather than just how well they perform. The real-time capability enables new applications like video-saliency for autonomous vehicles, which is a significant practical contribution. The method outperforms existing approaches on standard benchmarks, and the new metric provides a better way to evaluate saliency explanations. The paper demonstrates that a model-based approach can produce both higher quality saliency maps and significantly faster inference than iterative methods, which is a meaningful advancement in the field.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Proposes a fundamentally different approach to saliency detection that enables real-time application
- Introduces a new, well-motivated metric that better assesses saliency map quality
- Demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods on standard benchmarks
- Thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets and classifiers
- Practical implementation details showing real-world feasibility

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient differentiation from concurrent work by Fong and Vedaldi [2]
- Lacks detailed training parameters and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis
- Does not fully explore limitations of the approach (e.g., performance on specific image categories)
- The claim that their method outperforms "existing saliency detectors" is not sufficiently supported by comparison to all relevant prior work

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of explainable AI. The real-time capability and improved quality of saliency maps address a clear need in the community. While the paper would benefit from more detailed comparison with the concurrent work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] and additional training details, these are minor issues for a high-quality technical contribution. The paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level, with particular strength in originality and significance.

I recommend acceptance. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses a meaningful problem in AI with practical implications. The real-time capability opens new application avenues, and the new metric provides a valuable tool for evaluating saliency methods. This paper represents a clear advancement over existing approaches and will be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on explainable AI.
