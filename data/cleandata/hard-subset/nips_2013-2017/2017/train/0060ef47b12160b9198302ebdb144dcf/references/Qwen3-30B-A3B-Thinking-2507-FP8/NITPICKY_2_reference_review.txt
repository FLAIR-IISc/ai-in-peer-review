

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a new approach for real-time saliency detection by training a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, rather than using iterative methods. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region concept and claim their method outperforms existing approaches in speed and quality.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important problem in model interpretability with practical applications.
- The proposed metric (equation 3) is theoretically grounded and provides a more nuanced evaluation than standard localisation accuracy.
- The method is genuinely faster than iterative approaches (100+ images/second), enabling real-time applications.
- The weakly supervised training approach is clever and avoids the need for saliency annotations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical citation oversight:** The paper references [2] as "very recently (and in parallel to this work)" but fails to provide any citation details for this parallel work, making it impossible to verify the claim. The authors should either provide the full citation or clarify that this work was submitted simultaneously but published later.

2. **Mathematical errors:** Equation (4) contains a critical typo: "4" should be "λ4" (or similar), and the text incorrectly states "Setting 4 to a value smaller than 1" instead of "Setting λ4 to a value smaller than 1." This is a significant error that would confuse readers.

3. **Inconsistent terminology:** The paper claims to be "model agnostic" but uses the gradient-based approach in their objective function (equation 4), which requires differentiability. This contradicts the claim and should be clarified.

4. **Missing implementation details:** The paper claims "The mask Ms has resolution four times lower than the input image" but doesn't explain why it's four times lower instead of 32 times lower (matching the ResNet-50 feature map downsampling). This is a critical architectural detail.

5. **Vague computational claims:** The statement "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU" lacks specificity. Which GPU? What's the exact throughput? Without this, the "real-time" claim is unsubstantiated.

6. **Figure references:** The paper references "figure 3" and "figure 4" but these figures aren't included in the provided text, making it impossible to assess their claims about adversarial artifacts and model architecture.

7. **Metric interpretation:** The paper states "s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p)" without explaining why this is better than alternative formulations (e.g., log(p/a)). The theoretical justification is insufficient.

8. **Statistical reporting:** Tables 1 and 2 report error rates without standard deviations, making it impossible to assess statistical significance of the improvements.

9. **Adversarial example oversight:** The paper discusses adversarial artifacts but fails to cite Szegedy et al.'s seminal work on adversarial examples (ICLR 2014), which is relevant to their claims.

10. **Training details:** The paper states "we use a Gaussian blur with = 10" but doesn't specify what "10" refers to (sigma value?).

**Additional concerns:**
- The claim that "the model itself acts as some sort of a 'regulariser'" is not supported by evidence or analysis.
- The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each term in the objective function.
- The CIFAR-10 experiment claims the masking model "does not need to rely on a pre-trained model" but doesn't explain how the model's performance compares to using a pre-trained ResNet encoder.

**Overall assessment:**
While the paper presents a novel approach to real-time saliency detection with potentially useful applications, the numerous minor technical errors, missing details, and inconsistent claims undermine the paper's credibility. The mathematical errors (equation 4 typo, inconsistent terminology) are particularly concerning as they suggest the authors may have overlooked fundamental aspects of their own method. The lack of proper citations to related work (especially [2] and Szegedy et al.) further diminishes the paper's contribution.

The paper's strengths in the problem statement and proposed metric are overshadowed by these technical inconsistencies. As a reviewer, I find the paper's claims about performance and novelty difficult to verify due to the missing details and errors. The paper would benefit from significant revision to address these issues before publication.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies that prevent a fair assessment of its contribution, and the errors are substantial enough to question the validity of the results. While the problem is important, the execution falls short of NIPS standards.
