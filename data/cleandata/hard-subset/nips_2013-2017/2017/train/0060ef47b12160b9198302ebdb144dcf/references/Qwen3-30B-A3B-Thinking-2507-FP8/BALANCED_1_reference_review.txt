

This paper proposes a model-based approach for real-time saliency detection on black-box classifiers, training a masking model (U-Net variant) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, avoiding slow iterative methods. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept and claim superior speed, accuracy, and interpretability over existing methods. The work is evaluated on ImageNet (using AlexNet/GoogleNet/ResNet-50 as black boxes) and CIFAR-10.

**Strengths**  
*Quality & Significance*: The paper addresses a meaningful problem—real-time saliency for black-box models—with a technically sound approach. The objective function (Eq. 4) properly balances mask smoothness (TV penalty), small region size (area penalty), and SSR/SDR performance. The new saliency metric (Section 3.2) is well-motivated and effectively captures interpretability better than localization accuracy alone. Experiments show competitive weakly supervised localization error (36.7% on ImageNet vs. 34.3% for supervised VGG) and strong results on the proposed metric (Table 3), outperforming baselines like "center box" and iterative methods. The CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates generalizability without pre-trained encoders, a notable strength.  
*Clarity*: The paper is well-organized with clear figures (e.g., Fig. 1, 5) illustrating saliency quality. The problem definition (SSR/SDR), metric rationale, and masking model architecture (Fig. 4) are effectively explained. The discussion of adversarial artifacts (Section 3.1) and mitigation strategies (e.g., randomized evidence removal) shows careful consideration of pitfalls.  
*Originality*: The core idea—training a model to predict saliency maps—represents a novel *application* of neural networks to accelerate existing saliency methods. While iterative approaches (e.g., [2]) exist, the paper’s model-agnostic, real-time training paradigm is distinct. The metric and evidence-removal techniques (Eq. 2) also offer fresh perspectives.

**Weaknesses**  
*Quality*: The "orders-of-magnitude faster" claim lacks concrete evidence. The paper states the model processes 100 images/sec on a GPU but omits comparisons to prior work (e.g., Grad-CAM [11] or [2]), which are also real-time (≈50-100ms/image). Without benchmarking, the speed advantage is unsubstantiated. The ablation on the masking model’s architecture (e.g., U-Net vs. other networks) is minimal, and the reliance on ResNet-50 pre-training (Section 4) may introduce biases not addressed. The metric’s assumption of resizing without aspect ratio (Section 3.2) could distort object representation (e.g., stretching a cat’s head), yet this is unaddressed.  
*Originality & Significance*: The contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The masking model is a straightforward adaptation of segmentation architectures (U-Net) to saliency, with no novel neural design. The performance gains (e.g., 2.4% localization error improvement over supervised VGG) are modest, and the "on par with ground truth" metric claim (Table 3) is misleading—ground truth boxes are optimal for localization, not SSR. The real-time application (e.g., video saliency) is promising but speculative without empirical validation.  
*Clarity*: The training process (Section 4.1) is opaque—e.g., "randomly generated A" lacks details on noise/color distributions. The "fake label" technique (30% probability) to prevent class-agnostic masking is clever but under-explained; how does it generalize beyond ImageNet? The comparison to [2] (Fong & Vedaldi) is superficial; the paper doesn’t clarify *why* a model-based approach outperforms their iterative method beyond speed.

**Decision**  
**Reject**. While the paper presents a technically competent method with a useful metric, the contributions are incremental and lack sufficient evidence to justify a NeurIPS acceptance. The speed claims are unsupported, performance gains are marginal, and the novelty is overstated relative to existing work (e.g., [2]). The significance is limited to a niche speed advantage without transformative impact on the field.

**Suggestions for Improvement**  
1. **Benchmark speed rigorously**: Compare inference time (ms/image) against [2], Grad-CAM, and other real-time methods on identical hardware. Quantify "orders-of-magnitude" explicitly.  
2. **Strengthen novelty**: Clearly differentiate from [2] by showing *why* the model-based approach generalizes better (e.g., via ablation on iterative vs. model-based optimization). Discuss limitations of the iterative method (e.g., overfitting) that the model avoids.  
3. **Refine the metric**: Address the aspect-ratio resizing issue in the metric (Section 3.2) or provide ablation on its impact. Validate the metric on datasets where aspect ratio matters (e.g., portraits).  
4. **Expand experiments**: Include a small-scale video saliency demo (as suggested in Future Work) to concretely demonstrate real-time utility. Add an ablation on the TV penalty (Section 3.1) to show its necessity.  
5. **Clarify training**: Detail the "random A" generation (e.g., Gaussian noise parameters) and explain how "fake labels" prevent overfitting beyond the current description.  

The paper’s core idea has potential, but it requires stronger evidence, clearer differentiation from prior work, and more compelling significance to meet NeurIPS standards. With the above improvements, it could be a strong contribution.
