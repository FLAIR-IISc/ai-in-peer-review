

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection that enables real-time generation of saliency maps for black box classifiers. The authors argue that existing methods are either too slow (iterative approaches) or produce low-quality explanations (gradient-based methods). Their solution trains a masking model (U-Net based) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, avoiding the need for iterative optimization. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which evaluates how small a region can be while still allowing confident classification. The approach is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, showing competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, the real-time capability of the approach (100+ saliency masks per second) is a practical advancement with clear applications in video analysis and real-time systems. Second, the new saliency metric (based on SSR) is well-motivated and addresses a limitation of standard localisation accuracy metrics. Third, the method is model-agnostic and works with any differentiable classifier, making it broadly applicable. The experimental results are thorough, with comparisons to multiple baselines on ImageNet and a successful CIFAR-10 validation. The masking model architecture is well-chosen (U-Net with ResNet encoder), and the implementation details are sufficiently clear.

## Weaknesses

The paper has several weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS. First, the novelty claim is overstated: the authors acknowledge that Fong and Vedaldi [2] developed a parallel approach, but don't thoroughly compare their method with this work. The differences between the two approaches aren't clearly articulated, and it's unclear how the proposed masking model offers significant advantages over the iterative optimization approach in [2].

Second, the paper lacks sufficient analysis of the method's limitations. For instance, the paper doesn't discuss when the method might fail (e.g., with certain classifier architectures or image classes), nor does it analyze the potential biases introduced by the masking model itself, despite mentioning this as a future research direction. The claim that "the quality of explanations produced by our model outperforms other popular saliency detectors and is on par with ground truth bounding boxes" is not sufficiently supported with error analysis or statistical significance testing.

Third, the paper doesn't adequately quantify the speed advantage claimed ("orders-of-magnitude faster"). The statement that the model can process "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second" is useful, but the paper doesn't compare this to the actual speed of iterative methods in a controlled setting.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Conduct a more thorough comparison with the parallel work [2], specifically analyzing the differences in approach and performance. The paper should clarify what makes their method superior to the iterative optimization approach.

2. Expand the analysis to include failure cases, limitations, and potential biases. A section on when the method might not work well would greatly strengthen the paper.

3. Provide more detailed speed comparisons with iterative methods (e.g., time per image for both approaches on the same hardware).

4. Include statistical significance tests for the performance claims, particularly for the localisation accuracy and saliency metric results.

5. Clarify the relationship between the new metric and existing metrics. How does the SSR-based metric differ from the existing saliency metrics used in the field?

## Recommendation

This paper presents a practical and well-executed approach to real-time saliency detection with promising results. However, the novelty claim is overstated, the limitations are not sufficiently addressed, and the paper lacks sufficient technical depth to be a strong contribution to NeurIPS. The work is valuable as a practical implementation of saliency detection, but it doesn't advance the state of the art in a way that would warrant acceptance at NeurIPS.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison with the parallel work [2], a deeper analysis of limitations and failure cases, and more precise quantitative comparisons of speed and performance. With these improvements, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Quality**: 7/10 - Technically sound but lacks depth in addressing limitations
**Clarity**: 8/10 - Well-written with clear methodology
**Originality**: 6/10 - Novel approach but not sufficiently distinct from parallel work
**Significance**: 7/10 - Practical contribution with real-world applications but limited theoretical advancement

Overall, this is a solid engineering contribution that would be better suited for a conference with a stronger focus on applied machine learning (like CVPR or ICCV) rather than NeurIPS, which emphasizes theoretical and methodological advances.
