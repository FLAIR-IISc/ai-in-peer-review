This paper proposes a learned, feed-forward saliency detection method as an alternative to iterative techniques. The core idea – training a model to directly predict saliency maps – is conceptually sound and addresses a key bottleneck in existing methods. However, while the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, its execution is marred by a concerning number of methodological and presentational flaws, ultimately leading to a recommendation for rejection.

The introduction adequately motivates the problem of understanding classifier behavior and the role of saliency maps. However, the discussion of “polar bear vs. snow” feels somewhat contrived and doesn’t strongly justify the need for the proposed approach beyond what is already established in the literature. The claim of “orders-of-magnitude faster” needs more rigorous quantification – merely stating it is insufficient.

The related work section is adequate, though somewhat disjointed. It lacks a critical synthesis of existing methods; it primarily *lists* approaches without clearly articulating their limitations beyond runtime. The parallel work of Fong & Vedaldi [2] is appropriately highlighted, but the authors fail to sufficiently differentiate their approach beyond simply claiming a more holistic objective function.

The introduction of the Smallest Sufficient/Destroying Region (SSR/SDR) concepts is reasonable, but the accompanying explanation feels underdeveloped. Figure 2 is helpful, but the qualitative observations lack supporting data. The discussion surrounding “introduced evidence” (section 3.1) is overly verbose and, frankly, introduces unnecessary complexity. While the concern about adversarial artifacts is valid, the proposed solutions – particularly equation (2) – appear ad-hoc and lack strong justification. The justification for choosing a blurred image ‘A’ feels weak. Why is a blurred version preferable to, say, the mean color? This section reads more like a brainstorming session than a carefully considered methodological contribution.

The proposed saliency metric (equation 3) is a novel attempt to address the limitations of localization accuracy, but its reliance on resizing the cropped region *disregarding aspect ratio* is a significant weakness. This introduces an uncontrolled variable that could artificially inflate performance. The interpretation through an information-theoretic lens feels tacked on and doesn’t add substantial value. The authors acknowledge the reliance on scale/aspect ratio invariance in the classifier, but don’t fully explore the implications.

The masking model architecture (section 4) is reasonably described, but lacks sufficient detail. The rationale for the specific number of channels in the upsampling blocks isn't explained. The justification for the non-standard nonlinearity (equation 8) is weak – “sigmoid and tanh… did not optimize properly” is not a sufficient explanation. The training optimizations (fake labels, random A) are presented as essential fixes, suggesting fundamental instability in the training process, which raises concerns.

The experimental results (section 5) are the weakest part of the paper. The ImageNet experiments are presented without sufficient detail. What specific hyperparameters were used? What were the training times? The claim of outperforming other methods in table 2 is underwhelming, given the described improvements in speed and quality. The baseline "Center" is surprisingly strong, suggesting that the proposed method's advantage isn’t substantial. The CIFAR-10 experiment, while demonstrating the generality of the approach, feels like an afterthought.

Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor issues: inconsistent notation (e.g., using both ‘X’ and ‘M’ to represent masks/images), grammatical errors, and a general lack of polish. Figure 1 is labeled as an “example” but doesn't clearly indicate *which* method generated the saliency map. Figure 3 lacks a clear visual explanation of the "adversarial artifacts". The lack of error bars or statistical significance testing in the results tables is concerning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Methodological flaws: The "introduced evidence" handling feels ad-hoc, the metric relies on a problematic resizing step, and training instabilities required multiple fixes.
*   Insufficient experimental detail: Lack of hyperparameter reporting, training times, and statistical analysis.
*   Modest results: The performance improvements over baselines are not compelling enough to justify the added complexity.
*   Poor presentation: Numerous minor issues with notation, grammar, and figure clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The concept of a learned saliency detector is innovative.
*   Potential Impact: A fast and accurate saliency method could enable new applications.



In conclusion, while the core idea of this paper is promising, the numerous methodological weaknesses, lack of rigorous experimentation, and presentational flaws outweigh its potential benefits. The paper requires substantial revisions and further validation before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection.
