

# Review: Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of real-time image saliency detection for black box classifiers. The authors propose a novel approach that trains a masking model (based on U-Net architecture) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, rather than using iterative methods that require hundreds of evaluations. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which better evaluates the interpretability of saliency maps than standard localisation accuracy metrics. The paper relates to previous work in saliency detection, including gradient-based methods (e.g., [11, 12, 16]), iterative patch removal approaches (e.g., [17]), and optimization-based methods (e.g., [2]). While Fong and Vedaldi [2] proposed a similar optimization-based approach, this paper differs in its model-based training approach that enables real-time performance.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, including the objective function (equation 4) that balances mask smoothness, region size, classification confidence on preserved regions, and classification confidence on removed regions. The masking model architecture is well-motivated, using ResNet-50 as an encoder and U-Net for upsampling to produce sharp saliency maps. The training process is carefully designed to prevent the model from ignoring class selectors (using fake labels) and to prevent overfitting to specific evidence removal schemes (using randomized alternative images). The experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 are thorough, with appropriate baselines including iterative methods and other saliency techniques. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the need to train a separate masking model for each black box classifier.

## Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem statement, motivation, and proposed method are well-explained with helpful figures (e.g., Figure 1 showing saliency maps, Figure 2 illustrating SSR and SDR concepts). The authors provide sufficient detail about their architecture, training process, and evaluation protocol for an expert to reproduce their results. The new saliency metric (equation 3) is well-justified and clearly explained. The writing is precise and technical without unnecessary jargon.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution by shifting from iterative to model-based saliency detection, enabling real-time performance. While related to Fong and Vedaldi [2], the authors' approach is fundamentally different in that it trains a model to predict saliency maps rather than performing iterative optimization for each image. This is a novel combination of familiar techniques (U-Net architecture, black box model explanation) that addresses a critical bottleneck in saliency detection. The paper clearly differentiates their work from previous approaches, particularly in terms of speed and quality of saliency maps. The new saliency metric is also a novel contribution that better evaluates interpretability than existing metrics.

## Significance
The proposed method has high significance for both research and practical applications. Real-time saliency detection opens up new applications such as video saliency for autonomous vehicles, where understanding classifier decisions in real-time is critical. The paper demonstrates strong performance on ImageNet (36.7% localisation error, comparable to fully supervised approaches) and CIFAR-10, showing the method's robustness across datasets. The new saliency metric provides a better way to evaluate explanation quality than existing metrics, which will benefit future research in model interpretability. The model-agnostic nature of the approach means it can be applied to any differentiable classifier, increasing its utility.

## Strengths
1. Proposes a fundamentally new approach to saliency detection that enables real-time performance (100+ images per second on standard GPU).
2. Introduces a new saliency metric that better evaluates interpretability than standard localisation accuracy.
3. Demonstrates strong performance on ImageNet localisation task without using any localisation training data.
4. Carefully addresses challenges like adversarial artifacts through innovative evidence removal techniques.
5. Provides a well-motivated, technically sound architecture and training process.

## Weaknesses
1. The method requires training a separate masking model for each black box classifier, which may be a limitation for applications with multiple classifiers.
2. While the paper claims "model agnostic" capability, the masking model relies on a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder, which might introduce biases (though they show it can be trained from scratch).
3. The evaluation is limited to ImageNet and CIFAR-10; more diverse datasets would strengthen the claims.
4. The paper mentions "real-time" but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of frame rates with other methods beyond the single GPU measurement.

## Pro and Con for Acceptance
**Pro:**
- Novel approach to real-time saliency detection with significant speed improvements over iterative methods
- Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks with a new, better-motivated metric
- Clear technical contribution with well-designed architecture and training process
- High practical significance for applications like autonomous vehicles

**Con:**
- Requires training a separate model for each black box classifier (potential scalability issue)
- Limited evaluation on diverse datasets beyond ImageNet and CIFAR-10
- The reliance on pre-trained ResNet might introduce biases that need more discussion

## Conclusion
This paper presents a technically sound, novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for black box classifiers. The authors make a significant contribution by shifting from iterative to model-based saliency detection, enabling real-time performance while maintaining high-quality saliency maps. The new saliency metric is well-motivated and provides a better way to evaluate explanation quality. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and addresses important challenges in the field. While there are some limitations regarding scalability and dataset diversity, the paper makes a strong contribution to the field of model interpretability. I recommend acceptance.
