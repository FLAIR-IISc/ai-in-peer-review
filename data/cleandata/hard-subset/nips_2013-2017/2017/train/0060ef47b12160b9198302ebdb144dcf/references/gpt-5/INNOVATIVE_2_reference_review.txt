Summary
The paper proposes a learned, class-conditional saliency generator that produces a mask in a single forward pass for a fixed image classifier. The core idea is to train a U-Net–style “masking model” against an objective that blends two desiderata: (i) smallest sufficient region (SSR) — preserving a compact region that maintains high class probability, and (ii) smallest destroying region (SDR) — removing a compact region that causes the probability to drop. To reduce adversarial artifacts from masking, the method employs total-variation regularization, downsample/upsample smoothing, and a randomized evidence-removal scheme that mixes heavy blur and random color+noise as the alternative image. The model is conditioned on a class embedding and trained with a “fake-label” trick to enforce class selectivity. The authors further introduce an SSR-inspired metric s(a,p) based on the log area of a tight crop and the log class probability on that crop. Experiments on ImageNet (with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 as target classifiers) show sharper maps and improved weakly supervised localization versus iterative or gradient-based baselines, at real-time speed. A CIFAR-10 variant trained from scratch yields interpretable maps as well.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against gradient-based explanations (Saliency, Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop) and iterative perturbation methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi’s meaningful perturbations; Cao et al. activation preservation). The contribution is to distill the iterative perturbation objective into a learned explainer network, combining SSR and SDR and achieving speed. This is close in spirit to learning to explain, rather than computing an explanation per input via optimization. CAM-like methods are cited, though a comparison to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ would be informative.

Strengths
- Clear, bold idea: train a saliency network to optimize an SSR+SDR objective, amortizing iterative search into a single pass.
- Real-time inference with strong qualitative sharpness; enables video explanations.
- Thoughtful handling of “introduced evidence” via random alternative images and smoothness constraints.
- Class conditioning and fake-label training are simple and effective to ensure specificity.
- New SSR-inspired metric, and competitive weakly supervised localization without using any localization labels.
- Demonstrates portability to CIFAR-10 with a smaller architecture trained from scratch.

Weaknesses
- “Black box” is somewhat misleading: training requires backprop through the fixed classifier (thus white-box access to a differentiable model). The conclusion clarifies “any differentiable classifier,” but the abstract/positioning could overclaim model-agnosticism.
- Faithfulness evaluation is limited. Beyond localization and the proposed metric (which depends on scale/aspect invariance and an arbitrary 5% area floor), the paper lacks standard causal metrics (deletion/insertion curves) and sanity checks. It would be reassuring to quantify: preserve-mask retains confidence; remove-mask reduces confidence; both compared to baselines, and to evaluate on a second, unseen classifier to test transfer/robustness.
- Per-classifier, per-dataset training cost is nontrivial (250k images). Discussion of generalization to a new target classifier or domain shift is limited.
- Metric details: Equation (3) appears to miss a minus sign (text suggests s = log(ã) − log(p), with lower better). Sensitivity to the 0.05 floor and aspect-ratio warping is not analyzed.
- Missing comparisons to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ and more recent backprop variants; tables are lightly described in the text and some numbers are absent here.
- Limited ablations: there is little analysis of the contributions of the four loss terms, TV strength, mask resolution, or the randomized A choices.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a sensible objective and training protocol. The artifact-mitigation discussion is good. The main concerns are (i) overclaiming black-box, and (ii) incomplete faithfulness evaluation and ablations. These are addressable.

Clarity
Well written and organized. Please fix Eq. (3) and clarify the “black box” claim (white-box during training, no access at inference). More explicit quantitative results in the main text (not just the appendix) would help, as would adding standard metrics and ablations.

Originality
High. The idea of amortizing perturbation-based saliency into a learned, class-conditional explainer with an SSR+SDR objective is a notable step beyond iterative methods. The random-A strategy and fake-label conditioning are practical innovations. The metric is a simple, interesting addition.

Significance
High. A fast, faithful explainer is valuable for interactive and video settings. If faithfulness holds broadly, this can influence how explanations are deployed in practice and spur further work on learned explanation models.

Questions and suggestions
- Please clarify access assumptions: are classifier gradients required during training? Can this handle nondifferentiable or API-only models?
- Provide deletion/insertion curves and “preserve/remove” tests vs. baselines; report cross-model tests (crop from your mask, evaluate a different classifier).
- Compare to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ quantitatively.
- Ablate the loss weights (λ1..λ4), mask resolution, and the two A choices; report sensitivity of the new metric to the 0.05 floor and to warp vs. letterbox resizing.
- Discuss behavior under misclassification: do masks explain the predicted class or the ground-truth class, and how does faithfulness change?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, compelling approach with real-time performance.
- Strong qualitative and promising quantitative results.
- Practical training tricks; clear path to impactful applications (video).
- Introduces a useful metric and framing (SSR/SDR) for saliency.

Cons for acceptance
- Overstated black-box claim; training needs gradients and per-classifier training.
- Limited faithfulness diagnostics and ablations; some missing comparisons.
- New metric’s assumptions and sensitivity not fully explored.

Recommendation
Accept (weak to medium). Despite some experimental gaps and wording issues, the central idea is novel and potentially impactful, and the presented results are encouraging. With added faithfulness evaluations and clearer claims, this could be a strong NeurIPS contribution.
