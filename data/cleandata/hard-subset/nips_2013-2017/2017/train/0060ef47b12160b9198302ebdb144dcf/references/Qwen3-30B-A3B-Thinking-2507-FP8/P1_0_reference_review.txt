

# Peer Review: "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection that operates in real-time, contrasting with existing iterative methods. The authors propose a trainable masking model that predicts saliency maps for any input image in a single feed-forward pass, rather than requiring hundreds of iterations as in previous methods. The key contributions include: (1) a new objective function for saliency detection that combines smoothness, small region size, classifier confidence on preserved regions, and low confidence after salient region removal; (2) a new saliency metric based on the Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) concept; and (3) a U-Net-based masking model that can be trained to produce high-quality saliency maps.

The paper relates well to recent work in interpretability, particularly Fong and Vedaldi's [2] work on optimizing saliency regions iteratively. The authors clearly position their work as addressing the speed limitations of such iterative approaches while maintaining or improving quality. The paper also builds on concepts from Zhou et al. [17] and Cao et al. [1] but differs in its model-based, non-iterative approach.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to saliency detection by shifting from iterative optimization to a trainable model. This is a significant departure from current methods and addresses a real bottleneck in the field.

2. **Quality of Experiments**: The authors conduct thorough experiments on ImageNet with three different black-box classifiers and include a CIFAR-10 validation. The evaluation using both standard localisation accuracy and their new saliency metric provides a comprehensive assessment.

3. **Novel Metric**: The proposed saliency metric based on the SSR concept is well-motivated and addresses a limitation of existing metrics (which conflate saliency with localisation). The results showing their method's performance being on par with ground truth bounding boxes is particularly compelling.

4. **Practical Impact**: The paper demonstrates real-time capability (100 saliency masks per second), which opens up applications like video-saliency for autonomous vehicles - a significant practical contribution.

5. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of concepts, figures that effectively illustrate key ideas, and a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges some challenges (e.g., adversarial artifacts), it doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach. For instance:
   - How does the model perform on images where the classifier's decision is based on non-visual features (e.g., metadata)?
   - How does the model handle cases where the classifier has learned spurious correlations that don't correspond to human understanding of the class?
   - The paper claims "model agnostic" but uses ResNet as part of the architecture, which might introduce biases.

2. **Comparison with Parallel Work**: The paper mentions that Fong and Vedaldi [2] was developed in parallel, but the comparison is limited. A more thorough comparison of the two approaches, including computational complexity and quality differences, would strengthen the paper.

3. **CIFAR-10 Experiment**: The CIFAR-10 experiment is underdeveloped compared to the ImageNet experiments. The paper states they used a randomly initialized masking model, but doesn't provide sufficient detail on why this approach was taken or how it compares to using a pre-trained model. The results on CIFAR-10 don't include comparison to other saliency methods.

4. **Training Data Requirements**: The paper states they trained on 250,000 ImageNet images, but doesn't discuss how the training data distribution might affect the model's performance on images outside this distribution.

5. **Lack of Ablation Study**: While the paper includes comparisons to other methods, it lacks a thorough ablation study of their objective function components (e.g., what happens when they remove the TV penalty or the area penalty).

## Specific Comments

1. **Figure 5**: The visual comparison of saliency masks is excellent and clearly demonstrates the quality of the proposed method. However, it would be helpful to include a side-by-side comparison with the iterative methods (like [2]) to better illustrate the speed-accuracy tradeoff.

2. **Equation 4**: The objective function is well-motivated, but the paper could provide more intuition about the relative weights of the different terms (1, 2, 3, 4). How were these hyperparameters determined? Were they sensitive to changes?

3. **Section 4.1**: The training procedure with the "fake class" trick is clever, but the paper could elaborate more on why this approach works better than other regularization techniques.

4. **Section 5.2**: The results in Table 3 are compelling, but it would be helpful to include statistical significance testing to confirm that the improvements are not due to random chance.

5. **Future Work**: The discussion of future work on "detecting biases embedded in our masking model" is promising but would benefit from more specific suggestions about how to investigate this.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The experimental design is comprehensive, and the authors are careful to evaluate both strengths and weaknesses (though they could be more thorough in discussing limitations). The work represents a complete piece of research, not just a position paper.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The writing is accessible to readers familiar with deep learning, and the figures effectively illustrate key concepts. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results.

**Originality**: The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than using iterative optimization is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, including the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2]. The new saliency metric also represents a significant contribution.

**Significance**: The ability to produce saliency maps in real-time has substantial practical implications, particularly for applications like video analysis. The method outperforms existing weakly supervised techniques on ImageNet, and the new metric provides a better way to evaluate saliency methods. This work advances the state of the art in model interpretability.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of model interpretability with a novel, practical approach to saliency detection. The technical soundness, thorough evaluation, and clear writing make it a compelling contribution to NIPS. While there are some limitations to address (particularly regarding the discussion of limitations and the CIFAR-10 experiment), these do not significantly undermine the paper's overall contribution.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- Novel approach to saliency detection that is orders of magnitude faster than existing methods
- Well-motivated new metric that better assesses saliency quality
- Strong experimental results across multiple datasets and classifiers
- Clear demonstration of real-time capability with practical applications
- Well-written and well-organized paper that advances the state of the art

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- Limited discussion of the method's limitations
- Underdeveloped CIFAR-10 experiment compared to ImageNet
- Comparison to parallel work [2] is not as thorough as it could be
- Lacks a comprehensive ablation study of the objective function components

Despite these minor concerns, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and it makes a valuable contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning.
