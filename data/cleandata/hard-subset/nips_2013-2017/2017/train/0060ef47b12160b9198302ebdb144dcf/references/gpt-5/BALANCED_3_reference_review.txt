Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a fast, feed-forward saliency method that learns to generate class-conditional masks for a given image classifier. The method trains a U-Net–style “masking model” (with a ResNet-50 encoder) to output a mask in one pass by minimizing an objective that (i) enforces smoothness (TV), (ii) penalizes area, (iii) preserves the target class probability on the preserved region (SSR-like), and (iv) suppresses it when the mask is inverted (SDR-like). To reduce artifacts and leakage of evidence, masking is performed with an alternative image A drawn at random (strong blur vs. random color + noise), and the model is trained with “fake” labels 30% of the time to force attention to the class selector. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric based on the log-area of a tight rectangular crop versus the log-probability of the target class on the crop. The approach is evaluated on ImageNet with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 as target classifiers for weakly supervised object localization and the proposed saliency metric; and on CIFAR-10 with a smaller architecture trained from scratch. The key claims are improved localization over prior weakly supervised baselines, competitive performance with fully supervised VGG localization, better scores under the new metric, and real-time speed.

Relative to prior work: gradient-based saliency (Simonyan et al., 2013), Guided Backprop (Springenberg et al., 2014), Excitation Backprop (Zhang et al., 2016), optimization-based perturbation explanations (Cao et al., 2015; Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), and CAM/weakly supervised localization (Zhou et al., 2016), the novelty here is to train a parametric masking network end-to-end against a saliency objective that combines SSR and SDR terms and to randomize the masking operation to reduce artifacts. This is close in spirit to “meaningful perturbations” (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017) but replaces per-image optimization by amortized inference.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: real-time, sharper, and less artifact-prone saliency than iterative perturbation methods.
- Solid architectural design: U-Net with multi-scale skip connections and a simple class-embedding gate; real-time throughput is compelling for video and interactive use.
- Thoughtful handling of “introduced evidence” via randomized A and TV/upsampling constraints; the fake-label training trick plausibly encourages class specificity.
- Combining SSR and SDR in the loss encourages both preservation and deletion fidelity, improving interpretability.
- Empirical signal: improved weakly supervised localization vs. several baselines and an ad hoc but intuitive saliency metric; a CIFAR-10 experiment shows feasibility without pretraining.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- “Black box” vs. differentiable assumption: training requires backpropagating through the classifier to optimize the masking model. This is not black-box in the sense of forward-only access; it is “white-box during training, no access at test.” Please clarify assumptions and positioning (contrast with methods that do not require gradients or model internals).
- Objective signs/ambiguities: Eq. (4) appears to add log(fc(⋅)) while minimizing L, which would push fc down. It likely should be −log(fc(⋅)) (or a margin). Similarly, the new metric in Eq. (3) shows “log(ã) log(p)” without an explicit minus; later text implies s = log(ã) − log(p). These sign errors need correction.
- Metric design: Using softmax probabilities makes the metric sensitive to calibration and saturation; logits or margins may be more robust. The rectangular crop and aspect-ratio ignoring resize can bias results; please analyze sensitivity. Also report insertion/deletion curves (AUC) or probability drop curves to evaluate SSR/SDR fidelity more comprehensively.
- Evaluation scope: Localization is measured with the ground-truth label as the target. For explanation, the more relevant setting is explaining the model’s predicted class(s); please report those results. Multi-object and multi-label scenarios also deserve attention (e.g., how the method handles co-occurring classes).
- Baselines: Important baselines are missing, notably Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017), Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017), and SmoothGrad (Smilkov et al., 2017). Please add these, including timing comparisons, localization, and fidelity metrics. Also compare to CAM/Grad-CAM for class-discriminative localization quality.
- Ablations: No ablation on loss weights, the choice/proportion of A, the fake-label probability, or the class-embedding gate (Eq. 7). Include ablations to support design choices and artifact mitigation claims.
- Reproducibility details are sparse: optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, training duration/epochs, data augmentations, thresholding choices, and validation selection are missing.

Clarity
- Overall exposition is good and the intuition for SSR/SDR and evidence introduction is valuable. However, please:
  - Fix sign/notation issues in Eqs. (3) and (4).
  - Be precise about the masking operator “·” (Hadamard product) and broadcasting.
  - Clarify whether the classifier is frozen during training (implied yes), and what access to gradients is assumed.
  - Provide more training details and describe how the threshold 0.5 for binarizing masks was chosen.
  - Discuss failure cases or limitations (e.g., low-frequency scenes where blur removal is weak).

Originality
- The idea of amortizing perturbation-based saliency via a learned masking network is novel relative to per-image optimization (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017) and backprop-based maps. The combination of SSR and SDR terms and randomized A is a useful contribution. That said, the paper would benefit from a broader and more up-to-date comparison set for 2017 (Grad-CAM, IG, SmoothGrad) to establish distinctiveness and practical advantages.

Significance
- If validated against stronger baselines and broader metrics, a real-time, high-fidelity saliency generator is quite useful for practice (debugging, interactive tools, video). The locality and sharpness of masks suggest potential for weakly supervised segmentation, which the authors note as future work. Current evidence is promising but not yet definitive due to missing baselines/ablations and metric ambiguities.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical and timely contribution: real-time, high-quality masks with a principled objective.
- Thoughtful treatment of artifact introduction and class-selectivity training.
- Promising localization and saliency metric results; architecture is sensible and broadly applicable.

Cons for acceptance
- Mischaracterization of “black box” assumptions; requires gradients during training.
- Missing key contemporary baselines and fidelity metrics; limited evaluation on predicted classes.
- Ambiguities/typos in core equations and metric; insufficient ablations and training details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the access assumptions; position as “white-box during training” and discuss feasibility when gradients are unavailable.
- Correct Eq. (3) and Eq. (4) signs; consider using logits/margins in both loss and metric. Report sensitivity to the crop protocol.
- Add comparisons with Grad-CAM, Grad-CAM++, Integrated Gradients, SmoothGrad; include deletion/insertion AUC curves and probability drop under masking/insertion.
- Report explanations for predicted classes, multi-object cases, and provide qualitative failure modes.
- Provide ablations on loss weights, A choices, fake-label ratio, mask resolution/upsampling, and the feature filter (Eq. 7).
- Include full training details and release code/models if possible.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The paper presents a promising and practical idea with encouraging results, but it falls short on clarity (equation errors), comparative evaluation against key baselines, and ablations needed to support its claims. With these issues addressed, it could be a strong submission.
