This paper proposes a learned, model-agnostic saliency detection method, aiming to overcome the computational cost of iterative approaches. The core idea – training a network to directly predict saliency maps – is conceptually straightforward. While the motivation of understanding classifier behavior and potential biases is valuable, and real-time saliency is a desirable goal, I find the presented work ultimately lacking in substantial novelty and rigorous validation.

The primary concern is the reliance on a newly introduced saliency metric (SSR/SDR-based) without sufficient justification for its superiority over established metrics like localization accuracy (already used in the paper) or, more critically, human evaluation. The theoretical grounding for this metric feels somewhat hand-wavy. While the authors attempt to address potential artifacts introduced by masking, the proposed solutions (blurring, random noise) feel ad-hoc and don’t fully convince. The paper spends considerable effort detailing these artifact mitigation strategies, suggesting an underlying concern about their robustness that isn’t fully resolved.

The experimental evaluation, while extensive, is limited by its focus on ImageNet and CIFAR-10. While demonstrating improved performance over some baselines (including a “Center” box and iterative methods) is positive, the comparisons lack depth. The performance gains relative to Fong and Vedaldi [2], a very recent and relevant work, are not convincingly demonstrated. Furthermore, the choice of baselines feels somewhat selective; comparison to more sophisticated, established saliency methods (beyond the iterative ones clearly outperformed) would strengthen the claims. The experiment on CIFAR-10, while showing applicability to different datasets, is less compelling given the simpler nature of the images.

The architecture, while leveraging a U-Net with a pre-trained ResNet backbone, doesn’t appear particularly novel. The modifications to the U-Net seem primarily motivated by the low resolution of CIFAR-10 rather than architectural innovation. The embedding learning strategy is also presented as a fix for a training instability, rather than a core methodological contribution.

The writing is generally clear, but the paper is quite verbose, and some sections (particularly the detailed discussion of artifact mitigation) could be more concise. The inclusion of many citations, while demonstrating awareness of related work, sometimes feels excessive and doesn't always add substantial value.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem: understanding and visualizing classifier decisions.
*   Proposes a potentially efficient, real-time approach to saliency detection.
*   Includes a new saliency metric, albeit one requiring further justification.
*   Demonstrates positive results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of compelling novelty beyond the straightforward idea of a learned saliency map.
*   Insufficient justification and validation of the proposed saliency metric.
*   Limited comparison to strong, established saliency detection methods.
*   Ad-hoc solutions to artifact mitigation without thorough evaluation.
*   Verbosity and lack of conciseness in certain sections.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

*   **Against:** The paper doesn’t present a sufficiently groundbreaking contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The novelty is incremental, the validation is limited, and the proposed metric lacks convincing justification.
*   **For:** The paper addresses a relevant problem, presents a functional approach, and offers some empirical evidence of improvement over certain baselines. However, these strengths are not substantial enough to overcome the weaknesses.

**Overall:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it falls short of the originality and significance criteria expected at NIPS. The authors would significantly strengthen the work by providing a more rigorous evaluation against stronger baselines, offering a more compelling justification for their proposed saliency metric (potentially with human evaluation), and providing a more thorough analysis of the limitations of their approach. The current work feels more appropriate for a more applied conference or workshop.
