This paper proposes a learned, model-agnostic saliency detection method, aiming to overcome the computational cost of iterative approaches. The core idea – training a network to predict saliency maps – is intuitively appealing and addresses a practical limitation of existing techniques. However, despite the promising premise and reported results, I find myself unconvinced of the significant novelty and robustness of the contribution, and harbor concerns regarding the experimental validation and proposed saliency metric.

The introduction adequately frames the problem of interpreting black-box classifiers and the role of saliency detection. The motivation is sound, but the claims of “orders-of-magnitude” speedup and “higher quality” saliency maps feel overstated given the lack of comprehensive comparison against well-established, optimized iterative methods (e.g., those leveraging GPU acceleration). While speed is certainly an advantage, it shouldn’t come at the expense of accuracy or interpretability.

The related work section is reasonably thorough, covering gradient-based methods, iterative patch removal, and optimization-based approaches. However, it glosses over the computational optimizations frequently applied to these iterative methods. Asserting they are “not acceptable for real-time saliency detection” requires a more direct quantitative comparison rather than a categorical statement. The parallel work by Fong & Vedaldi [2] is appropriately acknowledged, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contributions beyond the inclusion of both SSR and SDR objectives during training.

The introduction of the SSR/SDR concepts and the proposed saliency metric are a key component of this work. While the distinction between SSR and SDR is conceptually useful, the inherent difficulty in finding a region that performs well on *both* is downplayed. The discussion of “introduced evidence” during masking is valuable, and the proposed mitigations (blurring, noise addition, TV regularization) are reasonable. However, the framing of this as a novel issue feels somewhat overstated; the problem of adversarial artifacts in explanation methods is well-known. 

The proposed saliency metric, while aiming to capture both size and classification confidence, relies on resizing the cropped region, which introduces a potentially distorting factor. The justification that classifiers are “largely invariant” to scale and aspect ratio feels weak, particularly given the vulnerability of CNNs to adversarial perturbations.  Furthermore, the logarithmic scaling and thresholding (ã = max(a, 0.05)) seem somewhat arbitrary and lack clear justification. This metric *feels* right but needs better grounding.

The methodology section detailing the masking model (U-Net with ResNet backbone) is clear. The training process enhancements (fake labels, random evidence removal) are sensible and demonstrate attention to potential pitfalls. However, the reliance on a pre-trained ResNet encoder raises questions about the originality of the learned representations within the masking model itself. The CIFAR-10 experiment, while demonstrating transferability, doesn’t fully address this concern.  The statement that the masking model “does not need to rely on a pre-trained model” is contradicted by the ImageNet experiments.

The experimental results, while showing improved localization accuracy compared to baselines, are not compelling enough. The comparison to a “central box” baseline is too weak. More robust comparisons against optimized implementations of iterative methods (e.g., those in [1, 2]) are crucial. The saliency metric results are better, but the metric itself is the main contribution here, so showing superiority against alternatives isn’t surprising. Visual examples are helpful, but a quantitative, statistically significant comparison of mask quality is lacking.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a practical limitation of existing saliency methods (computational cost).
* Proposes a new saliency metric that attempts to capture important aspects of explanation quality.
* Demonstrates reasonable performance on localization and saliency metrics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited novelty – primarily combines existing techniques (U-Net, ResNet) with a new loss function and metric.
* Insufficient comparison to optimized implementations of established saliency methods.
* Saliency metric relies on potentially distorting resizing and has arbitrary parameters.
* Overstated claims regarding speedup and map quality.
* Reliance on a pre-trained encoder weakens claims of learning saliency directly from the classifier.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While the paper presents a reasonable approach to a relevant problem, it falls short of the originality and rigorous validation expected of a NIPS publication. The improvements over existing methods appear incremental, and the lack of comprehensive comparison against optimized baselines leaves the claimed advantages unsubstantiated. The proposed saliency metric is interesting, but its justification and potential limitations need further exploration. I recommend rejection with encouragement to perform more thorough comparisons, address the limitations of the saliency metric, and explore the model's behavior without relying on a pre-trained encoder.
