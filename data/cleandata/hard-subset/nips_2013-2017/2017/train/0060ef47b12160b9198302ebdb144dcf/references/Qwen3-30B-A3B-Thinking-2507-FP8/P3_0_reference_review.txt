

# Peer Review: "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach for real-time image saliency detection for black-box classifiers. Instead of using iterative methods that require hundreds of optimization steps per image (e.g., [1, 17]), the authors train a model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. The key contributions include: (1) a new objective function for saliency masks that balances smoothness, area minimization, and classification confidence; (2) a U-Net-based masking model that can predict high-quality saliency maps; and (3) a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept. The method achieves 100 saliency masks per second on a standard GPU, significantly faster than existing approaches, while outperforming them on standard benchmarks.

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in model interpretability: current saliency methods are too slow for real-time applications like video analysis. The authors position their work against related work in Section 2, noting that Fong and Vedaldi [2] (developed in parallel) also proposed an optimization-based method, but their approach differs by training a model rather than optimizing iteratively.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation**: The introduction effectively motivates the problem with compelling examples (snow-polar bear, adversarial perturbations) and clearly articulates the limitations of existing iterative saliency methods.

2. **Novel Metric**: The proposed saliency metric (section 3.2) is well-motivated and addresses a critical gap in the field—existing metrics like localisation accuracy don't fully capture saliency quality. The SSR-based metric provides a more direct measure of interpretability.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, comparing against multiple baselines. The localisation accuracy results (Table 1) are particularly impressive for a weakly supervised method, with the ResNet-50 trained model achieving 36.7% error rate compared to the fully supervised VGG's 34.3%.

4. **Real-World Applicability**: The demonstration of real-time performance (100 masks/sec) opens up new applications like video saliency, which is a significant contribution to the field.

5. **Well-Structured Methodology**: The masking model architecture (Section 4) is well-chosen, with the U-Net design appropriate for saliency prediction. The training strategy with "fake labels" to prevent the model from ignoring the class selector is clever and well-justified.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Parallel Work**: The paper mentions that Fong and Vedaldi [2] developed a similar method in parallel but doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs. This is a major issue for assessing novelty. The paper should explicitly compare the two methods on key aspects like training requirements, inference speed, and quality of saliency maps.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the experimental results are strong, the paper lacks theoretical analysis explaining *why* their approach works better than iterative methods. The claim that "the model itself acts as some sort of a regulariser" is vague and needs more rigorous justification.

3. **Incomplete Evaluation of Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address potential limitations:
   - How does the method perform on images where the classifier has learned spurious correlations (e.g., snow-polar bear)?
   - What happens when the black-box classifier makes incorrect predictions? Does the saliency map still make sense?
   - The paper mentions "adversarial artifacts" but doesn't provide quantitative analysis of how effective their evidence removal scheme is at preventing them.

4. **Hyperparameter Selection**: The choice of hyperparameters (λ1=10, λ2=10^-3, etc.) in the objective function (equation 4) is not well-justified. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper.

5. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper mentions [2] but doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison to this method. Given that it was developed in parallel, this is a critical omission.

6. **Overstated Claims**: The claim that their masks are "sharp and precise" is somewhat subjective. More quantitative comparisons to other methods (beyond the localisation accuracy metric) would be helpful.

## Specific Comments

1. **Section 3.1 (Fighting the Introduced Evidence)**: The discussion about adversarial artifacts is important, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that their proposed solution (randomized evidence removal) actually prevents such artifacts. A quantitative comparison of artifact rates between their method and others would strengthen this section.

2. **Section 4.1 (Training Process)**: The 30% fake label probability is an interesting technique, but the paper doesn't explain why this specific value was chosen. A brief analysis of how the performance changes with different fake label probabilities would be valuable.

3. **Figure 5**: The visual comparison of saliency maps is excellent, but the paper could better highlight specific examples where their method clearly outperforms others (e.g., where the iterative methods produce blurry masks).

4. **Section 5.3 (CIFAR-10 Experiment)**: The fact that the model works well on CIFAR-10 without pre-training is impressive, but the paper doesn't discuss how the model's performance might change if the black-box classifier has lower accuracy (e.g., if it's trained on a smaller dataset).

5. **Conclusion**: The mention of "biases embedded into our masking model" is important but superficial. The paper should have explored this more deeply, especially since they claim the method works well even when the black-box has biases.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of model interpretability with a practical, real-time saliency detection method. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is novel, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are some weaknesses, particularly around the comparison to parallel work and theoretical justification, these can be addressed with minor revisions.

The paper advances the state of the art in saliency detection by providing a method that is both significantly faster and higher quality than existing approaches. The new saliency metric is particularly valuable and could become a standard in the field. The real-time performance opens up new applications that weren't previously possible with saliency methods.

## Final Comments

This is a strong paper with clear practical value. The authors should address the following points in revision:

1. Provide a more detailed comparison with the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2], including how their method differs and why it's superior in key aspects.

2. Add a theoretical justification for why their approach works better than iterative methods, even if it's just a brief discussion.

3. Include a sensitivity analysis for the hyperparameters in the objective function.

4. Address the limitations of their method more thoroughly, especially regarding classifier biases and performance on images where the classifier has learned spurious correlations.

With these revisions, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of model interpretability, and their method has clear practical applications in areas like autonomous vehicles where real-time model explanations are crucial. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful advancement over existing work.
