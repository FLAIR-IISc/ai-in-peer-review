Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a learned, real-time saliency detector for image classifiers treated as black boxes at test time. Instead of computing a saliency mask per image via iterative optimization, the authors train a masking network (U-Net with a frozen ResNet-50 encoder) to predict class-conditional masks in a single forward pass. The training objective combines: (i) smoothness (TV) and sparsity (area) penalties, (ii) an SSR term that encourages high class probability on the preserved region, and (iii) an SDR term that encourages low class probability when the preserved region is removed. To reduce artifacts from masking (introduced evidence), the paper randomizes the background replacement image A (strong blur or random color+noise) and penalizes mask roughness. They also introduce a simple saliency metric based on the tight crop that contains the mask: s(a,p) = log(ã)·log(p), where lower is better.

Empirically, the authors train separate masking models targeting AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 on ImageNet (using only class labels) and evaluate on weakly supervised localization and their saliency metric; they also demonstrate a non-pretrained variant on CIFAR-10. They report improved localization errors compared to prior weakly supervised and gradient-based saliency approaches, with near real-time throughput (~100 images/s).

This work sits between backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop) and iterative masking approaches that remove evidence (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi’s meaningful perturbations; contemporaneous) and activation-sparsity methods (Cao et al.). The key novelty is to amortize saliency optimization across images via a trainable model that captures the black-box’s behavior and yields sharper, faster masks.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical limitation of iterative perturbation methods by amortizing computation; achieves real-time inference with improved mask quality and sharper boundaries.
- Joint SSR+SDR objective is well-motivated and aligns with interpretability desiderata; the randomization of the replacement image and TV penalty thoughtfully mitigate artifact-driven explanations.
- Clever training tricks (fake class selector, class embedding with feature filtering) enforce class-conditionality and stabilize optimization.
- Introduces a simple, operational saliency metric that is less confounded by masking artifacts (uses crops) and aligns with the SSR notion.
- Demonstrates generality across multiple classifiers and datasets (ImageNet and CIFAR-10), and shows strong weakly-supervised localization performance.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “Black-box/model-agnostic” claims are ambiguous. Training requires backpropagating through the classifier’s outputs wrt the input to update the masking model (the loss depends on fc(·) of masked images). Thus the classifier must be differentiable and accessible during training. The method is black-box only at inference; this should be clarified and the phrasing toned down.
- Empirical support is difficult to assess fully from the text alone: only one headline localization number (36.7% error) is given; Tables 1–3 are referenced but not shown here. The magnitude of gains over strong baselines (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM/Excitation Backprop) and over the iterative meaningful perturbations approach is not quantitatively clear.
- No ablations on the loss coefficients, the effect of SSR vs SDR terms, the choice and mixing of A, the TV weight, or the upsampling architecture; this limits insight into what design choices matter.
- The proposed metric, while intuitive, depends on a tight rectangular crop and rescaling that may penalize fragmented masks and can be sensitive to aspect ratio changes. It would help to compare it to other deletion/insertion-style metrics or report correlations with localization.
- The method requires training a separate masking network per target classifier, which can be costly. No analysis of cross-classifier transferability or robustness is provided.
- Some clarity issues: minor notational inconsistencies; the use of “model agnostic/black box” could be more precise; more details (optimizer, LR, training schedule, embedding size) would aid reproducibility.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the objective is coherent, addresses artifacts, and the architecture is appropriate. The training trick with fake labels is a good design choice to enforce class conditionality. The main methodological caveat is the “black-box” framing vs. the need for gradients during training. Experimental design appears reasonable (ImageNet localization, alternate metric), but ablations and more detailed quantitative comparisons would strengthen the claims. Without seeing the exact numbers in tables, the strength of the empirical advantage remains somewhat uncertain.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear motivation and architectural description. The discussion of introduced evidence and countermeasures is thoughtful. Clarity would improve by: precisely stating the training-time requirement for a differentiable classifier; providing more training details; and expanding on the behavior of the new metric (e.g., range, interpretation). Some figures/tables are essential to assess results.

Originality
Moderately original. It combines known ideas (mask-based perturbation, TV regularization, U-Net, SSR/SDR) but contributes the key idea of amortizing saliency via a learned predictor, the joint SSR+SDR objective, randomized evidence removal, and a simple crop-based saliency metric. The approach is distinct from prior backprop or iterative perturbation works and practically impactful.

Significance
Potentially significant: real-time, higher-quality saliency can enable new use cases (e.g., video) and improve interpretability tooling. If the reported improvements over strong baselines hold, this would be a useful step forward. The need to retrain per classifier and the requirement of differentiable access during training may limit some deployments, but this is acceptable for many research and production scenarios.

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the training-time dependency on the black-box classifier. Do you backpropagate through fc to update the masking network? If so, revise “model-agnostic/black-box” claims accordingly.
- Provide full quantitative results (Tables 1–3) and comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM and Fong & Vedaldi’s method with similar mask budgets. Include runtime comparisons.
- Ablate the loss terms (remove SSR, remove SDR), TV weight, and A selection scheme. How do these affect localization and the saliency metric?
- Does a masking model trained for one classifier transfer to another? Any results on cross-model generalization?
- How sensitive is the new metric to the rectangular crop and rescaling? Any correlation with human judgments or alternative metrics?

Pros
- Practical, fast, and sharper saliency with a principled objective; demonstrated on large-scale data.
- Thoughtful handling of artifact introduction; class-conditional control.
- New, intuitive saliency metric.

Cons
- Ambiguous “black-box” claim; requires differentiability at training.
- Limited quantitative evidence visible here; missing ablations.
- Per-classifier training overhead; unclear transferability.

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the black-box claim and providing fuller quantitative results and minimal ablations. The idea of amortized, high-quality saliency is useful and timely, and the method appears sound and potentially impactful.
