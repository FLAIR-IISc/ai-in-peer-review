### Summary

This paper introduces a novel, model-based approach for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. The core idea is a significant departure from existing methods. Instead of iteratively optimizing a mask for each input image to find salient regions, the authors propose training a separate, sophisticated neural network (a U-Net with a ResNet encoder) to predict the saliency mask in a single, fast, feed-forward pass. The training of this "masking model" is guided by a carefully designed objective function that encourages the generated masks to be smooth, small, and act as both a "Smallest Sufficient Region" (SSR) and a "Smallest Destroying Region" (SDR). To better evaluate such properties, the paper also introduces a new saliency metric based on information concentration. The authors demonstrate that their method is orders of magnitude faster than iterative techniques, while producing sharper, higher-quality saliency maps that achieve state-of-the-art results on weakly supervised object localization on ImageNet.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness of the Approach:** The primary strength of this paper is its highly novel conceptualization of the saliency problem. Shifting from per-instance, iterative optimization to a learned, amortized prediction via a dedicated generator network is a bold and creative leap. This reframing has the potential to define a new class of explanation methods and is precisely the kind of forward-thinking research that NIPS should champion. It trades the "on-the-fly" optimization cost for a one-time, upfront training cost, which is a very powerful and practical paradigm for real-world deployment.

2.  **Significant Practical Impact:** The method's ability to generate high-quality saliency maps at over 100 FPS is a major engineering and research achievement. This unlocks new applications previously infeasible for high-quality saliency methods, most notably real-time video analysis. The prospect of applying this to explain the decisions of models in autonomous driving or other safety-critical systems is compelling and significant.

3.  **Thoughtful Problem Formulation and Contributions:** The paper is well-written and demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem.
    *   The formalization of saliency into SSR and SDR objectives, and the subsequent encoding of both into a single objective function (Eq. 4), is elegant.
    *   The discussion on "introduced evidence" and the proposed mitigation strategies (e.g., randomizing the background fill) is insightful and addresses a subtle but critical issue in perturbation-based methods.
    *   The introduction of a new saliency metric (Eq. 3) is a valuable contribution in its own right. It is well-motivated by information-theoretic principles and appears to capture the essence of a good "explanation" better than localization accuracy alone.

4.  **Strong and Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The empirical results are convincing. The method not only achieves its goal of being fast but also outperforms strong baselines and prior art in localization accuracy on ImageNet, doing so in a weakly supervised setting. The performance being on par with ground truth bounding boxes under their new metric further strengthens their claims about the quality and interpretability of the generated masks. The inclusion of experiments on CIFAR-10 with a from-scratch model successfully demonstrates the general applicability of the approach beyond ImageNet-pretrained encoders.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The Explainer's Bias:** The most significant conceptual concern is that the masking model, being a powerful deep network itself (a ResNet-based U-Net), introduces its own strong priors and biases. How can we be certain that the generated saliency map reflects the reasoning of the black-box classifier, rather than the masking model's own learned understanding of what constitutes an object? For instance, the masking model might learn a strong prior for "faces" from its pre-training and consistently highlight them, even if the target black-box model is using a different feature (e.g., texture of fur). While the authors commendably acknowledge this in their conclusion as an area for future work, it remains a central philosophical question for this entire line of research. However, the experiments on multiple, distinct black-box architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) provide evidence that the model is indeed adapting to the target classifier, somewhat mitigating this concern.

2.  **Practical Overhead:** While the inference is real-time, the proposed solution requires training a large, separate model for *every single black-box model* one wishes to explain. This represents a significant computational and engineering overhead compared to methods that can be applied directly to any model. This is a practical trade-off for speed, but it is a limitation worth noting.

3.  **Sensitivity of the Objective Function:** The loss function (Eq. 4) involves four hyperparameters (λ1 to λ4) that appear crucial for balancing the competing objectives (smoothness, size, SSR, SDR). The paper provides the final values used but lacks a discussion on their selection or an analysis of the model's sensitivity to these choices. This slightly reduces the methodological transparency and makes it harder for others to adapt the method to new domains.

### Recommendation: Accept

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a genuinely new and bold direction for saliency detection and model interpretability.
*   The core idea of training a predictive model for saliency is highly significant and could inspire a great deal of follow-up work.
*   It solves a major practical problem: the speed/quality trade-off in existing methods.
*   The work is supported by strong empirical results, a thoughtful theoretical framing, and the contribution of a new, relevant metric.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The primary concern is the potential for the explainer model to inject its own biases, a deep issue that the paper acknowledges but does not resolve.
*   The methodological detail regarding hyperparameter selection could be improved.

**Conclusion:** This paper is an excellent example of high-risk, high-reward research. It proposes a fundamental shift in how we approach saliency generation. While it raises important new questions about the nature of "explanation models," its novelty, significance, and strong execution make it a standout submission. The potential impact of enabling real-time, high-fidelity model explanations is immense. I am willing to overlook the minor methodological gaps and the deeper philosophical questions (which are now open for the community to explore) in favor of the paper's bold and promising new direction. I strongly recommend acceptance.
