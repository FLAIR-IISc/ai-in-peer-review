Summary
This paper proposes a trainable, model-agnostic saliency detector that predicts, in a single forward pass, per-class masks indicating image regions that a classifier relies on. The approach optimizes a mask-generation network (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and class embeddings) against an objective that combines (i) smoothness and small area, (ii) Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR: preserve evidence to keep class confidence high), and (iii) Smallest Destroying Region (SDR: remove evidence to suppress class confidence). To reduce adversarial artifacts from masking, the authors apply masks against alternative images (blurred or random color+noise) and penalize total variation. They also introduce a “saliency metric” based on classification confidence on the tightest crop containing the salient region, intending to quantify information concentration. Experiments on ImageNet (with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 as black boxes) and CIFAR-10 show sharp, interpretable masks, improved weakly supervised localization over baselines and some prior saliency methods, and real-time throughput (~100 fps). A simple training trick (fake labels) makes the model respect the class selector.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself among gradient/backprop-based attribution (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), perturbation-based iterative masking (Zhou et al. 2014/2016; Cao et al. 2015), and the contemporaneous “Meaningful Perturbations” of Fong & Vedaldi. The key difference is learning a feed-forward model that amortizes the iterative search for salient regions while combining SSR and SDR in a single objective.

Quality
Strengths:
- The objective is well-motivated: combining SSR and SDR encourages masks that are both necessary and sufficient, while TV and alternative-image masking mitigate high-frequency artifacts known to plague perturbation methods.
- The architecture and training heuristics (class embedding gating, fake labels, randomization of alternative image) are sensible and likely important in practice; these are explained with enough detail to implement.
- Empirically, the masks look sharp and localized, and the ImageNet localization error reported for the ResNet-50 black box (36.7%) is competitive for weakly supervised methods and close to fully supervised VGG results cited.
- Throughput is a genuine practical advantage over iterative methods.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- “Black box/model-agnostic” is overstated. Training requires differentiability and backprop through the classifier’s score wrt the masked image; this is not black-box in the strict sense (e.g., cannot be trained against an API that returns scores only). Please clarify the precise assumptions and contrast to methods that truly treat the classifier as a non-differentiable oracle (e.g., RISE, later work).
- The new saliency metric is under-specified and potentially inconsistent. The formula s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p) appears to be missing a division; the text suggests a ratio (information “relative amount”). For desirable behavior (small area, high p), s = log(p)/log(ã) would yield small positive values; as written, the product behaves counterintuitively. Please fix the equation, discuss numerical stability (p near 1 implies log(p)≈0), and justify the 0.05 area floor.
- Empirical evaluation lacks some critical ablations: contributions of each loss term (SSR vs SDR vs TV), effect of masking strategy (blur vs noise), and of training tricks (fake label frequency). Without these, it is hard to attribute the gains.
- Comparison breadth. Given the claim of real-time, high-quality saliency, a direct, quantitative comparison against strong contemporary baselines (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM/Excitation Backprop) on the same protocol would be valuable. The text states outperformance but tables/figures are not shown here; ensure fair, side-by-side numbers, same thresholds, and same ground-truth usage.
- Generalization/scope. A separate masking model is trained per classifier. It would be useful to test whether the learned model generalizes across architectures or to unseen classes; as is, “model-agnostic” means re-trainable for any differentiable model, not a single universal explainer.
- Some implementation details that matter for reproducibility are missing (optimizer, learning rate schedule, data augmentation, exact training split).

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized with good intuitions and figures. However:
- Fix typos/notation: the saliency metric equation; loss term notation (the “4” at the end of Eq. 4 looks like a footnote vs exponent); hyperparameters written as “2 = 10 3” (is this 10^-3?).
- The “black box” terminology should be consistently qualified as “fixed, differentiable classifier with frozen weights.”
- Expand on how bounding boxes are derived from masks (threshold selection rationale) and whether the threshold is tuned.
- Briefly discuss multi-object, multi-label images and how the method behaves when multiple instances of the same class are present.

Originality
Moderately novel. The main novelty is amortizing perturbation-based saliency via a learned U-Net that optimizes a combined SSR/SDR objective, plus practical training heuristics. The new metric is a useful idea if clarified. While components (U-Net, TV penalty, blur/noise baselines, CAM-like gating) are known, the combination for fast, high-quality per-class saliency appears new at the time.

Significance
If the reported gains and speed hold under fair comparison, this is a useful contribution: it makes high-quality, perturbation-grounded explanations practical for large-scale and video settings, and it produces masks that are closer to segmentations. The metric, once corrected, could help standardize evaluation beyond localization. The per-class, per-image speedup alone can impact downstream interpretability workflows.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear problem formulation; practical, fast method with strong qualitative results.
- Solid, intuitive objective combining SSR and SDR; thoughtful artifact mitigation.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization; promising CIFAR-10 transfer without pretrained encoder.
- Potentially impactful in practice (real-time saliency, video applications).

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Overclaim on black-box/model-agnostic status; requires differentiability and retraining per classifier.
- Ambiguity/possible error in the new metric; needs correction and more analysis.
- Missing ablations and limited breadth of quantitative comparisons to strong baselines.
- Reproducibility gaps (optimizer/training details).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and correct the saliency metric; provide sensitivity analysis and correlation with human judgments/localization IoU.
- Add ablations for each loss term and masking strategy; report the impact on localization and the metric.
- Include direct comparisons with CAM/Grad-CAM/Excitation Backprop under identical protocols.
- Qualify the black-box claim and discuss applicability to non-differentiable or API-only classifiers.
- Provide training details and, if possible, code/models.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The idea of learning a fast, high-quality perturbation-based saliency predictor is timely and useful; with clarified metric and stronger empirical evidence, the paper would be a solid NIPS contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate.
