This paper proposes a novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, moving away from iterative methods which are computationally expensive. The core idea is to train a model to directly predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric based on the “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) concept and demonstrate strong performance on standard benchmarks and with their new metric, showcasing both speed and quality improvements over existing techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of learning a saliency detector, rather than computing it iteratively, is a significant shift and offers substantial speed advantages. This is particularly relevant for applications like video saliency.
* **Speed:** The claim of real-time performance is a strong asset, opening up possibilities previously inaccessible with slower methods.
* **Performance:** The results presented in the ImageNet experiments, particularly the localization accuracy comparison (Table 1), demonstrate competitive and often superior performance compared to existing methods, including iterative approaches and other weakly supervised techniques.
* **New Saliency Metric:** The proposed saliency metric (s(a, p)) is a thoughtful attempt to capture both the compactness and informativeness of a saliency map. The connection to information theory is insightful.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors address potential issues like introduced evidence during masking and propose mitigation strategies. The CIFAR-10 experiment further validates the generalizability of the approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. The inclusion of figures (though references to specific figures need checking - see weaknesses) helps illustrate the concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Figure References:** Several figure references are incorrect throughout the text (e.g., “figure 1” is referenced before it’s introduced, figure 2, figure 3, figure 4, figure 5, figure 6). This is a significant issue that needs to be corrected for readability and proper understanding.
* **Objective Function Components:** While the explanation of the objective function (Equation 4) is present, the weighting of each term (1, 2, 3, 4) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more detailed justification for these weights, or an ablation study demonstrating their impact, would strengthen the paper.
* **Ablation Studies:**  Beyond the weighting of the objective function, more comprehensive ablation studies are needed.  For example, the impact of the U-Net architecture versus simpler architectures, or the effect of the pre-trained ResNet encoder, would provide valuable insights.
* **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While comparisons to several methods are included, a comparison to more recent and advanced saliency methods (beyond those cited) would further solidify the contribution. Specifically, methods leveraging integrated gradients or SmoothGrad could be relevant.
* **Generalizability of Metric:** The saliency metric relies on the classifier’s ability to still recognize the object from the cropped region. This assumes a relatively robust classifier; if the classifier is easily fooled even with a slightly cropped image, the metric might be misleading.  A discussion of this limitation would be helpful.
* **Masking Scheme Details:** The description of the evidence removal function and the random selection of A (blurred vs. noise) could benefit from more detail and perhaps visual examples.  How is the Gaussian blur applied, and what are the parameters?
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While results are presented, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. What types of images or classes does the method struggle with, and why?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in explainable AI – understanding the decision-making process of black-box classifiers.
* The proposed approach is novel and offers significant advantages in terms of speed and performance.
* The authors have conducted thorough experiments and provide a new, well-motivated saliency metric.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The incorrect figure references detract significantly from the readability and credibility of the paper.
* The lack of detailed justification for the objective function weights and limited ablation studies weaken the empirical analysis.
* Comparisons to a broader range of state-of-the-art saliency methods are missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field of explainable AI. The proposed approach is innovative, the results are promising, and the paper is generally well-written. However, the numerous issues with figure references and the lack of more in-depth analysis are concerning.  

**Recommendation:**

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to resubmit after addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors *must* fix all figure references, provide more justification for the objective function parameters and conduct more comprehensive ablation studies. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its likelihood of acceptance.  A comparison to a wider range of state-of-the-art saliency techniques would also be highly beneficial.
