- Proposes training a U-Net–based model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass for black-box classifiers, avoiding iterative methods.  
- Introduces a new saliency metric (SSR s(a,p)) that balances region size and classifier confidence to evaluate saliency quality.  
- Demonstrates competitive localization accuracy on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 while achieving real-time speedups over iterative methods.  
- Positions work within explainable AI, contrasting learned saliency with gradient-based and optimization-based approaches.  
- Claims improved generalization and major computational savings compared to per-image iterative saliency methods.  
- Provides careful analysis and mitigation of adversarial artifacts from masking using blurred backgrounds, noise, and TV regularization.  
- Writing and organization are clear, with illustrative figures (though some are small).  
- Novelty: shifting from image-specific optimization to a learned, feedforward saliency detector is a significant conceptual contribution.  
- Speed: real-time performance enables new applications such as video saliency.  
- Performance: strong results, competitive with weakly- and some supervised localization methods.  
- Metric: SSR-based metric is a thoughtful attempt to quantify interpretability beyond localization accuracy.  
- Weak justification for choosing U-Net; ablation studies to show U-Net component effects are lacking.  
- Insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection and sensitivity for the objective function and training.  
- Objective function has multiple terms whose individual contributions and relative importance are not fully analyzed.  
- Limited ablation studies overall, beyond the CIFAR-10 experiment, to dissect design choices like feature filters and image selection strategies.  
- Method depends on the underlying black-box classifier, risking propagation of the classifier’s biases into saliency maps.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, impactful, fast, addresses practical artifacts, and introduces a useful metric.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks thorough ablations, incomplete hyperparameter discussion, and insufficient treatment of classifier-induced bias.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with high potential and minor analytical gaps; recommended accept with minor revisions.
