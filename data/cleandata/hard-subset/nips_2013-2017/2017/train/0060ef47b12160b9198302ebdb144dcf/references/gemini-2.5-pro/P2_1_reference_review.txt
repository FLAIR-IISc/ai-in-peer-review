**Paper Summary:**
This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers in real-time. The core problem addressed is that existing methods are either fast but produce low-quality maps (e.g., gradient-based methods) or produce high-quality maps but are computationally expensive due to iterative optimization (e.g., perturbation-based methods). The authors' key idea is to reframe saliency map generation as a prediction problem. Instead of iteratively optimizing a mask for each input image, they train a separate deep neural network (a "masking model" with a U-Net architecture) to predict the optimal saliency mask in a single feed-forward pass. The masking model is trained to minimize a carefully constructed objective function that encourages masks to identify both the "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) and the "smallest destroying region" (SDR). To evaluate their method, the authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet and introduce a new saliency metric designed to measure the "information concentration" within the salient region.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—training a model to *predict* saliency maps instead of iteratively finding them—is a significant and elegant conceptual leap. It transforms a slow, per-instance optimization problem into a fast, amortized inference problem. This is a powerful idea that could have a broad impact on the field of interpretability, particularly for applications requiring real-time explanations, such as video analysis or interactive systems, as the authors suggest.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-executed. The objective function (Eq. 4) is thoughtfully designed, combining four distinct and well-motivated terms: mask smoothness (TV), mask size (AV), SSR (preserving class score), and SDR (destroying class score). The U-Net architecture is a suitable choice for generating pixel-level masks, and the inclusion of a class-conditional "feature filter" is a clever mechanism to make the model responsive to the target class.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The method is tested against multiple strong black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) on the large-scale ImageNet dataset. The results on the weakly supervised object localization task are state-of-the-art, outperforming previous methods by a significant margin. The success on CIFAR-10 with a model trained from scratch further demonstrates the generalizability of the approach and shows it is not dependent on a pre-trained encoder.

4.  **Novel Evaluation Metric:** The introduction of the `s(a, p)` saliency metric is a valuable contribution in its own right. The authors correctly argue that localization accuracy is an imperfect proxy for saliency quality. Their proposed metric, which balances the area of a crop with the classifier's confidence, provides a more direct measure of a saliency map's ability to isolate the features most critical for classification. This is a more principled way to evaluate explanations based on the SSR concept.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Hyperparameters and Training:** The objective function (Eq. 4) relies on four weighting parameters (λ1 to λ4) that are critical to its performance. These are given specific values in the experiments section but without justification for how they were chosen. The paper would be stronger with a sensitivity analysis or at least a discussion on the effect of these hyperparameters. For example, how does the trade-off between the SSR term (λ3) and the SDR term (λ4) affect the final masks? This is crucial for reproducibility and for understanding the method's behavior.

2.  **Limitations of the New Metric:** The new saliency metric `s(a, p)` is promising, but its formulation could be better justified. The area threshold `ã = max(a, 0.05)` is arbitrary and included to prevent instability without much discussion. More importantly, resizing an arbitrary rectangular crop to a fixed input size (e.g., 224x224) can drastically alter the object's aspect ratio, potentially introducing artifacts or making the classification task artificially easier or harder for the black-box model. This is a significant confounding variable that should be acknowledged and discussed more thoroughly.

3.  **The "Explanation of an Explanation" Problem:** The paper frames the output as an explanation of the black-box classifier. However, it's more accurately a *prediction* by the masking model of what constitutes a good explanation, as defined by the objective function. The masking model is itself a complex black box (based on ResNet-50). The authors briefly acknowledge this in the conclusion ("our model might have biases of its own"), but this point is fundamental to the work's interpretation and should be discussed more prominently. The generated saliency maps are filtered through the inductive biases of the masking network, and it is not guaranteed that they faithfully represent the "true" reasoning of the original classifier.

4.  **Comparison with Parallel Work:** The paper mentions that Fong and Vedaldi [2] was developed in parallel and uses iterative optimization. While the distinction is made, the paper could emphasize more strongly that its core contribution is precisely moving *away* from this iterative paradigm. The key advantage of the proposed method is its speed, which stems directly from this architectural choice. A direct runtime comparison (e.g., seconds per image) against [2] in a table would make this crucial advantage much more explicit and impactful.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper with a novel and significant contribution to the field of machine learning interpretability. The core idea of training a predictive model for saliency is powerful and effectively addresses the speed-quality trade-off that has limited previous methods. The paper is well-written, the experiments are thorough, and the results are strong. While there are minor weaknesses regarding hyperparameter justification, the limitations of the new metric, and the philosophical implications of using one black box to explain another, these do not detract from the overall strength and impact of the work. The paper is technically solid, advances the state of the art, and is likely to inspire follow-up research. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
