This paper proposes a novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for black-box classifiers. Existing methods often rely on iterative processes, making them computationally expensive. The authors address this limitation by training a model to directly predict saliency maps. The core idea is to learn a masking function that highlights relevant image regions for a given classifier, utilizing a U-Net architecture with a novel loss function incorporating smoothness and classification preservation terms. A new saliency metric, based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, is also introduced. The work builds on prior research in gradient-based saliency methods [11, 12, 16], iterative patch removal techniques [17], and optimization-based approaches like those in [1, 2]. The paper clearly positions itself within this landscape and provides adequate references.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The learned masking model for rapid saliency map generation is a novel contribution. Moving away from iterative methods is a significant step towards real-time applicability, particularly for video analysis.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed U-Net architecture and objective function appear well-motivated and carefully designed. The inclusion of smoothness regularization and the preservation of classifier performance are strong technical elements. The modifications to prevent adversarial artifacts are thoughtful.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are relatively extensive, encompassing ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets with multiple black-box classifiers. The comparison with existing methods and the introduction of a new saliency metric demonstrate a rigorous evaluation. The use of a new metric is a good way to assess the quality of the saliency maps, and its justification is solid.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed method is explained in a logical manner, and the experimental results are presented in a concise and understandable way.
*   **Real-time performance:** The claim of real-time saliency map generation is significant and potentially impactful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Objective Function Complexity:** While the objective function is well-motivated, the numerous hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4) and their influence on the results aren't thoroughly investigated. A more comprehensive ablation study regarding these parameters would be beneficial.
*   **Alternative Image Selection:** The random selection of the alternative image *A* in the evidence removal function could be further justified. While the authors acknowledge the importance of avoiding adaptation to a single scheme, the rationale for the specific choices (blurred version vs. random noise) isn’t fully explained.
*   **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the results are generally positive, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of scenarios where the method fails or produces misleading saliency maps. Illustrating these cases would enhance the understanding of the method's limitations.
*   **CIFAR-10 Architecture Modification:** The architectural changes for CIFAR-10 are briefly mentioned but lack detail. A more thorough explanation of the changes and their rationale would be useful.
*   **Dependence on Pre-trained Models**: The ImageNet experiments rely on a pre-trained ResNet. While this speeds up training, it introduces a potential bias. The CIFAR-10 experiment does demonstrate training from scratch is possible, but the results are not presented with the same detail.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in explainable AI.
*   The proposed approach is novel and technically sound.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   The real-time performance is a significant advantage.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the objective function and the lack of a thorough ablation study.
*   The limited discussion of failure cases.
*   The reliance on pre-trained models in some experiments.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and technically sound contribution. The proposed method offers a significant improvement in terms of speed compared to existing saliency detection techniques. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The paper is well-written and clearly presented.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding the objective function hyperparameters and failure case analysis.
