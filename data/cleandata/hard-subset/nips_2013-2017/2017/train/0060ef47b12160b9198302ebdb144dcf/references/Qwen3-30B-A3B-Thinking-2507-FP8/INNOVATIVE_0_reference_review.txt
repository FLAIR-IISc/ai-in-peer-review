

# Review: Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers

This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection by training a model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass rather than using iterative methods. The core idea is compelling: instead of spending hundreds of iterations to generate saliency maps for each image (as in methods like Zhou et al. [17] or Fong and Vedaldi [2]), the authors train a masking model that can produce high-quality saliency maps in real-time. This represents a significant shift from current paradigms in the field.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a new model-agnostic saliency detection approach that runs orders of magnitude faster than existing methods, (2) a new saliency metric (s(a,p)) that better evaluates the quality of saliency explanations by directly measuring the smallest sufficient region, and (3) a U-Net based masking model that produces sharp, precise saliency maps. The authors demonstrate their approach on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, showing competitive results on weakly supervised object localization while achieving real-time performance (100 masks per second).

The novelty of this work is substantial. While iterative saliency methods have dominated the field (Zhou et al. [17], Fong and Vedaldi [2]), this paper proposes a fundamentally different paradigm: learning to predict saliency rather than computing it. This is a bold approach that could enable new applications like video saliency, which is currently infeasible with iterative methods. The new metric they introduce is also well-motivated and addresses a real limitation in the field—existing metrics conflate saliency with localization, while the authors' metric directly evaluates the smallest sufficient region concept.

The paper's strengths are clear: the core idea is genuinely novel, the method is practical (real-time performance), and the evaluation is thorough with multiple datasets and metrics. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms existing weakly supervised techniques on ImageNet object localization and achieves results comparable to ground truth bounding boxes according to their new metric. The ablation studies and architectural choices (U-Net with ResNet-50 backbone, random evidence removal scheme) are well-justified.

Minor weaknesses include some imprecise statements about speed (claiming "orders of magnitude faster" without concrete comparisons to the parallel work [2]), and the paper could better address how their approach differs from [2] since it was developed in parallel. The explanation of the evidence removal function (equation 6) could be clearer, and the paper would benefit from more detailed comparison with the state-of-the-art beyond the limited results presented.

However, these are minor concerns compared to the paper's significant contribution. The authors have successfully identified a fundamental limitation in current saliency approaches (iterative computation being too slow for real-world applications) and proposed a novel solution that addresses this limitation while improving explanation quality. The paper's focus on real-world applicability (video saliency, autonomous vehicles) makes it particularly relevant for practitioners.

The significance of this work is high. Saliency methods are crucial for model interpretability, and the ability to generate high-quality explanations in real-time opens up entirely new application areas. The authors have demonstrated their approach works across different datasets and classifiers, suggesting broad applicability. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a new method that is both faster and higher quality than existing approaches.

Compared to other NIPS papers, this work stands out for its bold approach to a well-known problem. While many papers incrementally improve existing methods, this one fundamentally changes the paradigm. The authors have clearly identified a limitation in the field (computational cost of saliency) and proposed a novel solution that addresses it while also improving quality.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (with thorough experiments), clearly written, highly original (the core idea is not just a modification of existing work), and significant (real-time saliency enables new applications). The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper presents a genuinely novel approach to image saliency that is both theoretically sound and practically significant. The authors have successfully shifted the paradigm from iterative computation to model-based prediction, enabling real-time applications while improving explanation quality. This represents a meaningful advance in the field of model interpretability, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to NIPS proceedings. The paper's focus on a real-world limitation (computational speed) and its successful solution make it stand out from incremental work in the field.
