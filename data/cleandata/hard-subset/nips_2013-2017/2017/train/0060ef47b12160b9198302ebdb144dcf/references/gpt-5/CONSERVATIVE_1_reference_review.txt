Summary
The paper proposes an amortized, feed-forward approach to generate saliency masks for image classifiers. Instead of per-image iterative optimization (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), the authors train a U-Net-like “masking model” (with a frozen ResNet-50 encoder) to output a class-conditioned mask in a single pass, by minimizing a loss that combines smoothness (TV), small area, recognition with the preserved region (SSR), and score reduction when the salient region is removed (SDR). To reduce artifacts from masking, they blend in either strong blur or noise as the alternative background and encourage smooth masks. They also introduce a new saliency metric intended to quantify how much of the image is needed to retain confidence. They present qualitative results, ImageNet weakly supervised localization performance (claimed to be near fully supervised VGG), their saliency metric comparisons, and a small CIFAR-10 experiment trained from scratch.

Strengths
- Clear motivation to amortize iterative saliency optimization for speed; single-pass masks are attractive for real-time and video settings.
- Objective combines SSR and SDR aims; use of TV and alternative backgrounds addresses common saliency pitfalls (adversarial artifacts) to some extent.
- Architecture and training heuristics (class embedding/gating, fake class regularization) are sensible and likely effective.
- Reported localization numbers suggest competitive performance under weak supervision, with qualitatively sharp masks.

Weaknesses
- Black-box claim is misleading. Training requires gradients of fc((X,M)) w.r.t. M and hence backprop through the classifier. This is not model-agnostic in the black-box sense (no gradients). At best, the method is “white-box at train time; no access needed at test time.” This distinction is critical and affects applicability.
- The proposed saliency metric is unclear and appears internally inconsistent. The formula s(a,p) = log(ã) log(p) implies that the “max box” (a=1 → log a=0) attains s=0, which would be optimal if “lower is better,” contradicting the claims that the method outperforms the max box baseline. The text also mentions “relative amount of information,” suggesting a ratio rather than a product. Without a correct, well-motivated definition and sensitivity analysis (e.g., capping both a and p near 0/1), the metric’s conclusions are not trustworthy.
- The loss in Eq. (4) is ambiguous/typo-ridden (“+ 3 fc((X,1−M)) 4”). It is not clear whether the third term is −log fc or log fc, nor what the fourth term is (probability, power, or log). This undermines reproducibility and makes it hard to judge technical soundness.
- Missing or incomplete quantitative results. The paper refers to tables/figures supporting claims (e.g., Tables 1–3, Figure 5) but they are not present in the manuscript. Without the actual numbers, standard deviations, and exact baselines, it is difficult to assess the contribution.
- Baseline coverage is insufficient. Important contemporaneous and widely used baselines like CAM/Grad-CAM (2016/2017), Grad-CAM++, and SmoothGrad are not compared or discussed. Iterative “meaningful perturbation” (Fong & Vedaldi) is mentioned but a careful, controlled comparison (including deletion/insertion curves) is missing.
- Generality and practicality are limited: one must train a separate masking network for each classifier. This is a heavy dependency and reduces the claimed model-agnostic appeal. No evidence is provided that a single masking model can generalize across architectures or to novel classes without retraining.
- Limited robustness analysis. While adversarial artifacts are discussed, there is no rigorous evaluation showing that masks are faithful and not exploiting artifacts (e.g., sanity checks, robustness to alternative deletion strategies, cross-model transfer).
- Several training tricks (fake labels, random A, gating term encouragement) are ad hoc and not ablated. It is unclear which components drive gains, how sensitive results are to λ’s, or whether the method overfits to the quirks of the training classifiers.

Quality
- The core idea (amortizing mask optimization) is reasonable, but the paper, as written, has significant clarity and correctness issues (equations, metric). Claims are not sufficiently supported without the missing tables/figures and with incomplete baselines. Reproducibility is hampered by ambiguous loss definitions and limited training details.

Clarity
- The manuscript is readable at a high level, but the technical definitions contain errors/omissions (notably the saliency metric and loss). Several references/figures/tables are missing or misformatted. Important implementation details (optimizer, learning rates, batch sizes, training time/hardware) are not provided.

Originality
- The idea of learning to predict perturbation masks is a natural extension of optimization-based saliency (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi). Combining SSR/SDR in the objective is incremental. Novelty is moderate. The metric, if fixed and justified, could be a contribution, but as presented it is flawed.

Significance
- Speed is a practical advantage, but the requirement to train per classifier and the lack of strong, comprehensive quantitative evidence reduce impact. Without rigorous comparisons to established baselines and robust faithfulness tests, it is hard to recommend adoption.

Questions for authors
1. Please clarify the exact loss in Eq. (4), including signs and operations for terms 3 and 4, and provide the full training details (optimizer, schedule, batch size).
2. Do you backpropagate through the classifier during training? If so, please revise the “black-box/model-agnostic” claims and discuss applicability to truly black-box settings.
3. Correct and justify the saliency metric; report its behavior on edge cases (a→1, p→1; a→0, p→0), and include sensitivity analyses and correlations with standard deletion/insertion metrics.
4. Provide complete tables/figures, with comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++/SmoothGrad and meaningful perturbation under matched deletion schemes and compute budgets.
5. Ablate the objective (remove SSR/SDR/TV/AV terms), the choice of A (blur vs. noise), and the fake label trick to understand contributions.
6. Can a single masking model generalize across multiple classifiers, or to new classes? What is the training cost per classifier?

Pros
- Addresses an important practical limitation (speed) of iterative perturbation saliency.
- Conceptually simple and potentially useful for video/interactive scenarios.
- Qualitative maps look sharp.

Cons
- Misleading “black-box” claim; unclear metric; ambiguous loss.
- Missing quantitative evidence and key baselines.
- Per-classifier training required; limited robustness analysis; reproducibility concerns.
