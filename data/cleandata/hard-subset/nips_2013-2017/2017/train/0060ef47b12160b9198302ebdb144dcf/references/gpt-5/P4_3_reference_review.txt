Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a trainable, feed-forward masking network that produces saliency maps for a given image and class in real time. The core idea is to learn a class-conditional mask that is simultaneously a Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and a Smallest Destroying Region (SDR), while discouraging artifacts and promoting smoothness. The objective combines: (i) total variation (TV) for smoothness, (ii) an area penalty to encourage compact masks, (iii) a term encouraging high class probability on the preserved region (SSR), and (iv) a term encouraging low class probability when the region is removed (SDR). To mitigate “introduced evidence” from masking, the authors replace the removed content by a randomly sampled alternative image A (blurred input or random color plus noise), and use TV to discourage adversarial artifacts.

Architecturally, the explainer is a U-Net with a frozen, ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-50 encoder and skip connections. A class embedding gates high-level features (via a sigmoid inner product) to produce class-specific masks. A two-channel head with a nonstandard ratio nonlinearity generates a low-resolution mask that is upsampled. Training includes a “fake label” strategy (30% of iterations) to ensure the network uses the class selector.

The paper introduces a saliency metric based on the SSR notion: compute the tightest rectangle enclosing the salient region, resize it to the classifier’s input, and define s(a, p) using the crop area fraction a (thresholded at 0.05) and the class probability p on the crop; lower is better. Experiments on ImageNet with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 black-box classifiers show improved weakly supervised localization vs. baselines and prior saliency methods, and a better score under the proposed metric. The approach runs at >100 images/s and generalizes to CIFAR-10 with a scratch-trained encoder.

This work builds on and differs from: gradient/backprop-based explanations (Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), CAM-style localization (Zhou et al.), and optimization-based perturbation saliency (Fong & Vedaldi; Cao et al.). It aims to retain the interpretability of perturbation saliency while removing the iterative cost by training a general explainer network.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The combined SSR/SDR objective is well-motivated, and the mitigation of introduced evidence via randomized A and TV is sensible. The “fake label” trick and class-conditional gating are reasonable design choices. However:
  - The method appears to require backpropagation through the target classifier during training (the loss depends on fc and gradients must flow to the mask). This conflicts with calling the classifier “black box” and “model agnostic.” Please clarify whether gradients from the classifier are used; if so, the method is applicable to differentiable, white-box access during training, not true black-box settings.
  - The proposed metric s(a, p) is underspecified (the equation in the text omits an operator between the logs). Its dependence on crop resizing without aspect-ratio preservation may bias comparisons across classes/scales; its sensitivity to the 0.05 area threshold is not analyzed. There is no study of correlation with human judgments or with existing saliency faithfulness metrics.
  - Experimental details are incomplete in the provided text: tables are referenced but not shown; per-method hyperparameters, threshold sensitivity (e.g., 0.5 mask threshold), and ablations on the loss weights (λ1–λ4), the alternative-image sampling, and the “fake label” rate are not reported. It would strengthen the paper to include these.
  - The evaluation compares to several baselines, but key contemporaneous methods like Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017), SmoothGrad (Smilkov et al., 2017), and Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017) are not included. These are standard saliency references and should be compared both qualitatively and quantitatively.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear and well organized with a good motivation, definitions (SSR/SDR), and a readable architecture description. The discussion of introduced evidence is useful.
- Minor clarity issues: the exact formula for the saliency metric (missing operator); explicit statement about gradient flow to/from the classifier; details of the upsampling and final resolution; and more explicit training details (data splits, optimizer, learning rates, schedule, and runtime measurements) would improve reproducibility.
- Figures and tables are referenced as demonstrating large gains; ensure they are detailed and legible in the final version.

Originality:
- The main novelty lies in training a feed-forward, class-conditional explainer to approximate perturbation-based saliency at inference time, jointly optimizing SSR and SDR terms, and the new metric. While related to Fong & Vedaldi and CAM-style methods, the learn-once, explain-many approach with the combined objective appears original. The artifact-mitigation via randomized alternatives is a thoughtful addition.
- The metric idea is interesting but would benefit from more rigorous validation and comparison to existing faithfulness measures.

Significance:
- If the empirical improvements hold, a real-time, high-quality saliency method is practically valuable for interactive and video settings and for weakly supervised localization. Training an explainer per classifier is a reasonable cost if inference is frequent.
- Impact is moderated by the need for differentiable access during training (if required), and by the lack of comparison to strong modern baselines. The new metric could be useful, but its adoption will depend on further validation.

Pros:
- Clear problem formulation (SSR/SDR) and well-motivated objective.
- Practical, fast, single-pass explainer with strong qualitative results.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization without bounding-box supervision.
- Sensible strategies to mitigate introduced evidence; class-conditional gating and training tricks are neat.

Cons:
- Likely not “black box” in the strict sense; clarify gradient requirements.
- Missing comparisons to Grad-CAM, SmoothGrad, Integrated Gradients.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses; metric definition and validation need tightening.
- Potential confound of using an ImageNet-pretrained encoder and overlap with the target classifier family; limited analysis of cross-classifier generalization and failure cases.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify whether and how gradients flow through the classifier during training; if not, explain the optimization path. If yes, revise terminology (“works with any differentiable classifier”) and discuss applicability to truly black-box models.
- Provide full quantitative results with confidence intervals, ablations on λ1–λ4, A-sampling scheme, fake-label rate, and mask thresholding. Report runtime vs. baselines.
- Add comparisons against Grad-CAM, SmoothGrad, and Integrated Gradients on the same metrics (localization, the proposed metric), and include deletion/insertion-style faithfulness tests if possible.
- Validate the proposed metric: fix the formula, study sensitivity to the 0.05 threshold and aspect-ratio distortion, and report correlation with localization and human assessments.
- Explore generalization across classifiers: can one masking model trained on classifier A explain classifier B without retraining?

Recommendation:
- Borderline accept. The approach is promising and potentially impactful, but the paper would benefit from clarifying the black-box claim, adding comparisons to contemporaneous baselines, and strengthening empirical validation and ablations.
