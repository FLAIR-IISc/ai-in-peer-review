

# Peer Review: "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection for black box classifiers that achieves real-time performance while producing high-quality saliency maps. The authors recognize that current state-of-the-art saliency methods either require iterative optimization (which is slow) or produce low-quality explanations (like gradient-based methods). Their key insight is to train a masking model (based on U-Net architecture) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, rather than optimizing for each image individually. They introduce a new objective function that balances multiple requirements (smoothness, small region size, classification confidence on preserved region, and low confidence on removed region) and propose a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept. The paper demonstrates that their approach outperforms existing methods in speed, saliency map quality, and localisation accuracy on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear methodology and thorough experimental evaluation. The authors address important challenges in saliency detection, particularly the problem of introduced evidence when masking images, and propose a thoughtful solution using a random evidence removal scheme and a total variation penalty.

However, there are several quality concerns that need addressing:

1. **Lack of theoretical justification for the objective function**: The paper proposes an objective function with four terms (equation 4) but provides no theoretical basis for the specific weights (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4) or the combination of terms. While the authors justify each term individually, they don't explain why this particular combination works best or provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component. For instance, how much of the performance improvement comes from the masking model itself versus the specific objective function?

2. **Insufficient generalization analysis**: The paper claims to be "model agnostic" but the masking model is trained on specific black-box classifiers. The paper doesn't investigate how well a masking model trained on one classifier generalizes to other classifiers. This is a critical limitation for a claimed "model agnostic" method.

3. **Missing bias analysis**: The paper mentions in the conclusion that "our model might have biases of its own" but doesn't analyze or quantify these biases. This is particularly important for a paper focused on explainability.

4. **Reproducibility issues**: The paper lacks sufficient detail for reproduction. For example, it doesn't specify the exact optimizer, learning rate, batch size, or training duration for the masking model. The "random scheme" for evidence removal (section 4.1) is described but not quantified in terms of how it affects results.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, the related work provides a good overview of the field, and the methodology is explained with sufficient mathematical detail and architecture diagrams.

However, there are clarity issues that need addressing:

1. **The new saliency metric (equation 3)**: The paper states "a low value for the saliency metric is a characteristic of good saliency detectors" but doesn't provide examples of good and bad metric values to help readers understand the scale. The interpretation as "the relative amount of information between an indicator variable with probability p and an indicator variable with probability a" is somewhat confusing without more explanation.

2. **Training process (section 4.1)**: The description of using "fake classes" to prevent the model from ignoring the class selector is somewhat vague. The paper states "we set the probability of the fake label occurrence to 30%" but doesn't explain how this is implemented in practice or why 30% is optimal.

3. **Speed claims**: The paper claims to achieve "orders-of-magnitude faster" results than iterative methods but doesn't provide specific speed measurements (e.g., milliseconds per image) for the iterative methods they're comparing against.

## Originality

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI by proposing a model-based approach to saliency detection rather than iterative optimization. While the related work section mentions [2] which is "very recently (and in parallel to this work)", the authors' approach of training a masking model to predict saliency maps rather than optimizing for each image is novel.

However, I'm concerned about potential overlap with recent work. The paper cites [2] as "very recently (and in parallel to this work)" but doesn't clearly distinguish how their approach differs from [2]. The authors should explicitly clarify the key differences between their method and [2], especially since [2] also proposes a black-box method using gradient descent in image space.

The introduction of the new saliency metric based on the SSR concept is also novel and well-motivated, though the paper doesn't sufficiently contrast it with existing metrics like the one used in [1].

## Significance

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in explainable AI with real-world applications. The ability to generate saliency maps in real-time enables new applications like video-saliency for autonomous vehicles, as the authors mention in the conclusion. The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods in both speed and quality.

However, the significance could be strengthened by:
1. Providing more concrete examples of real-world applications that would benefit from real-time saliency
2. A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the method and how they might be addressed
3. Clearer evidence of how this work advances the state of the art beyond previous research

The paper's claim that their method "achieves better localisation results" is supported by the experiments, but the paper could better articulate why this is important for explainable AI beyond just achieving better numbers on benchmarks.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with clear motivation, novel approach, and solid experimental validation. The proposed method addresses a real problem in explainable AI with significant practical implications. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, particularly with the new saliency metric and the model-based approach to real-time saliency detection.

However, the paper has significant limitations that need to be addressed before acceptance:

1. Lack of theoretical justification for the objective function
2. Insufficient analysis of the "model agnostic" claim
3. Missing bias analysis of the masking model
4. Insufficient detail for reproducibility

## Recommendations

**Major Revisions Required**: The paper has promising ideas but needs substantial revisions to address the quality and clarity issues identified above. The authors should:

1. Provide ablation studies to show the contribution of each component of their objective function
2. Conduct experiments to evaluate how well a masking model trained on one classifier generalizes to other classifiers
3. Add a section analyzing potential biases in the masking model itself
4. Include more detailed experimental setup information for reproducibility
5. Clarify the distinction between their method and [2] in the related work and introduction
6. Provide concrete examples of the saliency metric values to help readers understand the scale

**Strengths**: The paper addresses an important problem in explainable AI with a novel approach that enables real-time saliency detection. The new saliency metric is well-motivated and provides a better way to evaluate explanations. The experimental results are thorough and show clear improvements over baselines.

**Weaknesses**: The paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for the objective function, doesn't properly address the "model agnostic" claim, and doesn't analyze the potential biases in the masking model. The paper also lacks sufficient detail for reproduction.

**Recommendation**: **Reject with Resubmission** (after major revisions). The paper has significant potential but requires substantial revisions to address the quality and clarity concerns before it can be considered for publication at NeurIPS.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Novel approach to real-time saliency detection
- Well-motivated new saliency metric
- Strong experimental results demonstrating clear improvements over baselines
- Addresses a meaningful problem in explainable AI with practical applications

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Lack of theoretical justification for key components
- Insufficient analysis of the "model agnostic" claim
- Missing bias analysis of the masking model
- Insufficient detail for reproducibility
- Potential overlap with [2] not clearly addressed

The authors should carefully address all the concerns raised in this review before resubmitting. If they can provide the requested analyses and clarifications, this paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field of explainable AI.
