

# Peer Review: "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses the critical problem of model interpretability for image classifiers by introducing a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method that operates in real-time. The authors propose a masking model that predicts saliency maps through a single feed-forward pass, overcoming the computational limitations of iterative approaches like those in [1, 2, 17]. The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, (2) a U-Net-based masking model architecture, and (3) a training methodology that prevents the model from ignoring the class selector. This work builds upon but significantly advances the literature on model interpretability, particularly the recent work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] which shares a similar objective but lacks the real-time capability.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Innovation**: The authors provide a well-motivated objective function (equation 4) that balances mask smoothness, minimal area, high classification confidence on preserved regions, and low confidence on removed regions. Their solution to adversarial artifacts through randomized evidence removal (equation 2) and TV regularization is particularly thoughtful and addresses a critical issue in saliency detection.

2. **Novel Metric**: The proposed saliency metric (s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p)) is a significant contribution. It directly evaluates the quality of saliency maps by measuring how small a region can be while maintaining classification confidence, which is more aligned with the SSR concept than existing metrics like localization accuracy. This metric is well-justified and provides a more principled evaluation framework.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task (36.7% error rate for ResNet-50 model, compared to 34.3% for fully supervised VGG). The CIFAR-10 experiments demonstrate the method's generalizability without relying on pre-trained models, which is an important validation.

4. **Practical Impact**: The real-time capability (100 masks/second) enables new applications like video saliency, which the authors appropriately highlight in their future work section. This is a significant practical contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees about why the proposed masking model architecture works well. The authors don't provide analysis of the optimization landscape or generalization properties of their approach.

2. **Incomplete Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper references Fong and Vedaldi [2] but doesn't adequately compare with other recent saliency methods. For instance, Grad-CAM [18] and Grad-CAM++ [19] are now standard benchmarks in the field and should be included for a fair comparison.

3. **Mask Smoothness vs. Precision Trade-off**: The paper emphasizes mask smoothness to prevent adversarial artifacts but doesn't thoroughly analyze the trade-off between smoothness and precision. Figure 5 shows sharp masks, but it's unclear whether they're as precise as methods without TV regularization.

4. **Insufficient Analysis of Model Biases**: The authors briefly mention "the masking model might have biases of its own" but don't investigate this thoroughly. A more rigorous analysis of how the model's biases affect the saliency maps would strengthen the paper.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes significant contributions to the field of model interpretability. The method is technically sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the new metric provides a valuable evaluation framework. The real-time capability represents a substantial practical advancement over existing methods.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it's technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, original (with clear differentiation from prior work), and significant (enabling new applications like video saliency).

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper's contributions are substantial, the methodology is well-executed, and the results are compelling. While there are minor weaknesses in theoretical analysis and comparison with some recent work, these do not undermine the core contribution.

## Pro and Con Arguments

*Pro:*
- Real-time capability (100 masks/sec) enables new applications like video saliency
- New saliency metric provides more principled evaluation than existing methods
- Outperforms state-of-the-art on weakly supervised object localization
- Model-agnostic and works with any black box classifier

*Con:*
- Lack of theoretical guarantees for the proposed method
- Incomplete comparison with recent saliency detection methods (e.g., Grad-CAM)
- Insufficient analysis of mask smoothness vs. precision trade-off

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief theoretical discussion about why the proposed objective function is well-suited for learning saliency maps.

2. Include a comparison with Grad-CAM and Grad-CAM++ methods, which are now standard benchmarks.

3. Provide more detailed analysis of how the TV penalty parameter affects the precision-smoothness trade-off.

4. Conduct a more thorough analysis of the masking model's biases and how they might affect the saliency maps.

This paper represents an important step forward in making model interpretability practical for real-world applications, and I believe it will be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
