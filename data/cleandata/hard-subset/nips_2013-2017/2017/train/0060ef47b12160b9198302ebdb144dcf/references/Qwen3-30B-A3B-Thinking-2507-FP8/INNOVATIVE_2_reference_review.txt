

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper presents a novel approach to image saliency detection that fundamentally shifts from iterative methods to a trainable model-based approach. The authors propose training a masking model (based on a U-Net architecture) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, rather than using computationally expensive iterative optimization. The key innovation is the paradigm shift toward real-time saliency detection, which enables applications like video-saliency that were previously infeasible.

The paper makes several significant contributions: a new saliency metric (s(a,p)) that better evaluates interpretability than existing localisation metrics, a detailed analysis of "introduced evidence" during saliency computation (a critical but overlooked issue in prior work), and a practical, real-time implementation that achieves 100 saliency masks per second. The authors demonstrate their method outperforms existing weakly supervised approaches on ImageNet (achieving 36.7% localisation error compared to 34.3% for fully supervised VGG), and the new metric shows their method produces saliency maps comparable to ground truth bounding boxes.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with strong connections to related work. It effectively distinguishes itself from prior work by addressing the real-time limitation of existing methods (Zhou et al., Cao et al., Fong and Vedaldi) while introducing a more meaningful evaluation metric. The authors' insight that saliency and localisation are not equivalent is particularly valuable and well-supported by their new metric.

The most compelling aspect of this work is its bold rethinking of the saliency detection problem. Rather than incrementally improving existing iterative methods, the authors propose a completely different paradigm that enables new applications. This aligns with NIPS' emphasis on novel directions rather than incremental improvements. The paper's emphasis on real-world applicability (e.g., video-saliency for autonomous vehicles) further enhances its significance.

The methodological approach is sound, though not perfect. The authors acknowledge that their masking model is trained to minimize a specific objective function (equation 4), and they provide thorough experimental validation across ImageNet and CIFAR-10. The training process with the "fake class" technique is clever and addresses a key challenge in training such models. The paper's focus on practical implementation details (e.g., how they handle image resizing, evidence removal) demonstrates strong engineering sensibility.

Minor weaknesses include: the paper doesn't fully explore the theoretical limits of the approach (e.g., why the specific objective function parameters work well), and the generalization to unseen black-box classifiers could be further explored. However, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's bold new direction.

This work represents a significant step toward practical, real-time explainability for image classifiers. The authors have not only proposed a new method but have fundamentally changed how we think about saliency detection - from a post-hoc analysis problem to a model-based prediction problem. The real-time capability alone would be a major contribution, but the new metric and the thoughtful analysis of "introduced evidence" elevate this to a truly novel contribution.

The paper advances the state of the art in explainable AI by making saliency detection practical for real-world applications. While the method is not perfect, the novelty and practical significance of the approach outweigh the minor methodological limitations. The authors have not merely improved existing techniques but have opened a new research direction that could significantly impact how we build and interpret image classifiers.

In summary, this paper makes a bold contribution to the field with a novel approach that enables real-time saliency detection. The authors have addressed a critical limitation in the field (computational speed) while introducing a more meaningful metric for evaluating saliency. The work has clear practical significance and opens up new research directions, particularly in video analysis and real-time explainable AI. For a NIPS reviewer who values novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance.
