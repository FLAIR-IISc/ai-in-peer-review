Summary
The paper proposes a trainable “masking model” that predicts class-conditional saliency maps for a given image classifier in a single forward pass. The objective encourages both smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR): the mask should preserve the class score when kept and reduce it when removed, while being smooth and small. To mitigate artifacts introduced by masking, the authors mix the input with either a strong blur of the image or random color+noise and enforce total-variation regularization. The masking network is a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder (frozen), gated by a learned class embedding at the coarsest level, and trained against the black-box classifier’s softmax outputs. They introduce a saliency metric s(a,p)=log(a~)−log(p), where a~ is the area of the tightest bounding box enclosing the mask (floored at 0.05), and p is the classifier’s probability on the crop. Experiments on ImageNet report improved weakly supervised localization over prior methods and better scores on the proposed metric; a CIFAR-10 experiment shows training from scratch.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to optimization-based saliency via meaningful perturbations (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), occlusion/patch-removal methods (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014; Zhou et al., 2014/2016), and backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al., 2013; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop). The main difference is amortizing the mask optimization with a learned, class-conditional predictor that emulates the optimization objective, akin to meta-learning a saliency generator conditioned on the target classifier. The paper also links to top-down attention/class activation mapping (CAM), though a direct comparison to CAM/Grad-CAM is missing.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: amortizing saliency optimization into a fast predictor for a specific classifier is novel and practically useful for real-time use and video.
- Objective combines SDR and SSR with explicit regularization and randomized evidence-removal schemes to reduce adversarial artifacts; the fake-class training trick encourages true class conditioning.
- Architecture leverages a simple class-gated U-Net producing sharp, high-resolution masks.
- The proposed crop-based SSR metric is intuitive, avoids masking artifacts, and complements localization-based evaluation.
- Empirical results on ImageNet suggest competitive weakly supervised localization (close to fully supervised VGG) and improved SSR metric vs baselines; real-time inference achieved.

Weaknesses
- “Black-box” and “model-agnostic” claims are overstated. Training requires differentiability and backpropagation through the target classifier; a separate masking model must be trained per classifier. This is not black-box in the function-evaluation sense.
- Evaluation lacks important baselines and ablations:
  - No comparison to CAM/Grad-CAM (widely used for localization/saliency at the time).
  - No ablations on the loss terms (SSR vs SDR, TV, area penalty), class embedding, or the randomized A choices; no study of thresholding the mask.
  - No fidelity metrics such as deletion/insertion curves or pointing game; the proposed metric is only SSR-like and may bias toward compact part-based evidence.
- Details are sometimes unclear or inconsistent:
  - Equation 4 formatting is ambiguous (is the last term raised to a power, and what are λ’s actual values? “10 3” suggests 10−3 but is unclear).
  - How exactly is the “tightest” crop defined for a soft mask (threshold?), and is that threshold tuned on validation?
  - The claim that the method generalizes without access to the classifier post-training is true only for that specific classifier; unclear transfer to different architectures or to out-of-distribution data.
- Limited analysis of failure modes and artifact robustness; the argument that the model “acts as a regularizer” against adversarial masks is plausible but untested.
- Comparisons primarily to iterative methods are favorable but training cost is nontrivial; a discussion of train-time vs amortized test-time trade-offs is missing.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with thoughtful objective design (SSR+SDR) and practical mitigations for artifacts (TV, randomized A). The empirical support is promising but incomplete due to missing baselines and ablations, and some parameter/implementation details are under-specified.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable with good intuition and figures, but important mathematical and experimental details are ambiguous (loss coefficients, exponent, crop definition, mask thresholding). References are inconsistently formatted. Tightening notation and clarifying training/evaluation protocols would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The core novelty is amortizing meaningful-perturbation-style optimization with a learned, class-conditional saliency predictor, plus a simple SSR-inspired metric. While components (U-Net, class embeddings, TV, mix-with-blur/noise) are known, the combination and framing are original and useful.

Significance
Potentially impactful for practitioners needing fast, high-resolution, faithful explanations, especially in video settings. If supported with stronger baselines and fidelity tests, this could be a solid step toward deployable saliency. As written, significance is moderate to high but tempered by the evaluation gaps.

Questions for authors
- Please clarify Equation 4: exact loss, coefficient values (λ1–λ4), and whether any term uses an exponent. Provide ablations on these choices.
- How is the “tightest crop” computed from a soft mask (threshold value, sensitivity analysis)?
- Compare against CAM/Grad-CAM (and, if possible, occlusion baselines) for both localization and the proposed metric.
- Report deletion/insertion curves to assess faithfulness independently of box overlap.
- Provide ablations: remove SDR term; remove fake-class trick; vary A; train encoder vs freeze; effect of mask resolution.
- Does the masking model transfer across classifiers (train on ResNet-50, test on GoogleNet) or across datasets?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, practical idea with real-time inference and sharp masks.
- Thoughtful objective targeting both SSR and SDR with artifact mitigation.
- Promising results on ImageNet; intuitive new metric.

Cons for acceptance
- Overstated black-box claim; per-classifier training required.
- Missing key baselines (CAM/Grad-CAM) and fidelity analyses.
- Ambiguities in loss and evaluation details; limited ablations.

Overall rating: Weak accept (assuming authors add missing comparisons/clarifications); otherwise borderline.
Reviewer confidence: High.
