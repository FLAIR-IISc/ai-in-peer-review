**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. The central idea is to train a separate, dedicated neural network—termed a "masking model"—to predict a saliency mask for any given image and class in a single feed-forward pass. This approach contrasts with existing methods that are either fast but produce low-quality maps (e.g., gradient-based methods like Simonyan et al., 2013) or produce high-quality maps but are computationally expensive due to per-instance iterative optimization (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017).

The authors formulate a comprehensive objective function for training the masking model. This objective is designed to produce masks that satisfy several desirable properties simultaneously: 1) the masked-in region should be a "Smallest Sufficient Region" (SSR), meaning it is small yet sufficient for the classifier to make a confident prediction; 2) the masked-out region should be a "Smallest Destroying Region" (SDR), meaning its removal causes the classification confidence to drop significantly; 3) the mask should be smooth to avoid adversarial artifacts, enforced by a Total Variation (TV) penalty.

To evaluate their method, the authors test it on the ImageNet dataset using AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50 as black-box models. They show that their method achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly-supervised object localization task. Furthermore, they introduce a new metric for saliency that measures the concentration of class-relevant information in a cropped region, and demonstrate that their method outperforms others on this metric as well. The method's applicability is also shown on the CIFAR-10 dataset. The primary contribution is a framework for generating high-quality saliency maps in real-time, amortizing the computational cost of saliency detection through a one-time training process.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed objective function (Eq. 4) is well-motivated, elegantly combining the complementary concepts of SSR and SDR with regularization terms for mask area and smoothness. The discussion of "introduced evidence" and the potential for adversarial masks is insightful, and the proposed mitigation strategies (e.g., randomizing the background between blurred and noisy constant color) are practical and well-reasoned. The choice of a U-Net architecture for the masking model is appropriate for the image-to-image prediction task. The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on standard benchmarks (ImageNet, CIFAR-10) against multiple, diverse black-box classifiers. The results demonstrate a clear and significant improvement over previous methods in both localization accuracy and the newly proposed saliency metric.

A minor weakness in the technical presentation is the lack of a sensitivity analysis for the hyperparameters (λ1 to λ4) in the objective function. The chosen values are provided, but there is no discussion of how they were selected or how they affect the trade-off between the different terms in the objective. Such an analysis would improve the completeness of the work and aid in reproducibility.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for a fast, high-quality saliency method is established effectively in the introduction. The concepts of SSR and SDR are explained well and provide a strong conceptual foundation for the objective function. The architecture of the masking model and the training procedure, including the clever trick of using "fake" labels to ensure the model attends to the class input, are described in sufficient detail. The figures are illustrative and support the paper's claims effectively, especially Figure 5, which provides a compelling qualitative comparison against other methods. The paper provides enough information for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work, contingent on the hyperparameter sensitivity mentioned above.

**Originality:** The core contribution—framing saliency detection as a prediction problem to be solved by a separate, trained model rather than a per-instance optimization problem—is highly original. This represents a significant conceptual shift from prior perturbation-based works like Fong & Vedaldi (2017), which this paper correctly identifies as parallel work. While the components of the objective function (e.g., minimizing area, maximizing/minimizing classifier score, TV regularization) are familiar concepts in the saliency literature, their combination into a single objective that balances both SSR and SDR criteria is novel. The introduction of a new, principled saliency metric is also a valuable and original contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant. The ability to generate high-quality saliency maps at over 100 frames per second fundamentally changes the landscape of classifier interpretability. Previous high-quality methods were too slow for interactive or large-scale analysis. This work enables real-time applications, such as explaining the decisions of models in autonomous vehicles or providing immediate visual feedback in interactive systems. The method's strong performance on the weakly-supervised localization task demonstrates its practical utility and advances the state of the art. Given its model-agnostic nature and superior performance, the proposed approach is likely to be widely adopted by both researchers and practitioners seeking to understand and debug deep learning models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Framework:** The paper introduces a novel and powerful paradigm for saliency detection by training a predictive model, enabling real-time performance that was previously unattainable for high-quality methods.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The method significantly outperforms existing weakly-supervised and saliency-based approaches on the standard ImageNet object localization benchmark.
3.  **Well-Motivated Method:** The objective function is built on a solid conceptual foundation (SSR and SDR) and includes thoughtful considerations for preventing adversarial artifacts.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** The real-time capability opens up new avenues for research and application in video analysis and interactive model debugging, making the work highly significant for the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **High Training Cost:** The method shifts the computational burden from inference time to a substantial one-time training phase, which requires a large dataset and significant resources. This trade-off could be discussed more explicitly.
2.  **Explaining a Black Box with Another Black Box:** The method uses a complex neural network to explain another. This raises philosophical questions about the faithfulness of the explanation and whether the masking model might introduce its own biases, a point the authors acknowledge but do not investigate deeply.
3.  **Lack of Hyperparameter Analysis:** The paper does not provide an ablation study or sensitivity analysis for the crucial weighting parameters in the loss function, which slightly detracts from its technical completeness.

In conclusion, the paper's strengths, particularly its novelty, significance, and strong empirical results, far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a complete and impactful piece of research that advances the field of model interpretability.
