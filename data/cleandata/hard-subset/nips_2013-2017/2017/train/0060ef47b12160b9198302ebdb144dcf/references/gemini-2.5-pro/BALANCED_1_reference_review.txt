This paper proposes a novel, model-based approach for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers in real-time. The core problem addressed is the trade-off between speed and quality in existing saliency methods: gradient-based methods are fast but often produce noisy, low-quality maps, while optimization-based perturbation methods produce higher-quality maps but are prohibitively slow. The authors reframe the task from a per-instance optimization problem to a learning problem. They train a U-Net-based generator model to predict a saliency mask for a given image and target class in a single forward pass. This generator is trained to optimize a carefully designed objective function that encourages the predicted mask to be smooth, small, and effective at both identifying the "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) for classification and the "smallest destroying region" (SDR). A key aspect of the work is that the generator is trained using a target classifier as a black box, only requiring access to its output probabilities. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric to evaluate the information density of the salient region. Experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 show that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task and produces visually compelling, sharp saliency maps, all while being orders of magnitude faster at inference time than iterative methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The main contribution—learning a generative model to approximate the output of a complex saliency optimization—is highly original and significant. It represents a paradigm shift from slow, iterative, per-image optimization to fast, amortized inference. This leap in efficiency enables real-time applications like video saliency, which is a major advance for the field of model interpretability.

2.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-executed. The design of the objective function (Equation 4), which combines notions of SSR, SDR, mask area, and smoothness (TV regularization), is principled and captures multiple desirable properties of a good saliency map. The choice of a U-Net architecture is well-suited for generating dense, high-resolution masks. The training strategy, including the use of "fake" labels to ensure the model attends to the class selector, is a clever solution to a potential training pitfall.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation.
    *   The performance on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet is state-of-the-art, outperforming previous methods by a significant margin (Table 2). Achieving an error rate of 36.7% is impressive for a method not explicitly trained with localization data.
    *   The qualitative results (Figure 5) are compelling. The generated masks are sharp, precise, and clearly highlight semantically meaningful regions, comparing favorably to other methods.
    *   The experiments across multiple black-box architectures (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) and a different dataset (CIFAR-10) demonstrate the general applicability of the approach.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and generally easy to follow. The concepts of SSR and SDR are explained well, and the motivation for the new saliency metric is clear.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Faithfulness of the Explanation:** A critical aspect of any explanation method is its *faithfulness*—does it truly reflect the reasoning of the model being explained? This paper trains a separate, powerful masking model (a U-Net) to generate explanations. A major concern is that this masking model may not be learning the true saliency patterns of the black-box classifier, but rather learning a highly effective object segmentation/localization function that happens to correlate well with the classifier's predictions and thus minimizes the training objective. For example, if a classifier incorrectly uses background "snow" to detect "polar bears," would this method highlight the snow (a faithful explanation) or the bear (a plausible but unfaithful explanation)? The paper does not sufficiently address this fundamental challenge.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors could strengthen the paper significantly by including experiments designed to test faithfulness. For instance, they could train a black-box model on a dataset with known spurious correlations and demonstrate that their method correctly identifies these spurious features as salient.

2.  **Clarity of the Objective Function:** The objective function in Equation 4 is confusingly presented, which hinders reproducibility and raises questions about its technical formulation.
    *   The text describes four terms (TV, Area, SSR, SDR), but the equation seems to combine the Area and SSR terms into `λ2 * AV(M) * log(fc(φ(X,M)))`. For a loss function to be minimized, this term should decrease when `AV(M)` is small and `fc(...)` is high (close to 1). However, `log(fc)` is negative and approaches 0 as `fc` approaches 1. If `λ2 > 0`, minimizing the term would encourage making `fc` *smaller*, which is the opposite of the stated goal. This needs urgent clarification. Is there a typo (e.g., should it be `-log(fc)` or is `λ2` negative)?
    *   The text refers to four regularization parameters `λ1` to `λ4`, but the equation only lists `λ1`, `λ2`, and `λ3`.
    *   **Suggestion:** Please rewrite and clarify Equation 4 and the surrounding text. Provide a clear breakdown of each term of the loss function, its purpose, and why its sign and form are correct for minimization.

3.  **High Training Overhead:** While the method is extremely fast at inference time, it requires training a large, separate masking model for *every single black-box classifier* one wishes to explain. This is a significant computational cost and a practical limitation compared to methods that can be applied to any model without retraining. This trade-off is central to the paper's contribution and should be discussed more explicitly.
    *   **Suggestion:** Add a discussion in the conclusion or a dedicated "Limitations" section that acknowledges this trade-off between high one-time training cost and fast amortized inference.

4.  **The New Saliency Metric:** While the idea behind the new metric is interesting, its formulation has some potential issues.
    *   The reliance on cropping and resizing can alter the input distribution in ways that might unpredictably affect the classifier's performance, a known confounder in saliency evaluation.
    *   The threshold `a_tilde = max(a, 0.05)` is arbitrary. A sensitivity analysis showing how the metric changes with this value would be beneficial. The information-theoretic justification feels somewhat informal; it could be strengthened or presented more cautiously.

### Final Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and powerful idea for generating real-time saliency maps, backed by strong empirical results. The shift from per-instance optimization to a learned, amortized approach is a significant contribution to the field. However, the work is weakened by a lack of discussion on the critical issue of faithfulness, a confusing and potentially erroneous formulation of the core objective function, and a downplaying of the high training overhead.

Despite these concerns, the strengths and novelty of the core idea are substantial. I believe the paper is a valuable contribution that will inspire future work. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, especially the clarification of the objective function and a more thorough discussion of the faithfulness problem, in the final version of the paper.
