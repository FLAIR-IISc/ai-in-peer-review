- Summary: Proposes a trainable U-Net that predicts saliency maps in one forward pass and introduces an SSR-based saliency metric, claiming faster, higher-quality localization than iterative/optimization methods.  
- Relation to prior work: Positions itself against gradient-based and iterative/removal methods and as a learned, feed-forward alternative to optimization-based approaches (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi).  
- Strength — Speed: Achieves real-time saliency detection, enabling applications like video saliency.  
- Strength — Novelty: Learning a saliency-map predictor is a novel departure from existing iterative/optimization techniques.  
- Strength — Experimental validation: Comprehensive experiments on ImageNet (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and CIFAR-10 demonstrate strong empirical results.  
- Strength — New metric: Introduces the SSR saliency metric with an information-theoretic justification that is more nuanced than simple localization accuracy.  
- Strength — Adversarial artifacts: Proposes techniques to mitigate adversarial artifacts from evidence removal.  
- Strength — Presentation: Paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.  
- Weakness — Training complexity: Training requires careful tuning of many hyperparameters, fake labels, and random removal schemes; sensitivity and robustness are not fully explored.  
- Weakness — Unexplained design choices: The 30% fake label probability and the specific choice of a U-Net architecture lack thorough justification.  
- Weakness — Dependence on classifier: Saliency quality depends on the black-box classifier; more analysis across diverse architectures is needed.  
- Weakness — Limited ablations: Missing targeted ablation studies (e.g., contribution of pretrained ResNet encoder, impact of each loss term).  
- Qualitative assessment: Method is technically sound, original, and significant, but training complexity raises reproducibility concerns.  
- Pro for acceptance: Addresses explainability, offers a novel and sound solution, strong benchmarks, a new metric, and real-time performance.  
- Con against acceptance: Complex training, limited ablations, and reliance on classifier quality.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept — significant contribution despite some minor weaknesses.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
