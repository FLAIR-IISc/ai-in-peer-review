Summary
The paper proposes a fast, learned approach to generate saliency maps for image classifiers. Rather than optimizing a mask per image (as in iterative perturbation methods), the authors train a “masking model” to predict, in one forward pass, a class-conditional mask that simultaneously (i) preserves classification when applied (smallest sufficient region, SSR) and (ii) destroys classification when removed (smallest destroying region, SDR). The objective includes: total variation for smoothness, area penalty, a term encouraging high class probability on the preserved region, and a term encouraging low class probability on the removed region. To mitigate artifact-induced “introduced evidence,” they randomize the alternative image used when masking (blur vs noisy color) and penalize non-smooth masks. The masking model is a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and a class embedding “feature filter” at the highest level. They train separate masking models for AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50. They evaluate on ImageNet via weakly supervised localization (thresholding the mask and taking the tightest box) and introduce a new “saliency metric” based on cropping the tightest rectangle that covers the salient region and measuring a function of crop area and predicted probability. They also provide a CIFAR-10 variant trained from scratch, and argue the approach enables real-time saliency and potential video applications.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself relative to gradient-based saliency (e.g., Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), CAM-style methods (Zhou et al.), and especially iterative perturbation approaches, notably Fong & Vedaldi’s “Meaningful Perturbations” (concurrent) and Cao et al. The key novelty is to distill the iterative optimization into a trainable, feed-forward masker that learns to produce high-quality, sharp masks with a single pass, while combining SSR and SDR in the loss and explicitly managing introduced evidence. This is a natural and useful evolution of perturbation-based saliency.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: Treating saliency mask generation as a learned prediction task conditioned on image and class is compelling, enabling real-time explanations and generalization across images.
- Well-motivated objective: Joint SSR/SDR optimization with smoothness and area regularization, plus randomized masking “fill” to mitigate artifacts, is thoughtful and grounded in prior observations.
- Practical architecture: U-Net with class embedding gating at the top level makes sense for precise masks, and the training trick with fake class selectors addresses the “ignore the selector” failure mode.
- Empirics: Demonstrates competitive weakly supervised localization on ImageNet (close to fully supervised VGG in error), and substantially better results than baselines/iterative methods (per text). Speed is excellent (100+ images/s), a clear advantage over iterative optimization. CIFAR-10 results suggest the approach is not reliant on pretrained encoders.
- New metric proposal: Attempts to quantify interpretability via an SSR-aligned crop-based score is valuable, and avoids adversarial artifacts by using cropping rather than masking.

Weaknesses and questions
- “Black-box” claim: Training requires backpropagation through the fixed classifier (i.e., differentiability and gradient access). At test time the masker is independent, but the method is classifier-specific and not black-box in the strict sense (e.g., cannot explain an opaque API without retraining via gradient signals). Please clarify “any differentiable classifier” and the need to train a separate masker per target model.
- Saliency metric definition: s(a,p) = log(ã) log(p) appears problematic. Because log(p) ≈ 0 when p→1, the product tends toward 0 regardless of area, potentially over-emphasizing classification probability and under-emphasizing compactness. The intended trade-off might be better captured by a sum (e.g., −log p + λ log a) or a Pareto/frontier analysis (area vs probability curves), or reporting multiple operating points (e.g., fix p≥τ and minimize area). Please double-check the formula (possible typo) and provide sensitivity to the threshold ã=0.05 and resizing policy.
- Evaluation details: The weakly supervised localization protocol depends on thresholding the mask at 0.5. Is this fixed across all methods and classes? How sensitive are results to the threshold? Are comparisons to iterative baselines fairly tuned? Report runtimes for baselines for concreteness. Also report localization using predicted labels in addition to ground-truth labels.
- Ablations: The loss has four terms and the evidence-removal A is randomized. Ablation studies (remove SSR/SDR term; vary TV/area weights; fix A to blur vs noise; remove fake labels; vary mask resolution) would greatly strengthen claims about what drives performance and robustness to introduced evidence.
- Generalization and scope: Since a separate masker is trained per classifier, discuss portability to new models/classes and the data needed to train a new masker. Can a single masker learn to explain multiple classifiers? How does performance degrade when the black-box changes?
- Potential leakage/bias: Using a ResNet-50 encoder to explain ResNet-50 black-box may bias the masks toward the encoder’s features. Although encoder weights are frozen, some discussion or a cross-architecture study (e.g., use VGG encoder to explain ResNet and vice versa) would be helpful.
- CIFAR-10: Only qualitative results are shown. Please provide quantitative metrics (e.g., your saliency score, localization accuracy with provided boxes or human annotations) to support the claim that results are “clearly interpretable.”
- Clarity/notation: Some equations are terse or ambiguous (e.g., elementwise vs scalar operations in E=X⊙M, clarity of equation numbers, missing explicit thresholds). The metric and the removal operator could use more precise definitions and justification.

Quality
Overall technically solid, with a well-designed objective and architecture, and reasonable experiments. The main technical concerns are the saliency metric formulation and the absence of key ablations/sensitivity analyses. Empirical claims would benefit from more rigorous and transparent comparisons and reporting.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. However, tighten notation (clear Hadamard products, consistent equation references), explicitly state training/test access assumptions to the classifier, and clarify the saliency metric rationale and behavior.

Originality
The core idea—learning a feed-forward masker that distills iterative perturbation objectives, jointly optimizing SSR and SDR while mitigating introduced evidence—is novel and timely relative to prior gradient-/CAM-/optimization-based explanations.

Significance
High practical significance: real-time, sharp saliency maps are valuable for interactive/streaming settings and for auditing model biases. Results on ImageNet localization are competitive and suggest the approach can be a new state-of-the-art among weakly supervised saliency/localization methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, practical approach enabling real-time, high-quality saliency
- Thoughtful objective combining SSR/SDR with artifact mitigation
- Strong empirical performance and speed
- Broad applicability and potential impact

Cons against acceptance
- Saliency metric may be ill-posed; needs correction/analysis
- Limited ablations and sensitivity studies
- “Black-box” claim is overstated; method is classifier-specific and requires gradients for training
- Some evaluation details and comparisons could be more rigorous

Recommendation
Lean accept. The contribution is timely, useful, and empirically strong. Addressing the metric formulation, adding ablations, and clarifying the black-box assumption would make this a very strong NIPS paper.
