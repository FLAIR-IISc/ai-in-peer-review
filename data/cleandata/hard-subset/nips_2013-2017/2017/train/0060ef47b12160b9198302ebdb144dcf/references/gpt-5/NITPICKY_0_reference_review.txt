Summary
The paper proposes a learned, feed-forward saliency generator that predicts per-class saliency masks for a given image classifier in a single pass. The method optimizes an objective designed to capture both SSR (smallest sufficient region) and SDR (smallest destroying region) while discouraging adversarial artifacts via TV regularization and by mixing different evidence-removal baselines (heavy blur or noise-colored images). The saliency network is a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and a class-embedding “feature filter.” During training, the classifier is kept fixed; the saliency net is trained end-to-end to minimize the objective. The authors also propose a new saliency metric based on the area of the tightest bounding crop and the classifier confidence on that crop. Experiments on ImageNet (with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50 as black boxes) claim improved weakly supervised localization error and better scores on the proposed metric; a CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates qualitative results with a smaller architecture trained from scratch.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself relative to backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), CAM (Zhou et al. 2015), and optimization-based perturbation methods (Fong & Vedaldi; Cao et al.). The main difference claimed is speed (single forward pass) and improved quality via a learned mask generator. However, several contemporary attribution methods that directly address artifacts and faithfulness (e.g., Grad-CAM, Integrated Gradients, SmoothGrad) are not discussed or compared against; the comparison to [2] (Fong & Vedaldi) remains largely qualitative, and evaluation protocols differ.

Strengths
- Clear practical motivation: faster, sharper, more interpretable saliency suitable for real-time and potentially video.
- Combining SSR and SDR in a single objective is sensible and the discussion on introduced evidence is thoughtful.
- Architecture choice (U-Net with multi-scale features and class embedding) is plausible and generally well motivated.
- Training trick with fake labels to force conditioning on class is useful.
- Empirical results suggest improved weakly supervised localization (though details are sparse), and runtime appears high.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological and conceptual
- “Black box” vs differentiable access: The training objective requires backprop through the classifier logits/probabilities to the image. This contradicts the repeated “black box/model-agnostic” phrasing. If gradients are needed, the classifier is not a black box in the standard sense. Please clarify the access assumptions and, if truly black-box, describe how gradients are obtained.
- New metric definition is unclear/incoherent: s(a,p) = log(ã) log(p) is ambiguous (product or ratio?). The text says “low value is good,” but for the product of two negatives, decreasing area or increasing probability tends to increase the product (contradicting the stated preference). Clamping ã at 0.05 further biases/limits sensitivity. Resizing the crop ignoring aspect ratio may confound p across methods and classes. As written, the metric seems ill-posed and possibly tuned to favor the proposed method.
- Objective (Eq. 4) notation errors: the last term “3fc( (X,1−M)) 4” is unclear. If it is λ3 fc(Φ(X,1−M))^λ4, the rationale for λ4<1 and its effect on optimization (gradient behavior near 0) needs explanation. Also missing sign on −log fc(Φ(X,M))? Please present a precise loss.
- Evidence-removal baseline A: while mixing blur and noise is a good idea, no ablation quantifies its effect on mask quality, artifact suppression, or downstream metrics. The discussion remains qualitative.

Experimental and evaluation
- Incomplete reproducibility: Key training details are missing (optimizer, learning rate/schedule, batch size, number of iterations, data augmentations, regularization, threshold selection beyond 0.5). The value “2 = 10 3” is ambiguous (10−3 vs 10^3). No seeds/hardware specs; the “>100 images/s” claim is not rigorously benchmarked.
- Comparisons: No quantitative comparison to CAM/Grad-CAM on standard localization protocols; no comparisons to Integrated Gradients or SmoothGrad on deletion/insertion metrics; only limited discussion of Fong & Vedaldi under differing metrics. Iterative baselines could be substantially improved with strong TV and better perturbation baselines; fairness of comparisons is unclear.
- Metric reporting: Tables are referenced but not shown here; without detailed numbers and statistical significance the claims (“clearly outperforms”, “on par with GT boxes”) are difficult to verify. Also, evaluating only at a fixed threshold (0.5) without sensitivity analysis may overfit to that threshold.
- CIFAR-10: purely qualitative; no localization or fidelity metrics. The architectural change and from-scratch training are interesting but unsubstantiated quantitatively.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous formatting/notation errors: broken equation formatting, missing multiplication symbols, ambiguous constants, random text (“st Conference…”), inconsistent reference formatting (missing years, venues, broken titles/links).
- Ambiguities: “learned embedding speed-up” hint lacks a concrete loss term. The feature filter (Eq. 7) uses sigmoid of a dot product; the choice and scale are not justified, nor is normalization discussed. The two-channel abs nonlinearity in Eq. 8 is ad hoc; an ablation or rationale would help.

Originality
- Moderate. A learned mask generator supervised by a fixed classifier is a reasonable extension of optimization-based perturbation methods and prior top-down attention work. Combining SSR/SDR terms and the training protocol are useful, but the conceptual novelty is incremental.

Significance
- Potentially useful if validated rigorously: real-time saliency and improved localization could have impact. However, given the current evaluation gaps and metric concerns, it is premature to conclude state-of-the-art fidelity/interpretability.

Specific suggestions/questions
- Precisely define the loss (with correct symbols/signs) and provide all hyperparameters and training details.
- Clarify classifier access assumptions and whether the method works when only function evaluations are available (true black-box).
- Fix the metric: remove ambiguity (product vs ratio), justify “low is good,” avoid arbitrary clamping, and evaluate with established insertion/deletion curves. Report sensitivity to aspect-ratio handling.
- Add strong baselines (CAM/Grad-CAM, Integrated Gradients, SmoothGrad, Guided Backprop, Fong & Vedaldi) under identical protocols; include ablations for A, TV, λ’s, resolution, and threshold.
- Provide quantitative CIFAR-10 results (localization, metric).
- Clean up references and notation; include runtime benchmarks with hardware details.

Pros
- Practical, fast saliency generation with plausible architecture/training.
- Thoughtful treatment of introduced evidence and adversarial artifacts.
- Promising qualitative results and improved weakly supervised localization (pending details).

Cons
- Ambiguous/possibly flawed new metric; incomplete and potentially unfair evaluation.
- Unclear “black box” assumption; lack of rigorous baselines and ablations.
- Reproducibility gaps; numerous presentation/formatting issues.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is promising and the qualitative results look good, the accumulation of methodological ambiguities (especially the metric), insufficient comparisons, and clarity/reproducibility issues outweigh the contributions in the current form. A revised version with precise definitions, stronger and fairer evaluations, and cleaned presentation could be competitive.
