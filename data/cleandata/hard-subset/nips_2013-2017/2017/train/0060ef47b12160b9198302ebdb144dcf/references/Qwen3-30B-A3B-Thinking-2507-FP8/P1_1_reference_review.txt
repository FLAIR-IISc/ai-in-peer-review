

# Peer Review: "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. Current state-of-the-art methods for saliency detection either rely on iterative optimization (e.g., removing patches until classification score drops) or gradient-based approaches, both of which have limitations in speed or quality. The authors propose a novel approach where they train a model (a masking model) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, rather than optimizing per image. The core innovation is a masking model based on a U-Net architecture with a ResNet-50 encoder that learns to predict saliency maps that satisfy a new objective function combining mask smoothness, size, and classification confidence. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric based on the "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) concept, which better captures the interpretability of saliency maps than standard localisation accuracy.

The paper relates to previous work on saliency detection, particularly the iterative patch removal methods [17], optimization-based approaches [1, 2], and gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16]. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] by emphasizing that they train a model to predict saliency maps rather than optimizing for each image separately. They also address the problem of introduced evidence during saliency generation, which is a critical issue for iterative methods.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper presents a well-motivated approach to real-time saliency detection. The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than optimizing per image is novel and well-justified. The new objective function (Equation 4) effectively balances multiple important criteria for saliency maps (smoothness, size, classification confidence on preserved region, and low confidence on removed region).

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods. The localisation error (36.7% for ResNet-50 trained model) is competitive with fully supervised approaches (34.3% for VGG), which is impressive given the model was trained with only image-class label pairs. The new saliency metric (Equation 3) provides a more meaningful evaluation of saliency map quality than standard localisation accuracy.

3. **Practical Significance**: The paper's claim that their method can produce 100 saliency masks per second (sufficient for real-time applications) is compelling. This opens up new applications like video-saliency for autonomous vehicles, which the authors mention in their future work. The real-time capability is a significant practical contribution.

4. **Thorough Treatment of Key Challenges**: The paper properly addresses the critical issue of introduced evidence during saliency generation (Section 3.1), which is often overlooked in related work. Their solution using randomized evidence removal (50% blurred, 50% random noise) is well-justified and effective.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the technical contributions and sufficient detail about the model architecture (Figure 4) and training process.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Evaluation of the New Metric**: While the new saliency metric (Equation 3) is well-motivated, the paper doesn't sufficiently evaluate how well it correlates with human perception of saliency. The metric is used to compare methods but not validated against human judgments, which is a standard practice in saliency evaluation. Without this, it's difficult to be confident that the metric truly captures interpretability.

2. **Training Complexity and Computational Requirements**: The paper mentions that the masking model can process "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second" on a standard GPU, but doesn't provide details about the training time or computational resources required to train the model. This is important for assessing practical adoption. The need for a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder also introduces a dependency that might not be available for all classifiers.

3. **Lack of Comparison with Recent Methods**: While the paper compares to methods like [1, 2, 17], it doesn't compare with some of the most recent state-of-the-art saliency methods. For example, methods like Grad-CAM [18] or Score-CAM [19] (published after the cited work) could provide more relevant comparisons.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the limitations of their approach. For instance:
   - How well does the method work for classifiers with different architectures or for non-image classification tasks?
   - How robust is the model to changes in the black-box classifier's architecture?
   - What are the failure cases of the method?

5. **Ambiguity in "Model Agnostic" Claim**: The paper claims to be "model agnostic" but relies on a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder. While they note it can be trained from scratch, the pre-training introduces potential biases that might affect the saliency maps. The paper doesn't explore how these biases might manifest.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with a well-justified approach and thorough experimental evaluation. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the experiments adequately support the claims. However, the paper could have provided more detailed analysis of the model's limitations and failure cases. The authors are honest about the method's requirements (e.g., need for pre-trained encoder), but could have been more transparent about potential biases.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The architecture diagram (Figure 4) is particularly helpful for understanding the masking model. The new saliency metric is clearly defined and motivated. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproduction, though more information about hyperparameters would be helpful.

**Originality**: The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than optimizing per image is novel. The new objective function and metric are also original contributions. The paper clearly differentiates from previous work, particularly the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2]. The combination of a U-Net architecture with the novel objective function represents a significant technical contribution.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a practical problem with real-world applications (real-time saliency for autonomous vehicles, video analysis). The speed advantage (100+ saliency masks per second) is significant compared to iterative methods. The quality improvements over existing methods (both in terms of localisation error and the new saliency metric) demonstrate a meaningful advance in the field. The work could be widely adopted by practitioners.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Novel approach to real-time saliency detection that outperforms existing methods
- Strong experimental results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10
- New saliency metric that better captures interpretability
- Practical significance with real-time applications
- Well-written, clear, and technically sound paper

**Arguments against Acceptance**:
- Limited evaluation of the new saliency metric against human perception
- Lack of comparison with some of the most recent state-of-the-art saliency methods
- Insufficient discussion of limitations and failure cases
- Ambiguity in the "model agnostic" claim due to reliance on pre-trained ResNet
- Missing details about computational requirements for training the masking model

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of model interpretability. The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than optimizing per image is novel and well-executed. The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods in terms of speed and quality. The new saliency metric is particularly valuable as it better captures the interpretability of saliency maps than standard localisation accuracy.

The paper has some limitations, particularly regarding the evaluation of their new metric and the lack of comparison with some recent methods, but these are not fatal flaws. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a practical problem with significant real-world applications.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The contribution is significant, the methodology is sound, and the results are compelling. While the paper could be strengthened by addressing the limitations mentioned above (particularly regarding the evaluation of their new metric), these are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's strengths and contributions. The work represents a meaningful advance in the field of model interpretability with clear practical applications.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a human evaluation study to validate the new saliency metric against human perception of saliency.

2. Add a comparison with more recent saliency methods (e.g., Grad-CAM, Score-CAM) to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

3. Expand the discussion of limitations to include potential failure cases and the impact of the pre-trained ResNet encoder on the saliency maps.

4. Provide more details about the computational requirements for training the masking model (e.g., training time, GPU requirements).

5. Clarify the "model agnostic" claim by discussing how the pre-trained encoder might introduce biases and how these might be mitigated.

6. Include a brief analysis of the method's performance on a more diverse set of classifiers beyond ImageNet and CIFAR-10.
