

# Review: "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection for black box classifiers, addressing the need for both speed and accuracy in explaining classifier decisions. The authors introduce a trainable masking model that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass (as opposed to iterative methods requiring hundreds of steps), making real-time application feasible. They also propose a new saliency metric based on the Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) concept and address the problem of "introduced evidence" during image manipulation. The method is evaluated on ImageNet with three black box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and CIFAR-10, showing improved performance on both the new metric and standard localisation accuracy compared to existing approaches.

## Strengths

### Technical Quality and Novelty
The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of explainable AI. The core idea of training a masking model to predict saliency maps rather than using iterative optimization is both novel and well-motivated. The authors clearly distinguish their work from the parallel method by Fong and Vedaldi [2] (which they acknowledge), particularly through their focus on both SSR and SDR objectives in the loss function. The introduction of the new saliency metric (equation 3) is particularly valuable, as it directly measures the quality of saliency maps by assessing how small a cropped region can be while maintaining classification confidence. This addresses a key limitation of previous metrics that conflated saliency with localization.

The technical approach is sound, with a well-designed U-Net architecture for the masking model and thoughtful handling of the "introduced evidence" problem through random image manipulation schemes. The training process with the 30% fake class selector is a clever solution to the problem of the model ignoring class information.

### Experimental Rigor
The evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed. The authors compare against multiple baselines (including iterative methods and the "center box" baseline) on standard ImageNet object localization tasks. The results are convincing, with their ResNet-50 trained model achieving 36.7% localization error compared to 34.3% for a fully supervised VGG approach. The CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates the method's robustness across different datasets and architectures, including training from scratch without pre-trained weights.

The paper also thoughtfully discusses limitations, including the potential bias in the masking model (mentioned in the conclusion) and the need for further research into the model's own biases.

### Practical Significance
The real-time capability (100 saliency masks per second) is a significant practical contribution, enabling applications like video saliency that were previously infeasible with iterative methods. The paper's discussion of applications in autonomous vehicles demonstrates clear relevance to real-world problems. The method's black-box nature is particularly valuable for practical deployment where access to model internals is limited.

## Weaknesses

### Limited Exploration of Model Biases
While the paper acknowledges potential biases in the masking model (Section 6), it doesn't thoroughly investigate this issue. Given that the model is trained to predict saliency for black box classifiers, it's crucial to understand how the masking model's own biases might affect the saliency maps it produces. A deeper analysis of this aspect would strengthen the paper's claims about the quality of the explanations.

### Questionable "Model Agnosticism"
The paper claims to be "model agnostic," but the masking model uses a ResNet-50 encoder (pre-trained on ImageNet). This creates a subtle dependency on a specific architecture, which might influence the saliency maps produced. While the CIFAR-10 experiment shows the model can work without pre-training, the claim of complete model agnosticism is overstated. The authors should clarify this limitation.

### Comparison to Fong and Vedaldi [2]
The paper acknowledges the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2], but the comparison is limited. It would be valuable to have more detailed comparisons of computational efficiency and saliency quality between the two methods. The paper claims to outperform iterative methods, but doesn't directly compare to Fong and Vedaldi's approach in the experiments.

### Metric Limitations
While the new saliency metric is innovative, the paper doesn't sufficiently address its limitations. The metric assumes classifiers are invariant to scale and aspect ratio, which may not hold for all classifiers. The authors should discuss how the metric might perform with classifiers that are sensitive to these factors.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of explainable AI. The core idea of training a masking model for real-time saliency detection is novel, well-executed, and addresses a genuine practical need. The new saliency metric is a valuable addition to the field, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over existing approaches.

The paper meets all four review criteria well:
- **Quality**: The technical approach is sound, the claims are well-supported by experiments, and the authors are careful in evaluating both strengths and weaknesses.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- **Originality**: The method represents a significant departure from iterative approaches and includes a novel metric.
- **Significance**: The real-time capability enables new applications, and the method advances the state of the art in saliency detection.

The minor weaknesses (model bias exploration, "model agnosticism" claim, and metric limitations) do not substantially undermine the paper's contribution. The authors have made a strong case for the value of their approach.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a substantial advancement in the field of explainable AI, with clear practical implications and rigorous experimental validation. The method's speed, accuracy, and real-world applicability make it a valuable contribution to NIPS.

The paper is well-positioned to advance the field of explainable AI, and its focus on practical deployment (real-time applications) makes it particularly relevant to current industry needs. The new saliency metric alone would be a valuable contribution to the field, but combined with the efficient methodology, it represents a significant step forward in the practical application of saliency detection.
