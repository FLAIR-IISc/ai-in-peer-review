Summary
This paper proposes a fast, model-conditioned saliency method for image classifiers. Instead of optimizing a saliency mask per image via iterative perturbation (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), the authors train a masking network (U-Net with a fixed, pretrained ResNet-50 encoder) to directly output a saliency mask in one forward pass given an image and a class selector. The training objective combines: (i) a smoothness prior (TV), (ii) an area penalty, (iii) an SSR term encouraging high class probability on the preserved region, and (iv) an SDR term encouraging low probability when the preserved region is removed. To mitigate “introduced evidence” from masking, they stochastically mix two alternatives for the replaced region (heavy blur or random color plus noise) and enforce smoothness. They also introduce a saliency score s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p) using the tightest rectangular crop containing the salient region (low is better), intended to capture “smallest sufficient region” behavior while discouraging adversarial artifacts. On ImageNet, separate masking models are trained for AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 “black boxes.” They report improved weakly supervised localization over prior saliency/weakly supervised methods and favorable values under their saliency metric. A small CIFAR-10 experiment (with a randomly initialized encoder) suggests the approach can work at low resolution.

Relation to prior work
The method builds on and contrasts with gradient-based saliency (Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), perturbation-based methods (Fong & Vedaldi; Zhou et al.’s iterative removal), and weakly supervised localization (CAM/Grad-CAM, Zhou et al. 2016). The key novelty is training a generalizable mask predictor per classifier to approximate the solution of a perturbation objective, yielding real-time inference and allegedly sharper, less overfit masks.

Strengths
- Clear formulation that unifies SSR and SDR into a single training objective; masking network is a practical way to amortize expensive per-image optimization.
- Sensible engineering to reduce introduced evidence (randomized replacement A, smoothness via TV) and to force class conditioning (fake label trick).
- Strong qualitative results and promising weakly supervised localization performance; speed is a practical advantage (100 masks/s).
- CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates potential without a pretrained encoder.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- “Black box” claim is overstated: training requires backpropagating through the classifier to compute gradients for the masking model. The approach applies to differentiable, accessible models, not true black-box (API-only) models.
- The proposed saliency metric s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p) is ad hoc and its behavior is unclear. It can be negative, and its monotonicity and sensitivity to scale/aspect distortions are not rigorously analyzed. Cropping and resizing without aspect ratio may confound p for reasons unrelated to true evidence sufficiency.
- Evaluation details are sparse. Tables are referenced but exact comparative numbers for baselines are not provided here; no statistical variance, ablations, or sensitivity analyses (e.g., weights λ1–λ4, choice of A, thresholding at 0.5) are reported.
- Limited comparisons: important contemporaneous baselines like CAM/Grad-CAM are not reported; deletion/insertion-style curves or “pointing game” are absent; robustness to different masking operators is not quantified.
- The need to train a separate masking model per classifier (on 250k images) is heavy, raises practicality questions, and may limit adoption.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but several equations contain typos/ambiguities (e.g., Eqs. (1)-(2) formatting; s(a, p) not clearly explained; Eq. (4) variables and signs could be clearer). Some implementation details are missing (optimizer, LR, training time, class embedding size).
- The term “model-agnostic” is used in a way that conflicts with the need for gradients.

Originality
- The idea of amortizing perturbation-based saliency via a trainable, class-conditioned mask predictor is novel relative to iterative approaches and standard backprop saliency. Architectural choices (U-Net, ResNet encoder) are standard.

Significance
- If validated with stronger experiments and comparisons, the speed/quality trade-off could be impactful for real-time explanation and weakly supervised localization. However, per-classifier training overhead and the black-box limitation reduce practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptual contribution: unifying SSR/SDR in a single training objective and amortizing optimization with a mask predictor.
- Strong qualitative saliency and competitive localization using only image-level labels.
- Practical speed advantages; potentially useful for video.

Cons for acceptance
- Overstated “black-box”/“model-agnostic” claims; requires differentiable access.
- Saliency metric is insufficiently justified and may be misleading.
- Missing key baselines, ablations, and rigorous quantitative evidence; limited reproducibility detail.
- Training a dedicated explainer per classifier is costly; generalization across classifiers or to API-only settings is unaddressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify access assumptions: explicitly require a differentiable classifier during training; discuss applicability to API-only settings or provide a gradient-free variant (e.g., NES/REINFORCE).
- Strengthen evaluation:
  - Add CAM and Grad-CAM baselines, and report quantitative results (means, stds) across the ImageNet val set.
  - Include insertion/deletion curves or equivalent perturbation curves, and the pointing game; analyze sensitivity to masking operator A.
  - Provide ablations for λ1–λ4, TV strength, resizing factor, fake-label probability, and the choice of A.
- Analyze and refine the saliency metric: justify its form, monotonicity, and scaling; report behavior across object sizes and aspect ratios; consider crop with preserved aspect ratio or padding to reduce distortion confounds.
- Report training cost, hardware, optimizer, hyperparameters, and release code to ensure reproducibility.
- Explore generalization:
  - Train one masking model that transfers across similar classifiers or across minor model updates.
  - Test cross-model transfer (train on GoogLeNet, evaluate on ResNet) to see whether the learned masks reflect general class evidence vs. model-specific quirks.
- Add a segmentation evaluation if claiming sharp masks (e.g., weakly supervised segmentation metrics).
- Discuss potential biases introduced by the fixed pretrained encoder and analyze their effect on explanations.

Recommendation: Reject (weak). The idea is promising and could be impactful, but the current submission overclaims black-box applicability, introduces an insufficiently justified metric, and lacks thorough, quantitative comparisons and ablations needed for NeurIPS acceptance. With stronger experiments/clarifications and more rigorous evaluation, this work could become a solid contribution.
