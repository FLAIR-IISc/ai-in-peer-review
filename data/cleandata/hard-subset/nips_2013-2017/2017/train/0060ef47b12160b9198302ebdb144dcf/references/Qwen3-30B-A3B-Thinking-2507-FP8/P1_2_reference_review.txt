

# Peer Review: "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical limitation in current saliency detection methods for image classifiers: the computational inefficiency of iterative approaches. The authors propose a novel model-based approach that trains a neural network to predict saliency maps directly in a single feed-forward pass, rather than iteratively optimizing masks for each image. This enables real-time saliency detection (100+ masks per second), which opens up new applications like video-saliency. The paper introduces a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which better evaluates the quality of saliency maps than standard localisation accuracy metrics. The authors demonstrate their approach on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets, showing that their method outperforms existing weakly supervised techniques while being orders of magnitude faster than iterative methods.

The paper builds on relevant prior work in saliency detection, including gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative patch removal [1, 17], and recent black-box approaches [2]. It positions its contribution as a model-based alternative to iterative optimization, addressing the speed limitations of previous work while maintaining or improving upon saliency quality.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Approach to a Real Problem**: The paper addresses a genuine bottleneck in saliency detection - the computational cost of iterative methods. The shift from iterative optimization to a model-based prediction approach is both elegant and practical, with clear advantages for real-world applications.

2. **Strong Technical Contribution**: The proposed masking model (based on U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) is well-designed for the task. The architecture effectively captures multi-scale features necessary for precise saliency prediction.

3. **New Saliency Metric**: The introduction of the saliency metric s(a,p) = log(ã)/log(p) is particularly valuable. It directly evaluates the relationship between the size of the salient region and the classifier's confidence, addressing a fundamental limitation in existing evaluation metrics.

4. **Thorough Experimental Validation**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, including comparisons with multiple baselines and ablation studies. The results consistently demonstrate superior performance in both speed and quality.

5. **Practical Impact**: The demonstrated speed (100+ masks per second) enables real-time applications that were previously impossible with iterative methods, such as video-saliency for autonomous vehicles.

6. **Careful Treatment of Adversarial Artifacts**: The authors thoughtfully address the challenge of introduced evidence during saliency generation, proposing multiple techniques to prevent adversarial artifacts in the saliency maps.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: While the paper mentions that the masking model is trained specifically for each black-box classifier (making it less "model agnostic" in practice), it doesn't fully explore the implications. How does the performance degrade when the masking model is trained on a different classifier than the one it's applied to?

2. **Training Complexity**: The training process for the masking model is complex with several non-trivial optimizations (e.g., 30% fake class labels, random evidence removal schemes). The paper would benefit from more detailed ablation studies showing the impact of these design choices.

3. **Comparison with Parallel Work**: The paper mentions that their work was developed in parallel to [2] but doesn't provide a direct comparison of speed and quality metrics. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the contribution claim.

4. **Bias in Saliency Maps**: While the paper mentions that the masking model might have its own biases, it doesn't explore how these biases affect the saliency maps, especially in cases where the black-box classifier has known biases (like the snow/polar bear example in the introduction).

5. **Implementation Details**: The paper states the model can process "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU" but doesn't provide specific benchmark numbers for comparison with other methods.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors provide a clear objective function, a detailed architecture for the masking model, and comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets and baselines. The paper is a complete piece of work, not a position paper. The authors are careful to evaluate both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly regarding the limitations of their new metric and the practical constraints of the model-agnostic claim.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the technical sections are logically presented. The figures (especially Figures 1-6) are helpful for understanding the approach and results. However, some sections could be clearer:

- The explanation of the evidence removal function (Equation 6) could benefit from a more intuitive explanation.
- The discussion of the mask smoothness and adversarial artifacts could be more detailed.
- The training process section would benefit from a more visual description of the training pipeline.

The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, though some implementation details (like the exact hyperparameters used for CIFAR-10) are omitted.

## Originality Assessment

The paper presents a novel approach to saliency detection that is significantly different from previous NIPS work. While the general idea of model-based saliency detection has been explored (e.g., [2]), the specific implementation, speed improvements, and new metric represent a meaningful contribution. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work, particularly highlighting the speed advantage over iterative methods. The new saliency metric is also a significant contribution to the evaluation of saliency methods.

## Significance Assessment

The results are highly significant for both practical applications and theoretical understanding of model explanations. The ability to generate saliency maps in real-time enables new applications that were previously infeasible. The method outperforms existing weakly supervised techniques on standard benchmarks, advancing the state of the art in saliency detection. The new metric provides a better way to evaluate saliency methods, addressing a fundamental limitation in the field. The paper's focus on practical, real-time applications makes it particularly relevant for practitioners working with black-box classifiers.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of model interpretability. It addresses a real problem with a practical solution, provides novel technical contributions (the masking model architecture and new saliency metric), and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides comprehensive experimental validation.

The weaknesses noted are important but not severe enough to outweigh the paper's strengths. The authors could strengthen their submission by addressing the limitations mentioned in the weaknesses section, but these are not critical to the paper's core contribution.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a direct comparison with the parallel work [2] in terms of speed and quality metrics (not just a mention).

2. Provide more details on the limitations when the masking model is trained on one classifier but applied to another.

3. Add a section discussing how the masking model might inherit or amplify biases from the black-box classifier, especially in cases like the snow/polar bear example mentioned in the introduction.

4. Include specific benchmark numbers for the speed of the method (e.g., "X masks per second on a standard GPU") rather than just stating "more than a hundred."

5. Provide more details about the hyperparameters used for the CIFAR-10 experiments, particularly the impact of the number of training epochs.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-executed and significant contribution to the field of model interpretability. The proposed method for real-time saliency detection is both practical and theoretically sound, with clear advantages over existing approaches. The new saliency metric provides a valuable tool for evaluating saliency methods. The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and makes a compelling case for its contribution to the field. It represents a meaningful advancement in the state of the art for model interpretability and would be of interest to a broad audience at NeurIPS.
