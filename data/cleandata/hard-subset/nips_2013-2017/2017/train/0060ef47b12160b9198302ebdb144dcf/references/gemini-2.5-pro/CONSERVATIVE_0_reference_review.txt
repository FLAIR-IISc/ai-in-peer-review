**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate deep neural network, specifically a U-Net architecture with a ResNet-50 encoder, to predict a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This stands in contrast to existing iterative methods that optimize a mask for each input image, which is computationally expensive. The authors formulate a composite objective function for training this "masking model," which encourages the resulting masks to be smooth, small (SSR), and effective at both preserving the class score when applied and destroying it when inverted (SDR). The authors also introduce a new saliency metric based on cropping the image to the salient region and measuring the resulting classification probability relative to the crop size. The method is evaluated on ImageNet for weakly supervised object localization and using the new metric, as well as on CIFAR-10, demonstrating strong performance and real-time inference speeds.

**Strengths**

1.  **Performance and Efficiency:** The primary contribution and strength of this work is the significant speedup in saliency map generation. By replacing a slow, iterative optimization process with a single feed-forward pass, the method achieves real-time performance (>100 FPS), which is a substantial practical advantage over methods like [2]. This opens up new potential applications, such as video saliency, as the authors note.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results on Localization:** The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, outperforming several previous methods. Achieving a 36.7% error with the ResNet-50 black box, close to some fully supervised methods, is an impressive empirical result and provides strong evidence for the quality of the generated masks.

3.  **Qualitative Mask Quality:** The saliency maps shown in Figure 5 are qualitatively sharp and well-localized, appearing superior to the often noisy or diffuse maps produced by simple gradient-based methods.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Complexity:** The main weakness of this paper lies in its core methodology. The approach introduces a highly complex, non-trivial neural network (the "masking model") to explain the behavior of another complex network (the "black box classifier"). This raises the concern of explaining one black box with another. The masking model itself contains millions of parameters and its own potential biases, a point the authors briefly concede in the conclusion but do not investigate.

2.  **Heuristic Objective Function without Ablation:** The objective function in Equation 4 is a weighted sum of four distinct, and potentially competing, terms (smoothness, area, SSR, SDR). The paper provides the chosen weights (`λi`) for the ImageNet experiment but offers no justification for these choices, nor does it include any sensitivity analysis or ablation study. How does performance change with different weights? Is the TV penalty truly necessary given the implicit regularization of the U-Net? Is the combination of both SSR and SDR terms critical? The lack of this analysis makes the objective feel ad-hoc and undermines the methodological rigor of the work.

3.  **Introduction of an Unvalidated Metric:** The authors propose a new saliency metric (Equation 3) to evaluate their method. While novel metrics can be valuable, introducing one in the same paper that proposes a method that excels at it is questionable without extensive validation. Established metrics for evaluating saliency, such as insertion and deletion curves which directly probe the SSR and SDR concepts, are not used. The proposed metric relies on a rectangular crop and an arbitrary area threshold of 0.05, and its information-theoretic justification is tenuous. The paper would be much stronger if it demonstrated superiority using established and widely accepted evaluation protocols before introducing its own.

4.  **Incomplete Comparison to Baselines:** The most direct competitor to this work is the iterative optimization method of Fong and Vedaldi [2], which the authors acknowledge was developed in parallel. However, the comparison to this method is insufficient. The qualitative results in Figure 2 show a blurry mask for the iterative approach, but it is unclear if this is a fair representation or a result of specific parameter choices (e.g., a high TV penalty). A more direct and rigorous quantitative comparison on established metrics (e.g., deletion/insertion scores) is necessary to properly situate this work. The current comparison largely rests on speed, where the proposed method is the clear winner, but quality claims are less substantiated against the most relevant baseline.

5.  **Practicality of Training:** The proposed method requires training a separate, large masking model for *every* black-box classifier one wishes to explain. This is a significant computational undertaking, especially compared to iterative methods which require no such model-specific training. This practical limitation is not discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel and original approach to saliency map generation.
*   The achievement of real-time performance is a significant practical contribution to the field of explainable AI.
*   The empirical results on the standard weakly supervised localization task are very strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the generated masks for that purpose.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The method lacks methodological rigor, particularly regarding the un-analyzed, heuristic objective function. The absence of ablation studies is a major flaw.
*   The approach adds a significant layer of complexity, essentially using one black box to explain another, without fully exploring the implications.
*   The introduction of a new metric without sufficient justification or comparison to established ones weakens the evaluation.
*   The comparison against the most relevant iterative baseline [2] is not thorough enough to fully support claims of superior mask quality.

**Conclusion**

While this paper presents an interesting and original idea with impressive real-time performance and strong localization results, its methodological foundation is not sufficiently robust for a NIPS publication. The reliance on a complex, heuristic objective function without proper analysis or ablation, coupled with the introduction of a custom, unvalidated evaluation metric, raises significant concerns about the work's soundness and reproducibility. While the speed and localization results are notable, the paper reads more as a proof-of-concept for a new direction rather than a complete and rigorously validated piece of scientific work. The core claims of producing "higher quality saliency masks" are not convincingly proven against the most relevant established baselines under standard metrics. Therefore, I lean towards rejection.
