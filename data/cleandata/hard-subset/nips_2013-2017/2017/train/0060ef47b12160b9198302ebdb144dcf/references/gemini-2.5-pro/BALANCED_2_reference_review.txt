**Summary:**
This paper proposes a novel method for generating image saliency maps for pre-trained image classifiers. The core problem addressed is that existing high-quality saliency methods are often iterative and slow, precluding real-time applications. The authors' key idea is to "amortize" the expensive iterative optimization process by training a separate, dedicated neural network (a "masking model" with a U-Net architecture) to generate a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This masking model is trained to optimize a composite objective function that encourages masks to be small, smooth, and effective as both a "Smallest Sufficient Region" (SSR) and a "Smallest Destroying Region" (SDR) for the black-box classifier's decision. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric that quantifies the trade-off between the explanation's size and its sufficiency. Through experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, they demonstrate that their method is orders of magnitude faster than iterative approaches, produces high-quality, sharp saliency maps, and achieves state-of-the-art performance on the task of weakly supervised object localization.

The work is well-situated in the context of prior art. It contrasts itself with fast but lower-quality gradient-based methods (e.g., Simonyan et al., 2013) and high-quality but slow perturbation/optimization-based methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017; Zhou et al., 2015). The main conceptual leap is framing saliency map generation not as a per-instance optimization problem, but as a prediction problem to be solved by a learned model.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Novelty:** The core contribution—training a generator network to produce saliency explanations for another model—is a powerful and novel idea. By amortizing the optimization, the method achieves real-time performance, which is a significant practical advancement for the field of explainable AI and could enable new applications like video saliency. This represents a new paradigm for generating perturbation-based explanations.
*   **Technical Quality and Experimental Rigor:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-executed. The objective function (Eq. 4) is thoughtfully designed, combining multiple desirable properties of a good saliency map (smoothness, conciseness, SSR, and SDR). The experimental results are very strong, particularly the state-of-the-art performance on weakly supervised object localization on ImageNet. This provides compelling quantitative evidence that the generated masks are not just plausible but highly meaningful. The ablation on CIFAR-10 with a from-scratch model is also a good addition.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the concepts of SSR/SDR and "introduced evidence" are well explained, and the model architecture is described in sufficient detail. The inclusion of a new, well-motivated saliency metric is also a commendable contribution.
*   **Thoroughness:** The authors show foresight in addressing potential failure modes. For instance, the strategies to combat "introduced evidence" (using randomized blurring/noise) and to ensure the model attends to the class selector (using fake labels during training) are clever solutions to non-trivial problems that demonstrate a deep understanding of the task.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **"Black Box" Terminology:** The paper repeatedly refers to the target classifier as a "black box". However, the training of the masking model requires backpropagating gradients from the classifier's output through the masked image to the masking model's parameters. This implies that the classifier must be differentiable and accessible, not a true black box where one might only have API access to the final predictions. This should be clarified, as the current terminology could be misleading. A more accurate description might be a "white-box" or "grey-box" approach during training, which produces a model that can then be applied in a black-box fashion at inference time.
*   **Faithfulness of Explanations:** The masking model itself is a powerful deep network (ResNet-50 based) which is trained to *emulate* the behavior of the target classifier. A potential concern is that the generated saliency map may reflect the inductive biases of the masking model as much as the internal logic of the target model. For example, the masking model might learn to identify objects using its own powerful features and generate a mask that happens to satisfy the objective, without that mask being a truly faithful representation of what the (potentially simpler) target model is "looking at". The paper touches on this in the conclusion, but it is a central limitation in the context of explainability that warrants more discussion.
*   **Reproducibility Details:** The objective function in Equation 4 contains four weighting hyperparameters (λ1-λ4) that are critical to the method's performance. The paper provides the final values used but offers no discussion on their selection process or a sensitivity analysis. Understanding the trade-offs governed by these weights is important for others wishing to reproduce the work or apply it to new domains.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  In the introduction and methods section, please clarify the requirements for the target classifier (i.e., that it must be differentiable for training). This will set more precise expectations for the reader.
2.  To strengthen the claims of faithfulness, it would be interesting to see an experiment on a "controlled" black box. For instance, one could train a model on a dataset where spurious correlations are known to exist (e.g., a "camel on sand vs. camel on grass" dataset). A faithful explanation should highlight the sand, not just the camel. This would test whether the masking model truly captures the target model's quirks or simply defaults to its own object-centric priors.
3.  An ablation study or sensitivity analysis of the λ hyperparameters, even if brief and in the appendix, would greatly improve the paper's value for practitioners and researchers aiming to build upon this work.
4.  The comparison with Fong & Vedaldi [2] is good, but it would be even stronger if their method was also evaluated using your proposed saliency metric in Table 3 to provide a more direct comparison of explanation quality under that new lens.

**Decision Rationale:**
Despite the weaknesses noted, the core contribution of this paper is significant, novel, and impactful. The idea of learning a fast approximator for a slow, iterative explanation process is a major step forward. The impressive empirical results on a challenging benchmark strongly support the authors' claims. The weaknesses are largely related to the positioning of the work and details that can be clarified or addressed in a final version. The paper presents a complete and high-quality piece of research that is very likely to inspire follow-up work. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
