Summary
This paper proposes an amortized, feed-forward approach to generate class-conditional saliency masks for image classifiers. Instead of solving a per-image iterative optimization (e.g., Meaningful Perturbations, Fong & Vedaldi), the authors train a U-Net-style “masking model” (with a frozen ResNet-50 encoder) to produce a mask in one forward pass given an image and a class embedding. The training loss combines (i) total variation for smoothness, (ii) an area penalty, (iii) a “keep” term to preserve class confidence on the masked image (SSR), and (iv) a “destroy” term to reduce confidence on the complementary region (SDR). To reduce adversarial artifacts, the masking operation mixes the input with a randomly chosen alternative image (blur or noisy color). The paper also introduces a new saliency metric that combines the crop area and class probability from the tightest rectangle enclosing the mask; lower is better. Experiments on ImageNet with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 as the target classifier claim improved weakly supervised localization vs. prior saliency/weak-label methods and substantial speedup over iterative methods; a small qualitative CIFAR-10 experiment is also presented.

Strengths
- Clear motivation to amortize iterative saliency optimization; the SSR+SDR joint objective is a reasonable design and the “fake label” training trick is sensible to enforce class-conditionality.
- Sensible discussion of introduced evidence and adversarial masking; randomized alternative images and TV regularization are appropriate practical mitigations.
- Architecture choices (U-Net with skip connections, class embedding “feature filter”) are technically sound, and the model appears fast enough for real-time use.
- Qualitative masks look sharp and class-focused; claimed weakly supervised localization performance is competitive.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “Black-box/model-agnostic” claim is misleading. Training requires gradients through the classifier to optimize the mask; this is not black-box in the standard sense (no internals/gradients). The paper should either (i) restrict scope to differentiable, gradient-accessible models, or (ii) provide a truly black-box variant (e.g., score-function/ES training) and evaluate it.
- Evaluation is incomplete and appears tuned. The new saliency metric is ad hoc (“highly tuned” by the authors’ admission), lacks a principled derivation, and its exact definition is unclear in the text (product vs ratio of logs). The metric depends on non–aspect-ratio-preserving resizing, which can systematically bias probabilities. No sensitivity analysis (e.g., area floor at 0.05), no correlation to human judgments, and it may favor methods that produce compact masks with sharp edges (like the proposed one).
- Missing key baselines and ablations. Beyond Guided Backprop/Excitation Backprop/CAM, Grad-CAM (2017), Integrated Gradients (2017), and SmoothGrad (2017) are not compared; Fong & Vedaldi is discussed but quantitative comparisons are not clearly reported. No ablations of the loss terms (λ1–λ4), the alternative image A choices, TV strength, mask upsampling, or the fake-label training probability. No analysis of class embedding vs. one-hot, or encoder choice. This makes it hard to attribute gains.
- Limited and potentially biased validation. The masking model is trained on 250k ImageNet training images per classifier and evaluated on ImageNet; no cross-dataset generalization or robustness tests. The CIFAR-10 study is qualitative only. The weakly supervised localization protocol uses threshold 0.5 and tightest box around the mask; both choices can favor compact masks—no sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Clarity issues. The proposed metric’s formula is ambiguous and the information-theoretic justification is hand-wavy. It is unclear whether gradients flow through the target classifier and whether its parameters are frozen (text suggests yes, but then “treated as a black box” is confusing). Speed claims lack precise hardware and batch size details. Some references are incomplete.
- Scope of claims. The paper suggests real-time “video saliency” and strong biases analysis, but does not evaluate temporal stability or bias diagnostics.

Quality
Technically coherent, but the empirical evidence does not yet meet the bar for a new state-of-the-art saliency method. The lack of strong, fair baselines, ablations, and robustness checks undermines the central claims. The new metric is insufficiently justified and could be gaming the evaluation.

Clarity
Generally well-written with helpful intuition, but important details (metric definition, gradient access, exact evaluation protocol, training hyperparameters) need clarification. Reference formatting is incomplete.

Originality
Moderate. The core idea—amortizing perturbation-based saliency via a learned mask predictor trained against SSR/SDR-inspired losses—is a reasonable extension of recent perturbation saliency work, but not a substantial conceptual departure. The metric is novel but questionable.

Significance
Potentially useful in practice (speed) if convincingly validated, but current evidence is insufficient. Without strong comparisons to established baselines and extensive ablations, it is hard to gauge the true impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify scope: replace “black box/model-agnostic” with “fixed, differentiable classifier with gradient access,” or add a true black-box variant and compare.
- Provide comprehensive baselines (Grad-CAM, CAM/Grad-CAM++, Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop, IG, SmoothGrad, and Meaningful Perturbations) under identical settings, plus insertion/deletion-style faithfulness metrics and pointing-game/localization metrics with sensitivity analyses.
- Ablate λ1–λ4, A choices, fake-label probability, TV, mask resolution/upsampling, and encoder choice. Report variance across seeds.
- Precisely define and justify the saliency metric; test robustness to aspect ratio, crop resizing, and show it correlates with other faithfulness/interpretability measures.
- Report hardware, throughput, training cost, and exact splits to avoid leakage. Include cross-dataset or cross-domain tests.

Pros
- Practical idea with real-time inference
- Thoughtful treatment of introduced evidence/adversarial artifacts
- Clean architecture and training strategy
- Promising qualitative results

Cons
- Mischaracterization as black-box/model-agnostic
- Insufficient, potentially biased evaluation and missing baselines
- Ad hoc, underspecified metric
- Lack of ablations/robustness/generalization analysis

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: 0.8
