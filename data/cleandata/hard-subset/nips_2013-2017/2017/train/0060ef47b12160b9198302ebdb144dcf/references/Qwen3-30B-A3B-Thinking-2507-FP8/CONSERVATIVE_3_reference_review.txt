

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a new method for real-time image saliency detection by training a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. The authors claim their approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than iterative methods while producing higher quality saliency masks. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region concept and demonstrate results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to method to evaluation. The authors acknowledge related work, including [2] which developed a similar method in parallel. The architecture is sensible, using a U-Net with ResNet features to produce sharp saliency masks. The weakly supervised object localization results are promising, with their ResNet-trained model achieving 36.7% localization error compared to the 34.3% of a fully supervised approach.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings that undermine its claims. Most critically, the authors do not provide sufficient evidence to support their key claims, particularly regarding the "orders-of-magnitude faster" speed advantage. They fail to provide concrete comparison of inference speed with established iterative methods, making the claim unsubstantiated.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a specific implementation of a concept that was already being explored in parallel work [2]. The authors acknowledge this but do not clearly differentiate their method from [2]. The training procedure with "fake labels" and random evidence removal is clever, but it's unclear how much of the performance gain is due to this trick versus the model architecture itself. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate these effects.

The new saliency metric (equation 3) is introduced without sufficient justification. The authors claim it better assesses interpretability, but they don't demonstrate how it correlates with human judgment of saliency. The metric's threshold of 0.05 for area is arbitrary and lacks empirical validation. The metric is also not compared against established saliency metrics like AUC-Judd or F-measure, which are standard in the field.

The paper makes several unsupported claims about the quality of their saliency maps. For example, they claim their masks "would produce accurate object segmentations" (figure 5), but don't provide segmentation metrics or comparison to segmentation methods. The comparison to ground truth bounding boxes in Table 3 is misleading since ground truth boxes are not saliency maps—they're object locations, not explanations of what the classifier is looking at.

Most concerning is the lack of thorough validation against established baselines. The paper compares to "central box" and "max box" baselines but fails to compare with the most relevant state-of-the-art saliency methods. The paper cites [1], [2], and [17] but doesn't include them in the quantitative comparisons. The claim that their method "outperforms other weakly supervised techniques" is unsubstantiated without direct comparisons.

The paper also fails to address limitations. For example, they don't discuss how their method performs on images with multiple objects or how the "random evidence removal" scheme affects different image classes. The paper claims their method is "model agnostic" but the training process requires access to the black box classifier during training, which contradicts this claim.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper presents a technically sound method that improves upon weakly supervised object localization, it fails to provide the rigorous validation required for a NIPS submission. The claims of "orders-of-magnitude faster" speed, "higher quality" saliency masks, and "real-time" capability are unsubstantiated by proper comparisons with established methods. The new metric lacks proper justification and validation, and the paper doesn't clearly differentiate its contribution from parallel work.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Providing concrete speed comparisons with iterative methods
2. Including direct comparisons with established saliency metrics and methods
3. Conducting ablation studies to isolate the contribution of their specific training tricks
4. Validating the new metric against human evaluation
5. Addressing the limitations of their approach

As currently written, the paper presents a marginal improvement over existing methods without sufficient evidence to support its claims. While the approach is interesting, the lack of thorough validation against established baselines and the unsubstantiated claims prevent this from being a significant contribution to the field. I recommend rejection.
