Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a fast, trainable masking model that predicts class-conditional saliency maps for a fixed image classifier in a single forward pass. The key idea is to replace slow, per-image iterative optimization (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017; Cao et al., 2015) with a U-Net-style network that learns, from data, to output masks that simultaneously satisfy two desiderata: smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR). The objective blends four terms: total variation for smoothness, an area penalty to prefer small regions, a preservation term encouraging high class probability on the preserved region (SSR), and a deletion term discouraging high probability when the salient region is removed (SDR). To mitigate “introduced evidence” and adversarial artifacts, the authors apply masks using a randomized alternative image (strong blur vs random color+noise) and encourage mask smoothness.

They also propose a new saliency metric that crops the tightest rectangle enclosing the predicted saliency and scores s(a,p)=log(ã)·log(p), with a as area fraction (thresholded at 0.05) and p the classifier’s probability on the cropped region. The method is evaluated on ImageNet using three frozen classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50), with a separate masking model trained for each, and on CIFAR-10 with an encoder trained from scratch. The approach claims improved weakly supervised localization error over prior saliency/localization methods and orders-of-magnitude speedups (∼100 224×224 images/s).

The work sits between gradient/backprop saliency (Simonyan et al., 2013; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop) and optimization-based perturbation approaches (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017; Cao et al., 2015), aiming to combine the interpretability of perturbation-based saliency with real-time speed via amortized inference. It is related to CAM-like localization (Zhou et al., 2016), though CAM requires architectural constraints and tends to be coarse; Grad-CAM (not cited) is a strong baseline in this space.

Strengths
- Clear formulation that unifies SSR and SDR in a single objective and explicitly addresses introduced evidence/artifacts via randomized alternative images and TV regularization.
- Practical architecture (U-Net with class embedding) that yields sharp masks and runs in real time, enabling potential video use.
- Empirical coverage across multiple fixed classifiers and an additional dataset (CIFAR-10), including a from-scratch encoder variant.
- Weakly supervised localization results reported as competitive with fully supervised VGG and superior to several weakly supervised baselines.
- Thoughtful training tricks (fake labels to force conditioning on the class selector) and an explicit discussion of pitfalls.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Black-box claim vs differentiability: Training requires backpropagation through the fixed classifier to optimize the masking model’s parameters (the loss depends on fc(φ(X,M))). This is not strictly black-box in the standard sense; it is “white-box with frozen weights” or “gray-box.” Please clarify the access assumptions and evaluate against truly black-box methods (e.g., score-only).
- Separate masking model per classifier: Generality is limited by training a new masking network for each target classifier. Do masks transfer across classifiers or datasets? Can a single masking model explain multiple architectures?
- New metric design: s(a,p)=log(ã)·log(p) is ad hoc and its behavior is non-intuitive (both logs are negative in (0,1), their product is positive; “lower is better” is not obviously aligned with SSR). The “information-theoretic” interpretation is hand-wavy. Please justify the multiplicative form, analyze monotonicity and sensitivity, and compare to alternatives (e.g., deletion/insertion curves; AUC-based metrics).
- Potential circularity: The proposed metric uses the same classifier that the masking model was trained to optimize. This risks inflating scores. It would be more convincing to evaluate the metric using a different, unseen classifier and/or human studies.
- Missing or incomplete baselines: Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ are standard and strong baselines for class-conditional localization/saliency by 2017; they should be compared both qualitatively and quantitatively. Also consider RISE-like black-box sampling (later work, but relevant to the black-box claim) and score-based deletion/insertion curves (Petsiuk et al., later).
- Objective clarity: Equation (4) appears to have a formatting inconsistency in the last term (“4” at the end). Is there an exponent or is λ4 the coefficient? The text says “Setting 4 to a value smaller than 1 helps reduce this probability,” which is counterintuitive if λ4 is a weight; if the probability is raised to a power <1, that has the opposite effect. Please fix notation and justify the choice.
- Evaluation details missing: Tables are referenced but numbers are not in the submission excerpt. Please ensure exact metrics, datasets/splits, thresholds, and variance over multiple runs are reported. Ablations on λ’s, alternative-image sampling, TV, class-embedding design, and the two-channel mask nonlinearity would strengthen claims.
- Scope of “model-agnostic”: The evidence removal relies on specific perturbations (blur, noise). How sensitive are results to the choice of A? Does the masking model exploit artifacts in A distributions? An experiment with held-out A distributions would help.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with a coherent loss and sensible architectural choices. However, the black-box claim is overstated, the new metric needs stronger theoretical and empirical justification, and some notational/clarity issues remain. More comprehensive baselines and ablations are needed.

Clarity
- Generally well written with helpful motivation and illustrations. Some equations and terminology (“black box”) are confusing or inconsistent; add precise assumptions, fix Eq. (4), and better explain the metric’s behavior.

Originality
- The amortized, trainable masking approach to approximate optimization-based saliency is novel and interesting. Combining SSR and SDR in the loss is a nice contribution. The metric is new but requires more rigor.

Significance
- If validated, a real-time, high-quality saliency generator is valuable for interpretability and weakly supervised localization/segmentation. Demonstrated speed and qualitative sharpness are promising. The requirement to train per classifier and the questions around metric validity temper the impact.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide comparisons to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ and CAM, both for localization error and saliency faithfulness (e.g., deletion/insertion).
- Evaluate the proposed metric using a different classifier than the one used in training to avoid circularity; also report correlation with localization metrics or human judgments.
- Clarify access assumptions to the classifier and, if possible, present a variant that uses only function evaluations (no gradients) to better support the “black-box” claim.
- Add ablations: λ’s, A sampling strategy, fake-label probability, mask nonlinearity, encoder freeze vs fine-tune.
- Report failure cases and per-class behavior (e.g., classes requiring context).
- Consider transfer: train on one classifier, test on another; or train a single explainer across multiple backbones.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, practical approach that achieves real-time saliency with competitive localization.
- Thoughtful handling of introduced evidence and adversarial artifacts.
- Broad applicability if access assumptions are clarified.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overstated black-box/agnostic claim; requires differentiable access and per-classifier training.
- New metric is insufficiently justified and potentially circular.
- Missing strong baselines (Grad-CAM) and detailed ablations; some notation/clarity issues.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The core idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the paper needs stronger, clearer experimental validation (including standard baselines and ablations), a precise statement of access assumptions, and a more rigorous treatment of the proposed metric. I encourage resubmission after addressing these points.
