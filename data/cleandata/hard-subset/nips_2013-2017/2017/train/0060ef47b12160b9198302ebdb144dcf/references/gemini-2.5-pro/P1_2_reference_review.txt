### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate, dedicated "masking model" to predict a saliency map for a given image and class in a single, fast, feed-forward pass. This contrasts with previous high-quality methods that rely on slow, iterative optimization for each input image. The authors formulate a comprehensive objective function for training this masking model, which encourages the generated masks to be smooth, small (sparse), and effective at both preserving classifier confidence on the masked-in region (Smallest Sufficient Region) and destroying confidence on the masked-out region (Smallest Destroying Region). To address the issue of adversarial artifacts being introduced by the masking process itself, they propose a robust masking application technique and a smoothness regularizer. The masking model uses a U-Net architecture with a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder, made class-conditional through a learned class embedding filter. The paper also introduces a new saliency metric that quantifies the trade-off between the size of a preserved region and the classifier's confidence in it. Experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 show that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster than iterative approaches, achieves state-of-the-art results in weakly-supervised object localization, and produces visually sharp and compelling saliency maps.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The primary contribution—amortizing the per-image optimization of saliency masks by training a predictive model—is a powerful and novel idea in the field of explainable AI. It directly addresses the critical limitation of speed in perturbation-based saliency methods, enabling real-time applications which were previously infeasible.
2.  **High-Quality Results:** The method produces saliency maps that are qualitatively sharp and precise (Fig. 5), a clear improvement over many existing techniques. Quantitatively, it achieves excellent performance on the weakly-supervised object localization benchmark, outperforming other saliency and weakly-supervised methods and approaching the performance of some fully-supervised models.
3.  **Well-Designed Objective and Training:** The objective function (Eq. 4) is thoughtfully constructed, elegantly combining principles of sufficiency (SSR), necessity (SDR), sparsity, and smoothness. The training strategies, particularly the use of "fake labels" to enforce class-conditioning and the randomization of the evidence removal scheme, are clever solutions to potential training pitfalls.
4.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The authors validate their approach across multiple standard black-box architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and on a different dataset (CIFAR-10) with a model trained from scratch. This demonstrates the generality and robustness of the proposed framework.
5.  **New Saliency Metric:** The proposed metric `s(a, p)` is an interesting and intuitive contribution for evaluating saliency maps based on the "Smallest Sufficient Region" principle. While evaluating a method with a metric it is designed to optimize can be circular, the metric itself is well-motivated and provides a useful new tool for the community.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Terminology of "Black Box":** The paper repeatedly refers to the target classifier as a "black box." However, the training process for the masking model requires backpropagation through the target classifier to compute the loss function. This means the method requires full, white-box access (i.e., a differentiable model with accessible weights/gradients) during the training phase. A true black-box model would only allow input-output queries. This should be clarified; the model is "black box" at inference time for the user of the saliency map, but not during the training of the saliency generator.
2.  **High Overhead:** The proposed solution requires training and storing a separate, large masking model (of comparable size to ResNet-50) for *each* classifier one wishes to explain. This represents a significant computational and storage overhead compared to methods that work directly on the target model without requiring their own training phase. This trade-off (high offline training cost for extremely fast online inference) should be discussed more explicitly.
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The objective function has four weighting parameters (λ1 to λ4) whose values are provided but not justified. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how these were chosen and an analysis of the model's sensitivity to them, as they likely have a significant impact on the final mask's properties (e.g., size vs. confidence).
4.  **Limited Evaluation Metrics:** The quantitative evaluation relies heavily on weakly-supervised localization and the newly proposed metric. While these are valuable, the inclusion of other standard saliency evaluation metrics (e.g., pointing game accuracy, or metrics that measure the drop/increase in probability as pixels are perturbed according to saliency) would provide a more comprehensive picture of the method's performance relative to the literature.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-motivated, the architecture is sound, and the experimental results strongly support the claims of speed and accuracy. The work is a complete and significant contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the method is described in sufficient detail, and the experiments are easy to follow. Equation 7, describing the feature filter, is a key component and is explained well. The figures are illustrative and support the text effectively. The only point of confusion is the use of the term "black box," as discussed above.

**Originality:** The core idea of training a generative model to produce saliency masks is highly original. While it builds upon concepts from perturbation-based methods like Fong & Vedaldi [2] (SSR/SDR), the shift from iterative optimization to amortized prediction is a novel leap. The specific architecture and training procedure are also novel contributions. The work is clearly differentiated from prior art.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. The ability to generate high-quality saliency maps in real-time is a major advance for model interpretability. It opens the door to new applications, such as explaining the decisions of models used in autonomous driving or other real-time systems. Researchers and practitioners working on model explainability are very likely to build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and impactful idea of amortizing saliency generation.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark (weakly-supervised localization).
*   Enables real-time saliency, a significant practical advancement.
*   The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The use of the term "black box" is imprecise and potentially misleading, as white-box access is required for training.
*   The practical overhead of training a separate large model for each target classifier is a non-trivial limitation that could be discussed more.

**Recommendation:** I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in a final version (e.g., by clarifying the "black box" terminology and adding a brief discussion on hyperparameters and overhead). The core contribution is significant, original, and likely to have a lasting impact on the field of machine learning interpretability.
