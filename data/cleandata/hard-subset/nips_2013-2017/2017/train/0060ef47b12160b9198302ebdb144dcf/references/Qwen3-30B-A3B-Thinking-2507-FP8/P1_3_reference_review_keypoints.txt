- Summary: Proposes a U-Net masking model that produces real-time saliency maps for black-box classifiers in a single forward pass, introducing an SSR-based metric and techniques to avoid adversarial artifacts; evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 with strong speed and quality gains.

- Strength 1: Delivers a significant practical contribution by enabling 100+ saliency maps/sec, making saliency usable in real-time applications.

- Strength 2: Introduces a well-motivated SSR saliency metric that better measures the classifier's actual evidence than standard localization metrics.

- Strength 3: Provides thorough experiments across datasets and architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) showing consistent improvements in speed and localization performance.

- Strength 4: Thoughtfully addresses adversarial artifacts via evidence removal and mask smoothness (TV penalty), demonstrating awareness of key failure modes.

- Strength 5: Paper is clearly written with effective figures and a well-structured presentation of the method and results.

- Weakness 1: Insufficient comparison with parallel work (Fong & Vedaldi [2]); lacks detailed differentiation between SSR vs. SDR objectives and model-based vs. iterative methods.

- Weakness 2: No analysis of saliency behavior when the black-box classifier makes incorrect predictions, leaving interpretability for misclassifications unexamined.

- Weakness 3: Limited discussion and analysis of potential biases learned by the masking model, which may highlight spurious correlations.

- Weakness 4: Missing implementation/training details for full reproducibility (exact U-Net configuration, hyperparameters, and random evidence removal specifics).

- Weakness 5: Claim of model-agnosticism is questionable since the masking model is tailored to CNNs and may not generalize to non-CNN classifiers.

- Recommendation: Accept — the practical impact, novel metric, and strong empirical results outweigh the listed weaknesses.

- Final comment: With minor revisions clarifying comparisons to related work and elaborating limitations (classifier errors, biases, reproducibility), the paper would be an excellent NIPS contribution.
