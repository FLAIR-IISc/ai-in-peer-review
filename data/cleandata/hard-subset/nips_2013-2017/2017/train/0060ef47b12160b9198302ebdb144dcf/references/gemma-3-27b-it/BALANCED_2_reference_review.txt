This paper proposes a novel approach to generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers, moving away from iterative methods towards a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea is to train a U-Net to directly predict saliency masks, enabling real-time performance and, importantly, higher quality masks than existing techniques. The paper introduces a new saliency metric based on the Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) concept and provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, demonstrating competitive results.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of learning a saliency detector instead of iteratively computing it is a significant contribution. This addresses a key bottleneck in existing saliency methods, enabling real-time applications like video saliency.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments on ImageNet demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms several baseline approaches, including iterative methods and a recent optimization-based technique [2], in terms of localization accuracy and the proposed saliency metric.  The results on CIFAR-10 further validate the generalizability of the approach.
* **Well-Defined Problem and Metric:** The authors clearly articulate the challenges of defining and evaluating saliency and introduce a new, well-motivated metric (based on SSR) that better captures the desired properties of a saliency map.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a good overview of existing saliency methods, categorizing them and highlighting their limitations.  They appropriately position their work within the broader context of explainable AI.
* **Detailed Implementation and Ablation:** The paper provides a good level of detail regarding the model architecture, training process, and parameter choices. The discussion of adversarial artifacts and techniques to mitigate them is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas. The figures are informative and help illustrate the concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Objective Function Complexity:** While the proposed objective function (L) is well-motivated, it contains several terms (TV, AV, classification probability) with potentially sensitive hyperparameters (1, 2, 3, 4). The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of the impact of these hyperparameters on performance. A sensitivity analysis could strengthen the robustness of the findings.
* **Reliance on Pre-trained ResNet:** While the authors demonstrate that the masking model can be trained from scratch on CIFAR-10, the ImageNet experiments rely on a pre-trained ResNet encoder. This introduces a potential bias and limits the full end-to-end learnability of the system. Exploring training the encoder jointly could be beneficial.
* **Saliency Metric Limitations:** The proposed saliency metric, while conceptually sound, relies on resizing crops to a fixed size, which could distort the aspect ratio and potentially affect the classification probability. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but a more robust evaluation that accounts for aspect ratio variations would be desirable.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on presenting successful examples. Including a discussion of cases where the method fails, along with potential reasons for the failures, would provide a more balanced assessment of its limitations.
* **Comparison to Gradient-Based Methods:** While the paper acknowledges gradient-based saliency methods, the experimental comparison is limited. Including a direct comparison against a strong gradient-based method (e.g., Guided Backpropagation) would provide a more complete benchmark.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and promising approach to saliency detection that addresses a significant limitation of existing methods.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The introduction of a new saliency metric provides a valuable tool for evaluating and comparing different saliency techniques.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a thorough analysis of related work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The objective function contains several hyperparameters that could be sensitive and require further tuning.
* The reliance on a pre-trained ResNet encoder in the ImageNet experiments introduces a potential bias.
* The proposed saliency metric has limitations related to aspect ratio distortion.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong empirical results. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Conduct a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters in the objective function.
* Explore training the ResNet encoder jointly with the masking model.
* Investigate more robust evaluation methods for the saliency metric that account for aspect ratio variations.
* Include a discussion of failure cases and potential reasons for the failures.
* Add a direct comparison to a strong gradient-based saliency method.
* Expand the analysis of potential biases in the trained model (beyond just the ResNet pre-training).
