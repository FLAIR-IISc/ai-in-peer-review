Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a trainable masking model that produces class-conditional saliency maps for arbitrary images in a single forward pass, targeting real-time performance. The key idea is to replace per-image iterative optimization (typical of perturbation-based saliency such as Fong & Vedaldi, 2017) with a learned network (a U-Net with a fixed ResNet-50 encoder and class-embedding gating) trained to minimize an objective that jointly encourages (i) smooth, compact masks, (ii) preservation of class evidence in the kept region (SSR), and (iii) removal of class evidence in the complementary region (SDR). To mitigate “introduced evidence” artifacts caused by masking, the edited image is constructed by blending with a randomly chosen alternative image (either a strong blur of the input or random color plus noise), and a TV penalty enforces mask smoothness. The authors also propose a saliency metric derived from the SSR notion: s(a,p) as a function of the cropped area fraction a and the classifier probability p on the crop; lower is better.

Empirically, separate masking models are trained for AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 black-box classifiers on ImageNet. The method achieves competitive weakly supervised localization (by thresholding masks and taking tight boxes) and outperforms several baselines and iterative optimization on both localization error and the proposed metric. Runtime exceeds 100 224×224 images per second on a standard GPU. A CIFAR-10 variant trained from scratch produces qualitatively interpretable masks.

This fits within the literature on saliency/explanations for CNNs, including gradient-based saliency (Simonyan et al., 2013), deconvnet/guided backprop (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014; Springenberg et al., 2014), excitation backprop (Zhang et al., 2016), and perturbation-based deletion methods (Zhou et al., 2014; Fong & Vedaldi, 2017). The novelty is to learn a feed-forward masker aligned to a given classifier and objective capturing both SSR and SDR, while explicitly addressing introduced-evidence artifacts.

Quality
Strengths:
- The objective combines SSR and SDR terms, with regularization (TV and area) and a randomized evidence-removal operator. This is a thoughtful design that addresses known pitfalls (adversarial artifacts from masking).
- The architecture and training protocol (class-embedding gating, fake-label training to enforce conditioning) are well-motivated and practical.
- Experiments span three ImageNet classifiers and a CIFAR-10 variant; speed claims are supported by throughput numbers.
- Weakly supervised localization performance is strong and close to a fully supervised baseline in at least one case.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- “Black box” and “model agnostic” claims are overstated: training requires backpropagation through the classifier’s input-output mapping (albeit with frozen weights), so the approach is restricted to differentiable, gradient-accessible models. Please clarify the interface assumptions and contrast with truly black-box (query-only) methods.
- The proposed metric s(a,p) is unclear as written: s(a,p)=log(ã)+log(p) would favor very low p (more negative log), which contradicts the stated intent. If the intended form is log(ã)−log(p) (or equivalently log(ã/p)), please correct and analyze its properties. In addition, resizing crops without preserving aspect ratio can conflate content with distortions; a sensitivity analysis would help.
- Fairness and completeness of comparisons are hard to judge. Details are missing for how baselines (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi) were tuned, what masking operator was used for them, and whether insertion/deletion-type curves were considered. Comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM-style methods are only briefly alluded to; quantitative results for Grad-CAM would be informative.
- Limited ablation: There is no systematic study of the contribution of each loss term, the randomization of A, TV strength, or the class-gating mechanism. An ablation would strengthen causal claims about design choices.
- Generalization: The masker is trained per classifier. It is unclear whether a single masker can generalize across architectures, or how performance degrades when applied to a different classifier than it was trained on.
- Reproducibility: Important training details (optimizer, learning rates, batch sizes, training time, data preprocessing) are not fully specified. Release of code or more exhaustive details would improve reproducibility.
- Evaluation scope: CIFAR-10 results are qualitative only. Additional quantitative metrics (e.g., deletion/insertion curves, pointing game, fraction of relevant pixels) would provide a more rounded assessment. Reporting results when conditioning on the predicted class (not only the ground-truth label) would also be informative.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-structured and motivates SSR/SDR clearly, with a careful discussion of introduced evidence.
- Some equations and notational details need correction/clarification (the saliency metric sign; define all symbols at first use; ensure Eq. (4) terms are clearly motivated and consistently scaled).
- Experimental setup needs more detail (see reproducibility comments). References and figure captions should be cleaned up; some references are incomplete.

Originality
- Training a feed-forward, class-conditional masker to approximate perturbation-based saliency appears novel relative to prior backprop saliency and iterative optimization methods at the time. The combined SSR+SDR objective with randomized evidence removal is a distinctive contribution. The class-embedding gating within a U-Net is a reasonable architectural choice; similar ideas exist in attention/gating, but the application here is specific.

Significance
- If validated, the method offers an appealing speed/quality trade-off for saliency, enabling real-time applications (e.g., video). The weakly supervised localization results suggest practical utility. The proposed metric (once corrected) could be a useful addition, though it would benefit from a broader analysis and comparisons to alternative evaluation protocols.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the “black box” assumption; explicitly state that gradients w.r.t. inputs are required and quantify any reliance on classifier calibration.
- Correct and justify the saliency metric; report sensitivity to crop aspect ratio and include deletion/insertion curves for a more comprehensive evaluation.
- Add ablations on loss terms, TV strength, random A selection, and class-gating. Report training hyperparameters and provide code if possible.
- Compare against Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ quantitatively on the same metrics and datasets.
- Evaluate on predicted-class conditioning, and report robustness of masks to different masking operators at test time (to assess artifact reliance).
- Explore cross-architecture generalization (train on one classifier, test on another) to assess how tied the masker is to a specific backbone.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, practical contribution: fast, high-quality, class-conditional saliency via a learned masker.
- Thoughtful objective addressing SSR/SDR and introduced evidence.
- Strong empirical localization performance and substantial runtime gains over iterative methods.
- Potentially impactful for real-time interpretability applications.

Arguments against acceptance
- Ambiguity in the proposed metric and limited evaluation breadth.
- Overstated “model-agnostic” claim; dependence on differentiability and per-classifier training.
- Missing ablations and some key experimental details, making it hard to attribute gains to specific design choices and to reproduce results.
- Limited quantitative evaluation on CIFAR-10 and lack of comparisons to some strong baselines (e.g., Grad-CAM).
