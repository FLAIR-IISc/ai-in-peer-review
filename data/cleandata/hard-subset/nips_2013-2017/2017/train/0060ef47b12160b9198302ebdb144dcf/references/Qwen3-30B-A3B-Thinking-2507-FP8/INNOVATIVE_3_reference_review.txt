

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection that fundamentally shifts the paradigm from iterative optimization to a trainable model-based solution. The core idea—training a model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass rather than performing hundreds of iterations per image—is bold and transformative. The authors propose a masking model that learns to generate high-quality saliency maps for any black-box classifier, enabling real-time applications previously impossible with iterative methods.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it introduces a model-agnostic saliency detection method that operates orders-of-magnitude faster than existing approaches (100+ masks per second) while producing higher quality saliency maps. Second, it proposes a new saliency metric based on the "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) concept, directly evaluating whether the saliency map preserves enough evidence for correct classification. This metric addresses a fundamental limitation of using localization accuracy as a proxy for saliency quality. Third, the paper demonstrates strong performance on ImageNet weakly supervised object localization (36.7% error rate for ResNet-50 trained model, comparable to fully supervised methods), and shows applicability to CIFAR-10 without pretraining.

The most compelling aspect of this work is its paradigm shift. While prior work (e.g., [1, 2]) focused on iterative optimization for saliency, this paper treats saliency detection as a learning problem. The masking model architecture (U-Net with ResNet backbone) is well-motivated and effectively captures spatial relationships needed for precise saliency maps. The training strategy using fake class labels (30% of the time) to prevent the model from ignoring the class selector is clever and addresses a critical challenge in weakly supervised learning.

The paper distinguishes itself from the parallel work [2] by not only proposing a different approach (model-based vs. iterative optimization) but also by introducing a new metric that evaluates the actual utility of saliency maps for classification. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms both iterative approaches and other weakly supervised methods on standard benchmarks.

Strengths:
- **Novel paradigm**: The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps is fundamentally new and enables real-time applications.
- **Strong metric**: The SSR-based metric directly evaluates what saliency should achieve, avoiding the pitfalls of localization accuracy.
- **Broad applicability**: Works across multiple classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet) and datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10).
- **Significant speed improvement**: 100+ saliency masks per second enables new applications like video-saliency.

Weaknesses:
- The comparison with [2] could be more detailed—while the paper acknowledges the parallel work, it doesn't fully articulate how the model-based approach specifically improves upon the optimization-based method.
- The explanation of why the model doesn't develop adversarial masks is somewhat vague (calling it a "regulariser" without evidence).
- The paper could provide more insight into potential biases in the masking model itself, though the authors note this as future work.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS should prioritize. The core idea is transformative, not incremental—it changes how we think about saliency detection rather than improving an existing method. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach is both faster and higher quality than existing methods, and the new metric provides a more meaningful evaluation framework.

While there are minor methodological gaps (like the incomplete comparison with [2] and the explanation of adversarial artifacts), these do not undermine the fundamental novelty and significance of the work. The paper provides sufficient experimental validation to support its claims, and the authors are appropriately cautious about limitations.

In an era where interpretability is increasingly important, this work opens the door to real-time interpretability tools that could be integrated into applications like autonomous vehicles. The authors have laid the groundwork for a new class of fast, accurate saliency detectors that could significantly impact both research and practice.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper offers a genuinely new direction in interpretability research with clear practical implications. The novelty of the approach outweighs the minor methodological concerns, and the work represents a significant step toward making interpretability tools practically usable in real-world applications.
