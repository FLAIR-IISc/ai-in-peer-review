Summary
This paper proposes training a dedicated “masking model” that predicts class-conditional saliency maps for a fixed image classifier in a single forward pass. The key idea is to replace per-image iterative optimization (e.g., Meaningful Perturbations, Fong & Vedaldi 2017) with a learned U-Net-style network that, given an image and a class selector, outputs a smooth, sharp mask that simultaneously satisfies Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and Smallest Destroying Region (SDR) criteria. The objective includes (i) TV smoothness, (ii) area penalty, (iii) classification confidence on the preserved region, and (iv) low confidence when the preserved region is removed. To reduce adversarial artifacts from masking, the evidence-removal operator randomly uses either strong blur or random color + noise for the “background,” and the training includes a “fake class” trick to force conditioning on the class selector. The authors also introduce a saliency metric based on tight crops of the salient region and report improved weakly supervised localization on ImageNet and qualitative results on CIFAR-10. The approach is claimed to be model-agnostic to the target classifier (AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50), and real-time (≈100 images/s).

Relation to prior work
Compared to gradient/backprop methods (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), this learns a parametric saliency predictor and optimizes fidelity to the classifier via SSR/SDR losses rather than relying on raw gradients. Relative to optimization-based perturbation methods (Cao et al.; Fong & Vedaldi’s Meaningful Perturbations), it amortizes the optimization, achieving orders-of-magnitude speedups while improving sharpness and avoiding adversarial artifacts through explicit regularization and randomized evidence removal. It is related in spirit to weakly supervised localization (CAM/Grad-CAM), but differs by training a separate network to emulate the black-box’s evidence under masking.

Strengths
- Novel direction: amortizing saliency via a learned masking network conditioned on class, trained to satisfy both SSR and SDR in a single objective. This is a bold, impactful idea with practical benefits (real-time explanations, potential for video).
- Careful treatment of artifact-inducing masking: randomized backgrounds, TV smoothness, low-res mask upsampling, and clear articulation of the “introduced evidence” problem.
- Practical training innovations: fake-class sampling to enforce class-conditionality; simple yet effective feature filter with class embeddings; robust nonlinearity for mask generation.
- Empirical results: improved weakly supervised localization on ImageNet relative to several baselines and iterative methods; competitive with fully supervised VGG; strong qualitative sharpness; generalization to CIFAR-10 (even without a pretrained encoder).
- New metric for SSR-style evaluation that avoids adversarial masking by using tight crops; attempts to ground interpretability beyond localization.

Weaknesses and questions
- “Model-agnostic”/“black-box” terminology is imprecise. Training appears to require differentiability and backprop through the classifier to update the masking model. If so, the method is not black-box in the strict sense (no gradients). Please clarify whether gradients through fc are used; if not, how are masking parameters trained?
- Saliency metric definition needs clarification. The paper writes s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p) with lower-is-better. Given logs are negative for a, p in (0,1), the product is positive. The rationale and monotonicity are unclear. Was division intended? How sensitive are conclusions to this form, the 0.05 floor, and aspect-ratio distortions from resizing crops?
- Missing comparisons: CAM/Grad-CAM and Grad-CAM++ are natural baselines for localization and interpretability; LIME is also relevant for black-box explanations. Including these (at least CAM/Grad-CAM) would strengthen claims.
- Faithfulness vs plausibility: The masking model has its own inductive biases (U-Net, pretrained encoder). Although the SDR term enforces causal deletion, an ablation showing the effect of each loss term (SSR, SDR, TV, AV, evidence-removal choices) on deletion/insertion behavior would better support faithfulness claims.
- Generality/cost: A separate masking model is trained per target classifier. Some discussion of data requirements, convergence time, and transfer across classifiers with similar feature spaces would be useful.
- Evaluation details: Tables are referenced but not visible here; please report absolute runtimes vs iterative methods, and provide standard deviations or statistical tests where applicable. Clarify whether hyperparameters were tuned on the evaluation metric.
- Potential leakage in “Center” baseline strength is acknowledged; nonetheless, ensure evaluation splits and the 250k training images are standard to avoid overfitting concerns.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a thoughtfully designed objective and training protocol. Some theoretical claims (metric “information-theoretic” interpretation, exponent choice in the SDR term) are informal and could be better justified. The empirical section is compelling but would benefit from stronger ablations and broader baselines.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The introduced-evidence discussion is excellent. Please:
- Precisely define “black-box/differentiable” assumptions.
- Clarify the saliency metric (formula, sign, intuition).
- Provide more concrete training details (optimizer, schedule, time to train), and ablations per loss term.

Originality
High. The idea of learning a fast saliency predictor via SSR/SDR-driven training against a fixed classifier is a substantial conceptual step beyond per-image optimization and gradient maps.

Significance
High practical impact (real-time, video). If the faithfulness holds across architectures and datasets, this could become a standard tool. The proposed metric, even if imperfect, pushes toward more robust evaluation.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel idea with clear practical benefits.
- Good empirical results and clear qualitative improvements.
- Thoughtful handling of adversarial artifacts and conditioning.
- Opens new application avenues (real-time explanations, video).

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity about “black-box” assumptions and reliance on gradients.
- Saliency metric needs clearer justification; missing key baselines and ablations.
- Limited analysis of faithfulness vs masking-model bias.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). This is a bold and promising direction with substantial novelty and utility; the noted issues are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution.

Confidence
3.5/5 (familiar with saliency/explainability; some details missing from the provided text/tables).
