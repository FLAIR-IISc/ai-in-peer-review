### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel method for generating image saliency maps for pre-trained image classifiers. The central idea is to train a separate, model-based saliency generator instead of relying on slow, iterative optimization techniques for each input image. The proposed saliency generator is a U-Net-like architecture that takes an image and a target class as input and produces a saliency mask in a single forward pass, enabling real-time performance. The training process optimizes a carefully designed objective function that encourages masks to be smooth, small (Smallest Sufficient Region), and effective at both preserving the class score when applied and destroying it when inverted (Smallest Destroying Region). The method treats the target classifier as a differentiable black box, requiring only forward and backward passes through it during training, but no access during inference.

The authors evaluate their method on ImageNet by training separate saliency models for AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50. They demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the weakly-supervised object localization task, outperforming previous saliency and localization methods. To better evaluate the quality of explanations, the paper introduces a new saliency metric that measures the concentration of class-relevant information within a cropped region. Under this metric, the proposed method achieves results comparable to ground-truth bounding boxes. The approach is also validated on the CIFAR-10 dataset to show its generality. This work positions itself as a significant advancement over both fast but low-quality gradient-based methods and high-quality but slow perturbation-based methods, offering a practical combination of speed and accuracy.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments.

*   **Technical Soundness**: The proposed objective function (Eq. 4) is well-motivated, combining principles of Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and Smallest Destroying Region (SDR) with a smoothness prior (Total Variation). The architecture of the masking model, based on a U-Net with a ResNet encoder, is a strong and standard choice for image-to-image tasks. The training methodology includes thoughtful considerations, such as using "fake" labels to ensure the model attends to the class input and randomizing the evidence removal strategy to improve robustness. These details suggest a thorough and careful implementation.
*   **Experimental Rigor**: The experiments are comprehensive. The method is tested against three different and popular classifier architectures on the large-scale ImageNet dataset. The evaluation is performed using a standard, established benchmark (weakly-supervised object localization) and a novel, well-justified metric proposed by the authors. The inclusion of the CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates that the approach is not limited to a single dataset or dependent on a pre-trained encoder for the masking model. The results consistently show the superiority of the proposed method over relevant baselines and prior art.

### Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The core concepts are explained logically, building from the definition of saliency to the objective function and finally the model architecture.

*   **Organization**: The structure flows well, motivating the problem, reviewing related work, defining the technical approach, and then presenting experimental validation. Figures are used effectively to illustrate concepts (e.g., SSR vs. SDR, model architecture) and results.
*   **Reproducibility**: The paper provides key details about the model architecture and training process, such as hyperparameter values for the loss function and the use of fake labels. However, more detail on the optimization process (e.g., optimizer, learning rates, schedules) would further aid reproducibility. The motivation behind the non-standard final activation function (Eq. 8) is briefly mentioned but could benefit from a more detailed explanation or ablation study.

### Originality

The paper presents a novel and original approach to saliency map generation.

*   **Novelty of Approach**: While concepts like SSR/SDR and perturbation-based saliency exist (e.g., Fong and Vedaldi [2], which the authors correctly cite as parallel work), the idea of framing saliency map generation as a learning problem and training a dedicated feed-forward model to produce them is a significant departure from previous iterative methods. This work effectively amortizes the optimization cost into a one-time training phase, which is a novel and impactful contribution to the field of model interpretability.
*   **Relation to Prior Work**: The paper does an excellent job of situating its contributions within the existing literature. It clearly distinguishes its approach from gradient-based methods (faster but less accurate) and optimization-based methods (more accurate but much slower), carving out a new space that combines the benefits of both. The introduction of a new saliency metric is also an original contribution.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are important and likely to have a significant impact on both researchers and practitioners in machine learning.

*   **State-of-the-Art Advancement**: The method achieves a new state-of-the-art in weakly-supervised object localization, demonstrating a tangible improvement on a difficult and widely studied problem.
*   **Practical Impact**: The real-time performance (>100 FPS) is a major practical breakthrough for saliency methods. Iterative techniques can take seconds or minutes per image, rendering them unusable for applications requiring low latency, such as analyzing video streams or providing interactive feedback. This work makes high-quality saliency analysis practical for such applications.
*   **Future Research**: The model-based framework is extensible. As the authors note, the architecture and objective function can be modified to generate different kinds of explanations (e.g., segmentations) or incorporate other desirable properties, opening up new avenues for research in interpretable AI.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Novel Framework**: The core contribution—training a generative model for saliency maps—is a novel and powerful paradigm that addresses the key speed-accuracy trade-off in existing methods.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Results**: The paper demonstrates significant empirical gains on the challenging task of weakly-supervised object localization on ImageNet.
3.  **High Practical Value**: The real-time inference speed makes high-quality model explanation practical for a new range of applications, most notably video analysis.
4.  **Strong Technical Execution**: The proposed objective function, model design, and training strategy are well-designed and thoroughly evaluated.
5.  **New Metric Contribution**: The paper also introduces a novel and intuitive metric for evaluating saliency maps based on information concentration.

### Arguments Against Acceptance / Points for Clarification:

1.  **"Black Box" Terminology**: The term "black box" may be slightly imprecise. The training procedure requires gradient access to the target classifier, making it a "differentiable black box" method. It cannot be applied to models where only query access is available. A clarification of this distinction would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Per-Classifier Training Cost**: The method requires training a new saliency model for each target classifier. This trade-off—a heavy offline training cost for fast online inference, and the need to retrain if the target model changes—should be discussed more explicitly.
3.  **Potential Bias of the Masking Model**: The masking model itself, particularly when using a pre-trained encoder, may have its own biases that could influence the generated saliency maps. While the CIFAR-10 experiment without pre-training partially addresses this, a brief discussion of this potential confounding factor would be welcome.
