Summary
The paper proposes a trainable masking network (U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) that predicts saliency masks for a given image and class in a single forward pass. The network is trained against a fixed image classifier using a loss that combines: (i) total variation for smoothness, (ii) area penalty, (iii) a “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) term encouraging the preserved region to keep the class probability high, and (iv) a “smallest destroying region” (SDR) term encouraging the class probability to drop when the salient region is removed. To mitigate adversarial artifacts from masking, the authors randomize the “alternative image” (blur/noisy color) and regularize masks. They also propose a new saliency metric s(a,p) intended to quantify how much class evidence is concentrated in a small crop. They evaluate on ImageNet with three black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) and on CIFAR-10, reporting improved weakly supervised localization error and better scores on the proposed metric compared to several baselines and iterative perturbation methods.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on perturbation-based saliency (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi 2017; Cao et al. 2015) and backprop-based methods (saliency, Guided BP, Excitation BP, CAM). The main claimed novelty is replacing per-image iterative optimization with a trained, feed-forward “masking model,” and a combined SSR/SDR training objective.

Strengths
- Addresses an efficiency limitation of iterative perturbation methods; shows plausible real-time throughput.
- Joint SSR/SDR objective is reasonable and may reduce trivial solutions.
- Architectural choice (U-Net) and training tricks (fake labels, randomized alternative images) are sensible and clearly described.
- Weakly supervised localization results are competitive, and qualitative maps look sharp.
- Attempt to formalize evaluation via a metric is welcome.

Weaknesses
- “Black-box” claim is misleading: training requires backpropagating through the classifier’s probabilities. This is not black-box in the standard sense (score-only access), and the method must be retrained per classifier.
- Limited and partially non-standard evaluation. There is no rigorous runtime comparison or wall-clock benchmarking vs iterative methods; key baselines (CAM/Grad-CAM, Excitation BP, Guided Grad-CAM) are not convincingly compared quantitatively. The new metric is not validated against human judgments or established insertion/deletion curves.
- The proposed metric s(a,p) is ad hoc, sensitive to resize and the area floor (0.05), and can be gamed by background cues; reliance on SSR-like cropping favors methods that pick small discriminative patches, not necessarily faithful evidence. No analysis of correlation with trustworthiness.
- Potential artifact/priors leakage is not thoroughly addressed. The masking model uses a pre-trained ImageNet encoder that likely encodes class priors similar to the target classifier; this weakens the “agnostic” claim. No ablation on encoder choice, TV weight, or the alternative-image scheme.
- Generalization and robustness are underexplored: no cross-model generalization (train on one classifier, test on another), cross-dataset tests, or OOD robustness. CIFAR-10 experiment is minimal and qualitative.
- Several design choices are heuristic (30% fake labels, loss weights, area threshold, mask upsampling), with limited sensitivity analysis.
- Theoretical grounding is limited: the joint SSR/SDR objective can be conflicting; trade-offs and failure modes are not studied.

Quality
The method is technically plausible, and training details are mostly transparent. However, the empirical evidence falls short of the rigor needed to endorse a new saliency procedure: missing strong baselines, limited metrics, no statistical analysis, and insufficient ablations. Claims of being “model-agnostic” or “black-box” are overstated given the need for gradients and per-classifier training.

Clarity
Generally well organized and readable. Some notational issues and ambiguities:
- The saliency metric formula appears as s(a,p) = log(ã) log(p); if the intent is log(ã) − log(p), please clarify and justify.
- The resizing of arbitrary crops “disregarding aspect ratio” can bias the metric; discuss its impact.
- Provide explicit tables with numbers and splits; the current narrative references figures/tables that are not fully available here.

Originality
Moderate. Training a separate, feed-forward mask estimator to mimic a perturbation objective is a natural extension but not a major conceptual leap. The combined SSR/SDR loss and metric are incremental.

Significance
Potentially useful in practice if validated more rigorously, as it addresses speed and yields sharp maps. However, without thorough comparisons to established baselines and stronger evaluation protocols, the impact is uncertain.

Questions for the authors
1. Clarify the exact form and rationale of the saliency metric and report its sensitivity to the area floor and aspect-ratio distortion.
2. Provide quantitative comparisons against CAM/Grad-CAM and Excitation BP on the same localization protocol, as well as insertion/deletion AUC (or similar) to assess causal faithfulness.
3. Report wall-clock times vs iterative meaningful perturbations (same hardware), including training amortization.
4. Ablate: TV weight, alternative-image variants, fake-label probability, encoder choice (from scratch vs ImageNet), and the SDR term weight.
5. Does a model trained on classifier A generalize to B without retraining? If not, how “agnostic” is the approach in practice?
6. Analyze failure cases and the effect of adversarial mask artifacts quantitatively.

Pros
- Addresses a real practical limitation (speed) of perturbation-based saliency.
- Clear method and reasonable engineering.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization without using boxes.
- Real-time inference is attractive.

Cons
- Overstated “black-box/model-agnostic” claim; requires gradients and per-classifier training.
- Insufficiently rigorous evaluation and missing key baselines/metrics.
- New metric is ad hoc and not validated.
- Limited ablations and robustness/generalization analysis.
- Heavily reliant on a pre-trained encoder that may inject priors.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and could be impactful with stronger, standard evaluations, ablations, and clearer positioning relative to established methods. I encourage the authors to strengthen the empirical section and clarify the “black-box” claim.
