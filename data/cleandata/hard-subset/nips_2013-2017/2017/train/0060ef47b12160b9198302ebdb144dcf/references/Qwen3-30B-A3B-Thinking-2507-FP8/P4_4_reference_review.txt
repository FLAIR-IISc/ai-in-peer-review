

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection for black box classifiers, addressing the need for fast and accurate saliency maps that can be used to interpret classifier decisions. The authors present a trainable model (based on U-Net architecture) that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass, contrasting with iterative methods that require hundreds of evaluations. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which evaluates how small a region can be while still allowing confident classification. The paper also addresses the problem of "introduced evidence" when removing image regions, proposing a masking function that avoids adversarial artifacts through smoothness constraints and random alternative images.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on saliency detection, particularly [11, 12, 16, 17, 2], which focus on gradient-based methods or iterative optimization. The authors position their work as a significant improvement by enabling real-time saliency detection while maintaining high quality.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors provide a clear objective function (equation 4) that balances multiple requirements: mask smoothness (TV penalty), small region size, classification confidence on preserved regions, and low classification confidence on removed regions. The training process is well-described, including specific strategies to prevent the model from ignoring class selectors. The experimental evaluation on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 with three different black box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) provides robust evidence for the method's effectiveness. The new saliency metric (equation 3) is well-motivated and provides a more appropriate evaluation criterion than standard localization accuracy. The ablation studies in section 5.1 and 5.2 further support the claims about the importance of the different components.

The paper is a complete work, not just a position paper, with sufficient theoretical analysis and extensive experimental validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the method's reliance on the black box classifier's behavior and the need for further research on model biases.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively frames the problem and motivation, and the related work section provides a comprehensive overview of relevant prior work while clearly distinguishing this contribution. The methodology is described with sufficient detail, including architecture diagrams (figure 4) and mathematical formulations. The experimental results are presented clearly with appropriate metrics and comparisons. The figures (1-6) are well-chosen to illustrate key concepts and results. The paper provides enough information for an expert reader to reproduce the main findings, including the specific architecture choices, training parameters, and evaluation protocols.

## Originality
This work makes several novel contributions. First, it proposes a model-based approach to saliency detection that trains a single model to predict saliency maps for any input image, rather than using iterative optimization for each image. This is distinct from parallel work [2] which optimizes masks iteratively. Second, the authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region concept, which better aligns with the actual purpose of saliency maps than standard localization accuracy. Third, they address the important issue of "introduced evidence" when removing image regions through a carefully designed masking function with smoothness constraints and randomized alternative images. The paper demonstrates how these contributions combine to create a method that is both faster and more accurate than existing approaches.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. The proposed method achieves real-time performance (100 saliency masks per second) while outperforming iterative methods and other saliency detection techniques on standard benchmarks. The paper demonstrates that the method can be applied to different image classifiers without retraining, making it broadly applicable. The new saliency metric provides a more meaningful way to evaluate saliency explanations, which is important for the field. The potential applications in video-saliency and real-time interpretation of black box classifiers (e.g., in autonomous vehicles) are compelling and well-motivated. The work advances the state of the art in explainable AI by providing a practical solution to a significant problem.

## Strengths
- Proposes a novel model-based approach to saliency detection that enables real-time performance
- Introduces a new saliency metric that better aligns with the purpose of saliency maps
- Effectively addresses the "introduced evidence" problem through careful masking design
- Demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple datasets and classifiers
- Provides clear analysis of the method's components and limitations
- Well-written with clear organization and appropriate figures

## Weaknesses
- The paper could provide more detailed comparison to more recent saliency methods that might be faster than iterative approaches (e.g., [17] mentions iterative methods but doesn't compare to other fast methods)
- The discussion of limitations is somewhat brief, particularly regarding how the method might perform with very complex or ambiguous images
- The claim about outperforming "other weakly supervised techniques" would be stronger with more specific comparisons
- The potential application to video saliency is mentioned but not demonstrated with any experiments

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of explainable AI. The proposed method for real-time saliency detection is technically sound, novel, and significant. The paper is well-written and provides comprehensive experimental validation. While there are some areas for improvement in the comparison to recent work and discussion of limitations, these do not detract from the overall quality and contribution of the work.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning interpretation with a technically sound and practically useful solution. The work advances the state of the art in saliency detection and provides a foundation for future applications in real-time explainable AI.

**Recommendation: Accept**
