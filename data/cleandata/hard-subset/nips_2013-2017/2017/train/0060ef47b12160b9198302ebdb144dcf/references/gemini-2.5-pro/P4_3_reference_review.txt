This paper proposes a novel, model-based approach for generating image saliency maps for black-box classifiers. The primary motivation is to overcome the trade-off between speed and quality in existing methods. Current techniques are either fast but produce low-quality maps (e.g., gradient-based methods) or are slow and iterative but yield high-quality, interpretable maps (e.g., perturbation-based optimization methods). The authors propose to train a deep neural network, termed a "masking model," to generate a saliency mask for a given image and class label in a single feed-forward pass. This amortizes the optimization process, enabling real-time performance.

The core of the method is a carefully designed objective function that the masking model is trained to minimize. This objective treats the target classifier as a black box and combines four terms: (1) a total variation penalty to ensure mask smoothness and prevent adversarial artifacts; (2) an area penalty to encourage small masks; (3) a term to maximize the classifier's confidence on the preserved region (Smallest Sufficient Region, SSR); and (4) a term to minimize the classifier's confidence on the removed region (Smallest Destroying Region, SDR). The masking model itself uses a U-Net architecture with a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder. To accompany their method, the authors also introduce a new saliency metric that evaluates the "concentration of information" by measuring the classifier's confidence on the smallest rectangular crop containing the salient region, normalized by the crop's area.

This work relates to a large body of literature on classifier explainability. It directly contrasts with gradient-based methods like Simonyan et al. (2013) and perturbation-based methods like Fong and Vedaldi (2017). The key distinction from the latter, which also optimizes a mask in the input space, is the paradigm shift from a slow, per-instance optimization to a fast, learned, amortized prediction. The work also builds upon ideas from weakly supervised localization, using localization accuracy as a key benchmark.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of training a generative model to produce saliency maps is novel and represents a significant conceptual advance in the field of explainable AI. By replacing per-instance optimization with a single forward pass, the method achieves real-time performance (over 100 images/sec), which makes high-quality saliency analysis practical for applications like video processing, which was previously infeasible with iterative methods. This is a major contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-executed. The objective function is well-motivated, thoughtfully combining several desirable properties (smoothness, SSR, SDR) for a saliency map. The experimental evaluation is thorough, demonstrating the method's effectiveness across multiple standard black-box architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10). The results are compelling, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, outperforming prior saliency methods and even approaching the performance of some fully supervised models.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-articulated, the methodology is described in sufficient detail, and the experiments are clearly presented. The figures, particularly the qualitative comparisons in Figure 5, are highly effective at demonstrating the superiority of the generated masks compared to alternatives.

4.  **New Evaluation Metric:** The introduction of a new saliency metric is a valuable contribution. This metric provides a principled way to evaluate how well a saliency method identifies a compact region that is sufficient for classification, which is a core goal of saliency. This moves beyond using localization as a proxy for saliency and offers a more direct measure of explanation quality.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **High Training Cost:** The primary practical limitation is the need to train a new, computationally expensive masking model for *each* black-box classifier that one wishes to explain. While inference is fast, this high upfront training cost could be a significant barrier to adoption, particularly when compared to methods that require no training. The paper demonstrates this with large models on ImageNet, and the cost could be prohibitive for practitioners who need to analyze many different models.

2.  **Complexity of Training:** The training procedure appears complex and reliant on specific heuristics. The authors note the necessity of introducing fake labels during training to ensure the model attends to the class selector and the use of a randomized scheme for evidence removal (blurring vs. noise). The robustness of the training and its sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., the λ weights in the objective function, the 30% probability of a fake label) are not fully explored, which raises questions about the ease of applying this method in new settings.

3.  **Minor Lack of Detail:** Some design choices could be justified more thoroughly. For example, the non-standard nonlinearity in Equation 8 is motivated by stating that standard functions "did not optimise properly." A more detailed explanation of the failure modes of sigmoid/tanh and the specific benefits of the proposed two-channel formulation would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and highly significant paradigm for generating saliency maps via amortized inference.
*   Achieves real-time performance, enabling new applications for high-quality interpretability.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark (weakly supervised localization).
*   The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and clear.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The high training cost per explained model is a significant practical drawback.
*   The training process seems complex and may not be robust or easily generalizable without careful tuning.

In summary, this paper presents a complete and impactful piece of work. The conceptual contribution of a model-based, real-time saliency generator is highly original and significant. This is supported by strong empirical results that advance the state of the art. While the practical limitation of requiring a separate trained model for each black-box classifier is notable, the demonstrated benefits in speed and quality are substantial enough to make this a highly valuable contribution to the NIPS community. The strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses.
