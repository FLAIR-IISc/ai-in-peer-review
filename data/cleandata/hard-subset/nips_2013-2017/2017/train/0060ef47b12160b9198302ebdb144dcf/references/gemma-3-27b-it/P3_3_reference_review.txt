## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and practical approach to generating saliency maps for black-box classifiers. The core idea of learning a saliency detector rather than iteratively computing it is a significant contribution, enabling real-time performance without sacrificing (and arguably *improving*) quality. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental results. While the presented work builds upon existing techniques, the combination of a trainable masking model, the proposed loss function, and the new saliency metric represent a solid and original contribution suitable for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.

**Summary:**

The authors tackle the problem of explaining the decisions of image classifiers, specifically addressing the limitations of existing saliency methods which are often slow or produce low-quality explanations. They propose learning a model (based on a U-Net architecture) to directly predict saliency maps for any input image, bypassing the need for iterative optimization.  The key innovation lies in the objective function used to train this model, which balances smoothness, small region preservation (SSR), and disruption of classification (SDR). They also introduce a new saliency metric based on the size of the minimal cropped region necessary to preserve classification confidence.  The method is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, demonstrating superior performance in terms of speed, localization accuracy, and interpretability (according to their proposed metric) compared to existing techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Practicality & Speed:** The ability to generate saliency maps in real-time is a major strength. This opens doors to applications like video saliency and interactive debugging of classifiers.
* **Improved Saliency Map Quality:**  The results demonstrate that the learned saliency maps are sharper and more accurate than those generated by iterative methods like those based on patch removal or optimization. The visual examples (Figures 5 & 6) are convincing.
* **Novel Objective Function:** The proposed loss function effectively balances competing goals (smoothness, SSR, SDR) and encourages the model to learn meaningful saliency masks.  The careful consideration of avoiding the introduction of spurious evidence via masking is well-motivated and addressed.
* **New Saliency Metric:** The SSR-inspired metric is a valuable contribution, providing a more nuanced evaluation of saliency than traditional localization accuracy.  It intuitively captures the desired properties of a good saliency map – minimal region, maximal information preservation.
* **Thorough Experiments:**  The experiments on ImageNet with multiple black-box classifiers, coupled with the CIFAR-10 demonstration, provide strong evidence of the method's generalizability.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow.  The authors clearly explain the motivation, methodology, and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention the choice of parameters for the objective function, a more detailed sensitivity analysis of these parameters would be beneficial. How robust is the method to changes in  λ1, λ2, λ3, and λ4?
* **U-Net Architecture Justification:** The choice of a U-Net architecture is somewhat justified by its ability to leverage multi-resolution features. However, a more thorough exploration of alternative architectures could potentially yield further improvements.  Was a more extensive architecture search conducted?
* **Reliance on Pre-trained ResNet:** While using a pre-trained ResNet is understandable for speeding up training, the success on CIFAR-10 demonstrates the method *can* be trained from scratch. The implications of the pre-training (potential biases) could be discussed more explicitly.
* **Metric Limitations:** The saliency metric, while novel and insightful, relies on the classifier's robustness to scale and aspect ratio changes. This is acknowledged, but it's a potential limitation worth emphasizing. It would be interesting to see how the metric correlates with human perception of saliency.
* **Adversarial Artifacts - Still a Concern:** While the authors address adversarial artifacts in their masking approach, the issue isn't completely eliminated. It would be good to provide examples of cases where the masks still exhibit such artifacts and discuss potential mitigation strategies.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important problem in the interpretability of deep learning models.
* The proposed method is a significant improvement over existing techniques in terms of speed and quality.
* The novel objective function and saliency metric are valuable contributions to the field.
* The experimental results are strong and convincing.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The method's sensitivity to hyperparameters is not fully explored.
* The U-Net architecture choice is not exhaustively justified.
* The reliance on a pre-trained ResNet introduces potential biases.



**Specific Comments/Suggestions:**

* **Section 3.1:** The discussion of adversarial artifacts is well-motivated. However, could the authors include a visual example of a problematic adversarial mask and its effect on classification?
* **Section 4.1:** Explain why the sigmoid nonlinearity wasn't effective, as that would be helpful to understand the rationale for the alternative nonlinearity (Equation 8).
* **Table 1 & 2:** Please provide confidence intervals for the localization accuracy numbers.
* **Future Work:**  The suggestion of exploring biases in the masking model itself is excellent and should be highlighted.



**Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI. The method is practical, effective, and well-supported by experimental results.  I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2017.**
