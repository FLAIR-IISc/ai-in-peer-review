This paper tackles the important problem of explaining the decisions of black-box classifiers via saliency detection. The core idea – learning a saliency *model* rather than computing saliency maps iteratively – is genuinely novel and potentially impactful. While the paper isn’t perfectly polished, the boldness of the approach and the promising results warrant strong consideration. It feels like a significant shift in thinking about saliency, moving away from post-hoc analysis and towards a learned explanation mechanism.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing saliency methods and the need for a faster, more accurate approach. The introduction effectively highlights the unintuitive behavior of modern classifiers and the value of understanding *why* a classifier makes a particular decision. The proposed solution – a U-Net architecture trained to predict saliency maps – is clever and leverages existing deep learning infrastructure effectively. The introduction of the SSR/SDR concepts and the associated saliency metric are valuable contributions, providing a more nuanced way to evaluate saliency maps than simple localization accuracy.  The attempts to mitigate introduced evidence during mask creation demonstrate a thoughtful consideration of potential pitfalls.

However, the paper does have weaknesses. The experimental setup, while reasonably comprehensive, lacks a detailed ablation study. It’s unclear how much each component of the objective function (smoothness, area, classification probability) contributes to the final performance. Furthermore, the discussion of hyperparameter tuning feels a bit cursory. The CIFAR-10 experiment is welcome, but feels somewhat tacked on – a more thorough analysis comparing the learned features to those of the base classifier would be beneficial. The writing is generally clear, but occasionally suffers from slightly awkward phrasing and repetition.  The adversarial artifact discussion is a little hand-wavy; while the proposed mitigations are sensible, more quantitative analysis would be stronger.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of learning a saliency model is a significant departure from existing iterative methods.
*   **Speed:** The promise of real-time saliency detection opens up new application areas (video saliency).
*   **Performance:**  The results demonstrate competitive performance on standard benchmarks, and the new saliency metric provides a compelling evaluation framework.
*   **Thoughtful Considerations:** The authors proactively address potential issues like introduced evidence and adversarial artifacts.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper provides a strong rationale for the work, highlighting the importance of explainable AI.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Ablation Study:**  Insufficient analysis of the contribution of individual components to the objective function.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Discussion lacks depth.
*   **CIFAR-10 Experiment:** Feels somewhat superficial.
*   **Adversarial Artifacts:** Discussion is qualitative; quantitative analysis would strengthen the argument.
*   **Writing:**  Occasional awkward phrasing and repetition.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a fundamentally new approach to saliency detection with the potential to significantly impact the field.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate competitive performance.
*   The novel saliency metric provides a valuable tool for evaluating saliency maps.
*   The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the challenges and limitations of existing methods.
*   The work opens up exciting new avenues for research, such as real-time video saliency and weakly-supervised segmentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks a comprehensive ablation study to fully understand the contributions of each component.
*   The experimental analysis could be more rigorous, particularly regarding hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   The writing could benefit from further polishing.



**Overall:**

Despite minor flaws, this is a strong paper with a compelling idea. The novelty of the approach, the promising results, and the potential for significant impact outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper has the potential to be highly influential and recommend **acceptance**. It’s a bold move that prioritizes a new direction over methodological perfection, which I find commendable. I would encourage the authors to address the ablation study and hyperparameter tuning points in a revision, but these are not critical for acceptance.
