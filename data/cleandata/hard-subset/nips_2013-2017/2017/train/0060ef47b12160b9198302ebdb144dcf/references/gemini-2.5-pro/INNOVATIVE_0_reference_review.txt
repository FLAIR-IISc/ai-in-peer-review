**Summary**

This paper addresses the trade-off between speed and quality in saliency methods for explaining black-box image classifiers. Current methods are either fast but produce low-quality maps (e.g., gradient-based) or are high-quality but computationally prohibitive for real-time use due to their iterative, per-instance optimization nature (e.g., perturbation-based methods). The authors propose a novel and compelling alternative: instead of iteratively *finding* a saliency mask for each input, they train a separate deep neural network (a "masking model") to *predict* it in a single, fast forward pass. This masking model is trained using a carefully designed objective function that encourages masks to be small, smooth, and effective at both preserving classifier confidence (Smallest Sufficient Region) and destroying it (Smallest Destroying Region). This approach is model-agnostic, treating the classifier being explained as a black box. The authors also introduce a new metric for saliency that measures the "concentration of information" in a cropped region. Through experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, they demonstrate that their method is orders of magnitude faster than iterative approaches while achieving state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Boldness of the Core Idea:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its conceptual shift. It reframes saliency map generation from a per-instance optimization problem to an amortized prediction task. This idea of learning a dedicated model to explain another model is a significant and exciting new direction for the field of interpretability. It moves beyond simply refining existing iterative techniques and proposes a fundamentally different, and potentially much more scalable, paradigm. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-concept work that pushes the field forward.

2.  **Major Practical Significance and Impact:** The proposed method is not just an incremental improvement; it represents a step-change in capability. By achieving real-time performance (over 100 FPS), the authors unlock new applications, such as video saliency, which are simply impossible with previous high-quality methods. This practical utility, combined with the method's high performance, ensures that this work will likely be widely used and built upon by both researchers and practitioners.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide compelling evidence that their approach does not sacrifice quality for speed. In fact, their method outperforms prior art, including the slower iterative methods, on the standard benchmark of weakly supervised object localization on ImageNet. The results are consistent across multiple black-box architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50), demonstrating the general applicability of the framework. The CIFAR-10 experiment further shows the method's flexibility and that it does not strictly rely on a pre-trained ImageNet encoder.

4.  **Thoughtful Methodological Details:** The paper includes several well-considered contributions beyond the main idea. The objective function (Eq. 4) is principled, elegantly combining the dual goals of SSR and SDR with necessary regularizers for smoothness and size. The introduction of a new saliency metric, while not perfect, shows a commitment to thinking deeply about what makes a good explanation and moving beyond proxy tasks like localization.

**Weaknesses**

While this is a strong paper, there are some minor points that could be improved:

1.  **Complexity of the Training Procedure:** The training process appears to require several specific heuristics to work well, such as introducing "fake" labels to force the model to use the class selector and randomizing the evidence removal scheme (blur vs. noise). While the authors are commendably transparent about these details, it suggests that training may be somewhat delicate and less robust than a simpler end-to-end approach. However, given the novelty and effectiveness of the overall framework, this is a minor and acceptable flaw.

2.  **Limited Analysis of the "Explainer's Bias":** The authors briefly acknowledge in the conclusion that their masking model might have its own biases. This is a critical point. The explanation is now generated by another complex model, which could potentially learn to produce plausible-looking but unfaithful saliency maps. While the strong quantitative results suggest this is not a practical issue here, a deeper discussion or preliminary analysis of this potential meta-problem would have strengthened the paper. I view this more as an exciting avenue for future work inspired by this paper than a critical omission.

3.  **Ad-hoc Formulation of the New Metric:** The new saliency metric is intuitively appealing, capturing the idea of finding a small region that preserves class probability. However, its specific mathematical form—a log-log ratio with a hardcoded threshold—feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks a rigorous information-theoretic grounding despite the authors' brief comment. This does not detract from the main contribution of the paper but is a point of methodological softness.

**Recommendation: Accept**

Arguments for acceptance:
*   The core idea is highly original and constitutes a new paradigm for saliency generation.
*   The work has enormous potential for impact, enabling real-time applications previously out of reach.
*   The method is not just fast; it achieves state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and the experiments are thorough.

Arguments against acceptance:
*   The training procedure seems to rely on a number of heuristics.
*   A deeper analysis of the potential biases of the explainer model itself is missing.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The weaknesses are minor and do not undermine the significance of the core contribution. This work presents a bold, novel, and highly effective approach to a well-known problem. It prioritizes a powerful new idea and demonstrates its value with compelling results, even if some of the finer methodological details could be further polished in future work. This is exactly the type of forward-looking research that NIPS should showcase.
