## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach to real-time image saliency detection. The authors tackle a practical problem – understanding the decisions of black-box classifiers – with a novel and effective solution. While the paper isn't groundbreaking in terms of fundamental theoretical advances, it delivers a significant engineering contribution with a clear demonstration of performance improvements over existing methods. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address clarity in certain sections and further discussion of limitations.

**Summary:**

The paper addresses the challenge of generating saliency maps for image classifiers in a computationally efficient manner. Traditional iterative methods for saliency detection are too slow for real-time applications. The authors propose a learned model – a U-Net – trained to directly predict saliency maps given an image and class label.  This bypasses the iterative optimization, offering substantial speedups. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the “smallest sufficient region” and “smallest destroying region” concepts, and demonstrate competitive performance on standard benchmarks and their novel metric.  Crucially, they show results on ImageNet and a smaller CIFAR-10 experiment showcasing generalizability.

**Strengths:**

* **Practicality and Speed:** The key strength is the practical impact. Achieving real-time saliency detection is a significant advancement, opening possibilities for applications like video saliency analysis. The reported speed is impressive.
* **Novel Approach:** Training a dedicated model to predict saliency maps instead of iteratively optimizing is a smart and effective idea. This shifts the computation from inference time to training time, a valuable trade-off.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on ImageNet, particularly the competitive performance on object localization and the new saliency metric, are convincing. The CIFAR-10 experiment provides further validation.
* **Well-defined Saliency Metric:** The SSR/SDR-based saliency metric is a valuable contribution. It provides a more nuanced evaluation than simple localization accuracy, attempting to capture the *quality* of the explanation.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clear, and the technical details are presented adequately.
* **Addressing Introduced Evidence:** The discussion of adversarial artifacts and techniques to mitigate them (Eq. 2, random A) demonstrates a strong awareness of potential pitfalls and a careful approach to methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Depth:** While the approach is effective, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis. Why *does* this approach work so well? Are there theoretical guarantees on the quality of the saliency maps?  A more detailed discussion of the model’s capacity and potential biases would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The objective function (Eq. 4) contains several hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4). The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive sensitivity analysis of these parameters. How robust are the results to changes in these weights?  What guidelines can be offered for setting them?
* **U-Net Architecture Choice:**  While the U-Net is a reasonable choice, the justification for its specific architecture (ResNet-50 encoder, specific number of channels) is limited.  Was any experimentation done with alternative architectures?
* **Dependency on Black-Box Classifier:** The performance of the saliency model is naturally dependent on the quality of the underlying black-box classifier. The paper acknowledges this, but doesn't fully explore the implications. How does performance vary across different classifier architectures and accuracies?  Would a poorly performing classifier lead to misleading saliency maps?
* **Clarity on "Fake Label" Training:** The description of the “fake label” training technique (Section 4.1) is a bit convoluted. More detailed explanation on the motivation and empirical results of this technique would be helpful.



**Specific Comments/Questions:**

* **Figure 4:**  The diagram of the masking model could be slightly clearer. Specifically, the flow of information through the upsampling blocks.
* **Equation 8:** The rationale behind the nonlinear activation function used to produce the mask is not entirely convincing. A bit more explanation on why sigmoid/tanh failed would be appreciated.
* **Table 3:** It would be useful to report the standard deviation of the saliency metric values to understand the variability.
* **Future Work:**  The discussion of future work is good, but could be more specific.  What are the key challenges in extending this to video saliency?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a practical and effective solution to a challenging problem.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The introduced saliency metric is a valuable contribution to the field.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The approach has the potential to enable new applications in explainable AI and computer vision.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper lacks deep theoretical analysis.
* The hyperparameter sensitivity is not thoroughly explored.
* The U-Net architecture choice is not fully justified.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017. The strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses, and the potential impact is significant. The authors should address the minor revisions suggested above, specifically focusing on providing more clarity on the experimental setup, hyperparameter choices, and theoretical limitations. Addressing these points will further strengthen an already compelling submission.
