This paper proposes a learned, model-agnostic saliency detection method intended to overcome the computational cost of iterative approaches. The core idea – training a network to predict saliency maps directly – is conceptually sound and addresses a practical limitation of existing techniques. However, the execution and presentation contain a disconcerting number of flaws, ranging from superficial to potentially undermining the core claims.

**Summary:** The authors present a U-Net based model to predict saliency maps for a given classifier and image. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient rectangular region and demonstrate improvements in speed and perceived quality over iterative methods on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a real problem:** Iterative saliency methods *are* computationally expensive, and a fast, accurate alternative is valuable.
*   **Novelty (moderate):** While the idea of learning a saliency detector isn’t entirely new, the specific architecture and the combined objective function (smoothness, small region, classification preservation, classification destruction) represent a worthwhile exploration.
*   **Empirical results:** The quantitative results (localisation accuracy, new metric) show some improvement over baselines, though the significance is debatable (see weaknesses).
*   **Clear motivation:** The introduction effectively argues the need for saliency techniques and highlights the shortcomings of existing classifiers.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor in Experimental Setup:** The experimental details are vague and raise concerns. For example, the specific hyperparameters used for the objective function (1=10, 2=10\^3, 3=5, 4=0.3) are presented without justification. Why these values? A sensitivity analysis is missing.
*   **Metric Justification:** The proposed saliency metric, while intuitively appealing, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The threshold of 0.05 for the area (ã = max(a, 0.05)) is arbitrary and unexplained. Why not 0.01? Or 0.1? The impact of this threshold isn’t explored.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The statement "Our model is able to produce real-time saliency maps" is misleading. While the model itself is fast, the paper doesn’t detail the full pipeline – including any pre-processing or post-processing steps – which could impact overall speed. Real-time performance requires demonstration on a specific hardware setup with a complete workflow.
*   **Poor Writing and Presentation:** Numerous instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors detract from readability. For example, the transition between sections 3 and 3.1 feels abrupt. The use of equation numbers is inconsistent, and the descriptions of figures are often vague ("examples of such masks can be seen in figure 3").
*   **Insufficient Comparison:** The comparison to existing methods is limited. The authors should include a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art saliency techniques, including those specifically designed for speed. Specifically, a comparison against Guided Backpropagation and similar methods would be helpful.
*   **Minor Technical Flaws:** Equation (2) uses 'A' without defining it previously. The explanation of the nonlinearity in Equation (8) is unclear, and its justification feels weak.
*   **Adversarial Artifacts – Untested Mitigation:** While the authors correctly identify the problem of adversarial artifacts, the proposed mitigation strategies (adding blur, noise) are not rigorously evaluated. Are these strategies truly effective? Do they introduce other artifacts?
*   **CIFAR-10 Experiment – Limited Scope:** The CIFAR-10 experiment feels tacked on. It’s good to see the method generalizes, but the details are sparse. Were the same hyperparameters used as in the ImageNet experiment?
* **Missing citations:** The introduction mentions [6, 11, 13] but these are not explicitly used further in the text to support claims.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The core idea has merit, and the experimental results suggest a potential improvement over iterative methods. The problem addressed is relevant.

**Con:** The numerous flaws in the experimental setup, writing, and justification of design choices significantly undermine the credibility of the results. The claims are overstated, and the comparison to existing methods is insufficient. The lack of detailed analysis and the numerous minor errors raise serious concerns about the overall rigor of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a potentially valuable idea, the paper suffers from a significant lack of polish and rigor. The numerous flaws, both major and minor, are deeply concerning. While the authors demonstrate a proof-of-concept, the work is not yet of sufficient quality for NIPS. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address the raised concerns and resubmit to a future conference. The authors need to significantly improve the experimental validation, clarify the writing, and provide a more thorough comparison to existing methods. I would be willing to reconsider a revised version if these issues were adequately addressed.
