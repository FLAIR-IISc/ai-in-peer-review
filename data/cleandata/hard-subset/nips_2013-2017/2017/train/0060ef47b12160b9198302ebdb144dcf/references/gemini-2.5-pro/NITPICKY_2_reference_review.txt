**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel, model-based approach for generating saliency maps for image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate U-Net-like network (the "masking model") to produce a saliency mask for a given image and class, targeting a pre-existing, "black-box" classifier. Instead of iteratively optimizing a mask for each new image, this method produces a mask in a single forward pass, enabling real-time performance. The masking model is trained to minimize a composite objective function that encourages small, smooth masks that are both sufficient for classification when applied (Smallest Sufficient Region - SSR) and destructive to classification when inverted (Smallest Destroying Region - SDR). The authors also introduce a new saliency metric based on the SSR principle, evaluating the classifier's confidence on a tight crop of the salient region. Experiments on ImageNet show that this method outperforms other weakly-supervised localization techniques and achieves strong results on the proposed new metric.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Efficiency:** The primary contribution—framing saliency map generation as a prediction task for a trained model rather than an iterative optimization problem—is novel and compelling. This approach achieves a significant speed-up over perturbation-based methods like Fong and Vedaldi [2], making real-time applications feasible.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The weakly supervised object localization results reported in Table 1 and 2 are impressive, outperforming previous methods and achieving performance competitive with some fully supervised techniques. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the learned masks.

3.  **Thoughtful Objective and Metric:** The paper makes a good effort to formalize the desired properties of a saliency map. The objective function (Eq. 4) which combines SSR, SDR, area, and smoothness constraints is well-motivated. The introduction of a new metric `s(a, p)` is a valuable attempt to move beyond localization accuracy and quantitatively measure a mask's explanatory power.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper is riddled with a substantial number of minor-to-moderate flaws in its methodology, experimental reporting, and overall presentation, which collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper does not meet the expected presentation standards for a top-tier conference.
    *   **References:** The bibliography is exceptionally poorly formatted and incomplete. Many entries are missing years, publication venues, or are formatted inconsistently (e.g., [11] is just "maps. CoRR,"). This sloppiness is concerning.
    *   **Figure References:** The text refers to Figures 1 through 6, but none are provided in the document. This is a critical omission, as it prevents any evaluation of the qualitative results, the proposed architecture, or the examples illustrating SSR/SDR.
    *   **Equation Numbering:** There are two distinct equations labeled as (1), which is a basic formatting error.
    *   **Self-Referential Footnote:** The footnote on page 1 referencing "NIPS 2017" appears to be a template artifact and is unprofessional.

2.  **Methodological Ambiguity and Justification:** Several key design choices lack rigorous justification.
    *   **"Black-Box" Misnomer:** The method is described as being for "black box classifiers," which is misleading. The training process requires calculating the gradient of the classifier's output with respect to the masked image, meaning the classifier must be differentiable and fully accessible (i.e., white-box) during training. The "black-box" property only applies at inference time. This is an important distinction that is not clearly made.
    *   **Arbitrary Hyperparameters:** The objective function (Eq. 4) depends on four crucial hyperparameters (`λ1-λ4`), and the training process relies on a 30% probability of using a "fake label." The paper simply states the values used without any sensitivity analysis, ablation study, or justification for their selection. The robustness of the method to these choices is entirely unknown.
    *   **Unconventional Nonlinearity:** The use of `Ms = abs(C0) / (abs(C0) + abs(C1))` in Eq. (8) is ad-hoc. The justification that "sigmoid and tanh nonlinearities did not optimise properly" is insufficient. A deeper analysis of why standard activation functions failed is required, rather than simply presenting a non-standard solution that "worked."
    *   **New Metric Concerns:** The new metric `s(a, p)` introduces an arbitrary threshold `ã = max(a, 0.05)` without justification. Furthermore, the practice of resizing the crop to a fixed size while "disregarding aspect ratio" is a major experimental confounder. It fundamentally alters the object's appearance, and the evaluation relies heavily on the classifier's (unevaluated) invariance to such severe distortions.

3.  **Experimental Deficiencies:**
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The experimental comparison omits a crucial class of saliency methods: fast, gradient-based techniques like Vanilla Gradients [11], Guided Backpropagation [12], or Grad-CAM. While the authors dismiss these as producing "explanations of limited quality," this claim should be substantiated with quantitative comparisons (e.g., using their localization protocol or new metric). Without this comparison, the trade-off between speed and quality is not properly contextualized.
    *   **Incomplete CIFAR-10 Experiment:** The CIFAR-10 experiment is presented as evidence of the method's versatility. However, it is purely qualitative (referencing the missing Figure 6) and lacks any quantitative results. This section feels like an afterthought and adds little concrete evidence.
    *   **Reproducibility:** Key training details are missing, such as the optimizer used, learning rate, and the number of epochs for the ImageNet experiments. The rationale for training on a 250,000-image subset of ImageNet is also not provided.

**Decision**

I recommend **Rejection**.

While the central idea of training a predictive model for saliency is strong and the performance numbers are promising, the paper is undermined by a pervasive lack of rigor. The numerous flaws—ranging from unacceptable presentation quality and formatting errors to unsubstantiated methodological choices and incomplete experiments—are too substantial to overlook. The work feels preliminary and rushed. For this paper to be acceptable at NIPS, it would require a major revision to address the misleading "black-box" claim, properly justify its architectural and objective function choices, conduct a more thorough experimental comparison including relevant baselines, and adhere to basic standards of academic presentation and reproducibility.
