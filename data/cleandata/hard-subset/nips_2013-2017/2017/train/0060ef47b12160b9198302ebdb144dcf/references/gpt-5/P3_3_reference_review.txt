Summary
The paper proposes a trainable, fast saliency generator for image classifiers. Instead of per-image iterative optimization (e.g., meaningful perturbations), the authors learn a U-Net-like masking network that, given an image and a class selector, predicts a soft mask in a single forward pass. The training objective combines: (i) total variation to encourage smoothness, (ii) area minimization, (iii) “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) by maximizing the target class confidence on the preserved region, and (iv) “smallest destroying region” (SDR) by minimizing target class confidence on the removed region. To mitigate adversarial artifacts and introduced evidence, masks are applied by blending with an “alternative image” A that is randomly chosen as a strong blur of X or a random color plus noise; the mask is also encouraged to be smooth and low-resolution upsampled. A class-conditional “feature filter” gates encoder features using learned class embeddings. The method is trained with fixed black-box classifier weights and uses “fake label” instances to force dependence on the class selector. The authors evaluate on ImageNet using AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 as black boxes for weakly supervised localization and introduce a new saliency metric s(a,p)=log(ã)−log(p) (described as log(ã)−log(p), though the equation in the text is ambiguous). They report superior localization and saliency metric scores vs. backprop-based baselines and iterative optimization, and show speed suitable for real-time. A CIFAR-10 variant trained from scratch further supports generality.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: combines SSR and SDR within a single objective, addressing limitations of purely gradient-based or purely iterative perturbation methods.
- Practical contributions: U-Net architecture with a simple class-gating “feature filter”; training tricks (random A, fake labels) that reduce artifacts and force class-conditional behavior.
- Speed: single forward-pass saliency with reported throughput >100 images/s, enabling real-time use.
- Empirical results: competitive weakly supervised localization on ImageNet (close to fully supervised VGG) and favorable saliency metric vs. baselines; convincing qualitative masks that are sharper than iterative perturbation outputs.
- Attention to pitfalls: thoughtful discussion of introduced evidence and adversarial artifacts; TV regularization and randomized A are sensible choices.

Weaknesses
- Black-box/model-agnostic claims are overstated. Training requires differentiability and backpropagation through the classifier to obtain gradients w.r.t. the masked image. This excludes true black-box (API-only, non-differentiable) models. Please temper the claim and clarify the requirement (“works for any differentiable classifier with accessible gradients”).
- New metric needs clearer justification and specification. The equation s(a,p)=log(ã)−log(p) is described inconsistently (paper shows “log(ã) log(p)”). Please fix the sign, explain the choice of the 0.05 area floor, and analyze sensitivity. The metric penalizes spatially disjoint but truly minimal evidence (since the enclosing rectangle becomes large), which could bias against more faithful explanations; discuss this limitation.
- Missing or incomplete baselines. Important contemporary methods such as Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ (Selvaraju et al., 2017), SmoothGrad (Smilkov et al., 2017), and Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017) are not compared or not cited. Even if some were concurrent, a discussion is needed, and where feasible, quantitative comparisons should be added.
- Ablation and sensitivity analysis are limited. The objective combines four terms with fixed weights; there is no analysis of how performance varies with λ1–λ4, the role of TV vs. low-res upsampling, the effect of the random A choices, or the threshold 0.5 used to binarize masks. The “feature filter” and class embedding are introduced without ablations against simpler heads.
- Reproducibility details are sparse. Optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, training time/hardware, data augmentation, and exact evaluation splits are not specified. Speed claims lack hardware details and precise throughput numbers for different resolutions and models.
- Use of a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder in the masking network may inject dataset priors, complicating the “agnostic” narrative. The CIFAR-10-from-scratch experiment helps, but a clearer discussion is warranted.
- Evaluation scope. Localization is reported with a single 0.5 threshold over the saliency map and an IoU>0.5 criterion; no per-class breakdowns or multi-instance analysis are given. The “Center” baseline performing strongly indicates dataset bias; additional tests (e.g., off-center subsets) would strengthen claims.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the objective is coherent, and the training scheme is plausible. However, the paper would benefit from clearer definitions (metric), stronger baselines, and ablations. Empirical support for the real-time claim and robustness to different A choices is limited.

Clarity
Generally well written and well motivated. Key issues to fix:
- Clarify the saliency metric equation and interpretation.
- Clarify the precise meaning of “black-box”/“model-agnostic.”
- Add missing training/evaluation details for reproducibility.
- Equation (4): the notation “+ 4fc(…) 4” is confusing; is there an exponent or a weighting coefficient? Please correct.

Originality
The idea of learning a feed-forward saliency predictor supervised by perturbation-based objectives is a useful and novel synthesis relative to prior iterative approaches (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi) and gradient-based methods. The combination of SSR and SDR in a single loss and the “feature filter” are reasonable contributions. That said, related methods like CAM/Grad-CAM should be discussed more thoroughly.

Significance
Potentially impactful: a fast, trainable explanation module is valuable for interactive and video settings. If the method can be robustly reproduced and shown to outperform strong baselines, it could influence how saliency is used in practice.

Questions for authors
1) Clarify the differentiability requirement and access to classifier gradients; can your method work with an API-only black box?
2) Correct and justify the saliency metric formula; provide sensitivity studies (area floor, resizing with aspect ratio).
3) Provide ablations on λ1–λ4, the A choices, TV vs. low-res upsampling, feature filter, and the 0.5 threshold.
4) Add comparisons with Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++/SmoothGrad/Integrated Gradients.
5) Report precise speed numbers with hardware specs and include timing vs. iterative methods.
6) Provide full training details (optimizer, LR, batch size, epochs, augmentations) and release code if possible.

Pros
- Fast, single-pass saliency generation
- Joint SSR/SDR objective and artifact-aware masking
- Strong qualitative results and competitive localization
- Sensible training tricks (fake labels, randomized A)

Cons
- Overstated black-box claim; needs gradients
- Metric ambiguity and limited ablations
- Missing strong baselines (Grad-CAM family, Integrated Gradients)
- Reproducibility details missing

Overall recommendation: Weak accept (if metric is clarified and baselines/ablations are strengthened); otherwise borderline. Confidence: High.
