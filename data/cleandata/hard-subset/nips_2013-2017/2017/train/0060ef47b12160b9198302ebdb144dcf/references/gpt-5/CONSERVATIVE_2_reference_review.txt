Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a learned, feed-forward masking network that predicts saliency maps for a specified class and a given image classifier. The model is a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and a learned class embedding; it is trained to minimize a loss combining: (i) total variation (smoothness), (ii) area penalty (promoting compactness), (iii) sufficiency (the preserved region alone supports the class), and (iv) destruction (removing the region suppresses the class), with evidence removal implemented via blending with either strong blur or random color+noise. The authors also introduce an ad hoc saliency metric based on the tightest rectangular crop around the mask and the classifier’s confidence on the resized crop: s(a,p) = log(max(a,0.05))/log(p), arguing it reflects the “smallest sufficient region” criterion.

They evaluate on ImageNet using three black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50), training a separate masking network per classifier, and report weakly supervised object localization performance and their new metric, along with qualitative results and a small CIFAR-10 qualitative experiment. They claim orders-of-magnitude speedup over iterative perturbation-based approaches (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi 2017) and better localization.

This work is closely related to perturbation-based saliency (Fong & Vedaldi 2017) and WSOL/attention/CAM-style methods (Zhou et al. 2016; Zhang et al. Excitation Backprop; Simonyan et al. gradient saliency). The main novelty is amortizing the perturbation optimization into a trainable network and combining both sufficiency and destruction terms.

Strengths:
- Clear and practically motivated goal: producing real-time, class-conditional saliency for arbitrary inputs.
- Thoughtful treatment of “introduced evidence” via randomized alternative images and TV regularization; discussion of adversarial artifacts is pertinent.
- Unifying objective that encourages both sufficiency and destruction, rather than only one side.
- Training tricks (fake class labels to force class-conditioning) are sensible.
- Architecture and training details are described at a level that experienced readers could implement.
- Reported runtime is compelling for applications that need per-frame saliency.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- “Black-box” and “model-agnostic” claims are overstated. Training requires backpropagating through the classifier (the method assumes a differentiable model and uses its softmax and gradients), and a separate masking model must be trained per classifier. This is not black-box in the query-only sense and limits generality.
- Evaluation is limited and lacks rigor: no ablations on loss components (weights, TV strength), alternative image choices, fake-label probability, mask upsampling, or architectural choices. It is unclear which parts drive the improvements.
- The proposed metric s(a,p) is ad hoc, sensitive to classifier calibration and to resizing distortions, and biased toward methods that produce compact, contiguous masks. Its theoretical justification is weak, and there is no comparison to established insertion/deletion curves (as in Fong & Vedaldi) or robustness checks.
- The localization protocol is sensitive to thresholding the mask at 0.5 and to the “tightest box around mask” post-processing, which may unfairly favor methods like the proposed one. There is no analysis of threshold sensitivity or per-class performance, and no statistical significance or variance is reported.
- Comparisons: gradients/GuidedBP/ExcitationBP/CAM baselines are briefly mentioned, but it is not clear whether the strongest WSOL baselines were tuned under the same protocol. The Center baseline is not competitive on images without centered objects; its inclusion is informative but not sufficient. The claim of approaching fully supervised VGG is tenuous without controlled settings.
- Claims about mitigating adversarial artifacts are not validated quantitatively. The cropping-based metric aims to avoid artifacts in evaluation, but the training still uses blending masks; no tests show that the learned masks are robust under alternative perturbation models.
- Generalization is unclear: the masking model is trained on 250k ImageNet images and evaluated on ImageNet; it is unclear how it performs on out-of-distribution images, on predicted vs ground-truth classes when they disagree, or across different black-boxes without retraining.
- CIFAR-10 results are purely qualitative; no quantitative metrics or comparisons.

Quality:
The method is technically plausible and the objective is well-motivated. However, the empirical support is insufficient: core claims (superior quality, robustness to artifacts, generality) rely on limited metrics and qualitative figures. Lack of ablations and sensitivity analyses make it hard to assess soundness. The black-box assumption is not met in the standard sense.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The description of the architecture, loss, and training tricks is clear. Important experimental details are missing: exact datasets/splits, training schedules, optimizer settings, data augmentation, how thresholds were chosen, and precise baselines/tuning. The definition and properties of the new metric would benefit from deeper analysis.

Originality:
Amortizing perturbation-based saliency into a learned predictor is a natural extension but not, in my view, a substantial conceptual leap beyond Fong & Vedaldi’s meaningful perturbations and WSOL/attention literature. Combining sufficiency and destruction in one loss is a reasonable idea. The metric is novel but ad hoc.

Significance:
If validated more rigorously, a fast, learned saliency predictor could be useful in practice. As presented, the empirical evidence is not strong enough, and the method’s dependence on per-classifier training and differentiability limits broad impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide strong, standardized evaluation: insertion/deletion curves, robustness to perturbation types, threshold sensitivity, per-class stats, and confidence intervals.
- Add ablations on loss terms and architecture; quantify the effect of the randomized alternative image.
- Clarify the extent of “black-box” access and, if possible, test a variant that uses only scores (no gradients) to align with black-box claims.
- Compare against tuned CAM/Grad-CAM/ExcitationBP and iterative meaningful perturbations under identical post-processing.
- Evaluate on predicted vs ground-truth classes and analyze failure cases.
- Release code/models to aid reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a practical bottleneck (speed) with a coherent, implementable approach.
- Combines SSR and SDR objectives and proposes practical training heuristics.
- Qualitative saliency maps appear sharp and class-specific; runtime is attractive.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Overstated black-box/model-agnostic claims; requires per-classifier training and gradients.
- Limited and biased evaluation (ad hoc metric, no ablations), making it hard to trust the claimed superiority.
- Incremental novelty over existing perturbation and WSOL literature.
- Missing quantitative CIFAR-10 results and broader generalization analyses.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising, but the paper needs stronger, more rigorous evaluation and clearer positioning relative to prior work to meet NeurIPS standards.
