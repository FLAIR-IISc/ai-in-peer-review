This paper presents a novel approach to saliency detection for black-box classifiers, moving away from iterative methods towards a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea – training a network to *predict* saliency maps – is a significant departure from existing techniques and addresses a key bottleneck in applying saliency methods to real-time applications like video analysis. While the experimental validation isn't exhaustive, the results on ImageNet are promising, and the introduction of a new saliency metric is a valuable contribution.

The strengths of this work are considerable. First, the speed advantage is compelling. Iterative methods are simply too slow for many practical scenarios, and this work directly tackles that issue. Second, the paper’s framing of saliency in terms of “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) and “smallest destroying region” (SDR) is a useful conceptualization, and the corresponding saliency metric feels intuitively aligned with the goal of finding truly informative regions. Third, the authors demonstrate an awareness of potential pitfalls (introduced evidence) and attempt to mitigate them through thoughtful design choices (blurred masks, TV penalty). Finally, the successful transfer of the approach to CIFAR-10, even without pre-training, highlights the robustness of the underlying concept.

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. While the ImageNet results are good, the comparison to existing methods feels somewhat limited. The analysis largely focuses on localization accuracy, which, as the authors acknowledge, is not synonymous with saliency. A more detailed qualitative analysis of the generated saliency maps, alongside comparisons to visualisations produced by methods like Guided Backpropagation or integrated gradients, would strengthen the claim of improved “quality.” The ablation studies, while present, could be more comprehensive – for instance, demonstrating the impact of each term in the loss function more definitively. Furthermore, the discussion of biases in the masking model, though acknowledged in the conclusion, feels underdeveloped. A brief exploration of potential bias sources would add valuable nuance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The learned saliency model is a genuinely new approach, moving beyond the limitations of iterative techniques.
*   **Significance:** Real-time saliency detection opens up new avenues for interpretability in dynamic settings (video, robotics).
*   **Conceptual Clarity:** The SSR/SDR framing and the proposed saliency metric are insightful contributions.
*   **Promising Results:** The initial experimental results demonstrate the feasibility and potential of the approach.
*   **Bold Approach:** The authors tackle a challenging problem with a creative and potentially impactful solution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The comparison to existing methods could be more thorough and nuanced.
*   **Incomplete Validation:** The experimental evaluation, while solid, lacks the depth of analysis found in some other submissions.
*   **Potential Bias Concerns:** The discussion of model bias is cursory.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor flaws, this paper represents a significant step forward in the field of saliency detection. The core idea is highly novel, the potential impact is substantial, and the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the challenges involved. The paper prioritizes a novel idea and doesn't get bogged down in extensive polishing, which is commendable. I believe this work has the potential to inspire further research and development in this important area. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the minor concerns regarding comparative analysis and bias in a future version of the work. The benefits of a novel direction for solving a hard problem outweigh the drawbacks of a slightly incomplete validation.
