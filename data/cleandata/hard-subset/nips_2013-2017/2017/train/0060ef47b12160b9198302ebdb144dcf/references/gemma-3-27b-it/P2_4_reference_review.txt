## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers. Instead of iterative methods which are computationally expensive, the authors propose a trainable model (a U-Net architecture) that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the "Smallest Sufficient Region" (SSR) concept, and demonstrate that their method achieves faster performance, higher quality saliency masks, and better localization results compared to existing techniques, including recent optimization-based methods.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper effectively positions itself within the landscape of image saliency detection methods. It acknowledges gradient-based approaches (Simonyan et al., 2014), iterative removal methods (Zhou et al., 2016; Cao et al., 2015), and optimization-based techniques (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017).  The key contribution lies in moving *away* from iterative and optimization-based approaches (which are slow) and *towards* a learned, feed-forward model for real-time saliency. The authors appropriately situate their work in relation to the concurrent Fong & Vedaldi (2017) paper, highlighting the benefits of their model-based approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Speed:** The most significant strength is the proposed method's speed. Achieving real-time saliency detection is a valuable contribution, opening the door to applications like video-saliency.
* **Novelty:** The concept of learning a saliency map predictor is novel and a clear departure from existing iterative and optimization methods.
* **Strong Experimental Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons on ImageNet with multiple black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and a demonstration on CIFAR-10.
* **New Saliency Metric:** The introduced saliency metric, grounded in the SSR concept, offers a more nuanced evaluation than simple localization accuracy. The theoretical justification connecting it to information theory is also commendable.
* **Addressing Introduced Evidence:** The paper thoughtfully addresses the problem of adversarial artifacts introduced during evidence removal, proposing techniques to mitigate this issue.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The explanations of concepts like SSR and SDR are clear and accompanied by helpful figures.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Training:** The training process appears complex, requiring careful tuning of multiple hyperparameters and the use of “fake labels” and random evidence removal schemes. While effective, this complexity isn’t fully explored in terms of its sensitivity and robustness.  The rationale behind the 30% fake label probability isn't fully explained.
* **U-Net Architecture Choice:** While the U-Net is a reasonable choice, the justification for using *specifically* a U-Net isn’t thoroughly discussed.  Are there other architectures that could be equally or more effective?
* **Dependence on Black-Box Classifier:** The performance is inherently tied to the quality of the black-box classifier. The paper acknowledges this, but a more in-depth analysis of how the saliency map quality varies with *different* black-box architectures and their inherent biases would be valuable. The CIFAR-10 experiment mitigates this somewhat, but more diverse classifiers would be beneficial.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the paper presents thorough experimental results, there’s a lack of targeted ablation studies. For instance, how much does the pre-trained ResNet encoder contribute to performance? What is the impact of each term in the objective function (equation 4)?

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Technically sound. The method appears well-reasoned, and the experimental results support the claims. However, the training complexity raises some concerns about reproducibility and robustness.
* **Clarity:**  Generally excellent. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. However, some sections (e.g., the detailed training process) could benefit from further simplification.
* **Originality:** Highly original. The approach of learning a saliency map predictor is a novel contribution.
* **Significance:** Significant. Real-time saliency detection has the potential to impact various applications, including explainable AI, visual debugging, and human-computer interaction.  The new saliency metric is also a valuable contribution.



**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses an important problem (explainability of black-box classifiers).
* Presents a novel and technically sound solution.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on standard benchmarks.
* Introduces a useful new saliency metric.
* Achieves real-time performance.

**Con:**

* Training process is complex and requires significant tuning.
* Limited ablation studies.
* Dependence on the quality of the black-box classifier.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. The originality, technical soundness, and experimental validation are compelling.  While the training process is complex and some ablation studies are missing, these are minor weaknesses compared to the significant contributions of the work. The real-time performance and the novel approach to saliency detection are particularly noteworthy. The paper offers a valuable tool for understanding and debugging black-box classifiers and has the potential to advance the field of explainable AI.

**Confidence:** 8/10.
