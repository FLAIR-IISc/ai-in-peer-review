Summary
The paper proposes to amortize iterative saliency-map optimization into a single feed-forward “masking model” trained to produce a per-class spatial mask in real time for a given image classifier. The objective combines: (i) a TV smoothness penalty, (ii) an area penalty (SSR pressure), (iii) a term that preserves class score when masking-in salient regions (SSR), and (iv) a term that suppresses the class score when removing salient regions (SDR). To mitigate adversarial artifacts from masking, the method randomizes the replacement image (blurred original or random color+noise) and regularizes for smoothness. The architecture is a U-Net with a frozen, ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-50 encoder and a learned class embedding used to gate deep features. The approach is evaluated on ImageNet weakly supervised localization and via a new metric s(a,p)=log(ã)·log(p) based on area and retained class probability. Qualitatively and quantitatively, the authors report better localization error and saliency metric than selected baselines. A small CIFAR-10 qualitative demonstration is also provided.

Strengths
- Practical idea: amortizing iterative meaningful-perturbation-style saliency into a single pass is attractive for real-time settings and video.
- Combining SSR and SDR objectives in a single loss is reasonable and likely to yield sharper, more faithful masks than enforcing only deletion or only preservation.
- Architectural details and training tricks (class gating, fake labels to enforce conditioning, randomized replacement) are clearly described and likely useful to practitioners.
- Weakly supervised localization results are competitive given no bounding-box supervision, and speed claims are plausible.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “Black box” and “model agnostic” claims are overstated. Training requires gradients through the classifier (at minimum, w.r.t. input) and a differentiable model. This is not black-box in the standard sense (no access to gradients). The title and framing are misleading.
- Baseline coverage is incomplete. Important contemporaneous methods are missing from comparisons/citations: CAM/Grad-CAM, Guided Grad-CAM, Integrated Gradients, SmoothGrad. Runtime and quality comparisons to these strong, single/backward-pass methods are essential, especially given the “real-time” claim.
- New metric is ad hoc and insufficiently validated. The product of logs lacks clear theoretical grounding, its behavior with respect to p≈1 and small a is non-intuitive, and the 0.05 area floor is heuristic. No sensitivity analysis, human studies, or correlation with established metrics (e.g., deletion/insertion curves) are provided.
- Evaluation protocol favors the method. Localization uses ground-truth class as input to the explainer; explanations should also be reported for the model’s predicted class. No statistics (variance/confidence intervals), no per-class breakdowns, and no robustness checks to replacement strategy A are given.
- Potential leakage/bias: the masking network uses a fixed, ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-50 encoder. For black-boxes that are not ResNet-50 (AlexNet, GoogLeNet), this encoder may steer masks toward its own biases rather than faithfully reflecting the target model. No ablation with a randomly initialized encoder (on ImageNet) is provided, and the CIFAR-10 experiment is only qualitative.
- Insufficient ablations. There is no analysis of the effect of the four loss terms and their weights, the TV term, or the randomized replacement distribution on metrics, nor evidence that these design choices reduce adversarial artifacts beyond intuition.
- Clarity/notation issues: the fourth term in Eq. 4 appears as “+ 3 fc(φ(X,1−M)) 4”; the exponent/weighting is ambiguous. The metric’s interpretation is loosely described and could be incorrect or easily gamed by resizing/cropping distortions.

Quality
The methodology is plausible and builds on established ideas (perturbation-based saliency, total variation regularization, U-Net). However, several central claims are weakly supported: the “model-agnostic/black-box” framing, the superiority over strong baselines, and the validity of the new metric. Experimental design lacks key comparisons, statistical rigor, and ablations that would make the case compelling. The CIFAR-10 results are qualitative only.

Clarity
Generally readable and well-organized. Architectural/training details are described, though important hyperparameters (optimizer, LR schedule, batch size) are missing for reproducibility. Some notation is ambiguous (Eq. 4, saliency metric behavior). Figures and tables (not visible here) are heavily relied upon; quantitative results should be stated more explicitly in text.

Originality
Moderate. The core idea is an amortized predictor for perturbation-based masks with SSR+SDR terms. This is a natural extension of prior iterative methods, and the architectural choices (U-Net, class gating) are conventional. The new metric is novel but insufficiently justified.

Significance
Potentially useful if validated: real-time saliency maps enabling video explanations could be impactful. However, without strong comparisons to established and faster methods (Grad-CAM variants, Integrated Gradients) and without rigorous evaluation of faithfulness (e.g., deletion/insertion, Pointing Game), the contribution’s practical significance is uncertain.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the “black-box/model-agnostic” claim and scope: require differentiability; discuss applicability to non-differentiable or API-only settings.
- Add strong baselines: CAM/Grad-CAM/Guided Grad-CAM, Integrated Gradients, SmoothGrad; compare both quality (localization, saliency metrics) and runtime on the same hardware.
- Evaluate explanations for the model’s predicted class as well as ground truth.
- Provide deletion/insertion curve evaluations and pointing game scores; report mean±std over multiple random replacement strategies A.
- Ablate: each loss term and weight; TV strength; replacement distribution; with/without fake labels; with randomly initialized vs pretrained encoders; with encoders matched/mismatched to the black-box.
- Validate the new metric: analyze sensitivity to a and p, alternatives (e.g., −log p per area), correlation with human judgments and other faithfulness metrics; justify the 0.05 area floor.
- Disambiguate Eq. 4 and provide full training details; release code.

Pros
- Practical idea to amortize iterative saliency into one pass with plausible speedups.
- Thoughtful training tricks to reduce artifacts and enforce conditioning.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization without box labels.

Cons
- Misleading “black-box” positioning; requires gradients and a differentiable model.
- Missing key baselines and runtime comparisons to established methods.
- Ad hoc metric with limited validation.
- Limited ablations and statistical rigor; potential bias from pretrained encoder.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
While the idea is promising and could be impactful with rigorous validation, the current empirical evidence and positioning fall short of NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the evaluation, clarifying claims, and adding comprehensive comparisons and ablations could make a strong resubmission. Confidence: medium.
