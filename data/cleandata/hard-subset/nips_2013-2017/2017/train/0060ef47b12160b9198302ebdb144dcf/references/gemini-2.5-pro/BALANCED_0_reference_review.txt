This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. The central idea is to train a separate, feed-forward neural network—termed a "masking model"—to predict a saliency mask for a given image and target class in a single pass. This approach contrasts with two dominant paradigms in the field: 1) fast but often low-quality gradient-based methods, and 2) high-quality but computationally expensive iterative perturbation/optimization-based methods. By learning to predict the outcome of an optimization process, the proposed method aims to achieve the best of both worlds: high-quality masks at real-time speeds. The authors formulate a comprehensive objective function for training this masking model, which encourages masks that are smooth, small, and simultaneously preserve classifier confidence on the salient region (a Smallest Sufficient Region) while destroying it when the region is removed (a Smallest Destroying Region). Additionally, the paper introduces a new metric for evaluating saliency based on the trade-off between the area of a cropped salient region and the classifier's confidence. The method is evaluated on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, where it achieves state-of-the-art results, and is also shown to produce compelling qualitative results on CIFAR-10.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—amortizing the expensive iterative search for a saliency map by training a predictive model—is a highly novel and significant idea. This reframing of the problem from optimization to prediction for each new instance is powerful and opens up new applications for high-quality saliency methods, such as real-time video analysis, which were previously intractable. This represents a conceptual leap over prior work like Fong & Vedaldi [2], which focuses solely on the iterative optimization itself.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness. The reported results on the ImageNet weakly supervised object localization task are excellent, outperforming previous methods by a significant margin and approaching the performance of some fully supervised techniques. This demonstrates that the model is not just fast but also highly effective at identifying semantically relevant regions as judged by the black-box classifier.

3.  **Thoroughness of the Approach:** The authors have clearly thought deeply about the problem. The design of the objective function (Eq. 4), which balances four distinct and desirable properties of a saliency map (smoothness, smallness, sufficiency, and destructiveness), is well-motivated. Furthermore, the introduction of a new saliency metric, while requiring more justification (see weaknesses), shows an effort to move beyond localization-as-proxy and evaluate saliency on its own terms. The experiments across multiple black-box architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) further strengthen the claims.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly, the proposed method is described in sufficient detail, and the figures (especially Fig. 5) effectively illustrate the qualitative advantages of the proposed method over alternatives.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Justification for the New Saliency Metric:** The new metric, `s(a, p) = log(ã) / log(p)`, is introduced with a brief information-theoretic intuition but lacks a rigorous justification. It is not clear why this specific log-log form is preferable to other simpler formulations that also trade off area (`a`) and probability (`p`), such as `p/a` or `p * (1-a)`. The behavior of this metric could be non-intuitive; for example, its sensitivity to changes in `p` is highly dependent on the value of `p` itself. A more detailed analysis or ablation study justifying this choice would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution in this area.

2.  **Potential for Adversarial Explanations:** The masking model is trained to optimize an objective that directly queries the black-box classifier. This setup creates a risk that the masking model could learn to generate subtle, high-frequency patterns within the mask's boundaries that are not salient to a human but adversarially exploit the black-box model to achieve a high score on the objective. The authors acknowledge this risk and employ smoothness priors (TV penalty, upsampling) to mitigate it, but the analysis does not go far enough. It is difficult to be certain that the generated masks represent a "genuine" explanation rather than a clever hack of the objective function.

3.  **Lack of Generality:** The current framework requires training a separate, dedicated masking model for *each* black-box classifier to be explained. This is a practical limitation. While training is a one-time cost, it is still substantial. A more significant contribution would be a single, universal masking model that could generate saliency maps for an arbitrary black-box model, perhaps by being conditioned on the model's architecture or weights. The paper should more explicitly acknowledge this limitation and discuss it as a key direction for future work.

4.  **Comparison to Iterative Methods:** In Table 2, the baseline "iteratively optimised" method performs surprisingly poorly, even worse than a simple central box. Given the strong results reported in papers like Fong & Vedaldi [2] (which this paper cites as parallel work), this baseline seems weak. The authors should clarify exactly which method was implemented for this baseline and ensure it was tuned fairly, as the large performance gap may overstate the proposed method's relative advantage.

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Highly novel and significant core idea of learning a predictive model for saliency.
    *   Opens up real-time applications for high-quality saliency.
    *   State-of-the-art empirical results on a challenging benchmark.
    *   The paper is well-written and the approach is well-designed.

*   **Con:**
    *   The newly proposed metric lacks sufficient justification.
    *   The risk of the masking model learning "adversarial" rather than "genuine" explanations is not fully addressed.
    *   The method requires training a new model for every black box, limiting its practical generality.
    *   The experimental comparison against iterative methods may not be completely fair.

**Decision and Suggestions for Improvement:**

I lean towards accepting this paper. The central idea of amortizing the search for saliency maps is a strong and impactful contribution that is likely to inspire follow-up work. The impressive empirical results provide solid support for the method's promise. The weaknesses, while notable, do not undermine the core contribution and can be addressed in future work.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:
1.  **Bolster the Metric's Justification:** In Section 3.2, either provide a more formal derivation for the chosen metric or, more pragmatically, include a short analysis (perhaps in the appendix) showing its properties and comparing its behavior to simpler alternatives like `p/a` to demonstrate its advantages.
2.  **Test for Adversarial Robustness:** To address the concern about "adversarial" explanations, I recommend a simple experiment: take the cropped regions identified by your method, apply minor, non-semantic transformations (e.g., small translation, rotation, JPEG compression), and measure if the classifier's confidence is robust. A sharp drop in confidence would suggest the explanation might be brittle and exploiting artifacts.
3.  **Clarify Baselines and Limitations:** Please provide more details on the implementation of the "iteratively optimised" baseline in Table 2. Also, explicitly discussing the limitation of needing one masking model per black-box classifier in the conclusion would make for a more balanced paper.
