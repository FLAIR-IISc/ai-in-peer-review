This paper proposes a learned, model-agnostic saliency detection method, aiming for speed and accuracy improvements over iterative approaches. The core idea – training a network to directly predict saliency maps – is conceptually sound and addresses a practical bottleneck in interpretability research. However, the execution, while presenting promising results, is riddled with inconsistencies, questionable design choices, and a concerning level of detail-oriented flaws that collectively undermine confidence in the presented work.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Novelty (moderate):** The direct prediction of saliency maps via a trained network is a reasonable and potentially valuable direction.
*   **Potential Significance (moderate):** Real-time saliency detection could unlock applications in video analysis and dynamic systems.
*   **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates improvements over some baselines on ImageNet localisation and a new saliency metric.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Methodological Inconsistencies:** The introduced “evidence removal” problem and proposed solutions (Eq. 2, A selection) feel unnecessarily complex and lack strong justification. The rationale for choosing specific alternatives for ‘A’ is vague. The constant shifting of strategies to avoid artifacts (blur, noise, TV penalty) suggests a fundamental instability in the approach rather than a well-defined solution.
*   **Metric Validity:** The newly proposed saliency metric (Eq. 3), while conceptually interesting, relies on resizing crops disregarding aspect ratio – a potentially significant distortion that invalidates its comparison to ground truth bounding boxes and introduces a bias towards square objects. The argument that it's suitable for classifiers "largely invariant to scale and aspect ratio" is a weak defense given the diversity of classifiers it claims to be agnostic to.
*   **Objective Function Imbalance:** The weighting of terms in the objective function (Eq. 4) is arbitrary and lacks justification. The interplay between TV penalty, area penalty, and classifier confidence terms isn't clearly explained or analyzed. The seemingly arbitrary value of 4 is concerning.
*   **Implementation Details & Reproducibility:** The random selection of A during training and the lack of details regarding its distribution raises concerns about reproducibility. The authors claim the masking model "acts as a regulariser" but provide no evidence of this.
*   **Clarity & Organisation:** The paper suffers from an overly verbose style and a lack of conciseness. The introduction meanders, and the connections between sections are not always clear. There are multiple instances of informal language and unnecessarily complex phrasing.
*   **Minor Errors & Typos:** Numerous grammatical errors and typos throughout the manuscript (e.g., “st Conference”, “Interpretable Explanations...maps.”, "CIFAR-10 dataset [4]" when [4] is a much older publication) detract from the paper’s credibility.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** No ablation studies were performed to justify the use of each component of the objective function.

**Detailed Critique:**

*   **Section 3.1:** The discussion of "introduced evidence" feels like a lengthy justification for a self-imposed problem. The authors spend considerable effort addressing artifacts created by their own manipulation of the input image, diverting attention from the core saliency detection task.
*   **Section 3.2:** The saliency metric’s reliance on forced resizing is a major flaw. This compromises the comparability of results and introduces a systematic bias.
*   **Section 4.1:** The rationale for introducing fake labels during training is weakly explained and seems ad-hoc. The claim of a meaningful learned embedding needs visual corroboration beyond a vague mention in the appendix.
*   **Section 5:** The experimental results are presented without a critical discussion of limitations. The comparison to baselines is selective, and the authors fail to acknowledge potential confounding factors. The claim of outperforming existing methods on weakly supervised localization is made despite the metric's flaws.
*   **Figure Captions:** Several figures lack informative captions, leaving the reader to guess their purpose.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   Significant methodological inconsistencies and questionable design choices.
*   Flawed saliency metric that compromises the validity of the results.
*   Lack of reproducibility due to unspecified implementation details.
*   Poor writing quality and numerous errors.
*   Lack of ablation studies.

**For:**

*   Novel approach to real-time saliency detection.
*   Demonstrated improvements over some baselines.
*   Potential for broader impact in interpretability research.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite a conceptually interesting idea, this paper falls short of NIPS standards due to its numerous flaws in methodology, execution, and presentation. The paper feels incomplete and prematurely submitted. The authors need to address the concerns raised above, particularly regarding the saliency metric, the evidence removal strategies, and the objective function weighting. The writing quality must be significantly improved. 

**Recommendation:** **Reject.** I believe that the substantial revisions required to address these concerns would constitute a significantly different paper. The flaws are too numerous and fundamental to justify acceptance in its current form.
