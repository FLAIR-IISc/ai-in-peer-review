

# Peer Review: "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection for black box classifiers that significantly improves upon existing iterative methods. The authors address a critical problem in AI interpretability: understanding what parts of an image a classifier is focusing on to make predictions. Current state-of-the-art methods rely on iterative processes (e.g., progressively removing image patches) that require hundreds of iterations per image, making them unsuitable for real-time applications. The authors propose training a masking model (based on a U-Net architecture) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) concept and address key challenges like adversarial artifacts through mask smoothness and random evidence removal techniques. The method is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets, demonstrating superior speed (100+ saliency maps per second) and performance compared to existing approaches. This work extends previous NIPS contributions on saliency methods (e.g., Grad-CAM, Guided Backpropagation, and recent iterative methods like [1, 2]) by providing a model-based approach that is both faster and produces higher quality saliency maps.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Contribution**: The paper addresses a genuine need for real-time saliency detection, which has been a bottleneck in applications like video analysis and autonomous systems. The demonstrated speed (100+ images per second) is orders of magnitude faster than iterative methods, making it suitable for real-world applications.

2. **Novel Metric and Evaluation Framework**: The proposed saliency metric (based on SSR) is well-motivated and directly evaluates the interpretability of saliency maps in terms of what the classifier actually uses for decision-making. This is a meaningful improvement over standard localisation accuracy metrics that don't capture the "sufficient region" concept.

3. **Thorough Experimental Validation**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments across multiple datasets (ImageNet and CIFAR-10) and classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50). The results consistently show that their method outperforms existing techniques in both speed and quality, with localisation error rates (36.7% for ResNet) approaching fully supervised methods (34.3%).

4. **Addresses Critical Challenges**: The paper thoughtfully tackles important issues like adversarial artifacts in saliency maps through their evidence removal scheme (equation 2) and mask smoothness constraints (TV penalty). This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem space.

5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations of both the problem and their solution. The architecture diagram (Figure 4) and experimental results (Figures 5-6) are particularly effective in conveying the method's strengths.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Comparison with Parallel Work**: The paper mentions that Fong and Vedaldi [2] proposed a similar method developed in parallel, but doesn't provide a detailed comparison. This is a significant omission since [2] is a recent NIPS paper (2017) that directly addresses the same problem. The authors should clarify the key differences between their approach and [2], particularly regarding the SSR vs. SDR objective and the model-based vs. iterative nature of the methods.

2. **Lack of Analysis on Classifier Errors**: The paper doesn't explore how their method performs when the black box classifier makes incorrect predictions. If a classifier misclassifies an image, does the saliency map correctly highlight the features that led to the error? This is a critical aspect of interpretability that should be addressed.

3. **Limited Discussion of Model Biases**: While the paper mentions in the conclusion that the masking model might have its own biases, it doesn't explore this thoroughly. Given that the masking model is trained on image-class pairs, it could potentially learn to highlight spurious correlations rather than true salient features. This limitation should be addressed.

4. **Implementation Details for Reproducibility**: The paper lacks sufficient detail about the training process and hyperparameters to allow for full reproducibility. For instance, the exact configuration of the U-Net architecture (number of channels, layer configurations) isn't fully specified, and the random evidence removal scheme needs more detail.

5. **Potential Limitation for Non-CNN Classifiers**: While the paper claims model-agnosticity, the masking model architecture (U-Net with ResNet backbone) is specifically designed for CNN-based classifiers. The paper doesn't address whether their approach would work for non-CNN black box classifiers (e.g., random forests or SVMs on hand-crafted features).

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of AI interpretability with a novel, practical approach to real-time saliency detection. The method is both theoretically sound and practically valuable, with clear demonstrations of superiority over existing techniques. The new saliency metric is particularly noteworthy for better aligning with the interpretability goals of saliency methods.

While the paper has some weaknesses regarding comparison with parallel work and limited analysis of classifier errors, these are not fundamental flaws in the approach. The strengths of the paper—particularly its practical impact, novel metric, and strong experimental results—significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The authors have clearly made a meaningful contribution to the state of the art in model interpretability.

I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and would interest both researchers and practitioners working on AI interpretability. The work has clear potential for real-world impact in applications requiring real-time model understanding, such as autonomous systems and medical imaging.

## Final Comments

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in AI interpretability. The authors have made a significant contribution by developing a method that is both faster and produces higher quality saliency maps than existing approaches. With minor revisions to address the comparison with [2] and provide more detail on the limitations (particularly regarding classifier errors), this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
