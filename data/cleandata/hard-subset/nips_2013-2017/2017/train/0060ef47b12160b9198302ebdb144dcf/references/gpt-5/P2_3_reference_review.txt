Summary
The paper proposes a fast, model-driven approach to image saliency for black-box classifiers. Instead of optimizing a saliency mask per image via iterative perturbations, the authors train a masking network (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and class-embedding “feature filter”) to predict saliency masks in a single forward pass. The training objective is designed to jointly encourage (i) small, smooth masks; (ii) sufficiency (the preserved region alone yields high class probability; akin to SSR); and (iii) destructiveness (removing the region suppresses the class; akin to SDR). To mitigate “introduced evidence,” the method randomizes the alternative image A (heavy blur vs random color+noise) and enforces smoothness/low-frequency masks via TV regularization and up/downsampling. The authors also introduce a saliency metric based on the log-area of the tightest crop containing the salient region and the classifier’s confidence on that crop, and evaluate both weakly supervised localization accuracy on ImageNet and the proposed metric. They report sharp, interpretable maps, real-time throughput (~100 images/s), and improved localization and saliency scores versus gradient-based/iterative baselines; they also show a CIFAR-10 variant trained from scratch.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned against backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), CAM-style localization (Zhou et al.), and perturbation/optimization methods (Cao et al.; Fong & Vedaldi, concurrent). The main differences: learning a general-purpose masking model that amortizes optimization; combining SSR and SDR in the training objective; and a new metric aimed at interpretability via sufficiency under cropping. This is close in spirit to Meaningful Perturbations (Fong & Vedaldi) but trades per-image optimization for training a predictor; it also shares goals with CAM/Grad-CAM-style methods, though it directly optimizes in image space via masking and works with a fixed downstream classifier.

Strengths
- Practical contribution: a saliency generator with single-pass inference and real-time speed, which is valuable for video and interactive analysis.
- Well-motivated objective: explicitly balances sufficiency and destructiveness, while addressing introduced evidence and adversarial artifacts through randomized A and TV regularization.
- Architecture/design: the U-Net with class-conditioned feature filtering is sensible; the training trick with “fake labels” to force attention to the class selector is thoughtful.
- Empirical signals: better weakly-supervised localization error than several baselines and similar performance to fully supervised VGG; much improved values on the proposed saliency metric; generalization to CIFAR-10 without a pretrained encoder.
- Clarity of the “introduced evidence” discussion and mitigation strategies is good and practically relevant.

Weaknesses
- Claims vs assumptions: the method is described as “model agnostic” and “black box,” but training requires differentiating through the classifier to optimize the masking network. This is only applicable to accessible, differentiable models with usable gradients. Please clarify the exact assumptions and contrast to truly score-only black-box settings.
- Evaluation breadth: localization is a reasonable proxy but not saliency per se; results rely on thresholding masks at 0.5 and bounding boxes, which are sensitive to choices. The proposed metric is interesting but bespoke and depends on classifier invariance to scale/aspect ratio; stronger justification or correlation with human assessments would help.
- Missing/weak baselines: At minimum, CAM/Grad-CAM should be included (Grad-CAM is a highly relevant 2016 method). Comparisons to Meaningful Perturbations (with comparable compute budget), and to simple “amortized” baselines (e.g., training a network to imitate Grad-CAM) would make the case stronger. Quantitative ablations of the objective (drop SSR or SDR terms, constant vs randomized A, TV strength) are missing.
- Technical clarity: There are typos and possible sign ambiguities in Eq. 4 (the “third term makes sure the classifier recognizes the selected class,” which should suggest a negative log-likelihood term, but the equation shows +log(fc(·))). Eq. 3’s definition of s(a,p) (“=” vs “−”) is unclear from the text. Some references are truncated. These need correction for reproducibility.
- Scope of “real-time”: While inference is fast, the training cost and sensitivity to the chosen encoder/backbone are not discussed; transferability of a trained masking model across different classifiers (e.g., trained on GoogLeNet but applied to ResNet) is not studied.
- Robustness to adversarial artifacts: The paper argues for defenses (TV, resizing, randomized A), but does not quantify artifact prevalence or show stress tests (e.g., high-frequency backgrounds, texture bias cases).

Quality
Technically plausible and largely sound, with a coherent objective, reasonable architectural choices, and practical training heuristics. However, there are issues with equation correctness/typos and incomplete ablations. The evaluation supports the headline claims, but stronger and more comprehensive baselines are needed. The new metric is sensible but requires validation.

Clarity
Generally readable and well-organized, but marred by equation typos/ambiguities and truncated citations. Important implementation details are provided (loss weights, architecture, training tricks), aiding reproducibility, though ablation details and exact experimental protocols (e.g., thresholding choices, N, variance) could be expanded.

Originality
Moderate to good. The idea of amortizing perturbation-based saliency into a learned predictor and combining SSR/SDR in a single objective is novel relative to contemporaneous work. The saliency metric is a nice, if simple, addition. The architectural components are standard.

Significance
Potentially significant for practical interpretability tooling: saliency at real-time speed with improved sharpness is useful. The weakly supervised localization improvements are encouraging. That said, without stronger baselines and broader evaluation, the impact is somewhat limited.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Clarify Eq. 4 signs and Eq. 3’s exact form; fix typos. Provide complete references (e.g., include Grad-CAM).
- Explicitly state the assumption that gradients through the classifier are available during training; discuss applicability to non-differentiable or proprietary APIs.
- Add baselines: Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++, CAM; Meaningful Perturbations with matched compute; possibly RISE-style randomized masking (if contemporaneous).
- Provide ablations on λs, A choices, TV strength, and the “fake label” probability; show how each term affects sharpness and the two objectives (SSR/SDR).
- Validate the proposed saliency metric: correlation with human judgments; sensitivity to aspect ratio/scale; per-class analysis.
- Test transfer: train on one black-box, evaluate on another; and test robustness under distribution shift or adversarially textured backgrounds.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, fast, and simple approach; good idea to amortize saliency optimization.
- Clear improvements on localization and the proposed metric; sharp, interpretable maps.
- Thoughtful handling of introduced evidence and adversarial artifacts.

Arguments against acceptance
- Ambiguity in equations; incomplete baselines (notably Grad-CAM, MP).
- Custom metric not yet validated; limited evaluation breadth and ablations.
- “Model-agnostic black-box” claim is overstated given gradient requirements.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept (leaning weak accept). The core idea is useful and timely, and the method appears to work well; if the authors can fix clarity issues and strengthen comparisons/ablations, this would be a solid contribution.
