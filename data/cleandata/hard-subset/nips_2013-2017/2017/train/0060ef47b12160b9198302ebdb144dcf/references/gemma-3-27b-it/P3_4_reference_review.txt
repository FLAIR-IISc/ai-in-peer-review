## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and promising approach to generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. The key contribution is a learned, model-based approach that significantly speeds up saliency detection compared to iterative methods while achieving competitive or superior performance. The paper is generally well-written and the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. While the novelty isn't groundbreaking – it's a shift from iterative optimization to learned prediction – the practical implications for real-time applications, combined with the introduced saliency metric, are significant. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the crucial problem of understanding *why* image classifiers make their decisions. Current classifiers, despite high accuracy, can exhibit bizarre behavior due to reliance on spurious correlations. Saliency maps offer a way to visualize the image regions driving a classifier’s prediction. Existing methods are broadly categorized as gradient-based (fast, but low quality) or iterative optimization-based (high quality, but slow). This work bridges the gap by proposing a U-Net architecture trained to *predict* saliency maps directly, achieving real-time performance while maintaining high quality. 

The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers like Simonyan et al. (2014) for visualizing CNNs, Zhou et al. (2016) and Cao et al. (2015) for iterative optimization, and Fong & Vedaldi (2017) which also takes a black-box approach. They acknowledge the parallel work of Fong & Vedaldi and clearly differentiate their method by including both SSR and SDR objectives in the loss function, resulting in better map quality.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Speed:** The core strength is the real-time performance. This opens up possibilities for dynamic applications like video saliency, which the authors briefly mention.
* **Quality:** The proposed method consistently produces high-quality saliency maps, as evidenced by both visual inspection (Figure 5) and quantitative results (Tables 1 & 3). The maps appear sharper and more focused than those produced by iterative methods.
* **Novel Saliency Metric:** The introduction of the 's' metric, based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, is a valuable contribution. It provides a more informative measure of saliency quality than simple localization accuracy, considering both map size and classification probability on the cropped region.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets, using multiple black-box classifiers. The ablation study (training from scratch on CIFAR-10) is also a positive point.
* **Addressing Adversarial Artifacts:** The authors thoughtfully address the issue of introducing spurious evidence during masking (Section 3.1) and propose several mitigation techniques. This demonstrates a careful consideration of potential pitfalls.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the methodology and results easy to follow.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the results are generally positive, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of scenarios where the method *fails*. Are there specific image types or classifier architectures where the learned saliency maps are less accurate or interpretable?
* **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The performance likely depends on carefully tuning the hyperparameters in the objective function (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4). A sensitivity analysis would be beneficial to understand how the results change with different parameter settings.
* **U-Net Architecture Justification:** While the U-Net is a reasonable choice, a more detailed justification for this specific architecture, beyond its ability to handle multiple resolutions, would be helpful. Were alternative architectures considered?
* **Embedding Visualization:** The mention of a meaningful class embedding (Appendix) is intriguing, but its visualization is limited. Showing more examples or analyzing the embedding space could provide further insights.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art Saliency Methods:** Although they compare to Fong & Vedaldi (2017), a more comprehensive comparison to more established, non-iterative saliency detection methods (e.g., those based on frequency tuning or center-surround mechanisms) would strengthen the paper.



**4. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro-Acceptance:**

* Solves a practical problem (fast, high-quality saliency maps).
* Introduces a novel and insightful saliency metric.
* Strong experimental results on multiple datasets and classifiers.
* Addresses potential issues like adversarial artifacts.
* Well-written and clear presentation.

**Con-Acceptance:**

* Limited discussion of failure cases.
* Lack of detailed hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
* U-Net architecture choice could be better justified.
* Comparison to broader range of saliency methods would be beneficial.




**5. Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Include a section discussing failure cases and limitations of the method.**
* **Perform a sensitivity analysis on the key hyperparameters of the objective function.**
* **Provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the U-Net architecture.**
* **Expand the comparison to other saliency detection methods, including non-iterative approaches.**
* **Show more examples of the learned class embeddings and discuss their characteristics.**
* **Consider including a qualitative analysis where humans are asked to judge the interpretability of the saliency maps.**




**Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above will further strengthen the work and increase its impact.** I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2017.
