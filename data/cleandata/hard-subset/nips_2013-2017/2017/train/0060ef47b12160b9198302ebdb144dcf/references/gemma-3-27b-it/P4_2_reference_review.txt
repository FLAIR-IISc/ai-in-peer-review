This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, moving away from iterative methods to a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea is to train a network to directly predict saliency maps, offering significant speed advantages. The paper also proposes a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR) concepts, aiming to better capture the interpretability of saliency maps. The work builds upon existing saliency techniques like gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative removal techniques [17], and optimization-based methods [1, 2], positioning itself as a faster and potentially more accurate alternative. The authors address the issue of introducing artifacts during evidence removal and propose techniques to mitigate this.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of learning a saliency map predictor is a significant departure from iterative methods and offers a compelling solution for real-time applications.
*   **Speed:** The method’s feed-forward nature offers a substantial speed advantage over iterative techniques, a key motivation and successfully achieved contribution.
*   **New Metric:** The SSR/SDR-based saliency metric is a thoughtful attempt to define and measure saliency quality beyond simple localization accuracy and is well motivated.
*   **Thoroughness:** The paper addresses potential pitfalls like adversarial artifacts and attempts to mitigate them. The CIFAR-10 experiments demonstrate generalization beyond ImageNet.
*   **Strong Experimental Validation:**  The experiments on ImageNet demonstrate competitive performance, particularly in localization accuracy, and the ablation studies help justify design choices. The comparison against other saliency methods and the baseline "Center" box is informative.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the concepts clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Equation 4) contains multiple terms with hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4). The sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters is not thoroughly explored, and the choices seem somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic investigation of their impact would strengthen the work.
*   **U-Net Architecture:** The choice of a U-Net architecture, while reasonable, is not extensively justified. Why is a U-Net particularly well-suited for this task compared to other architectures? A brief discussion of alternatives and their potential drawbacks would be helpful.
*   **Masking Model Training Details:**  The training procedure (e.g., the 30% fake label probability) seems somewhat ad-hoc. A more detailed explanation of the rationale behind these choices, and potentially an ablation study, would improve understanding.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper reports localization accuracy, a more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. What types of images or classes does the method struggle with? Are there common failure modes?
*   **Dependence on Black-Box Classifier:** The model’s performance is inherently tied to the quality of the black-box classifier. While the authors evaluate with multiple classifiers, the potential impact of classifier biases on the generated saliency maps isn't fully discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and practical solution to a challenging problem in interpretability.
*   The speed improvement is substantial and opens up new possibilities for real-time saliency analysis.
*   The proposed saliency metric is a valuable contribution.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The objective function’s complexity and the lack of thorough hyperparameter tuning raise concerns about the robustness of the results.
*   The lack of detailed error analysis and discussion of potential classifier biases limit the understanding of the method's limitations.
*   The justification for certain design choices (e.g., U-Net architecture, training procedure) is somewhat weak.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper addresses an important problem and proposes a novel and effective solution. The speed improvement and the new saliency metric are significant contributions. While there are some weaknesses, particularly related to the objective function and lack of detailed analysis, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should provide a more thorough analysis of the hyperparameters in their objective function, justify the architectural choices, and expand on the error analysis.
