### Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. The core problem addressed is the prohibitive computational cost of existing high-quality, perturbation-based saliency methods, which iteratively modify an image to find salient regions. The authors' key idea is to reframe this optimization problem as a prediction task. They train a separate, model-based saliency generator (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) to produce a saliency mask for any given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This generator is trained to optimize a comprehensive objective function that encourages masks to be smooth, small, and effective at identifying both the "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) and the "smallest destroying region" (SDR) for the black-box classifier's prediction. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric to evaluate the information density of the identified region. Experiments on ImageNet show that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster than iterative approaches, achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task, and produces high-quality, sharp saliency maps.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution—training a predictive model to generate saliency maps instead of iteratively optimizing them per-instance—is a significant and practical idea. It directly addresses the major limitation (speed) of high-quality perturbation-based explanation methods like the one by Fong and Vedaldi [2], making them viable for real-time applications such as video analysis. This shift from a slow optimization process to a fast inference one represents a valuable conceptual advance in the field of explainable AI.

2.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The proposed method is technically well-designed.
    *   The objective function (Equation 4) is principled and thoughtfully constructed. It combines multiple desirable properties for a saliency map: smoothness (TV penalty), compactness (AV penalty), sufficiency (SSR term), and destructiveness (SDR term). This multi-faceted objective is more comprehensive than those used in many related works.
    *   The architecture (U-Net with a class-conditional feature filter) is a strong choice for the image-to-mask generation task, allowing for both high-level semantic understanding and fine-grained spatial precision.
    *   The training procedure includes clever techniques to ensure robustness, such as introducing "fake" class labels to prevent the model from ignoring the class conditioning, and randomizing the evidence removal strategy (blur vs. noise) to prevent the model from overfitting to a specific perturbation type.

3.  **Strong Experimental Results:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing.
    *   The method demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the challenging weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, outperforming previous saliency and weakly supervised methods by a clear margin. Achieving a 36.7% error rate, close to some fully supervised methods, is a very strong result.
    *   The qualitative results (Figure 5) clearly show the superiority of the generated masks over competing methods. They are sharp, precise, and semantically meaningful, in contrast to the often noisy or blurry outputs of gradient-based or iterative methods.
    *   The generalization of the approach is demonstrated by training it for multiple distinct black-box models (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and on a different dataset (CIFAR-10) from scratch, proving its versatility.

4.  **Additional Contribution of a New Metric:** The paper introduces a new saliency metric (Equation 3) that directly measures the information concentration within a region by evaluating the classifier's confidence on a tight crop of the salient area. This is a more direct assessment of a saliency map's explanatory power than localization IoU, which conflates saliency with object detection. The fact that their method performs on par with ground-truth bounding boxes on this metric further validates the quality of the generated explanations.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on "Black Box" Definition:** The term "black box" is used throughout, but the training process requires backpropagation through the target classifier to compute the loss terms involving `fc`. This means the training phase requires full white-box access (i.e., differentiable access to the model's logits). While the final saliency model operates on the classifier as a black box at *inference time*, this distinction should be made clearer. The current phrasing could be misinterpreted as a method that requires only query access during training.

2.  **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The objective function (Equation 4) is a key component of the method, involving four weighted terms. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study analyzing the contribution of each term. For example, how do the results change if only the SSR term (`λ3`) is used, or only the SDR term (`λ4`)? How sensitive is performance to the choice of the `λi` hyperparameters? Such an analysis would provide deeper insight into why the method works so well.

3.  **Comparison with Iterative Optimization of the Same Objective:** The paper argues that its model-based approach is superior to iterative optimization because iterative methods can overfit and produce blurry maps. A compelling piece of evidence would be to directly compare the maps from their trained model against maps generated by iteratively optimizing their own objective function (Equation 4) for a few test images, as done in Fong and Vedaldi [2]. This would provide a more direct and powerful validation of their core claim.

4.  **Discussion on Potential Biases:** The authors rightly note in the conclusion that their masking model might have its own biases (e.g., from the pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder). This is a crucial point that deserves more discussion in the main body of the paper. For instance, if the masking model's encoder is pre-trained on ImageNet, could it be biased towards finding ImageNet-like features, potentially misrepresenting what a black-box model trained on a different domain (e.g., medical images) is actually "seeing"? Acknowledging and discussing this potential limitation would make the work more complete.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, effective, and practical solution to a significant problem in model interpretability. The core idea of replacing slow, per-instance optimization with a fast, predictive model for saliency is a strong contribution. The method is technically sound, supported by extensive and convincing experiments that demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, and the introduction of a new, well-motivated metric is a valuable bonus. While the paper could be improved with a clearer discussion of the "black-box" training requirements and the inclusion of ablation studies, its strengths far outweigh its weaknesses. The work is likely to be highly influential and will enable new applications of saliency methods where real-time performance is critical.

I strongly recommend acceptance.
