## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of generating saliency maps for image classifiers in a fast and efficient manner.  Existing methods, like iterative patch removal or optimization-based approaches, are computationally expensive. The authors propose a novel approach: training a U-Net model to directly predict saliency maps given an input image and class label. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) concept, and demonstrate superior performance in terms of speed, localization accuracy, and interpretability compared to existing techniques on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of saliency detection methods. It acknowledges gradient-based approaches ([11, 12, 16]) as fast but low-quality, and iterative methods ([17, 1, 2]) as accurate but slow. The contribution lies in bridging this gap – offering a method that is *both* fast and accurate.  The paper does a good job referencing relevant work, especially [2] which proposes a similar black-box optimization approach, and clearly delineates its differences (training a predictive model vs. iterative optimization).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of learning a saliency *function* rather than computing it iteratively is a significant shift and a genuinely novel approach. This addresses a key bottleneck in existing saliency methods.
* **Speed:** The authors explicitly target and achieve real-time performance, opening up possibilities for applications like video saliency analysis, as noted.
* **Performance:** The experimental results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 demonstrate strong performance. The localization accuracy is competitive with supervised methods, despite being trained in a weakly supervised manner.  The proposed saliency metric also appears well-motivated and demonstrates good correlation with interpretability.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the proposed solution. The architecture of the masking model is described in sufficient detail.
* **Thorough Experiments:**  The authors conduct experiments with multiple black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and on two different datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10), demonstrating the generalizability of their approach.
* **Addressing Introduced Evidence:** The discussion around mitigating introduced evidence during mask application (section 3.1) demonstrates a thoughtful awareness of potential pitfalls in saliency generation and attempts to address them.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention several parameters in the objective function (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4) and the training process. While the specific values used are reported, a more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of performance to these hyperparameters would strengthen the paper.  How robust is the method to different hyperparameter settings?
* **U-Net Architecture Justification:** While the U-Net architecture is a reasonable choice, the rationale for *specifically* using a U-Net isn't deeply explored. The paper mentions leveraging multi-resolution feature maps, but a more detailed explanation of why this architecture is particularly well-suited for this task would be beneficial.
* **Embedding Visualization:** While the authors mention learning a meaningful class embedding, the visualization in the appendix is rather small and hard to interpret. A more prominent and detailed visualization of the learned embeddings could provide further insight into the model's behavior.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While a comparison to other methods is provided, a more detailed ablation study, systematically removing components of the proposed objective function (e.g., the TV penalty), would help to pinpoint the contribution of each term and better understand the model’s workings.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The technical aspects of the paper appear sound. The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results are clearly presented. The introduction of the SSR-based saliency metric is a solid contribution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, although some sections (e.g., the mathematical formulation of the objective function) could benefit from further simplification and explanation.
* **Originality:** The idea of learning a saliency map is novel.  While building upon existing saliency techniques, the authors present a significant departure from iterative methods.
* **Significance:** The potential impact of real-time saliency detection is substantial, particularly for applications in explainable AI and video understanding. The method offers a practical and efficient way to interpret black-box classifiers.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel approach to a challenging problem.
* Achieves state-of-the-art performance in terms of speed and accuracy.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Thorough experimental evaluation.
* Significant potential impact for explainable AI.

**Con:**

* Hyperparameter sensitivity not fully explored.
* Justification for U-Net architecture could be strengthened.
* Limited ablation study.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of explainable AI. The proposed method is novel, efficient, and performs well in practice. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and architecture justification.  The paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program.
