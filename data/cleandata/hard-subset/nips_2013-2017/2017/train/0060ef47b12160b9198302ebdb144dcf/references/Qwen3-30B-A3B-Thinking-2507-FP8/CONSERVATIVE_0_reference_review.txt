

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a new method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers, claiming to be significantly faster and more accurate than existing iterative approaches. The authors train a masking model (based on a U-Net architecture) to produce saliency maps in a single forward pass, avoiding the time-consuming iterative optimization process used by methods like [2] and [1]. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region concept and claim their method outperforms existing approaches on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a genuine need for faster saliency methods, and the proposed approach does offer a speed advantage over iterative methods. The introduction of a new metric (s(a,p) = log(a) - log(p)) is interesting, and the results on ImageNet localization tasks show promising performance, particularly for ResNet-50 and GoogleNet. The method's real-time capability for video applications is a potentially valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient novelty and comparison:** The paper claims their work is "developed in parallel" to [2], which is a significant concern for a NIPS submission. The method bears strong resemblance to [2] with only minor modifications to the objective function. The paper fails to adequately distinguish their approach from [2] and doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art saliency methods (e.g., Grad-CAM, Score-CAM, LayerCAM).

2. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The authors claim their method "produces higher quality saliency masks" without providing sufficient theoretical justification for why their objective function (equation 4) should yield better results. The motivation for the specific weights (1=10, 2=10^-3, 3=5, 4=0.3) is not adequately explained.

3. **Inadequate evaluation:** The paper relies heavily on localization accuracy as the main evaluation metric, despite acknowledging that "saliency is not equivalent to localisation." Their new metric (s(a,p)) is introduced but not convincingly justified as superior to established metrics like AUC-Judd or S-measure. The evaluation is limited to ImageNet and CIFAR-10, without testing on standard saliency benchmarks (e.g., the MIT Saliency Benchmark).

4. **Methodological concerns:** The training process for the masking model involves several arbitrary choices (30% fake labels, random evidence removal schemes) without thorough ablation studies to demonstrate their necessity. The paper claims their method "outperforms" baselines but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these comparisons.

5. **Lack of critical analysis:** The paper doesn't address important limitations, such as:
   - How their method handles multi-object images
   - Whether the masks are robust to different types of classifiers
   - How the "bias" mentioned in the conclusion actually manifests in the saliency maps
   - The impact of their evidence removal scheme (equation 6) on the final results

6. **Reproducibility concerns:** The paper lacks sufficient detail for reproduction, including specific hyperparameters, training details, and code availability. The claim that the model "can process more than a hundred 224x224 images per second" is unsubstantiated by actual timing measurements.

**Overall assessment:**

While the paper addresses a legitimate need for faster saliency methods, it fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and superiority over existing approaches. The method appears to be a minor variation on existing iterative optimization techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach. The evaluation is incomplete, and the paper does not adequately distinguish itself from closely related work [2].

The proposed saliency metric is interesting but lacks sufficient justification compared to established metrics. The paper's claims about "orders-of-magnitude faster" and "higher quality" saliency maps are not convincingly supported by the experimental results.

**Recommendation:**

Reject. The paper does not meet NIPS standards for novelty, rigor, and thorough evaluation. While the problem is important, the contribution is incremental, and the evidence provided is insufficient to justify the claims made. The authors should substantially revise the paper to provide a more compelling comparison with state-of-the-art methods, address the methodological concerns raised, and provide more thorough evaluation on standard benchmarks before resubmission. The paper's claims about novelty and performance would require significantly more evidence to be considered for publication at NIPS.
