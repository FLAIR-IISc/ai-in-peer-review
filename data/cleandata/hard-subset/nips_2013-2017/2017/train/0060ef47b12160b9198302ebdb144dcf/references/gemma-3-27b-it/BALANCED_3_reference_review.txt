This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, shifting from iterative methods to a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea – training a model to directly predict saliency maps – is a significant departure from existing techniques like backpropagation and iterative patch removal, which are computationally expensive. The authors also propose a new saliency metric, grounded in the concepts of smallest sufficient and destroying regions, and a loss function designed to encourage smooth, informative saliency maps.

The paper is well-motivated. The challenges of interpreting black-box classifiers, and the limitations of existing saliency methods (speed, quality, interpretability) are clearly articulated. The related work section is thorough, covering gradient-based approaches, iterative methods, and recent optimization-based techniques like Fong & Vedaldi (2017). The introduction of the SSR/SDR concepts and the associated metric are valuable contributions, offering a more nuanced way to evaluate saliency beyond simple localization accuracy. The U-Net architecture is a sensible choice, leveraging pre-trained weights for faster training and incorporating features at multiple resolutions. The experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The learned saliency model is a novel approach, addressing the computational bottleneck of iterative methods.
* **Speed:** The ability to generate saliency maps in real-time opens up new possibilities, such as video saliency analysis (mentioned in future work).
* **Performance:** The proposed method achieves competitive results on standard benchmarks (ImageNet localization) and demonstrates strong performance according to the introduced saliency metric.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The explanations of the objective function and the training process are clear.
* **Thoroughness:** The experiments are comprehensive, including comparisons to various baselines and ablation studies (e.g., CIFAR-10 experiment).
* **New Saliency Metric:** The SSR/SDR inspired metric provides a more intuitive and theoretically grounded way to evaluate saliency maps.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Equation 4) involves several terms with hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4). The sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters isn’t extensively explored. A more detailed ablation study on the impact of each term would be beneficial.
* **Evidence Removal Details:** The explanation of evidence removal and the choice of 'A' (blurred image or random noise) could be expanded. While the rationale is presented, a more rigorous analysis of the impact of different 'A' choices on the generated masks would be valuable.
* **Limited Ablation on U-Net Architecture:** The CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates the method’s generalizability, but the architectural modifications to the U-Net are presented without extensive justification. Exploring the impact of different architectural choices would strengthen the argument for the chosen configuration.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparisons to existing methods are provided, there's a lack of comparison against more recent, potentially stronger baselines in saliency detection (e.g., methods based on transformers or contrastive learning).
* **Adversarial Artifacts:** While the authors acknowledge the potential for adversarial artifacts and attempt to mitigate them, a more thorough evaluation of the robustness of the generated masks to small perturbations would be reassuring.  Demonstrating that the masks are not themselves vulnerable to adversarial attacks would be a significant contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem (interpreting black-box classifiers).
* The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on relevant benchmarks.
* The introduction of a new saliency metric provides a valuable tool for evaluating saliency maps.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and thoroughly documented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The objective function is complex, and the sensitivity of the results to its hyperparameters isn’t fully explored.
* The comparison to other methods could be more comprehensive.
* The potential for adversarial artifacts, while acknowledged, isn’t thoroughly addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning. The proposed method is novel, efficient, and performs well. While there are some areas for improvement (particularly regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and robustness to adversarial attacks), the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I would lean towards **acceptance**, but strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version. Specifically, a more detailed ablation study on the objective function’s hyperparameters and a more robust analysis of potential adversarial vulnerabilities would significantly strengthen the paper.
