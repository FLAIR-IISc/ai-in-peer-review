Summary
The paper proposes a learned, feed-forward “masking model” that predicts class-conditional saliency maps for an image classifier. The core idea is to amortize an otherwise slow, image-specific perturbation-based optimization (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017) by training a U-Net-like network to output masks in a single pass. The loss blends four terms: (i) TV to encourage smoothness, (ii) an area penalty, (iii) an SSR-style term encouraging the class to remain recognized when preserving the salient region, and (iv) an SDR-style term encouraging the class probability to drop when removing the salient region. To mitigate “introduced evidence” artifacts, the masked-out region is filled with a randomly sampled alternative (strong blur or colored noise), and masks are constrained to be smooth. The authors also propose an evaluation metric based on the log of the fraction of the area of the tightest crop containing the saliency region and the log-probability of the class on that crop. Experiments on ImageNet weakly supervised localization using AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 black boxes and on CIFAR-10 suggest sharper masks, improved localization over prior weakly supervised methods, and real-time throughput.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing idea: amortized, model-agnostic saliency that combines SSR and SDR objectives and runs in real-time.
- Thoughtful treatment of “introduced evidence” via random alternatives and TV regularization; the cropping-based metric is less sensitive to masking artifacts.
- Solid architectural instantiation (U-Net with class embedding and gating), and evidence the model generalizes across classes and datasets; CIFAR-10 results from scratch mitigate the reliance on pretraining.
- Empirically competitive weakly supervised localization (close to fully supervised VGG numbers) and improved saliency metric relative to baselines.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Black-box vs differentiability: The method requires gradients through the target classifier during training (to backpropagate the loss into the masking model). This contradicts repeated “black-box” or “model-agnostic” claims, and differs from Fong & Vedaldi’s truly black-box setting. Please clarify the exact requirements (differentiability, access to logits/probabilities, query budget).
- Objective clarity: Equation (4) is ambiguous. It appears to mix λ2 AV(M) log fc(·) and a final term “3 fc(Φ(X,1−M)) 4”. Is the last term multiplied by λ4 or raised to the 4th power? Later you set “4” to 0.2 as if it were a coefficient. This must be corrected, and the sign/scale of each term justified. As written, λ2 AV(M) log fc(·) is odd: minimizing a product of area and log-probability is hard to interpret; a more standard form would be λ2 AV(M) − λ3 log fc(·).
- Evaluation gaps:
  - Limited quantitative comparisons against strong baselines like CAM/Grad-CAM (and Guided Backprop/Excitation Backprop) on standard metrics (localization error, pointing game, deletion/insertion curves). The paper claims qualitative superiority; quantitative evidence is needed.
  - No ablations of the loss components (SSR vs SDR), the random alternative A, TV weight, class-embedding gating, or mask resolution. It is hard to attribute the gains.
  - Runtime comparisons vs iterative methods (e.g., Meaningful Perturbations) are asserted but not reported as wall-clock numbers on the same hardware.
  - The proposed metric s(a,p) is underspecified (is it log(a) − log(p), or log(a)·log(p)? The text shows a product). Its sensitivity to resizing distortions and classifier scale/aspect invariance is acknowledged but not analyzed; robustness across classifiers should be reported.
- Claims and scope:
  - “No direct access to the classifier after training” is true, but the model must be trained per classifier; portability across black boxes is not demonstrated. Please clarify whether the same masking model can transfer to a different target net.
  - The term “real-time” should specify whether timing includes mask prediction only or also target classifier inference (if needed).
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Several typos and notational inconsistencies (missing operators, ambiguous coefficients, “4” in Eq. 4), which impede reproducibility.
  - Training details are incomplete (optimizer, learning rate, batch size, data augmentations, early stopping), and thresholds (e.g., 0.5 for binarization) lack justification.
- Validation bias: Most evaluations condition on the ground-truth class. Additional experiments using the model’s predicted class would better reflect the explanation of the classifier’s own decision. Human studies or qualitative failure analyses would also strengthen claims about interpretability.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but the loss is insufficiently specified and ablations are missing. The mitigation of introduced evidence is sensible but purely empirical. The new metric is intuitive but ad hoc; more rigorous analysis and comparisons to established deletion/insertion metrics would help.

Clarity
Overall readable, but key equations and training details need fixing. Figures reportedly show clear improvements, but quantitative tables are not fully included here; ensure all numbers, setups, and splits are explicit.

Originality
Moderate. The central novelty is amortizing perturbation-based saliency into a learned predictor with a combined SSR/SDR objective and random alternative masking. Related works (CAM/Grad-CAM, Excitation Backprop, Fong & Vedaldi) are cited, and the differences are plausible, but a clearer positioning (especially w.r.t. black-box assumptions) is needed.

Significance
Promising. If validated with stronger baselines and ablations, a real-time, generalizable saliency predictor is likely to be used by practitioners. The localization results are competitive; the approach could impact video explanations.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Clarify the exact objective (Eq. 4), including signs, exponents, and λs; add an ablation of each term.
- Precisely state the assumptions on the target classifier (gradient access? logits?).
- Report wall-clock runtime vs iterative methods; include deletion/insertion curves and pointing game.
- Compare quantitatively against CAM/Grad-CAM/Guided Backprop/Excitation Backprop.
- Evaluate using the predicted class and test transfer to a different target classifier.
- Provide sensitivity to the alternative A choices and mask upsampling; analyze the proposed metric and report robustness across aspect ratios.
- Release code/models for reproducibility.

Recommendation: Weak accept (borderline). The idea is compelling and likely useful, but the paper needs fixes to the objective, clearer claims about black-box assumptions, and stronger empirical comparisons.

Confidence: High.
