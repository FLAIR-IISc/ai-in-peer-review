Summary
This paper proposes a learnable, real-time saliency method for image classifiers. Instead of optimizing a saliency mask per image (as in iterative perturbation methods), the authors train a masking network (U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and a class-embedding “feature filter”) to produce a per-class saliency mask in a single forward pass. The training objective blends two desiderata: smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR), and includes total variation and area penalties to encourage smooth, compact masks. To mitigate artifacts from masking (“introduced evidence”), they stochastically blend with two alternative images (strong blur and random color + noise). They also introduce a new SSR-oriented saliency metric based on the tightest bounding crop of the mask and the classifier’s confidence on the crop, intended to reduce susceptibility to artifacting. Experiments on ImageNet with three black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) show strong weakly supervised localization numbers and favorable scores under the proposed saliency metric; they also present a CIFAR-10 experiment trained from scratch.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself among backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), CAM/Grad-CAM-style localization, and optimization-based perturbation saliency (Zhou et al.; Fong & Vedaldi’s “Meaningful Perturbations”). The main novelty is to train a generic predictor of perturbation-style masks, combining SSR and SDR in a single objective, achieving real-time inference. Relative to Fong & Vedaldi (concurrent), this is a significant conceptual step: shifting from per-image optimization to amortized inference, plus the randomization of the evidence-removal operator and a new evaluation metric.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: learning a saliency generator for a black-box classifier via a combined SSR/SDR objective is a bold and practically impactful idea that enables real-time use (e.g., video).
- Practicality: producing >100 masks/sec is a genuine capability upgrade over iterative methods.
- Sound engineering to combat “introduced evidence” and adversarial artifacts: randomization of the alternative image A, TV regularization, resizing/smoothness, and the “fake class” trick to force attention to the class selector.
- New saliency metric grounded in SSR using cropped regions; it explicitly reduces dependence on the masking operator’s artifacts.
- Empirical results: competitive weakly supervised localization on ImageNet (close to fully supervised VGG) and superior to several weakly supervised baselines; consistent performance across three different classifiers; a CIFAR-10 demonstration without a pretrained encoder.

Weaknesses
- “Model-agnostic” vs differentiability: training requires gradients through the classifier’s probability to the masking model. Thus the method is agnostic in the sense of not changing the classifier, but it is not black-box in the strict gradient-free sense. Clarify this and discuss scenarios without gradient access.
- Per-classifier training cost and portability: a masking model must be trained for each target classifier. The paper does not examine transfer across classifiers or datasets (e.g., train on ResNet-50, test on GoogLeNet).
- Limited ablations: the paper would benefit from ablation of the loss terms (SSR vs SDR components), the TV/area penalties, the two choices for A, and the class-embedding mechanism. Likewise, sensitivity to hyperparameters (λ1..λ4) is not explored.
- Metric definition clarity: s(a,p) = log(ã) log(p) is ambiguous (product vs ratio) and has unintuitive behavior when p≈1 (log(p)≈0) and for low a (a floor is used). A derivation or calibration analysis would help, and comparison to deletion/insertion or pointing-game metrics would strengthen claims.
- Comparisons: While some baselines are included, quantitative comparisons to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ and Integrated Gradients (where appropriate) would be useful, especially on localization and faithfulness metrics.
- Robustness/generalization: No analysis of robustness to distribution shift, multiple-instance images, or multi-label settings; the method assumes a single “requested class” selector and evaluates mostly with ground-truth class.

Quality
Technically reasonable and thoughtfully engineered. The objective design (SSR+SDR) is well-motivated; the artifacts discussion is valuable. Empirical support is solid but misses key ablations and some contemporaneous baselines/metrics. The claim of model-agnosticism needs careful framing.

Clarity
Overall clear and well-organized, with helpful intuition. Some notation is loose (the loss mixes log on one term and not the other; the saliency metric formula needs disambiguation). A few editorial issues and missing figure context do not impede understanding but should be polished.

Originality
Moderately high. Amortizing perturbation-based saliency via a learned predictor, combining SSR and SDR, and the randomized evidence-removal strategy are novel contributions beyond concurrent meaningful perturbations. The proposed metric is a useful perspective, though incremental.

Significance
High practical significance due to real-time capability and applicability to video and interactive explanation. If adopted, this could shift practice from slow per-image optimization to learned explainers. The need to retrain per classifier is a limitation for widespread adoption.

Questions for authors
- Do you backpropagate through the classifier during masking-model training? If so, please revise “model-agnostic/black-box” claims and discuss applicability when gradients are not available.
- Can the trained masking model transfer across architectures or fine-tuned variants?
- Ablate λ1..λ4, the A choices, and remove either SSR or SDR term to quantify their contributions.
- Clarify the saliency metric formula (product vs ratio) and its numerical stability; compare to deletion/insertion metrics.
- Report results for Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ and Integrated Gradients under your metric and localization protocol.

Pros
- Novel, bold idea: learning to predict perturbation-style masks; SSR+SDR objective.
- Real-time saliency enabling new applications.
- Careful treatment of artifact introduction; practical training tricks.
- Strong empirical localization; sensible new metric.

Cons
- Requires gradient access; not strictly black-box.
- Per-classifier training; no transfer studies.
- Missing ablations and some baseline comparisons; metric clarity.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Rationale: The paper introduces a compelling and practically important direction—amortized, real-time, perturbation-based saliency with a combined SSR/SDR objective—and provides promising evidence of quality and speed. While some evaluations and clarifications are missing, the core idea is sufficiently novel and impactful to merit presentation.
