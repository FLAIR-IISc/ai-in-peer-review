## Review of “Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers”

This paper proposes a learned approach to image saliency detection, aiming for real-time performance and improved accuracy over iterative methods. The core idea – training a model to directly predict saliency maps rather than computing them on-the-fly – is intuitively reasonable. However, despite a seemingly comprehensive experimental evaluation, I remain unconvinced of the significant contribution and novelty of this work, primarily due to a reliance on comparisons to methods that, while historically relevant, are not state-of-the-art and a lack of rigorous ablation studies.

The introduction adequately motivates the need for interpretable machine learning and frames saliency detection as a tool for understanding classifier behavior. The discussion of limitations of current classifiers and the potential for spurious correlations is well-placed. However, the claim of laying the "groundwork for a new class of fast and accurate model-based saliency detectors" feels overstated. The underlying concept of learning a saliency predictor isn’t fundamentally new; it’s more of an engineering optimization applied to existing ideas.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, covering gradient-based methods, iterative removal/optimization techniques, and recent work by Fong & Vedaldi. However, it focuses heavily on methods that are now relatively outdated. While acknowledging Fong & Vedaldi is appreciated, a more detailed comparison with contemporary learned saliency methods (e.g., those leveraging attention mechanisms or gradient *free* optimization techniques) is crucial.

The proposed saliency metric – based on the Smallest Sufficient/Destroying Region (SSR/SDR) concepts – is a novel attempt to quantify saliency quality. However, the metric's reliance on cropping and resizing introduces potential artifacts and its sensitivity to classifier invariance to scale/aspect ratio is acknowledged but not thoroughly explored. The theoretical justification drawing parallels to information theory is somewhat tenuous.

The technical details of the masking model are clearly presented. The U-Net architecture and the incorporation of a pre-trained ResNet encoder are sensible choices. However, the training process details, especially the introduction of “fake labels” and random evidence removal schemes, lack sufficient justification and appear somewhat ad-hoc. The rationale behind the chosen hyperparameters is not adequately explained.  Furthermore, the claim that the masking model acts as a “regularizer” preventing adversarial masks feels unsubstantiated; this requires more in-depth analysis.

The experimental results on ImageNet are presented with several quantitative evaluations. Achieving a localization error of 36.7% with the ResNet-trained model is respectable, but the comparison to baseline methods like “Center” and iterative approaches feels somewhat weak. The comparison to existing saliency methods is limited.  The evaluation of the new saliency metric is helpful, but relies heavily on comparisons to the same baselines. The CIFAR-10 experiment, while demonstrating the model's adaptability, doesn't address the core concerns about novelty and competitiveness on more challenging datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear problem statement and motivation.
*   Novel saliency metric (SSR/SDR based).
*   Real-time performance is a valuable practical contribution.
*   Demonstrated applicability to different datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10).

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty; the core idea is an incremental improvement over existing learned saliency approaches.
*   Comparison to outdated baselines and lack of a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
*   Ad-hoc training procedures with insufficient justification.
*   Lack of ablation studies to isolate the impact of individual components (e.g., fake labels, evidence removal).
*   Saliency metric's sensitivity to classifier properties is not fully addressed.
*   Overstated claims regarding the significance of the contribution.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents an engineering optimization rather than a fundamentally new scientific contribution.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks rigor due to weak baselines and missing ablation studies.
*   The novelty of the approach is questionable given the existence of similar learned saliency methods.

**For Acceptance (with major revisions):**

*   The real-time performance is a practical benefit.
*   The proposed saliency metric is a potentially useful addition to the field.
*   Addressing the weaknesses outlined above through more comprehensive experiments and analysis could elevate the paper to an acceptable standard.



**Overall Assessment:**

I currently recommend **rejection**. While the paper describes a functional system with some potential benefits, it falls short of the rigor and novelty expected of a NIPS publication. The authors need to significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation, provide a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and better justify the design choices within their training procedure. A more modest framing of the contribution would also be appropriate. Without substantial revisions, the paper does not represent a significant advancement in the field of saliency detection.
