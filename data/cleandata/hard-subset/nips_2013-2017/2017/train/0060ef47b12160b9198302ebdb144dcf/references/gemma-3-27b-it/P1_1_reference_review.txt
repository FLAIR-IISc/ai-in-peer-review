## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, moving away from iterative methods to a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea is to train a U-Net architecture to directly predict saliency maps, enabling real-time performance. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) concept and demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, outperforming existing techniques in both localization accuracy and the proposed saliency metric. They also address the issue of introducing artifacts during evidence removal and propose strategies to mitigate this.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within the growing body of work on explaining black-box models, particularly image classifiers. It acknowledges and builds upon previous saliency methods such as gradient-based approaches (Simonyan et al., 2014), iterative masking techniques (Zhou et al., 2016; Cao et al., 2015), and optimization-based methods (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017). It differentiates itself by offering a *learned* model, achieving significantly faster performance than iterative approaches while maintaining, and often exceeding, their quality.  The authors correctly identify Fong & Vedaldi (2017) as the closest concurrent work and clearly articulate their distinct contribution - a learned model that considers both SSR and SDR principles.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of learning a saliency map directly is a significant departure from iterative methods, offering a promising path towards real-time applications.
* **Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate compelling performance gains in both localization accuracy and the proposed saliency metric compared to existing techniques, including the closely related work by Fong & Vedaldi (2017).
* **Practicality:** The real-time capability is a major strength, opening possibilities for applications in areas like video analysis and explainable AI for autonomous systems.
* **Addressing Artifacts:** The authors thoughtfully address the problem of introduced evidence during masking, proposing and implementing strategies to minimize these artifacts.
* **New Saliency Metric:** The SSR-based metric provides a valuable new lens for evaluating saliency maps, going beyond simple localization accuracy. The theoretical justification drawing from information theory is also well-presented.
* **Thorough experimentation:** Experiments on both ImageNet and CIFAR-10 showcase the generalizability of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Objective Function:** The objective function (Equation 4) has several components and hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4). While the authors provide values used in their experiments, a more thorough ablation study investigating the sensitivity of performance to these parameters would be beneficial.
* **U-Net Architecture Choice:** The justification for using a U-Net architecture is reasonable, but a brief discussion of alternative architectures considered (and why they were rejected) would strengthen the rationale.
* **Dependence on Pre-trained Encoder:** While demonstrating the method can work without it, the reliance on a pre-trained ResNet encoder might limit the model's ability to learn truly novel saliency patterns not already captured by ImageNet pre-training.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on successes, but a more in-depth discussion of cases where the method fails (and *why*) would provide a more complete picture. Including examples of poor quality saliency maps and analyzing the potential reasons behind them would be helpful.
* **Saliency Metric Interpretation:** While the new saliency metric is well-motivated, its interpretation and relationship to human perception could be further explored.  Is a lower score *always* better, or are there scenarios where a slightly higher score with a more spatially coherent map is preferable?



**Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The work has the potential for significant impact due to its real-time performance and practical applications.
* The authors have addressed potential issues (artifact introduction) and introduced a useful new metric.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The objective function is complex and requires careful tuning.
* The dependence on a pre-trained encoder may limit the model’s generalization ability.
* A more in-depth analysis of failure cases is needed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI. The proposed method is novel, performs well, and addresses important practical considerations. The authors have clearly articulated the problem, presented a thoughtful solution, and thoroughly evaluated their approach. While there are a few areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. The authors should consider performing an ablation study on the objective function hyperparameters and including a more detailed discussion of failure cases.
