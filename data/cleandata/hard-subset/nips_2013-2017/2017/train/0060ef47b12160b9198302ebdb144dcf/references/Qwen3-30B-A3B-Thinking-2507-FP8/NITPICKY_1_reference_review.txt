

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, aiming to produce high-quality saliency maps in real-time. The authors introduce a masking model based on U-Net architecture that predicts saliency maps in a single feed-forward pass, claiming to be orders-of-magnitude faster than iterative methods while achieving better localization. The paper introduces a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept and evaluates their method on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in explainable AI, and the real-time aspect is potentially valuable for applications like video analysis. The U-Net architecture is well-suited for saliency tasks, and the authors provide a clear rationale for their objective function. The new saliency metric (equation 3) is conceptually interesting and appears to capture aspects of saliency that localization accuracy alone misses.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
The paper contains several critical technical errors that undermine its credibility:

- Equation (1) states "E = X M (1)" which is mathematically meaningless. The authors likely intended "E = X ⊙ M" (element-wise multiplication), but the "(1)" is confusing and should be removed.
- Equation (2) is written as "E = X M +A (1 M)" without proper multiplication symbols, suggesting "E = X ⊙ M + A ⊙ (1 - M)" but the notation is ambiguous.
- Equation (4) contains a severe typo: "4fc( (X, 1 M)) 4" - the "4" appears both as a coefficient and as an exponent, making the equation impossible to interpret.
- Equation (3) defines "s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p)" as a product, but it should be a subtraction "log(ã) - log(p)" to make sense as a metric (as the authors later describe it).

These errors indicate a lack of careful proofreading and undermine confidence in the technical content.

### 2. Methodological Contradictions
The paper claims to be "model agnostic" but relies on a pre-trained ResNet-50 as part of their architecture, which directly contradicts this claim. The authors state "we use a pre-trained ResNet as a part of this model in order to speed up the training," but this means their approach is not truly model-agnostic. This is a significant methodological flaw that needs to be addressed.

### 3. Insufficient Experimental Details
The paper lacks critical details necessary for reproducibility:
- The paper claims "orders-of-magnitude faster" than iterative methods but provides no concrete timing comparisons.
- The "real-time" claim is unsupported as they don't specify frame rates or latency measurements.
- Hyperparameter choices (1=10, 2=10^-3, etc.) are not justified, making it difficult to reproduce their results.
- The paper mentions "250,000 images from the ImageNet training set" but doesn't specify which subset or how they were selected.

### 4. Inadequate Evaluation
The evaluation lacks sufficient depth:
- The paper claims "better localisation results" but doesn't provide enough detail on how these results were achieved or compared to state-of-the-art.
- The new saliency metric is introduced without sufficient justification for why it's superior to existing metrics.
- The comparison with existing methods (table 2) is incomplete - they don't compare to the most recent state-of-the-art methods like those in [2] (which was developed in parallel to this work).
- The paper doesn't address potential biases in the saliency maps, which is a critical concern for explainable AI.

### 5. Clarity and Organization Issues
- The paper refers to figures (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6) that are not provided, making it difficult to assess their claims.
- The description of the feature filter in equation (7) is unclear: "Yij = Xij (X T ijCs)" - the notation is ambiguous and lacks proper mathematical operators.
- The paper claims their masks are "sharp and precise" but doesn't provide quantitative measures of sharpness or precision beyond the new metric.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection for the following reasons:

1. The paper contains multiple critical technical errors in equations that undermine its credibility.
2. The "model agnostic" claim is contradicted by the use of a pre-trained ResNet.
3. The experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail and comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
4. The paper makes strong claims about performance improvements without providing adequate supporting evidence.

While the paper addresses an important problem and proposes an interesting approach, these fundamental issues prevent it from being considered a strong contribution to the field. The technical errors and methodological contradictions are particularly concerning for a NIPS submission, where technical rigor is paramount.

The authors should thoroughly revise the paper to fix the technical errors, provide more detailed experimental results, and clarify their methodological claims before considering resubmission.
