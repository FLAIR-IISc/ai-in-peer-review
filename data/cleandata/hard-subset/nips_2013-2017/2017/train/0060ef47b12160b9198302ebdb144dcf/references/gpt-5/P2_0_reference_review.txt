Summary
The paper proposes a trainable masking network that produces class-specific saliency maps for a given image classifier in a single forward pass. The key idea is to learn a mask generator (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and class embedding gating) that optimizes a composite objective combining: (i) total-variation smoothness, (ii) area penalty, (iii) a “smallest sufficient region” term (preserve a compact region that maintains high class probability), and (iv) a “smallest destroying region” term (remove a compact region that drops the class probability). To mitigate adversarial artifacts and introduced evidence, the method applies the mask by mixing with an alternative image (either a blurred image or random color + noise) and enforces smoothness. The authors also propose a new saliency metric based on the tightest crop containing the salient region: s(a, p) = log(max(a, 0.05)) log(p), where a is area fraction and p is class probability on the crop (lower is better). Experiments on ImageNet (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50 as black boxes) and CIFAR-10 show sharper masks, much faster runtime (100+ fps), competitive weakly-supervised localization, and improved scores under the proposed metric compared to gradient-based and perturbation-based baselines.

Relation to prior work
This builds on iterative perturbation-based explanations (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, “Meaningful Perturbations”) and earlier backprop-based saliency methods (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), as well as CAM-style localization. The novelty is learning a generalizable mask generator trained end-to-end against a classifier, combining SSR and SDR objectives, and addressing introduced evidence via randomized alternative images and TV penalties. Compared to iterative methods, this trades per-image optimization for an amortized, real-time predictor.

Strengths
- Practical impact: orders-of-magnitude speedup while yielding crisper, more interpretable maps; real-time opens video applications.
- Sound formulation: combining SSR and SDR objectives, with explicit measures to reduce adversarial artifacts (randomized A, TV, lower-res mask + upsampling).
- Architecture/design: U-Net with class-embedding gating is plausible and effective; training tricks (fake labels to enforce conditioning on class; randomized A) are sensible.
- Empirical results: competitive weakly supervised localization (close to fully supervised VGG) and better qualitative maps than iterative perturbation approaches; works from scratch on CIFAR-10.
- Clarity of high-level narrative and motivation; careful discussion of introduced evidence.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “Black-box” vs differentiable: Training requires backpropagation through the classifier to optimize the masking model, so the classifier must be differentiable and accessible. The claim of model-agnostic black-box saliency should be clarified: the method is classifier-agnostic in that it doesn’t use internals, but it is not black-box in the strict sense (no gradients). Inference does not require access to the classifier.
- New metric definition: The proposed s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p) is confusing. The paper claims lower is better (small a, high p), but for a < 1 and p near 1, both logs are negative and the product is positive; moreover, larger a (still <1) yields a less negative log(a), which can reduce the product, contradicting the stated intent. The sign or form likely needs correction (e.g., -log p + λ log a, or ratio), and the “information-theoretic” interpretation is not substantiated. This needs fixing and sensitivity analysis (e.g., the 0.05 clamp, aspect-ratio stretch).
- Evaluation breadth: Missing comparisons to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ (contemporaneous and very common), and to plain CAM for networks with GAP (though CAM is referenced). The weakly supervised localization protocol is reasonable, but additional metrics (e.g., pointing game, pixel-wise overlap where available) would strengthen claims about “sharp and precise” masks.
- Specialization to a given classifier: A separate masking model is trained per classifier, limiting generality. It would be informative to test transfer (train on classifier A, test on B) to support the claim that the learned model captures general saliency patterns rather than classifier-specific quirks.
- Ablations: No ablation on loss terms (weights λ1–λ4), alternative-image choices, TV strength, mask resolution, fake-label probability, or the gating mechanism. Given the many design choices, ablations would help attribute gains.
- Reproducibility details: Optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, training time/hardware are not reported. It would be hard to reproduce exact numbers without code.
- Localization protocol details: It is implied the masking model was trained on 250k training images and evaluated on validation, but this should be stated clearly to exclude leakage. Also, the evaluation feeds the ground-truth label to the saliency model; while standard in weakly-supervised localization, explanation quality for the predicted class (without oracle labels) is also relevant.

Quality
Technically sound and well-motivated overall. The masking objective and training heuristics are reasonable and the speed/quality tradeoffs are compelling. However, the saliency metric appears misstated and needs correction, and the experimental section would benefit from stronger baselines, ablations, and clearer evaluation protocol.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with informative figures and a thoughtful discussion of introduced evidence. A few notational issues (metric sign), and some claims (information-theoretic interpretation) are insufficiently justified. Please clarify the “black-box” terminology and provide training details.

Originality
Moderately novel: amortizing perturbation-based saliency via a learned mask generator with combined SSR/SDR objective and randomized evidence removal is a meaningful extension beyond prior iterative methods and gradient-based explanations. The class-gated U-Net is a sensible, if incremental, architectural choice.

Significance
High practical significance due to real-time saliency with competitive quality; likely to be used and extended. The idea of training an explainer model against a target classifier is broadly applicable. With stronger evaluation and a corrected metric, this could influence the saliency/explainability literature.

Questions for authors
- Please clarify whether gradients through the classifier are required during training and revise “black-box” claims accordingly.
- Can you correct/justify the saliency metric form and provide sensitivity analyses?
- How does your method compare to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ on both localization and your metric?
- Provide ablations on λ1–λ4, alternative image choices, fake-label probability, and mask resolution.
- Does a masking model trained on one classifier transfer to others?

Pros
- Real-time saliency with improved interpretability
- Well-motivated objective combining SSR and SDR
- Thoughtful treatment of introduced evidence
- Competitive weakly supervised localization without bbox supervision

Cons
- Metric likely flawed as written
- Limited baselines and no ablations
- Classifier-specific training; “black-box” claim overstated
- Reproducibility details missing

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the metric, tightening the “black-box” claim, and adding at least a comparison to Grad-CAM plus brief ablations. The approach is promising and practically valuable. Confidence: medium.
