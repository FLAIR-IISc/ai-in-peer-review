Summary
This paper proposes a trainable, model-agnostic masking network that produces class-conditional saliency maps in a single forward pass. Unlike iterative perturbation-based methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi’s meaningful perturbations) or fast but often noisy gradient/backprop methods, the authors learn a U-Net-style generator that outputs a smooth, sharp mask M conditioned on an image and a class selector. The mask is trained to jointly satisfy both “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) and “smallest destroying region” (SDR) desiderata via a composite loss: TV regularization and area penalty for smooth/small masks; a preservation term encouraging high class probability on the preserved region; and a deletion term reducing the class probability when the complementary region is kept. To mitigate artifacts and “introduced evidence,” the masked image replaces removed regions with either a strong blur or color+noise, sampled randomly.

The paper also introduces a saliency metric aligned with SSR: compute a tight bounding box of the predicted mask, crop and resize, and score s(a,p) = log(ã)·log(p), where a is area fraction (floored at 0.05) and p is the classifier’s probability on the crop; lower is better. Experiments on ImageNet with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 as black boxes show improved weakly supervised localization error over prior saliency/WSOL baselines and favorable values on the proposed metric; inference runs at >100 images/s. A CIFAR-10 experiment (trained from scratch) suggests the approach does not require a pre-trained encoder.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: training a feed-forward saliency generator to approximate the outcome of iterative perturbation objectives is a bold and useful direction. The joint SSR/SDR objective and the randomization of the replacement image are thoughtful and practically motivated.
- Real-time interpretability: producing sharp, class-conditional masks in a single pass is a clear step forward over iterative black-box optimization, enabling video and interactive use cases.
- Architecture/training design: the class-conditional “feature filter,” U-Net with skip connections, fake-label regularization to enforce class conditioning, and TV/area penalties collectively yield crisp masks and mitigate adversarial artifacts.
- New metric: the SSR-oriented crop-based metric is simple, interpretable, and plausibly more aligned with human judgment than pure localization IOU.
- Empirical gains: improved WSOL errors versus several prior weakly supervised and saliency baselines; competitive with fully supervised localization (VGG) despite using only image-level supervision.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “Black-box” claim: training appears to require differentiability and backpropagation through the classifier to optimize the masking network. That is not black-box in the query-only sense; it is closer to “model-agnostic at inference.” Please clarify whether gradients through the classifier are used during training and discuss feasibility when only logits/scores are available.
- Potential leakage/bias: the masking network uses a frozen ResNet-50 encoder even when the black-box is AlexNet/GoogLeNet, which may inject its own biases. While the CIFAR-10 from-scratch result is encouraging, a clearer study disentangling encoder choice vs. faithfulness to the target classifier would strengthen the claim that masks explain the black box rather than the masking model.
- Loss clarity: Equation (4) is ambiguous (the last term looks like “+ 3 fc(...) 4”). Please clean up the notation (e.g., whether the last term is λ4·fc(·) or λ4·fc(·)^4). Also discuss the choice of mixing log(fc) for preservation and linear (or powered) probabilities for deletion.
- Evaluation breadth/ablations: missing ablations on the loss terms (SSR vs SDR only; impact of TV/area), mask smoothness mechanisms (upsampling vs explicit TV), and replacement schemes A. It would help to quantify the tradeoff between mask sparsity and faithfulness, and test sensitivity to the 0.5 threshold used to binarize masks for WSOL.
- Baselines: several contemporaneous saliency/localization methods (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM variants) could be stronger baselines; please ensure fair, tuned comparisons and report runtimes. Also report quantitative comparisons against the iterative meaningful perturbations method on your proposed metric and WSOL.
- Metric caveats: the crop-resize metric presumes scale/aspect invariance and discards context. It is useful but “highly tuned,” as you note; complementary diagnostics (e.g., deletion/preservation curves, pointing game) would make the case more robust.

Quality
Overall technically sound and well-motivated. Some clarity issues in the loss and incomplete ablations limit the conclusiveness, but the method is coherent and results are compelling.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with helpful figures. Please fix formula typos, complete/standardize references, and more explicitly describe optimization details (optimizer, LR schedule, batch size, training time), and whether gradients flow through the classifier.

Originality
High. Training a general-purpose, class-conditional masking network to approximate saliency derived from perturbation objectives is novel, as is the specific SSR+SDR objective and training tricks to enforce class conditioning and artifact mitigation.

Significance
High practical impact due to real-time saliency and improved localization from only image-level labels. Likely to spark follow-on work in fast, faithful explanations and weakly supervised segmentation.

Questions for authors
- Do you backprop through the classifier during training? If not, how are gradients estimated? If so, can you comment on extending to truly black-box settings (e.g., score-only access)?
- How faithful are the masks to the black box versus the masking encoder? Can you train the masking network with different encoders or from scratch on ImageNet to quantify changes?
- Ablation on λ’s (especially the preservation vs deletion terms) and on the randomization of A?
- How sensitive are results to the 0.5 threshold for binarizing masks?

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, novel approach; practical and fast.
- Thoughtful objective that blends SSR and SDR with artifact mitigation.
- Strong qualitative and improved quantitative results on WSOL and a new metric.
- Clear potential for video/interactive applications.

Cons against acceptance
- “Black-box” wording is misleading; training likely requires gradients through the classifier.
- Missing ablations and some key baselines; formula/notation issues.
- Potential bias introduced by the masking encoder not fully studied.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some clarity and evaluation gaps, the core idea is fresh and impactful, and the results and speed are compelling. I would prioritize this contribution for its novelty and potential to shape subsequent work.

Confidence
Moderate.
