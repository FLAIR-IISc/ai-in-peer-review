

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection for black box classifiers, addressing the need for fast, accurate, and interpretable explanations of classifier decisions. The authors argue that existing saliency methods either suffer from being too slow (iterative methods) or produce low-quality explanations (gradient-based methods). Their solution is to train a masking model (based on U-Net architecture) that can predict high-quality saliency maps in a single forward pass, enabling real-time performance. The key contributions include a new masking model architecture, a novel saliency metric that better evaluates interpretability, and extensive validation showing improvements over existing methods.

The paper builds upon related work in saliency detection (e.g., gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative patch removal [17], and optimization-based approaches [1, 2]), but distinguishes itself by proposing a model-based approach rather than an iterative one. The authors introduce a new saliency metric (equation 3) that evaluates the quality of saliency maps by measuring how small a region can be while still maintaining classification confidence.

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper is technically sound with a well-motivated objective function (equation 4) that balances multiple important aspects: smoothness (TV penalty), small mask size, classification confidence on the preserved region, and low confidence after salient region removal. The experimental design is thorough, with validation on ImageNet using three different black box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and an additional CIFAR-10 experiment. The authors provide clear ablation studies (e.g., the effect of different parameters, the random evidence removal scheme) and compare against multiple baselines, including the "centered box" baseline, Max box, and iterative optimization methods. The results consistently show improvements in both localisation accuracy (36.7% error rate for ResNet-50 model) and the proposed saliency metric.

### Originality
The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than optimizing them for each image is novel and well-motivated. While the paper mentions concurrent work by Fong and Vedaldi [2], the distinction between their approach and [2] is clear: the authors' method optimizes for both SSR (smallest sufficient region) and SDR (smallest destroying region), while [2] only optimizes for SDR. This distinction is important and well-justified. The new saliency metric (equation 3) is also a meaningful contribution that better aligns with the conceptual definition of saliency.

### Significance
The paper addresses an important practical problem in explainable AI: the need for fast, high-quality explanations for real-world applications. The real-time capability (100+ saliency masks per second) opens up new applications like video-saliency, which the authors discuss in their future work. The results showing performance comparable to ground truth bounding boxes (in the new saliency metric) are significant, as they demonstrate that the method produces explanations that are as interpretable as human-annotated regions. The fact that the model can be trained without localisation data (weakly supervised) makes it particularly valuable for real-world applications where such data is scarce.

### Clarity
The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the motivation, methodology, and results. The figures (particularly Figure 1, Figure 2, and Figure 5) are effective in illustrating key concepts. The U-Net architecture description (section 4) is sufficiently detailed for replication. The new saliency metric is well-explained, though some aspects of the objective function (equation 4) could be clarified.

## Weaknesses

### Comparison with Concurrent Work
While the paper cites the concurrent work by Fong and Vedaldi [2], it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of their method with the authors' approach. The paper mentions that [2] only optimizes for SDR (smallest destroying region) while their method optimizes for both SSR and SDR, but it would be valuable to see a quantitative comparison of the two approaches on the same metrics. Without this, it's difficult to fully assess the relative advantages of the proposed method.

### Explanation of the Saliency Metric
The new saliency metric (equation 3) is a significant contribution, but its rationale could be clearer. The paper states it's "almost a direct translation of the SSR" but doesn't elaborate on how this specific formulation better captures the concept than existing metrics. The connection between the metric and interpretability needs more explanation.

### Technical Details
The paper would benefit from more details about the training process, particularly regarding the class embedding and the fake class mechanism. The explanation of how the model learns to pay attention to the class selector (section 4.1) is somewhat vague. Additionally, the paper mentions that the masking model doesn't need to rely on a pre-trained model (section 5.3), but doesn't provide a clear comparison between using a pre-trained ResNet and training from scratch.

### Real-Time Claims
While the paper claims "real-time" performance (100+ saliency masks per second), it doesn't provide a detailed comparison to other methods in terms of actual processing time. A table comparing the time per image for the proposed method versus iterative methods would strengthen the claim.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

### Arguments for Acceptance
- Addresses a significant problem in explainable AI with a practical solution
- Proposes a novel model-based approach to saliency detection that is orders of magnitude faster than existing methods
- Introduces a new saliency metric that better evaluates interpretability
- Demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods in both speed and quality
- Validated on multiple datasets and with multiple black box classifiers
- Provides clear evidence of real-time capability with potential for video applications

### Arguments against Acceptance
- Incomplete comparison with the concurrent work by Fong and Vedaldi [2]
- Saliency metric explanation could be clearer
- Some technical details about the training process are lacking
- Limited discussion of the real-world impact beyond the technical results

## Recommendation

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of explainable AI. The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than optimizing them for each image is novel and well-executed. The results are convincing, and the new saliency metric is a valuable addition to the field. While there are some weaknesses in the presentation and comparison with concurrent work, these are not severe enough to outweigh the paper's significant contributions.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the comparison with Fong and Vedaldi [2] more thoroughly, clarify the rationale for their new saliency metric, and provide more details about the training process.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning (explainability) with a novel technical approach that advances the state of the art. The work has the potential to significantly impact both research and practice in explainable AI.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison table showing the processing time per image for the proposed method versus iterative methods and other baselines.

2. Provide a more detailed comparison with Fong and Vedaldi [2], including quantitative results on the same metrics.

3. Expand the explanation of the saliency metric (equation 3) to clarify how it better captures the concept of interpretability than existing metrics.

4. Include more details about the class embedding training process and the fake class mechanism in section 4.1.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of the method (e.g., potential biases in the masking model) to strengthen the evaluation.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and would be of interest to the NIPS community. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to the conference proceedings.
