Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an amortized, model-based approach to generate saliency masks for image classifiers in real time. Instead of optimizing a per-image mask via iterative procedures (as in Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), the authors train a U-Net style masking network (with a fixed ResNet-50 encoder and learned class embeddings) to output a mask in a single forward pass that both (i) preserves class evidence in a small region (SSR) and (ii) destroys class evidence when removed (SDR). The training objective combines: total variation for smoothness, area penalty for compactness, a term encouraging high class probability when the preserved region is kept, and a term penalizing class probability after removing the salient region. To mitigate “introduced evidence” and adversarial masking artifacts, the image outside the mask is synthesized with a randomized “alternative image” A (blur or random color+noise), plus a TV penalty and down/up-sampling for smoothness. The authors also introduce a new “saliency metric” s(a, p) = log(max(a,0.05)) / log(p) that favors small retained areas a that still preserve high class probability p on the cropped-and-resized rectangular region surrounding the mask.

Experiments train separate masking models for AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 classifiers on ImageNet, reporting improved weakly supervised localization (via thresholded masks and tightest bounding boxes) relative to baselines and some prior methods, and favorable values for the proposed saliency metric (comparable to ground-truth boxes). A CIFAR-10 experiment (with a simplified architecture and a FitNet classifier) shows qualitatively plausible saliency maps. The approach is fast (~100 224×224 images/sec), enabling potential video saliency applications.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and framing around SSR/SDR and introduced-evidence pitfalls, with a thoughtful objective combining both preservation and deletion criteria.
- Amortized saliency prediction via a class-conditional U-Net is novel relative to prior iterative, per-image optimization (e.g., Meaningful Perturbations); large speedup while maintaining or improving quality is valuable.
- Attention to adversarial artifacts and introduced evidence via randomized alternative images, TV smoothness, and architectural choices.
- Empirical results indicate competitive weakly supervised localization and improved values on the proposed saliency metric; qualitative masks appear sharp and interpretable.
- The method works across datasets/architectures (ImageNet and CIFAR-10), and the class-embedding “feature filter” is interesting.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “Model-agnostic black box” is overstated. Training the masking model requires differentiating through the classifier to optimize the objective; thus access to classifier gradients is needed and a model must be fixed per classifier. The method is not agnostic in the LIME/RISE sense, nor evidently transferable across classifiers without retraining.
- Evaluation scope: Missing strong and widely used baselines such as CAM/Grad-CAM (Zhou et al., 2016; Selvaraju et al., 2017). Iterative deletion/insertion curves (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017) would directly test SSR/SDR without reliance on a single metric.
- Proposed saliency metric may be biased. It uses the same classifier that the masking model was trained to optimize, risking circularity. It also depends on cropping the tightest rectangle (penalizes discontiguous masks) and on classifier invariance to aspect ratio/scale; the choice of log and area floor is ad hoc. No human or cross-model evaluation of interpretability is provided.
- Ablations and sensitivity analyses are limited. The method has many design choices (λ weights, TV strength, choice/probability of A, mask resolution and upsampling, fake-label probability, exponent in the deletion term); their effects on performance, sharpness, and artifact suppression are not quantified.
- Clarity/technical details: Equation 4 signs appear inconsistent with the stated objectives (minimizing +log fc would discourage recognition; likely a minus sign is intended). Training details (optimizer, LR schedule, batch size, data augmentation, hardware for speed claims) are missing. Some notational issues and figure reliance reduce reproducibility.
- Generalization and robustness: No transfer tests (train on one classifier, evaluate on another), no evaluation on predicted vs. ground-truth class, and no video experiment despite the real-time claim. Artifact suppression is argued but not stress-tested (e.g., adversarially optimizing the masking network or testing with different A-distributions at evaluation).

Quality
The approach is technically plausible and the objective is well-motivated. However, some methodological claims (model-agnostic black box) are imprecise, and the evaluation leaves gaps: missing strong baselines, limited ablations, and a metric potentially aligned with the training objective.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable with good intuition and figures (as described), but has notational/sign errors (Eq. 4), incomplete training details, and some inconsistent terminology (“black box” vs requirement for gradients). The references are inconsistently formatted.

Originality
Amortizing SDR/SSR-style mask optimization into a trainable class-conditional U-Net is a fresh idea in this context, going beyond backprop saliency, CAM/Grad-CAM, and iterative meaningful perturbations. The introduced metric is new, albeit with caveats.

Significance
If substantiated with stronger evaluation, this could be impactful: fast, sharp, class-conditional saliency useful for interactive and video applications, and potentially a bridge from per-instance optimization to learned explainers. Current evidence is promising but not yet sufficient for NeurIPS standards.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the formulation: fix Eq. 4 signs; precisely state whether/when gradients through the classifier are required; be explicit about “black-box” assumptions.
- Add strong baselines: CAM and Grad-CAM; guided backprop and SmoothGrad; and iterative meaningful perturbations with deletion/insertion curves. Include the “pointing game” or related measures.
- Reduce metric circularity: evaluate the saliency metric using a different classifier than the one used for training the masking model; report human preference studies if possible.
- Provide ablations: λ weights, mask resolution/upsampling, choice/proportion of A, fake-label probability, exponent in the deletion term, freezing vs. fine-tuning the encoder, removing the TV term, and training from scratch vs. with ResNet encoder.
- Evaluate on predicted class as well as ground-truth; analyze failure cases (e.g., multi-object scenes, small objects).
- Test cross-classifier transfer (train on one black box, evaluate on another) and out-of-distribution robustness; consider cross-dataset experiments.
- Report full training details and release code/models to ensure reproducibility; quantify runtime on specified hardware.

Pros vs. cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel amortized saliency generator with compelling intuition
- Significant speed-up with sharp masks
- Reasonable localization results and qualitative improvements
- Thoughtful handling of introduced evidence

Cons:
- Overstated “model-agnostic/black-box” claim; per-classifier training and gradient dependence
- Missing key baselines and metrics; potential metric circularity
- Limited ablations and robustness analyses
- Clarity/reproducibility issues (equation sign, training details)

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and may warrant publication after stronger empirical validation, clearer claims, and more comprehensive comparisons.
