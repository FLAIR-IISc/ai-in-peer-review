### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method for generating image saliency maps for pre-trained, black-box image classifiers. The primary motivation is to overcome the limitations of existing methods: gradient-based approaches are fast but often produce noisy or low-quality maps, while perturbation or optimization-based methods yield higher-quality results but are computationally prohibitive for real-time applications due to their iterative nature.

The core contribution is to reframe saliency map generation as a prediction problem. Instead of iteratively optimizing a mask for each input image, the authors propose training a deep neural network (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) to produce a high-quality saliency mask in a single forward pass. This "masking model" is trained to minimize a carefully designed objective function that encourages masks to be smooth and to simultaneously satisfy the properties of a Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and a Smallest Destroying Region (SDR). To train this model, the authors require access to the target classifier's output probabilities but not its internal gradients or activations, treating it as a black box in that regard.

The authors also make two secondary contributions: (1) a discussion of and mitigation strategy for "introduced evidence"—artifacts created by the masking process itself, and (2) a new saliency metric that quantifies the "concentration of information" in the salient region, aiming to capture the SSR objective more directly than standard localization metrics. The proposed method is evaluated extensively on ImageNet using AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50 as target classifiers, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on the weakly supervised object localization task and showing superior results on their proposed metric. An additional experiment on CIFAR-10 demonstrates the method's versatility.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The central idea of amortizing the search for a saliency map by training a predictive model is both novel and highly significant. It directly addresses the critical trade-off between speed and quality in saliency methods. Achieving real-time performance (over 100 images/sec) for high-quality, perturbation-style saliency maps is a major advance and opens up new applications, such as video saliency, which were previously impractical.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Quality:** The proposed approach is technically strong.
    *   The choice of a U-Net architecture is well-suited for the image-to-mask generation task.
    *   The composite loss function (Eq. 4) is well-motivated and thoughtfully designed, incorporating objectives for smoothness (TV), mask area (AV), sufficiency (SSR term), and destructiveness (SDR term). This is a more comprehensive objective than those used in related works like Fong and Vedaldi [2], which focuses primarily on the SDR objective.
    *   The training strategy, particularly the use of "fake" class labels to ensure the model conditions on the class input, is a clever and effective solution to a potential training pitfall.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and supports the paper's claims.
    *   The method is tested against three different and widely-used black-box architectures, demonstrating its generality.
    *   The performance on the weakly supervised object localization benchmark is state-of-the-art, outperforming previous methods by a significant margin (Table 2). This provides a strong, quantitative validation of the quality of the generated masks.
    *   The introduction and evaluation of a new saliency metric is a valuable contribution in its own right, as evaluating interpretability methods is a notoriously difficult problem. The results in Table 3 show that the proposed method performs on par with ground-truth bounding boxes, which is a very strong result.
    *   The CIFAR-10 experiment successfully shows that the approach is not limited to ImageNet-style images and does not strictly depend on a pre-trained encoder for the masking model.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is explained in sufficient detail, and the results are presented effectively. The figures, particularly Figure 5, provide compelling qualitative evidence for the method's superiority.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Nuance of "Black Box" Claim:** The paper repeatedly refers to the target classifier as a "black box." While this is true at inference time, the training process requires repeated queries to the classifier to compute the loss function (specifically, its softmax outputs). This is a "query-access" black-box setting, but not a fully opaque one. It would improve clarity to explicitly state this distinction early on, e.g., "our method treats the classifier as a black box in that it does not require access to gradients or internal network structure, but training does require access to the model's output probabilities."

2.  **Potential for Explainer-Induced Bias:** The method introduces a second, complex model (the U-Net) to explain the first. This raises the question of whether the saliency maps faithfully represent the reasoning of the original black-box model or if they are influenced by biases within the masking model itself. The authors briefly acknowledge this in the conclusion, but it is a fundamental conceptual point that warrants more discussion in the main paper. For example, since the masking model's encoder is pre-trained on ImageNet, might it have a prior to find "ImageNet-like" objects, potentially misrepresenting a classifier that uses unusual features? The CIFAR-10 experiment (training from scratch) helps alleviate this concern, but a more direct discussion of this limitation would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The objective function in Equation 4 contains four weighting parameters ($\lambda_1$ to $\lambda_4$). The paper provides the values used but offers no justification for their selection or analysis of the model's sensitivity to them. A brief ablation study or discussion in the appendix regarding the effect of these hyperparameters would improve the work's reproducibility and robustness.

4.  **Potential Issue with the New Metric:** The new saliency metric is a good idea, but its implementation has a potential confounder. By resizing the arbitrary-aspect-ratio crop to the classifier's required square input, significant geometric distortion may be introduced. This distortion could itself be the cause of a drop in classifier confidence, rather than the information content of the crop. The authors note this but could strengthen the paper by either analyzing its impact or suggesting an alternative evaluation protocol (e.g., placing the crop on a neutral background and padding to the required size, preserving its aspect ratio).

### **Decision**

This is an excellent paper with a novel and impactful core idea. The execution is technically sound, and the results are thoroughly validated through strong experiments. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to clarifying the method's limitations and assumptions. They do not detract from the significance of the contribution. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in interpretable machine learning by providing a practical path to high-quality, real-time saliency analysis. I recommend **acceptance**.
