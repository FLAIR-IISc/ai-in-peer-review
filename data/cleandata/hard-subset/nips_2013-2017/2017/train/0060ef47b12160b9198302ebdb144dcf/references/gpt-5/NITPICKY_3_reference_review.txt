Summary
The paper proposes a class-conditional, feed-forward “masking model” that predicts a saliency mask for an input image and a target class in a single pass. The mask is trained to (i) be smooth (TV penalty), (ii) be small (area penalty), (iii) preserve the class score when applied (SSR-like), and (iv) suppress the class score when removed (SDR-like), with the edited image constructed via E = X ⊙ M + A ⊙ (1−M). To reduce adversarial artifacts, A is randomized between heavy blur and noisy constant color. The masking network is a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder; a class embedding gates deep features to localize the class. Training uses a trick with fake class labels (30% probability) to force the model to use the class selector. A new evaluation metric is introduced that crops the tight bounding box around the salient region and scores s(a,p) as a function of crop area and predicted class probability, intended to embody SSR. Experiments on ImageNet with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 black boxes report improved weakly supervised localization and better values of the proposed metric compared to several baselines; a CIFAR-10 variant trained from scratch provides qualitative saliency.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: replacing slow iterative perturbation-based saliency (e.g., Meaningful Perturbations) with a learned, real-time predictor.
- Thoughtful objective combining SSR and SDR incentives, plus TV regularization and randomized evidence-removal A to mitigate artifacts.
- Practical training tricks (class embedding gating, fake labels) that address common failure modes (class-agnostic masks).
- Real-time inference speed claims (100+ fps on 224×224), potentially enabling video applications.
- Weakly supervised localization results appear competitive with prior work; idea of evaluating saliency via crop-based SSR is reasonable in spirit.
- Demonstrates portability to a small-resolution dataset (CIFAR-10), including training the masking model from scratch.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical soundness and clarity
- The “model-agnostic/black-box” claim is inconsistent with the method: training requires backpropagation through the classifier to optimize M. The paper later admits applicability to “any differentiable image classifier,” which contradicts “black box” in the strict sense and should be stated clearly and consistently. It is not truly model-agnostic at training time.
- Objective ambiguities: Equation (4) is poorly typeset/defined. The last term “+ 3 fc((X,1−M)) 4” is unclear: is 4 a coefficient or an exponent? The text “Setting 4 to a value smaller than 1 (e.g. 0.2) helps reduce this probability” is contradictory (a fractional exponent <1 would not penalize high probabilities more strongly). Precise form and coefficients are needed for reproducibility.
- New metric definition appears incorrect as written. s(a,p) = log(ã) log(p) (product) conflicts with the text that “lower is better” and the information-theoretic interpretation (which suggests s ≈ log(ã) − log(p) = log(ã/p)). With the product, best-case high p yields s≈0, not “significantly lower,” and the sign behavior is unintuitive. Please fix the formula, rationale, and monotonicity.
- Mask extraction thresholding for metric/localization is under-specified: “tightest rectangular crop that contains the entire salient region” requires a threshold on M; 0.5 is used for localization but thresholding for metric is not specified. Sensitivity to this choice is not analyzed.
- Evidence removal: while A is randomized, there is no quantitative analysis of artifact suppression (e.g., insertion/deletion curves, noise sensitivity, Fourier spectrum of masks), nor ablations showing the contribution of TV vs. random A vs. upsampling smoothness.
- It is unclear whether the black-box class used during training is always the ground-truth label or the classifier’s top prediction. Training to preserve the ground-truth class even when the classifier is wrong may produce unrealistic masks and inflates localization utility relative to explaining actual model behavior.
- The gating in Eq. (7) is underspecified: how is Cs normalized? How is X_ij vector-normalized? How is the class embedding trained (loss/regularization)? What margin or scaling is used in the trick “ensure max(X^T_ij Cs) is high for correct label and low for fake label”?
- No ablations: no study of the effect of TV weight, area penalty, SDR/SSR terms, fake-label rate, alternative A composition, or freezing vs. finetuning the encoder. This makes it hard to attribute gains or to reproduce.
- Runtime details are vague: GPU type, batch size, latency vs. throughput, and comparison (wall-clock) to iterative methods are not reported.

Empirical evaluation
- Weakly supervised localization: only one number (36.7%) is quoted; full tables are missing in the text. It is not clear which validation/test split, which class set, and whether single-label images were filtered. Threshold selection (0.5) is arbitrary; robustness to thresholding is not reported.
- Baselines: Grad-CAM/CAM variants are discussed but quantitative comparisons are not fully shown; guided backprop, Excitation Backprop are claimed inferior, but exact metrics/curves are not included in the text. For saliency evaluation, insertion/deletion AUCs (a de facto standard) are not reported.
- The proposed metric’s fairness is uncertain: resizing crops ignoring aspect ratio can advantage/disadvantage different methods; many classifiers are not aspect/scale invariant. This needs control experiments.
- CIFAR-10 results are qualitative only; no quantitative metric (localization, proposed metric, or any deletion/insertion measure) is reported.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous typos and formatting issues: broken equation numbering (Eq. (2) reused), missing ⊙ for elementwise ops, dangling “st Conference…” in the body, inconsistent use of spaces around minus signs, undefined notation (e.g., “i are regularisers” with i ambiguous), and several grammatical errors.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (missing years, venues, incorrect titles/URLs). Important related work such as Grad-CAM (2016) should be compared quantitatively; LIME is cited but not an appropriate image baseline; clarify positioning relative to [2] (Fong & Vedaldi), and specify what is truly novel beyond “amortizing” the optimization.

Originality and significance
- The main novelty is amortizing an iterative perturbation-based saliency method via a learned, class-conditional U-Net and adding a crop-based SSR metric. While potentially useful, the technical ingredients are largely standard (TV, area, gating, U-Net) and the experimental section does not convincingly establish SOTA or broad impact due to the issues above.

Pros
- Practical idea: one-pass, class-conditional masks trained against an SSR/SDR objective.
- Sensible regularization and training heuristics; potentially useful in real-time settings.
- Promising qualitative results; weakly supervised localization numbers are competitive.

Cons
- Ambiguities/errors in loss and metric definitions; inconsistent “black box” claim.
- Missing ablations and critical baselines; limited, partially reported quantitative results.
- Reproducibility concerns due to under-specified training details and equations.
- Numerous formatting/reference issues reduce clarity.

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: High

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the loss and metric definitions, and provide ablations for all loss terms and training tricks.
- Clarify the “black box” assumption; if gradients are required, say so explicitly.
- Report comprehensive quantitative comparisons (localization, deletion/insertion AUCs) against CAM/Grad-CAM/Excitation Backprop/Meaningful Perturbations, with thresholds and hardware details.
- Analyze robustness to thresholding, crop resizing, and choice of A; include runtime/latency numbers.
- Clean up notation, equations, and references substantially.
