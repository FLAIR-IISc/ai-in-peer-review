**Summary:**
This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers in real-time. The core idea is to train a separate generative model, based on a U-Net architecture with a ResNet-50 encoder, to produce a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This approach stands in contrast to existing methods which are either fast but produce low-quality maps (e.g., gradient-based) or are slow and iterative (e.g., perturbation-based). The generator model is trained to optimize a complex objective function that encourages masks to be smooth, small, and act as both a "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) and a "smallest destroying region" (SDR). The authors also introduce a new metric for evaluating saliency based on the classifier's performance on a cropped region. The method is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on the weakly-supervised object localization task and showing visually compelling results.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a well-known and important trade-off in saliency methods between speed and quality. The proposed approach of learning a dedicated mask-generating model is novel and offers a potential solution to achieve both, enabling new applications like real-time video saliency.
2.  **Empirical Performance on a Proxy Task:** The experimental results for weakly-supervised object localization on ImageNet are strong. The method outperforms previous weakly-supervised and saliency-based approaches, achieving an error rate comparable to some fully supervised methods. This demonstrates that the generated masks effectively capture object locations.
3.  **Model Agnosticism:** The method treats the target classifier as a black box during inference and only requires its gradients during the training of the generator. This makes it broadly applicable to any differentiable classifier.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core concepts, including the motivation for the objective function and the discussion of "introduced evidence," are clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Methodological Soundness and Lack of Ablation:** The proposed objective function in Equation 4 is a key contribution, but its formulation raises several concerns. It is a complex combination of four weighted terms (TV, area, SSR, SDR). The paper provides a single set of hyperparameters (λ1 to λ4) for the ImageNet experiments without any justification for their selection or analysis of their sensitivity. The performance of the model may be highly dependent on this specific tuning, which undermines the robustness of the proposed method. A thorough ablation study is required to understand the contribution of each term and the effect of the hyperparameters.
2.  **Introduction and Evaluation of a New Metric:** The paper introduces a new saliency metric in Section 3.2. While novel metrics are welcome, proposing a new metric and then showing one's own method performs best on it is circular reasoning. The metric itself has arbitrary components, such as the area threshold of 0.05, and relies on resizing cropped regions, which can introduce artifacts and confound the evaluation, particularly for models not robust to aspect ratio changes. The claim that the metric measures "concentration of information" is an informal interpretation and lacks rigorous theoretical backing. Without independent validation (e.g., via user studies demonstrating correlation with human interpretability), it is difficult to assess the metric's value or the strength of the results presented in Table 3.
3.  **Conceptual Issues with the Explainer Model:** The fundamental approach of using a complex, trained neural network to explain another black-box model is questionable. The "explainer" model (the U-Net generator) has its own learned biases and priors, especially since its encoder is a ResNet-50 pre-trained on ImageNet. It is unclear whether the generated saliency maps faithfully represent the target classifier's decision process or simply reflect the generator's own learned object-detection capabilities. The authors acknowledge this potential issue in the conclusion, but it is a central concern that warrants investigation within the paper itself. For example, how does the pre-training of the generator affect the "explanations" it produces? The CIFAR-10 experiment with training from scratch is a good first step, but the main results on ImageNet rely on the pre-trained encoder.
4.  **Insufficient Comparison with Baselines:** While the comparison on the localization task is extensive, the core claim is about producing higher-quality *saliency maps*. The comparison with iterative methods like Fong and Vedaldi [2] is primarily qualitative (Fig 5). The paper claims that iterative methods "overfit to the image" and produce "blurry" masks, whereas the proposed method captures "general behaviour". These are strong claims that are not adequately supported by quantitative evidence. A more direct comparison on established faithfulness metrics (e.g., deletion/insertion scores as used in other saliency papers) would be necessary to substantiate the claim of superiority over established, albeit slow, perturbation-based methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful idea for generating real-time explanations.
*   The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the challenging task of weakly-supervised object localization, demonstrating its practical effectiveness.
*   The problem it addresses—the speed-quality trade-off in saliency—is highly relevant to the community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The methodology lacks rigor, particularly concerning the un-justified hyperparameters in the core objective function and the lack of ablation studies.
*   The evaluation relies heavily on a newly proposed metric that is not independently validated, making the results difficult to interpret.
*   The paper fails to address the fundamental conceptual problem of whether the "explainer" network is truly explaining the target model or projecting its own biases.
*   The claims of superiority over established iterative methods are not sufficiently supported by quantitative comparisons on relevant saliency metrics.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper introduces a novel and promising direction for fast saliency map generation with strong results on a proxy task, the methodological and evaluative shortcomings are significant. The lack of rigor in the design of the objective function, the reliance on a self-proposed and unvalidated metric, and the unaddressed conceptual concerns about using one black box to explain another prevent a strong endorsement of this work in its current form. I believe the paper is not yet ready for publication at NIPS and would require substantial revision to address these critical issues.
