- Proposes a U‑Net based learned saliency detector that predicts saliency maps in real time instead of using slow iterative methods.  
- Introduces a novel "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) saliency metric with an information-theoretic motivation.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, including improved object localization vs. weakly supervised baselines.  
- Tackles practical needs for speed (e.g., video analysis) and addresses introduced-evidence issues during masking.  
- Provides detailed implementation, architecture, training procedure, and objective specification to aid reproducibility.  
- Limited comparison to established saliency methods (e.g., Grad-CAM, Guided Backprop) in both speed and qualitative/quantitative evaluation.  
- Objective function is complex with multiple weighted terms and unclear rationale for specific weight choices; lacks ablation study.  
- Use of TV penalty for mask smoothness appears ad‑hoc and lacks comparison to alternative regularizers.  
- Dependence on the black‑box classifier raises concerns that saliency maps may reflect classifier biases rather than true importance.  
- Risk of learned adversarial masks that exploit classifier vulnerabilities remains despite mitigation efforts; robustness evaluations are insufficient.  
- Quality: 8/10 — technically sound and well-executed with convincing experiments.  
- Clarity: 8/10 — generally well written but some sections (notably objective explanation) could be simplified.  
- Originality: 8/10 — novel direction of learning a saliency detector and the SSR metric are valuable contributions.  
- Significance: 8/10 — real‑time saliency and a better interpretability metric have strong practical impact.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, particularly adding comparisons to existing saliency methods and deeper justification/ablation of objective choices.
