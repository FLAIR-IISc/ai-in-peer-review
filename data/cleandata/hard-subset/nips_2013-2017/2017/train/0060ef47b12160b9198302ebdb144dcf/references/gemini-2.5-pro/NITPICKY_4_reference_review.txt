**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers in real-time. The core idea is to train a separate generative model, based on a U-Net architecture with a ResNet-50 encoder, to produce a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single feed-forward pass. This approach stands in contrast to existing iterative, optimization-based methods which are computationally expensive. The authors formulate a composite objective function for training this generator, incorporating terms that encourage the mask to be a Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and a Smallest Destroying Region (SDR), while also promoting spatial smoothness (Total Variation) and small area. To evaluate their method, the authors introduce a new saliency metric designed to measure the "concentration of information" in a cropped region. Experiments on ImageNet show that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization and produces qualitatively sharp and accurate saliency maps, significantly outperforming previous methods in both speed and quality. An additional experiment on CIFAR-10 demonstrates the method's applicability to different datasets and model architectures.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Practicality:** The primary contribution—amortizing the expensive iterative search for a saliency map by training a deep generator—is a strong and novel idea. The resulting speed-up (over 100 images/sec) is a significant practical achievement, enabling new applications like real-time video saliency analysis, which is a commendable advance over prior art.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the challenging task of weakly supervised object localization on ImageNet, achieving an error rate (36.7%) competitive with some fully supervised methods. This provides compelling evidence for the quality of the generated masks.

3.  **Thoughtful Methodological Contributions:** The discussion around "introduced evidence" from masking operations is insightful. The proposed mitigation strategies, such as randomizing the background fill between blurring and noise, are clever and likely contribute to the model's robustness. The training trick of using "fake" labels to ensure the model attends to the class conditioning is also a noteworthy detail.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core idea and results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws ranging from critical clarity issues to minor formatting inconsistencies, which collectively undermine its technical soundness and readiness for publication.

1.  **Critical Lack of Clarity in the Objective Function:** The central technical contribution, the objective function in Equation 4, is presented in a way that is incomprehensible and contradictory. The equation reads `L(M) = λ1TV(M) + λ2AV(M) log(fc( (X,M))) + λ3fc( (X, 1 M)) 4`.
    *   The term `λ2AV(M) log(fc( (X,M)))` is syntactically ambiguous. Does `log` multiply `AV`?
    *   The text states the third term is for the SSR objective (i.e., maximizing `fc`), which would require minimizing `-log(fc)`. However, the equation shows a positive `+log(fc)` term, which would *minimize* the class probability, directly contradicting the paper's description.
    *   The final term ends with a stray "4", which is presumably a typo for the hyperparameter `λ4`.
    *   This is not a minor typographical error; it is a fundamental flaw in the presentation of the paper's core methodology, making the work impossible to reproduce or validate without significant guesswork.

2.  **Insufficient Justification for Methodological Choices:**
    *   **New Metric (Eq. 3):** The threshold `ã = max(a, 0.05)` is arbitrary and its sensitivity is not analyzed. The claim that this metric relates to the "concentration of information" is hand-wavy and lacks a formal derivation. More importantly, the metric's reliance on resizing an arbitrary aspect-ratio crop to a fixed square input is a major confounder, as it conflates the quality of the saliency map with the classifier's robustness to severe deformations.
    *   **Mask Generation (Eq. 8):** The use of a "nonstandard" two-channel output `Ms = abs(C0) / (abs(C0) + abs(C1))` is poorly motivated. The claim that standard sigmoid/tanh activations "did not optimise properly" is insufficient without further analysis or empirical comparison. An ablation study is required to justify this ad-hoc design choice.
    *   **Total Variation (Eq. 5):** The formulation provided is for the squared L2-norm of the mask's spatial gradient, not the L1-norm typically associated with Total Variation. While this is a valid regularizer, the use of the term "TV" is imprecise and the choice is not justified.

3.  **Lack of Experimental Rigor and Detail:**
    *   Crucial experimental details are missing. A "standard GPU" is not a reproducible specification. The hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4) are provided without any discussion of their selection process or a sensitivity analysis.
    *   The threshold of 0.5 used for binarizing masks in the localization experiment is arbitrary. The evaluation would be more robust if it showed performance across a range of thresholds (e.g., via a precision-recall curve).
    *   The paper claims that iterative methods like [2] produce "blurry, imprecise, and overfit" masks but provides no direct visual or quantitative comparison in any figure or table to substantiate this. Figure 2, which is referenced in this context, does not show the output of [2].

4.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper exhibits a general lack of polish. The reference list is poorly formatted, with inconsistent styles, missing venues, and stray characters (e.g., `arXiv preprint arXiv:1704.03296,, , ,`). The footnote on the first page (`st Conference...`) appears to be a leftover from a template. These numerous small errors give the impression of a hastily prepared manuscript.

**Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** Low. While the underlying ideas are sound, the paper is technically compromised by the severe clarity issues in the objective function and the lack of justification for key methodological choices. The claims are not fully supported due to these ambiguities and missing experimental details.
*   **Clarity:** Very Low. The paper is poorly written. The objective function, a cornerstone of the method, is presented in a contradictory and nonsensical manner. Key design choices are not explained, and experimental details are vague.
*   **Originality:** High. The core concept of training a generator to produce saliency maps for a black-box model is novel and a significant departure from previous iterative approaches.
*   **Significance:** Potentially High. If the technical issues were resolved, the ability to generate high-quality saliency maps in real-time would be a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI, with clear applications in areas like autonomous systems.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a novel and potentially significant contribution with strong headline results, its current state is unacceptable for publication at NIPS. The critical flaws in the presentation of the core objective function, combined with a pervasive lack of methodological justification, experimental rigor, and basic academic polish, make the work scientifically unsound as presented. I cannot recommend acceptance until these fundamental issues are addressed in a major revision. The authors have a promising direction, but the execution and presentation in this manuscript fall well below the required standard.
