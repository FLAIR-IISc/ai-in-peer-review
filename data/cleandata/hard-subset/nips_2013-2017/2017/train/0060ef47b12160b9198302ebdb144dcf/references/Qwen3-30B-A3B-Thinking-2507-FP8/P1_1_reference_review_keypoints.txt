- Proposes a masking model (U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass, avoiding per-image optimization.  
- Introduces a novel objective (Equation 4) balancing mask smoothness, size, preserved-region confidence, and low confidence on removed regions.  
- Proposes a new saliency metric based on the "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) to better capture interpretability than standard localization accuracy.  
- Demonstrates strong experimental results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, with competitive localization error despite weak supervision.  
- Claims real-time performance (100+ 224x224 saliency masks per second), enabling practical applications like video saliency for autonomous vehicles.  
- Explicitly addresses the problem of introduced evidence during saliency generation using randomized evidence removal (blur/noise).  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly presented, with helpful architecture diagrams and training details.  
- Novelty: training a model to predict saliency maps rather than per-image optimization is a significant contribution.  
- Practical significance: speed and quality improvements make the method attractive for real-world use.  
- Limited validation of the new metric: lacks human perceptual correlation studies to confirm interpretability.  
- Missing details on training complexity and computational resources required to train the masking model.  
- Relies on a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder, raising dependency and potential bias concerns; "model-agnostic" claim is ambiguous.  
- Lacks comparisons with some recent saliency methods (e.g., Grad-CAM, Score-CAM) for a more comprehensive benchmark.  
- Insufficient discussion of method limitations, robustness across classifier architectures, and failure cases.  
- Evaluation criteria: technically sound and well-justified, clear writing, original approach, and significant practical impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel real-time approach, strong empirical results, useful new metric, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: metric not human-validated, missing recent method comparisons, unclear limitations, and training/resource ambiguities.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with meaningful contributions to interpretability; recommended for acceptance.  
- Suggested improvements: add human evaluation for the metric, compare to recent methods, detail training resources, clarify model-agnostic claim, and analyze failure cases and wider classifier performance.
