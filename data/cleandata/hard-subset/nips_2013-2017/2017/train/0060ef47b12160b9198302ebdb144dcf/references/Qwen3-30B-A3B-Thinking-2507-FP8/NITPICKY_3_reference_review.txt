

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary
The paper proposes a model-based approach to image saliency detection that trains a masking model to predict saliency maps for any input image in a single forward pass, claiming to be orders-of-magnitude faster than iterative methods while producing higher quality saliency masks. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region concept and demonstrate their method on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in model interpretability and proposes a potentially valuable real-time solution. The new saliency metric is well-motivated and provides a more nuanced evaluation than standard localisation accuracy. The experimental results show promising performance, particularly on the ImageNet dataset where the method achieves competitive localisation error rates (36.7% with ResNet-50) compared to fully supervised approaches (34.3% with VGG).

## Weaknesses

### Technical Inaccuracies and Ambiguities
1. **Critical error in architecture description**: The paper claims "ResNet-50 contains feature maps of five different scales, where each subsequent scale block downsamples the input by a factor of two." This is factually incorrect. ResNet-50 downsamples by factors of 2 at specific blocks (not each block), with the final feature map for 224×224 input being 7×7 (32× downsampling), not 4× as claimed in the text. The paper states "the mask Ms has resolution four times lower than the input image" but ResNet-50 Scale 5 is 32× lower, not 4×. This fundamental misunderstanding of the ResNet architecture undermines the technical credibility of the approach.

2. **Equation errors**: Equation 4 contains multiple critical notational errors: "L(M) = 1TV(M) + 2AV(M) log(fc((X,M))) + 3fc((X, 1 M)) 4" lacks proper multiplication symbols and has an unexplained "4" at the end. The fourth term should be a coefficient multiplied by the classification probability, not written as "3fc(...)) 4". This makes the objective function mathematically ambiguous.

3. **Metric definition issue**: The saliency metric s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p) with ã = max(a, 0.05) is problematic because log(p) is negative (p ≤ 1), making the metric negative. The paper claims "a low value for the saliency metric is a characteristic of good saliency detectors," but since the metric is negative, lower values would actually be more negative, which is confusingly phrased.

### Methodological Concerns
1. **Contradictory claims**: The paper claims to be "model agnostic" but uses ResNet-50 as part of their masking model architecture (Section 4), which directly contradicts this claim. The model's performance would likely be dependent on the pre-trained features from ResNet-50.

2. **Lack of proper comparison**: The paper claims "orders-of-magnitude faster than iterative methods" but provides no concrete speed measurements or comparisons to the iterative methods they're claiming to outperform. The only speed claim is "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU," without specifying which GPU or comparison point.

3. **Training details missing**: The paper states "the probability of the fake label occurrence to 30%" but provides no justification for this specific value. Similarly, the Gaussian blur parameter is mentioned as "σ=10" but the text says "Gaussian blur with = 10" without specifying the parameter.

4. **Inconsistent evidence removal**: The paper claims that randomizing the evidence removal scheme "greatly improves the quality of the produced masks," but provides no quantitative evidence to support this claim. The benefits of this approach are not validated with ablation studies.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
1. **Citation mismatch**: The related work section cites "Zhou et al. [17]" as proposing an approach for saliency, but reference [17] in the bibliography is "Learning deep features for discriminative localization" by Zhou et al., which is about object localization, not saliency. This is a significant error that undermines the literature review.

2. **Figure references**: The paper references figures (e.g., "figure 1," "figure 2") but the text doesn't provide clear descriptions of what these figures contain, making it difficult to assess the claims.

3. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper switches between "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) and "smallest destroying region" (SDR) without consistently defining which is which. In Section 3.2, the metric is described as being based on SSR, but the evaluation method (using the tightest rectangular crop) actually corresponds to SDR.

4. **Reference formatting inconsistencies**: The bibliography has inconsistent formatting (some entries have URLs, others don't), which is unprofessional for a NIPS submission.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and presents a potentially valuable approach to real-time saliency detection, it is marred by multiple technical inaccuracies, methodological ambiguities, and presentation flaws that significantly undermine its credibility. The fundamental misunderstanding of ResNet architecture, critical equation errors, contradictory claims about model agnosticism, and lack of proper experimental comparisons make it difficult to trust the technical claims. The paper would benefit from significant revision to correct these issues before being considered for publication.

## Recommendation
Reject

The paper's technical inaccuracies and ambiguities are too severe to overlook, despite the potentially valuable problem it addresses. The errors in core technical descriptions (ResNet architecture, objective function formulation) suggest a lack of careful technical review that would be unacceptable for a NIPS publication. The paper would require substantial revision to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for acceptance.
