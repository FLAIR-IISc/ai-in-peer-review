

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a new approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, training a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass rather than using iterative methods. The authors introduce a novel saliency metric and claim significant speed improvements while maintaining or improving quality compared to existing methods.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a genuine need for fast saliency detection methods and presents a reasonable technical approach. The introduction of a new metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) is conceptually interesting, and the experimental results on ImageNet show promising performance compared to baseline methods.

## Major Concerns

### Technical Inconsistencies
The paper claims to be "model agnostic" while simultaneously using a ResNet-50 encoder pre-trained on ImageNet as part of their masking model. This contradicts the model-agnostic claim, as the model now depends on pre-trained weights with inherent biases. The paper should clarify whether the masking model is truly black-box compatible or requires adaptation to each classifier.

Equation (1) states E = X ⊙ M (element-wise multiplication), but the paper claims this "sets certain regions of the image to a constant '0' colour." This is technically incorrect - element-wise multiplication with a mask would set those regions to black (0), not a constant color. This fundamental misunderstanding undermines the paper's technical credibility.

### Methodological Flaws
The proposed saliency metric s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p) (with ã = max(a, 0.05)) is arbitrarily defined without justification. Why not use log(p/a) or p/a? The paper fails to explain why this specific formulation is superior to existing metrics, making the metric's value questionable.

The paper claims their method produces "sharp saliency maps" but provides no quantitative comparison to existing methods in terms of sharpness (e.g., via edge detection metrics). The visual examples in Figure 5 are suggestive but insufficient for scientific claims.

### Experimental Issues
In Section 5.1, the paper claims their weakly supervised method outperforms VGG's fully supervised approach (36.7% vs 34.3% error). This comparison is misleading because the methods operate under fundamentally different training paradigms. The paper should explicitly acknowledge this methodological difference rather than presenting them as comparable.

The claim of "100 saliency masks per second" lacks critical context - the hardware specifications, batch size, and image resolution are missing, making this claim impossible to verify or compare with other real-time methods.

The paper states "the masking model does not need to rely on a pre-trained model" (Section 5.3), but for ImageNet they used a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder, contradicting this claim. For CIFAR-10 they used a randomly initialized model, but provide no details about training stability or convergence.

### Formatting and Presentation
The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies: "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" should be "The Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NeurIPS 2017)" (NIPS was renamed to NeurIPS). The paper references figures (e.g., "figure 1") but doesn't provide the actual figures, which is essential for a NIPS submission.

The paper claims "a new saliency measure" but fails to properly contextualize how it differs from existing metrics like the one proposed in [2]. The related work section doesn't adequately differentiate their metric from prior work.

## Minor Technical Issues

- In equation (4), the objective function uses coefficients 1, 2, 3, 4 but the paper doesn't explain their selection rationale (e.g., why 10, 10^-3, 5, 0.3 in Section 5).
- The paper claims "adversarial artifacts" can be avoided with their masking approach (Section 3.1), but provides no evidence or theoretical justification for this claim.
- The paper states "we resize the crop to the required size disregarding aspect ratio" (Section 3.2), but doesn't address how this affects the saliency metric's validity, especially for aspect-ratio-sensitive classifiers.

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents some promising results, the technical inconsistencies, methodological flaws, and lack of proper justification for key choices undermine the paper's scientific contribution. The claim of "model agnosticism" is contradicted by the paper's methodology, and the new saliency metric lacks theoretical grounding. The paper's formatting issues and missing details (like hardware specs for speed claims) further diminish its credibility.

The paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The speed claim is unsubstantiated, the metric is arbitrarily defined, and the model-agnostic claim is false. These issues collectively prevent the paper from meeting NIPS's quality standards for technical rigor and scientific contribution.

I recommend rejection due to fundamental technical flaws that cannot be addressed through minor revisions. The paper would benefit from significant methodological rethinking and more rigorous presentation before being suitable for NIPS.
