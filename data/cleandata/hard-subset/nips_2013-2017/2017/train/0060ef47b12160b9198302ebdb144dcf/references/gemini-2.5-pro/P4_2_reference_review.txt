### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the trade-off between speed and quality in saliency map generation for image classifiers. Existing methods are either fast but produce low-quality maps (e.g., gradient-based) or produce high-quality maps but are computationally expensive due to per-image iterative optimization (e.g., perturbation-based methods). The central contribution of this work is a novel framework for training a separate neural network, termed a "masking model," to generate high-quality saliency maps for a given classifier in a single, real-time forward pass.

The authors propose a comprehensive objective function to train this masking model. The objective encourages the predicted masks to be smooth (via a total variation penalty), small, and effective at satisfying two key saliency criteria: being a "Smallest Sufficient Region" (SSR), where the preserved area is enough for classification, and a "Smallest Destroying Region" (SDR), where removing the area hinders classification. The framework is designed to be model-agnostic at inference time, treating the target classifier as a black box. The training process, however, requires access to the target classifier's output probabilities to compute the loss. The authors also thoughtfully address the issue of "introduced evidence" from masking, proposing a robust technique of using randomized backgrounds. To evaluate their method, they introduce a new saliency metric that quantifies the concentration of class-relevant information in a cropped region and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet.

This work builds upon concepts from perturbation-based saliency methods, such as the one developed in parallel by Fong and Vedaldi (2017), which uses a similar optimization objective. However, the key distinction and novelty of this paper lie in amortizing the expensive iterative optimization process by training a generative model to predict the saliency mask directly, thereby enabling real-time performance.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the objective function is well-motivated, combining multiple desirable properties (smoothness, sparsity, SSR, SDR) into a single, differentiable loss. The architecture of the masking model (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) is a suitable choice for a dense prediction task. The training methodology, including the clever use of "fake" labels to force the model to be class-conditional, demonstrates careful implementation. The claims of speed and accuracy are well-supported by extensive experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, using multiple target classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50). The results presented in Tables 1, 2, and 3 convincingly show the method's effectiveness compared to several baselines and prior art.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem with intuitive examples. Section 3 provides an excellent conceptual build-up, defining the core principles of saliency (SSR/SDR) and thoughtfully discussing practical issues like introduced evidence before presenting the final objective function. The model architecture and training process are described with sufficient detail, aided by a clear diagram (Figure 4). The experimental setup and results are presented in a straightforward and easy-to-interpret manner.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—training a deep generative model to produce saliency masks—is highly original. It represents a significant conceptual departure from prior work, which has focused on per-instance gradient computations or iterative optimization. This "amortization" approach is a novel application of generative modeling to the problem of classifier explainability. Furthermore, the proposed saliency metric, while simple, offers a new and useful perspective for evaluating saliency maps by directly measuring information concentration, moving beyond the proxy task of object localization.

**Significance:** The work is of high significance. By enabling real-time saliency map generation (over 100 FPS), the method opens the door to new applications in video analysis, interactive model debugging, and safety-critical systems like autonomous driving, where immediate explanations for a model's decisions are crucial. The fact that the method achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised localization benchmark, despite not being its primary goal, demonstrates its power and potential impact on related fields. The framework is also general and extensible, inviting future work on improving the masking model architecture or tailoring the objective function for specific needs.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty:** Proposes a new and effective paradigm for saliency detection by training a separate generative model, shifting away from slow, iterative, per-instance methods.
*   **Performance:** Achieves orders-of-magnitude speedup over high-quality iterative methods while simultaneously outperforming them and other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task.
*   **Significance:** The real-time capability is a major advance that enables a new class of applications for model interpretability.
*   **Soundness:** The methodology is technically sound, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated across multiple datasets and target models.

**Arguments against Acceptance (and suggestions for improvement):**
*   **"Black Box" Terminology:** The paper repeatedly refers to the target classifier as a "black box." While this is true for the *trained* masking model at inference time, the *training process* requires access to the target classifier's outputs and, implicitly, its gradients for backpropagation. This is more accurately described as a "white box" or "grey box" requirement during training. Clarifying this distinction would make the description of the method more precise.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The objective function relies on four weighting hyperparameters (λ1 to λ4). The paper provides the values used but lacks a discussion on how they were chosen or an analysis of the model's sensitivity to them. This information would be valuable for reproducibility and for others seeking to apply this method.
*   **Bias in the Masking Model:** The authors briefly mention the potential for the masking model to have its own biases. This is a crucial point that deserves more discussion. For example, using a pre-trained ResNet-50 as the encoder could bias the masking model towards finding features that ResNet-50 considers important, even when explaining a different architecture like AlexNet. An ablation study or further analysis of this potential limitation would strengthen the paper.
