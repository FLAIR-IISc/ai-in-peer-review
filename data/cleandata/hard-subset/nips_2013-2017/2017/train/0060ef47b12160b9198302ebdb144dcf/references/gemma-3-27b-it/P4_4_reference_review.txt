This paper proposes a novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, moving away from iterative methods. The core idea is to train a model to directly predict saliency maps, enabling significant speedups. The paper introduces a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept and presents a U-Net architecture for the masking model. The experimental results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of localization accuracy and interpretability.

The paper builds upon existing saliency detection techniques like gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] and iterative patch removal [17, 1, 2]. It addresses the limitations of these methods, particularly their computational cost. The approach differs from Fong & Vedaldi [2] by learning a dedicated model for saliency prediction instead of iteratively optimizing the mask, thereby improving speed and potentially generalization.  The introduced saliency metric aims to refine existing evaluation approaches by focusing on both sufficiency and destruction of evidence.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The learned model for saliency prediction is a significant departure from iterative methods and represents a novel approach in the field.
*   **Speed:** The claim of real-time performance is a crucial advantage, opening up possibilities for applications like video-saliency.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup clearly.  The problem definition and the rationale behind the proposed solution are well articulated.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed U-Net architecture and objective function appear well-designed and justified.  The inclusion of adversarial artifact mitigation is a thoughtful addition.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, using multiple black-box classifiers and comparing with several baselines, provide a robust evaluation of the proposed method.  The new saliency metric offers a valuable complement to existing evaluation measures.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Equation 4) has several terms and hyperparameters.  The sensitivity of the results to these parameters is not thoroughly investigated. A more in-depth ablation study on the contribution of each term would be beneficial.
*   **Ablation Study Limited:** While CIFAR-10 experiments show performance from scratch, the ImageNet experiments rely on a pre-trained ResNet.  An ablation study investigating the contribution of this pre-training would be helpful to understand the method’s reliance on it.
*   **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on successful examples. Discussion of cases where the saliency map is misleading or inaccurate would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Metric Interpretation:** While the new saliency metric is well-motivated, its correlation with human perception of saliency isn't explicitly evaluated. Demonstrating this correlation would provide stronger validation of the metric’s usefulness.
*   **Alternative Image Selection:** The random selection of ‘A’ in the evidence removal function is a good idea, but the specific distributions used are not clearly justified.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem in explainable AI.
*   The technical contributions are significant, particularly the learned saliency model and the new evaluation metric.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method across different datasets and black-box classifiers.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach and its evaluation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity of the objective function and the lack of a comprehensive ablation study raise some concerns about the robustness of the results.
*   The limited discussion of failure cases and the lack of correlation with human perception of saliency weaken the overall impact of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective solution. While some aspects of the evaluation could be strengthened, the technical contributions and experimental results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a more detailed ablation study of the objective function parameters and a discussion of failure cases. The authors should also consider including a qualitative comparison with human-annotated saliency maps.
