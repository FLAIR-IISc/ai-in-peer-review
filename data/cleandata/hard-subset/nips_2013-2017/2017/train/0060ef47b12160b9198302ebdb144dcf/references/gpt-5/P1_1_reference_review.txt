Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a trainable, model-agnostic saliency detector that predicts, in one forward pass, a class-conditioned mask highlighting image regions most responsible for a black-box classifier’s decision. The method formulates a saliency objective that blends two desiderata: smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR), with regularization for smoothness and sparsity, and explicit countermeasures against adversarial artifacts introduced by masking. The mask is produced by a U-Net-style network with a ResNet-50 encoder and a class embedding–based feature filter, trained to minimize the stated objective while the classifier remains fixed. The authors also introduce a saliency metric based on cropping to quantify how much of the image suffices to support high confidence.

Compared to gradient/backprop-based explanations (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop, CAM), and optimization-based meaningful perturbations (Fong & Vedaldi), the main novelty is amortizing mask inference: learning a predictor that approximates the perturbation objective over a dataset and delivers real-time saliency maps, while combining SSR and SDR terms. The approach is evaluated on ImageNet across three black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) for weakly supervised localization, and on CIFAR-10. The new metric aims to better reflect interpretability than standard localization IOU.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled objective combining SSR and SDR, with thoughtful discussion of “introduced evidence” and practical defenses (randomized alternative image A, TV regularization, mask smoothing via down/up-sampling).
- Amortized inference is compelling: orders-of-magnitude faster than iterative perturbation while producing sharper, more precise masks. The architecture choice (U-Net + class embedding gating) is well-motivated for multiscale precision.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization on ImageNet (36.7% error with ResNet-50 black box), close to a fully supervised VGG baseline and better than other weakly supervised/gradient methods cited.
- New saliency metric encourages small, sufficient crops that retain class confidence; it correlates better with interpretability than localization IOU and avoids adversarial artifacts by cropping instead of in-place masking.
- Demonstrates generality on CIFAR-10 and that pretraining of the masking model is not strictly required.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- “Black box” and “model agnostic” claims are overstated. Training requires backpropagating through the classifier to optimize the masking model; thus the classifier must be differentiable and accessible during training (white-box). At test time the predictor does not need the classifier, but the paper should clarify this distinction.
- The new saliency metric s(a, p) is ambiguously defined. The text writes s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p); from the interpretation and “lower is better,” it seems the intent is s = log(ã) − log(p). Please disambiguate and justify the threshold ã = max(a, 0.05). Also discuss sensitivity to resizing-without-aspect-ratio, which can bias p for non-square crops/classifiers not invariant to aspect ratio.
- Evaluation details are incomplete. The localization protocol references [1], but key choices are not fully specified (e.g., whether ground-truth class is provided, top-1 vs top-5, binarization threshold at 0.5 only, sensitivity to threshold). Tables are referenced but not included here; more quantitative comparisons (numbers for baselines like CAM/Grad-CAM/Excitation Backprop) would strengthen claims beyond qualitative visuals.
- Lack of ablation studies. No analysis on the contribution of SSR vs SDR terms (λ3 vs λ4), TV weight, area penalty, randomization of A, class-embedding feature filter, or mask resolution/upsampling. It is hard to assess which design choices drive the gains and robustness to introduced evidence.
- Generalization and portability. A separate masking model must be trained per classifier (250k images each). How well do masks transfer across classifiers? Can a single masking model handle multiple black boxes? What is the data/compute cost vs iterative methods amortized over use?
- Robustness to adversarial/peculiar artifacts is argued qualitatively. It would help to quantify the extent to which random A and TV reduce adversarial masking compared to naive masking, e.g., via failure modes or attack success rates.
- Reproducibility gaps: optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, epochs, data sampling, class embedding size/init, loss weights and tuning rationale, and exact alternative-image distributions are not fully specified. Code release would be valuable.

Quality:
The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The objective and architecture are coherent and likely to yield the reported behavior. However, the paper would benefit from clearer math (eq. (3) sign), more comprehensive quantitative evaluation, and ablations to support design choices. Claims about “black box” should be tempered.

Clarity:
Generally well written and organized, with helpful intuition around introduced evidence and SSR/SDR. Some notation errors (eq. (3), the last term in eq. (4) formatting), and missing implementation details detract from reproducibility. Figures are referenced to substantiate mask quality; ensure numbers/tables are complete in the camera-ready.

Originality:
Training a general saliency predictor to emulate perturbation-based objectives and combining SSR and SDR in a single objective appears novel relative to contemporaneous work (Fong & Vedaldi’s per-image optimization, CAM/Grad-CAM/excitation backprop). The randomized evidence-removal scheme is a pragmatic and useful contribution.

Significance:
If substantiated with stronger quantitative comparisons and ablations, the approach could be impactful: real-time, class-specific saliency with better interpretability is valuable for deployment, video, and auditing models. The new metric is a useful addition to the evaluation toolbox, though it needs clearer definition and caveats.

Questions for the authors:
1) Please clarify the exact definition of s(a, p) and include sensitivity analysis to crop resizing and the 5% area floor.
2) Do you require gradients through the classifier during training? Can you train with only function evaluations (true black box)? Any results with non-differentiable or proprietary models?
3) Provide ablations on λ1–λ4, the choice and randomization of A, and the effect of SSR term vs SDR alone.
4) Compare quantitatively to CAM/Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ under the same protocol and to Fong & Vedaldi with matched compute budgets.
5) How robust are the masks to out-of-distribution backgrounds and to adversarially optimized masks? Any failure cases?
6) Can one masking model handle multiple classifiers or transfer across them?

Minor comments/typos:
- Eq. (3) operator is ambiguous; likely s = log(ã) − log(p).
- Eq. (4) last term formatting (“4” superscript) seems wrong; clarify it is λ4 fc(…).
- Some references are incomplete; consider adding Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) and Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017) for context.

Reasons to accept:
- Novel amortized saliency prediction with a principled objective (SSR+SDR) and practical defenses.
- Strong qualitative results and competitive weakly supervised localization.
- Real-time performance; promising for video and interactive use.
- Useful new evaluation metric.

Reasons to reject:
- “Black box” claim is misleading; requires differentiable access during training.
- Incomplete quantitative evaluation and missing ablations.
- Ambiguities/typos in key equations; limited reproducibility details.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept (7/10). With clarifications and additional experiments, this could be a solid NIPS contribution. Confidence: 0.7.
