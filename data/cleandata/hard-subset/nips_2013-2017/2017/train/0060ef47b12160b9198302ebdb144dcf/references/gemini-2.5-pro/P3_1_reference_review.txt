**Summary:**
This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers in real-time. The core idea is to train a separate, model-based saliency generator (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) that learns to predict a saliency mask for a given image and class in a single feed-forward pass. This contrasts with prior high-quality methods that rely on slow, iterative optimization for each input image. The authors formulate a comprehensive objective function that encourages masks to be smooth, small, and simultaneously satisfy the criteria for being a "Smallest Sufficient Region" (SSR) and a "Smallest Destroying Region" (SDR). To evaluate their method, they introduce a new saliency metric that quantifies the trade-off between the size of a preserved image region and the classifier's confidence. Experiments on ImageNet show that their method achieves state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization and produces qualitatively sharp and accurate saliency maps, all while being orders of magnitude faster than iterative approaches.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution—amortizing the optimization of saliency masks by training a predictive model—is a significant and novel idea. It directly addresses the critical trade-off between speed and quality in saliency methods. While gradient-based methods are fast, they often lack fidelity. Perturbation-based methods, like the concurrent work of Fong & Vedaldi [2], produce high-quality maps but are computationally prohibitive for real-time applications. This work offers a compelling path to achieving both, potentially enabling new applications like real-time video saliency for autonomous systems, as the authors suggest.

2.  **Comprehensive Methodology:** The design of the objective function (Eq. 4) is a major strength. By combining terms for smoothness (TV), area (AV), sufficiency (SSR term), and destruction (SDR term), the authors provide a well-principled and more complete formulation for what constitutes a good saliency map than many prior works. The training strategy, including the use of "fake" labels to ensure the model attends to the class selector, is clever and well-justified.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides convincing experimental results. Achieving a 36.7% localization error on ImageNet in a weakly supervised setting is very impressive, outperforming previous methods and nearing the performance of some fully supervised techniques. The qualitative results in Figure 5 clearly demonstrate the sharpness and accuracy of the generated masks compared to alternatives. The experiments across different black-box architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and on a different dataset (CIFAR-10, trained from scratch) demonstrate the robustness and generality of the approach.

4.  **Thoughtful Evaluation:** The authors rightly point out the limitations of using localization as the sole proxy for saliency quality. The proposed metric `s(a, p)` is an interesting and well-motivated attempt to formalize the SSR concept. It provides a more direct way to measure the "information concentration" that a good saliency map should identify. The results in Table 3, showing the method is on par with ground-truth bounding boxes by this metric, are compelling.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

1.  **The "Explainer's Bias" Problem:** A major conceptual concern is that the paper proposes training a very powerful model (a ResNet-50-based U-Net) to explain another model. This introduces a second complex system with its own potential biases. The ResNet encoder in the masking model is pre-trained on ImageNet, meaning it already possesses significant knowledge about object location and features. To what extent is the model truly *learning to explain the black box's behavior* versus simply *leveraging its own pre-existing knowledge to perform object segmentation*? The paper acknowledges this briefly in the conclusion, but it's a critical point that warrants a deeper discussion and potentially an ablation study (e.g., using a randomly initialized encoder on ImageNet to see how performance degrades). The CIFAR-10 experiment is a good step in this direction, but the main claims rest on the ImageNet results.

2.  **Missing Comparison to Key Baseline:** The work of Fong & Vedaldi [2] is cited as the most relevant parallel work, employing an iterative optimization approach. However, a direct quantitative and qualitative comparison is notably absent from the tables and figures. The paper claims iterative methods produce "blurry, imprecise" masks, but this needs to be substantiated with a side-by-side comparison. Including [2] in Tables 1, 2, and 3 would provide a crucial point of reference for the trade-offs between the iterative and the proposed amortized approach.

3.  **Training Complexity and Hyperparameters:** The training process appears quite involved, with specific tricks like fake labels and randomized evidence removal schemes. The performance of the method seems contingent on the four λ hyperparameters in the loss function. A discussion of the sensitivity to these hyperparameters would be valuable. How were these values chosen? Is the method robust to their variation? The high cost of training a separate, large saliency model for *each* black-box classifier is a practical limitation that should be acknowledged more explicitly.

4.  **Clarity on the Feature Filter:** The formulation of the feature filter in Eq. 7 (`Yij = Xij * sigmoid(Xij^T * Cs)`) acts as a class-conditional attention mechanism. While intuitive, more details would be helpful. How are the class embeddings `Cs` initialized and trained? Is there a separate loss for them, as hinted at in section 4.1 ("greatly speed up the embedding training by ensuring that the maximal value..."), or are they learned purely through the main objective?

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and important contribution to the field of model interpretability. The core idea of training a fast, feed-forward model to predict saliency maps is compelling and well-executed. The work is supported by strong methodology, extensive experiments, and state-of-the-art results. While there are some important conceptual questions about the explainer's own bias and a missing comparison to a key baseline, these points do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is well-written, clear, and is likely to inspire significant follow-up work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and significant idea of amortizing saliency optimization.
*   Solves the critical speed-vs-quality dilemma in saliency methods.
*   State-of-the-art results on a standard weakly supervised benchmark.
*   Well-motivated and comprehensive objective function and a new evaluation metric.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Insufficient investigation into the inherent biases of the powerful explainer model.
*   Lacks a direct comparison against the most relevant iterative baseline [2].

**Recommendation:** Accept. The strengths and novelty of the work strongly outweigh the weaknesses, which can be addressed in a final version.
