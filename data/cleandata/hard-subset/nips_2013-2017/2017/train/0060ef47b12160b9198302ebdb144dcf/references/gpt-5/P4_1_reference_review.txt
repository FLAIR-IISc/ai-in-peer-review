Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic approach to generate image saliency maps for a target classifier by training a separate “masking model” that produces a saliency mask in a single forward pass. The key idea is to approximate the “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) and “smallest destroying region” (SDR) objectives jointly via a differentiable loss: a total-variation term for smoothness, an area penalty to favor small masks, a term that encourages the class probability to remain high under the preserved region (SSR), and a term that drives the class probability low when the salient region is removed (SDR). To mitigate artifacts from masking (introduced evidence), the method randomizes the replacement content (blurred original vs. noisy constant color) and enforces smoothness. The masking network adopts a U-Net-like architecture with a ResNet-50 encoder (frozen) and learned class embeddings to gate high-level features, followed by upsampling blocks to produce sharp masks. The authors also introduce a saliency metric based on the area of the tight bounding crop around the mask and the classifier’s confidence on that crop.

This situates among prior saliency approaches: gradient/backprop methods (Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), iterative image-space perturbation/optimization (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi’s meaningful perturbations), and weakly-supervised localization such as CAM/Grad-CAM. The novelty here is training a parametric predictor of saliency masks that generalizes across images, avoiding per-image optimization while aiming to preserve interpretability and speed.

The paper reports qualitative improvements and quantitative weakly-supervised localization on ImageNet using AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 as target classifiers. It claims improved localization error over other weakly supervised methods and better scores under the proposed saliency metric, and shows qualitative results on CIFAR-10 with a smaller masking model.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution: a unified objective that encourages both SSR and SDR behavior, plus practical countermeasures against introduced evidence (randomized replacement A, TV regularization, mask upsampling).
- Architectural design: a U-Net with class-conditional feature gating via learned embeddings, producing sharp masks in real time. The training strategy (fake labels to enforce reliance on class selector) is sensible.
- Efficiency: orders-of-magnitude faster than iterative perturbation; >100 images/s on 224×224 reported.
- Empirical scope: results across multiple target classifiers; weakly supervised localization on ImageNet; additional CIFAR-10 demonstration.
- Metric proposal: attempts to quantify an SSR-like trade-off between preserved area and retained class confidence, with evaluation against baselines and ground-truth boxes.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
Quality/technical soundness:
- Objective sign/definition: In Eq. (4), the term “+ λ3 log f_c(Φ(X,M))” is stated to “make sure the classifier is able to recognise the selected class” under the preserved region. Minimizing the loss would instead decrease log f_c unless λ3 is negative; the intended form is likely “−λ3 log f_c(Φ(X,M)).” This should be corrected/clarified. Similarly, the precise exponent in the last term and its motivation (fc(...)^4) merit justification and sensitivity analysis.
- Metric ambiguity: The saliency metric is written as s(a,p) = log(ã) log(p). It is unclear whether this is subtraction, addition, or a typographical omission. The intended behavior and exact definition should be made explicit, along with rationale and sensitivity to the 0.05 area floor.
- “Black box” characterization: Training requires backpropagation through the target classifier (the method is said to apply to “any differentiable image classifier”), so the approach is not black-box in the strict, query-only sense. The paper should reconcile this with the title/claims and clarify assumptions at training vs. inference time.
- Masking/procedure details: The replacement scheme A is randomized (blur/noise), but there is no ablation showing its effect vs. fixed blur/noise. No analysis on the impact of TV strength, area penalty, or fake-label probability is provided.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: The experiments reference prior methods, but it is unclear whether CAM/Grad-CAM and Excitation Backprop are included quantitatively; thresholding protocols and any per-method postprocessing should be specified to ensure fairness. The CIFAR-10 experiment reports only qualitative results.

Clarity:
- Several equation typos/formatting issues (elementwise operators, missing symbols, Eq. (1)/(2) notation, σ for blur, Eq. (7) inner product and nonlinearity) reduce readability and reproducibility.
- Important training details are missing: optimizer, learning rates, batch sizes, training epochs/iterations, data sampling, and normalization. The evaluation tables referenced are not fully specified in the text (only one number is quoted).
- Reference list has inconsistencies and potential mismatches (e.g., [17] used to describe an iterative masking variant; ensure correct citations to CAM/Grad-CAM and meaningful perturbations).

Originality:
- Training a parametric saliency generator conditioned on class to approximate SSR/SDR is a meaningful step beyond per-image optimization (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi). The combination of objective terms and practical training tricks appears novel at the time.
- The metric is new, though relatively simple; its relationship to information-theoretic interpretation is stated but not formally derived.

Significance:
- If validated with stronger baselines and clearer ablations, the approach can materially impact saliency practice by enabling real-time, sharper explanations and facilitating video saliency. The weakly supervised localization performance close to fully supervised VGG is promising.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear motivation and practical contribution (speed and sharpness).
- Novel parametric approach to saliency with a principled objective spanning SSR and SDR.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization on ImageNet and useful qualitative results.
- Thoughtful handling of introduced evidence and training stability.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Issues with loss/metric definitions and clarity detract from technical rigor.
- Limited quantitative comparison against the strongest contemporaneous baselines (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM), and lack of ablations/reproducibility details.
- The “black-box” claim is overstated given training requirements.
- CIFAR-10 results are qualitative only.

Suggestions for improvement/questions:
- Correct and disambiguate Eq. (4) and the saliency metric; provide derivations and sensitivity analyses.
- Add comprehensive baselines (CAM/Grad-CAM, Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop, meaningful perturbations) with matched postprocessing and thresholds; report multiple metrics (localization error, proposed metric).
- Provide ablations on λ weights, TV strength, replacement strategy A, mask resolution/upsampling, and fake-label probability; include deletion/insertion-style robustness analyses if feasible.
- Clarify training details (optimizer, schedule, epochs, hardware) and release code/models for reproducibility.
- Discuss generalization across target classifiers and the cost of training per classifier; evaluate cross-model transfer if possible.
- Quantify CIFAR-10 results (localization or metric) and demonstrate video-saliency throughput if claimed.

Scope: The paper fits NeurIPS topics (interpretability, vision, deep learning).
