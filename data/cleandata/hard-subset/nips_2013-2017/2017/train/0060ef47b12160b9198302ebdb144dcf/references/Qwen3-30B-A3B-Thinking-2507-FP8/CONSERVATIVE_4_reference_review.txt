

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a new approach to image saliency detection that aims to generate saliency maps in real-time by training a masking model instead of using iterative methods. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region concept and claim their method outperforms existing approaches in speed and quality.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on existing saliency detection methods, particularly referencing the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] which also proposes an optimization-based approach. The authors claim their method is novel for training a model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, avoiding the time-consuming iterative process of existing methods. They introduce a new metric (equation 3) that evaluates saliency based on the area of a tight crop and the classifier's confidence on that crop.

## Strengths

1. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good visualizations that help explain the approach.
2. The proposed U-Net based masking model is a reasonable architectural choice for the task.
3. The paper includes experiments on both ImageNet and CIFAR-10, demonstrating the approach on different datasets.
4. The authors acknowledge the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] and attempt to differentiate their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of convincing novelty**: The paper claims to be the first to propose training a model for saliency detection, but acknowledges that [2] was developed in parallel. This significantly undermines the novelty claim. The authors should have provided a more thorough comparison with [2] to establish their contribution, rather than treating it as a footnote.

2. **Insufficient evidence for speed claims**: The paper claims their method is "orders-of-magnitude faster" than iterative methods, but provides no concrete benchmarks. They state their model processes "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second" but don't compare this to the speed of existing methods. Without clear speed comparisons, this claim lacks credibility.

3. **Questionable new metric**: The proposed saliency metric (equation 3) appears to be a minor modification of existing localization-based metrics. The authors don't convincingly demonstrate why this metric is superior to existing approaches, and they don't compare it to established metrics in a meaningful way.

4. **Inadequate validation of the "introduced evidence" solution**: The paper identifies a problem with adversarial artifacts introduced by mask operations (section 3.1), but doesn't provide evidence that their proposed solutions (equation 2, TV penalty) actually work. They describe the methods but don't show that they prevent the artifacts they claim to solve.

5. **Weak CIFAR-10 experiment**: The CIFAR-10 experiment (section 5.3) uses a completely different architecture (3 downsampling blocks instead of 5) and doesn't explain why this change is necessary or how it affects results. This weakens the claim that the approach generalizes well.

6. **Overstated significance**: The paper claims their method "enables new applications such as video-saliency" but provides no concrete examples or demonstrations of this. The significance of their contribution is overstated without evidence of real-world application.

7. **Insufficient comparison with baselines**: The paper compares to a "Centrally placed rectangle" baseline but doesn't provide detailed comparisons with state-of-the-art saliency methods beyond the few mentioned in Table 1. They don't address how their method compares to the most recent and relevant approaches.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a well-structured approach to saliency detection, it falls short of the rigorous validation required for NeurIPS. The claims of novelty are undermined by the parallel work [2], the speed claims lack proper benchmarks, and the new metric isn't convincingly justified. The paper primarily demonstrates that a U-Net can be trained to generate saliency maps, which is not a significant advance beyond existing methods.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with the parallel work [2], clearer benchmarks for speed claims, a more rigorous evaluation of the new metric, and stronger evidence for their claims of improved performance. Without these, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and superiority. The work appears to be a minor extension of existing approaches rather than a significant contribution to the field. The paper would need substantial revisions to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly in providing proper comparisons with the parallel work and rigorous validation of the claimed advantages.
