

# Review: Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel model-based approach to real-time image saliency detection for black box classifiers. Instead of using iterative methods that require hundreds of passes to generate saliency maps, the authors train a masking model (based on U-Net architecture) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. The work introduces a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which better evaluates the interpretability of saliency maps compared to traditional localization accuracy. The paper relates to recent work on saliency detection (e.g., [2], [1], [11]) but distinguishes itself by focusing on real-time performance and addressing the problem of adversarial artifacts introduced by mask operations.

## Strengths

1. **Practical Significance**: The paper addresses an important practical need for real-time saliency detection, which enables applications like video saliency analysis for autonomous vehicles. The claimed speed of 100 masks per second is a significant improvement over iterative methods.

2. **Novel Metric**: The proposed saliency metric (equation 3) is well-motivated and addresses a key limitation of existing metrics (localization accuracy vs. actual saliency). The metric's performance (Table 3) shows the method outperforms baselines and approaches ground truth quality.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The method achieves competitive results on ImageNet weakly supervised object localization (36.7% error rate for ResNet-50 trained model) and demonstrates generalization to CIFAR-10. The results (Figures 5-6) clearly show high-quality saliency maps.

4. **Addressing Key Challenges**: The paper thoughtfully addresses the problem of adversarial artifacts in saliency maps by introducing a randomized evidence removal scheme and a total variation penalty.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the method works well empirically, the paper lacks a theoretical analysis explaining why the model-based approach outperforms iterative methods. A formal comparison of convergence rates or optimization landscapes would strengthen the claims.

2. **Insufficient Differentiation from Parallel Work**: The paper briefly mentions Fong & Vedaldi [2] (developed in parallel) but doesn't adequately position its contribution relative to this work. The paper should clarify how its model-based approach differs fundamentally from their optimization-based method.

3. **Limited Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component in the objective function (equation 4). It's unclear which terms are most critical for performance.

4. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions "randomly generating alternative image A" but doesn't specify the exact distribution or how this affects the model's performance. More details would help with reproducibility.

5. **Metric Justification**: While the new metric is useful, the paper could better justify why it's superior to existing metrics from a theoretical perspective (e.g., information-theoretic interpretation).

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Clarify Relationship to Fong & Vedaldi [2]**: The paper should more thoroughly position itself relative to this parallel work. A clear comparison of the approaches (model-based vs. optimization-based) would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Add Ablation Studies**: Include ablation experiments showing the impact of each term in the objective function (equation 4) and the importance of the randomized evidence removal scheme.

3. **Theoretical Analysis**: Provide a brief theoretical analysis explaining why the model-based approach can achieve better results than iterative methods, perhaps by discussing the optimization landscape or generalization properties.

4. **More Detailed Implementation**: Specify the exact distribution for generating alternative images A and include more details about the randomization scheme.

5. **Compare with More Recent Work**: The paper references [2] but should also discuss how it relates to more recent work in the field (e.g., recent NIPS papers on explainable AI).

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI with a practical, real-time saliency detection method that outperforms existing approaches. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the new metric provides a meaningful evaluation criterion. While the paper has some weaknesses in theoretical justification and differentiation from related work, these can be addressed with reasonable revisions.

The work has significant potential for practical applications in real-time systems and advances the state of the art in saliency detection. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing techniques. With the suggested revisions, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

**Decision**: Accept (with minor revisions)
