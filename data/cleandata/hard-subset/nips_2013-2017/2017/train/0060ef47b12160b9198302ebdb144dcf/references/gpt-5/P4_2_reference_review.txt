Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a trainable, model-conditioned masking network that produces class-conditional saliency maps in a single forward pass for a fixed image classifier. The core idea is to learn a function M(X, c) that highlights a minimal, smooth, and sharp region of the image X sufficient for the classifier to recognize class c (SSR) while also destroying evidence when removed (SDR). The method optimizes a composite loss with four terms: total variation (smoothness), area penalty (compactness), preservation of class probability under masked-preserve, and suppression of class probability under masked-remove. To reduce adversarial artifacts from masking, the authors propose using an “alternative image” A (strong blur or random color + noise) for the complement of the mask and randomize A during training. They also propose a new saliency metric based on the area of the tight crop around the salient region and the classifier’s confidence on the cropped image. The masking model uses a U-Net with a fixed, pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder and is conditioned on a learned class embedding via a spatial feature filter.

The approach is positioned against: (1) gradient/backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), which are fast but often noisy; and (2) iterative perturbation/optimization approaches (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi’s “meaningful perturbations”), which are more interpretable but slow. The proposed method seeks to combine the interpretability of perturbation-based saliency with the speed of a single forward pass. Empirically, the method is evaluated on ImageNet with three “black-box” classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) and on CIFAR-10, reporting improved weakly supervised localization error over baselines and a favorable value of the proposed saliency metric. The idea of optimizing saliency masks in image space is closely related to Fong & Vedaldi (2017), while CAM/Excitation Backprop relate as weakly supervised localization approaches; the novelty here is explicitly learning a generalizing predictor of masks conditioned on class, with a loss that blends SSR and SDR and an emphasis on speed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall formulation—learning a mask predictor with TV and area penalties and two likelihood terms (preserve vs remove)—is reasonable and builds on established perturbation-based principles. The architectural choice (U-Net with a class-conditioning filter) is appropriate. The training trick of using “fake labels” to force conditioning is sound and helpful.
- However, there are clarity issues in the objective and metric that may affect reproducibility and interpretation:
  - Equation (4) is ambiguous: “+ 3fc(Φ(X, 1 − M)) 4” appears to have a missing operator and/or exponent; the narrative about setting “4” to 0.2 is inconsistent with a standard weighting term (smaller weight would reduce, not increase, the suppression pressure).
  - The proposed saliency metric s(a, p) is written as “log(ã) log(p)” with no operator; the text claims “lower is better,” but with multiplication of two negative logs this would not hold. It likely intends s = log(ã) − log(p) (or similar). This needs correction.
  - The approach is called “model agnostic,” yet training requires gradients through the classifier (the paper later restricts to “any differentiable classifier”). This inconsistency should be resolved.
- Empirical evaluation: The ImageNet localization results are promising (e.g., error 36.7% vs 34.3% for a fully supervised baseline). Comparisons to gradient-based and iterative methods are appropriate, but many details (exact baselines, thresholds, robustness to adversarial artifacts) and full tables are missing in the text provided. The new metric is only validated against a few baselines; sensitivity analysis is not shown. There is no quantitative analysis of adversarial artifact suppression beyond qualitative arguments.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-motivated and organized, but notation and typographical issues in equations and the metric need fixing. Some references are incomplete. The distinction between “black-box” in the sense of not updating the classifier vs genuinely not requiring gradients is unclear. Implementation details (hyperparameters, exact alternative-image sampling, thresholding, resize choices) are given, which aids reproducibility, but the ambiguities above must be corrected.

Originality
- Training a feed-forward, class-conditional masking network to approximate perturbation-based saliency for a fixed classifier is a novel and useful combination, beyond prior gradient-based or per-image optimization methods. The dual SSR+SDR objective within a single loss is a reasonable contribution. The proposed saliency metric is new, though its current definition is unclear and requires theoretical/empirical justification.

Significance
- If the reported speed and accuracy hold, the method is practically significant: real-time, sharper masks than gradients, and improved weakly supervised localization. The need to train a separate explainer per classifier reduces plug-and-play generality but may be acceptable in practice. The metric could be useful if clarified and validated more broadly. The discussion of artifact suppression is pertinent to the reliability of perturbation-based explanations.

Strengths
- Clear motivation; addresses a known speed–interpretability trade-off in saliency.
- Principled loss blending smoothness, compactness, preservation, and destruction.
- Practical training tricks (fake labels, randomized alternative images) to encourage conditioning and robustness.
- Strong qualitative results and improved weakly supervised localization on ImageNet.
- Real-time inference; applicability to video is plausible.

Weaknesses
- Ambiguities in the main objective and the proposed metric; “black-box” terminology inconsistent with differentiability requirements.
- Limited quantitative analysis of adversarial artifact suppression; no deletion/insertion curves or robustness checks.
- New metric lacks derivation/validation; potential bias since p is measured with the same classifier whose behavior the mask is optimized to satisfy.
- Evaluation scope could be broader: transferability across classifiers, generalization to classes not seen during training, effect of λ’s and architectural choices (ablations), and multi-object scenes.
- Some references and presentation details are incomplete.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify Equation (4): exact form and role of the fourth term; provide gradients and intuition; reconcile the statement about setting λ4 to 0.2.
- Fix the saliency metric definition and justify “lower is better”; compare against deletion/insertion metrics and report sensitivity to crop/resize choices.
- Quantify adversarial artifact suppression (e.g., with Fourier energy analyses, adversarial tests, or insertion/deletion curves).
- Evaluate cross-model faithfulness: do masks learned for classifier A retain predictive power when evaluated with classifier B?
- Provide ablations on: fake-label probability, alternative-image choices, TV strength, resolution/upsampling factors.
- Clarify “black-box” terminology: training requires gradients through the classifier; at test time you do not need the classifier.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and practical approach to fast, high-quality saliency via a learned masking network.
- Empirically competitive weakly supervised localization and compelling qualitative results.
- Thoughtful handling of artifact issues and conditioning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Ambiguous core equations/metric hinder rigor and reproducibility.
- Limited and partly qualitative evaluation of artifact suppression and metric validity.
- Inconsistent “black-box” framing; dependence on differentiable access during training.
- Missing ablations and broader comparisons.

Overall recommendation
Promising idea with practical significance, but the paper needs to correct and clarify the objective and metric, strengthen quantitative evaluation (especially artifact robustness and metric validation), and tighten the “black-box” claims. With these addressed, it would be a strong contribution.
