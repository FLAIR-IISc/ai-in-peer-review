

# Review: Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers

This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection that fundamentally shifts the paradigm from iterative optimization to model-based prediction. The core idea is to train a masking model (using U-Net architecture) that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass, rather than relying on time-consuming iterative methods that require hundreds of iterations per image. The authors argue this approach is not only orders-of-magnitude faster but also produces higher quality saliency masks with better localization properties. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region concept that better captures the interpretability of saliency maps compared to traditional localization accuracy metrics.

The paper makes several significant contributions: 1) a novel model-based approach to saliency detection that works with any black-box classifier; 2) a new saliency metric that better aligns with human interpretation; 3) a practical implementation that achieves real-time performance (100 masks/second); and 4) promising results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 demonstrating strong performance against baselines.

This work stands out from related literature (including parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2]) by fundamentally changing how saliency maps are generated. While previous work focused on iterative optimization of masks (e.g., [1, 2, 17]), this paper proposes training a model to directly predict saliency maps, which is a bold and novel conceptual shift. The authors effectively argue that their approach addresses key limitations of existing methods: iterative methods are too slow for real-time applications, and gradient-based methods produce lower quality explanations.

The paper's most compelling aspect is its novelty of concept, which aligns perfectly with the NIPS community's interest in innovative approaches. The authors provide strong empirical evidence for their claims, showing their method outperforms existing weakly supervised techniques on ImageNet localization tasks (36.7% error vs. 34.3% for fully supervised VGG) and achieves saliency metrics comparable to ground truth bounding boxes. The CIFAR-10 experiments further demonstrate the approach's generality across datasets and architectures.

The new saliency metric (s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p)) is particularly noteworthy. It directly measures the trade-off between the size of the salient region and the classifier's confidence in the predicted class, which better captures the essence of saliency than traditional localization metrics. This metric represents a thoughtful contribution to the evaluation methodology in the field.

While the paper acknowledges some limitations (the metric assumes classifiers are scale-invariant, the method requires differentiable classifiers), these are minor in the context of the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The implementation details are well-described, and the training process (including the use of 30% fake labels to prevent the model from ignoring class selectors) is clearly explained.

The paper's greatest strength is its bold, novel approach that could enable new applications like video-saliency. The authors convincingly argue that their real-time capability opens doors for applications that were previously infeasible with iterative methods. This represents a meaningful step forward in model interpretability research.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a compelling case for its contribution. It provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including architecture diagrams and training parameters. While some details about the evidence removal function (equation 6) could be expanded, these are minor concerns.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a fundamentally new approach to saliency detection. The novelty of the core idea, combined with strong empirical results and a thoughtful new evaluation metric, makes this a compelling submission that advances the state of the art in model interpretability. The real-time capability is particularly valuable for practical applications, and the method's model-agnostic nature makes it broadly applicable.

While the paper references related work appropriately, it could better distinguish itself from [2] by more explicitly contrasting their objective function and training approach. However, this is a minor point given the paper's overall strength.

In summary, this paper offers a bold, novel approach that significantly advances the field of model interpretability. The conceptual shift from iterative optimization to model-based prediction is both elegant and impactful. The paper's contribution is substantial, with real-world implications for applications requiring real-time model explanations. I strongly recommend acceptance, as the novelty and potential impact outweigh any minor methodological concerns. This represents the kind of innovative work that NIPS should champion.
