## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of explaining the decisions of black-box image classifiers via saliency detection. The authors propose a learned model to directly predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, offering a significant speedup compared to iterative methods. They introduce a new saliency metric (based on smallest sufficient/destroying regions) and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, showing competitive performance in object localization and saliency map quality.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of saliency detection methods, referencing gradient-based approaches (e.g., [11, 12, 16]), iterative patch removal methods ([17, 1, 2]), and highlighting the limitations of these techniques (speed, quality, adversarial artifacts). The authors differentiate their work by offering a *learned* saliency detector, trained end-to-end, rather than relying on iterative optimization or direct gradient computation. They acknowledge parallel work [2] and build upon its concepts, specifically the SDR/SSR idea, but extend it with a learnable model and improved handling of adversarial artifacts.

**Strengths:**

*   **Speed:** The primary strength is the speed of the proposed method. A single forward pass for saliency map generation is a major advantage for real-time applications (as highlighted in the conclusion).
*   **Novelty:**  The learnable saliency model is a novel approach.  Moving away from iterative optimization is a significant step forward.  The introduced saliency metric, based on SSR and SDR, is a thoughtful attempt to formalize the notion of “good” saliency.
*   **Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on object localization (Table 1) and the new saliency metric (Table 3), particularly when compared to existing weakly-supervised methods. The CIFAR-10 results further demonstrate the method’s generalizability.
*   **Addressing Adversarial Artifacts:** The careful consideration and mitigation of adversarial artifacts (Section 3.1) is a notable strength. The techniques described (blurring, random noise, TV penalty) are well-motivated and likely contribute to the quality of the saliency maps.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the key ideas accessible. The use of figures to illustrate concepts like SSR and SDR is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While the authors mention different choices for the alternative image *A* in the evidence removal function, a more comprehensive ablation study exploring the impact of each component of the objective function (TV penalty, regularization terms, choice of *A*) would be valuable.  Understanding the contribution of each term would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Dependence on Pre-trained Encoder:** The reliance on a pre-trained ResNet encoder (for ImageNet experiments) raises the question of how much of the performance is due to the learned features of the encoder versus the U-Net’s ability to learn the saliency map.  While the CIFAR-10 experiment mitigates this to some extent, a more detailed analysis of the learned weights within the U-Net would be insightful.
*   **Saliency Metric Limitations:** The proposed saliency metric, while conceptually sound, is tied to the performance of the *underlying* black-box classifier. A good saliency map should, ideally, be interpretable *regardless* of the classifier’s imperfections.  This dependence could potentially skew the results.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The number of hyperparameters (especially in the objective function – 1, 2, 3, 4) might require substantial tuning for different black-box classifiers and datasets. The paper doesn't explicitly discuss this tuning process.
* **Presentation of Results**: The presentation of the results are somewhat crowded, especially tables 1, 2 and 3.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to real-time saliency detection.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate competitive performance.
*   The authors address a relevant and important problem in the field of interpretable machine learning.
*   The consideration of adversarial artifacts is a thoughtful addition.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The dependence on a pre-trained encoder and the limited ablation study weaken the analysis.
*   The saliency metric is tied to the black-box classifier’s performance and might not be a universally applicable measure of saliency quality.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty, speed, and performance of the proposed method are compelling. The authors address an important problem and present a well-reasoned and technically sound solution. The weaknesses are not critical and could be addressed in future work. This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning and is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.



**Rating:**  Strong Accept (7/10)
