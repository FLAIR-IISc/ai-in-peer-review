

# Review

This paper presents a novel approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers by training a model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass rather than using iterative optimization methods. The core idea is compelling: instead of spending hundreds of iterations to compute saliency maps for each image, the authors train a masking model (based on U-Net architecture) that directly outputs high-quality saliency masks. The paper introduces a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept and addresses the "introduced evidence" problem through a randomized evidence removal scheme.

The paper stands out for its bold conceptual shift in the saliency detection field. While most prior work focuses on iterative methods (Zhou et al., Cao et al., Fong and Vedaldi), this paper proposes a paradigm where saliency prediction becomes a supervised learning problem. This approach enables real-time performance (100 masks/sec on standard GPU) and produces sharper, more accurate saliency maps than existing methods. The authors validate their method on ImageNet with three different black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and show competitive results in weakly supervised object localization, achieving 36.7% error rate (vs. 34.3% for fully supervised VGG). The new metric s(a,p) = log(a) - log(p) provides a more meaningful evaluation than standard localization accuracy by directly measuring the information concentration in the salient region.

The novelty of this work is significant. The authors successfully transform a time-consuming process into a single forward pass, opening doors to real-time applications like video saliency. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a fundamental rethinking of how saliency detection can be approached. The paper's strength lies in this conceptual leap rather than methodological perfection, which aligns well with NIPS's appreciation for bold, novel ideas.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong submission. The most critical issue is the lack of clarity about how the training data is generated for the masking model. Since the black-box classifier is treated as a "black box," it's unclear how the authors obtain training examples. The paper mentions "we train a separate masking model" for each black box classifier, but doesn't explain how they generate the ground truth saliency masks needed for training. Without this crucial detail, the proposed method appears to be self-contradictory—the black-box nature is undermined by the need to interact with the classifier during training.

The paper also fails to adequately distinguish itself from the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2], which similarly optimizes saliency masks in image space without needing gradient access to the classifier. The authors mention this work but don't explain how their approach differs significantly. The training process description is vague, particularly regarding the "fake class" technique used to prevent the model from ignoring class selectors.

The new metric s(a,p) lacks strong theoretical grounding. The paper claims it "can be seen as the relative amount of information between an indicator variable with probability p and an indicator variable with probability a," but this interpretation is not rigorously justified. The metric's value range (negative values being better) is also confusing without proper normalization.

The experiments, while demonstrating the approach's potential, are somewhat limited. The CIFAR-10 results are presented but without sufficient comparison to recent saliency methods. The paper also doesn't compare directly with other recent state-of-the-art saliency detection methods beyond the weakly supervised object localization metric.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper's core idea is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance. The conceptual shift from iterative computation to model-based prediction is bold and valuable, even if the implementation details need more refinement. The real-time capability opens up practical applications that previous methods couldn't support. The authors' claim that their method "produces higher quality saliency masks and achieves better localisation results" is supported by their experiments, even if the comparison to the parallel work [2] is not fully resolved.

Strengths:
- Bold conceptual shift from iterative to model-based saliency prediction
- Real-time performance (100 masks/sec) enabling new applications
- New saliency metric that better aligns with SSR concept
- Competitive results on weakly supervised object localization

Weaknesses:
- Unclear training data generation process for black-box setting
- Insufficient differentiation from parallel work [2]
- Theoretical justification for new metric is weak
- Limited comparison to recent saliency methods

This paper presents a promising new direction in saliency detection that could significantly impact the field. While the implementation details need more clarity, the core idea is sufficiently novel and significant to merit acceptance at NIPS. The paper's value lies in its conceptual contribution rather than methodological perfection, which aligns with the conference's emphasis on bold, novel approaches. With minor clarifications on the training process and better differentiation from related work, this could be an excellent contribution.

Recommendation: Accept
