**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate, model-based saliency detector (a U-Net architecture) to predict a saliency mask for a given image and class in a single feed-forward pass. This contrasts with existing perturbation-based methods that iteratively optimize a mask for each input image, a process that is computationally expensive. The authors formulate a composite objective function for training the saliency model, aiming to find masks that are smooth, small, and simultaneously satisfy criteria for being a "Smallest Sufficient Region" (SSR) and a "Smallest Destroying Region" (SDR). The proposed method is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, demonstrating significant speed advantages (real-time performance) and achieving state-of-the-art results on weakly supervised object localization. The paper also introduces a new metric for evaluating saliency based on classification confidence within a tightly cropped salient region.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution—amortizing the expensive iterative search for a saliency mask into a single forward pass of a trained network—is a significant and practical idea. Achieving real-time performance opens up new applications for saliency methods, such as in video analysis, which is a compelling motivation.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method demonstrates impressive results on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, outperforming prior art and even approaching the performance of some fully supervised methods. This provides strong evidence that the generated masks are meaningful.
3.  **Model-Agnostic Approach:** At inference time, the method treats the classifier as a black box, which is a desirable property. The approach is general and can be applied to different underlying classifier architectures, as demonstrated by the experiments with AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is promising, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its methodology, presentation, and clarity, which collectively undermine its contribution and readiness for publication.

1.  **Methodological Opacity and Ad-Hoc Choices:**
    *   **Misleading "Black-Box" Claim:** The training process requires full backpropagation through the target classifier to compute the objective function in Eq. 4. This is fundamentally a white-box (or at least "differentiable-box") requirement during training. The claim of being a "black box" method is only true at inference time and should be stated with much greater precision throughout the paper.
    *   **Unjustified New Metric:** The proposed saliency metric in Eq. 3, `s(a, p) = log(ã) / log(p)`, is poorly motivated. The "information theory" interpretation is mentioned casually without any formal derivation or justification. The choice of a `log/log` form is not explained, nor are its properties analyzed. The area threshold `ã = max(a, 0.05)` is an arbitrary magic number introduced simply to "prevent instabilities," which is not a rigorous justification.
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The main objective function (Eq. 4) depends on four crucial hyperparameters (λ1-λ4). The paper provides the values used but offers no discussion on how they were selected or any analysis of the model's sensitivity to them. This is a critical omission for reproducibility.
    *   **Unusual Architectural Choices:** The non-standard nonlinearity in Eq. 8 for mask generation is justified only by the vague statement that "sigmoid and tanh nonlinearities did not optimise properly." This is insufficient for a top-tier publication. The authors should analyze *why* standard nonlinearities failed and provide a more principled reason for their proposed alternative.
    *   **Vague Training Details:** The training process includes several crucial but ill-defined steps. The use of "fake classes" is interesting, but the method for selecting them is not specified. More importantly, the paper mentions speeding up training by adding a constraint on the class embedding (ensuring `(X^T_ij C_s)` is high/low for correct/fake labels), which sounds like an additional loss term that is not formally included in the main objective function. This lack of precision is concerning.

2.  **Conceptual Issues:**
    *   The paper briefly mentions in the conclusion that the masking model "might have biases of its own" and leaves this for future work. This is a fundamental, not a minor, issue. The saliency model, which uses a pre-trained ResNet-50, has its own strong inductive biases about what constitutes an object or a salient feature. The resulting "explanation" is therefore a confounding of the target classifier's logic and the explainer's own biases. It is unclear if the model is truly explaining the target network or simply learning to be a good object segmenter that is loosely guided by the target's output score. This potential circularity is a major conceptual weakness that is not adequately addressed.

3.  **Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   **Formatting Errors:** The manuscript is littered with formatting problems. A stray line, "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017), Long Beach, CA, USA.", appears at the bottom of the first page. Equation numbering is duplicated: there are two distinct equations labeled as (1).
    *   **Inadequate Referencing:** The bibliography is of very poor quality and does not meet the standards of a NIPS submission. Many entries are incomplete, missing years, venues, or proper formatting (e.g., "[11] maps. CoRR", "[16] URL..."). This suggests a lack of care in the paper's preparation.
    *   **Missing Figures and Captions:** The provided text refers to six figures, but none are included, nor are their captions. Without these figures, crucial results and architectural details (e.g., Figure 4) cannot be assessed, making the paper incomplete.
    *   **Clarity and Writing Style:** The writing is at times informal (e.g., "Fighting the Introduced Evidence"). Key concepts, such as the exact mechanism of the feature filter (Eq. 7), could be explained more clearly.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a promising research direction, its execution is marred by a large number of minor and major flaws. The methodological description lacks the rigor and detail required for reproducibility and critical evaluation. The conceptual issue of the explainer's own bias is a significant weakness that is acknowledged but then dismissed. Finally, the poor state of the manuscript, with its numerous formatting errors and an incomplete bibliography, suggests that the work is premature and was not prepared with the care expected for a submission to NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea of a model-based, real-time saliency generator is novel and significant.
*   The empirical results on weakly supervised localization are very strong.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The "black-box" claim is misleading; training requires white-box access.
*   The new saliency metric and several key model components (objective hyperparameters, mask nonlinearity) are poorly justified and appear ad-hoc.
*   Critical training details are vague or omitted.
*   The conceptual problem of the explainer model's own bias is a major unresolved issue.
*   The manuscript is poorly formatted, with an unprofessional bibliography and missing figures, rendering it incomplete and difficult to fully evaluate.
