

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a new approach for real-time image saliency detection that trains a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, claiming to be orders-of-magnitude faster than iterative methods while producing higher quality maps. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region concept and claim their method outperforms existing techniques on ImageNet.

The paper builds on existing saliency detection work, particularly referencing the parallel work of Fong and Vedaldi [2] which also proposes an optimization-based method. However, the paper fails to adequately differentiate its approach from [2], particularly since the authors note their method is "developed in parallel to this work." The proposed masking model appears to be a straightforward application of a U-Net architecture to predict saliency maps, with minimal theoretical justification for why this architecture is particularly suited for the task.

The main weaknesses of the paper are significant. First, the claim of being "model agnostic" is misleading, as the method requires training a separate masking model for each specific black-box classifier, contradicting the black-box assumption. Second, the paper's speed claims are unsubstantiated with concrete timing comparisons against relevant baselines. The paper states the method processes "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second" but doesn't provide comparison numbers to iterative methods like those in [1] or [2].

The proposed saliency metric (s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p)) is problematic. The authors claim it better assesses saliency than localization accuracy, but they provide no theoretical justification for this claim. The metric appears to be a simple rephrasing of existing concepts without clear advantages. The paper also fails to establish how their metric correlates with human perception of saliency, which is a critical aspect of saliency evaluation.

The experimental evaluation is insufficient. The baselines used (Center box and Max box) are extremely weak and not representative of current state-of-the-art methods. The paper compares against "iteratively optimised saliency masks" without naming specific methods or providing comparison numbers. The reported localisation error of 36.7% for ResNet-50 is not compared against the most recent methods in the field, making it difficult to assess the significance of their improvement.

The paper also contains methodological concerns. The approach to handling "introduced evidence" by randomly selecting between blurred images or random noise for the alternative image A is not theoretically justified. The claim that this prevents adversarial artifacts lacks empirical evidence. Furthermore, the paper's claim that "the model can still learn to develop adversarial masks" is acknowledged but not properly addressed.

The paper's significance is questionable. While real-time saliency detection is valuable, the paper doesn't demonstrate how their approach solves a critical problem that existing methods can't address with minor modifications. The paper's claim of "new applications such as video-saliency" is speculative without any concrete evidence or experiments.

The paper has some strengths: the U-Net architecture is well-known and appropriate for the task, and the concept of using a single forward pass for saliency detection is conceptually sound. The paper also provides a clear description of their objective function.

However, the weaknesses significantly outweigh these strengths. The paper lacks rigorous comparison with the most relevant existing methods, the new metric is poorly justified, the "model agnostic" claim is misleading, and the experimental evaluation is inadequate. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of being "orders-of-magnitude faster" or producing "higher quality" saliency masks.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses an interesting problem, it fails to provide sufficient evidence for its claims and doesn't demonstrate significant advancement over existing work. The methodological shortcomings and inadequate evaluation prevent me from recommending acceptance at NIPS. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons with state-of-the-art methods, stronger theoretical justification for the proposed metric, and clearer differentiation from the parallel work [2].

I recommend rejection. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the following:
1. Provide concrete timing comparisons with iterative methods
2. Compare against more relevant state-of-the-art baselines
3. Justify the new saliency metric more rigorously
4. Clarify the "model agnostic" claim
5. Address the potential for the masking model to develop its own adversarial artifacts
