This paper proposes a learned saliency detection method, moving away from iterative approaches. The core idea – training a model to predict saliency maps – is conceptually sound and addresses a practical limitation of existing techniques. However, the paper is riddled with minor, yet cumulatively substantial, flaws in methodology, presentation, and justification, leading to a negative assessment.

The introduction establishes the motivation well, highlighting the shortcomings of current classifiers and the need for interpretable saliency maps. However, the leap to proposing a *learned* saliency detector feels somewhat abrupt. A more thorough discussion of why existing learned approaches (if any) are inadequate would strengthen this transition. The claim of “orders-of-magnitude faster” requires more rigorous benchmarking beyond what’s presented later.

The related work section is adequate but suffers from inconsistencies. Citations [5] and subsequent ones referencing works from 2012-2017 feel oddly weighted; a more balanced overview of the broader saliency detection landscape is needed. The description of Fong & Vedaldi [2] is accurate but overly brief, missing opportunities to contrast it more sharply with the proposed method.

Section 3 introduces the concepts of SSR and SDR. While intuitively understandable, the definitions are somewhat imprecise. The example with the seal is helpful, but the assertion that a human would find it difficult to recognize the preserved image is subjective and weakens the argument. The discussion of “introduced evidence” is crucial, but becomes convoluted. Equation (1) is trivial and adds little value. The reasoning behind the choice of ‘A’ in Equation (2) feels ad-hoc. While the authors acknowledge the potential for adversarial artifacts, the proposed solutions (blurring, noise) appear insufficiently robust and lack theoretical justification. The discussion on the TV penalty is perfunctory.

The saliency metric, presented in Section 3.2, is novel, but its reliance on resizing crops irrespective of aspect ratio is a significant weakness. The authors acknowledge this, but dismiss it too readily. This introduces a confounding factor and potentially biases the results. The interpretation connecting the metric to information theory feels forced and doesn't add substantial insight.

Section 4 details the masking model. The U-Net architecture is reasonable, but the justification for specific choices (e.g., feature filter, two-channel output, non-standard nonlinearity) is weak. The training process section reveals several concerning details: the use of a "fake label" feels like a workaround rather than a principled solution, and the random generation of 'A' raises concerns about reproducibility and controlled experimentation.

The experimental section (Section 5) is the most problematic. The ImageNet experiments lack sufficient detail. What specific parameters were used for training the black-box classifiers? A thorough ablation study demonstrating the impact of each component of the objective function (Equation 4) is missing. Table 1 shows improved localization accuracy, but the gains over existing methods are modest, particularly given the complexity of the proposed approach. The claim of outperforming other approaches in Table 2 is undermined by the specific baselines chosen ("Center" is a weak comparator). Table 3 reinforces the issue – the gains in the saliency metric are marginal. The CIFAR-10 experiment is a welcome addition but suffers from the same lack of detailed methodology.

Throughout the paper, the writing quality is uneven. The numerous equations, while mathematically correct, are often introduced without sufficient explanation. Figures are helpful but lack clear captions and sufficient explanation in the main text. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased or grammatically incorrect.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The core idea of learning saliency maps is promising.
*   The new saliency metric is a potential contribution, despite its flaws.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The methodology is riddled with questionable design choices and lacks rigorous justification.
*   The experimental results are incremental and don't convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.
*   The writing quality is inconsistent and hampers clarity.
*   The paper feels incomplete; many aspects are treated superficially.
*   The overall contribution, despite the initial promise, is not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the paper explores an interesting direction, the numerous methodological weaknesses, lack of thorough experimentation, and uneven presentation preclude acceptance. The authors need to significantly refine their approach, provide more compelling evidence, and improve the clarity and rigor of their work before it would be suitable for NIPS.
