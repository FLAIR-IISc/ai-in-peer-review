

# Review: Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers

This paper proposes a new approach for real-time image saliency detection for black box classifiers, claiming to produce high-quality saliency maps orders of magnitude faster than iterative methods. The authors train a masking model (based on U-Net architecture) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass rather than using iterative optimization. They introduce a new metric (s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p)) to evaluate saliency maps and report improved performance on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.

The paper makes several strong claims that require substantial evidence to support. First, the authors claim their method is "orders of magnitude faster" than iterative approaches. However, they provide no concrete timing comparisons between their method and existing approaches, making this claim unsubstantiated. They mention processing "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU" but fail to compare this against existing methods' inference times.

Second, the paper claims their method "produces higher quality saliency masks and achieves better localisation results" than existing approaches. While they report lower localisation errors (36.7% for ResNet-50) compared to the baseline "Center" method (41.2% error), they fail to provide a comprehensive comparison against the most recent state-of-the-art saliency methods. For instance, they cite Fong & Vedaldi [2] as "very recently proposed" but don't provide a detailed comparison with this parallel work, which appears to be the closest competitor.

The proposed saliency metric (s(a,p)) seems to be a simple adaptation of existing localisation metrics without sufficient theoretical justification. The paper claims this metric better assesses saliency quality than standard localisation accuracy, but provides no rigorous analysis to support this claim. The metric's interpretation as "the relative amount of information between an indicator variable with probability p and an indicator variable with probability a" is vague and lacks theoretical grounding.

The paper also lacks critical ablation studies to understand which components of their approach are most important. For example, they don't evaluate the impact of their proposed evidence removal strategy (equation 2) versus simpler methods like masking with constant color. They also don't investigate how sensitive their results are to the choice of hyperparameters (1, 2, 3, 4 in equation 4).

The claim that their method "is able to produce real-time saliency maps, enabling new applications such as video-saliency" is speculative without concrete demonstrations. The paper doesn't provide any video-saliency examples or benchmarks to support this application claim.

The paper's technical approach is not significantly different from recent work. The masking model architecture (U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) is a standard approach, and the objective function (equation 4) builds on existing iterative optimization methods like [1] and [2]. The paper's key contribution appears to be applying a standard neural network architecture to predict saliency maps rather than optimizing them iteratively, but this is a relatively small step from existing work.

The paper's evaluation is also limited in scope. They only test on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, which are standard datasets, but fail to provide analysis on more challenging benchmarks or diverse image classes. The CIFAR-10 experiment (section 5.3) is particularly weak since they train the masking model from scratch without any pre-training, which is a significant limitation given the small dataset size.

While the paper addresses an important problem in model interpretability and presents a reasonable approach, it falls short of providing the rigorous validation expected for a NIPS submission. The claims about performance improvements are not sufficiently supported by evidence, the comparison to the most relevant recent work is inadequate, and the proposed metric lacks theoretical justification.

The paper would benefit from:
1. Comprehensive timing comparisons showing "orders of magnitude" speedup
2. Detailed comparison against Fong & Vedaldi [2] and other recent methods
3. Ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components
4. More rigorous justification of the new metric
5. Concrete demonstrations of real-time applications (e.g., video-saliency examples)

Given these significant gaps in evidence and validation, I am unable to recommend acceptance. While the problem is important and the approach has some merit, the paper fails to provide the rigorous empirical validation required to support its claims. The authors present a new method but don't sufficiently demonstrate that it represents a meaningful advancement over existing techniques.

Strengths: Addresses important problem in model interpretability, reasonable architecture choice, introduces a new metric.

Weaknesses: Unsubstantiated performance claims, inadequate comparison to recent work, weak justification for new metric, lack of ablation studies, limited evaluation scope.

The paper would be suitable for a venue with lower standards for empirical validation, but does not meet the high standards expected for NIPS. It represents an incremental contribution that would benefit from more thorough validation and comparison against the most recent state-of-the-art methods.
