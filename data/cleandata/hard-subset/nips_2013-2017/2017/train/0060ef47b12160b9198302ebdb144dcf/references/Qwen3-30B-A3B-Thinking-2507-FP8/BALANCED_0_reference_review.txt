

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, addressing a significant limitation of existing methods that require iterative optimization for each image. The authors train a U-Net-based model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, claiming orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over iterative methods while producing higher-quality saliency masks. The paper introduces a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept and demonstrates competitive performance on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.

## Strengths

The paper makes several valuable contributions. First, the proposed method is genuinely practical for real-time applications, which is a significant advancement over existing approaches that require hundreds of iterations per image. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their model achieves 100+ saliency maps per second on standard hardware, enabling applications like video saliency that were previously infeasible.

Second, the new saliency metric (s(a,p)) is well-motivated and addresses a key limitation of existing evaluation methods. By directly measuring the smallest crop that preserves classification confidence, it better captures the essence of saliency than traditional localisation accuracy metrics. The metric's performance on the ImageNet dataset (with their method approaching ground-truth bounding box quality) is particularly compelling.

Third, the paper demonstrates strong empirical results. The proposed method achieves 36.7% localisation error on ImageNet, outperforming other weakly supervised approaches and approaching the performance of fully supervised methods. The ablation of different black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) provides useful insights into how model architecture affects saliency generation.

## Weaknesses

The primary weakness is the insufficient contrast with the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi (2017), which the authors mention but don't adequately distinguish from their own approach. The paper states that their method differs by including "the third term of our objective function" (SSR), but the description of Fong & Vedaldi's method is too brief to understand the actual technical differences. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper's claim of novelty.

Second, while the paper claims "orders-of-magnitude faster" performance, it lacks a concrete computational complexity analysis. The authors should provide actual runtime comparisons with existing methods (e.g., time per image for both iterative and their approach) to substantiate this claim.

Third, the paper would benefit from a more thorough ablation study. The authors propose an objective function with four terms (smoothness, area penalty, SSR, SDR), but there's no analysis of how each term contributes to the final results. This would help readers understand which components are most critical.

Fourth, the paper doesn't sufficiently address limitations. For instance, while the authors claim "model agnostic" capability, their method requires the black-box classifier to be differentiable, which excludes many practical classifiers (e.g., tree-based models). The paper also lacks discussion of failure cases, particularly for images with multiple salient objects.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI, with clear practical benefits for real-time applications. The proposed method is technically sound, with thorough experiments and a well-motivated new metric. The work advances the state of the art in speed and quality of saliency detection, particularly for applications requiring real-time processing.

However, the insufficient contrast with the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi (2017) and the lack of detailed computational analysis prevent this from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS. The paper would be significantly improved by:

1. Providing a detailed comparison with Fong and Vedaldi's method, highlighting technical differences and advantages
2. Including actual runtime measurements for different methods
3. Adding an ablation study of the objective function components
4. Discussing limitations more thoroughly, particularly regarding model differentiability requirements

The paper's strengths in practical applicability and the new metric are compelling, but these weaknesses need to be addressed for the paper to meet NeurIPS's high standards.

## Recommendation

Acceptance Recommendation: Weak Accept

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of explainable AI with clear practical applications. While the technical approach is sound and the results are compelling, the insufficient contrast with parallel work and lack of detailed computational analysis are significant concerns. With the suggested revisions, this paper would be an excellent fit for NeurIPS.
