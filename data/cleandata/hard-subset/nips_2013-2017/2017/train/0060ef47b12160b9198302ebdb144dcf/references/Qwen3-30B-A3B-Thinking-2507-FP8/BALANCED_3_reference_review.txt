

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection for black box classifiers, proposing a model-based method that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass rather than through iterative optimization. The authors develop a U-Net based masking model that learns to generate high-quality saliency maps for any black box classifier. They also introduce a new saliency metric that better evaluates the interpretability of saliency maps by measuring how well a classifier can recognize the object from a cropped salient region. This work builds on previous saliency methods such as gradient-based approaches [11,12,16], iterative patch removal [17], and optimization-based methods [2], but addresses limitations in speed and quality.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-structured objective function that effectively addresses key challenges in saliency detection, particularly the problem of introduced evidence when masking images. The proposed solution using random alternative images and total variation regularization is thoughtful and well-justified.

2. **Novel Contributions**: The core idea of training a model to predict saliency maps rather than using iterative optimization is compelling. The new saliency metric (Section 3.2) is a significant contribution that better captures the conceptual definition of saliency as the smallest sufficient region than existing localisation accuracy metrics.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The method achieves state-of-the-art weakly supervised object localization results (36.7% error rate on ImageNet, compared to 34.3% for fully supervised VGG) without using localization data during training. The new metric also demonstrates that their method's saliency quality is comparable to ground truth bounding boxes.

4. **Practical Impact**: The demonstrated speed (100+ saliency masks per second) makes the approach suitable for real-time applications like video saliency, which is a significant practical contribution.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Parallel Work**: The paper acknowledges that a similar optimization-based method [2] was developed in parallel, but doesn't sufficiently distinguish their approach. The key difference (training a model vs. iterative optimization per image) isn't clearly articulated in the context of the broader literature. A more detailed comparison with [2] would strengthen the novelty claim.

2. **Limited Evaluation of Model-Agnosticity**: While the paper claims their method is model-agnostic, they only test with CNN classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet). It would strengthen the claim to demonstrate results with non-CNN classifiers or with classifiers trained on different tasks.

3. **Lack of Concrete Speed Benchmarks**: The claim of "real-time" performance is unsubstantiated without specific speed metrics (e.g., milliseconds per image) compared to iterative methods. A table comparing speed with other approaches would be valuable.

4. **Training Process Clarity**: The training process with "fake labels" is described but could be more clearly explained. The mechanism by which this prevents the model from ignoring class selectors needs more detail.

5. **Metric Justification**: While the new metric is well-motivated, its theoretical foundation could be strengthened. The connection to information theory (log(a) - log(p)) is intriguing but not thoroughly explained.

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper with significant practical contributions to the field of explainable AI. The method addresses a real need for fast, high-quality saliency detection and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches. The new metric is particularly valuable for evaluating saliency methods. The paper meets the quality standard with sound technical contributions and thorough experiments.

The main concerns relate to the insufficient differentiation from parallel work [2] and the lack of concrete speed metrics to support the "real-time" claim. These are fixable issues that could be addressed in a revision.

## Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of model interpretability with a novel, practical approach to saliency detection. The technical soundness, strong results, and clear practical implications outweigh the identified weaknesses, which can be addressed in a revision.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Add a detailed comparison table showing speed metrics (ms per image) for the proposed method versus iterative approaches like [2] and [17].

2. Expand the discussion of how this work differs from [2] by explicitly comparing the optimization approach versus the model-based approach in terms of computational complexity and practical applicability.

3. Include results with a non-CNN classifier to better demonstrate the model-agnostic property.

4. Provide more detail on the training process with fake labels, including a clear explanation of how this prevents the model from ignoring class selectors.

5. Strengthen the theoretical justification of the new saliency metric, particularly the information-theoretic interpretation.

6. Consider adding a discussion of potential limitations, such as whether the method works well for highly ambiguous images where multiple interpretations are possible.
