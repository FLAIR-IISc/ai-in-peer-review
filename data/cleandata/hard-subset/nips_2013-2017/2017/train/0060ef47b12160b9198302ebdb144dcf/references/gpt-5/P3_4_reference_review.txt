Summary and contributions
The paper proposes an amortized, feed-forward approach to perturbation-based saliency for image classifiers. Instead of optimizing a mask per image (as in Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), the authors train a U-Net-style “masking model” that, given an image and a target class, outputs a saliency mask in one pass. The training objective combines: (i) smoothness (TV), (ii) small area (AV), (iii) recognizability from the preserved region (SSR-like), and (iv) suppressibility when the region is removed (SDR-like). To mitigate “introduced evidence” and adversarial artifacts, the method uses randomized replacement images A (strong blur or colored noise) and a TV penalty. A new saliency metric is proposed: take the tightest rectangle covering the mask, feed only that crop to the classifier, and compute s(a,p)=log(ã)/log(p), arguing that smaller crops with high confidence are better. The method is evaluated on ImageNet with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 as fixed “black boxes,” reporting improved weakly supervised localization error and better scores under the proposed metric; a CIFAR-10 variant trained from scratch is also shown.

Relation to prior work
The work sits between gradient/backprop attribution methods (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop, CAM) and optimization-based perturbation methods (Cao et al.; Fong & Vedaldi). The novelty is amortizing perturbation-based saliency with a trainable mask-predictor network. The paper compares to gradient-based baselines and iterative masking, but omits comparisons to Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) and Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017), both contemporary and widely used.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: amortizing optimization-based saliency via a learned predictor is elegant and practically useful (orders of magnitude speedup).
- Careful design to reduce artifacts: randomized replacement schemes, TV, and downsampled-upsampled masks address known pitfalls of perturbation-based explanations.
- Empirical breadth: trained against three distinct classifiers; qualitative saliency maps are sharp; weakly supervised localization results are competitive with fully supervised VGG.
- Practicality: >100 fps on 224×224 makes real-time use (and potential video) plausible.
- Clear articulation of SSR vs SDR and a training objective that targets both.

Weaknesses and issues
- “Black-box” claim: Training requires backpropagation through the classifier (differentiable fc). This is not black-box in the usual sense (no gradients). The method is compatible with any differentiable fixed classifier, but not model-agnostic in the strict sense. Please adjust claims and title accordingly.
- Objective sign error: Eq. (4) adds +λ3 log fc(Φ(X,M)) while minimizing L, which would push fc→0. To encourage recognizability, it should be −λ3 log fc(·), or equivalently add a negative sign. As written, the text and equation conflict.
- Metric definition appears ill-posed: s(a,p)=log(ã)/log(p) is minimized when p is small (since log(p)→−∞), contrary to the stated SSR desire (high p, small a). The behavior of this ratio is counterintuitive: for fixed a<1, increasing p toward 1 makes |log(p)| small and s→−∞/+∞ depending on signs. The text claims “low value is good,” but the formula promotes lower p. This needs correction (e.g., −log p vs log p, invert ratio, or use a composite like −log p + α log a). Also, the reduction to a tight rectangle disadvantages multi-part objects and introduces background; discuss this bias.
- Fairness of comparisons: Missing comparisons to Grad-CAM and Integrated Gradients. Grad-CAM in particular is the de facto baseline for class-conditional, localization-friendly saliency. Without them, it is hard to assess significance.
- Evaluation protocol choices: Localization derived from thresholding at 0.5 and taking the tightest box; sensitivity to threshold is not reported. The new metric depends on classifier invariance to aspect ratio/scale, which is often violated; no sensitivity analysis is shown. The use of the ground-truth class as selector during evaluation can mask failure modes when a model’s internal evidence differs from the target class.
- Potential overfitting and bias: The masking model uses a fixed ImageNet-pretrained ResNet encoder and is trained on ImageNet labels; it may learn dataset priors rather than the specific black-box’s idiosyncrasies. The CIFAR-10 experiment is helpful but small-scale; no cross-dataset generalization is shown.
- Missing ablations: No study of λ weights, TV strength, fake-class probability, or replacement A choices. No quantitative analysis of adversarial artifacts reduction.
- Clarity and details: Some training tricks are described informally (e.g., “ensuring max X^T C_s is high for correct labels”) without a formal loss term. The final mask nonlinearity (abs-based two-channel) is ad hoc; an explanation of why it helps would be useful.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but the apparent sign errors in the objective and metric need correction. Experimental results are promising but lack key baselines and ablations. The claims around “black-box/model-agnostic” are overstated.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. However, equations (3) and (4) conflict with the narrative; please fix. Provide more explicit training losses for the class-embedding trick and specify all hyperparameters. Include more quantitative tables in the main text.

Originality
The amortized perturbation approach is novel and timely. The combination of SSR/SDR in training is sensible. The metric idea (crop-based SSR proxy) is interesting but currently flawed.

Significance
If validated against stronger baselines and with corrected objectives/metric, this could be a practical step forward for fast, class-conditional saliency and weakly supervised localization.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical and conceptually appealing amortization of perturbation-based saliency
- Strong qualitative saliency and competitive localization with real-time performance
- Thoughtful mitigation of introduced evidence/adversarial artifacts

Cons for acceptance
- Incorrect signs in objective and proposed metric as written
- Overstated “black-box”/model-agnostic claims
- Missing key baselines (Grad-CAM, Integrated Gradients) and ablations
- Metric design and evaluation biases not fully justified

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify and correct Eq. (4) and Eq. (3); report results with the corrected formulations.
- Add Grad-CAM and Integrated Gradients baselines (localization and your metric).
- Provide ablations on λ’s, TV, fake-class probability, and replacement schemes; report sensitivity to mask threshold.
- Evaluate multi-object images and class selectors for non-present classes to probe specificity.
- Discuss generalization to classifiers not trained on ImageNet and to different domains; try cross-dataset tests.
- Calibrate “real-time” at batch size 1 and report latency.

Overall
Borderline accept if the objective/metric issues are corrected and stronger baselines/ablations are added; otherwise, as is, the methodological and evaluation gaps limit confidence in the claims despite a promising idea and encouraging results.
