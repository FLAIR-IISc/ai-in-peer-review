

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a new approach for real-time image saliency detection using a trainable masking model that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass. The authors argue that existing methods are either slow (iterative approaches) or produce low-quality explanations (gradient-based methods), and they claim their approach is both fast and high-quality. The paper introduces two formal definitions of saliency (SSR and SDR), proposes a new metric for evaluating saliency maps, and presents a U-Net-based masking model trained to minimize an objective function that combines mask smoothness, region size, and classification performance.

## Strengths
The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field of interpretability for deep learning models. The proposed saliency metric is novel and addresses a gap in the literature by focusing on the interpretability of the saliency region rather than just localization accuracy. The experiments on ImageNet with multiple black-box classifiers are comprehensive, and the results show that the method outperforms existing weakly supervised approaches. The training procedure with the "fake class" trick is clever and addresses a significant challenge in training such models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Contradiction in "model agnostic" claim**: The paper repeatedly claims to be "model agnostic," yet the proposed method requires training a separate masking model for each black-box classifier. The paper states "we train a model to predict such a map for any input image in a single feed-forward pass," but this is only true for a fixed classifier. For a different classifier, a new model must be trained, which contradicts the model-agnostic claim. This is a critical flaw in the paper's main contribution.

2. **Methodological inconsistencies in the objective function**: Equation (4) defines the objective function as L(M) = 1/TV(M) + 2*AV(M) - log(fc((X,M))) + 3*fc((X,1-M)) - 4. The negative sign before the log term is confusing because it encourages the classifier to still recognize the class from the preserved region (which is correct), but the positive sign before the last term encourages the classifier to recognize the class after removing the salient region (which is incorrect). This needs clear justification.

3. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper uses "localisation accuracy" in the text but presents "localisation error" in Table 1. These are inverses of each other, creating confusion for readers. The paper should consistently use one term.

4. **Technical details missing**: The paper states "the mask Ms has resolution four times lower than the input image and has to be upsampled by a factor of four with bilinear resize," but doesn't justify why bilinear resize was chosen over other methods like bicubic or nearest neighbor. This is a minor but important technical detail.

5. **Inadequate explanation of "random alternative image A"**: In section 4.1, the paper states that "the alternative image A is randomly generated every time the function is called," but provides no details about the distribution or implementation. How is the random choice made? Is it truly random or deterministic? This affects reproducibility.

6. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The header states "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" which is grammatically incorrect (should be "2017 Conference"). The references have inconsistent formatting (some with DOIs, some with URLs, some without).

7. **Lack of statistical significance**: The paper claims their method outperforms baselines, but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the results, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements are meaningful.

8. **Inconsistent use of "black box"**: The paper claims to treat the classifier as a black box, but the training process requires access to the classifier's output (softmax probabilities). This is not truly black-box since the method needs to query the classifier for training.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a potentially useful approach to real-time saliency detection, the methodological inconsistencies and contradictions in the core claims undermine its contribution. The "model agnostic" claim is particularly problematic and contradicts the described methodology. The paper would benefit from significant revision to address these issues, particularly clarifying the model-agnostic nature of the approach and justifying the objective function's formulation.

The paper's technical quality is good but marred by these critical inconsistencies. The new saliency metric is a strong contribution, but it's overshadowed by the methodological flaws. The experiments are well-conducted but lack statistical significance testing.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper's core claim of being "model agnostic" is contradicted by the described methodology, which requires training a separate model for each classifier. This is a fundamental flaw that cannot be easily fixed in a revision. While the paper has some interesting ideas and the new metric is valuable, the methodological inconsistencies make it difficult to assess the true contribution of the work. The minor technical details and formatting issues further indicate a lack of attention to detail that would be expected in a NIPS paper.

The authors should reconsider their claims about model agnosticism and provide a more accurate description of the method's limitations. Without this, the paper does not make a strong enough contribution to justify acceptance at NIPS.
