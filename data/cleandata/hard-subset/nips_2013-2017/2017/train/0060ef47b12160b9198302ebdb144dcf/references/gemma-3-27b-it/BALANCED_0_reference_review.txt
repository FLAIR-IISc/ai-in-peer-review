This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, moving away from iterative methods towards a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea—training a model to directly predict saliency maps—is compelling and addresses a key bottleneck in existing techniques, namely computational cost. The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the need for faster and more accurate saliency methods, particularly in the context of understanding potentially flawed classifier behavior. The introduction does a good job of positioning the problem and relating it to prior work on adversarial examples and classifier interpretability (e.g., [6, 13]).

The related work section is comprehensive, covering gradient-based approaches [11, 12, 16], iterative removal methods [17, 1, 2], and contextualizes the authors' contribution within this landscape. The introduction of the SSR and SDR concepts (building on [2]) provides a useful framework for defining and evaluating saliency. The proposed saliency metric (s(a, p)) is a thoughtful attempt to capture both the localization accuracy and the preservation of class probability within the salient region, and the discussion of potential artifacts introduced by masking is insightful. The architecture of the masking model (U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) is reasonable and well-justified.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed approach of learning a saliency map directly is a significant departure from iterative methods and offers a substantial speed advantage.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The concepts are explained clearly, and the notation is consistent.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed objective function (L(M)) appears well-formulated, incorporating smoothness, area penalties, and classifier confidence. The training procedure is described in sufficient detail.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, both in terms of localization accuracy and the proposed saliency metric.  The comparisons with other techniques (including baselines like “Center” and iterative methods) are convincing. The ablation study on CIFAR-10 further strengthens the findings.
* **Practical Significance:** The real-time capability of the method opens up possibilities for applications like video saliency detection, as discussed in the Future Research section.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions several hyperparameters (e.g., λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4) and their chosen values. However, there is limited discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters.  A brief ablation study exploring the impact of different hyperparameter settings would be beneficial.
* **Masking Scheme Limitations:** The discussion of "fighting introduced evidence" is valuable, but the described techniques (e.g., adding a blurred background) feel somewhat ad-hoc. While effective, a more principled approach to minimizing unwanted evidence might be possible.
* **Metric Interpretation:**  While the proposed saliency metric `s(a, p)` is a good idea, its connection to information theory could be expanded upon. The explanation feels a little superficial, and a more rigorous justification would strengthen the argument.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper presents quantitative results, but lacks a qualitative error analysis. Showing examples of failure cases (e.g., images where the saliency map is misleading) would provide a more complete picture of the method's limitations.
* **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons to iterative methods are provided, a comparison to more recent and advanced saliency detection methods (beyond those explicitly cited in the Related Work) would be beneficial to fully establish the state-of-the-art performance. For example, newer attention mechanisms or transformer-based approaches could be relevant.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in the field of interpretability and explainable AI.
* The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and demonstrably effective.
* The experimental results are compelling and support the claims made by the authors.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The hyperparameter sensitivity and masking scheme limitations represent areas for improvement.
* The lack of extensive error analysis and comparison to the very latest techniques slightly weakens the evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of image saliency detection. The proposed method is novel, efficient, and performs well in comparison to existing techniques. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should consider performing a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, elaborating on the theoretical justification for the saliency metric, and adding a qualitative error analysis. A brief discussion of more recent saliency methods and a comparison would also strengthen the paper.
