This paper presents a compelling approach to real-time image saliency detection for black-box classifiers. The core idea – training a model to *predict* saliency maps rather than iteratively computing them – is genuinely novel and addresses a significant bottleneck in existing methods. While the presentation isn’t flawless and some experimental details feel slightly rushed, the boldness of the idea and its initial promising results warrant strong consideration.

The motivation is well-articulated: understanding *why* classifiers make decisions is crucial, and current saliency techniques are often too slow for practical applications like video analysis. The proposed solution, a U-Net architecture trained to directly output saliency masks, is elegant and, crucially, achieves the desired speed-up. The introduction of a new saliency metric based on the “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) concept is also a welcome addition and feels intuitively aligned with the goal of interpretable explanations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The shift from iterative saliency computation to a learned, feed-forward model is a significant conceptual leap. This isn't a minor tweak; it's a fundamentally different way of tackling the problem.
*   **Speed:** Achieving real-time performance is a major accomplishment and opens doors to applications previously inaccessible with slower methods.
*   **Strong Experimental Results:** The results on ImageNet, demonstrating competitive localization accuracy compared to other weakly supervised approaches and outperforming simpler baselines, are encouraging. The CIFAR-10 experiment further demonstrates the generalizability of the approach.
*   **New Saliency Metric:** The SSR-based metric provides a more nuanced evaluation of saliency maps than simple localization accuracy.
*   **Addressing Introduced Evidence:** The discussion and mitigation strategies for artifacts introduced during evidence removal show thoughtful consideration of potential pitfalls.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Eq. 4) feels somewhat ad-hoc, with several hyperparameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4) that aren't extensively justified. While the ablation studies would be valuable to understand the impact of each term, they are missing.
*   **Training Details:** The training process description, particularly the "fake label" trick, is a bit hand-wavy. A more detailed explanation of why this works and how the probability of 30% was chosen would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the paper demonstrates competitive results, a more thorough ablation study—exploring different network architectures, loss function components, and training strategies—would strengthen the claims.
*   **Black-Box Dependency:** While model-agnostic in the sense that it doesn't require access to classifier gradients, the performance *clearly* depends on the quality of the black-box classifier itself. This dependency isn’t fully explored, although the different classifier experiments start to address this.
*   **Figure Quality:** Some figures could be improved for clarity (e.g., Figure 3).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and impactful idea with the potential to significantly advance the field of explainable AI.
*   The results demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   The identified weaknesses, while present, are not fatal and do not overshadow the overall contribution.
*   The potential for real-time applications is compelling.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of thorough ablation studies leaves some questions unanswered.
*   The objective function could benefit from further justification and tuning.
*   The presentation, while generally clear, could be more polished in certain areas.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor flaws, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty of the approach, combined with its promising results and potential impact, outweighs the identified weaknesses. The paper presents a bold step forward in the field of saliency detection and offers a valuable contribution to the broader goal of building more interpretable and trustworthy AI systems. The authors have successfully laid the groundwork for a new class of fast and accurate saliency detectors. Future work building upon this foundation will undoubtedly be of significant interest to the NIPS community. I’d encourage the authors to address the weaknesses mentioned above in a future version or as a follow-up publication.
