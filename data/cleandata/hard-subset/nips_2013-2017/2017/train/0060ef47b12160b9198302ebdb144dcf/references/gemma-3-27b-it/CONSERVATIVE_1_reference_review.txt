This paper proposes a learned approach to saliency detection for image classifiers, aiming to overcome the slowness of iterative methods. The core idea – training a model to directly predict saliency maps – is straightforward. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient/destroying region (SSR/SDR) concepts and an objective function to train their model, incorporating smoothness and preservation of classification performance. They demonstrate results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, showing competitive performance in localization and a novel saliency metric.

While the problem addressed—efficient and accurate saliency detection—is relevant given the increasing need to understand black-box classifier behavior, and the proposed approach is conceptually sound, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing due to insufficient novelty and a lack of comprehensive comparison to established, albeit slower, methods. The claims of “orders-of-magnitude faster” are important, but need to be rigorously demonstrated *alongside* a much more thorough evaluation of quality.

The primary weakness lies in the limited and somewhat superficial comparison. While the authors compare to some existing saliency methods, the comparison mostly centers around a single metric (localization accuracy) and lacks a detailed analysis of the saliency *maps* themselves. Simply stating that their masks "clearly outperform" others based on figure 5 is insufficient. A proper visual comparison, with qualitative analysis of the generated maps, is critical. The evaluation of the proposed saliency metric is a positive step, but its correlation with human interpretability remains unestablished. Just because it aligns with ground truth bounding boxes doesn’t guarantee it reflects meaningful saliency.

The proposed objective function, while attempting to address the issue of introduced evidence, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The choice of terms and their weighting (1, 2, 3, 4) lacks strong justification. While the authors acknowledge the potential for adversarial artifacts, the mitigation strategies appear reactive rather than preventative. The “random scheme” for evidence removal is a reasonable attempt at robustness, but its effectiveness isn't thoroughly evaluated.

Furthermore, the architecture itself, a U-Net with a ResNet encoder, is a fairly standard choice. The novelty isn't in the architectural components, but in their application to this specific problem and the proposed objective function. This makes a strong demonstration of performance gains even more crucial. The CIFAR-10 experiment is positive, but doesn’t address concerns about the complexity of ImageNet. 

The paper also suffers from a degree of looseness in its presentation. The discussion of SSR and SDR, while conceptually useful, isn’t rigorously formalized, and the connection to the proposed metric could be made clearer. The frequent use of phrases like “seems to work well in practice” and “possibly” weakens the overall scientific rigor.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem.
*   Presents a conceptually sound approach to improving saliency detection efficiency.
*   Introduces a novel saliency metric.
*   Demonstrates promising initial results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Limited and superficial comparison to existing methods.
*   Lack of rigorous justification for the objective function and its parameters.
*   Insufficient demonstration of performance gains compared to slower, but established, methods (e.g., iterative removal approaches).
*   Weaknesses in the presentation and a lack of scientific rigor.
*   The novelty of the approach is incremental, requiring strong evidence of substantial improvement.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper presents a reasonable idea, it falls short of demonstrating sufficient novelty or convincingly showing significant improvements over existing techniques. The lack of a comprehensive evaluation and the somewhat ad-hoc nature of the objective function raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the approach. The authors need to provide a much more thorough comparison, a more rigorous justification for their design choices, and more compelling evidence of the benefits of their method to warrant acceptance at NIPS. I would be open to reconsideration if the authors significantly address these concerns in a revised submission.
