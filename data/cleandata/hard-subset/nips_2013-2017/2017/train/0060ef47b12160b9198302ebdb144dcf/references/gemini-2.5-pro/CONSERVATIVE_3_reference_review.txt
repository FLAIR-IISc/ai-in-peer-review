**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate, deep neural network (a "masking model" with a U-Net architecture) to predict a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This contrasts with existing perturbation-based methods that iteratively optimize a mask for each specific input, a process that is computationally expensive. The masking model is trained to minimize a composite objective function that encourages masks to be small and smooth, while also ensuring that the preserved region is sufficient for classification (Smallest Sufficient Region - SSR) and the removed region is critical for classification (Smallest Destroying Region - SDR). The authors also introduce a new saliency metric based on the classifier's performance on a tight crop around the salient region. The method is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, demonstrating state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization and showing qualitatively plausible saliency maps, all while operating in real-time.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel framing of saliency detection. Shifting from a per-instance iterative optimization to an amortized, model-based prediction is a significant conceptual contribution. If proven robust, this approach could make high-quality saliency analysis practical for real-time applications like video processing, which is a major advantage over slower, established methods.
2.  **Performance:** The reported performance on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet is impressive. Outperforming other methods, including iterative optimization techniques, provides strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach on this particular proxy task. The real-time processing speed (>100 images/sec) is a key practical achievement.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the method is described in sufficient detail, and the experiments are presented logically.

**Weaknesses**

My main reservations about this paper stem from its reliance on a new, complex methodology whose faithfulness to the black-box model is not rigorously established. The work presents an unproven approach without the extensive validation needed to justify its adoption over more direct, established techniques.

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Faithfulness:** The central premise is to use one complex model (the U-Net masking model) to explain another. This introduces a significant layer of abstraction and potential for error. How can we be certain that the masking model is faithfully capturing the reasoning of the target black-box classifier, rather than learning its own biases or exploiting spurious correlations from the training data? The paper suggests the model acts as a "regulariser," but this claim is not substantiated. The masking model might learn, for instance, to be a very good object segmenter for ImageNet classes, which would yield high localization scores but would not necessarily reflect the specific, and potentially non-intuitive, features the *target classifier* is using. This fundamental question of faithfulness is not adequately addressed.
2.  **Lack of Thorough Validation and Ablation:** The evaluation, while showing strong localization results, feels incomplete for a method proposing a new paradigm.
    *   The objective function (Eq. 4) contains four terms weighted by four hyperparameters (λ1-λ4). The paper provides the chosen values but offers no sensitivity analysis or ablation study. How critical is the exact balancing of these terms? The performance could be highly sensitive to these choices, which would undermine the method's robustness.
    *   The training process relies on several heuristics, such as the 30% fake label injection and the specific update rule for class embeddings. These feel ad-hoc and are not justified beyond the claim that they "make the training process work properly." This detracts from the principled nature of the approach.
    *   The comparison with the most similar iterative method [2] is not comprehensive enough. The paper claims the masks from [2] are "blurry, imprecise, and overfit," but this is a strong statement to base on a single figure. A more systematic visual and quantitative comparison across a range of examples is needed to firmly establish the superiority of the proposed method's mask quality.
3.  **Introduction of a New, Unproven Metric:** The paper introduces a new saliency metric (Eq. 3) and then uses it to demonstrate the strength of its own method (Table 3). While the metric is intuitively appealing, it is itself an unproven contribution. Its properties, potential biases (e.g., its dependence on classifiers robust to aspect ratio changes), and correlation with human judgment of explanation quality are unknown. Relying on a new metric to validate a new method is circular reasoning and less convincing than demonstrating strong performance on established, widely accepted benchmarks for explanation quality (if any existed, which highlights the difficulty of the field, but does not excuse the issue).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a genuinely novel and potentially paradigm-shifting approach to saliency detection (amortized prediction vs. per-instance optimization).
*   Achieves impressive empirical results on a standard proxy task (weakly supervised localization), outperforming prior work.
*   The real-time capability is a significant practical advantage that could enable new applications.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The fundamental premise of using a trained model to explain another model lacks rigorous verification of faithfulness. The method may not be explaining the target model but rather learning a related task.
*   The method's robustness is questionable due to a complex objective function with unanalyzed hyperparameters and several ad-hoc training procedures.
*   The evaluation relies in part on a new, unvalidated metric introduced in the same paper, which weakens the claims of interpretability and mask quality.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces an interesting and ambitious new direction for saliency map generation. The idea of learning a predictive model for explanations is novel, and the strong localization results and real-time performance are noteworthy. However, the work feels premature. The core methodological weakness is the unproven assumption that the learned masking model faithfully represents the behavior of the target classifier. This, combined with a lack of ablation studies for its complex objective and training scheme, and its reliance on a bespoke evaluation metric, leaves too many open questions about the method's soundness and reliability. While the engineering achievement is commendable, the paper does not yet provide the rigorous evidence and analysis required to be accepted as a new, established method for classifier explanation. I would encourage the authors to focus on experiments that directly measure faithfulness and to perform a more thorough analysis of their model's components before resubmission.
