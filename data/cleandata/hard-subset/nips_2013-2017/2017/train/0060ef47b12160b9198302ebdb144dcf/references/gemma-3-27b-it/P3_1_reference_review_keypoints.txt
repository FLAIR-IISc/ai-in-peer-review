- Tackles real-time saliency for black-box image classifiers using a learned feed-forward model.  
- Proposes a U-Net-based saliency predictor trained to find smallest sufficient and destroying regions with a novel objective.  
- Objective balances smoothness, sufficiency (SSR), destruction (SDR), and includes measures to mitigate adversarial artifacts.  
- Introduces a new saliency metric relating crop area to classification probability for assessing informativeness of regions.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance on ImageNet (localization and new metric) and generalization to CIFAR-10.  
- Provides a comprehensive and relevant related-work overview positioning the method against gradient and iterative masking approaches.  
- Novelty: learning a saliency detector enables real-time inference and potential video applications.  
- Well-defined loss function and explicit treatment of adversarial artifacts are important methodological contributions.  
- Clear presentation with informative figures and architecture diagrams.  
- Over-reliance on pre-trained ResNet-50 and U-Net backbone; limited exploration of alternative architectures.  
- Hyperparameter choices (loss coefficients) lack justification and sensitivity analysis.  
- Adversarial artifact mitigation may be insufficiently evaluated; subtle artifacts could still bias the metric.  
- Limited ablation studies — unclear contributions of individual loss terms and regularization strengths.  
- Missing discussion connecting the method to attention mechanisms and how it differs from them.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses key interpretability problem, presents a novel real-time approach, strong results, new metric, and good writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy reliance on established architectures, hyperparameter sensitivity, limited ablations, and residual adversarial concerns.  
- Overall assessment: strong, well-motivated paper recommended for acceptance (strongly consider) with requests to address hyperparameter sensitivity, ablations, and adversarial artifact analysis.
