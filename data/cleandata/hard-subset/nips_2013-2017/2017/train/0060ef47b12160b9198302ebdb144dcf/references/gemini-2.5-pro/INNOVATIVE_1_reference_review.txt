**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel, model-based approach for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. The central problem addressed is that existing high-quality saliency methods are iterative and computationally expensive, precluding real-time applications. In contrast, fast gradient-based methods often produce low-quality, noisy explanations. The authors' core contribution is to reframe saliency map generation as a prediction task. Instead of iteratively optimizing a mask for each input image, they train a deep neural network (a U-Net architecture) to directly predict a high-quality saliency map in a single forward pass. This "masking model" is trained to optimize a carefully designed objective function that encourages the predicted masks to be small, smooth, and effective at identifying both the "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) and the "smallest destroying region" (SDR) for the black-box classifier's decision. The approach is validated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on weakly supervised object localization and introducing a new metric to better evaluate the quality of saliency-based explanations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its conceptual leap. Moving from per-image, iterative optimization to an amortized, predictive model for saliency is a bold and highly original idea. It fundamentally changes how one can approach the problem of classifier explanation, opening a new and promising research direction. The potential impact is significant; by enabling high-quality saliency maps at over 100 FPS, this work makes real-time applications like video saliency not just possible, but practical. This could have major implications for interpreting models in safety-critical domains like autonomous driving.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The proposed method is not just a novel idea; it is backed by impressive results. Outperforming existing weakly supervised methods on the challenging ImageNet localization task is a strong validation of the approach's effectiveness. The fact that a model trained without any bounding box information can approach the performance of a fully supervised VGG model is remarkable. The qualitative examples in Figure 5 are also very compelling, showing sharp, precise, and highly interpretable masks.

3.  **Thoughtful Problem Formulation and Evaluation:** The authors provide a clear and insightful discussion of the properties of a good saliency map, formalizing the concepts of SSR and SDR. The proposed objective function (Eq. 4) is a principled combination of these concepts along with necessary regularizers for size and smoothness. Furthermore, the introduction of a new saliency metric (`s(a, p)`) is a valuable contribution in its own right. It directly measures the "information concentration" that saliency maps are meant to capture and provides a more nuanced evaluation tool than localization accuracy alone. The finding that their method is on par with ground-truth bounding boxes under this new metric is a powerful result.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Overhead and Scalability:** The proposed solution requires training a separate, large masking model for each black-box classifier one wishes to explain. This represents a significant computational and engineering overhead compared to methods that work out-of-the-box. While the inference is fast, the training is expensive. This trade-off might limit the approach's utility in scenarios where one needs to explain many different models or models that are frequently retrained.

2.  **The "Explaining the Explainer" Problem:** The masking model itself is a complex deep neural network. While it is trained to reveal the behavior of a target black box, it inevitably learns its own priors and biases from the training data (e.g., priors about object shape and smoothness). The paper acknowledges this limitation in the conclusion, but it remains a key conceptual concern. It is not fully clear whether the generated mask perfectly reflects the black-box model's reasoning or a "plausible" explanation filtered through the masking model's own inductive biases.

3.  **Lack of Sensitivity Analysis:** The objective function in Equation 4 contains four hyperparameters (λ1-λ4) that appear crucial for balancing the different goals (smoothness, size, SSR, SDR). The paper presents a single set of values without discussing how they were selected or how sensitive the model's performance is to their variation. This makes the method seem somewhat brittle and could pose a challenge for reproducibility or application to new domains.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

The core idea of training a predictive model for saliency is a significant conceptual advance that has the potential to redefine how we generate explanations for classifiers in real-time contexts. The novelty and potential impact are exceptionally high. While there are weaknesses, such as the training overhead and the deeper philosophical questions about the explainer's own biases, these are acceptable trade-offs and exciting avenues for future research, not fatal flaws. This paper prioritizes a bold new direction over perfecting an existing one, and it backs up its proposal with strong empirical evidence and thoughtful analysis. This is precisely the kind of forward-looking, high-impact research that NIPS should champion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and significant conceptual shift from iterative optimization to amortized prediction for saliency.
*   Enables real-time, high-quality saliency, opening up new application domains.
*   State-of-the-art performance on a standard, challenging benchmark (weakly supervised localization).
*   Introduces a well-motivated new metric for evaluating saliency.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Requires training a separate, complex model for each black-box classifier, which is a high upfront cost.
*   The explainer model may introduce its own biases, a point that is acknowledged but not investigated.
*   Minor methodological details, like hyperparameter sensitivity, are not fully explored.
