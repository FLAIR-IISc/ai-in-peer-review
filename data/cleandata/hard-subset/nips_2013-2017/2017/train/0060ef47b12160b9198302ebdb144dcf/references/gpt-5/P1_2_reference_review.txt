Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a fast, trainable saliency detector that, given an image and a target class for a fixed classifier, predicts a per-pixel mask in a single forward pass. The model is trained to minimize an objective that combines two complementary notions of saliency: smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR). To reduce adversarial artifacts introduced by masking, the authors randomize the masking baseline (strong blur or random color plus noise), add a total-variation (TV) smoothness penalty, and downsample/upsample the mask. The masking model uses a U-Net-like architecture with a ResNet-50 encoder and class-dependent feature gating. They also introduce a saliency metric based on the trade-off between the area of the preserved region and the classifier confidence on the cropped region: s(a,p) = log(max(a,0.05)) · log(p), where smaller is better.

The work is positioned relative to: (1) gradient/backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), which is fast but often noisy and less faithful; (2) iterative, perturbation-based approaches that optimize an image-space mask (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi’s “Meaningful Perturbations” and Cao et al.), which tend to be more faithful but slow; and (3) weakly supervised localization (CAM and related). The main novelty is learning a feed-forward masking network trained end-to-end against the black-box classifier’s outputs to approximate the outcome of expensive iterative optimization, and combining SSR and SDR explicitly in the loss.

Quality
- Methodology: The objective captures desiderata for saliency (small, smooth masks that both preserve evidence for the target and destroy it when removed). Randomizing the baseline image A and using TV to discourage high-frequency artifacts are sensible design choices. The class-conditional feature gating is a reasonable way to bootstrap localization in the encoder.
- Experiments: On ImageNet, the trained masking models for AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 achieve competitive weakly supervised localization (reported best error 36.7% with ResNet-50-based saliency), outperform baselines and several prior weakly supervised methods, and produce visually sharp masks. The proposed metric also favors the method, approaching ground-truth boxes. A CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates the approach without a pretrained encoder, suggesting the method is not dependent on ImageNet pretraining.
- Concerns and missing details:
  - “Black box” requirement: training the masking network appears to require backpropagating through fc to optimize the mask generator. Thus the classifier must be differentiable and accessible for gradients during training; at test time it is not needed. This is not fully black-box in the sense of not requiring gradients; please clarify the access assumptions.
  - Loss clarity: Equation (4) is ambiguously typeset, especially the last term “+ 3 fc(Φ(X,1−M)) 4”. Is this a power, a weight, or both? The text suggests raising fc to a power < 1; please formalize.
  - Evaluation: Localization is a standard proxy, but saliency is not localization. While the proposed metric is interesting, it is somewhat ad hoc (choice of rectangular crop, aspect-ratio distortion, 0.05 floor) and may favor methods that produce compact, high-confidence crops. Sensitivity analysis (e.g., varying the floor, using tight polygonal crops) would strengthen claims. Also, report class-wise results or failure modes (e.g., low-frequency backgrounds where blurring is ineffective).
  - Comparisons: It would be important to compare against CAM/Grad-CAM (and possibly Guided Grad-CAM) in addition to Guided Backprop and Excitation Backprop, given their popularity and strong localization performance. Ensure thresholds and post-processing are comparable across methods.
  - Ablations: The paper would benefit from ablations on (i) the random-A scheme (blur vs random color/noise), (ii) fake-label training probability, (iii) the contribution of SSR vs SDR terms, and (iv) TV weight. Report how each affects artifact suppression, mask sharpness, and metrics.
  - Runtime: Claims of 100 fps are compelling; please specify hardware, batch size, and whether this includes preprocessing and upsampling. Also report per-image runtime of the best iterative baseline for a fair speedup factor.
  - Generalization and practicality: The need to train a separate saliency model per classifier limits plug-and-play applicability; a discussion or preliminary results on transfer across classifiers or training a universal saliency model would be valuable.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with clear motivation and figures that illustrate SSR/SDR and artifacts. Minor issues:
- Tighten notation for Φ(X,M) and make the loss unambiguous (weights, signs, logs).
- Explicitly state the need for gradients through the classifier during training.
- Provide full details for thresholding, binarization, and post-processing used to derive boxes from masks for all methods compared.
- Include actual numbers in tables and hardware details.

Originality
Moderate to good. The idea of learning a feed-forward network to approximate iterative perturbation-based saliency is, to my knowledge, novel at the time of writing, as is the explicit joint SSR/SDR training objective and the randomized baseline scheme during training. The architecture components (U-Net, ResNet encoder, TV) are standard. The saliency metric is new but tailored; it is an incremental contribution.

Significance
If validated with stronger comparisons and ablations, the contribution could be impactful: real-time, high-quality, perturbation-faithful saliency could enable video applications and practical auditing of large models. The improved localization under weak supervision is promising. The per-classifier training requirement and limited evaluation scope slightly temper immediate applicability.

Pros
- Clear motivation; unifies SSR and SDR in a single, trainable objective.
- Produces sharp, interpretable masks and competitive localization.
- Orders-of-magnitude speedup over iterative perturbation methods.
- Sensible defenses against adversarial artifacts (randomized A, TV).
- Simple, scalable architecture; works even without pretraining on CIFAR-10.

Cons
- Requires gradient access to the classifier during training; “black box” wording is potentially misleading.
- Evaluation misses key baselines (CAM/Grad-CAM) and ablations.
- New metric is somewhat ad hoc; limited analysis of its behavior and biases.
- Need to train a separate saliency model per classifier; limited discussion of transfer.
- Some notation/implementation details unclear; tables not fully specified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify access assumptions and loss definition; add ablations and comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM.
- Analyze the sensitivity of the proposed metric; consider alternative crops or insertion/deletion curves.
- Report hardware and runtime details; include code/models for reproducibility.
- Explore cross-classifier generalization and threshold sensitivity for deriving boxes.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a timely, practically useful approach with promising empirical results and a thoughtful training objective, but would benefit from clearer exposition, stronger comparative evaluation, and ablations to solidify its claims.
