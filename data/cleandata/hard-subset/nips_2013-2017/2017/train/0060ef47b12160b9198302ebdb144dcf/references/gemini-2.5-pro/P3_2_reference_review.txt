### Summary

This paper presents a novel method for generating saliency maps for image classifiers in real-time. The core problem addressed is the trade-off between speed and quality in existing saliency methods: gradient-based methods are fast but often produce noisy or uninterpretable maps, while optimization-based perturbation methods produce higher-quality maps but are computationally expensive due to their iterative nature. The authors' key idea is to "amortize" this optimization by training a separate, dedicated neural network (a U-Net architecture) to predict a saliency mask in a single forward pass. This "masking model" is trained to generate masks that optimize a comprehensive objective function, which aims to find a small, smooth region that is both sufficient for classification (Smallest Sufficient Region - SSR) and necessary for it (Smallest Destroying Region - SDR). The method is model-agnostic at inference time, and experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 show that it achieves state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization while being orders of magnitude faster than iterative approaches. The authors also introduce a new information-theoretic metric for evaluating saliency quality.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The primary contribution of training a network to predict saliency masks is both novel and significant. It directly addresses a major bottleneck in a class of high-quality explanation methods (perturbation-based), making them practical for real-time applications like video analysis. This shift from per-instance iterative optimization to a learned, amortized prediction is a powerful concept that could influence future work in explainable AI.

2.  **Technically Sound Methodology:** The proposed objective function (Eq. 4) is well-designed and principled. It thoughtfully combines multiple desiderata for a good saliency map: smoothness (TV regularization), conciseness (area penalty), sufficiency (SSR term), and necessity (SDR term). The architectural choice of a U-Net is appropriate for generating dense, pixel-aligned masks, and the use of a pre-trained encoder is a practical choice for leveraging powerful features. The training strategy, including the use of "fake" labels to force attention to the class selector, is clever and addresses a potential failure mode.

3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing.
    *   The method is validated on three different, widely-used ImageNet classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50), demonstrating its general applicability.
    *   The weakly supervised object localization results (Table 1 & 2) are impressive, outperforming previous state-of-the-art methods and establishing a new benchmark. This provides a strong quantitative signal of the quality of the generated masks.
    *   The introduction and evaluation of a new saliency metric (Sec 3.2, Table 3) is a valuable contribution in its own right. It moves beyond localization to more directly measure the "information concentration" that saliency aims to capture, and the results show the proposed method performs on par with ground-truth bounding boxes under this metric.
    *   The CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates that the approach is not limited to ImageNet and can be trained from scratch, alleviating concerns that the performance is solely due to the powerful pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is explained in detail, and the connection between the objective function and the desired properties of a saliency map is explicit. The figures are illustrative and support the text effectively.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Clarification of "Black Box" Access:** The paper repeatedly refers to the target classifier as a "black box". While this is true during *inference* (the trained masking model does not need the classifier), it is potentially misleading. The entire *training* process requires full, differentiable access to the classifier's output probabilities to compute the loss function (the `fc` terms in Eq. 4). This is a critical distinction from methods like LIME [6] which can truly treat the model as a black box by only probing its inputs and outputs. The authors should clarify this distinction early on, stating that the method requires a "white-box" classifier during training to produce a "black-box-compatible" explainer for inference.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The objective function (Eq. 4) relies on four hyperparameters (λ1 to λ4) that balance competing goals (smoothness, size, SSR, SDR). The paper states the values used but provides no discussion on how they were selected or how sensitive the model's performance is to these choices. A brief ablation study or sensitivity analysis in the appendix would significantly strengthen the paper's reproducibility and provide insight into the trade-offs governed by the objective function.

3.  **Potential Bias of the Masking Model:** The authors astutely mention in the conclusion that the masking model itself might have biases. This point is very important and perhaps deserves more prominence than a brief mention in future work. The masking model (especially its ImageNet pre-trained encoder) has learned its own set of priors about the visual world. How does this affect the "faithfulness" of the explanations it provides for a target classifier? For instance, if the target classifier has learned a spurious correlation (e.g., snow for polar bears), but the masking model's pre-trained encoder is very good at identifying bears, could the masking model produce a "sanitized" explanation that highlights the bear, thereby obscuring the target model's actual flawed reasoning? A short discussion on this potential limitation would add depth and honesty to the evaluation.

4.  **Minor Clarity in Methodology:** The description of the feature filter (Eq. 7) could be more precise. It's described as a mechanism to "attenuate spatial locations," but it is effectively a class-conditional attention or gating mechanism on the feature map. Framing it in these more standard terms might make its function clearer to readers.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper with a novel and impactful core idea, supported by a sound methodology and excellent experimental results. It makes a significant contribution to the field of explainable AI by making high-quality, perturbation-based saliency methods practical for real-time use. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to clarification and the need for deeper analysis of certain aspects, rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. They can be addressed with minor revisions.

**Recommendation: Accept.** The paper is of high quality, originality, and significance, making it an excellent fit for NIPS.
