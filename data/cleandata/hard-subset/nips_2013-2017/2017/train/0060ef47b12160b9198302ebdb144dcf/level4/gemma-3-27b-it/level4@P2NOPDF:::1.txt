This paper introduces a neural network-based approach to learning saliency masks, achieving a processing speed of multiple images per second. Overall, I find this work borderline; I would not strongly oppose either acceptance or rejection. The core concept of learning to explain model behavior is valuable, and the paper presents some promising ideas. While I did not identify any fundamental errors, the method has notable limitations that should be addressed.

A primary concern is the indirect measurement of saliency. The paper relies either on weakly supervised localization or a proposed saliency metric, both of which have inherent weaknesses. These limitations deserve more thorough discussion. The use of weakly supervised localization as a proxy for saliency quality is problematic. Object localization may not reliably reflect saliency when contextual information is crucial for accurate classification. The presented results using this approach are interesting, but this caveat significantly impacts their interpretation as a robust saliency metric. 

The proposed saliency metric also introduces potential artifacts. The process of cropping the estimated salient region and then rescaling it to the original image size, while maintaining the original aspect ratio, could distort the classification results. Specifically, altering the aspect ratio may hinder performance, and the metric inherently favors smaller salient regions. The substantial upscaling applied to these smaller regions could present the classifier with a non-ideal scale, potentially exceeding the network’s learned scaling invariance (while CNNs demonstrate translation invariance, scaling invariance is a learned property with inherent limits). 

The paper lacks exploration of the masking model’s sensitivity to network architecture. Did the authors investigate the impact of different architectures on the learned masks and resulting saliency explanations? This analysis is critical for understanding the generalizability and robustness of the approach.

I have a few minor questions: Are the results in Table 1 reported for all classes, or only the correctly classified ones? Furthermore, please specify the exact LRP variant and parameter settings used for comparison, as multiple variants (with parameters like epsilon and alpha-beta) exist.

**(Post-Rebuttal Edit)**

A key limitation, reinforced by the rebuttal, is the strong dependence of the saliency metric's effectiveness on the classifier’s quality and scale invariance. This restricts the method's applicability to networks possessing these properties. Consequently:

*   The method cannot be applied to models during training, or to models lacking inherent scale invariance.
*   This limits its application to domains beyond standard image classification (e.g., spectrogram analysis using CNNs).
*   The claim of general applicability to “black-box classifiers” in the title is inaccurate.

Furthermore, the rebuttal suggests a significant influence of the masking network’s architecture on the results. This raises concerns about *what* is actually being visualized – the saliency of the underlying U-network, or the characteristics of the masking network itself?

If the authors substantially address these points within the paper, providing a balanced discussion of the method’s strengths and weaknesses, I believe publication is warranted. However, without a more critical evaluation of these limitations, I recommend rejection.
