- Introduces a neural approach to learning saliency masks that is computationally efficient (multiple images/sec).
- Reviewer rates paper borderline; acknowledges valuable ideas but is ambivalent about acceptance.
- No clear errors but several important limitations need addressing.
- Saliency evaluation is indirect, relying on weakly supervised localization and a proposed saliency metric, both with limitations.
- Weakly supervised localization may not reflect true saliency because object context affects class and localization may not correlate with explanation quality.
- The proposed saliency metric has limitations: cropping/rescaling with preserved aspect ratio can introduce artifacts and affect classification.
- The metric’s preference for small salient regions may present suboptimal scale to classifiers, given CNNs’ limited scale invariance.
- Paper omits discussion/analysis of how the masking model’s architecture affects mask learning; experiments across architectures are needed.
- Minor clarifications requested: whether Table 1 reports results for all classes or only the correct class.
- Minor clarifications requested: specify which LRP variant and parameter settings (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) were used for comparison.
- Post-rebuttal: effectiveness of the metric depends strongly on classifier quality and scale invariance, limiting applicability.
- Consequences: method unsuitable for models in training or without scale invariance, limited for other domains (e.g., spectrograms), and not generally applicable to black-box classifiers as claimed.
- Post-rebuttal: results suggest strong dependence on the masking network, making it unclear whether visualized saliency reflects the U-network or the masking network.
- Recommendation: acceptability hinges on properly discussing these limitations; without that discussion, the paper should not be published.
