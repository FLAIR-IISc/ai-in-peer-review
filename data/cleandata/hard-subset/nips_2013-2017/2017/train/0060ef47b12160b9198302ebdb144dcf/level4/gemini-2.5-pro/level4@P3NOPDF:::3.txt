This paper proposes a highly efficient approach for generating image saliency masks, capable of processing one hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The core contribution is a masking model trained to identify the tightest rectangular crop containing the salient region of a target object class. This is achieved in a weakly supervised manner, using only the output of a pre-trained, black-box image classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet) for guidance. The model's architecture leverages multi-scale feature maps from a backbone network like ResNet-50. A feature filter operating on the coarsest feature map performs an initial rough localization, which is then progressively refined by a series of upsampling blocks to produce the final, high-resolution mask. Experimental results on the ImageNet localization task demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Performance:** The paper introduces an interesting and effective method for weakly supervised localization. The reported performance, which surpasses other methods in the same category on the ImageNet localization benchmark, is a significant achievement.
*   **Efficiency:** The claim of processing 100 images per second is impressive and represents a substantial practical advantage over many competing methods. This high throughput makes the approach potentially suitable for real-time applications.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The authors provide good intuition and thorough discussion regarding their architectural design choices, making the method easy to follow and understand.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The approach appears technically sound and is well-situated within the context of related work, with sufficient references provided.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

Overall, this paper presents strong technical results. My main concerns relate to the practical details of the speed claim and the method's robustness to real-world image variations.

1.  **Real-Time Performance Clarification:** The claim of processing 100 images per second is a key selling point. Could the authors clarify if this timing includes the necessary pre-processing step of resizing input images to 224x224? Furthermore, it would be highly beneficial to understand the performance on higher-resolution images, such as 640x480, which are common in many real-world applications (e.g., smartphone photography). This would provide a more complete picture of the method's practical efficiency.

2.  **Robustness to Object Scale:** The experiments and figures seem to feature images where the salient object is relatively large and centrally located. How does the model perform when the target object is small within the frame? Is the 224x224 input resolution sufficient to capture the necessary details for accurately localizing small objects, or does this fixed input size pose a potential limitation? Some examples or analysis on this aspect would strengthen the paper's claims of general applicability.

3.  **Experimental Comparison:** In Table 3, the paper presents a detailed ablation study of its components. For a more comprehensive comparison, it would be very helpful if the authors could include results for key baselines from Table 2 (e.g., Feed-forward [2]) under the same evaluation metrics. This would allow readers to more directly contextualize the improvements gained from the proposed architectural components relative to prior art.
