This paper proposes an image saliency mask generation approach that can process about one hundred 224×224 images per second on a standard GPU. The method trains a masking model that, for a particular requested class defined by a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet), finds the tightest rectangular crop that contains the entire salient region for that class. The architecture consumes multi-scale image feature maps (e.g., from ResNet-50). The final-scale feature map is passed through a feature filter to perform initial localization, and subsequent upsampling blocks refine the produced masks. Experiments show that the method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task.

The references and related work appear sufficient, though I did not check them exhaustively. The paper also appears technically correct, though I did not verify every detail. It provides useful intuition and discussion about the design choices, and the overall presentation is good.

Overall, this paper presents interesting technical results. I have some concerns about the real-time speed claim and the applicability to real-world images.

Comments and questions:
- Does the reported throughput of ~100 images per second include the image resizing operation? If so, what is the running time for larger images, such as 640×480 photos taken from an iPhone 6s?
- The salient objects shown in the paper are quite large. What happens if the requested-class object is small in the image? Is a 224×224 input sufficient in that case?
- In Table 3, is there a corresponding metric reported for other works listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2]?
