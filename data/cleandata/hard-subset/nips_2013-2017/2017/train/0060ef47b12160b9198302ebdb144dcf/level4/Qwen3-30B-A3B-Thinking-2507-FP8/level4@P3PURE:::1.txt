

The paper proposes a neural network-based approach for learning saliency masks, demonstrating real-time performance (processing multiple images per second). While I find the concept of learning to explain models compelling and the paper contains several good ideas, the work presents significant limitations that require careful consideration. The paper is borderline; it would be acceptable if the limitations are adequately addressed, but currently lacks sufficient discussion to warrant acceptance.

The primary concern lies in the indirect measurement of saliency. Both the weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric have substantial limitations that are not thoroughly discussed.

1.  **Weakly Supervised Localization as a Metric:** The use of weakly supervised localization results as a quality metric for saliency is problematic. If the context is essential for class determination, object localization may not correlate with saliency quality. This introduces a significant caveat when interpreting these results as a saliency evaluation.

2.  **Saliency Metric Artifacts:** The proposed metric involves cropping the estimated salient region, rescaling it to the original image size while preserving aspect ratio, and then reclassifying it. This process introduces two key artifacts:
    *   **Aspect Ratio Distortion:** Rescaling with preserved aspect ratio may alter the spatial relationships critical for classification, potentially impacting the metric's validity.
    *   **Scale Distortion:** The metric favors small salient regions. Rescaling a small region to full size subjects the classifier to a non-standard scale. While CNNs exhibit translation invariance, their scale invariance must be learned and has inherent limits; the upscaling process may present the object at a scale where the classifier performs poorly, invalidating the metric.

Furthermore, the paper does not discuss how the masking model's performance depends on the underlying network architecture. Crucially, the authors should address: Did they experiment with different architectures, and how did this influence the results?

**Minor Comments:**
*   Clarify whether the results in Table 1 are obtained for all classes or only for the correctly classified classes.
*   Specify which LRP variant and parameter settings (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) were used for comparison, as multiple variants exist.

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment:**
The authors' response confirms that the saliency metric's validity is highly dependent on the scale invariance properties of the classifier. This dependence severely limits the method's applicability:
*   It cannot be used for models during training or for models lacking sufficient scale invariance.
*   It is not generally applicable to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis using CNNs).
*   It directly contradicts the claim in the title that the method is applicable to "black-box classifiers."

Additionally, the response hints at a strong dependence on the masking network architecture. This raises a fundamental question: Are the visualizations reflecting the saliency of the *original* U-network or the *masking network* itself?

**Conclusion:**
If the paper comprehensively discusses these limitations—including the critical dependency on classifier scale invariance, the architectural dependence of the masking model, and the specific constraints on applicability—the work becomes balanced and acceptable. However, as currently written, the lack of discussion on these points renders the method's claims unsupported. Therefore, the paper should be **rejected** unless these critical limitations are thoroughly addressed in a revision.
