The paper proposes a neural-network-based approach to learn saliency masks and achieves throughput of multiple images per second, which is commendable.

Overall, I find the paper borderline; I would not object to either rejection or acceptance. I believe in the broader goal of learning to explain models, and this paper contains some good ideas. There are no obvious technical errors, but there are important limitations that should be discussed more thoroughly.

Main concern: saliency is only evaluated indirectly—either via weakly supervised localization or through the proposed saliency metric—and both proxies have clear limitations.

- Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect measure of saliency quality. When contextual cues are essential for classification, object localization need not correlate with saliency: a method might localize the object while ignoring critical context, or vice versa. The reported localization results are interesting, but using them as a saliency quality metric carries a significant caveat.

- The proposed saliency metric is also problematic due to how it is applied. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size (implying a change in aspect ratio if the crop is not proportional). This introduces two artifacts:
  1) Aspect ratio changes may affect how well the image can be classified.
  2) The metric implicitly favors small salient regions. Small crops are heavily upscaled for re-classification, altering the object’s scale in ways the classifier may not handle well. While ConvNets are often fairly translation-invariant, scale invariance must be learned and is typically limited.

Architectural dependence: The paper does not discuss how sensitive the masking model is to the chosen architecture. Did the authors experiment with different architectures for the masking network, and how did these choices affect performance and the learned masks?

Minor comments:
- In Table 1, are the reported results computed over all classes or only for the ground-truth class?
- Please specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparison (e.g., epsilon, alpha–beta, and relevant parameter values).

Post-rebuttal update: The effectiveness of the saliency metric appears to depend strongly on the classifier’s quality and, in particular, its scale invariance, which substantially limits the method’s applicability. It can only be applied to networks that exhibit sufficient scale invariance. This has several consequences:
- The method cannot be used for models during training, nor for models that do not possess this invariance.
- Applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs) is limited.
- The method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers, contrary to the claim in the title.

Furthermore, the response suggests a strong dependence on the masking network. As a result, it is unclear whether the visualization reflects the saliency of the target (U-)network or artifacts of the masking network.

If these limitations and dependencies are explicitly and carefully discussed in the paper, I consider it balanced enough for publication. If not, it should not be published.
