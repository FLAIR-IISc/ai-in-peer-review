

This paper presents a novel neural network architecture designed to predict salient image regions critical for object categorization by a separate neural network. The work addresses a meaningful problem at the intersection of interpretability and efficiency, with the key insight that identifying these regions can accelerate downstream tasks without compromising accuracy. The writing is clear and well-structured, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating strong performance across challenging benchmarks like CIFAR-10 and ImageNet using state-of-the-art classification networks. The results are compelling, showing consistent improvements over prior methods in both speed and accuracy, particularly under latency constraints.

The primary strength lies in the practical relevance of the approach. By enabling faster inference through region prioritization, the method directly supports real-time applications—such as autonomous driving, augmented reality, or mobile vision systems—where computational resources are limited. While the paper acknowledges efficiency gains, it would benefit from a more explicit discussion of specific deployment scenarios where sub-second processing is non-negotiable. For instance, highlighting how the framework could reduce latency in drone-based object tracking or real-time medical imaging analysis would strengthen the motivation and contextualize the contribution within current industry needs.

A second opportunity for enhancement lies in the analysis of learned saliency patterns. The paper reports quantitative results but lacks qualitative insights into *why* certain regions are deemed salient. A deeper investigation into category-specific biases would significantly enrich the contribution. For example: Do saliency maps consistently highlight background elements (e.g., grass for "cow" or sky for "bird") that classifiers might over-rely on? Do regions differ for objects with high visual ambiguity (e.g., "horse" vs. "deer")? Visualizing these patterns across categories—perhaps through t-SNE embeddings of saliency maps or statistical analysis of background/foreground attention—would reveal whether the model exploits spurious correlations or genuinely identifies semantically relevant features. Such analysis would not only validate the method’s interpretability but also inform future work on robustness against dataset biases.

The technical execution is sound, with thorough ablation studies and comparisons to baselines like Grad-CAM and CAM. However, the discussion of limitations could be expanded. For instance, how does the approach handle occlusions or low-contrast scenes where saliency might mislead? A brief analysis of failure cases would add nuance. Additionally, while the paper claims "real-time efficiency," the exact latency metrics (e.g., FPS on specific hardware) should be quantified to substantiate this claim.

Overall, this is a well-executed, timely contribution with clear potential for impact. The core idea is novel, the experiments are rigorous, and the results are convincing. With minor refinements to the motivation for real-time applications and deeper analysis of saliency patterns, the paper would be significantly strengthened. The suggestions above are straightforward to address and would elevate the work from strong to exceptional. I recommend acceptance.
