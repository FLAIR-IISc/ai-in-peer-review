This paper proposes a neural network-based approach for learning saliency masks. A key advantage of the proposed method is its computational efficiency, allowing it to process multiple images per second.

Overall, I find this paper to be borderline. While I strongly support the concept of learning to generate model explanations and believe the paper contains several promising ideas, it also has significant limitations. There are no obvious technical errors, but these limitations are substantial.

My primary concern is that saliency is only measured indirectly, either through weakly supervised localization or a novel proposed metric. Both evaluation methods have clear limitations that I believe should be thoroughly discussed in the paper.

Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy for saliency. For instance, if the context surrounding an object is essential for correct classification, the most salient regions may extend beyond the object's bounding box. Therefore, object localization does not necessarily correlate with the quality of a saliency map. While the results on this task are interesting, using it as a primary quality metric for saliency comes with this significant caveat.

The proposed saliency metric is also flawed in its application. The metric involves cropping the estimated salient region and rescaling it to the original image dimensions while preserving the aspect ratio. This process can introduce two potential artifacts. First, altering the aspect ratio of the input patch could negatively impact classification performance. Second, the metric inherently prefers smaller salient regions. Consequently, a small cropped region is significantly magnified before being passed to the classifier. This presents the object at a scale that may be far from the ideal for the network. While ConvNets are generally translation-invariant, scale invariance is a learned property with inherent limits.

The paper also does not discuss the sensitivity of the results to the architecture of the masking network. It would be valuable to know if the authors experimented with different architectures and how these choices influenced the final saliency maps.

**Minor Comments:**
*   In Table 1, please clarify whether the reported results are averaged over all classes or calculated only for the ground-truth class.
*   Please specify which LRP variant (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) and corresponding parameter settings were used for the comparison, as different choices can yield different results.

**Post-Rebuttal Edit:**
The authors' rebuttal confirms that the proposed saliency metric's effectiveness is highly dependent on the scale invariance of the classifier being explained. This is a significant limitation on the method's applicability, as it restricts its use to networks that already possess strong scale invariance.

This has several important consequences:
*   The method is unsuitable for analyzing models during training or any model that has not learned this specific invariance.
*   The applicability to other domains where scale invariance is not a given (e.g., CNNs for spectrogram analysis) is questionable.
*   Most importantly, this dependency undermines the claim in the title that the method is generally applicable to "black-box" classifiers.

Furthermore, the rebuttal suggests a strong dependence on the architecture of the masking network itself. This raises a critical question: are the generated maps visualizing the saliency of the original classifier, or are they reflecting the biases and artifacts of the masking network?

If these limitations and their consequences are thoroughly and transparently discussed in the final manuscript, I believe the paper could be acceptable for publication. However, without such a discussion, the paper's claims are not sufficiently supported, and I would recommend rejection.
