- Proposes training a neural network to predict saliency masks for fast saliency detection, contrasting with costly iterative methods.  
- Reviewer is neutral/borderline: would not object to acceptance or rejection.  
- No obvious technical mistakes, but clear limitations exist.  
- Evaluation is indirect, relying on weakly supervised localization and a proposed saliency metric; both need deeper discussion.  
- Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy for saliency when contextual cues matter; localization accuracy may not reflect object saliency.  
- The proposed saliency metric crops and resizes salient regions, risking aspect-ratio distortion and artifacts that can affect classification.  
- Emphasizing very small salient regions is problematic because upscaling changes scale and classifiers have limited scale invariance.  
- The masking model likely depends strongly on the underlying classifier architecture; experiments across architectures are needed.  
- Post-rebuttal: metric effectiveness depends on classifier quality and scale/rotation invariance, significantly limiting applicability.  
- Consequence: method cannot reliably be used during training or on models lacking scale/rotation invariance.  
- Consequence: limits applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis) and undermines claim of general black-box applicability.  
- Concern that the masking network may learn its own biases—unclear if we visualize the original classifier’s saliency or the masking network’s learned saliency.  
- If these limitations are properly discussed in the paper, it is balanced enough for publication; otherwise it should be rejected.  
- Minor queries: clarify whether Table 1 results are computed for all classes or only the correct class.  
- Minor queries: specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparison.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel fast saliency approach; competitive weakly supervised localization; new evaluation perspective; tested on ImageNet and CIFAR-10.  
- Arguments for rejection: reliance on classifier invariances; indirect/flawed saliency assessment; potential masking-network bias; lack of discussion on architectural sensitivity.
