Summary
This paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency method for differentiable image classifiers. Instead of producing a saliency map via iterative perturbation for each input (e.g., occlusion or gradient-based methods), the authors train a separate masking network that predicts a class-conditional saliency mask in a single forward pass. The mask is trained to simultaneously (i) preserve classifier confidence when the salient region is retained (SSR) and (ii) suppress confidence when the salient region is removed (SDR), while encouraging smooth, compact masks to avoid adversarial artifacts. The masking network adopts a U-Net architecture with a ResNet-50 encoder and a learned class embedding that gates high-level features. The method runs at >100 images/s for 224×224 inputs and is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10. The authors also introduce a saliency metric based on cropping the tightest salient region and measuring the trade-off between retained area and class probability. On weakly supervised localization, the approach outperforms prior saliency and weak supervision baselines on ImageNet, and achieves competitive results with some fully supervised approaches.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself among gradient-based saliency (Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), CAM-like localization (Zhou et al.), and perturbation-based/occlusion methods (Zeiler & Fergus; Fong & Vedaldi’s “Meaningful Perturbations”). Compared with iterative perturbation approaches (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi; Cao et al.), the contribution here is to learn a saliency predictor across images/classes, drastically reducing inference time while improving mask sharpness and localization accuracy. The emphasis on mitigating introduced evidence via randomized alternative images and TV regularization is aligned with concerns raised in prior perturbation work, but is operationalized in a learned masking model.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation: targets both SSR and SDR criteria and explicitly addresses “introduced evidence” in perturbations.
- Practical and fast: one forward pass per image/class enables real-time saliency and promises applicability to video.
- Strong empirical results: better weakly supervised localization on ImageNet than competing saliency/weak-supervision methods; competitive with fully supervised VGG for some configurations; new metric indicates high-quality, interpretable masks.
- Sensible architecture: U-Net with class gating, skip connections for spatial precision, and training tricks (fake labels, random A) to enforce class specificity and robustness.
- Generality: works across black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) and scales to CIFAR-10, including training the masking model from scratch.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity/notation issues: The saliency metric is written as s(a, p) = log(ã) log(p); please confirm whether this is subtraction (log(ã) − log(p)). The last term in Eq. (4) also appears to conflate a coefficient and an exponent; please disambiguate λ4 vs fc(·)^4.
- “Black-box” assumption: Training requires backpropagation through the classifier to optimize the mask. At test time the classifier is not needed, but during training the method is not strictly black-box. Please clarify the required access (gradients vs scores) and discuss applicability when gradients are unavailable.
- Metric validity: The proposed crop-based metric depends on resizing crops without preserving aspect ratio and on classifier invariances. Some analysis of sensitivity to resizing/aspect ratio and comparison to standard deletion/insertion-style faithfulness measures or CAM/Grad-CAM baselines would strengthen the case.
- Ablations: Limited analysis of:
  - Loss coefficients (λ1–λ4), TV strength, and the effect of SSR vs SDR terms;
  - Choice of alternative image A (blur vs noise) and its mixing probability;
  - Mask resolution and threshold choice for localization (0.5). Sensitivity plots would improve confidence in robustness.
- Bias analysis: While the method can expose background biases in classifiers, the paper does not systematically analyze which classes rely on context vs object evidence or quantify discovered biases. A brief analysis (e.g., correlation between saliency overlap with background and class frequency/context) would be informative.
- Comparisons: Please include CAM/Grad-CAM and Excitation Backprop as baselines in both localization and the proposed saliency metric, given their widespread use and relevance for weakly supervised localization in 2016–2017.
- Generalization: The masking model is trained per classifier. Discuss whether a single masking model can generalize across multiple architectures or to architectures not seen during training.

Quality
Technically sound and thoughtfully designed. The training protocol (fake labels, randomized A, TV) addresses common pitfalls (class-agnostic masks, adversarial artifacts). Empirical results are strong, but the paper would benefit from clearer equations and more thorough ablations and baseline comparisons, especially on the proposed metric.

Clarity
Overall well written and easy to follow. Please fix notational ambiguities in the objective and metric, and add implementation details for reproducibility (optimizer, LR schedule, batch size, GPU used for throughput claims). A brief discussion of failure cases would also help.

Originality
The key novelty is learning a feed-forward saliency predictor optimized for SSR and SDR across a dataset, rather than solving a per-image optimization. This is a meaningful and timely contribution relative to iterative perturbation and backprop-based saliency methods.

Significance
High. The approach makes perturbation-based saliency practical at scale (including video), improves weakly supervised localization, and proposes a metric that emphasizes interpretability and faithfulness. With broader baselines and ablations, this could be a reference method for real-time saliency.

Arguments for acceptance
- Substantial speedup with better or comparable qualitative/quantitative saliency and localization.
- Solid architectural and training innovations to mitigate introduced evidence and enforce class specificity.
- Clear impact for real-time and video applications.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient clarity in equations and limited ablations on key design choices.
- Incomplete comparisons to widely used baselines (e.g., CAM/Grad-CAM).
- Limited analysis of when/why the method fails and of biases uncovered in the classifier.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify metric and loss notation; include sensitivity and ablation studies for λ’s, mask threshold, and A selection.
- Add CAM/Grad-CAM and Excitation Backprop to comparisons; discuss computational cost vs performance.
- Provide qualitative failure cases and a short bias analysis across classes.
- Specify training/inference details and hardware for throughput; consider releasing code/models.
- Briefly motivate real-time use cases (e.g., autonomous driving, interactive diagnostic tools, video analytics) and report preliminary video saliency results if possible.

Overall, this is a strong and relevant submission with clear potential impact. With improved clarity and broader comparisons, it would make a valuable contribution to NIPS.
