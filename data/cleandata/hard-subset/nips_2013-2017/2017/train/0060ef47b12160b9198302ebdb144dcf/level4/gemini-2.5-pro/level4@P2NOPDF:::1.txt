### Summary

The paper proposes a neural network-based approach for learning to generate saliency masks. A key advantage of the proposed method is its efficiency, capable of processing multiple images per second.

### Overall Assessment

This paper is borderline; I could support either acceptance or rejection. The core concept of learning to generate explanations for a model's predictions is valuable, and the paper presents several interesting ideas in this direction. While there are no overt errors, the work has significant limitations that need to be addressed and discussed transparently.

### Major Weaknesses

My primary concern is that the quality of the generated saliency masks is evaluated only indirectly, using two metrics that have considerable drawbacks. These limitations should be explicitly discussed in the paper.

1.  **Weakly Supervised Localization as a Saliency Metric:** Using object localization performance as a proxy for saliency quality is problematic. For many classes, the surrounding context is essential for correct classification. In such cases, a good saliency map should highlight these contextual cues, which would not necessarily correlate with a tight bounding box around the primary object. While the localization results are interesting, this fundamental caveat limits their utility as a definitive metric for explanation quality.

2.  **Flaws in the Proposed Quantitative Saliency Metric:** The proposed saliency metric is also problematic due to its application. The process involves cropping the estimated salient region and then resizing it to the original input dimensions for re-classification. This introduces at least two potential artifacts:
    *   **Scale and Aspect Ratio Distortion:** Resizing a cropped region can significantly alter its aspect ratio and scale, presenting the object to the classifier in a way it may not have seen during training. While CNNs possess some translation invariance, scale invariance must be learned and is often limited. A small salient region, when heavily magnified, may fall outside the range of scales the classifier can reliably handle.
    *   **Bias Towards Smaller Regions:** This metric implicitly favors smaller salient regions, which may not always correspond to better or more complete explanations.

3.  **Dependence on Masking Network Architecture:** The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the results to the architecture of the masking network itself. It is unclear how different architectural choices for the mask generator would influence the final saliency maps. An ablation study exploring this aspect would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about the method's generalizability.

### Minor Comments

For clarity and reproducibility, please address the following:

*   In Table 1, are the reported results calculated across all classes or only for correctly classified instances?
*   Which variant of LRP (e.g., epsilon-LRP, alpha-beta-LRP) and which specific parameter settings were used for the comparison? This information is essential for a fair and reproducible comparison.

### Post-Rebuttal Analysis and Recommendation

The authors' rebuttal confirms that the proposed saliency metric is highly dependent on the target classifier's robustness to changes in scale and aspect ratio. This admission reveals a critical limitation on the method's applicability, which has several important consequences that must be discussed in the final paper:

1.  **Limited Generality:** The method is not generally applicable to any black-box classifier, as claimed in the title. It is restricted to models that already exhibit strong scale and aspect-ratio invariance. This precludes its use for models that lack this property or for analyzing models during their training phase before such invariances have been fully learned.

2.  **Domain Restriction:** The applicability to other domains where such invariances are not guaranteed (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs) is questionable.

3.  **Ambiguous Interpretation:** The rebuttal also suggests a strong dependence on the architecture of the masking network. This raises a fundamental question: are the generated maps explaining the saliency of the target classifier, or are they primarily reflecting the biases and artifacts of the masking network itself? Without a clear answer, the interpretability of the method is compromised.

**Conclusion:** While the paper presents a novel and fast approach, its core evaluation is predicated on assumptions that severely limit its generality and interpretability. For the paper to be acceptable for publication, these limitations must be thoroughly and transparently discussed. The authors should explicitly state the constraints on the types of classifiers to which the method can be applied, revise the claim of general black-box applicability, and acknowledge the ambiguity regarding whether the explanation is of the target model or the mask generator. Without these critical additions, the paper's claims are not sufficiently supported, and it should be rejected.
