- Novel approach: trainable masking model as a black-box interface that predicts saliency masks in a single forward pass using a U-Net, avoiding iterative optimization.  
- Key contributions: new tight-crop saliency metric and competitive ImageNet weakly supervised localization (36.7% error vs. 34.3% fully supervised VGG).  
- High speed: real-time performance (~100 masks/sec on a standard GPU), significantly faster than iterative methods.  
- Architecture: U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder; design justified and validated with ablations (e.g., CIFAR-10 without pre-training).  
- New metric benefit: evaluates tight-crop classification confidence and yields results approaching ground-truth bounding boxes (Table 3).  
- Empirical results: outperforms weakly supervised baselines (including “Center” and “Max Box”) on ImageNet (Table 1).  
- Metric limitation: depends on classifier scale invariance—crops are resized without preserving aspect ratio, which can misrepresent classifier performance.  
- Applicability concern: metric and claims invalid for classifiers lacking scale invariance (e.g., non-square inputs or spectrogram models).  
- Overstated "black-box" claim: metric requirements and method constraints mean the approach is not universally applicable to arbitrary black-box classifiers.  
- Masking-model dependence: model is trained per classifier (AlexNet/GoogleNet/ResNet), raising ambiguity whether masks reflect classifier saliency or masking-model biases.  
- Need for further ablations: authors should vary masking architectures to demonstrate robustness and disentangle masking model bias from classifier saliency.  
- Missing discussion: paper should explicitly acknowledge metric constraints and classifier-dependence limitations.  
- Overall judgment: technically strong and useful but overreaches on generality; recommend weak acceptance contingent on addressing metric and bias issues.  
- Required revisions for acceptance: (1) qualify metric’s dependence on classifier properties, (2) address masking-model bias with ablations, and (3) revise the "black-box" narrative.
