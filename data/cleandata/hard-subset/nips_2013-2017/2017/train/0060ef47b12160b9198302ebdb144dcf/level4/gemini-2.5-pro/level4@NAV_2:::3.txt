This paper introduces a novel method for generating saliency maps for any differentiable black-box image classifier. The core idea is to train a separate masking model, based on a U-Net architecture, which learns to produce a high-quality saliency mask for a given image and class in a single feed-forward pass. This approach stands in contrast to previous work that relies on slow, iterative optimization procedures for each image. The masking model is trained to optimize a custom objective function that encourages masks to be smooth and to identify the smallest region that is sufficient for classification (SSR) and whose removal is destructive to classification (SDR). The authors also propose a new metric for evaluating saliency based on the concentration of class-information within a cropped region. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, where it achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task, outperforming other existing techniques.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution is the shift from slow, per-instance optimization for saliency map generation to a learned, amortized approach. By training a dedicated model, the method achieves real-time performance (over 100 images/sec), which is a significant advantage over iterative methods and opens up new applications, such as video saliency analysis.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing. The method substantially outperforms other weakly supervised approaches on the ImageNet localization benchmark, demonstrating its effectiveness. The reported localization error of 36.7% with a ResNet-50 backbone is competitive even with some fully supervised methods.
*   **Thoughtful Problem Formulation:** The paper provides a clear and intuitive discussion of the challenges in saliency detection, such as the problem of "introduced evidence" from masking. The proposed objective function is well-motivated, balancing mask area, classifier confidence, and smoothness to produce sharp and interpretable maps. The introduction of a new saliency metric is also a valuable contribution to the field.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The architectural choices and training procedures are explained in sufficient detail. The qualitative results in Figure 5 effectively showcase the high quality of the generated masks compared to alternatives.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

*   **Substantiation of Real-Time Claims:** While the processing speed is a key advantage, the claim could be strengthened with more details. Does the reported "100 images per second" figure include pre-processing steps such as resizing the input image to 224x224? It would be valuable for the authors to report how latency scales with larger input resolutions, such as those common in real-world applications (e.g., 640x480).
*   **Sensitivity to Object Scale:** The experiments primarily use 224x224 inputs where salient objects are often prominent. It is unclear how the method would perform on images where the object of interest is very small. Downsampling a high-resolution image to 224x224 might cause the loss of crucial details, potentially hindering the masking model's ability to generate an accurate saliency map. Including a qualitative or quantitative analysis on images with small objects would improve the paper.
*   **Incomplete Comparison with Proposed Metric:** The new saliency metric proposed in Section 3.2 is an interesting idea. However, in Table 3, it is used to compare the proposed method only against baselines and ground truth. A more compelling evaluation would involve computing this metric for the other competing saliency methods listed in Table 2 (such as Fong & Vedaldi [2]). This would provide a more direct and comprehensive comparison and better validate the superiority of the proposed masks.
