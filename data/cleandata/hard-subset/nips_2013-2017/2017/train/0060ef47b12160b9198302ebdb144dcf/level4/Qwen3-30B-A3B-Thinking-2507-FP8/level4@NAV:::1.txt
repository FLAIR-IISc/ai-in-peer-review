

The paper introduces a neural network-based approach for learning saliency masks, achieving high throughput (processing multiple images per second). The concept of learning to explain models is compelling, and the paper presents several promising ideas. However, the work is borderline, with arguments supporting both acceptance and rejection. While no obvious errors are present, significant limitations require addressing.

The saliency assessment relies on indirect metrics—weakly supervised localization or the proposed saliency metric—both with notable drawbacks. Weakly supervised localization is problematic as an evaluation metric: if contextual cues are critical for classification, object localization may not correlate with saliency quality. The results using this method are interesting but carry a significant caveat for its use as a saliency quality measure. The proposed saliency metric also has limitations due to its implementation: the estimated salient region is cropped, rescaled to the original image size while preserving aspect ratio, and reclassified. This introduces two artifacts: (1) aspect ratio changes may distort object representation for classification, and (2) the metric favors small salient regions, which are heavily upscaled during reclassification. This scaling may present non-ideal object scales to the classifier, potentially violating assumptions about scale invariance (which CNNs must learn and may not fully possess).

The paper does not address how the masking model's performance depends on the underlying network architecture. Crucially, it omits whether experiments were conducted with alternative architectures and how architectural choices influenced results.

Minor comments:  
- Clarify whether Table 1 results are aggregated across all classes or specific to the correct class.  
- Specify the exact LRP variant and parameter settings (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) used for comparison.

Post-rebuttal note:  
The reliance of the saliency metric on the classifier’s scale invariance severely restricts applicability. The method is only viable for classifiers exhibiting this invariance, which has critical implications:  
1. It cannot be applied during model training or to classifiers lacking scale invariance.  
2. It is not generalizable to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs).  
3. The claim of applicability to "black-box classifiers" in the title is invalid.  
Additionally, the rebuttal suggests strong dependence on the masking network architecture, raising ambiguity: is the method visualizing saliency for the U-network or the masking network itself?  

If these limitations (metric dependencies, architectural sensitivity, and restricted applicability) are thoroughly discussed in the paper, it may be acceptable for publication. If not, rejection is warranted.
