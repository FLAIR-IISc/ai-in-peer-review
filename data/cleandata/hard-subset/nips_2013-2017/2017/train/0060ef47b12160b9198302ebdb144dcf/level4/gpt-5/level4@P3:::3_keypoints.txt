- Summary: Proposes a trainable, class-conditional U-Net masker that produces saliency maps in a single forward pass by optimizing for smallest sufficient/destroying regions (SSR/SDR) with TV and randomized evidence removal.  
- Relation to prior work: Learns a dedicated masking network as a fast alternative to iterative saliency optimization and complements gradient-based/iterative methods.

Strengths:
- Novel single-pass, model-specific masking network that approximates iterative saliency optimization with clear SSR/SDR motivation.  
- Practical defenses against introduced evidence and artifacts (randomized alternative images, TV regularization, down/up-sampling) with convincing qualitative results.  
- Introduces a simple saliency metric s(a,p) aligning with SSR that the method performs well on.  
- Strong empirical results on weakly supervised localization, approaching fully supervised performance in some cases.  
- Clear exposition of training tricks (fake class sampling) and sensible architecture choices (ResNet-50 encoder, class embedding, feature filter).

Weaknesses / concerns:
- “Black box” claim is misleading: training requires backprop through the fixed classifier (gradient access), not score-only black-box access.  
- Real-time throughput claim (100 imgs/s) lacks clarity on whether it includes pre/post-processing and classifier forward pass, and needs evaluation for higher resolutions and batch size 1.  
- Unclear performance on small, off-center, or multiple instances—qualitative examples mostly show large centered objects.  
- Masking model must be trained per classifier; cross-classifier transfer or a universal masker is not evaluated.  
- Missing ablations on loss weights (λ1–λ4), alternative image choices, mask resolution/upsampling, and comparison to iterative optimization under the same metric.  
- Saliency metric is somewhat ad hoc (area threshold 0.05); needs sensitivity analysis, confidence intervals, and discussion of resizing effects on p.  
- Comparisons: the new metric should be reported for all baselines (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi) to align tables and claims.

Quality:
- Method is technically sound and well motivated with strong empirical localization results; additional ablations and small-object analysis would improve completeness.

Clarity:
- Paper is well written and organized; objectives, architecture, and figures are clear, with only minor editorial issues.

Originality:
- Learning a dedicated single-pass masking network combined with SSR/SDR loss and randomized evidence removal is a novel and useful contribution relative to prior iterative/gradient methods.

Significance:
- Potentially impactful for real-time/interactive applications if speed claims and small-object performance are validated.

Questions for authors:
- Does 100 images/s include resizing, mask upsampling, edited image formation, and the classifier forward pass; what is throughput at higher resolutions and batch size 1?  
- How does performance vary with object size; provide quantitative breakdowns by object size.  
- Can you report the proposed saliency metric for all methods in Table 2 (including Fong & Vedaldi)?  
- How sensitive are results to λ1–λ4, choice of alternative image A, and mask resolution; include ablations.  
- Can a masker trained on one classifier generalize to others, or can a single model handle multiple classifiers?

Minor comments and typos:
- Ln 273: “More over” → “Moreover”; Ln 151: “optimise” → “optimises”; clarify “black box” (differentiable vs score-only); report CPU/GPU types for throughput.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and practical single-pass saliency approach with strong qualitative and localization results.  
- Thoughtful SSR/SDR objective and artifact suppression strategies; useful new metric aligned with interpretability.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Real-time claim needs clearer, resolution-dependent evidence.  
- Insufficient analysis of small-object and multi-instance scenarios.  
- Missing ablations and full metric comparisons to all baselines.
