

The paper proposes a neural network-based approach for learning saliency masks, achieving real-time performance (processing multiple images per second). I consider this paper borderline and would not object to either rejection or acceptance. I strongly support the concept of learning to explain models and acknowledge the paper presents several promising ideas. However, while no obvious errors are present, significant limitations require careful discussion.

The saliency is measured indirectly via weakly supervised localization or the proposed saliency metric, both of which have clear limitations that the paper fails to address adequately. Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect quality metric: if an object's context is essential for classification, its localization may not correlate with saliency quality. The results using this method are interesting but carry a substantial caveat when used to evaluate saliency. The saliency metric itself is flawed due to its implementation: the estimated salient region is cropped, then rescaled to the original image size while preserving aspect ratio. This process introduces two artifacts: (1) aspect ratio changes may degrade classification performance, and (2) the metric favors small salient regions; since these are heavily upscaled for reclassification, the object's presentation scale to the classifier may be suboptimal (convolutional networks exhibit translation invariance but require explicit learning for scale invariance, which has inherent limits). Additionally, the paper omits discussion of how much the masking model depends on the underlying architecture—did the authors experiment with different architectures and observe impacts on results?

Minor comments:  
- Were Table 1 results computed across all classes or only the correct class?  
- Please specify which LRP variant (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) and parameter settings were used for comparison, as multiple variants exist with distinct parameters.

Post-rebuttal edit:  
The dependence of the saliency metric on the classifier's quality and scale invariance fundamentally limits the method's applicability. It can only be applied to classifiers exhibiting this invariance, with critical consequences:  
- The method is inapplicable during model training or for classifiers lacking this invariance.  
- It cannot be generalized to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs).  
- It does not support black-box classifiers as claimed in the title.  
Furthermore, the rebuttal suggests strong dependence on the masking network architecture, raising ambiguity: it remains unclear whether the method visualizes the saliency of the U-network or the masking network itself. If these limitations are thoroughly discussed in the paper, it would be balanced enough for publication; otherwise, it should not be accepted.
