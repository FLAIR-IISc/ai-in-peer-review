- Proposes a neural-network method to learn fast saliency masks for explanation-like visualizations.  
- Strength: clear motivation and efficient implementation for learning saliency masks.  
- Strength: empirical results are sensible and weakly supervised localization (WSL) findings are interesting.  
- Strength: positioned as model-agnostic and computationally practical.  
- Concern: WSL is a problematic proxy for saliency because contextual cues may make localization unrelated to true saliency.  
- Concern: The saliency metric’s crop-and-rescale procedure introduces confounds by altering aspect ratio and object scale, affecting classifier performance independently of saliency.  
- Concern: The metric implicitly favors smaller regions which, when upscaled, may expose classifier scale-sensitivity rather than saliency quality.  
- Concern: Validity of the metric depends on classifier invariances (scale/aspect/distribution robustness), limiting applicability to models during training, other domains, and truly arbitrary black-box classifiers.  
- Concern: The masking network’s architecture likely influences learned masks; it’s unclear whether masks reflect the target model’s saliency or the masking network’s inductive biases.  
- Request: Report experiments with multiple masking architectures/capacities and analyze stability and architectural influence on masks and performance.  
- Reporting issue: Clarify whether Table 1 reports results for all classes or only the predicted/correct class per image.  
- Reporting issue: Specify which LRP variant(s) and parameter settings (epsilon, alpha-beta, values) were used for reproducibility.  
- Suggestion: Discuss WSL limitations and cropping/rescaling artifacts and consider alternative evaluations (deletion/insertion without geometric distortion, counterfactual occlusion, human studies, diagnostic experiments).  
- Post-rebuttal: Rebuttal reinforced concerns about dependence on classifier invariance and masking-network design, limiting generality and portability.  
- Recommendation: Borderline—supports the idea but asks for explicit limitations, recalibrated claims, and additional diagnostics/analyses before stronger acceptance.
