The paper introduces an approach to learning saliency masks using a neural network, capable of processing multiple images per second, thus demonstrating efficiency.

In my opinion, the paper falls on the borderline, and I would not strongly object to either acceptance or rejection. I appreciate the concept of learning to explain a model, and the paper presents some valuable ideas.

Although there are no apparent errors, there are noticeable limitations. 
- The saliency measurement is indirect, relying on either weakly supervised localization or the proposed saliency metric, both of which have limitations that should be discussed in the paper.
Weakly supervised localization is not a perfect measure, as object context can be crucial in determining its class, and object localization may not correlate with saliency quality. The results on weakly supervised localization are interesting but should be interpreted with caution when used as a quality metric for saliency.
The saliency metric also has limitations due to its application. The estimated salient region is cropped and rescaled to the original image size, maintaining the aspect ratio, which could introduce artefacts. The change in aspect ratio might impact classification, and the preference for small salient regions may lead to suboptimal scale presentation to the classifier, as convolutional networks generally learn translation invariance but have limited scaling invariance.

The paper does not discuss the masking model's dependence on the architecture for learning masks. It would be beneficial to know if the authors experimented with different architectures and their impact on the results.

Minor comments:
- Please clarify whether the results in Table 1 are for all classes or only the correct class.
- Specify the LRP variant and parameter setting used for comparison, as different variants and parameters (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) exist.

Post-rebuttal edit:
The effectiveness of the saliency metric heavily relies on the classifier's quality and scale invariance, significantly limiting the proposed method's applicability. This method can only be applied to networks with this invariance, which has important consequences:
- The method is not suitable for models during the training phase or those lacking this invariance.
- Its applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs) is limited.
- The method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers, as claimed in the title.
Furthermore, the response suggests a strong dependence on the masking network, making it unclear whether the saliency of the U-network or the masking network is being visualized.
If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, I believe it is balanced enough for publication. Otherwise, it should not be published.
