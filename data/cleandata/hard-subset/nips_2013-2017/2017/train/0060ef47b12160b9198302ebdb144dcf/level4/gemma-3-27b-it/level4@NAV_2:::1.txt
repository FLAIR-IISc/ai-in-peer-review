The paper proposes a novel approach to rapidly estimate saliency maps for differentiable image classifiers by training a masking model to predict salient regions in a single forward pass. This circumvents the iterative nature of existing methods, enabling real-time applications. The authors demonstrate competitive results on ImageNet localization and introduce a new saliency metric.

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of explainable AI, addressing the need for efficient and accurate saliency detection. The core idea of learning a saliency mask generator is sound and demonstrably effective, achieving impressive speed and visual quality. The experiments on both ImageNet and CIFAR-10 are well-executed and provide strong evidence for the method’s generalizability. The introduction of a new saliency metric is a positive step towards more rigorous evaluation in this area. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a thorough review of related work, including gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative patch removal techniques [17], and optimization-based approaches [1, 2]. The connections to previous work, such as highlighting the limitations of iterative methods and building upon the model-agnostic spirit of Fong and Vedaldi [2], are well-articulated.

However, several limitations should be addressed. While the weakly supervised localization results are promising, the authors acknowledge the inherent disconnect between localization accuracy and true saliency, especially when contextual information is crucial for classification [6]. The proposed saliency metric, while novel, is also susceptible to artifacts introduced by the resizing operation applied to the cropped salient region. Specifically, altering the aspect ratio and scale during resizing could potentially impact classification performance and bias the metric towards smaller, but potentially less representative, regions. The impact of the masking model architecture on the resulting saliency maps is not sufficiently explored. Further investigation into the sensitivity of the approach to different network architectures would strengthen the findings. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The proposed method offers a significant speed improvement over iterative saliency detection techniques, enabling real-time applications.
*   The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on standard benchmarks and showcase the method’s generalizability across datasets and classifiers.
*   The introduction of a new saliency metric provides a valuable tool for evaluating saliency detection methods.
*   The approach is model-agnostic, meaning it can be applied to any differentiable classifier.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The evaluation metrics (weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric) have inherent limitations and may not fully capture the quality of saliency explanations.
*   The potential impact of the resizing operation on the proposed saliency metric is not adequately addressed.
*   The sensitivity of the approach to the masking model architecture is not thoroughly investigated.
*   The reliance on scale and translation invariance in the underlying classifier limits the general applicability of the proposed method, contradicting the claim of being applicable to *any* differentiable classifier. The method may not be suitable for models lacking these invariances or for applications beyond standard image classification (e.g., spectrogram analysis).
