

The paper introduces a novel model-based approach for real-time image saliency detection applicable to any differentiable classifier, addressing a key limitation of iterative methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017; Zhou et al., 2016). Unlike prior work that requires hundreds of classifier evaluations per image, the proposed masking model predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass, achieving 100 FPS on standard GPUs. The authors develop a new metric, $s(a, p) = \log(\tilde{a}) - \log(p)$, which better assesses interpretability than standard localization accuracy by evaluating the smallest rectangular crop preserving classification confidence. This metric demonstrates that their method achieves saliency quality comparable to ground-truth bounding boxes (Table 3), while outperforming weakly supervised baselines (e.g., 36.7% localization error on ImageNet vs. VGG's 34.3% in fully supervised settings). The work also rigorously addresses adversarial artifacts through smoothness constraints (TV penalty) and randomized evidence removal (Eq. 2–6), a critical gap in existing methods.  

**Strengths:**  
- **Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with comprehensive experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, and the new metric provides a demonstrably better evaluation framework. The masking model generalizes well across classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and datasets without task-specific fine-tuning.  
- **Originality:** The shift from iterative to model-based saliency detection is novel, and the adversarial artifact mitigation strategy (Section 3.1) significantly advances the field beyond prior black-box methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi).  
- **Significance:** Enables real-time applications (e.g., autonomous vehicles, video analysis) previously infeasible with iterative methods, and demonstrates strong weakly supervised localisation performance without explicit segmentation data.  
- **Clarity:** Well-organized, with clear problem formulation (Section 3), precise technical contributions (Section 4), and effective visualizations (Figures 1, 5, 6).  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Significance depth:** While real-time capability is compelling, the paper could strengthen significance by elaborating on specific deployment scenarios (e.g., latency-sensitive autonomous driving systems) beyond the generic "video-saliency" mention in Section 6.  
- **Bias analysis:** The introduction highlights classifier biases (e.g., snow vs. polar bear), but the experiments lack analysis of whether the saliency maps reveal similar biases in the learned masks (e.g., does the model prioritize background cues for certain classes?).  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Model-based speed (100 FPS vs. iterative methods), novel $s(a,p)$ metric, robust artifact mitigation, strong weakly supervised results (Table 1–3), generalization to CIFAR-10 without pretrained biases (Section 5.3).  
*Con:* Limited concrete real-time application examples; insufficient analysis of bias in salient regions.  

The paper squarely addresses NIPS' scope on interpretable machine learning, with contributions that advance both methodology (real-time saliency) and evaluation (new metric). It is technically rigorous, well-motivated, and significantly outperforms state-of-the-art weakly supervised approaches. With minor expansion on application contexts and bias analysis, this would be an exceptional contribution. I recommend acceptance.
