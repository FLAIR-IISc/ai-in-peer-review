This paper proposes a novel neural network-based approach for generating saliency maps. The model is trained to predict image regions that are most influential for the predictions of a separate, pre-trained object categorization network. The core idea is interesting, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this work lies in its comprehensive empirical evaluation. The authors demonstrate that the proposed method significantly outperforms prior work on several challenging, large-scale datasets. The validation against state-of-the-art deep learning models for object recognition effectively showcases the relevance and scalability of the approach.
2.  **Novelty and Efficiency:** The method provides an elegant and efficient solution for generating saliency maps. Compared to traditional gradient-based or perturbation-based methods that can be computationally intensive, the proposed model produces saliency maps in a single forward pass, making it suitable for applications where speed is a concern.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is clearly structured and the methodology is explained well, making the work accessible to the reader.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

While this is a strong paper, there are a couple of areas that could be strengthened to increase its impact:

1.  **Motivation for Real-Time Efficiency:** The paper correctly identifies computational efficiency as a key advantage of the proposed method. However, the motivation for why real-time saliency prediction is necessary could be more explicitly detailed. The introduction would benefit from a discussion of specific downstream applications where this capability is critical. For example, real-time model interpretability could be invaluable in fields like autonomous driving (to verify the vehicle's focus), interactive robotics, or high-throughput medical image analysis. Providing concrete use-cases would better ground the contribution in practical needs.
2.  **Qualitative Analysis of Saliency Maps:** The analysis is currently focused on quantitative metrics, which convincingly show that the method works well. However, the paper would be significantly enhanced by a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the learned salient regions. Such an analysis could offer valuable insights into the inner workings and potential biases of the object recognition models being explained. For instance, do the saliency maps reveal cases where a classifier is exploiting biases in the background to recognize an object (e.g., identifying a "boat" by focusing on the water)? Are there specific object categories where the salient regions are counter-intuitive or surprisingly sparse? Including a section dedicated to these kinds of observations would add a compelling layer of scientific insight beyond the strong engineering contribution.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, efficient, and effective method for saliency prediction. The work represents a valuable contribution to the important and growing field of model interpretability. I recommend acceptance. Addressing the minor suggestions regarding application motivation and deeper qualitative analysis would further strengthen an already excellent submission.
