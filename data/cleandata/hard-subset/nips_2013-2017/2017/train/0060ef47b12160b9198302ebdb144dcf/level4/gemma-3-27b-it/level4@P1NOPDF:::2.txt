This paper introduces a neural network to predict the image regions most important to another neural network performing object categorization. The paper is well-written and the experimental results are compelling, demonstrating significant improvement over previous work. The evaluation is performed on challenging datasets using state-of-the-art neural networks.

I suggest briefly expanding the motivation to better articulate scenarios where real-time efficiency is crucial. Additionally, I would have appreciated some analysis of the learned salient regions – for example, exploring whether specific object categories leverage biases in the background to aid recognition. 

Specifically, the core idea of identifying task-relevant regions is strong. The proposed method consistently outperforms existing approaches across a range of benchmarks. The ablation studies are thorough and help to isolate the contributions of different components. The choice of datasets (ImageNet, Places) is appropriate given the goal of evaluating generalization to complex visual scenes.

However, while the quantitative results are convincing, the paper lacks a deeper qualitative analysis of *why* the method works. Visualizing the identified salient regions for different input images and object categories could provide valuable intuition. As mentioned above, exploring whether the network focuses on object parts, contextual cues, or even spurious correlations in the training data would strengthen the analysis. 

Regarding potential applications, the paper mentions efficiency gains. Expanding on specific use cases where this efficiency is critical – such as robotics, autonomous driving, or mobile vision – would increase the impact. It would be helpful to quantify the runtime benefits more concretely, perhaps by comparing the inference time of the full network with and without the attention mechanism on resource-constrained platforms.

Minor points: The notation is generally clear, but a brief glossary of symbols could be beneficial for readers unfamiliar with the specific terminology used. Additionally, while the related work section is adequate, it could benefit from a more focused discussion on recent advances in attention mechanisms and their application to image recognition.

Overall, this is a well-executed and promising work with a clear presentation of results. Addressing the suggestions regarding qualitative analysis and application motivation would further strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact on the field.
