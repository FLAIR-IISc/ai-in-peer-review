### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method for generating saliency maps for any differentiable black-box image classifier. The primary motivation is to overcome the speed limitations of iterative optimization-based methods while producing higher-quality maps than faster gradient-based techniques. The core idea is to train a separate "masking model," based on a U-Net architecture with a ResNet-50 encoder, to generate a saliency mask for a given image and class in a single feed-forward pass.

The masking model is trained to minimize a well-formulated objective function that combines several desiderata for a good saliency map. This objective encourages the mask to identify both the smallest sufficient region (SSR) for classification and the smallest destroying region (SDR), while simultaneously penalizing the mask's total area and promoting spatial smoothness to prevent adversarial artifacts. To evaluate the quality of the generated maps, the authors propose a new saliency metric that measures the "concentration of information" by assessing the classifier's confidence on a tight crop of the salient region relative to the crop's area.

Experiments are conducted on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, using AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50 as the black-box classifiers to be explained. The proposed method demonstrates state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, outperforming prior work. The authors claim the method is fast enough for real-time applications, capable of processing over 100 images per second.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Approach:** The central contribution—amortizing the search for a saliency map by training a dedicated generative model—is a compelling and novel idea. It effectively reframes the problem from slow, per-instance optimization to fast inference, which could enable new real-time applications for model interpretability (e.g., in video analysis or autonomous systems) that are infeasible with previous high-quality methods like [2].

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper provides strong quantitative evidence of its effectiveness. The method achieves a new state-of-the-art in weakly supervised object localization on ImageNet, significantly outperforming previous approaches. This demonstrates that the learned masks capture semantically meaningful and well-localized object parts.

3.  **Thoughtful Methodological Design:** The objective function in Equation 4 is well-motivated, elegantly combining concepts of sufficiency (SSR), necessity (SDR), sparsity (area minimization), and smoothness (TV penalty). The introduction of a new saliency metric is also a valuable contribution, providing a more direct way to assess whether a saliency method preserves class-discriminative information within a compact region.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established, the technical details of the model and training procedure are explained thoroughly, and the figures (e.g., Figure 5) provide strong qualitative support for the claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Clarity on "Real-Time" Performance:** The claim of processing 100 images/sec is impressive, but it would be more impactful with additional details.
    *   Does this timing account for pre-processing steps like resizing the input image to 224x224?
    *   How does the performance scale to larger, more realistic image resolutions, such as 640x480 or megapixel-sized images? Providing performance figures for larger inputs would help substantiate the "real-time" claim for a broader range of applications.

2.  **Performance on Small Objects:** The experiments and examples primarily feature images with large, centrally-located objects. The ResNet-50 encoder used in the masking model has a large downsampling factor (32x), which raises concerns about its ability to generate accurate masks for small objects whose features might be lost or diminished in the bottleneck feature map. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion of this potential limitation or to present results on a dataset known to contain smaller objects.

3.  **Incomplete Comparison using the Proposed Metric:** The new saliency metric is an interesting contribution for evaluating interpretability. However, in Table 3, the comparison is missing a key and highly relevant baseline: the iterative optimization method of Fong and Vedaldi [2]. Since this method is a main point of comparison in the localization experiments (Table 2), including it in the evaluation under the new metric would provide a more complete and convincing assessment.

4.  **Practical Overhead:** The approach requires training a separate, complex masking model for each black-box classifier that needs to be explained. This introduces significant training time and computational overhead compared to methods that work directly with a given model without requiring their own training phase. A brief discussion of this trade-off between inference speed and setup cost would add valuable context for practitioners.

**Minor Comments**

*   Line 151: "difference that [2] only optimise the mask" -> "difference that [2] only optimises the mask"
*   Line 273: "More over, because our model" -> "Moreover, because our model"

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel, well-executed, and high-performing method for saliency map generation. The weaknesses identified are primarily areas where the evaluation and discussion could be strengthened, rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. The work represents a significant advance in making high-quality model explanations practical for real-time use.
