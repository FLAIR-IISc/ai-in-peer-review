### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel method for generating saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier, treating it as a black box. The core contribution is to train a separate generative model, based on a U-Net architecture, to predict a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This "masking model" is trained to optimize a composite objective function that balances several desirable properties for a saliency map: 1) ensuring the classifier can still recognize the class from the masked-in region (akin to finding a "smallest sufficient region"), 2) ensuring the classifier fails when the masked-in region is removed (a "smallest destroying region"), 3) encouraging a small mask area, and 4) promoting mask smoothness via a total variation penalty.

This approach is contrasted with prior work, particularly iterative, optimization-based methods that are computationally expensive as they must optimize a mask for each new image. By learning a mapping from image to mask, the proposed method achieves real-time performance. The authors also introduce a new metric for saliency that measures the concentration of class-relevant information within a cropped region. The method is validated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on the weakly-supervised object localization task and producing qualitatively sharp and interpretable masks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Originality:** The central idea of framing saliency map generation as a prediction task for a trained generative model, rather than an iterative optimization problem for each individual image, is highly original. This is a significant conceptual shift from previous perturbation-based methods.

2.  **Performance and Speed:** The method's primary advantage is its efficiency. Achieving real-time performance (>100 FPS) is a major practical breakthrough compared to iterative techniques. Furthermore, this speed does not come at the cost of quality; the experimental results on the ImageNet weakly-supervised localization task are state-of-the-art, outperforming other weakly-supervised methods and even approaching the performance of some fully-supervised techniques.

3.  **High-Quality Results:** The qualitative results shown in the figures are compelling. The generated masks are sharp, well-defined, and appear more precise than those from competing methods like gradient-based approaches or the iterative method of Fong and Vedaldi [2].

4.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The design of the objective function (Equation 4) is well-reasoned, combining principles from both "smallest sufficient region" and "smallest destroying region" concepts, along with practical regularizers for smoothness and size. The introduction of a new saliency metric is also a thoughtful contribution to the field.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed method is explained in sufficient detail, and the experiments are thorough and well-designed.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Motivation for Real-Time Applications:** The paper’s main selling point is its real-time capability. While video saliency is mentioned as future work, the introduction would be stronger if it provided more concrete examples of applications where real-time saliency is a critical need. For example, explaining its potential utility in interactive debugging tools, safety monitoring for autonomous systems, or real-time feedback for human-in-the-loop systems would better frame the significance of the speedup.

2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of Classifier Behavior:** The paper successfully demonstrates *that* the method can find salient regions, but it stops short of using this powerful tool to provide new scientific insights into the black-box classifiers themselves. A qualitative analysis section would be a valuable addition. For instance, the introduction mentions the "polar bear vs. snow" problem. It would be highly insightful to see if the trained masking model actually confirms such biases by generating masks that highlight snow for the "polar bear" class when analyzing a model like AlexNet. Showing examples where different classifiers (e.g., AlexNet vs. ResNet) attend to different features for the same class would elevate the paper from a strong methodological contribution to one that also advances our understanding of deep neural networks.

3.  **Potential Biases of the Masking Model:** The masking model itself is a complex neural network (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder). There is a risk that its own inductive biases could influence the saliency maps it produces. For example, since the encoder is pre-trained on ImageNet, it may be biased towards producing "object-like" masks regardless of the black-box classifier's true reasoning. While the authors acknowledge this as an area for future work, a brief discussion of this potential limitation in the main body of the paper would make the work more complete.

**Qualitative Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by strong quantitative results on challenging benchmarks (ImageNet, CIFAR-10) and convincing qualitative examples. The work is complete and well-executed.
*   **Clarity:** High. The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivations are clear, the method is described in detail, and the figures are informative.
*   **Originality:** High. The core idea of training a separate network to predict saliency maps in a single pass is a novel and significant departure from previous iterative methods. The proposed objective function and saliency metric are also valuable contributions.
*   **Significance:** High. This work has the potential to be very influential. By making high-quality saliency map generation a real-time process, it opens up new applications and research directions in model interpretability. The combination of superior performance, speed, and model-agnosticism makes it a highly practical and powerful tool that other researchers and practitioners are likely to adopt and build upon.
