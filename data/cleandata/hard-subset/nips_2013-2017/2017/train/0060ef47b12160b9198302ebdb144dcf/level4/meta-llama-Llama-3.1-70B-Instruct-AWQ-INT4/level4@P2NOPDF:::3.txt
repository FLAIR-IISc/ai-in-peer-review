This paper proposes an image saliency mask generation approach that achieves high efficiency, processing a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The approach involves training a masking model that identifies the tightest rectangular crop containing the entire salient region of a specific class, as determined by a black box classifier such as AlexNet, GoogleNet, or ResNet. The model architecture leverages image feature maps, such as those generated by ResNet-50, across various scales. The final scale feature is passed through a feature filter for initial localization, while subsequent upsampling blocks refine the produced masks. Experimental results demonstrate that this method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques in the ImageNet localization task.

The paper appears to provide sufficient references and related works, although a thorough examination is not feasible within the review timeframe. The technical correctness of the paper also seems satisfactory, but a comprehensive verification is not possible. The authors present insightful discussions and intuitions on their approach design, and the overall presentation is well-organized.

The paper presents interesting technical results, but some concerns arise regarding the real-time speed claim and its applicability to real-world images. Specifically, it is unclear whether the reported processing time of 100 images per second includes image resizing operations. Additionally, it would be beneficial to know the running time for larger images, such as 640x480 images taken from an iPhone 6s. 

Furthermore, the paper primarily focuses on salient objects of considerable size. It remains uncertain how the approach would perform if the requested class object is small in the images. Would a 224x224 image be sufficient in such cases?
