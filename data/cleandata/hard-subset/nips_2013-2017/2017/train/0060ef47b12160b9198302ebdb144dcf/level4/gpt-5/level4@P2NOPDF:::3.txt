This paper proposes a fast saliency-mask generation method that processes roughly 100 images per second at 224×224 resolution on a standard GPU. The approach trains a masking model to produce the tightest rectangular crop that encloses the salient region for a specified class, as determined by a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogLeNet, or ResNet). The architecture consumes multi-scale image feature maps (e.g., from ResNet-50). Features at the final scale are passed through a feature-filter module for initial localization, followed by upsampling blocks that refine the mask. Experiments indicate that the method outperforms other weakly supervised approaches on the ImageNet localization task.

The related work appears broadly sufficient, though I did not exhaustively verify coverage. The method appears technically sound, but I did not perform a complete correctness check. The paper presents useful intuition and design rationale, and the overall presentation is clear.

Overall, the paper reports interesting technical results. My main concerns are about the real-time speed claim and the applicability to diverse, real-world images.

Comments:
- Throughput claim: Does the 100 images/second figure include all preprocessing, such as image resizing, and postprocessing steps? Please clarify what is included in the timing pipeline. Additionally, what is the runtime for larger inputs (e.g., 640×480 photos from an iPhone 6s)? If the model requires resizing to 224×224, please report end-to-end time, including resize, and discuss the trade-off between input resolution and localization accuracy.
- Small objects: Many examples in the paper appear to involve relatively large salient objects. How does the method perform when the requested class occupies a small fraction of the image? Is a 224×224 input sufficient for such cases, or is performance sensitive to downsampling? Any ablations on input resolution or multi-scale inference would help.
- Comparability of metrics: In Table 3, can you report the corresponding metric for the other methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., Feed [2]) to enable a fair, apples-to-apples comparison?
