Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency method for differentiable image classifiers. Instead of computing a saliency mask per image via iterative optimization, the authors train a “masking model” (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) to predict class-conditional masks in a single forward pass. The training objective combines: (1) a total variation regularizer to encourage smooth masks and reduce adversarial artifacts, (2) an area penalty, (3) a term that encourages the preserved region to retain high confidence for the target class (SSR-like), and (4) a term that encourages the removed region to suppress that class (SDR-like). To reduce artifacts introduced by masking, they randomly choose alternative image contents (blur or noise/color) for the removed region. They also propose a new evaluation metric inspired by the “smallest sufficient region” idea: find the tightest rectangular crop enclosing the predicted salient region, resize it to the classifier’s input size, and score s(a,p)=log(max(a,0.05))−log(p), where a is area fraction and p is the target-class probability. Experiments on ImageNet (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) and CIFAR-10 show sharp, interpretable masks, competitive weakly supervised localization, and real-time throughput.

Relative to previous work, this extends gradient/backprop-based saliency (e.g., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop) and optimization-based perturbation methods (e.g., Zhou et al., Cao et al., Fong & Vedaldi) by training a feed-forward predictor for saliency, combining SSR and SDR terms and carefully addressing artifact induction.

Strengths
- A well-motivated and carefully engineered objective that addresses both SSR and SDR, with explicit measures against adversarial artifacts.
- A practical, fast approach that produces high-quality, sharp masks in a single pass; throughput is sufficient for real-time applications.
- Clear improvements on weakly supervised localization vs. prior saliency/weak-supervision baselines; the AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet experiments are informative.
- The proposed metric targets interpretability (SSR) and helps compare methods beyond localization overlap.
- Sensible training tricks (fake labels to force class-conditioning; randomized alternative image A) and an architecture that leverages multi-scale features.

Weaknesses and limitations
Evaluation metrics:
- Localization is an imperfect proxy for saliency. Context that is crucial for classification (e.g., “snow” for “polar bear”) may be non-overlapping with object bounding boxes; thus localization gains do not necessarily imply better explanations. This caveat should be discussed explicitly.
- The proposed saliency metric depends on cropping and then resizing the crop to the classifier’s fixed input size, disregarding aspect ratio. This creates two confounds: (a) aspect-ratio distortion may change class probability p independent of saliency quality; (b) the metric inherently favors very small crops, which, after upscaling, present the object at non-native scales that many CNNs are not invariant to. In effect, the metric’s validity depends on the classifier’s scale/aspect-ratio invariance, which is only approximate and dataset/model dependent.

Claimed scope and “black-box” status:
- The method is trained per classifier and depends on the classifier’s response characteristics (including scale/shape invariance) to be a faithful explainer. As a result, the “generally applicable to black-box classifiers” claim seems too strong. In particular: (a) the metric cannot be trusted for classifiers lacking robust scale invariance; (b) using the approach during model training (when invariances are not yet established) may be problematic; and (c) the applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrograms) is unclear without domain-specific evaluation and potentially different evidence-removal strategies.

Dependence on the masking network:
- The learned masks may reflect inductive biases of the masking architecture as much as properties of the classifier being explained. While the CIFAR-10 from-scratch experiment helps, the paper would benefit from ablations across masking architectures (e.g., varying encoder depth, with/without pretraining, different decoders) and analyses that disentangle the masking model’s bias from the black-box model’s saliency. For example, sanity checks where the classifier’s weights are randomized should collapse the saliency; cross-model transfer (train masking model on classifier A, evaluate against B) could further probe dependence.

Experimental clarity and completeness:
- Table 1: Please clarify whether localization is evaluated using the ground-truth label only (as suggested) or across all classes per image.
- Comparisons: Specify precisely which variants and hyperparameters were used for competing methods (e.g., LRP variant and settings, if LRP is included; parameters for Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop, CAM/Grad-CAM/CAM variants).
- Report sensitivity of results to the alternative-image choices for masking (blur vs. noise/color, blur strength), and to the objective weights (λ1–λ4).
- Provide failure cases and an analysis of when the metric/localization disagree with human intuition.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The approach is technically sound and thoughtfully regularized. The main concerns are the reliance on a particular metric with known confounds, and the lack of ablations isolating architectural dependence.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Please add a limitations section discussing metric dependence on scale invariance, the scope of “black-box” claims, and masking-network bias. Clarify evaluation protocols and baselines as noted above.
- Originality: Training a feed-forward masking model supervised only by classifier responses, combining SSR and SDR, and carefully mitigating artifacts is a meaningful step beyond prior iterative perturbation methods.
- Significance: A real-time, high-quality saliency predictor could be impactful for interactive and video scenarios. However, the current evaluation leaves open questions about generality across classifiers, training phases, and domains.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Discuss the limitations of both localization and the proposed metric; consider metric variants that preserve aspect ratio (e.g., crop-and-pad), evaluate across multiple rescaling factors, or use masked images with randomized A for scoring rather than crops.
- Add ablations across masking architectures and encoders; report training from scratch on ImageNet-scale data or with lighter encoders to probe dependence.
- Include robustness checks: stability of masks under input rescaling/cropping; sensitivity to A; classifier-randomization sanity checks.
- Temper the “black-box, generally applicable” claim; delineate conditions under which the method and metric are reliable.
- Expand comparisons with precise settings, and clarify Table 1’s evaluation label protocol.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, fast, and high-quality saliency maps; strong qualitative results.
- Novel training objective combining SSR and SDR, with artifact mitigation.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization and a thoughtful new metric.

Cons for acceptance
- Evaluation relies on metrics with important, currently unaddressed confounds (scale/aspect-ratio invariance).
- Overstated generality; method’s effectiveness and metric validity depend on classifier properties.
- Limited ablations and analysis of masking-network dependence; unclear how much saliency reflects the explainer vs. the target.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. I would support acceptance if the paper (i) clearly discusses the metric’s limitations and classifier invariance requirements, (ii) tempers the generality claims, and (iii) adds architectural ablations and evaluation clarifications. Without these, I lean toward rejection.
