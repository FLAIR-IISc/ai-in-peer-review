

This paper proposes an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing 100 images of size 224×224 per second on a standard GPU. The method trains a masking model to identify the tightest bounding rectangle containing the entire salient region for a specified class, as determined by a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet). The architecture utilizes multi-scale feature maps (e.g., from ResNet-50), where the final-scale features pass through a feature filter for initial localization, followed by upsampling blocks to refine the masks. Experiments demonstrate that this approach outperforms other weakly supervised methods on the ImageNet localization task.  

The paper includes sufficient references and related work discussion. Technical correctness appears sound, though a full verification is not performed. The authors provide several insightful discussions on their design choices, and the presentation is clear and well-organized.  

However, I remain concerned about the real-time speed claim and practical applicability to real-world images. Specifically:  
- Does the 100 images/second throughput include image resizing? If so, what is the processing time for larger images (e.g., 640×480, typical of iPhone 6s captures)?  
- The salient objects in the evaluation are relatively large. How would the method perform when the target class occupies a small region in the image? Would 224×224 resolution suffice for such cases?  
- In Table 3, are metrics provided for other methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., Feed [2]) for direct comparison?  

Minor language corrections:  
- Line 273: "More over" → "Moreover"  
- Line 151: "optimise" → "optimizes"  

Overall, the technical results are promising, but the speed claim and robustness to small objects warrant further clarification before acceptance.
