Summary
The paper proposes a neural-network-based approach to learn saliency masks, aiming to provide fast, explanation-like visualizations (processing multiple images per second). The idea of learning to explain a model is timely and valuable, and the paper contains several interesting ideas. However, key limitations in the evaluation and the dependence on architectural assumptions need to be addressed for a balanced contribution.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for learning saliency masks and an efficient implementation.
- Empirical results are generally sensible, and the weakly supervised localization results are interesting.
- The approach is positioned as model-agnostic and computationally practical.

Main Concerns
1) Evaluation via indirect proxies
- Weakly supervised localization (WSL) as a proxy for saliency quality is problematic. If contextual cues are crucial for classification, object localization may not correlate with actual saliency. Thus, WSL performance should not be over-interpreted as saliency quality.
- The proposed saliency metric introduces confounds due to its application procedure:
  - The method crops the estimated salient region and rescales it to the original image size while preserving the original aspect ratio. This can change the apparent aspect ratio of the content inside the crop, potentially affecting classification performance independently of saliency quality.
  - The metric implicitly favors smaller salient regions. Small crops are blown up substantially, changing the object’s scale in ways the classifier may not handle well. While modern CNNs are somewhat translation-invariant, scale invariance must be learned and is limited. As a result, part of the measured effect may reflect classifier sensitivity to scale/aspect changes rather than genuine saliency quality.

2) Dependence on classifier invariances and quality
- The validity of the proposed saliency metric depends heavily on the underlying classifier’s robustness to changes introduced by the cropping/rescaling step (scale, aspect ratio, distribution shift). This materially limits applicability:
  - It cannot reliably evaluate or explain models during training or models lacking sufficient scale invariance.
  - It may transfer poorly to other domains (e.g., spectrograms or non-natural images), where scale/aspect manipulations have different semantic implications.
  - The approach is not universally applicable to arbitrary black-box classifiers as implied by the title; it assumes particular invariances of the black-box model.

3) Architectural dependence of the masking network
- The paper does not sufficiently analyze how the masking model’s architecture affects learned masks and final performance. The authors’ response suggests a strong dependence on the masking network, raising the question of whether we are visualizing the saliency of the target model (e.g., the U-network) or the inductive biases of the masking network itself.
- It would strengthen the paper to report experiments with multiple masking architectures and capacities, analyze stability across them, and discuss how architectural choices influence masks and metrics.

Clarity/Reporting Issues
- Please clarify whether Table 1 reports results for all classes or only the predicted/correct class for each image.
- Specify exactly which LRP variant(s) and parameter settings were used (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta, and their values). This is essential for reproducibility and fair comparison.
- Discuss the limitations of using WSL as a saliency quality metric and the potential artifacts introduced by the cropping/rescaling pipeline. Consider alternative or complementary evaluations (e.g., deletion/insertion curves without geometric distortion, counterfactual occlusion with consistent scale/aspect, human studies where feasible, or diagnostic experiments isolating the effects of scale and aspect changes).

Post-rebuttal assessment
The rebuttal reinforced my concerns about dependence on classifier invariances and masking-network design. Given that the metric’s reliability hinges on the classifier’s scale robustness, the method’s generality is more limited than claimed:
- It is not broadly applicable to models lacking sufficient invariance or during training, where invariances are not yet established.
- Its portability to other data modalities is questionable.
- The “black-box” claim should be tempered to reflect these prerequisites.

Additionally, the results appear sensitive to the choice of masking network, which complicates interpretability: are we measuring the target model’s saliency or the masking network’s priors and failure modes?

Recommendation
Borderline. I am supportive of the overarching goal and find the approach promising and fast. However, the paper should be explicit about:
- The limitations of WSL and the proposed metric, including the role of scale/aspect artifacts.
- The dependence on classifier invariance and the resulting constraints on applicability.
- The sensitivity to masking-network architecture, with empirical analyses to disentangle its influence from the target model’s saliency.

If these issues are thoroughly discussed, the claims recalibrated, and additional diagnostics provided (even in the supplement), I would be inclined to support acceptance. Without these clarifications and analyses, the work risks overclaiming generality and misattributing improvements to saliency rather than to confounds introduced by the evaluation pipeline or architecture choices.
