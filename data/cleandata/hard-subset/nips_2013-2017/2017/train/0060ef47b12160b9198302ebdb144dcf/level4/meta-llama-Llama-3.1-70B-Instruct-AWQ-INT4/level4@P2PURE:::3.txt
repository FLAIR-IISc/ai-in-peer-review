This paper proposes an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The approach involves training a masking model that identifies the tightest rectangular crop containing the entire salient region of a specific class, as determined by a black box classifier such as AlexNet, GoogleNet, or ResNet. The model architecture relies on image feature maps, such as those generated by ResNet-50, across various scales. The final scale feature is passed through a feature filter for initial localization, while subsequent upsampling blocks refine the produced masks. Experimental results demonstrate that this method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques in the ImageNet localization task.

The paper appears to provide sufficient references and related works, although a thorough check has not been conducted. The technical correctness of the paper also seems to be sound, without a comprehensive verification. The authors present insightful discussions and intuitions on the design of their approach. The presentation of the paper is well-organized and clear.

Overall, this paper presents interesting technical results. However, some concerns remain regarding the real-time speed claim and its applicability to real-world images.

Comments:
- Does the reported processing time of 100 images per second include image resizing operations? If so, what is the running time for larger images, such as 640x480 images taken from an iPhone 6s?
- The salient objects in this paper are relatively large; how would the approach perform if the requested class object is small in the image? Would a 224x224 image be sufficient in such cases?
- In Table 3, are there corresponding metrics for other works listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2]?
