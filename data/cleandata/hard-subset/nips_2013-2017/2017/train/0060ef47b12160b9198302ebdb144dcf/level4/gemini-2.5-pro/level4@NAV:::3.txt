This paper introduces a high-throughput approach for generating image saliency masks, reportedly capable of processing one hundred 224x224 images per second on a conventional GPU. The method involves training a masking model to identify the tightest rectangular bounding box that encapsulates the entire salient region of a class specified by a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet). The model's architecture relies on multi-scale image feature maps, such as those produced by ResNet-50. An initial localization is performed on the final scale feature map using a feature filter, while subsequent upsampling blocks progressively refine the generated masks. The experimental evaluation shows that the proposed technique surpasses other weakly supervised methods in the ImageNet localization task.

The paper appears to be well-referenced and situated within the context of related work.
The technical contributions seem to be sound.
The authors provide substantial intuition and discussion regarding their design methodology.
The paper's presentation quality is high.

Overall, this work presents compelling technical results. However, I have some reservations concerning the real-time speed claim and its applicability to images captured in unconstrained, real-world scenarios.

**Comments for the Authors:**

*   The reported processing speed of 100 images per second is notable. Does this figure account for necessary preprocessing steps like image resizing? Additionally, could you provide performance benchmarks for larger, more common image resolutions, for instance, 640x480 images as captured by a device like an iPhone 6s?
*   The examples provided seem to focus on images where the salient objects are relatively large. How does the method's effectiveness change when the target class object is small, and is a 224x224 resolution sufficient to handle such cases?
*   For a more comprehensive comparison, would it be possible to report the metrics from Table 3 for the alternative methods listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2]?
