This paper proposes a novel image saliency mask generation approach, achieving a processing speed of 100 images per second on a standard GPU for 224x224 images. The authors train a masking model that identifies the tightest rectangular crop containing the entire salient region of a specific class, as determined by a black box classifier such as AlexNet, GoogleNet, or ResNet. Their model architecture relies on image feature maps, such as those generated by ResNet-50, across various scales. The final scale feature is passed through a feature filter for initial localisation, while subsequent upsampling blocks refine the produced masks. Experimental results demonstrate that their method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques in the ImageNet localisation task.

The paper appears to provide sufficient references and related works, although a thorough check has not been conducted. The technical correctness of the paper also seems satisfactory, without a comprehensive verification. The authors offer insightful discussions and intuitions on their approach design. The presentation is well-organised and clear.

Overall, this paper presents intriguing technical results, but I have some concerns regarding the real-time speed claim and its applicability to real-world images.

Comments:

* Does the reported processing time of 100 images per second include image resizing operations? If so, what is the processing time for larger images, such as 640x480 images from an iPhone 6s?
* Given that the salient objects in this paper are relatively large, how would the approach perform if the requested class object is small in the images? Would a 224x224 image be sufficient?
* In Table 3, are there corresponding metrics for other works listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2]?
