Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a trainable, class-conditional masking network that produces saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass, enabling real-time use. The core idea is to learn a mask that simultaneously preserves the classifier’s score when applied to the selected region (smallest sufficient region, SSR) and suppresses the score when the complementary region is kept (smallest destroying region, SDR), while discouraging adversarial artifacts via smoothness (TV) regularization and randomized evidence removal. The loss combines: (i) total variation for smoothness; (ii) an area penalty; (iii) a term encouraging high class probability on the preserved region; and (iv) a term encouraging low class probability when the salient region is removed.

Architecturally, the masking model is a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and a learned class embedding; a “feature filter” gates high-level feature maps by the selected class. The model is trained with the classifier fixed (“black box” in the sense of not updating it) and uses randomized alternative images (strong blur or random color + noise) when applying the mask to mitigate introduced evidence. The authors also introduce a saliency metric s(a, p) = log(max(a, 0.05)) − log(p) that proxies SSR: good methods achieve small area a while maintaining high class probability p on the cropped region.

On ImageNet, separate masking models are trained for AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 black boxes. The method outperforms other weakly supervised approaches on localization and approaches fully supervised performance. Qualitative maps are sharp and interpretable. A CIFAR-10 experiment (trained from scratch) suggests the approach is not tied to a pretrained encoder.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: a single-pass, model-specific masking network that learns to approximate iterative saliency optimization, with clear SSR/SDR motivation.
- Practical design to combat introduced evidence and artifacts (randomized alternative images, TV regularization, downsample/upsample strategy), with convincing qualitative results.
- New, simple saliency metric that aligns with SSR and is easy to compute; the method performs well under this metric.
- Strong empirical results in weakly supervised localization, close to fully supervised baselines in some settings.
- Clear exposition of training tricks (fake class sampling to force attention to the class selector), and sensible architecture choices.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “Black box” assumption: training requires backpropagating through the fixed classifier (i.e., a differentiable white-box to gradients). Please clarify that true score-only black-box access is not supported and discuss feasibility under gradient-free settings.
- Real-time claim: 100 224×224 images/s is promising, but it is unclear whether this includes pre/post-processing (resizing, mask upsampling, forming edited images) and the classifier’s forward pass. Throughput at higher resolutions (e.g., 640×480) and for batch size 1 would help substantiate “real-time” in practical deployments (e.g., video).
- Small-object sensitivity: Most qualitative examples show relatively large, centered objects. It is unclear how well the approach localizes small or off-center instances, or multiple instances of the same class. The coarse-to-fine U-Net should help, but evidence is limited.
- Generalization and training cost: The masking model is trained separately for each black-box classifier. It would be useful to assess cross-classifier transfer (train on ResNet-50, test on GoogLeNet) or the feasibility of a single masking model that conditions on features from arbitrary classifiers.
- Ablations: More analysis of the loss terms (weights λ1–λ4), the choice of the alternative image A, and mask resolution/upsampling would strengthen the causal link between design choices and performance. Likewise, a comparison to iterative optimization under the same metric would clarify the speed/quality trade-off.
- Saliency metric: The log formulation is reasonable but somewhat ad hoc (e.g., area threshold at 0.05). Sensitivity analysis and reporting confidence intervals would increase trust. Also, resizing crops without preserving aspect ratio may bias p for some classes; please discuss.
- Comparisons: It would be helpful to include the new metric for all methods in Table 2 (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi [2]) to align Tables 2 and 3 and strengthen claims about interpretability.

Quality
The method is technically sound and well motivated, with an appropriate architecture and training protocol. The empirical evaluation is strong on localization and the new metric; additional ablations and small-object analysis would improve completeness.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized. The introduction and related work are informative, the objective and architecture are clearly specified, and figures are helpful. Minor editorial issues listed below.

Originality
Learning a dedicated masking network for single-pass saliency, coupled with the SSR/SDR-inspired loss and randomized evidence removal, is a novel and useful combination relative to gradient backprop and iterative optimization methods (e.g., [1,2,16]).

Significance
The approach appears practically valuable: fast, visually sharp masks; improved weakly supervised localization; and a metric that better reflects SSR. If the speed and small-object performance are validated, this could be impactful for real-time and interactive applications.

Questions for the authors
- Does the reported 100 images/s include image resizing, mask upsampling, and forming the edited image, and does it include the classifier’s forward pass? What is the throughput for higher resolutions (e.g., 640×480 on an iPhone 6s–like input) and batch size 1?
- How does performance change for small objects (e.g., small birds, distant people)? Is 224×224 sufficient, or do you adapt the input scale? Please provide quantitative breakdowns by object size.
- Can you report the proposed saliency metric for all methods in Table 2 (including Fong & Vedaldi [2]) to make Table 3 directly comparable?
- How sensitive are results to λ1–λ4, the choice of A (blur vs random color+noise), and mask resolution? Please include ablations.
- Can a masking model trained on one classifier generalize to another, or can a single model handle multiple classifiers with a selector?

Minor comments and typos
- Ln 273: “More over” → “Moreover”
- Ln 151: “difference that [3] only optimise the mask” → “difference that [3] only optimises the mask”
- Please clarify “black box”: differentiable (gradient-access) black box vs score-only black box.
- Consider reporting CPU/GPU types used for throughput.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and practical approach to saliency with single-pass inference.
- Strong empirical results and clear qualitative improvements.
- Thoughtful objective that addresses SSR/SDR and artifact suppression.
- Useful new metric aligned with interpretability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Real-time claim needs clearer, resolution-dependent evidence.
- Limited analysis of small-object and multi-instance scenarios.
- Missing ablations and full metric comparisons to all baselines.
