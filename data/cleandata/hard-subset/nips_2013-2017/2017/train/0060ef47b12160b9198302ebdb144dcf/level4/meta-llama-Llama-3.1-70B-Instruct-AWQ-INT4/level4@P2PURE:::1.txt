The paper proposes a neural network-based approach to learning saliency masks, capable of processing multiple images per second. In my opinion, the paper is borderline, and I would not object to either rejection or acceptance. I believe in the concept of learning to explain a model, and the paper has some good ideas. However, there are no obvious mistakes, but clear limitations exist.

The saliency measurement is indirect, relying on either weakly supervised localization or the proposed saliency metric, both of which have limitations that should be discussed in the paper. Weakly supervised localization is not a perfect measure, as object localization may not correlate with saliency quality if the context is essential for determining the object's class. Although the results on weakly supervised localization are interesting, using them as a quality metric for saliency has significant caveats.

The saliency metric is also imperfect due to its application. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size, maintaining the original aspect ratio, which could introduce two artifacts. First, the aspect ratio change may impact the classification performance. Second, the proposed metric prefers smaller salient regions, which, when heavily rescaled for re-classification, may not be presented to the classifier at an ideal scale. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are generally translation-invariant, but scaling invariance must be learned, and there are likely limits to this.

It is unclear how much the masking model depends on the architecture for learning the masks. Did the authors experiment with different architectures and how did this influence the results?

Post-rebuttal edit:
The fact that the saliency metric's performance depends on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier strongly limits the applicability of the proposed method. It can only be applied to networks with this invariance, which has important consequences:
- The method cannot be used for models during the training phase or for models lacking this invariance.
- Its applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs) is limited.
- The method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers, as claimed in the title.

Furthermore, the response suggests a strong dependence on the masking network. As a result, it is unclear whether we are visualizing the saliency of the U-network or the masking network. If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, I believe it is balanced enough for publication. If not, it should not be published.
