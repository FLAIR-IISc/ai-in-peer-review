

This paper presents an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing 100 images of size 224×224 per second on a standard GPU. The method trains a masking model that identifies the tightest rectangular crop encompassing the entire salient region for a specified class, as defined by a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet). The model architecture leverages multi-scale feature maps (e.g., from ResNet-50), where the final-scale features pass through a feature filter for initial localization, followed by upsampling blocks for mask refinement. Experiments demonstrate that this technique surpasses other weakly supervised methods on the ImageNet localization task.  
The paper includes sufficient references and related work. Technical correctness is not a major concern. The authors provide meaningful intuition and discussion regarding their approach design. The presentation is clear and well-organized.  
Overall, the paper offers compelling technical results, though I have some concerns regarding the real-time speed claim and applicability to real-world imagery.  
Comments:  
- Does the 100 images/sec processing time include image resizing? If so, what is the runtime for larger images (e.g., 640×480 from an iPhone 6s)?  
- Given the focus on large salient objects, how does the method handle cases where the requested class object is small? Is 224×224 resolution sufficient?  
- In Table 3, are metrics provided for other works (e.g., Feed [2]) comparable to those in Table 2?
