### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel method for generating saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier. The core idea is to train a separate, dedicated neural network—a "masking model" with a U-Net architecture—to produce a saliency mask for a given image and class in a single forward pass. This approach stands in contrast to existing methods that are either slow and iterative (e.g., optimization-based perturbation) or fast but produce lower-quality, noisy maps (e.g., gradient-based methods). The authors propose a composite objective function for training the masking model, designed to find masks that are smooth, small, and identify regions that are both sufficient for classification when isolated (Smallest Sufficient Region) and critical for classification when removed (Smallest Destroying Region). The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, demonstrating state-of-the-art results on the weakly-supervised object localization task. The authors also propose a new metric for evaluating saliency based on the classifier's performance on a cropped salient region.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central concept of training a dedicated network to generate explanations for another model is a significant and novel contribution. This "amortized" approach, which learns to predict the outcome of a complex optimization process, circumvents the need for slow, per-instance iterative optimization. The ability to generate saliency maps in real-time opens up new applications, such as video saliency, which are infeasible with previous high-quality methods.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method achieves impressive results on the weakly-supervised object localization task on ImageNet, outperforming prior weakly-supervised and saliency-based methods. This provides strong evidence that the generated masks effectively capture object-relevant regions.

3.  **Well-Designed Objective Function:** The proposed loss function (Equation 4) is thoughtfully designed, incorporating terms that encourage desirable properties in a saliency map: smoothness (TV penalty), conciseness (area minimization), and effectiveness under both preservation (SSR) and removal (SDR) conditions.

4.  **Introduction of a New Metric:** While the proposed saliency metric has limitations (discussed below), the effort to create a new, interpretable evaluation standard beyond localization accuracy is commendable and contributes to the broader discussion on how to evaluate model explanations.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection):**

1.  **Validity of the Evaluation Metrics and Overstated Generality:** The paper's claims of quality and generality are heavily reliant on two evaluation methods, both of which have significant, undiscussed limitations.
    *   **Weakly-Supervised Localization:** Equating saliency with object localization is a strong assumption. A classifier might rely on contextual cues outside an object's bounding box (e.g., snow for a polar bear), in which case a "correct" saliency map would not align perfectly with the localization ground truth. This caveat should be explicitly discussed.
    *   **Proposed Saliency Metric:** The new metric involves cropping the salient region and resizing it to the classifier's input dimensions, potentially disregarding the original aspect ratio. This process implicitly assumes that the black-box classifier is highly invariant to both scale and aspect-ratio distortions. This is a strong limitation that undermines the claim that the method is applicable to *any* differentiable classifier. The method's evaluation is therefore biased towards classifiers that happen to possess this specific invariance. This has two major consequences:
        a. The method cannot be reliably used to explain models that lack this invariance or are still in training.
        b. Its applicability to other domains where such invariances are not present (e.g., CNNs for spectrogram analysis) is questionable.
    The title's claim of a method for "Black Box Classifiers" is therefore too strong, as its success is tied to specific, unverified properties of the classifier.

2.  **Potential for Confounding Explanations:** The masking model itself is a powerful deep network (a U-Net with a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder). It is unclear whether the generated saliency maps are a pure reflection of the target classifier's internal logic or if they are heavily influenced by the biases and priors learned by the masking network itself. In essence, we might be visualizing the properties of the U-Net as much as we are visualizing the properties of the black-box model. The paper lacks a discussion or analysis of this potential confounder.

3.  **Lack of Architectural Ablation:** The paper does not explore how the choice of the masking model's architecture influences the resulting saliency maps. Would a simpler or different architecture produce significantly different explanations? Understanding this sensitivity is crucial for assessing the robustness and reliability of the generated explanations.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Table 1:** Are the localization error rates in Table 1 calculated by providing the model with the ground-truth class, or are they averaged over all possible classes? Please clarify this in the paper.
*   **LRP Comparison:** The paper compares against "LRP" but does not specify which variant (e.g., epsilon-LRP, alpha-beta-LRP) or what parameter settings were used. Given the sensitivity of LRP to these choices, this information is necessary for a fair comparison.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a dedicated section discussing the limitations outlined above. Specifically, acknowledging that the proposed saliency metric relies on the scale/aspect-ratio invariance of the target classifier is critical for accurately positioning this work. The paper should also address the potential for the masking network to introduce its own biases into the explanation.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a creative and promising direction for generating real-time saliency maps by training a dedicated explanation model. The approach is original, and the empirical results on object localization are strong. However, the work in its current form overstates its generality and fails to address critical limitations regarding its evaluation methodology and the potential for confounding factors from the masking network itself.

I am currently borderline on this paper. The core idea is valuable, but the lack of discussion around the method's core assumptions and limitations is a significant weakness. If the authors were to thoroughly revise the manuscript to address these points—particularly by tempering the "black box" claims and explicitly discussing the dependence on classifier invariance and the potential biases of the masking model—I would be inclined to recommend acceptance. Without these revisions, the paper's claims are not sufficiently supported.
