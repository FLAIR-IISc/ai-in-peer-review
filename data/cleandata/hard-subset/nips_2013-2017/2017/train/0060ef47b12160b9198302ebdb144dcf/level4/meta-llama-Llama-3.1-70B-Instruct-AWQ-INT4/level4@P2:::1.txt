The paper proposes a novel approach to learn saliency masks using a neural network, achieving fast processing speeds of multiple images per second. The concept of learning to explain a model is compelling, and the paper presents some promising ideas.

The main contribution of the paper is a trainable masking model that can produce high-quality saliency masks in a single forward pass, without direct access to the image classifier after training. The model is trained to minimize an objective function that encourages smoothness, small region size, and accurate classification. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric that evaluates the quality of the produced masks.

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach on the ImageNet dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization. The authors also show that their method outperforms other saliency detection techniques and is on par with ground truth bounding boxes in terms of interpretability.

However, there are some limitations and potential issues that need to be addressed. Firstly, the saliency metric is not perfect and may introduce artifacts due to the cropping and resizing of the estimated salient region. This could impact the classification accuracy and may not be ideal for models that are not scale-invariant.

Another concern is the dependence of the masking model on the architecture. The authors do not discuss how the choice of architecture affects the results, and it is unclear whether the proposed approach is generally applicable to black-box classifiers.

The paper also has some minor issues, such as not specifying which LRP variant and parameter setting were used for comparison. Additionally, it is unclear whether the results in Table 1 are obtained for all classes or only for the correct class.

In the rebuttal, the authors acknowledge the limitations of the saliency metric and the dependence on the masking network. However, these limitations have important consequences, such as limiting the applicability of the proposed method to networks with scale invariance and making it not generally applicable to black-box classifiers.

To address these concerns, the authors should discuss the limitations of the saliency metric and the dependence on the masking network in the paper. They should also provide more information on the choice of architecture and its impact on the results. If these issues are properly addressed, the paper is balanced enough for publication.
