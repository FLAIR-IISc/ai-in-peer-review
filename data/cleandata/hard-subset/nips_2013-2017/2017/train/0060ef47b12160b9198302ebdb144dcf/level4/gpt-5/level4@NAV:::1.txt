Review- The paper introduces a method for learning saliency masks. The approach is neural-network–based and processes several images per second (i.e., it is fast).
I view the paper as borderline; I would not oppose either rejection or acceptance. I am supportive of the broader idea of learning to explain models, and this work contains some promising ideas.
There are no obvious errors, but there are definite limitations.
- Saliency is evaluated only indirectly—either via weakly supervised localization or through the proposed saliency metric. Both have clear drawbacks that, in my opinion, should be discussed in the paper.
Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy. When contextual cues are crucial for determining the class, object localization may not align with saliency quality. The weakly supervised localization results are interesting, but using them as a saliency-quality metric comes with a significant caveat.
The saliency metric also has issues stemming from how it is applied. The predicted salient region is cropped and then resized back to the original image dimensions while preserving the original aspect ratio. This may introduce two artifacts. First, the aspect-ratio change can affect how well the image is classified. Second, the metric inherently favors small salient regions; because small regions are upscaled aggressively for re-classification, the resulting scale presented to the classifier may be suboptimal. (Convnets are typically fairly translation-invariant, but scale invariance must be learned and is limited.)
What is not addressed is how strongly the mask-learning procedure depends on the architecture. Did the authors experiment with alternative architectures, and how did that affect the results?
Minor comments
Are the results in Table 1 computed over all classes or only the correct class?
- Please specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparison. LRP includes epsilon, alpha–beta, and other variants with tunable parameters.
Post rebuttal edit
The effectiveness of the saliency metric depends on the classifier’s quality and scale invariance, which severely constrains the method’s applicability. It can only be used with networks that possess such invariance.
This leads to important consequences:
- The method cannot be applied to models during training, nor to models lacking this invariance.
- It limits transfer to other domains (e.g., CNN-based spectrogram analysis).
- The method is not broadly applicable to arbitrary black-box classifiers as suggested by the title.
Furthermore, the response indicates a strong dependence on the masking network.
- Consequently, it is unclear whether the visualizations reflect the saliency of the U-network or of the masking network.
If these issues are properly discussed in the paper, I think it would be balanced enough for publication. If not, it should not be published.
