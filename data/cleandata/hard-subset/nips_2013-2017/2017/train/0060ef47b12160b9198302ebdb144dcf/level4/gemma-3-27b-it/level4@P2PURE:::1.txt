This paper introduces a neural network-based approach for learning saliency masks, demonstrating promising speed—processing multiple images per second. 

Overall, I find this paper to be borderline; I would not strongly oppose either acceptance or rejection. I am supportive of the core concept of learning explanations for model behavior, and the paper presents some worthwhile ideas. While I found no obvious errors, the work does have clear limitations that should be addressed.

A primary concern is the indirect measurement of saliency. Both the weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric have inherent limitations which require further discussion. The use of weakly supervised localization as a proxy for saliency quality is imperfect. Object localization may not correlate with true saliency if contextual information is crucial for classification. While the results utilizing this approach are interesting, interpreting them as a definitive measure of saliency quality requires significant caution.

The proposed saliency metric also has potential flaws in its application. The cropping and subsequent rescaling of estimated salient regions to the original image size, while maintaining the original aspect ratio, could introduce artifacts. Specifically, altering the aspect ratio may negatively impact classification performance. Furthermore, the metric appears to favor smaller salient regions; the substantial upscaling applied during re-classification may present the classifier with an object at a non-ideal scale (while convolutional networks exhibit translation invariance, scaling invariance is learned and has inherent limits).

The paper does not explore the influence of the underlying network architecture on the learned masks. Investigating different architectures and their impact on results would strengthen the work. 

Minor comments: 
*   Please clarify whether the results in Table 1 are reported for all classes or solely for the correctly classified class.
*   Please specify the exact variant and parameter settings used for the LRP comparison (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta, and other relevant parameters).

(Post-rebuttal edit)

The strong dependence of the saliency metric’s effectiveness on the classifier’s quality and scale invariance significantly limits the method’s applicability. It can only be reliably applied to networks possessing these properties. This has several important consequences: 
*   The method is unsuitable for evaluating models during the training phase or those lacking scale invariance.
*   This restricts its applicability to other domains (e.g., CNN-based spectrogram analysis).
*   The claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers, as stated in the title, is unsupported.

Moreover, the response suggests a notable dependence on the masking network itself. Consequently, it is unclear whether the visualizations represent the saliency of the underlying U-network or the masking network. 

If these limitations and effects are thoroughly discussed in the revised paper, I believe it would be balanced enough for publication. Otherwise, I recommend rejection.
