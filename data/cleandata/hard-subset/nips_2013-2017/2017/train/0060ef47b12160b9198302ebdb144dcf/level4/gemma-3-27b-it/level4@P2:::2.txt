This paper proposes a novel neural network-based approach for rapidly detecting image saliency – identifying the regions most important for a given classifier’s prediction. The core idea is to train a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, offering significant speed advantages over iterative methods. The paper is well-written and presents compelling results, demonstrating performance competitive with, and often exceeding, existing weakly supervised saliency detection techniques on challenging datasets like CIFAR-10 and ImageNet. The experimental evaluation utilizes state-of-the-art classifiers, lending further weight to the findings.

The authors effectively position their work within the context of recent advances in saliency detection, referencing gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative patch removal techniques [17], and optimization-based approaches [1, 2]. They clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods – particularly their computational cost – and highlight how their proposed approach addresses these limitations. The introduction motivates the need for saliency detection by illustrating the often-counterintuitive behavior of deep neural networks [6, 13]. 

**Strengths:**

* **Speed and Efficiency:** The primary strength of this work is its speed. Achieving real-time saliency detection opens up possibilities for applications where immediate feedback is crucial, such as video analysis and interactive visual debugging. While the paper briefly mentions video-saliency, expanding on potential real-time applications would strengthen the motivation.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates competitive performance on standard benchmarks, achieving superior object localization accuracy compared to other weakly supervised techniques (Table 1 & 2).
* **Novel Saliency Metric:** The introduction of a new saliency metric, based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept [2], provides a more nuanced evaluation of saliency maps than traditional localization accuracy alone (Section 3.2 & Table 3).
* **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and experimental setup.
* **Thorough Ablation & Design Choices:** The paper provides good justification for the design choices (e.g., evidence removal, smoothness regularization) and their impact on performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Analysis of Learned Saliency:** While the results demonstrate *that* the method works, there's limited investigation into *why* it works. A deeper analysis of the learned saliency regions – specifically, whether the model identifies spurious correlations or genuinely object-relevant features – would be valuable. For example, exploring if the model exploits background biases (as highlighted in the introduction regarding “polar bear” vs. “snow” [6]) could offer significant insight.
* **Dependence on Black-Box Classifier:** The performance is inherently tied to the specific black-box classifier used during training. The experiments demonstrate generalization across different architectures (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50), but further investigation into the robustness of the masking model to variations in classifier architecture and training data would be beneficial.
* **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Equation 4) contains multiple regularizers with empirically chosen weights. A more systematic exploration of the impact of these weights on the quality of the generated masks would strengthen the analysis.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem – explaining the decisions of black-box classifiers – with a novel and effective approach.
* The method achieves state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization and introduces a new, insightful metric for evaluating saliency maps.
* The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and includes a thorough experimental evaluation.
* The potential for real-time applications makes this work particularly impactful.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of in-depth analysis of the learned saliency regions limits our understanding of *why* the method performs well. 
* The dependence on a pre-trained black-box classifier introduces a potential limitation regarding generalization.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of saliency detection. The proposed method is novel, efficient, and achieves compelling results. While a more detailed analysis of the learned saliency regions would be beneficial, the current work is of high quality and warrants acceptance. I recommend **accepting** this paper to NIPS 2017.
