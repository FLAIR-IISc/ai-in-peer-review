The paper presents a learned approach to generate saliency masks: a neural network trained to alter a differentiable black-box classifier’s scores by masking salient image regions. The method is model-agnostic and operates at real-time speeds.

Overall, I view the paper as borderline; I would not object to either acceptance or rejection. I am supportive of the broader idea of learning to explain models, and this work includes several promising elements. I do not see obvious technical errors, but there are clear limitations.

- Saliency is assessed only indirectly. Both the weakly supervised localization results and the proposed saliency metric have notable shortcomings, which should be discussed more explicitly.

Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy for saliency quality. When contextual cues are integral to class prediction, localization accuracy need not correlate with the quality of a saliency explanation. The localization results are interesting, but using them as a primary saliency metric comes with important caveats.

The proposed saliency metric is also problematic in how it is applied. The estimated salient region is cropped and then resized to the classifier’s input size, potentially altering the aspect ratio. This introduces two concerns: (1) aspect ratio changes can affect classification confidence; and (2) the metric favors small regions, yet upscaling a tiny crop for re-classification may present the object at an unnatural scale for the network. While CNNs tend to be relatively translation-invariant, scale invariance must be learned and can be limited.

What is not discussed is the dependence of the approach on the masking model’s architecture. Did the authors explore alternative architectures, and how did such choices influence the results?

Minor comments
- Are the results in Table 1 computed for the ground-truth class only, or across all classes?
- Please specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparison (e.g., epsilon, alpha–beta, etc.).

Post rebuttal edit
The effectiveness of the saliency metric appears to depend heavily on the classifier’s robustness to scale and aspect-ratio variations, which substantially limits applicability. In practice, this restricts use to networks that have learned sufficient invariance.

This has several implications:
- The method is not suitable for models during training or for models lacking such invariance.
- Applicability to other domains (e.g., CNNs on spectrograms) is constrained.
- The claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers (as stated in the title) is overstated.

Moreover, the response indicates a strong dependence on the masking network. As a result, it is unclear to what extent the visualizations reflect the black-box classifier’s saliency versus biases of the U-Net masking model itself.

If these issues and dependencies are thoroughly acknowledged and discussed in the paper, I would consider the submission sufficiently balanced for publication. Otherwise, I would not recommend acceptance.
