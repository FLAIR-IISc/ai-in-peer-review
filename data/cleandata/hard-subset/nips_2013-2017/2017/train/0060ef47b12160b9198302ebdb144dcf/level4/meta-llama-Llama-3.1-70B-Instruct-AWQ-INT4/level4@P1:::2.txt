This paper introduces a neural network (NN) to predict the regions of an image that are important for another NN for object categorization. The paper is well-written and presents interesting results, demonstrating that the proposed approach works well compared to previous works on challenging datasets with state-of-the-art NNs.

The main idea of the paper is to develop a fast saliency detection method that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The authors propose a trainable masking model that can produce saliency maps in a single forward pass, making it suitable for real-time applications. The model is trained to minimize an objective function that encourages the mask to be smooth, small, and informative for the classifier.

The paper presents an extensive evaluation of the proposed approach on the CIFAR-10 and ImageNet datasets, using three different black-box classifiers: AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50. The results show that the proposed method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques at the ImageNet localisation task and achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CIFAR-10 dataset.

One of the strengths of the paper is the introduction of a new saliency metric that can be used to assess the quality of explanations produced by saliency detectors. The metric is based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR) concepts, which are well-motivated and easy to understand.

However, there are a few areas that could be improved. Firstly, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the learnt salient regions. For example, the authors could provide insights into which object categories exploit biases in the background to recognize the object. This would help to better understand the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.

Secondly, the paper could provide more motivation for the need for real-time efficiency in saliency detection. While the authors mention that the proposed approach is suitable for real-time applications, they do not provide specific examples or use cases that would benefit from this capability.

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated and well-executed approach to saliency detection, with promising results on challenging datasets. With some additional analysis and motivation, the paper could be even stronger.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to saliency detection.
* The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on challenging datasets.
* The introduction of a new saliency metric is a valuable contribution to the field.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the learnt salient regions.
* The need for real-time efficiency in saliency detection could be better motivated.
* Some additional experiments or comparisons with other methods could strengthen the paper.
