Summary
The paper proposes a neural-network-based approach to learn saliency masks, capable of processing multiple images per second. I find the overall idea of learning to explain models compelling, and the paper contains several good ideas. I view the paper as borderline: I would not object to either acceptance or rejection.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for learning saliency masks and a fast implementation.
- No obvious methodological errors.
- Interesting results on weakly supervised localization.

Main concerns and limitations
- Saliency is evaluated only indirectly, either via weakly supervised localization or via the proposed saliency metric. Both proxies have notable limitations that should be discussed more explicitly.
  - Weakly supervised localization as a proxy for saliency quality can be misleading. When contextual cues are essential for classification, object localization need not correlate with true saliency. While the localization results are interesting on their own, using them as a quality metric for saliency carries a significant caveat.
  - The proposed saliency metric, as applied, may introduce artifacts. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size with the original aspect ratio. This creates two issues:
    1) Aspect ratio changes can affect how well a region is classified.
    2) The metric implicitly prefers small salient regions; when small regions are heavily upscaled for reclassification, the resulting scale may be suboptimal for the classifier. Convnets are typically fairly translation-invariant, but scale invariance must be learned and is limited in practice.

- Architectural dependence is not addressed. It is unclear how sensitive the masking model is to architectural choices. Did the authors experiment with different architectures, and if so, how did these choices influence the learned masks and downstream performance?

Minor comments and questions
- For Table 1, are the reported results computed over all classes or only for the ground-truth class?
- Please specify precisely which LRP variant and parameter settings were used (e.g., epsilon, alpha–beta, etc.).

Post-rebuttal update
The effectiveness of the proposed saliency metric appears to depend strongly on the classifier’s quality and, in particular, on its scale invariance. This substantially limits applicability:
- The method cannot be reliably used for models during training, nor for models that lack sufficient scale invariance.
- This restricts applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs).
- The method is therefore not generally applicable to black-box classifiers as claimed in the title.

Moreover, the response suggests a strong dependence on the masking network itself. As a result, it is unclear whether the explanations reflect the saliency of the underlying U-network or of the masking network.

Recommendation
Borderline. If the paper explicitly and thoroughly discusses:
- the limitations of the indirect saliency evaluations (weakly supervised localization and the proposed metric),
- the dependence on classifier scale invariance and the resulting constraints on applicability,
- the architectural sensitivity of the masking network (including evidence from ablations),
then I believe the paper would be sufficiently balanced for publication. If these issues remain insufficiently addressed, I do not recommend publication.
