This paper proposes an image saliency mask generation method that can process about 100 224×224 images per second on a standard GPU. The approach trains a masking model to find the tightest rectangular crop that contains the entire salient region of a requested class, as determined by a black-box classifier such as AlexNet, GoogLeNet, or ResNet. The architecture consumes multi-scale image feature maps (e.g., from ResNet-50). The final-scale features are passed through a feature filter for initial localization, and subsequent upsampling blocks refine the produced masks. Experiments show that the method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task.

The paper appears to include sufficient references and related work, although I did not perform a thorough check. The paper also appears technically correct, though I did not verify all details. It provides several intuitions and discussions motivating the design choices. The presentation is good.

Overall, the paper presents interesting technical results, though I have some concerns about the real-time speed claim and applicability to real-world images.

Comments:
- Does the reported throughput of 100 images per second include the image resizing operation? If so, what is the runtime for larger images, such as 640×480 images from an iPhone 6s?
- The salient objects in the paper appear quite large. What happens if the requested-class object is small in the image? Is a 224×224 input sufficient in that case?
- In Table 3, is there a corresponding metric for other works listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2]?
