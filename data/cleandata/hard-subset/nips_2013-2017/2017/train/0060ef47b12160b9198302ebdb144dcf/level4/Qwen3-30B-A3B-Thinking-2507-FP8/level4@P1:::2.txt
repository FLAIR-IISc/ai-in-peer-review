

This paper introduces a novel masking model that predicts saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass, enabling real-time saliency detection. The authors address a critical gap in existing methods, which typically require iterative optimization (e.g., [1, 17]) and are prohibitively slow for real-time applications like video analysis. Their key contributions include a model-agnostic approach, a new saliency metric based on the Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) concept, and rigorous evaluation on ImageNet and CIFAR-10. The method trains a U-Net-based masking network to produce sharp, artifact-free saliency maps by optimizing a composite loss that enforces mask smoothness (via TV penalty), minimal preserved region size, and high classification confidence on the masked region. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that their model achieves superior localisation accuracy (36.7% error on ImageNet, outperforming weakly supervised baselines) and produces saliency maps with interpretability comparable to ground-truth bounding boxes—without using any localisation data during training.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Quality & Technical Soundness:** The paper provides thorough experimental validation across multiple classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10). The new saliency metric (Section 3.2) effectively captures interpretability by measuring the minimal crop size retaining classification confidence, addressing a key limitation of standard localisation-based metrics. Results are well-supported: their masking model achieves 100+ saliency maps per second (enabling real-time use), outperforms iterative methods (e.g., [2]) in both speed and quality, and generalizes to unseen images. The ablation studies (e.g., mask smoothness via TV penalty, random evidence removal) convincingly justify design choices.  
2. **Originality:** While prior work explores iterative saliency (e.g., [1, 17]), this is the first to train a single model for *fast, one-pass* saliency prediction applicable to *any* black-box classifier. The masking model’s architecture (U-Net with ResNet backbone) and the novel metric are distinct contributions, clearly differentiated from concurrent work [2] (which optimizes iteratively).  
3. **Significance:** The real-time capability unlocks new applications (e.g., video saliency for autonomous vehicles, as noted in the Future Work section), which iterative methods cannot support. The weakly supervised localisation results (36.7% error vs. 34.3% for fully supervised VGG [10]) demonstrate tangible progress for practical computer vision systems. The method’s compatibility with diverse classifiers also broadens its applicability.  
4. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized. Key concepts (SSR/SDR, evidence removal, metric definition) are introduced progressively with clear figures (Figs. 1–3). The architecture (Fig. 4) and training details (Section 4.1) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The writing remains precise despite technical depth.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Real-Time Application Motivation:** While the paper mentions video saliency in Future Work, it lacks concrete examples of *why* real-time efficiency matters in practice. For instance, autonomous vehicles require sub-100ms inference per frame to avoid collisions—this context would strengthen the significance argument.  
2. **Bias Analysis:** The authors note that classifiers may exploit background biases (e.g., "snow for polar bears" in Section 1), but they do not analyze whether their saliency maps reveal such biases. A brief case study (e.g., showing saliency maps for "polar bear" images where snow dominates) would validate their method’s ability to uncover unintended classifier reliance.  
3. **Metric Limitations:** The saliency metric assumes classifiers are scale-invariant (Section 3.2), but this may not hold for all architectures. The authors could acknowledge this constraint and discuss its impact on results.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pros:*  
- First model-agnostic, real-time saliency method with strong empirical results.  
- New metric advances the evaluation of interpretability beyond localisation accuracy.  
- High-quality experiments on ImageNet/CIFAR-10; clear ablation of design choices.  
- Real-world applicability (e.g., video, autonomous systems) is compelling.  

*Cons:*  
- Limited discussion of real-time use cases (beyond "video" in Future Work).  
- No analysis of whether saliency maps expose classifier biases (e.g., background reliance).  
- Metric’s scale-invariance assumption not fully validated.  

**Recommendation:**  
This paper makes a strong contribution to model interpretability with clear technical innovation and robust evaluation. The real-time capability, superior results on ImageNet localisation, and novel metric address critical gaps in the literature. While the application motivation could be strengthened and bias analysis added, these are minor enhancements to an otherwise outstanding work. The paper aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on high-impact, scientifically rigorous contributions. **I recommend acceptance.**
