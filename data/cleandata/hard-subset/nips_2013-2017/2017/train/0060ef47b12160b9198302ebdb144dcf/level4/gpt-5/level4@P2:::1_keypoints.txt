- Summary: Proposes a trainable masking network that predicts class-conditional saliency maps in one forward pass, trained to preserve (SSR) or remove (SDR) class evidence and evaluated with a new crop-reclassification metric on ImageNet/CIFAR-10.

- Strength: Conceptual — amortizes per-image optimization by learning a feed-forward masker, enabling real-time explanations.

- Strength: Practical — single-pass method is fast (>100 fps) and yields visually sharp, interpretable masks compared to iterative methods.

- Strength: Empirical — competitive weakly supervised localization on ImageNet and reasonable CIFAR-10 results without pretraining, with useful qualitative comparisons.

- Strength: Engineering — clearly identifies and addresses "introduced evidence" artifacts (alternative image A, TV regularization, low-res masks).

- Weakness: Localization vs saliency — localization accuracy often measures discriminative parts, not true saliency, weakening interpretation of localization results.

- Weakness: Crop-based metric confounds — cropping/resizing salient regions introduces scale/aspect distortions that conflate mask quality with classifier robustness.

- Weakness: Architectural sensitivity — unclear how performance depends on U-Net/ResNet-50 encoder, feature-filter design, pretraining, and loss-term weights; ablations needed.

- Weakness: Alternative image and regularization — unclear dependence on choice of alternative image A, TV strength, mask resolution, and upsampling; ablations required.

- Weakness: Black-box claim unclear — training appears to require classifier gradients; clarify whether classifier gradients are used and thus whether it’s truly black-box.

- Weakness: Limited generality — reliance on classifier scale/aspect invariance limits applicability to models or domains that lack these invariances.

- Weakness: Masker bias risk — possible that masks reflect masking-network priors rather than classifier saliency; test cross-class/model transfer and transfer experiments.

- Weakness: Missing experimental protocol details — unclear whether localization uses ground-truth class or top-1, and LRP baseline variant/parameters are unspecified.

- Weakness: Missing hyperparameter/reporting details — need thresholds, crop area floor, class selector sampling, random seeds, and λ weights.

- Quality: Method is plausible and engineered to reduce artifacts but requires more rigorous ablations and clearer metric discussion to substantiate claims.

- Clarity: Paper is well written but needs precise definition of "black box," training-time gradient usage, and a detailed evaluation protocol.

- Originality: Learning a feed-forward saliency predictor optimized for SSR/SDR meaningfully extends iterative perturbation and gradient-based methods.

- Significance: Fast, sharp maps are promising for real-time use, but metric limitations and missing ablations weaken empirical claims.

- Pros for acceptance: practical and fast approach, strong qualitative results, mitigation of adversarial-artifact issues, and a new compactness-preserving metric.

- Cons for acceptance: indirect saliency evaluation, metric confounds from scale/aspect distortions, unclear black-box status, missing architecture/loss ablations, and potential masker-induced biases.

- Post-rebuttal concern: Reliance on classifier scale/aspect invariance and masker dependence further restricts applicability and raises concern that visualizations reflect masker biases.

- Minor comments: Clarify Table 1 label protocol, specify LRP variant/parameters, provide ablations on A/TV/mask resolution/λ weights, and state whether classifier gradients are used during training.

- Overall: Borderline — likely acceptable with clearer scoping, thorough ablations, and expanded discussion of metric and architectural limitations; otherwise lean toward rejection.
