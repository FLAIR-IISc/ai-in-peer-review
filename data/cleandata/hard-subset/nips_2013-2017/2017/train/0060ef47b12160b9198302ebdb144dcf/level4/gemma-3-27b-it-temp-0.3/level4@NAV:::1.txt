This paper introduces a neural network-based approach for learning saliency masks, demonstrating fast processing speeds – on the order of multiple images per second. Overall, I find this work to be on the borderline of acceptance; I wouldn’t strongly oppose either a rejection or an acceptance decision. The core concept of learning to explain model behavior is valuable, and the paper presents several interesting ideas. While I found no significant errors, the work does have clear limitations that need to be addressed.

A primary concern lies in the indirect measurement of saliency. Both the weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric have inherent limitations that should be thoroughly discussed. The use of weakly supervised localization as a ground truth for saliency is problematic. Object localization doesn’t necessarily reflect saliency if contextual information is crucial for classification. While the results using this approach are interesting, interpreting them as a direct measure of saliency quality is questionable. 

Similarly, the proposed saliency metric introduces potential artifacts due to its implementation. Cropping the estimated salient region and then rescaling it to the original image size, while maintaining aspect ratio, could negatively impact classification performance. Specifically, altering the aspect ratio and the resulting scale change during rescaling may not be optimal for the classifier, even considering the translation invariance of convolutional networks – scaling invariance is learned and likely has limits.

The paper also lacks discussion regarding the influence of the masking model’s architecture on the learned masks. Exploring different architectures and their impact on the results would strengthen the work. A minor point: it’s unclear whether the results in Table 1 are reported for all classes or only the correct class. Additionally, please specify the exact LRP variant and parameter settings used for comparison, as numerous options exist with varying parameters (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta).

**Post-Rebuttal:** The strong dependence of the saliency metric’s effectiveness on the classifier’s quality and scale invariance significantly limits the method’s applicability. It can only be reliably applied to networks possessing these properties. This has several important consequences: the method is unsuitable for evaluating models during training or those lacking this invariance, restricts its application to domains like spectrogram analysis with CNNs, and, critically, contradicts the claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers made in the title. Furthermore, the authors’ response suggests a substantial dependence on the masking network itself, raising the question of whether the visualizations represent the saliency of the underlying network or the masking network. 

If these limitations and potential confounding factors are adequately discussed in the revised manuscript, the paper would be balanced enough for publication. However, without such a discussion, I believe publication is not warranted.
