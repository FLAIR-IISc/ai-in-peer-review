This paper presents a neural network that predicts, in a single forward pass, the image regions most important to a separate, black-box classifier for object recognition. The method is designed for real-time saliency detection and is evaluated on challenging datasets using state-of-the-art architectures. The experiments indicate that it performs strongly compared to prior work.

Strengths:
- The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.
- The idea of training a separate masking network to produce saliency maps for an arbitrary differentiable classifier is appealing and practically useful.
- The empirical results are compelling: performance is strong on challenging datasets with modern models, and the approach compares favorably to previous methods.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Please better motivate scenarios where real-time saliency is essential. Concrete applications (e.g., interactive systems, streaming or video settings, on-device explanations, or safety-critical uses) would clarify why the real-time constraint matters and how your approach enables them.
- I would have liked to see a deeper analysis of the learned salient regions. For example, which object categories rely on background cues (e.g., “snow” for “polar bear”)? Do the masks reveal dataset or model biases, and how consistent are these effects across classes and architectures? Such insights would strengthen the interpretability claims and help practitioners understand when and why the method succeeds.
