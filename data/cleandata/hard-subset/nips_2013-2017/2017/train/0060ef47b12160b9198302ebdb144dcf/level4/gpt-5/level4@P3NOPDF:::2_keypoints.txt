- Summary: Proposes a neural model that predicts image regions most relevant to a separate classifier, showing clear advantages over prior methods on challenging datasets and architectures.

- Strength: Clear problem formulation—predicting classifier-relevant regions is well defined and practically meaningful for interpretability and efficiency.

- Strength: Strong empirical results—the method consistently outperforms prior approaches across competitive benchmarks and modern networks.

- Strength: Sound experimental setup—comparisons appear fair and evaluations use realistic, challenging datasets.

- Improvement: Better motivate real-time constraints with explicit use-cases (interactive systems, on-device perception, robotics, large-scale retrieval).

- Improvement: Analyze learned saliency qualitatively and quantitatively—do predicted regions align with human intuition and existing explanation methods?

- Improvement: Provide per-class analysis to reveal biases or reliance on contextual/background cues (e.g., boats-water, airplanes-sky).

- Improvement: Test consistency of saliency maps across architectures and dataset shifts—do maps generalize when the target classifier changes?

- Improvement: Include efficiency–accuracy trade-off analysis (latency vs. accuracy across resolution, model size, compute budget) for deployment clarity.

- Improvement: Add qualitative visualizations and failure-case studies to illustrate limitations (clutter, occlusion, small objects).

- Improvement: Evaluate robustness to distribution shifts and adversarial or synthetic perturbations to check for spurious correlations.

- Overall assessment: Timely, well-executed paper with strong empirical claims; strengthening real-time motivation and deeper analysis of learned saliency and biases would increase impact.
