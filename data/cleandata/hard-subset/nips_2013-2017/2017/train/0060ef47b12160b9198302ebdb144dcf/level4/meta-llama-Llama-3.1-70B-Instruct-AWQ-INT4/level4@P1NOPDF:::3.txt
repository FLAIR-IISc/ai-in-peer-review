This paper presents an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The authors' approach involves training a masking model that identifies the tightest rectangular crop containing the entire salient region of a particular requested class, as determined by a black box classifier, such as AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet. Their model architecture requires image feature maps, such as those produced by ResNet-50, across different scales. The final scale feature is passed through a feature filter, which performs initial localisation, while subsequent upsampling blocks fine-tune the produced masks. Experimental results demonstrate that their method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques in the ImageNet localisation task.

The paper appears to have sufficient references and related works, although I have not thoroughly checked them. Similarly, the paper appears to be technically correct, but I have not completely verified this. The authors present a number of intuitive discussions and explanations on how they designed their approach, which is helpful for understanding their methodology. The presentation of the paper is also good.

Overall, this paper presents interesting technical results. However, I have some concerns regarding the real-time speed claim and its applicability to real-world images.

Comments:

* Does the processing time of 100 images per second include the image resizing operation? If so, what is the running time for larger images, such as 640 x 480 images taken from an iPhone 6s?
* The salient objects in this paper are quite large; what if the requested class object is small in the images? Will a 224x224 image be sufficient?
* In Table 3, are there corresponding metrics for other works listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2]?

Miscellaneous:

* LN 151: "difference that [3] only optimise the mask" should be changed to "difference that [3] only optimises the mask".
