This paper introduces a method for learning saliency masks using a neural network. A key advantage of the proposed approach is its speed, as it can process multiple images per second.

My assessment of this paper is borderline; I could support either its acceptance or rejection. The core concept of learning to explain a model is compelling, and the paper presents several promising ideas in this direction. While the paper appears to be free of major technical errors, it has several significant limitations.

- The evaluation of saliency is indirect. The paper relies on two proxy measures—weakly supervised localization and a proposed saliency metric—both of which have shortcomings that warrant discussion.
Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy for saliency. For instance, if the context surrounding an object is critical for classification, the most salient regions may not perfectly align with the object's bounding box. While the localization results are interesting, using them as a direct measure of saliency quality has this significant caveat.

The proposed saliency metric is also flawed in its application. The metric involves cropping the estimated salient region and then rescaling it to the original image size. This process could introduce two artifacts. First, altering the aspect ratio of the cropped region may negatively affect classification performance. Second, the metric implicitly favors smaller salient regions. Because these small regions are significantly upscaled for re-classification, the object may be presented to the classifier at a non-optimal scale. Although CNNs exhibit some translation invariance, scale invariance is a learned property with inherent limitations, which could be problematic for this metric.

- The paper fails to discuss the dependency of the results on the architecture of the masking model. It would be valuable to know if the authors experimented with different architectures for the mask generator and how these choices influenced the outcomes.

**Minor comments**
- In Table 1, please clarify if the reported results are for all classes or only for the ground-truth class.
- Please specify which variant of LRP (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) and its corresponding parameter settings were used for the comparison.

**Post-rebuttal edit**
The author response confirms that the effectiveness of the saliency metric is contingent on the scale invariance of the classifier being explained. This dependency severely limits the method's applicability, as it is only suitable for networks that already possess strong scale invariance.

This has important consequences:
- The method cannot be used to analyze models during their training phase or any model that lacks this specific invariance.
- This limitation restricts its applicability to other domains where scale invariance is not a given, such as CNN-based spectrogram analysis.
- Crucially, this contradicts the paper's claim in the title of being a general method for black-box classifiers.

Furthermore, the response suggests a strong dependency on the architecture of the masking network itself. This raises a critical question: is the method visualizing the saliency of the target classifier or that of the masking network?

For the paper to be acceptable for publication, these significant limitations and dependencies must be thoroughly discussed. Without such a discussion, I recommend rejection.
