This paper proposes an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The approach trains a masking model that finds the tightest rectangular crop containing the entire salient region of a particular requested class, as identified by a black box classifier such as AlexNet, GoogleNet, or ResNet. The model architecture requires image feature maps, such as those generated by ResNet-50, across different scales. The final scale feature is passed through a feature filter that performs initial localisation, while subsequent upsampling blocks fine-tune the produced masks. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localisation task.

The paper appears to have sufficient references and related works, although a thorough check has not been performed. The technical correctness of the paper also seems to be adequate, without a comprehensive verification. The authors present a number of intuitions and discussions on the design of their approach, providing a clear understanding of their methodology. The overall presentation of the paper is good.

The paper presents interesting technical results, but some concerns remain regarding the real-time speed claim and its application to real-world images.

Comments:
- The processing time for 100 images per second includes the image resizing operation, but it is unclear what the running time would be for larger images, such as 640x480 images taken from an iPhone 6s.
- The salient objects in this paper are quite large; it is uncertain how the approach would perform if the requested class object is small in the images. Would a 224x224 image be sufficient in such cases?
- In Table 3, it would be beneficial to include corresponding metrics for other works listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2].
