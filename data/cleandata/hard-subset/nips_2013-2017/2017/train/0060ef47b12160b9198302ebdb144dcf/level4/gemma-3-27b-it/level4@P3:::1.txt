## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a novel, fast method for generating saliency maps for differentiable image classifiers. The core idea is to train a masking model to predict salient regions, enabling single-pass saliency detection and, crucially, real-time performance. The authors evaluate their approach on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, demonstrating competitive performance on weakly-supervised object localization and introducing a new saliency metric.

This work builds upon the growing field of interpretable machine learning, specifically methods for visualizing what features drive classifier decisions. Earlier work, such as gradient-based approaches [11, 12, 16], offered speed but limited quality. Iterative methods like those in [1, 2, 17] provided higher-quality saliency maps, but at a significant computational cost. This paper directly addresses the speed-quality tradeoff, offering a learned approach that aims for both.  The introduction clearly articulates the motivation – understanding classifier behavior beyond mere accuracy – and positions the work within the context of recent findings highlighting the potential for classifiers to exploit spurious correlations [6, 13].

**Strengths:**

* **Speed:** The proposed method is a significant improvement in speed over iterative approaches, achieving real-time performance. This opens up possibilities for applications like video saliency detection.
* **Competitive Performance:** The results on weakly-supervised object localization are encouraging, demonstrating performance comparable to, and in some cases exceeding, existing methods. 
* **Novelty:** The idea of learning a saliency detector, rather than calculating it iteratively, is a novel contribution. The U-Net architecture, combined with the specific objective function, appears well-suited to the task.
* **New Saliency Metric:** The proposed metric, based on the “smallest sufficient region” concept, offers a potentially more nuanced evaluation of saliency than simple localization accuracy.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Saliency Metric Limitations:** The reliance on the proposed saliency metric and weakly-supervised localization as primary evaluation measures is a significant weakness. As the authors acknowledge, localization accuracy is an imperfect proxy for saliency, particularly when context is crucial for classification. The metric's sensitivity to classifier scale and aspect ratio invariance is also concerning. The cropping and resizing step introduces potential artifacts and biases the evaluation.
* **Classifier Dependence:** The method’s dependence on the scale and aspect ratio invariance of the target classifier limits its general applicability, as highlighted in the post-rebuttal edit. This constraint significantly weakens the claim of model agnosticism and restricts its use to specific types of architectures and potentially other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis).
* **Masking Model Dependence & Interpretability:** The contribution of the masking model itself versus the underlying classifier is unclear. Is the visualization reflecting the classifier's reasoning, or the masking network’s learned biases?  Further investigation into the masking model's learned features and its dependence on architecture would be beneficial.  The authors should more explicitly address this interpretability concern.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the CIFAR-10 experiment shows the method works on a different dataset, more thorough ablation studies are needed. Specifically, an investigation of the impact of pre-training the masking model and the influence of different architectural choices on performance would strengthen the findings.
* **Comparison Details:** The comparison to LRP methods lacks specificity regarding the variant and parameter settings used. Providing these details is crucial for a fair comparison.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem – explaining black-box classifiers – with a novel and promising approach.
* The method offers a significant speed improvement over existing techniques.
* The results demonstrate competitive performance on standard benchmarks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on potentially flawed evaluation metrics raises concerns about the validity of the claimed performance gains.
* The method's limited generalizability due to its dependence on classifier properties significantly weakens its broader impact.
* The interpretability of the generated saliency maps is unclear, potentially visualizing the masking network’s behavior rather than the classifier's reasoning.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning. However, the limitations regarding the evaluation metrics and the method’s dependence on classifier properties raise significant concerns. If the authors can adequately address these limitations by discussing them thoroughly within the paper, providing further analysis, and potentially exploring alternative evaluation approaches, the paper could be considered for acceptance. Otherwise, I would lean towards rejection.



**Rating:** Borderline Reject / Weak Accept (dependent on author response)

**Recommendations:**

* **Expand the Discussion of Limitations:**  Clearly articulate the limitations of the saliency metric and the dependence on classifier properties.
* **Investigate Interpretability:**  Explore the learned features of the masking model and discuss the potential for it to introduce biases into the saliency maps.
* **Provide More Detailed Comparisons:** Specify the LRP variant and parameter settings used for comparison.
* **Consider Alternative Evaluation Metrics:**  Investigate other methods for evaluating saliency maps that are less sensitive to classifier properties.
* **Add Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the impact of pre-training and architectural choices on performance.
