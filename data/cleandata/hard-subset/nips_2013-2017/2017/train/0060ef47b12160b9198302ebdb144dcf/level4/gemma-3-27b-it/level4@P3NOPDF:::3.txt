## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

This paper presents an efficient approach to image saliency mask generation, achieving impressive processing speeds – reportedly handling a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The core idea is to train a masking model that identifies the tightest rectangular bounding box encompassing the salient region of an object belonging to a specified class, utilizing a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet). The model architecture leverages multi-scale feature maps, such as those generated by ResNet-50. Initial localization is performed via a feature filter applied to the finest-scale feature map, followed by upsampling blocks that refine the generated masks. Experiments demonstrate that this method surpasses other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task.

The paper demonstrates a solid understanding of related work, with a comprehensive list of references. While a detailed verification of all references was not undertaken, the existing citations appear sufficient. Similarly, the technical correctness of the approach seems plausible, although a complete check was not performed during this review. The authors provide insightful intuition and justification for their design choices, clearly articulating the rationale behind key architectural decisions. The presentation of the paper is well-structured and readable. 

Overall, this paper presents interesting and technically sound results. However, I have some concerns regarding the claimed real-time speed and the generalizability to real-world image scenarios. 

**Strengths:**

* **Efficiency:** The reported processing speed is a significant achievement, potentially enabling practical applications where rapid saliency detection is crucial.
* **Weakly Supervised Learning:** The approach effectively addresses the ImageNet localization problem in a weakly supervised manner, reducing the need for extensive manual annotation.
* **Multi-Scale Feature Integration:** Utilizing feature maps from multiple scales appears to be a key factor in achieving accurate and refined masks.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.



**Weaknesses & Questions:**

* **Real-Time Performance Clarification:** The claim of processing 100 images per second requires further scrutiny. Does this figure include the time taken for image resizing? If so, what is the processing time for larger, more realistic image resolutions (e.g., 640x480 images as captured by an iPhone 6s)?  The performance characteristics on varying image sizes are critical for assessing practical applicability.
* **Handling Small Objects:** The method appears to perform well with relatively large, salient objects. However, its effectiveness when the requested class object is small within the image is unclear. Will the 224x224 input size consistently provide sufficient detail to accurately localize smaller objects?  Exploring performance on datasets with a wider range of object scales would be beneficial.
* **Comparative Metrics:** Table 3 presents results for the proposed method. However, are comparable metrics available for the approaches listed in Table 2, specifically Feed [2]? A direct comparison using consistent evaluation metrics would strengthen the analysis and provide a clearer understanding of the relative performance of different methods.
* **Generalizability to Real-World Images:** While ImageNet provides a robust benchmark, real-world images often exhibit significantly more noise, clutter, and variations in lighting conditions. Further investigation into the performance of the method on more complex, uncurated datasets would be valuable.

**Minor Comments:**

* Line 273: "More over, because our model" should be corrected to "Moreover, because our model".
* Line 151: "difference that [3] only optimise the mask" should be corrected to "difference that [3] only optimizes the mask".




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of weakly supervised image localization. The proposed method is efficient and demonstrates promising results on the ImageNet dataset. However, addressing the concerns regarding real-time performance, handling small objects, and providing more comprehensive comparative analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing these points to the satisfaction of the reviewers.
