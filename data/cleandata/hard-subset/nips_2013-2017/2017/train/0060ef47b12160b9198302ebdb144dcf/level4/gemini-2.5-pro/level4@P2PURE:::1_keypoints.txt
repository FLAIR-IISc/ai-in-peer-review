- Introduces a fast neural-network method for learning saliency masks (multiple images per second).
- Reviewer is borderline—could accept or reject.
- Core concept of learning to explain a model is compelling with promising ideas.
- Paper has no major technical errors but several significant limitations.
- Evaluation of saliency is indirect, relying on two proxy measures (weakly supervised localization and a proposed saliency metric).
- Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect saliency proxy because contextual cues can make salient regions misalign with object bounding boxes.
- The proposed saliency metric is flawed: cropping and rescaling can change aspect ratio and harm classification.
- The metric implicitly favors smaller regions that are heavily upscaled, exploiting limited scale invariance in CNNs.
- The method’s reliance on classifier scale invariance is problematic and limits validity of the metric.
- Paper fails to discuss how results depend on the masking model’s architecture; experiments with different mask generators are missing.
- Minor: clarify whether Table 1 reports results for all classes or only the ground-truth class.
- Minor: specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparisons.
- Post-rebuttal: confirmed dependency on classifier scale invariance severely limits applicability and contradicts the claim of a general black-box method.
- Consequences: cannot reliably analyze models during training or models/domains lacking scale invariance (e.g., CNN-based spectrogram analysis).
- Strong dependency on masking network architecture raises the question whether the method visualizes the target classifier or the masking network.
- Recommendation: authors must thoroughly discuss these limitations and dependencies; otherwise the paper should be rejected.
