- Proposes a fast neural-network method for learning saliency masks to visualize deep nets.  
- Reviewer is borderline: finds the idea valuable but has significant concerns.  
- Main concern: saliency is measured indirectly via weakly supervised localization and a new saliency metric with inherent limitations.  
- Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy for saliency because localized objects may not indicate which image parts drive classification, especially when context matters.  
- The proposed saliency metric suffers from cropping/rescaling artifacts and aspect-ratio changes that can harm classifier performance.  
- The metric biases toward smaller salient regions; heavy upscaling may exploit classifier scale invariance rather than true saliency.  
- It’s unclear whether observed performance reflects saliency or the classifier’s learned scale invariance limits.  
- Missing analysis of sensitivity to architectural choices of the masking model and whether masks depend on masking-network design.  
- Table 1 needs clarification: are scores aggregated across all classes or only on correctly classified instances?  
- LRP comparison lacks specifics: which LRP variant and parameter settings were used?  
- Post-rebuttal: saliency metric performance is tightly coupled to classifier scale invariance and overall classifier quality.  
- This dependence limits applicability: method may not work during training or for models/domains without scale invariance (e.g., spectrograms).  
- Claim of general applicability to “black-box” classifiers is overstated given these constraints.  
- Ambiguity remains whether visualizations reflect the original classifier’s saliency or biases from the masking network; paper needs to address these limitations to be publishable.
