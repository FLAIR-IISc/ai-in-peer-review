- Proposes a fast, model-agnostic "masking model" (U-Net) to produce saliency masks in a single forward pass instead of per-image iterative optimization.  
- Trains the masker with an objective combining total variation (smoothness), area (compactness), and terms for SSR and SDR saliency criteria.  
- Introduces a new metric evaluating saliency by classification accuracy on a tight crop of the salient area.  
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet using AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50 as black-box classifiers.  
- Achieves sharp, interpretable masks and state-of-the-art results on weakly-supervised ImageNet object localization.  
- Main novelty/significance: reframes saliency as a learning problem enabling real-time performance (~100 images/sec).  
- Technical quality: well-motivated objective and mitigation for introduced-evidence issues (e.g., randomized background).  
- Strong empirical results: outperforms other weakly-supervised and saliency-based methods and shows visually accurate masks.  
- Paper is clear and well-organized with good justification for design choices and a useful new metric.  
- Practicality concern: 100 images/sec reported at 224x224 — unclear if preprocessing included and how inference scales to higher resolutions.  
- Small-object performance concern: fixed 224x224 input may fail on small objects; need analysis or qualitative examples.  
- Incomplete comparison: new metric results (Table 3) should include other competing saliency methods for a fair evaluation.  
- Overall: high-quality, novel, and significant contribution; weaknesses are mostly requests for additional experiments/clarifications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: paradigm shift to learned saliency, real-time capability, state-of-the-art localization, technically sound and clearly presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: evaluation gaps on small objects and metric comparisons, and insufficient substantiation of real-time claims for practical, high-resolution settings.
