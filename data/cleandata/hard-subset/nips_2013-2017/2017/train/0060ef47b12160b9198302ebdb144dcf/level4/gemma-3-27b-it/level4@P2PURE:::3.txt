This paper introduces an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The approach trains a masking model to identify the tightest rectangular crop containing the entire salient region of a requested class, using a black-box classifier such as AlexNet, GoogleNet, or ResNet. The model architecture leverages image feature maps – such as those generated by ResNet-50 – at multiple scales. The feature map from the final scale is initially localized using a feature filter, and subsequent upsampling blocks refine the resulting masks. Experiments demonstrate that this method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task.

The paper includes a sufficient number of references and related works; a full check was not performed. The paper appears to be technically sound; a full check was not performed. The authors present a number of insightful intuitions and discussions regarding their design choices. The presentation is well-written and clear. 

Overall, this paper presents interesting technical results. However, I have some concerns regarding the claimed real-time speed and the applicability of the method to real-world images.

Comments:

- Does the reported processing time of 100 images per second include image resizing? If so, what is the running time for larger images, such as 640x480 images from an iPhone 6s?
- The salient objects considered in this paper appear relatively large. How does the method perform when the requested class object is small within the image? Would a 224x224 image size be sufficient in these cases?
- In Table 3, are there corresponding metrics available for other methods listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2]?
