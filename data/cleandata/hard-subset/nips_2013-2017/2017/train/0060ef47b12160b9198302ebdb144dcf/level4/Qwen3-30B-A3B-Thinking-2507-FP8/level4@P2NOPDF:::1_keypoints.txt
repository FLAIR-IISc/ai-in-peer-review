- Proposes a fast neural-network method for saliency masks that is conceptually interesting but has significant flaws preventing NIPS-level acceptance.
- Core flaw: saliency quality is measured indirectly and incorrectly, undermining validation.
- Weakly supervised localization is an unsuitable primary metric because contextual cues can decouple object location from saliency.
- The proposed saliency metric introduces artifacts by cropping and rescaling salient regions, causing aspect-ratio distortion and favoring small regions.
- The metric implicitly assumes classifier scale invariance, which is unexamined and invalid for many classifiers.
- The paper never evaluates how saliency/mask quality depends on the underlying classifier architecture (e.g., ResNet vs. VGG).
- Rebuttal confirms metric’s dependence on classifier scale invariance, implying restricted applicability, domain limitations, and invalid claims of generality.
- Masking model architecture likely biases results, but the paper fails to analyze whether outputs reflect the masking network or the target classifier.
- Minor clarifications missing: whether Table 1 averages across all classes or only correct predictions, and which LRP variant/parameters were used.
- Conclusion: the metric’s classifier-specific dependencies and unaddressed artifacts undermine the method’s claims; substantial revisions required—current paper should be rejected.
