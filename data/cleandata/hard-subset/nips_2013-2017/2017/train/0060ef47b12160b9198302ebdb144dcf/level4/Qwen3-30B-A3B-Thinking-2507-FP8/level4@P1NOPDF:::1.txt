

The paper proposes a neural network approach for learning saliency masks, capable of processing multiple images per second. While I strongly support the concept of learning to explain models and acknowledge the paper's promising ideas, the work presents significant limitations that warrant careful consideration. The paper is borderline; I would not object to either acceptance or rejection, but these limitations must be adequately addressed.

The primary concern is the indirect evaluation of saliency. Both the weakly supervised localization results and the proposed saliency metric suffer from fundamental flaws that require explicit discussion. Weak supervision relies on object localization, but this correlation with saliency quality is unreliable. If an object's context is critical for classification (e.g., a "dog" in a park vs. a "dog" on a sofa), localization may not reflect true saliency. Thus, using localization results as a saliency metric introduces a significant caveat that the paper does not acknowledge.

The proposed saliency metric also has critical issues. It evaluates saliency by cropping the estimated salient region, rescaling it to the original image dimensions while preserving aspect ratio, and then reclassifying it. This process risks introducing two artifacts: (1) aspect ratio changes may degrade classification performance, and (2) small salient regions undergo substantial scaling during rescaling. While CNNs exhibit translation invariance, scaling invariance is not inherent and has practical limits. A heavily scaled small region may present the object to the classifier at a non-optimal scale, undermining the metric's validity.

Furthermore, the paper does not investigate how the masking model depends on the underlying network architecture. Did the authors experiment with alternative architectures? How did architectural choices impact saliency quality? This omission is critical for assessing the method's generality.

Minor clarifications are needed:
- Are the results in Table 1 reported for all classes or only for correctly classified instances?
- Please specify the exact LRP variant and parameter settings (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) used for comparison, as these choices significantly affect results.

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment**  
The rebuttal confirms a core limitation: the saliency metric's efficacy depends entirely on the classifier's scale invariance properties. This severely restricts the method's applicability. Specifically:
- The approach cannot be used during model training or on classifiers lacking scale invariance.
- It is inapplicable to domains like spectrogram analysis where CNNs may not exhibit this invariance.
- The claim that the method applies to "black-box classifiers" is unsupported, as the metric inherently requires access to the classifier's internal representations.

Additionally, the rebuttal hints at a strong dependency on the masking network architecture. This raises a fundamental question: are we visualizing the saliency of the target model (e.g., the CNN) or the masking network itself? The paper does not clarify this distinction.

If the authors explicitly address these limitations—particularly the classifier invariance dependency and the masking network ambiguity—the paper could be balanced enough for publication. However, without these clarifications, the method's scope is overstated, and the evaluation framework is compromised. The current presentation risks misleading readers about the technique's generalizability. Therefore, acceptance hinges entirely on whether the revised manuscript thoroughly discusses these critical constraints.
