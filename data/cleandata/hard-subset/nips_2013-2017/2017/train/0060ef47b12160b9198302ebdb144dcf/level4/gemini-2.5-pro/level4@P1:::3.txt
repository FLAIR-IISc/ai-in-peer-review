This paper introduces a fast, model-agnostic method for generating saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier. The core idea is to train a dedicated "masking model," based on a U-Net architecture, to predict a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This approach stands in contrast to previous methods that rely on slow, iterative optimization for each image. The masking model is trained to minimize a well-designed objective function that balances mask smoothness, compactness, and performance on two saliency criteria: identifying the smallest sufficient region (SSR) for classification and the smallest destroying region (SDR). The authors also propose a new metric to evaluate saliency map quality based on classification accuracy on a tight crop of the salient area.

The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, using classifiers like AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50 as black boxes. The results demonstrate that the proposed technique produces sharp, interpretable saliency maps and achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly-supervised ImageNet object localization task.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The main contribution is reframing saliency detection from a per-image optimization problem to a learning problem solved by a dedicated generative model. This is a significant conceptual shift that enables real-time performance (~100 images/sec), making saliency analysis practical for applications like video processing, which is a major advantage over iterative techniques.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed objective function is sophisticated and well-motivated. It thoughtfully combines a total variation penalty for smoothness, an area penalty for compactness, and terms that explicitly optimize for both the SSR and SDR criteria. The discussion on "introduced evidence" and the proposed mitigation strategies (e.g., randomizing the masked-out background) are insightful and address a subtle but critical issue in perturbation-based explanation methods.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates excellent performance on the ImageNet weakly-supervised localization task, outperforming other weakly-supervised and saliency-based methods by a clear margin. The quality of the generated masks, shown in Figure 5, is visually impressive—they are sharp and accurately delineate the object of interest.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide clear intuition and justification for their design choices, from the objective function to the model architecture. The introduction of a new, intuitive saliency metric is also a valuable contribution to the field.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

While the paper presents compelling results, there are a few areas that could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **Practicality of Real-Time Claims:** The claim of processing 100 images per second is a key selling point. However, this is reported for 224x224 images. For many of the real-world applications envisioned, such as analyzing video from autonomous vehicles, input resolutions are significantly higher. Could the authors please clarify if the reported processing time includes pre-processing steps like image resizing? Furthermore, it would be valuable to provide information on how the model's inference time scales with increasing input image resolution (e.g., for 640x480 or HD images).

2.  **Performance on Small Objects:** The experiments and examples seem to focus on images where the salient object is relatively large and prominent. The reliance on a fixed 224x224 input size raises a question about the method's effectiveness on images containing small objects. Downsampling an image with a small object might erase the very features necessary for localization. An analysis or at least some qualitative examples demonstrating the model's performance on small objects would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of general applicability.

3.  **Incomplete Comparison with the New Metric:** The new saliency metric proposed in Section 3.2 is an interesting idea. However, in Table 3, the authors only compare their method against baselines and ground-truth boxes. To convincingly establish the utility of this new metric and demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method, it would be very helpful to include results for the other competing saliency methods from Table 2 (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi [2], Zhou et al. [17]). This would provide a more complete and persuasive evaluation.

### Summary

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-executed, and significant contribution to the field of model interpretability. The shift to a learning-based approach for saliency detection is impactful, and the results are state-of-the-art. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to requests for clarification and more comprehensive experiments, which do not detract from the core contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and significant paradigm shift for saliency detection (learning vs. iterative optimization).
*   Achieves real-time performance, opening up new application domains.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark (weakly-supervised ImageNet localization).
*   The methodology is technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly explained.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The evaluation could be strengthened by analyzing performance on small objects and providing a more complete comparison using the newly proposed metric.
*   Claims regarding real-time performance for practical applications need more substantiation.
