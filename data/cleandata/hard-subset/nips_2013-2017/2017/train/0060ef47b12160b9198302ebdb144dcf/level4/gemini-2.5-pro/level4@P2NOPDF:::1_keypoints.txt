- Proposes an efficient neural network method for generating saliency masks that can process multiple images per second.
- Overall assessment: borderline paper with valuable ideas but significant limitations and need for transparent discussion.
- Major concern: saliency quality is evaluated only indirectly using two flawed metrics that require explicit discussion.
- Weakly supervised localization metric is problematic because contextual cues important for classification may not correspond to tight object bounding boxes.
- The proposed quantitative metric is flawed due to cropping-and-resizing artifacts that can distort scale/aspect ratio and bias results toward smaller regions.
- Resizing cropped salient regions can present objects at scales/aspect ratios unseen by the classifier, harming validity of re-classification tests.
- The metric implicitly favors smaller regions, which may not equate to better or more complete explanations.
- The paper fails to analyze sensitivity to the masking network architecture; an ablation study is needed to assess generalizability.
- Minor reproducibility issues: unclear whether Table 1 results are over all classes or only correctly classified instances.
- Minor reproducibility issue: unspecified LRP variant and parameter settings used for comparison.
- Post-rebuttal: authors confirm metric depends strongly on classifier scale/aspect-ratio robustness, revealing a critical limitation.
- Consequently, the method is not generally applicable to any black-box classifier and is restricted to models with learned scale/aspect invariances.
- Applicability to other domains without such invariances (e.g., spectrogram analysis) is questionable.
- There is ambiguity whether generated maps explain the target classifier or reflect biases of the masking network, undermining interpretability.
- Conclusion: novel and fast approach but claims overstate generality; authors must transparently state limitations or the paper should be rejected.
