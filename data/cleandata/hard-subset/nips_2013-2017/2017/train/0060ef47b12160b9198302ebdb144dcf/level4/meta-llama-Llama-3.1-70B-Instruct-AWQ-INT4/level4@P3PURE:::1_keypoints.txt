- Proposes a neural-network method to learn saliency masks that is computationally efficient (multiple images per second).  
- Reviewer is neutral/borderline and sees merits but would not object to either acceptance or rejection.  
- Paper appears technically sound with no obvious mistakes.  
- Main concern: saliency is only indirectly measured via weakly supervised localization or the proposed saliency metric, both having limitations that need discussion.  
- Weakly supervised localization may not correlate with true saliency when object context is essential for class determination.  
- The proposed saliency metric’s crop-and-rescale procedure can introduce artifacts: aspect-ratio changes and preference for small regions, impacting classification due to CNNs’ lack of scale invariance.  
- The paper does not discuss how the masking model’s results depend on the architecture used or whether different architectures were tested.  
- Minor query: clarify whether Table 1 results are for all classes or only the correct class.  
- Minor query: specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparison (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta).  
- Post-rebuttal: the metric’s dependence on classifier scale invariance is a significant limitation, restricting use during training, across domains (e.g., spectrograms), and contradicting claims of black-box applicability.  
- Post-rebuttal: strong dependence on the masking network raises concern that the method may be visualizing the masking network (U-network) rather than the target model’s saliency.  
- Conclusion: if these limitations and dependencies are properly discussed in the paper, publication is acceptable; otherwise it should not be published.
