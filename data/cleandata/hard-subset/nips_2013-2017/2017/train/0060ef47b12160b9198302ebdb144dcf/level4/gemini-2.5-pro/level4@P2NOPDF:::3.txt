### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel and efficient method for generating image saliency masks. The proposed approach can process 224x224 images at a rate of 100 frames per second on a standard GPU. The core contribution is a masking model trained to identify the tightest rectangular crop enclosing the salient region of a class specified by a pre-trained, black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, ResNet). The model's architecture utilizes multi-scale feature maps from a backbone network like ResNet-50. An initial coarse localization is performed on the final, low-resolution feature map, which is then progressively refined through a series of upsampling blocks to produce a high-resolution mask. The authors demonstrate through experiments that their method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task.

### **Strengths**

*   **High Efficiency:** The reported processing speed of 100 images per second is a significant advantage, making the approach suitable for real-time applications.
*   **Strong Performance:** The method achieves state-of-the-art results compared to other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization benchmark.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The authors provide valuable intuition and discussion regarding their architectural design choices, which aids in understanding the method.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed methodology appears technically correct and is well-motivated.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

Overall, this paper presents interesting technical results. However, I have some concerns regarding the real-time speed claim and its applicability to real-world scenarios, which I hope the authors can address.

1.  **Details of Processing Time:** The claim of 100 images per second is impressive. Could the authors clarify if this processing time includes the image resizing operation to 224x224? Furthermore, could you provide benchmarks or an estimate of the running time for larger, more common image resolutions, such as 640x480 photos captured by modern smartphones?

2.  **Performance on Small Objects:** The experiments primarily feature images where the salient object is relatively large. How does the model perform when the target object is small within the image? Is the 224x224 input resolution sufficient to capture the necessary detail for localizing very small objects, or does performance degrade significantly in such cases?

3.  **Incomplete Comparisons in Table 3:** In Table 3, the authors present several performance metrics for their method. For a more complete comparison, would it be possible to include corresponding metrics for other relevant methods from the literature, such as Feed [2] which is mentioned in Table 2?
