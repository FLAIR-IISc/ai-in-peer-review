- Proposes learning saliency masks via a neural network with a significant speed advantage (multiple images per second).  
- Reviewer is borderline on acceptance — would not strongly object to either accept or reject.  
- Reviewer supports the concept of learning to explain model behavior and finds some ideas worthwhile.  
- No obvious errors, but clear limitations exist that must be addressed.  
- Saliency measurement is indirect: both weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric have inherent limitations.  
- Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy because object context can be crucial for class determination.  
- The proposed saliency metric’s implementation (crop + rescale keeping original aspect ratio) can introduce artifacts.  
- Two metric artifacts: altered aspect ratio may hurt classification; metric implicitly favors smaller regions due to magnification on rescaling.  
- Rescaling magnification may mismatch classifier scale invariance, biasing the saliency score.  
- Paper does not report how much masking-model performance depends on underlying network architecture or experiments across architectures.  
- Minor queries: Are Table 1 results computed over all classes or only correctly classified examples? Specify LRP variant and parameter settings.  
- Post-rebuttal: The saliency metric’s strong dependence on classifier quality and scale invariance greatly limits the method’s applicability.  
- Consequences: method cannot reliably analyze models during training, is unsuitable for models lacking invariances, limits cross-domain use (e.g., spectrograms), and undermines the “black-box” claim.  
- Strong dependence on the masking network raises uncertainty whether visualizations reflect the underlying classifier or the masking network.  
- Recommendation: if these issues are thoroughly discussed and contextualized, the paper could be acceptable; otherwise the reviewer would recommend rejection.
