This paper proposes an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing roughly 100 224×224 images per second on a standard GPU. The method trains a masking model to find the tightest rectangular crop that contains the entire salient region for a requested class, conditioned on a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet). The architecture operates on multi-scale image feature maps (e.g., from ResNet-50): a feature filter performs the initial localization, and subsequent upsampling blocks refine the produced masks. Experiments show that the method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task.

The paper appears to have sufficient references and related work, though I did not verify this exhaustively. It also appears technically correct, although I did not check every detail. The paper provides useful intuitions and design discussions, and the presentation is good.

Overall, the paper presents interesting technical results, though I have some concerns about the real-time speed claim and applicability to real-world images.

Comments:
- Does the reported throughput of ~100 images per second include image resizing? If so, what is the running time for larger inputs, such as 640×480 images taken from an iPhone 6s?
- The salient objects in the paper’s examples appear quite large. What happens when the requested-class object is small in the image? Is a 224×224 input sufficient in that case?
- In Table 3, are there corresponding metrics for the other works listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2]?
