- Introduces a fast, model-agnostic saliency method that trains a masking model to predict salient regions by manipulating classifier scores.  
- Enables single-pass saliency detection suitable for real-time applications (reported ~100 images/sec).  
- Evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, producing interpretable, sharp saliency maps and strong weakly supervised localization.  
- Proposes a new saliency evaluation metric intended to be more nuanced than simple localization accuracy.  
- Builds on gradient-based and iterative “black box” saliency work, notably differing from Fong & Vedaldi by using a learned masker instead of iterative optimization.  
- Technically sound: U-Net architecture with a novel loss combining smoothness, region size, and classification probability.  
- Experiments are well-designed, compare to relevant baselines, and include an informative ablation study on CIFAR-10.  
- Discussion of introduced evidence and adversarial artifacts is insightful, with sensible mitigation strategies like blurring or noise.  
- Strength: strong experimental results on standard benchmarks and clear, well-written presentation.  
- Strength: potential real-time applicability (e.g., video saliency) highlighted by the speed claim.  
- Weakness: unclear whether reported real-time speed includes pre- and post-processing costs (e.g., resizing).  
- Weakness: limited evaluation on complex, real-world images with cluttered backgrounds or multiple objects.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of how different classifier architectures affect masker performance and class-wise ImageNet results.  
- Weakness: the new saliency metric needs further justification and validation of its correlation with human perception.  
- Recommendation: overall positive — recommend acceptance pending clarifications on real-time claims and metric validation.  
- Minor issues: a few typos (LN 273, LN 151) and suggestion to report the proposed saliency metric for other methods in Table 2 (e.g., Feed [2]).
