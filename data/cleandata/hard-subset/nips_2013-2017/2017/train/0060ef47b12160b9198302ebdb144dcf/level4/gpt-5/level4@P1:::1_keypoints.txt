- Proposes a learned feed-forward masking model (U-Net + ResNet-50 encoder) that outputs class-conditional saliency maps in one pass.  
- Trains the mask network with a loss balancing TV/sparsity and two fidelity terms: preserve class score on kept region and lower it when salient region is removed.  
- Targets both Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and Smallest Destroying Region (SDR) notions of saliency.  
- Evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10; shows competitive weakly supervised localization and reasonable CIFAR-10 performance without pretraining.  
- Achieves real-time speed (>100 fps for 224×224), enabling potential video applications.  
- Introduces a new crop-based saliency metric that resizes the salient crop and measures class probability vs area.  

- Strength: conceptually appealing — amortizes costly per-image optimization via a separate masking network.  
- Strength: very fast single-pass inference suitable for real-time use.  
- Strength: empirically produces sharper, more interpretable masks and improves localization vs baselines.  
- Strength: objective design is thoughtful, using TV regularization and randomized evidence replacement to mitigate adversarial artifacts.  
- Strength: proposes a novel metric attempting to operationalize SSR.  

- Weakness: evaluation is indirect — localization and the crop metric are imperfect proxies for explanation quality.  
- Weakness: crop-based metric conflates scaling and aspect-ratio effects by resizing crops to classifier input size.  
- Consequence: aspect-ratio changes can systematically alter classification and bias the metric.  
- Consequence: metric favors small regions that are heavily upscaled, exploiting limited scale invariance of convnets.  
- Weakness: heavy dependence on classifier invariances limits applicability (not suitable during training or for models/domains lacking those invariances).  
- Weakness: generality claim to black-box classifiers is undermined by reliance on classifier-specific invariances.  
- Weakness: approach appears sensitive to masking network architecture and encoder choice; unclear if masks reflect classifier or masker biases.  
- Weakness: several implementation/baseline details are under-specified (e.g., which LRP variant/parameters, class selection in evaluation).  

- Quality: method is technically sound and well-regularized but evaluation depends on known-imperfect proxies, requiring deeper failure-mode analysis.  
- Clarity: paper is readable but should more explicitly discuss evaluation limitations, classifier-invariance dependence, and architectural sensitivity; clarify baseline/evaluation details.  
- Originality: training a feed-forward masking model targeting preservation and deletion is a meaningful extension of perturbation methods; crop metric is novel but flawed.  
- Significance: could be a useful practical tool if limitations are addressed; current generality claims are stronger than supported by evaluation.  

- Questions for authors: Are Table 1 localization results for ground-truth class only or across predicted classes?  
- Questions: Which LRP variant and hyperparameters were used?  
- Questions: How sensitive are results to masking network architecture (encoder, depth, skip connections)? Any ablations?  
- Questions: Can you quantify how the crop-based metric changes with controlled variations in object scale/aspect ratio for a fixed image/classifier?  

- Pros for acceptance: fast, practical method with clear speed and interpretability advantages and thoughtful defenses against adversarial artifacts.  
- Cons for acceptance: reliance on imperfect proxies and classifier invariances, overstated generality, and limited analysis of masking-network-induced biases.  

- Post-rebuttal: reviewer emphasizes the method’s reliance on classifier scale/aspect invariance and masking-network dependence; recommends explicit discussion of these limitations and views paper as borderline-accept if addressed, otherwise leaning to rejection.
