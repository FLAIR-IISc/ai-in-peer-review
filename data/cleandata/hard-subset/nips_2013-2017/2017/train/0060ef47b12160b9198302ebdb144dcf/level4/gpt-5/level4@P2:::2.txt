This paper proposes a trainable masking network that generates class-conditional saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass. The model is trained to manipulate the classifier’s scores by preserving or removing image evidence under a loss that balances smoothness (TV), sparsity (area), recognition from preserved regions (SSR), and score suppression when salient regions are removed (SDR). To reduce adversarial artifacts, the authors enforce mask smoothness and apply masks against randomized “alternative” images (blur or noise). The approach is evaluated on ImageNet (with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 as black boxes) and CIFAR-10, showing sharper, more interpretable maps and better weakly supervised localization than prior methods. The paper also introduces a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region principle and reports favorable results.

Strengths
- Clear, well-structured paper that motivates the problem and situates it among gradient-based and iterative optimization baselines.
- Method is model-agnostic, fast (single pass), and produces visually sharp, artifact-resistant maps suitable for real-time use.
- Comprehensive experiments on challenging datasets with state-of-the-art classifiers; competitive weakly supervised localization without using bounding boxes.
- The proposed saliency metric is sensible, grounded in SSR/SDR, and helps quantify interpretability beyond standard localization accuracy.

Areas for improvement
- Better motivate real-time requirements with concrete scenarios (e.g., interactive model debugging, on-device explainability, streaming/video analysis, or safety-critical monitoring), and clarify latency/throughput targets relative to those use cases.
- Provide more analysis of the learned saliency across classes to probe dataset and model biases (e.g., which categories rely on background context such as “snow” for “polar bear,” or exhibit spurious correlations). Class-wise statistics, qualitative failure cases, or correlations with known biases would add valuable insight.

Assessment by NIPS criteria
- Quality: The methodology is technically sound, with a well-justified objective and reasonable defenses against adversarial artifacts. Results are strong across multiple architectures and datasets. Additional analysis of failure modes and bias would further strengthen the work.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized, with an intuitive problem formulation and clear comparisons. Some sections (real-time motivation, analysis of learned biases) could be expanded.
- Originality: Training a general masking network to approximate iterative saliency optimization in a single pass, with a loss balancing SSR and SDR and randomized evidence removal, is a distinctive and useful contribution relative to backprop- and optimization-based methods.
- Significance: The approach is practical and broadly applicable, enabling real-time saliency for black-box classifiers and potentially facilitating video/interactive explainability. The new metric may influence future evaluation practices.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-executed paper with strong empirical performance. Clarifying real-time application scenarios and adding analysis of learned saliency patterns and biases would further enhance its impact. I lean toward acceptance.
