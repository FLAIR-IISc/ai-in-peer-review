

The paper introduces a novel approach for real-time saliency detection applicable to any differentiable image classifier. The authors propose training a masking model (a U-Net architecture) to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, contrasting with iterative methods that require hundreds of passes. This model generalizes well to unseen images and produces sharp, artifact-free saliency maps. The authors evaluate their method using a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR), which measures the smallest rectangular crop containing salient features that still allows confident classification. They demonstrate competitive results on ImageNet object localization (36.7% error rate for ResNet-50-based model) and CIFAR-10, outperforming existing weakly supervised approaches.

This work builds upon recent advances in model interpretability, particularly related to the "smallest sufficient region" concept introduced by Cao et al. (2015) and Fong & Vedaldi (2017), while addressing their computational limitations. Unlike gradient-based methods (e.g., Simonyan et al., 2013; Springenberg et al., 2014), which produce lower-quality explanations, or iterative methods (Zhou et al., 2016), which are computationally expensive, the authors' approach offers both speed and quality improvements. The paper also contributes a new metric that better captures interpretability than standard localization accuracy, which is particularly valuable given the limitations of existing evaluation methods.

The paper's main strengths are its technical soundness, practical contribution, and clear experimental validation. The masking model achieves real-time performance (100+ masks/second), making it suitable for applications like video saliency. The proposed saliency metric provides a more nuanced evaluation of interpretability than standard localization accuracy, and the authors demonstrate its validity by showing their method achieves scores comparable to ground truth bounding boxes. The architecture (U-Net with ResNet-50 backbone) is well-chosen for the task, and the training procedure with random evidence removal strategies effectively prevents adversarial artifacts. The experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets provide convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness across different architectures and resolutions.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. First, the evaluation methodology has important limitations that are not properly discussed. The new saliency metric relies on cropping salient regions and resizing them to classifier input dimensions, which introduces two critical issues: (1) the aspect ratio change during resizing may affect classification performance, and (2) the metric favors smaller regions, but resizing small regions to classifier input size may present objects at non-ideal scales. As the authors acknowledge in the post-rebuttal, this makes the metric highly dependent on the classifier's scale invariance properties. Consequently, the method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers as claimed in the title, as it requires classifiers that exhibit scale invariance (which many do not, especially during training or in domains like spectrogram analysis).

Second, the paper fails to adequately discuss the relationship between the masking model and the black-box classifier. The response hints at strong dependence between the masking model architecture and the performance, yet the paper does not investigate how different masking architectures affect results. This raises concerns about whether the saliency maps reflect the black-box classifier's behavior or the masking model's biases. The authors should clarify whether they're visualizing the classifier's saliency or the masking model's learned representations.

Third, the weakly supervised localization results, while interesting, have an important caveat: if an object's context is essential for classification (e.g., "polar bear" vs. "snow"), localization accuracy may not correlate with saliency quality. The paper should discuss this limitation more thoroughly, as it directly impacts the interpretation of their localization results.

The paper is well-written and clear overall, with good organization and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The technical approach is novel in its combination of a trainable masking model with the SSR concept, though the novelty is somewhat incremental given the parallel work by Fong & Vedaldi (2017). The significance is moderate: while the method enables new applications (e.g., video saliency), its limitations restrict broader applicability.

In summary, the paper makes a valuable contribution to real-time model interpretability but has significant limitations in its evaluation methodology and scope that undermine its claims of being "model agnostic" and applicable to "any differentiable image classifier." The authors should substantially revise the paper to address these limitations, particularly by discussing the scale invariance requirement for the metric and clarifying the relationship between the masking model and the black-box classifier.

Strengths:
- Real-time performance (100+ masks/sec)
- Novel masking model architecture
- New saliency metric that better captures interpretability
- Strong empirical results on ImageNet localization
- Clear experimental design

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation metric depends on classifier scale invariance
- Limited discussion of method's applicability constraints
- Ambiguity about whether saliency reflects classifier or masking model behavior
- Insufficient discussion of limitations in weakly supervised localization

I believe the paper has potential but requires significant revisions to address these limitations before publication. The current version overstates the general applicability of the method. With proper discussion of these constraints and more thorough analysis of the metric's limitations, the paper could make a meaningful contribution to the field. As written, however, the method's restrictions make it less broadly applicable than claimed, which is a significant concern for a paper targeting NIPS. I recommend rejection unless the authors provide a more nuanced discussion of the method's limitations and constraints.
