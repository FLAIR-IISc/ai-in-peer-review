This paper proposes a fast image saliency detection method that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The approach trains a masking model to find the tightest rectangular crop that contains the entire salient region of a particular requested class by a black box classifier. The model architecture requires image feature maps over different scales, and the final scale feature is passed through a feature filter that performs the initial localization, while the following upsampling blocks fine-tune the produced masks.

The paper provides a thorough discussion of related work, including various image saliency detection methods and their limitations. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric that assesses the quality of explanations produced by saliency detectors. The experimental results show that their method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques at the ImageNet localization task.

The paper appears to be technically correct, and the presentation is good. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its components, including the masking model, feature filter, and upsampling blocks. The paper also includes a number of intuitive discussions on how they design their approach.

However, I have some concerns about the real-time speed claim and applications to real-world images. Specifically, I would like to know if the processing time for 100 images per second includes the image resizing operation. If so, what is the running time for other larger images, such as 640 x 480 images taken from iPhone 6s? Additionally, I am concerned about the size of the salient objects in the images. What if the requested class object is small in the images? Will 224x224 image be enough?

* The paper proposes a fast and accurate image saliency detection method that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier.
* The approach is technically sound, and the presentation is good.
* The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work and introduce a new saliency metric.

Weaknesses:

* The real-time speed claim and applications to real-world images are not thoroughly evaluated.
* The size of the salient objects in the images is not thoroughly discussed.
* The comparison with existing approaches is not comprehensive.
