Summary
This paper proposes a trainable masking model that produces class-conditional saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass. The model is trained to manipulate the classifier’s output by masking image regions while encouraging smooth, compact masks that both preserve class evidence (SSR) and remove it (SDR). The authors build on prior work in gradient-based saliency (e.g., Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop) and optimization-by-perturbation methods (e.g., Zhou et al., Cao et al., Fong & Vedaldi), but differ by learning a feed-forward predictor of masks rather than optimizing a mask per image. They also introduce a new saliency metric based on cropping the predicted salient region and re-classifying it, and evaluate on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, reporting strong weakly supervised localization and real-time throughput.

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: learning to explain a classifier by training a separate masking network is a compelling idea that amortizes expensive per-image optimization and enables real-time use.
- Practical impact: the method is fast (single pass, >100 fps on 224×224) and produces visually sharp, interpretable masks compared to iterative methods.
- Empirical results: competitive weakly supervised localization on ImageNet; reasonable masks on CIFAR-10 even without pretraining; useful qualitative comparisons.
- Clear articulation of pitfalls with “introduced evidence” and steps taken to mitigate artifacts (alternative image A, TV regularization, low-res masks).

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Evaluation metrics and their limits:
  - Localization is not saliency: salient evidence is often a discriminative part (e.g., a dog’s head) rather than the entire object; thus localization accuracy only weakly correlates with saliency quality. This caveat should be discussed more explicitly when interpreting Table 1/2.
  - Proposed saliency metric depends on cropping and resizing the salient region to the classifier’s input size. This introduces aspect-ratio distortions and scale changes that can strongly affect classification probability. Since CNNs are not inherently scale-invariant, the metric conflates mask quality with the classifier’s robustness to scale/aspect changes.
- Dependence on architectural choices:
  - The masking model uses a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and a specific feature-filter mechanism. It is unclear how sensitive performance is to this design. Please add ablations across masking architectures, the role of pretraining, and the contribution of each loss term (λ1..λ4).
  - The method’s behavior may depend on the choice of alternative image A (blur vs. noisy color). An ablation on A, TV strength, mask resolution, and the upsampling scheme would strengthen claims about artifact avoidance and mask sharpness.
- “Black box” assumption: Training appears to require backpropagation through the classifier to optimize the masking network; if so, the classifier is not a true black box during training. Please clarify whether gradients from the classifier are required and, if not, how the masking model is optimized.
- Generality and applicability:
  - Because the proposed metric and success criteria rely on scale/aspect invariance, the method is not broadly applicable as-is to models that lack such invariance (e.g., during early training, or in domains like spectrograms). This conflicts with the “black-box” generality implied in the title and should be clearly scoped.
  - The paper suggests the masking model acts as a regularizer and could itself introduce biases. Please analyze whether the produced maps reflect the classifier’s saliency or the masking network’s learned priors (e.g., cross-class/cross-model transfer, training the masking model on one classifier and testing on another).
- Experimental details:
  - It is unclear whether Table 1 localization results are computed for the ground-truth class only or include top-1 predictions. Please specify the protocol.
  - If LRP is used as a baseline, please state the exact variant and parameters (epsilon, alpha-beta, etc.).
  - Provide details on thresholds (e.g., mask threshold 0.5), crop area floor (0.05), class selector sampling, and randomization seeds.

Quality
The approach is technically plausible and thoughtfully engineered to reduce artifacts. However, more rigorous ablations and clearer discussion of the metric’s assumptions are needed to substantiate generality and causal interpretation of the masks.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. It would benefit from: precise definition of “black box,” training-time gradient usage, a detailed evaluation protocol, and expanded discussion of metric limitations and architectural sensitivity.

Originality
Learning a feed-forward saliency predictor optimized for SSR/SDR is a meaningful extension of iterative perturbation methods and an advance over purely gradient-based techniques.

Significance
The speed and sharpness of the maps are promising for real-time applications. That said, the proposed metric’s dependence on classifier invariances and the lack of thorough ablations limit the strength of the current empirical claims.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, fast, and broadly interesting approach to learned saliency
- Strong qualitative results; competitive weakly supervised localization
- Clear articulation of adversarial-artifact issues and mitigation strategies
- New metric that encourages compact, evidence-preserving masks

Cons for acceptance
- Saliency evaluation is indirect; localization is an imperfect proxy
- Proposed metric is confounded by scale/aspect distortions and classifier invariance
- Unclear “black box” status during training; missing architectural and loss ablations
- Potential dependence on masking-network priors not sufficiently analyzed
- Applicability limited to classifiers with sufficient scale invariance

Post-rebuttal note
The method’s reliance on the classifier’s scale/aspect invariance further restricts applicability: it cannot be reliably used during model training or on models/domains lacking this invariance, and thus is not universally “black-box.” The response also suggests strong dependence on the masking network, raising the concern that visualizations may partly reflect the masker’s biases. These issues should be explicitly discussed and, where possible, evaluated.

Minor comments
- Clarify whether Table 1 uses ground-truth labels only.
- Specify the exact LRP variant and parameters if used as a baseline.
- Provide ablations on A, TV, mask resolution, and λ weights.
- Discuss whether gradients through the classifier are required during training.

Overall
Borderline. With clearer scoping of claims, stronger ablations, and an expanded discussion of metric limitations and architectural dependence, I would lean toward acceptance; without these, I would lean toward rejection.
