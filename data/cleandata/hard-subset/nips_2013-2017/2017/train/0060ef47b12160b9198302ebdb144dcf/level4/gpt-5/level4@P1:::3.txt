Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency method for black-box, differentiable image classifiers. Instead of iteratively optimizing a mask per image (as in Fong & Vedaldi [2] and related perturbation-based approaches), the authors train a masking network that, given an image and a target class, predicts in a single forward pass a saliency mask that both preserves class evidence in the retained region and destroys it when the salient region is removed. The network is based on a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder, leveraging multi-scale features; a feature filter guided by a learned class embedding performs coarse localization, and subsequent upsampling blocks refine the mask. The training objective enforces mask smoothness (TV), small area, high class probability on the preserved region (SSR-like), and low class probability when the salient region is removed (SDR-like). To mitigate adversarial artifacts from masking, the evidence-removal operator randomly uses either a strongly blurred image or a noisy constant image as the alternative background during training, and the mask is regularized for smoothness.

The paper also introduces a saliency metric s(a, p) = log(ã)/log(p), where a is the area fraction of the tightest rectangle enclosing the salient region and p is the classifier’s probability for the target class on the resized crop. This metric operationalizes the “smallest sufficient region” notion while discouraging adversarial artifacts by cropping rather than masking.

Experiments on ImageNet with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 (each treated as a black box and paired with its own masking model) show sharper, more interpretable maps than gradient/backprop methods and iterative perturbation, and improved weakly supervised localization accuracy. A CIFAR-10 experiment (with a FitNet black box) demonstrates the approach can be trained from scratch on low-resolution images. The method runs at over 100 images/s at 224×224 on a standard GPU.

Quality:
- The training objective and architecture are clearly described and the implementation details (e.g., randomization of the alternative image A, class-embedding gating, and fake-label training to enforce class conditioning) are sensible. The experiments support the claims about localization and map quality. I did not exhaustively verify technical details or proofs.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and easy to follow. The motivation for SSR/SDR and the discussion of introduced evidence/artifacts are helpful. Presentation is good overall.

Originality:
- The main novelty is learning a single-pass masking model that approximates the outcome of iterative, optimization-based perturbations while treating the classifier as a black box. The combined SSR+SDR objective and the proposed metric contribute to clarity and evaluation. The work is well situated among gradient-based, backprop-based, and perturbation-based saliency.

Significance:
- The approach is practical, potentially enabling real-time applications, and achieves strong weakly supervised localization on ImageNet. The new metric is a useful complement to localization. I remain a bit concerned about the real-time speed claim and applicability to higher-resolution, real-world images.

Pros:
- Single-pass, model-agnostic saliency with sharp, interpretable masks.
- Competitive or better weakly supervised localization on ImageNet.
- Thoughtful handling of adversarial artifacts and mask smoothness.
- Useful saliency metric aligned with SSR.
- Demonstrates generality across architectures and datasets (ImageNet and CIFAR-10).

Cons / concerns:
- Real-time performance characterization is limited to 224×224; scalability to larger, real-world resolutions is unclear.
- Behavior on small objects is not fully explored; 224×224 inputs may be limiting when the target occupies a tiny fraction of the image.
- It would help to report the new saliency metric for the full set of baselines used in the localization comparison.

Questions for the authors:
- Does the reported throughput (≈100 images/s) include image resizing? What is the runtime on larger inputs (e.g., 640×480 from an iPhone 6s), both per image and in images/s?
- Many examples show large, central objects. How does performance degrade when the requested class is small? Is 224×224 sufficient, or would larger inputs materially improve detection of small objects?
- In Table 3, can you report the proposed saliency metric for the same baselines used in Table 2, including Fong & Vedaldi [2], to make the comparisons directly commensurate?

Overall assessment:
This paper presents interesting technical results with a clear methodology, solid empirical evidence, and a useful new metric. I am positive overall but would like stronger validation of the real-time claim at higher resolutions and a more thorough analysis of small-object cases.
