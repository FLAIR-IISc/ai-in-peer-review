

This paper introduces a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method that generates high-quality saliency maps via a single forward pass, enabling real-time applications. The core innovation lies in training a masking model (based on a U-Net architecture with ResNet-50 features) to predict the tightest rectangular crop containing salient regions for a target class, avoiding iterative optimization. The authors propose a novel saliency metric (SSR-based) that evaluates both interpretability and minimal region size, and demonstrate superior performance over weakly supervised methods on ImageNet object localization (36.7% error vs. 34.3% for fully supervised VGG). Their approach generalizes across classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) and outperforms baselines in saliency interpretability (Table 3). The work extends prior methods like Fong & Vedaldi [2] and Zhou et al. [17] by eliminating iterative computation while addressing artifact generation via smoothness constraints (TV penalty) and randomized evidence removal.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The technical approach is sound, with comprehensive ablation studies (e.g., randomized evidence removal in §4.1) and thorough validation via the new saliency metric. The masking model’s architecture (U-Net with multi-scale features) effectively balances precision and speed, achieving 100+ images/sec on 224×224 inputs—demonstrably faster than iterative methods (e.g., [2], [17]).  
*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear definitions (SSR/SDR in §3), intuitive visualizations (Figs. 1–3, 5), and detailed methodological exposition (§4). The new saliency metric (Eq. 3) is well-motivated and directly addresses limitations of localization accuracy.  
*Originality*: The model-agnostic, single-pass framework significantly advances the state of the art beyond gradient-based methods [11,12,16] and iterative techniques [1,2,17]. The focus on *smoothness* to prevent adversarial artifacts (§3.1) and the novel metric (§3.2) are particularly novel contributions.  
*Significance*: The real-time capability unlocks new applications (e.g., video saliency for autonomous vehicles, §6). The method’s weakly supervised nature (no localization labels) and strong performance (Table 2) suggest high utility for practitioners. The CIFAR-10 results (§5.3) further validate generalizability beyond ImageNet.

**Weaknesses**  
*Significance*: The real-time claim requires qualification. The 100 images/sec rate applies *only* to 224×224 inputs (e.g., ImageNet preprocessing). For larger inputs (e.g., 640×480 iPhone 6s images), resizing overhead may drastically reduce throughput. The paper should clarify this limitation and provide timing benchmarks for non-standard resolutions.  
*Technical Gap*: The method’s performance on *small salient objects* (e.g., insects, text) is untested. Figure 5 shows masks for dominant objects; if the target class occupies <10% of the image, the 224×224 crop may not preserve sufficient detail (e.g., Fig. 2’s "seal" example). The authors should address this in experiments or discussion.  
*Comparison Gap*: Table 3 lacks metrics for competing methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi [2]) to contextualize their "on par with ground truth" claim. Including these would strengthen the metric’s validity (e.g., "Our model: 0.12; [2]: 0.25" for a sample class).

**Recommendation**  
This is a strong contribution to NIPS, offering a technically rigorous, original solution to a critical problem (real-time model interpretability). The method’s speed, accuracy, and generalizability are compelling, though the real-time claim needs refinement for non-ImageNet inputs. The minor gaps (small-object handling, [2] comparison) are addressable in revision. I recommend **acceptance** with the following clarifications:  
1. Specify processing times for non-224×224 inputs (e.g., 640×480).  
2. Discuss small-object limitations and potential scaling solutions.  
3. Add [2] metrics to Table 3 for direct comparison.
