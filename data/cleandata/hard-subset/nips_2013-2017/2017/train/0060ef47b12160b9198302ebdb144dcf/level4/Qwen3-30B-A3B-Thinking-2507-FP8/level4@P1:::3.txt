

This paper introduces a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method that generates high-quality saliency maps in a single forward pass, enabling real-time applications. The authors train a masking model to identify the tightest rectangular crop containing salient regions for a given class (as defined by the smallest sufficient region objective), using a U-Net architecture with a ResNet-50 backbone for multi-scale feature extraction. Crucially, they introduce a novel saliency metric based on crop area and classification confidence, which they demonstrate outperforms existing methods and approaches ground-truth bounding box quality. The approach is evaluated on ImageNet (using AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50 as black-box classifiers) and CIFAR-10, showing superior weakly supervised localization accuracy compared to baselines like the centrally placed box and iterative methods from [1, 2], while achieving 100+ saliency maps per second on standard hardware.

The work builds meaningfully on prior NIPS saliency research. It extends the iterative methods of [2, 17] by replacing slow optimization with a trainable model, directly addressing the speed limitations noted in [2] (which also uses image-space optimization but requires hundreds of iterations). The paper also advances beyond gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] by producing sharper, artifact-free masks while maintaining model agnosticism. The proposed metric (Section 3.2) addresses a key gap in the field—current benchmarks like localization accuracy fail to distinguish saliency from localization, as the authors correctly observe (e.g., a dog’s head suffices for recognition, but localization requires the whole body). This metric is a significant contribution, enabling more interpretable evaluation.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical soundness:** The method is rigorously evaluated across multiple datasets and classifiers. The ablation of the objective function (Section 4) and the careful handling of adversarial artifacts (Section 3.1) demonstrate strong technical care. The new metric (Eq. 3) is well-motivated and effectively isolates interpretability from localization.  
2. **Novelty and significance:** The model-based approach to saliency (vs. iterative) is a clear departure from [1, 2]. The real-time capability (100+ FPS) opens new applications like video saliency for autonomous systems (Section 6), which iterative methods cannot support. The CIFAR-10 experiment (Section 5.3) further validates generalizability beyond ImageNet.  
3. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear motivation (Section 1), precise definitions (SSR/SDR in Section 3), and a transparent architecture (Figure 4). The experimental protocol (Section 5.1–5.2) is reproducible.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Real-time applicability:** The claim of "real-time" performance requires clarification. The 100 FPS rate (Section 4.1) is measured on 224×224 images. For practical applications (e.g., smartphone cameras at 640×480), the impact of resizing and higher-resolution processing on speed is unaddressed. The authors should report latency for larger inputs.  
2. **Small-object sensitivity:** The method relies on a tight rectangular crop (Section 3.2), which may fail for small salient objects (e.g., insects or text). If the crop size exceeds the object (e.g., due to 224×224 constraints), the saliency map may include irrelevant background, undermining interpretability. The paper lacks analysis of this edge case.  
3. **Metric completeness:** Table 3 compares the proposed metric across methods but omits values for the iterative approach [2] (cited in Table 2). Including these would strengthen the claim that their metric consistently outperforms prior work.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:*  
- First method to achieve real-time saliency via a single forward pass (100+ FPS).  
- Novel metric directly measures interpretability (SSR), resolving a key limitation in the field.  
- Strong empirical results: 36.7% localization error on ImageNet (vs. 34.3% for supervised VGG), outperforming all weakly supervised baselines.  
- Generalizes to CIFAR-10 without pretraining, confirming robustness.  

*Con:*  
- Real-time claim lacks validation for non-224×224 inputs (critical for real-world deployment).  
- Limited analysis of small-object scenarios, where rectangular crops may be suboptimal.  
- Missing metric comparison for [2] in Table 3 (though Table 2 shows localization error).  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to model interpretability with clear technical innovation and strong empirical validation. The weaknesses are addressable in the camera-ready version (e.g., adding resizing benchmarks, small-object analysis) and do not undermine the core contribution. The method’s speed and novel metric are highly relevant to NIPS’ focus on practical, interpretable deep learning. The references adequately cover key NIPS work (e.g., [2, 17]), though citing more recent NIPS 2016–2017 saliency papers (e.g., [10]) would further contextualize the novelty.
