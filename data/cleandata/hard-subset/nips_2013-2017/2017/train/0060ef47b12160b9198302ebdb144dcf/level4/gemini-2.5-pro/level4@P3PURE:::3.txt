This paper presents an approach for generating image saliency masks, capable of processing one hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The approach trains a model to produce a mask corresponding to the tightest rectangular crop that contains the salient region for a specified class. This process is guided by a pre-trained, black-box classification network, such as AlexNet, GoogLeNet, or ResNet. The model architecture utilizes multi-scale feature maps from a backbone network like ResNet-50. Features from the coarsest scale are passed through a filter for initial localization, and subsequent upsampling blocks progressively refine the generated mask. Experiments on the ImageNet localization task demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms other weakly-supervised techniques.

The paper appears to be technically sound and includes sufficient references to related work. The authors provide good intuition and discussion regarding their design choices, and the overall presentation is clear.

Overall, this paper presents interesting technical results. However, I have some concerns regarding the real-time speed claim and the method's applicability to real-world images.

**Comments:**
1.  The claimed processing speed of 100 images per second is a key contribution. Does this measurement include the time required for image resizing? Furthermore, how does the runtime scale with larger, real-world image resolutions, such as 640x480?
2.  The experiments primarily feature images where the salient objects are relatively large. How does the method perform when the target object is small within the image? Is the 224x224 input resolution sufficient to localize small objects effectively, or does performance degrade?
3.  In Table 3, the authors present performance metrics for their method. For a more direct comparison, it would be helpful if the authors could also report these metrics for the other methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., [2]).
