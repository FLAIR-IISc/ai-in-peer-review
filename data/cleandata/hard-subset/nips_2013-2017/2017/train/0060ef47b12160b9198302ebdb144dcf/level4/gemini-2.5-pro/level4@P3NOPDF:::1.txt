This paper introduces a novel method for generating saliency masks for image classifiers. The approach utilizes a separate neural network to learn a mask that highlights regions of an image deemed important for classification. A key advantage of this method is its computational efficiency, allowing for rapid generation of saliency maps.

Overall, I find this to be a borderline paper with several interesting ideas but also significant limitations. The core concept of learning a model to explain another model is compelling and represents a valuable direction for research in interpretability. The proposed method's speed is also a notable strength. However, several concerns regarding the evaluation methodology and the general applicability of the approach prevent a more enthusiastic recommendation.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Indirect and Potentially Flawed Evaluation of Saliency:** My primary concern lies with the indirect nature of the saliency evaluation and the potential flaws in the proposed metrics. The paper relies on two main methods, both of which have significant caveats that are not adequately discussed.
    *   **Weakly Supervised Localization:** The use of object localization as a proxy for saliency quality is questionable. While intuitive, it assumes that the salient region for a classifier perfectly aligns with the object's bounding box. This may not hold true in cases where contextual information outside the object is critical for correct classification. This is a significant limitation when using localization as a quality metric for saliency, and this caveat should be explicitly discussed.
    *   **Proposed Saliency Metric (Crop and Rescale):** The proposed metric, which involves cropping the estimated salient region and re-classifying it, has limitations that could bias the results. The process introduces two potential artifacts:
        1.  Distorting the aspect ratio of the crop during rescaling may negatively impact the performance of the classifier being evaluated.
        2.  The metric inherently favors smaller salient regions, as these are magnified significantly during rescaling. This may push the object into a scale regime for which the classifier is not robust, confounding the evaluation. While convolutional networks possess some translation invariance, scale invariance must be learned and is often limited. This strong dependence on the classifier's scale-invariance is a major factor that could influence this metric's outcome.

2.  **Limited Generalizability and the "Black-Box" Claim:** A significant consequence of the reliance on the crop-and-rescale metric is that the proposed method's validity depends heavily on the classifier possessing strong scale and aspect-ratio invariance. This severely limits the method's applicability and contradicts the paper's claim of being a general "black-box" technique. Specifically:
    *   The method may be unsuitable for evaluating models during their training phase before such invariances have been robustly learned.
    *   It cannot be reliably applied to models that do not exhibit strong scale invariance, which limits its utility.
    *   The applicability to other domains where such invariances are less common (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs) is questionable.
    *   Crucially, because it requires specific properties from the model being explained, it is not a truly "black-box" method.

3.  **Ambiguity of What is Being Visualized:** The paper does not adequately investigate the influence of the masking network's architecture on the resulting saliency maps. How sensitive are the results to this choice? This leads to a more fundamental ambiguity: it is unclear whether the learned masks represent the saliency of the original classifier, or if they primarily reflect the inductive biases of the masking network itself. Without ablation studies on the masking architecture, it is difficult to disentangle these two effects and have confidence that we are explaining the intended model.

**Minor Comments and Questions**

*   For Table 1, please clarify if the reported results are averaged over all classes or calculated only for the ground-truth class.
*   For reproducibility, the specific variant of LRP (e.g., epsilon-LRP, alpha-beta-LRP) and the corresponding parameter settings used for the comparison should be explicitly stated in the paper.

**Recommendation**

In its current form, the paper's claims of generality are not fully supported due to the limitations of its evaluation methodology and the unexamined dependencies on both the classifier's properties and the masking network's architecture. However, the core idea is promising. I would be willing to raise my score if the authors were to thoroughly discuss these limitations in the manuscript, particularly the caveats surrounding the evaluation metrics and the conditions under which their method is applicable (i.e., for scale-invariant classifiers). Acknowledging these issues would make for a more balanced and scientifically sound contribution. Without such a discussion, I believe the paper is not ready for publication.
