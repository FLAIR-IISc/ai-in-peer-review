The paper proposes an approach to learning saliency masks using a neural network, demonstrating its efficiency by processing multiple images per second. In my assessment, the paper is borderline, and I would not object to either rejection or acceptance. I appreciate the concept of learning to explain a model, and the paper presents some valuable ideas.

Although there are no obvious errors, there are clear limitations that need to be addressed. One major concern is that saliency is only indirectly measured, either through weakly supervised localization or the proposed saliency metric, both of which have limitations that should be discussed in the paper.

Weakly supervised localization is not a perfect measure, as it may not correlate with saliency quality when the context in which an object appears is essential to determine its class. While the results on weakly supervised localization are interesting, they should be interpreted with caution when used as a quality metric for saliency.

The proposed saliency metric also has its limitations. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size, maintaining the original aspect ratio. This process may introduce two artifacts: the change in aspect ratio may impact classification performance, and the preference for small salient regions may lead to suboptimal scaling for re-classification. Since convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are generally translation-invariant but require learning scale invariance, there may be limits to this approach.

Another crucial aspect that is not discussed is the dependence of the masking model on the architecture used for learning the masks. Did the authors experiment with different architectures and analyze their influence on the results?

Minor comments include:

* Clarify whether the results in Table 1 are obtained for all classes or only for the correct class.
* Specify the LRP variant and parameter setting used for comparison, as there are multiple variants with parameters (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta).

Post-rebuttal edit:

The fact that the saliency metric's performance relies heavily on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier significantly limits the applicability of the proposed method. This approach can only be applied to networks with this invariance, which has important consequences:

* The method cannot be used for models during the training phase or for models lacking this invariance.
* Its applicability is limited to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs).
* The method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers, as claimed in the title.

Furthermore, the response suggests a strong dependence on the masking network. As a result, it is unclear whether the approach visualizes the saliency of the U-network or the masking network.

If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, I believe it is balanced enough for publication. Otherwise, it should not be published.
