- Introduces a real-time saliency method: train a U-Net masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass.  
- Produces sharp, artifact-free saliency maps that generalize to unseen images.  
- Proposes a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) measuring smallest crop that still allows confident classification.  
- Demonstrates competitive ImageNet localization (36.7% error with ResNet-50) and strong CIFAR-10 results, outperforming weakly supervised baselines.  
- Addresses computational limitations of prior SSR and iterative methods by offering speed and quality improvements.  
- Claims superiority over gradient-based and iterative methods in explanation quality and runtime.  
- Technical approach is sound, practically useful, and experimentally validated.  
- Real-time performance (100+ masks/sec) enables applications like video saliency.  
- U-Net with ResNet-50 backbone is an appropriate architecture; training strategy prevents adversarial artifacts.  
- New metric arguably captures interpretability better than standard localization accuracy and matches ground-truth bounding-box scores.  
- Major evaluation limitations: metric requires cropping and resizing, causing aspect-ratio and scale issues that affect classification.  
- Metric heavily depends on classifier scale invariance, undermining the “model-agnostic/any differentiable classifier” claim.  
- Paper insufficiently analyzes how masking model architecture influences results or whether maps reflect classifier behavior vs. masker bias.  
- Weakly supervised localization results are confounded when context (not object) drives classification (e.g., polar bear vs. snow).  
- Writing and reproducibility are strong, but novelty is somewhat incremental given related work (Fong & Vedaldi).  
- Significance is moderate; useful for certain applications but limited in broader applicability.  
- Recommendation: requires substantial revision to discuss scale-invariance dependency, masker/classifier relationship, and localization limitations; current claims are overstated.
