This paper introduces a neural network-based approach for learning saliency masks, demonstrating promising speed – processing multiple images per second. Overall, the work is on the borderline of acceptance; I see merit in the core concept of learning explanations for model behavior and find the ideas presented to be sound. While I didn’t identify any critical errors, the paper does have noticeable limitations.

A primary concern is the indirect measurement of saliency. Both the employed weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric suffer from inherent limitations that should be more thoroughly addressed. Specifically, weakly supervised localization isn’t a perfect proxy for saliency, as object context crucial for classification isn’t necessarily reflected in localization accuracy. While the results utilizing this method are interesting, caution is warranted when interpreting them as a definitive measure of saliency quality. 

The proposed saliency metric also introduces potential artifacts due to its implementation. The cropping and rescaling process – expanding salient regions to the original image size while maintaining aspect ratio – could negatively impact classification performance. The aspect ratio change may affect classification, and the significant upscaling of smaller salient regions might present a non-ideal scale for the classifier (despite the inherent translation invariance of CNNs, scaling invariance is a learned property with potential limits).

The paper lacks discussion regarding the masking model’s sensitivity to network architecture. Exploring different architectures and their impact on results would strengthen the work. 

I have a few minor points: please clarify whether the results in Table 1 are reported for all classes or only the correct class. Additionally, please specify the exact LRP variant and parameter settings used for comparison, as numerous options exist with varying parameters (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta).

**Post-Rebuttal:** The strong dependence of the saliency metric’s effectiveness on the classifier’s scale invariance is a significant constraint, limiting the method's broader applicability. Specifically, it restricts use to networks *possessing* this invariance, precluding its application during model training or to models lacking it. This also limits applicability to domains where scale invariance isn’t a given (e.g., CNNs used in spectrogram analysis). Furthermore, the claim of generalizability to black-box classifiers, as stated in the title, is unsupported. The authors’ response also suggests a substantial dependence on the masking network itself, raising the question of whether the visualizations represent the saliency of the U-network or the masking network. 

If these limitations and concerns are adequately discussed within the revised manuscript, I believe the paper could be considered balanced and suitable for publication. However, without such discussion, I would not support its acceptance.
