This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency mask generation, leveraging a trainable masking model to identify the most relevant regions of an image for a given class, as perceived by a black box classifier. The authors demonstrate that their method can process over a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU, making it suitable for real-time applications.

The paper provides a thorough discussion of related work, including various saliency detection methods and their limitations. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric, which assesses the quality of the produced saliency maps by evaluating the classifier's ability to recognize the object from the preserved region.

The proposed approach is based on a U-Net architecture, which utilizes feature maps from multiple resolutions to produce sharp and precise saliency masks. The authors employ a pre-trained ResNet-50 model as the encoder and modify the architecture to accommodate the specific requirements of their task.

The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques at the ImageNet localisation task, achieving a localisation error of 36.7% for the ResNet-50 black box classifier. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the CIFAR-10 dataset, where the masking model is trained from scratch without relying on a pre-trained model.

The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear and concise. The authors provide a detailed description of their approach, including the architecture, training process, and experimental setup. The discussion of related work and the introduction of a new saliency metric are also noteworthy.

However, there are some concerns regarding the real-time speed claim and its applications to real-world images. Specifically, the processing time for 100 images per second may not include the image resizing operation, and the performance on larger images, such as 640x480 images taken from an iPhone 6s, is unclear.

Additionally, the salient objects in the paper are relatively large, and it is uncertain how the approach would perform if the requested class object is small in the images. The use of 224x224 images may not be sufficient in such cases.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and efficient approach to image saliency mask generation.
* The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on the ImageNet localisation task.
* The authors introduce a new saliency metric, which provides a more comprehensive evaluation of the produced saliency maps.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The real-time speed claim may not be entirely accurate, and the performance on larger images is unclear.
* The approach may not be effective for small objects in images.
* The comparison with other works is not comprehensive, and additional metrics should be included.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper, but request the authors to address the concerns regarding the real-time speed claim and the performance on larger images.
* Suggest the inclusion of additional metrics for a more comprehensive comparison with other works.
* Encourage the authors to explore the application of their approach to small objects in images.
