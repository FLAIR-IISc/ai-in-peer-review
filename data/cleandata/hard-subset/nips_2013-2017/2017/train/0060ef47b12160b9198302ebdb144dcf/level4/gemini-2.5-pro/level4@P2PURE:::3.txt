This paper proposes an approach for generating image saliency masks that can process one hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The method involves training a masking model to find the tightest rectangular crop containing the salient region of a target class, as identified by a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet). The model architecture utilizes multi-scale image feature maps, such as those from ResNet-50. Features from the final scale are passed through a filter for initial localization, while subsequent upsampling blocks refine the generated mask. Experiments on the ImageNet localization task demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques.

The paper appears to cite sufficient and relevant related work.
The technical approach appears to be sound.
The paper presents clear intuition and discussion regarding its design choices.
The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

Overall, this paper presents interesting technical results. However, I have some concerns regarding the real-time speed claim and the model's applicability to more general real-world scenarios.

**Comments:**
*   The claimed processing speed of 100 images/second is impressive. Does this measurement include the initial image resizing operation? Furthermore, what is the expected inference time for larger, non-square images, such as 640x480?
*   The experiments seem to focus on images where the salient object is relatively large. How does the model perform when the target object is small? Is the 224x224 input resolution sufficient to localize small objects effectively?
