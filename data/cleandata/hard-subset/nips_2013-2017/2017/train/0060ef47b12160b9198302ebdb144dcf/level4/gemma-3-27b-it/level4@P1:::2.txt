This paper introduces a neural network to predict the regions of an image that are important for another neural network’s object categorization decisions. The paper is well-written and it’s compelling that the experiments demonstrate strong performance compared to previous work. The results are presented on challenging datasets using state-of-the-art neural networks.

I suggest briefly motivating the need for real-time efficiency with specific application examples. Additionally, I would have appreciated some analysis of the learned salient regions – for instance, exploring whether certain object categories exploit biases in the background to facilitate recognition.

The authors address the growing concern that high-performing image classifiers can exhibit unexpected and unintuitive behavior, even achieving perfect accuracy while relying on spurious correlations (e.g., identifying “polar bears” based solely on the presence of snow [6]). To address this, they propose a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method. Existing iterative methods for identifying salient regions are computationally expensive. Instead, the authors train a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, enabling real-time applications.

The core idea is to learn a mask that, when applied to the input image, reveals the regions most crucial for the classifier's decision. The proposed approach differs from prior work in its speed and efficiency. Gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] are fast but produce lower-quality explanations. Iterative methods [17, 1, 2] achieve better results but are too slow for real-time use. Fong and Vedaldi [2] offer a model-agnostic approach but still rely on iterative optimization. The authors present a trainable model (using a U-Net architecture [8] with a ResNet-50 [3] encoder) to directly predict saliency masks, achieving a favorable balance between speed and accuracy. They also introduce a novel saliency metric based on the “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) and “smallest destroying region” (SDR) concepts, aiming to find regions that are both informative and minimal.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed method offers a significant speed advantage over existing iterative saliency detection techniques.
*   **Performance:** The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet demonstrate competitive performance, achieving state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach and experimental setup.
*   **New Metric:** The introduction of a new saliency metric based on SSR and SDR is a valuable contribution.
*   **Real-time capability:** The single-pass nature of the model enables real-time saliency detection, opening up possibilities for dynamic applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivation for Real-time Efficiency:** While the authors mention the potential for real-time applications, the paper would benefit from more concrete examples and a stronger justification for the focus on speed.
*   **Analysis of Learned Masks:** A deeper analysis of the learned saliency masks could provide more insights into the classifier's behavior and potential biases. Investigating whether the model identifies expected features or exploits spurious correlations would be insightful.
*   **Objective Function Complexity:** The objective function (Equation 4) contains several terms and hyperparameters. While the authors provide justification for each term, a more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of each component would be beneficial.
*   **Evidence Removal:** While the authors address the issue of introducing introduced evidence, the discussion is somewhat dense and might benefit from more illustrative examples.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and effective approach to fast saliency detection.
*   The experimental results demonstrate strong performance on challenging datasets.
*   The introduced saliency metric is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The work has the potential to enable new applications in areas such as video understanding and explainable AI.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The motivation for real-time efficiency could be stronger.
*   A more in-depth analysis of the learned saliency masks would be beneficial.
*   The complexity of the objective function requires further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI. The proposed method is novel, efficient, and performs well in practice. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
