- Proposes a neural-network method for learning saliency masks that is computationally efficient (multiple images/sec).
- Reviewer views the paper as borderline—acceptance or rejection would both be defensible.
- The idea of learning to explain models is valuable and the paper contributes useful ideas.
- No obvious errors, but several important limitations exist.
- Saliency evaluation is indirect, relying on weakly supervised localization or a proposed saliency metric, both of which have inherent limitations.
- Weakly supervised localization can be misleading when object context matters, so it may not correlate with true saliency quality.
- The proposed saliency metric has flaws: cropping and rescaling (with aspect ratio changes) can introduce artifacts that affect classification.
- The metric favours small regions that are enlarged for re-classification, exposing limits due to CNNs’ imperfect scale invariance.
- The paper does not investigate how mask-learning performance depends on the architecture of the masking model.
- Minor clarifications needed: whether Table 1 results are for all classes or only the correct class, and which LRP variant/parameters were used.
- After rebuttal, the metric’s usefulness appears highly dependent on the classifier’s quality and scale invariance, limiting applicability.
- The method cannot be applied during model training or to models lacking scale invariance, and may not generalize across domains (contradicting claims of black-box generality).
- There is a strong dependence on the masking network, raising uncertainty about whether the U-network’s or masking network’s saliency is being visualized.
- If the paper thoroughly discusses these dependencies and limitations it may be acceptable; otherwise publication is not recommended.
