The paper proposes an approach to learn saliency masks using a neural network, which can process multiple images per second, making it a fast method. The concept of learning to explain a model is intriguing, and the paper presents some good ideas. However, there are clear limitations that need to be addressed.

One major limitation is that the saliency is only indirectly measured, either through weakly supervised localization or by the proposed saliency metric. Both of these methods have limitations that should be discussed in the paper. Weakly supervised localization is not a perfect measure, as the context in which an object appears can be essential to determine its class, and object localization may not necessarily correlate with saliency quality. The results on weakly supervised localization are interesting, but there is a big caveat when using them as a quality metric for saliency.

The proposed saliency metric also has limitations. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size, with the original aspect ratio, which could introduce two artifacts. First, the change of aspect ratio might impact how well it can be classified. Second, the proposed metric prefers a small salient region, which, when blown up heavily for re-classification, might not be presented to the classifier at an ideal scale. While convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are generally translation-invariant, scaling invariance must be learned, and there are probably limits to this.

Furthermore, the paper does not discuss how much the masking model depends on the architecture for learning the masks. Did the authors experiment with different architectures and how did this influence the results? This is an essential aspect to consider, as it affects the generalizability of the proposed method.

Minor comments include specifying whether the results in Table 1 are obtained for all classes or only for the correct class, and providing details on the LRP variant and parameter setting used for comparison.

In the rebuttal, the authors acknowledge that the saliency metric's performance depends on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier, which strongly limits the applicability of the proposed method. This has important consequences, including the inability to use the method for models during the training phase or for models that do not exhibit this invariance. Additionally, the method's applicability to other domains, such as spectrogram analysis with CNNs, is limited. The method is also not generally applicable to black-box classifiers as claimed in the title.

The rebuttal also hints at a strong dependence on the masking network, which raises concerns about whether the method is visualizing the saliency of the U-network or the masking network. If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, it would provide a more balanced view of the proposed method.
