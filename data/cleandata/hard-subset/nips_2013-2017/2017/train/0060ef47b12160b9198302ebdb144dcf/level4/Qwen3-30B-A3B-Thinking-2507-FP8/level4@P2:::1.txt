

This paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method that trains a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, enabling real-time application. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept and demonstrate their approach on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets. The method differs from prior work such as gradient-based approaches [11, 12, 16], iterative patch removal [17], and optimization-based methods [1, 2] by learning to predict saliency maps rather than computing them iteratively for each input.

Strengths: The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear advantages over existing methods. The masking model achieves 100+ saliency masks per second, making it suitable for real-time applications like video saliency detection. The new saliency metric provides a more interpretable evaluation than standard localization accuracy, and the method outperforms existing weakly supervised techniques on ImageNet object localization (36.7% error rate vs. 34.3% for fully supervised VGG). The architecture, based on a U-Net with ResNet-50 features, is well-designed for multi-scale processing, and the training methodology with random evidence removal effectively prevents adversarial artifacts.

Weaknesses: The evaluation metrics have significant limitations that undermine the paper's claims. First, the weakly supervised localization metric assumes that salient regions correspond to object locations, but context may be essential for classification (e.g., "polar bear" class may rely on snow presence), making localization accuracy an imperfect proxy for saliency quality. Second, the proposed saliency metric involves cropping and rescaling the salient region, which introduces two artifacts: (1) aspect ratio changes may impact classifier performance, and (2) small salient regions are heavily upscaled before classification, potentially presenting objects at non-optimal scales to the classifier. Since convolutional networks' scale invariance must be learned and has limits, this metric's validity depends on the classifier's specific properties.

The paper also fails to address how the masking model's performance depends on the classifier architecture. While the authors test on AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50, they don't explore whether different architectures would require different masking models or how the masking model's architecture affects results. This is particularly concerning because the post-rebuttal clarification reveals the method's strong dependence on the classifier's scale invariance properties, which contradicts the "black box" claim in the title.

Furthermore, the paper doesn't clarify whether the saliency maps reflect the original classifier's behavior or the masking network's biases. Given the masking model's strong dependence on the classifier architecture, it's unclear whether the authors are visualizing the saliency of the original classifier or the masking network itself.

Pro-acceptance arguments: The paper introduces a novel approach to fast saliency detection with clear technical merit. The method is well-validated on standard benchmarks, the new metric provides a useful evaluation framework, and the architecture is well-designed. The real-time capability opens new application possibilities for saliency detection.

Con-acceptance arguments: The evaluation metrics have fundamental limitations that reduce confidence in the claimed advantages. The method's applicability is restricted to classifiers with scale invariance, which limits its "black box" nature. The paper doesn't sufficiently address the masking model's dependence on classifier architecture or clarify whether the saliency maps reflect the original classifier's behavior. These limitations significantly weaken the paper's contribution.

The paper would be substantially stronger with a more thorough discussion of the metric limitations and their implications for the method's applicability. The authors should clarify how the masking model's performance depends on the classifier architecture and explicitly state the limitations of the method regarding classifier scale invariance.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound approach with promising real-time capabilities, but the limitations in evaluation metrics and the method's dependence on classifier properties undermine the claimed generality. While the concept of learning to explain models is valuable, the paper's specific claims are not fully supported by the evaluation. I believe the paper would benefit from more careful discussion of these limitations before publication.
