- Proposes a neural-network method for learning saliency masks with real-time performance.  
- Idea is compelling but paper has significant limitations and insufficient discussion; borderline accept/reject.  
- Primary concern: saliency is measured indirectly; both weakly supervised localization and the proposed metric are limited.  
- Weakly supervised localization is a problematic saliency metric because context-reliant classes break the localization–saliency correlation.  
- The proposed saliency metric creates artifacts by cropping and rescaling estimated regions, undermining validity.  
- Aspect-ratio distortion from rescaling can alter spatial relationships critical for classification.  
- Scale distortion: metric favors small regions and upscaling exposes classifier scale-sensitivity, invalidating the metric.  
- No analysis of how masking-model performance depends on underlying classifier architecture; experiments across architectures are missing.  
- Minor: clarify if Table 1 reports results over all classes or only correctly classified examples.  
- Minor: specify which LRP variant and parameter settings (epsilon, alpha-beta, etc.) were used.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors confirm metric validity heavily depends on classifier scale invariance, severely limiting applicability.  
- Method cannot be used during training, for models lacking scale invariance, or in other domains (e.g., spectrograms); contradicts "black-box" applicability claim.  
- Response indicates strong dependence on masking-network architecture, raising whether visualizations reflect the original model or the masker.  
- Conclusion: paper should be rejected unless it thoroughly addresses classifier scale-invariance dependency, masking-architecture dependence, and applicability constraints.
