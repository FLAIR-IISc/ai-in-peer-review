This paper presents an image saliency mask generation approach that can process a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. Their approach trains a masking model that finds the tightest rectangular crop that contains the entire salient region of a particular requested class by a black box classifier, such as Alexnet, GoogleNet, and ResNet. Their model architecture requires image feature maps, such as those by ResNet-50, over different scales. The final scale feature will be passed through a feature filter that performs the initial localisation, while the following upsampling blocks fine-tune the produced masks. Experiment shows that their method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques at the ImageNet localisation task.

The paper appears to have sufficient references and related works, although a thorough check has not been performed. The technical correctness of the paper also seems to be sound, although a complete check has not been performed. The authors present a number of intuitions and discussions on how they design their approach, which is helpful for understanding their methodology. The presentation of the paper is also good.

However, there are some concerns regarding the real-time speed claim and applications to real-world images. Specifically, it is unclear whether the processing time for 100 images per second includes the image resizing operation. If so, what is the running time for other larger images, such as 640 X 480 images taken from iPhone 6s? Additionally, the salient objects in this paper are quite large, and it is unclear how the method would perform if the requested class object is small in the images. Would 224x224 images be sufficient for such cases?

In Table 3, it would be helpful to include corresponding metrics for other works in Table 2, such as Feed [2], for a more comprehensive comparison.
