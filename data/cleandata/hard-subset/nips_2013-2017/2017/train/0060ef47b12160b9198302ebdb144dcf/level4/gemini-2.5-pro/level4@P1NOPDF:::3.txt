This paper presents an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing one hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The method involves training a masking model to find the tightest rectangular crop that contains the entire salient region for a given class, which is identified by a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet). The model architecture uses multi-scale image feature maps, such as those from a ResNet-50 backbone. Features from the final scale are passed through a filter for initial localization, while subsequent upsampling blocks refine the generated masks. Experiments demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms other weakly-supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task.

The paper appears to cite sufficient and relevant related work. The technical approach seems sound. The authors provide good intuition and discussion regarding their design choices, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented.

Overall, this paper presents interesting technical results. However, I have some concerns about the real-time speed claim and the method's applicability to real-world images.

**Comments:**

*   Regarding the claim of processing 100 images per second: Does this runtime include the initial image resizing operation? Furthermore, how does the performance scale to larger, higher-resolution images, such as 640x480 images captured by a typical smartphone?
*   The salient objects in the experiments appear to be relatively large within the image frame. How does the method perform when the target object is small? Is the 224x224 input resolution sufficient to localize small objects effectively?
*   Table 3 presents performance metrics for the proposed method. For a more direct comparison, could the authors provide corresponding metrics for the other methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., [2])?
