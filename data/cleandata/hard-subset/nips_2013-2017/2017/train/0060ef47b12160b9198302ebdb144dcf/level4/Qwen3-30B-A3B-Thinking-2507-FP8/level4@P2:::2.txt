

This paper introduces a novel approach for real-time saliency detection in image classifiers, training a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass rather than using iterative methods. The authors address a critical gap in the field by developing a model-agnostic technique that produces sharp, artifact-free saliency maps while achieving state-of-the-art performance on weakly supervised object localization tasks. The work builds upon recent advances in saliency detection (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017; Zhou et al., 2014) but significantly improves upon them by eliminating the computational bottleneck of iterative methods while enhancing output quality.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through comprehensive experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets using multiple state-of-the-art classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50). The authors introduce a new saliency metric that directly evaluates the interpretability of produced masks by measuring the smallest rectangular crop that preserves classification confidence, which is particularly valuable as it addresses the distinction between saliency and localization. This metric shows that their method produces saliency maps with interpretability comparable to ground truth bounding boxes, a significant achievement.

The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-organized explanations of the method, the objective function, and the masking model architecture. The U-Net-based masking model is clearly described, and the training process is thoroughly explained with appropriate technical details. The figures effectively illustrate the saliency maps, the masking model architecture, and the performance comparisons.

The originality of this work is substantial. While previous approaches to saliency detection have focused on iterative optimization (Zhou et al., 2014; Fong & Vedaldi, 2017) or gradient-based methods (Simonyan et al., 2013), this paper presents the first method that trains a model to generate saliency maps in real-time without iterative optimization. The authors also make a novel contribution with their saliency metric that better captures the quality of the produced explanations than standard localization accuracy.

The significance of this work is high. Real-time saliency detection enables new applications such as video saliency analysis, which the authors mention as future work. Their method achieves strong performance (36.7% localization error) on ImageNet without requiring any localization training data, outperforming other weakly supervised methods by a significant margin. The fact that the masking model can be trained from scratch without pre-trained features (as demonstrated on CIFAR-10) further enhances the practical applicability of the approach.

Strengths:
- Demonstrates a clear technical contribution to the field of model interpretability
- Provides strong experimental validation on challenging benchmarks
- Introduces a novel metric that better evaluates saliency quality
- Achieves real-time performance (100+ saliency masks per second)
- Works with any differentiable classifier without requiring access to internal layers
- The masking model architecture is well-designed and efficient

Weaknesses:
- The motivation for real-time applications could be strengthened. While the paper mentions video applications, it would benefit from more specific examples of real-world scenarios where sub-second latency is critical (e.g., autonomous driving systems where immediate interpretation of classifier decisions is needed).
- The analysis of what the saliency maps reveal about classifier biases is limited. The paper references the potential for classifiers to exploit background features (citing [6]), but does not provide concrete examples of which object categories exhibit such biases or how the proposed method reveals these patterns. A deeper analysis of the learned saliency regions would strengthen the paper's contribution to understanding classifier behavior.

The authors should consider expanding the discussion of real-time applications to better justify the significance of their speed advantage. Additionally, adding a subsection analyzing specific patterns in the saliency maps (e.g., which classes consistently rely on background features) would provide valuable insights into classifier behavior that would enhance the paper's contribution to the field.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of model interpretability with clear technical merit, practical significance, and novel methodological approaches. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful advancement over previous work in the area. While the proposed improvements would further strengthen the paper, they do not detract from the quality of the core contribution.

Pro: Novel method for real-time saliency detection with strong performance
Con: Limited analysis of classifier biases revealed by saliency maps

Pro: New saliency metric that better evaluates interpretability
Con: Motivation for real-time applications could be more specific

Pro: Works with any differentiable classifier without requiring internal access
Con: Some details about the masking model architecture could be more thoroughly explained

Pro: Demonstrates practical utility through real-time performance
Con: Comparison with more recent saliency methods would be beneficial

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of model interpretability that aligns well with NIPS's focus on high-quality scientific contributions. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results demonstrate clear significance for both research and practical applications. I recommend acceptance.
