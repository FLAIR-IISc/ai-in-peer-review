- Presents a fast neural method for learning saliency masks; reviewer is neutral (borderline accept/reject).
- Supportive of the idea and several contributions, but notes clear limitations without obvious mistakes.
- Saliency is only evaluated indirectly via weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric—both need discussion of limitations.
- Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy because contextual cues can decouple localization from saliency quality.
- The proposed saliency metric is problematic: cropping and rescaling imposes original aspect ratio, causing artifacts and distorting classification.
- The metric biases toward small salient regions, but heavy upsampling changes object scale and harms reclassification due to limited convnet scale invariance.
- The paper omits discussion/experiments on how masking-model architecture affects mask learning; architectural dependence should be evaluated.
- Clarify whether Table 1 results are computed over all classes or only the ground-truth class.
- Specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparison (e.g., epsilon, alpha–beta).
- Post-rebuttal: metric effectiveness depends on classifier quality and scale invariance, substantially limiting applicability.
- Consequences: method cannot be used during training, fails for models without required invariance, is limited across domains (e.g., spectrograms), and is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers as claimed.
- The response indicates strong dependence on the masking network, making it unclear whether visualizations reflect the target model’s saliency or the masking network’s behavior.
- Recommendation: paper should explicitly discuss these effects and limitations for acceptance; otherwise it should not be published.
