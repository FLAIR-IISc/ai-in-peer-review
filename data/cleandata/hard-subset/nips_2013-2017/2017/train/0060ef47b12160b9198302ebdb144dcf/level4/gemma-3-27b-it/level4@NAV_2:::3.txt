This paper introduces a fast image saliency detection method applicable to any differentiable image classifier. The core idea is to train a masking model to predict salient regions by manipulating classifier scores through masking input image parts. This allows for single-pass saliency detection, enabling real-time applications. The authors demonstrate their approach on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, achieving interpretable, sharp saliency maps and outperforming other weakly supervised methods on object localization. They also propose a new metric for evaluating saliency.

The work builds upon existing gradient-based saliency methods [11, 12, 16], which are fast but often produce low-quality explanations. It addresses the limitations of iterative methods like those in [17, 1, 2] that, while producing high-quality maps, are computationally expensive. Notably, the authors acknowledge and build upon the recent work of Fong and Vedaldi [2], highlighting a similar "black box" approach but differing in their use of a learned masking model instead of iterative optimization. The paper appropriately references relevant literature and positions itself within the existing body of work.

The strengths of this paper are significant. Technically, the approach appears sound, combining a U-Net architecture [8] with a novel loss function incorporating smoothness, region size, and classification probability. The experiments are well-designed, utilizing established datasets (CIFAR-10, ImageNet) and comparing against relevant baselines. The introduction of a new saliency metric is a valuable contribution, offering a more nuanced evaluation than simple localization accuracy. The reported speed – 100 images/second – is compelling and crucial for real-time applications. The ablation study on CIFAR-10 further demonstrates the robustness of the method. The discussion of introduced evidence and adversarial artifacts is insightful and the proposed solutions, such as blurring or adding noise, are sensible.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the authors claim real-time performance, it’s unclear if this includes the cost of any pre- or post-processing steps, like image resizing. The experiments focus on relatively clean datasets; performance on more complex, real-world images with cluttered backgrounds or multiple objects might be a concern. The effect of classifier choice on the masking model's performance should also be considered in more detail. Although the localization results are strong, the saliency metric – while innovative – needs further justification; its correlation with human perception of saliency could be explored. It is also unclear whether the performance on ImageNet holds for various object classes. Some classes might be more easily localized than others.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to fast, model-agnostic saliency detection.
*   Strong experimental results on standard benchmarks.
*   Introduction of a new and thoughtful saliency metric.
*   Clear and well-written paper with a comprehensive related work section.
*   Potential for real-time applications, such as video saliency.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Real-time performance claim requires clarification regarding pre/post-processing costs.
*   Performance on complex, real-world images needs to be demonstrated.
*   The correlation between the proposed saliency metric and human perception is unexplored.
*   Limited investigation of the impact of different classifier architectures.

**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of explainable AI. While there are some minor concerns, the strengths of the work – particularly the speed, accuracy, and novelty of the approach – outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding real-time performance and the saliency metric’s correlation with human perception.
