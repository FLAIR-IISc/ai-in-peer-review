This paper proposes a neural network-based approach to learn saliency masks. The method is fast, capable of processing multiple images per second.

I find this paper to be borderline. The core concept of learning to explain a model is promising, and the paper contains several good ideas. While there are no obvious technical errors, the work has clear limitations that are not adequately discussed. I would not object to either acceptance or rejection.

**Major Weaknesses and Concerns**

The primary weakness is that saliency is only measured indirectly, through two methods that both have significant limitations. These limitations should be discussed in the paper for the results to be interpreted correctly.

1.  **Weakly Supervised Localization as a Saliency Metric:** Using object localization as a proxy for saliency quality is problematic. The context in which an object appears can be essential for its classification. In such cases, a good saliency map should highlight this context, meaning high-quality saliency may not correlate directly with precise object localization. The results on weakly supervised localization are interesting, but this represents a major caveat when using it as a quality metric for saliency.

2.  **Flaws in the Proposed Saliency Metric:** The proposed metric involves cropping the estimated salient region, rescaling it to the original image size, and then re-running classification. This process introduces two potential artifacts:
    *   **Aspect Ratio Distortion:** Rescaling the cropped region to match the original image's aspect ratio can distort the object, which may negatively impact classification performance.
    *   **Scale Invariance Bias:** The metric inherently prefers smaller salient regions, as they are heavily magnified to fill the frame for re-classification. The assumption that the classifier is invariant to such drastic changes in scale is a strong one. While ConvNets possess some translation invariance, scale invariance is a learned property with practical limits.

3.  **Limited Applicability and Contradiction of the "Black-Box" Claim:** The method's strong dependence on the classifier's scale and deformation invariance severely limits its applicability and undermines some of the paper's central claims.
    *   It cannot be reliably used for models during their training phase or for any model that does not exhibit strong invariance to these transformations.
    *   This limits its transferability to other domains (e.g., analyzing spectrograms with CNNs) where such invariances may not hold.
    *   Crucially, this requirement contradicts the claim in the title that the method is generally applicable to "black-box" classifiers, as it presupposes specific, non-guaranteed properties of the model being explained.

4.  **Dependence on the Masking Network Architecture:** The paper fails to discuss how the choice of architecture for the masking network influences the resulting saliency maps. The rebuttal hints at a strong dependence on this choice. This raises a critical question: are we visualizing the saliency of the target classifier, or are we primarily visualizing the learned biases of the masking network itself? Without an ablation study on the masking architecture, the source of the generated explanations remains ambiguous.

**Conclusion**

If the authors properly discuss these limitations—specifically, the caveats of their evaluation metrics, the strong assumptions about classifier invariance that undermine the "black-box" claim, and the influence of the masking network—I believe the paper is balanced enough for publication. If these issues are not addressed, the paper should not be published.
