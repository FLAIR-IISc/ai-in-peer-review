This paper introduces a fast image saliency detection method applicable to any differentiable image classifier. The core idea is to train a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, avoiding the iterative approaches common in existing literature. This model leverages features from a pre-trained ResNet-50 architecture and is trained with a novel objective function incorporating smoothness and both Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and Smallest Destroying Region (SDR) concepts. The authors demonstrate competitive results on ImageNet localisation and introduce a new saliency metric, achieving performance superior to other weakly-supervised methods.

This work builds upon a rich history of saliency detection techniques stemming from the rise of CNNs in 2012 [5]. Early gradient-based methods [11] offered speed but lacked quality, prompting iterative refinement techniques like those in [17] and [1], which, while accurate, were computationally expensive. More recent work, such as Fong and Vedaldi [2], attempts a model-agnostic approach, but still relies on iterative optimization. The authors correctly position their contribution as addressing the speed limitations of existing methods while maintaining accuracy and interpretability. The introduction of a new saliency metric and demonstration of real-time performance are key differentiators.

**Strengths:**

* **Speed:** The claimed real-time performance (100 images/second on a standard GPU) is a significant advantage, enabling applications impractical for slower methods.
* **Novel Objective Function:** The combination of smoothness regularization, SSR, and SDR principles within the loss function appears well-motivated and contributes to the quality of the generated saliency maps.
* **Competitive Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on the ImageNet localization task, particularly in a weakly supervised setting, is a strong indicator of the method’s effectiveness.
* **New Saliency Metric:** The proposed metric, rooted in information theory and SSR principles, provides a potentially more nuanced evaluation of saliency maps compared to simple localization accuracy.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the methodology and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Real-time Claim Nuance:** The claim of 100 images per second should be clarified regarding the inclusion of image resizing. Performance on higher-resolution images (e.g., 640x480) is not reported and crucial for assessing practical applicability.
* **Small Object Handling:** The method’s performance on images containing small instances of the target class is not explicitly addressed. The use of 224x224 images might be limiting in such scenarios.
* **Metric Comparison:** A direct comparison of the proposed saliency metric with those used in related works (e.g., [2]) would strengthen the evaluation. Including the new metric's values for other methods in Table 2 would be insightful.
* **Ablation Study:** While the authors discuss the components of the loss function, a more detailed ablation study demonstrating the impact of each term (TV penalty, SSR, SDR) would be beneficial.
* **Bias Discussion:** While the authors mention potential biases, a deeper exploration of how the masking model's own learned biases might influence saliency map generation would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to fast saliency detection.
* The method achieves competitive results on a challenging benchmark.
* The introduction of a new saliency metric contributes to the field’s evaluation toolkit.
* The potential for real-time applications is significant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The real-time performance claim requires further clarification and validation on a wider range of image resolutions.
* The method's handling of small objects and potential biases require further investigation.
* The lack of a direct comparison of the new saliency metric with existing metrics weakens the evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of image saliency detection. The proposed method addresses a key limitation of existing techniques – computational cost – while maintaining competitive accuracy. The novelty of the approach, combined with the experimental results and the introduction of a new metric, makes this a strong submission. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly clarifying the real-time performance claims and providing a more comprehensive evaluation, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** contingent on minor revisions addressing the points raised.
