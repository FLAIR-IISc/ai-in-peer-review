This paper presents an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The proposed method trains a masking model that identifies the tightest rectangular crop containing the entire salient region of a particular requested class, as determined by a black box classifier such as AlexNet, GoogleNet, or ResNet. The model architecture leverages image feature maps, such as those produced by ResNet-50, across different scales. The final scale feature is passed through a feature filter that performs initial localization, while subsequent upsampling blocks fine-tune the produced masks. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques in the ImageNet localization task.

The paper appears to provide sufficient references and related works, although a comprehensive check was not conducted. The technical correctness of the paper also seems satisfactory, but a thorough examination was not performed. The authors present a number of intuitive discussions on the design of their approach, which adds to the paper's clarity. The overall presentation of the paper is well-organized and easy to follow.

The paper presents interesting technical results, but some concerns arise regarding the real-time speed claim and its applications to real-world images. Specifically, the following issues warrant further consideration:

1. The processing time for 100 images per second reportedly includes the image resizing operation. However, the running time for larger images, such as 640x480 images taken from an iPhone 6s, is not provided. It would be beneficial to include this information to demonstrate the method's scalability.

2. The salient objects in this paper appear to be relatively large. If the requested class object is small in the images, it is unclear whether a 224x224 image would be sufficient for accurate detection. The authors should address this potential limitation and discuss possible solutions for handling smaller objects.

3. In Table 3, the paper presents a comparison with other works, but it is unclear whether there are corresponding metrics for other works listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2]. Including this information would facilitate a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.
