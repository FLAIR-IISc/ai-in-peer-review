This paper proposes a novel approach to rapidly generate saliency masks for images, applicable to any differentiable classifier. The core idea is to train a masking model – a neural network – to predict salient regions in a single forward pass, circumventing the iterative methods common in existing techniques. The proposed method achieves impressive speed, enabling potential real-time applications, and demonstrates strong performance on ImageNet localization and a newly proposed saliency metric.

The paper addresses an important problem: understanding the decision-making process of complex image classifiers. Existing methods often struggle with computational cost, making them impractical for dynamic applications. This work offers a compelling solution by shifting from per-image optimization to a learned, feed-forward model. The results on weakly supervised localization, surpassing other methods, and the competitive performance on the proposed saliency metric are promising. The architectural details, utilizing a U-Net with a ResNet encoder, are well-described and motivated.

However, several limitations and points require further consideration. The evaluation relies heavily on weakly supervised localization accuracy and a novel saliency metric – both of which have inherent caveats. While weakly supervised localization provides a useful signal, it's important to acknowledge that a region crucial for classification might not align perfectly with human-perceived saliency, especially if contextual information is vital for accurate classification. The saliency metric, involving cropping and resizing, introduces potential artifacts. Resizing to a fixed aspect ratio could distort the crucial features for classification, and the scaling might push the classifier beyond its learned invariance, potentially affecting performance. The paper doesn’t sufficiently address these potential issues.

A critical question is the extent to which the masking model's performance is tied to the architecture of the underlying classifier. The paper would benefit from discussing experiments with different classifier architectures to determine the robustness and generalizability of the learned masks. Does the method perform equally well with models exhibiting varying degrees of translation and scale invariance? The reliance on a pre-trained ResNet encoder (though also demonstrated to work from scratch on CIFAR-10) suggests a potential dependency on the feature representations learned during ImageNet pre-training. 

Furthermore, the claim of model-agnosticism in the title is somewhat overstated given the observed dependence on classifier properties (scale and translation invariance) and potential architectural biases. The paper should clearly articulate the scope of applicability and acknowledge the constraints. It’s not truly “black-box” if the quality of saliency maps is tied to the classifier's internal workings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem with a novel and efficient solution.
*   Demonstrates strong quantitative results on established benchmarks and a new metric.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the method and experiments.
*   Potential for real-time applications and further research (e.g., video saliency).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Evaluation relies on metrics with inherent limitations that are not thoroughly discussed.
*   Potential for artifacts introduced by the saliency metric’s cropping and resizing steps.
*   Unclear extent to which the method’s performance depends on the underlying classifier’s architecture and inherent invariances.
*   The claim of model-agnosticism appears overstated given the demonstrated dependencies.



**Minor Comments:**

*   Clarify in Table 1 whether results are reported for all classes or only for the correctly classified images.
*   When comparing to LRP methods, specify the exact variant and parameter settings used (e.g., epsilon, alpha/beta values).



**Post-Rebuttal Considerations:**

The rebuttal highlights a strong dependence of the saliency metric’s validity on the classifier’s scale and translation invariance, severely limiting the method's general applicability. This dependence makes the method unsuitable for models lacking these properties, restricts its use in domains like spectrogram analysis with CNNs, and casts doubt on the "black-box" claim.  The hint of strong dependence on the masking network itself raises concerns about visualizing the saliency of the U-Net versus the original classifier. If the paper thoroughly discusses these limitations and their impact on the conclusions, a balanced publication might be justified. Otherwise, rejection is warranted.
