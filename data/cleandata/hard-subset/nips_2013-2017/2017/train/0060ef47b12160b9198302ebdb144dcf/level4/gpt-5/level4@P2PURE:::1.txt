The paper presents a neural-network–based method for learning saliency masks that can process multiple images per second (i.e., it is fast). I view the submission as borderline; I would not object to either rejection or acceptance. I am supportive of the broader idea of learning to explain models, and this paper contains several good ideas. While I do not see obvious mistakes, there are clear limitations.

- The saliency is only evaluated indirectly—either via weakly supervised localization or via the proposed saliency metric. Both have clear limitations that should be discussed.
  - Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy. When contextual cues are essential for determining the class, object localization need not correlate with saliency quality. The localization results are interesting, but using them as a saliency quality metric comes with a major caveat.
  - The proposed saliency metric is also problematic in how it is applied. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size, effectively imposing the original image’s aspect ratio on the crop. This introduces two artifacts: (1) aspect-ratio distortion can affect classification; and (2) the metric prefers small salient regions, but small crops are heavily upsampled for reclassification, potentially presenting the object at a non-ideal scale. Convnets tend to be reasonably translation-invariant, but scale invariance must be learned and is limited.

The paper does not discuss how strongly the masking model’s ability to learn masks depends on its architecture. Did the authors experiment with different architectures, and how did these choices influence the results?

Minor comments
- Are the results in Table 1 computed over all classes or only for the ground-truth class?
- Please specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparison (e.g., epsilon, alpha–beta, and associated parameters).

Post-rebuttal edit
The effectiveness of the saliency metric depends on the classifier’s quality and its scale invariance, which substantially limits the method’s applicability. It can only be applied to networks that exhibit sufficient scale invariance. This has important consequences:
- The method cannot be used for models during training, nor for models that do not exhibit the required invariance.
- This limits applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs).
- The method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers, contrary to the claim in the title.

Furthermore, the response suggests a strong dependence on the masking network. As a result, it is unclear whether the visualizations reflect the saliency of the U-network or of the masking network.

If these effects and limitations are properly discussed, the paper would be balanced enough for publication; otherwise, it should not be published.
