- Proposed neural-network saliency mask method is promising but has significant limitations that could warrant rejection if unaddressed.  
- Primary concern: saliency is evaluated indirectly, which undermines the validity of conclusions.  
- Weakly supervised localization is an unreliable proxy for saliency because object context can be critical for classification.  
- The proposed saliency metric (crop, rescale, reclassify) introduces artifacts from aspect-ratio changes and extreme scaling of small regions.  
- CNNs lack inherent scale invariance, so rescaling can present objects at non-optimal scales and distort metric results.  
- The metric’s validity depends entirely on the classifier’s scale-invariance properties.  
- Consequently, the approach cannot be reliably used during model training or with classifiers that are not scale-invariant.  
- The method is inapplicable to domains where classifiers do not exhibit scale invariance (e.g., spectrogram analysis).  
- Claim that the method works for “black-box classifiers” is unsupported because the metric requires access to classifier behavior/representations.  
- The masking model’s dependence on the underlying network architecture is not investigated, raising concerns about generality.  
- It is unclear whether the visualizations reflect the target model’s saliency or the masking network’s behavior.  
- Authors should report experiments with alternative architectures and how architectural choices affect saliency quality.  
- Minor clarifications needed: whether Table 1 reports results for all classes or only correctly classified instances.  
- Minor clarifications needed: specify the exact LRP variant and parameter settings (epsilon, alpha-beta) used for comparisons.  
- Post-rebuttal confirms core limitations; acceptance should be contingent on explicitly addressing classifier invariance and masking-network ambiguity.
