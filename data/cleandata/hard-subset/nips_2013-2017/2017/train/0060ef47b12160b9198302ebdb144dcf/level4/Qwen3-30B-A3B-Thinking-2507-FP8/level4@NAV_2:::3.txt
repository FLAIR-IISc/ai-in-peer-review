

This paper introduces a novel, fast saliency detection method that trains a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, rather than using iterative approaches. The authors build upon prior work in saliency detection (e.g., gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], patch removal techniques [17], and optimization-based approaches [1, 2]) but differ by training a model to predict saliency maps efficiently. Their approach is model-agnostic, applicable to any differentiable classifier, and designed to find the tightest rectangular crop containing the salient region for a requested class.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a well-designed objective function (equation 4) that balances mask smoothness, region size, classification accuracy on preserved regions, and classification accuracy on removed regions. The experiments on ImageNet demonstrate strong performance, with the ResNet-50 trained masking model achieving a localization error of 36.7% compared to VGG's 34.3% (Table 1), and significantly outperforming other weakly supervised methods (Table 2). However, the claim about processing "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second" requires clarification: does this include image resizing operations? What is the processing time for larger images (e.g., 640x480 from smartphone cameras)?

Clarity: The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results. The U-Net architecture for the masking model is well-described, and the new saliency metric (equation 3) is well-motivated. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion on handling small objects (see comments below).

Originality: The approach is novel in training a model to predict saliency maps rather than computing them per-image. While parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] also uses optimization in image space, this paper differs by training a model for single-pass prediction and introduces a new saliency metric. The related work section adequately references previous contributions and distinguishes this work from them.

Significance: The method has clear significance for real-time applications, enabling video-saliency for autonomous vehicles (discussed in Future Research). The performance improvements over weakly supervised methods (Tables 1-3) demonstrate a meaningful advancement, with the proposed metric showing that their saliency masks are interpretable at a level comparable to ground truth bounding boxes.

Strengths:
- Novel approach to fast saliency detection
- Strong experimental results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10
- Well-designed new saliency metric (equation 3)
- Clear explanation of the masking model architecture
- Applicable to any differentiable classifier

Weaknesses:
- Unclear processing time details for non-standard image resolutions
- Limited discussion on handling small objects in images
- Incomplete comparison in Table 3 (missing metrics for methods in Table 2)
- The 224x224 image resolution may be insufficient for small objects

Comments:
- Please clarify whether the "more than a hundred 224x224 images per second" claim includes image resizing operations, and provide processing times for larger image resolutions (e.g., 640x480).
- The paper demonstrates strong performance for objects that occupy significant portions of images (Figure 5), but it's unclear how the method handles small objects (e.g., a bird in a landscape). Is the 224x224 resolution sufficient for all cases?
- Table 3 should include corresponding metrics for methods referenced in Table 2 (such as [2]) for better comparison.
- LN 273: "More over, because our model" → "Moreover, because our model"
- LN 151: "difference that [3] only optimise the mask" → "difference that [3] only optimises the mask"
