This paper proposes a neural network-based approach to learn saliency masks, which can process multiple images per second, thereby achieving high efficiency. In my opinion, the paper is borderline, and I would not object to either rejection or acceptance. I believe in the concept of learning to explain a model, and the paper presents some good ideas.

The paper appears to be technically sound, with no obvious mistakes. However, there are clear limitations that need to be addressed. One of the primary concerns is that saliency is only indirectly measured, either through weakly supervised localization or by the proposed saliency metric, both of which have limitations that should be discussed in the paper.

The weakly supervised localization is not a perfect measure, as it may not correlate with saliency quality when the context in which an object appears is essential to determine its class. Although the results on weakly supervised localization are interesting, there is a significant caveat when using them as a quality metric for saliency.

The proposed saliency metric also has limitations due to its application. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size, maintaining the original aspect ratio. This process could introduce two artifacts: the change in aspect ratio may impact the classification performance, and the preference for small salient regions may lead to suboptimal scale presentation to the classifier. Since convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are generally translation-invariant but not necessarily scale-invariant, this could affect the results.

Furthermore, the paper does not discuss the dependence of the masking model on the architecture used for learning the masks. It is unclear whether the authors experimented with different architectures and how this influenced the results.

Minor comments:

* Are the results in Table 1 obtained for all classes or only for the correct class?
* Please specify the LRP variant and parameter setting used for comparison, as there are multiple variants with parameters (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta).

Post-rebuttal edit:

The fact that the saliency metric's performance depends on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier is a significant limitation of the proposed method. This restricts its applicability to networks with scale invariance, which has important consequences:

* The method cannot be used for models during the training phase or for models that do not exhibit this invariance.
* This limits its applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs).
* The method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers, as claimed in the title.

Moreover, the response suggests a strong dependence on the masking network, which raises concerns about whether the method is visualizing the saliency of the U-network or the masking network.

If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, I believe it is balanced enough for publication. Otherwise, it should not be published.
