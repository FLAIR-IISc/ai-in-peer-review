## Review for NIPS 2017 – Predicting Important Regions for Object Categorization

This paper presents a novel neural network approach to predict regions within an image that are most salient for a separate neural network performing object categorization. The core idea – to learn to focus attention on the features driving a classifier’s decision – is compelling and well-executed. The paper is clearly written and easy to follow, and the experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods on challenging, benchmark datasets employing state-of-the-art object detection networks.

The strength of this work lies in its strong empirical performance. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method can identify regions that substantially influence the categorization process. The quantitative results consistently outperform baseline approaches, and the visualizations provided offer intuitive support for the model’s efficacy. The choice of datasets (ImageNet, COCO) and the integration with powerful pre-trained models further solidify the robustness and practical relevance of the proposed technique. 

However, while the paper establishes *that* the method works well, further motivation regarding the *necessity* of real-time efficiency would strengthen the introduction. The benefits of identifying salient regions are clear in terms of interpretability and potential for resource optimization, but the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of application scenarios where speed is critical. Examples might include robotics, autonomous driving, or embedded systems where computational constraints are significant. Explicitly outlining these practical motivations would enhance the impact of the work.

Furthermore, while the visualizations are helpful, the paper would be significantly enriched by a deeper analysis of the learned salient regions. Exploring *why* the model focuses on particular areas would provide valuable insights into the decision-making process of the underlying object classifier. For example, do certain object categories consistently exhibit a reliance on background biases for recognition? Does the model learn to attend to parts of objects that are intuitively important, or does it discover unexpected correlations? A section dedicated to such an analysis – perhaps exploring category-specific attention patterns – would elevate the paper beyond a purely performance-driven study and contribute to our understanding of how deep neural networks categorize images. 

Specifically, investigating whether the identified salient regions align with human attention mechanisms or known object properties could provide valuable validation. Comparing the model's attention maps to saliency maps generated using traditional computer vision techniques, or even through human subject experiments, would offer an interesting and informative comparison.

Minor points include: A more thorough discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the attention mechanism would be useful. While the authors mention efficiency gains, quantifying these gains – in terms of FLOPs or inference time – would provide a more concrete assessment of the method’s practicality.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear presentation and compelling results. The proposed method represents a significant contribution to the field of explainable AI and attention mechanisms. Addressing the suggestions regarding motivation and analysis of learned regions would further enhance the paper’s impact and make it an even more valuable contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions addressing the suggested analysis and motivation points).
