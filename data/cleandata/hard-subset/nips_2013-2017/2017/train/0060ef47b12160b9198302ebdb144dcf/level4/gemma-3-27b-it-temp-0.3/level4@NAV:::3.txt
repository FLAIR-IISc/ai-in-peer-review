This paper introduces a novel image saliency mask generation technique capable of processing 100 224x224 images per second on standard GPU hardware. The proposed method learns a masking model to identify the minimal bounding box encompassing the salient region of a target class, as determined by a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet). The model leverages multi-scale image feature maps, such as those produced by ResNet-50, employing an initial localisation stage via a feature filter followed by upsampling blocks for refined mask generation. Experimental results demonstrate superior performance compared to existing weakly-supervised methods on the ImageNet localisation benchmark. 

The paper includes a comprehensive review of relevant literature and references. A detailed verification of all aspects was not undertaken within this review. 

The technical aspects of the paper appear sound. A complete technical validation was not performed as part of this review.

The authors provide insightful rationale and discussion supporting their design choices. The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

Overall, this work presents compelling technical contributions. However, I have some reservations regarding the reported real-time performance and its applicability to real-world images.

Comments:
- Is the reported processing time of 100 images per second inclusive of image resizing? If so, what is the processing time for larger images, such as 640x480 images captured by a device like an iPhone 6s?
- The current experiments focus on relatively large salient objects. How does the method perform when the target class object is small within the image? Would a 224x224 input resolution be sufficient in such cases?
- In Table 3, are comparable metrics available for other methods presented in Table 2, specifically Feed [2]?
