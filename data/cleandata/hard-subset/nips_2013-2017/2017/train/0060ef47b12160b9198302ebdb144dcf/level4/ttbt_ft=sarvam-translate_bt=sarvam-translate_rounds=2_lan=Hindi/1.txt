This paper proposes an approach for learning a silence mask. The proposed approach is based on a neural network and can process several images per second (meaning it is fast).

For me, this paper is borderline; I wouldn't object to either acceptance or rejection. I genuinely believe in the concept of learning to explain a model, and I think the paper has some good ideas.

There are no obvious mistakes, but there are clear limitations.
- Silence is only indirectly measured, either through weak supervised localization or by the proposed silence metric. Both methods have clear limitations, and I think these limitations should be discussed in the paper.

Weak supervised localization is not a perfect solution. If the context of an object's presence is necessary to determine its category, then object localization does not need to be correlated with silence quality. The results on weak supervised localization are interesting, but I think there is a major caveat when using them as a quality metric for silence.

The silence metric is not perfect due to the way it is implemented. The predicted silence area is cropped. This crop is then rescaled to the original image size with the original aspect ratio. This can lead to two artifacts. First, the change in aspect ratio can affect how well it can be classified. Second, the proposed metric favors a smaller silence area. Since a small area is too exaggerated for reclassification, the scale at which the object is now presented to the classifier may not be ideal. (Connet generally makes many translation changes immutable, but scaling immutability should be learned, and perhaps it has its limitations.)

The paper does not discuss how much the masking model depends on the architecture for mask learning. Did the authors experiment with different architectures at one point, and how did this affect the results?

Post-reurbation Edit
The silence metric works so well, it depends on the quality of the classifier and the scale immutability, which strongly limits the applicability of the proposed method. It can only be applied to networks with this immutability.
It has important consequences:
- This method cannot be used for models during the training phase, nor for models that do not exhibit this immutability.
- It limits applicability to other domains (such as spectrogram analysis with CNN).
- As claimed in the title, this method is generally not applicable to black-box classifiers.
Furthermore, the response indicates a strong dependence on the masking network.
- Consequently, it is not clear to me whether we are looking at the silence of a U-network or a masking network.
If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, I think it is balanced enough for publication. If not, it should not be published.
