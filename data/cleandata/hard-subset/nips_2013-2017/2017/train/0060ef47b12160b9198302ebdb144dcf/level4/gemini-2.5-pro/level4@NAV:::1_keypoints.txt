- Paper proposes a computationally efficient neural method for learning saliency masks, processing multiple images per second.
- Overall assessment is borderline; reviewer could accept or reject.
- Core idea of learning-based explainability is compelling with several promising contributions.
- Manuscript is free of obvious errors but has significant unaddressed limitations.
- Saliency evaluation is indirect: uses weakly supervised localization, which is an imperfect proxy as salient regions may not align with object bounding boxes when context matters.
- Proposed saliency metric is flawed: cropping and rescaling the salient region can introduce artifacts and alter aspect ratio, harming classification.
- The metric inherently favors smaller salient regions and depends on classifier scale invariance, which is limited in CNNs.
- Authors must discuss these evaluation limitations in the paper.
- Manuscript lacks analysis of sensitivity to the mask-generating network architecture; unclear how architecture choices affect saliency maps.
- Post-rebuttal concern: metric’s reliance on classifier scale invariance severely limits applicability (unsuitable during training, problematic in domains like spectrograms).
- Claim of general applicability to "black-box" classifiers is unsupported because it requires the classifier to have strong scale invariance.
- Response indicates significant dependency on the masking network, raising whether maps reflect the classifier’s saliency or the masker’s biases.
- Paper should only be accepted if these limitations and implications are thoroughly and transparently discussed.
- Minor requests: clarify whether Table 1 results are averaged over all classes or just the ground-truth class.
- Minor requests: specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparison.
