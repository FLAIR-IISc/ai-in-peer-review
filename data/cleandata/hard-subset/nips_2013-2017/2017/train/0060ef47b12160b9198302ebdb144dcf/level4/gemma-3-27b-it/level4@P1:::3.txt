This paper presents a novel image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. Their approach trains a masking model to identify the smallest rectangular crop containing the salient region for a given class, as determined by a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet). The model architecture leverages multi-scale image features, such as those from ResNet-50. The initial localization is performed by a feature filter operating on the lowest resolution feature map, with subsequent upsampling blocks refining the resulting masks. Experiments demonstrate that their method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques in the ImageNet localization task.

The paper demonstrates a solid understanding of related work, with sufficient references and appropriate citations. The technical approach appears sound, though a complete verification would require reproducing the results. The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the design choices and underlying intuition behind their method. The presentation is generally clear and well-organized.

Overall, this paper presents interesting and technically strong results. However, I have some concerns regarding the claimed real-time performance and its applicability to real-world images.

**Specific Comments:**

*   Does the reported processing time of 100 images per second include the image resizing operation? If so, what is the running time for larger images, such as 640x480 images taken from a smartphone camera?
*   The experiments focus on relatively large salient objects. How does the method perform when the object of the requested class is small within the image? Would a 224x224 image resolution be sufficient in such cases?
*   In Table 3, are corresponding saliency metric values available for other methods presented in Table 2, such as Fong and Vedaldi [2]? A direct comparison would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach offers a new way to generate saliency maps quickly and accurately, differing from iterative methods and optimization-based techniques.
*   **Performance:** The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the ImageNet localization task, outperforming other weakly supervised approaches.
*   **Efficiency:** The claim of real-time performance (100fps) is significant and opens up possibilities for new applications.
*   **Well-defined metric:** The proposed saliency metric provides a more nuanced evaluation of saliency maps than traditional localization accuracy.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Real-time claim:** The practical limitations of the real-time claim need further clarification, particularly concerning image resizing and varying image sizes.
*   **Generalizability:** The method’s performance on smaller objects and more complex scenes is unclear.
*   **Comparison:** A more direct comparison to Fong and Vedaldi [2] using the proposed saliency metric would strengthen the evaluation.
