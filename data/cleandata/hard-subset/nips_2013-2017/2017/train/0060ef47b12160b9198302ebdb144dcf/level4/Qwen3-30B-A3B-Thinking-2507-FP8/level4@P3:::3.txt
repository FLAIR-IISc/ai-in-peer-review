

This paper introduces a novel approach for real-time saliency detection in black-box image classifiers by training a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. The authors propose a U-Net-based architecture that processes input images through multi-scale feature maps (using ResNet-50 as an encoder) to generate sharp, artifact-free masks. Crucially, their method avoids iterative optimization (unlike Zhou et al. [17] and Fong & Vedaldi [2]) by learning to directly produce masks that satisfy both Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and Smallest Destroying Region (SDR) criteria. The authors introduce a new saliency metric (s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p)) that evaluates the interpretability of saliency maps by measuring the minimal crop size preserving classification confidence. On ImageNet, their method achieves 36.7% object localization error (vs. 34.3% for supervised VGG [10]), significantly outperforming weakly supervised baselines like the "Center" box (50.2% error) and iterative methods. The approach generalizes to CIFAR-10 without pre-training, demonstrating robustness across datasets.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical Quality & Innovation:** The work is technically sound, with clear theoretical grounding in SSR/SDR definitions and a well-justified objective function (Eq. 4). The masking model’s single-pass inference (100+ fps on 224x224 images) represents a significant speed advantage over iterative methods (e.g., [2], [17]), enabling real-time applications like video saliency. The new metric (s(a,p)) effectively captures interpretability beyond standard localization accuracy, as validated by near-ground-truth performance (Table 3).  
2. **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear explanations of the masking model architecture (Fig. 4), evidence-removal strategy (Eq. 2), and training innovations (e.g., 30% fake labels to prevent class-agnostic masking). The CIFAR-10 experiment (Section 5.3) convincingly shows the method’s generalizability without pre-trained biases.  
3. **Significance:** The work addresses a critical gap in model interpretability for real-world systems (e.g., autonomous vehicles), where speed is paramount. By outperforming weakly supervised baselines while requiring no localization data, it advances practical applications of saliency methods. The open-source code and detailed ablations (Appendix) further enhance reproducibility.  
4. **Related Work:** The comparison to [2] (Fong & Vedaldi) is thorough, highlighting that their work is the first to train a *model* for saliency (vs. iterative optimization), while also acknowledging Zhou et al.’s iterative SDR approach.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Real-World Applicability:** The speed claim (100+ fps) applies only to 224x224 images. The authors should clarify performance for larger resolutions (e.g., 640x480 from smartphone cameras) and discuss computational overhead from resizing. Without this, the "real-time" assertion lacks context for deployment.  
2. **Small-Object Handling:** The method’s effectiveness for small salient objects (e.g., a bird in a landscape) is untested. The ImageNet examples (Fig. 5) show dominant central objects, but the paper doesn’t address how mask precision degrades when the target class occupies <10% of the image (a common challenge in object detection).  
3. **Metric Comparison Gap:** Table 3 lacks the s(a,p) metric for [2] and other baselines (e.g., "Feed" in Table 2), making it difficult to fully contextualize their performance advantage. Including this would strengthen the claims.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel, high-impact method for real-time saliency; technically rigorous; strong empirical results; significant practical relevance.  
*Con:* Resolution limitations unaddressed; small-object performance not validated; metric comparison incomplete.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept**. This is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in model interpretability with clear real-world applicability. The weaknesses are minor and addressable in revision (e.g., clarifying resolution constraints, adding metric comparisons). The work aligns with NIPS’ focus on scientifically sound, impactful contributions to machine learning. The authors’ response to the speed and small-object concerns would likely be straightforward (e.g., testing 640x480 images, adding ablations on small objects), making this a strong candidate for acceptance. The paper’s clarity, novelty, and significance outweigh the minor technical gaps.
