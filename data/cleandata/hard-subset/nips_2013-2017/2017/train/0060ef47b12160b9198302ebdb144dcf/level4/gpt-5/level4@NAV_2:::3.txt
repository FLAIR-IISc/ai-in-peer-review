This paper introduces a method for generating image saliency masks in real time, reporting throughput of roughly 100 images at 224x224 per second on a standard GPU. The approach trains a masking network that, given a target class for a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet), predicts a mask whose tightest enclosing rectangle captures the salient evidence for that class. The architecture leverages multi-scale feature maps (e.g., from ResNet-50), applies a feature filter at the coarsest scale for initial localization, and then refines the masks via subsequent upsampling blocks. Experiments indicate the method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on ImageNet localization.

The related work and references seem adequate, though I did not check them exhaustively.

The technical claims appear reasonable, although I did not verify every detail.

The paper provides several intuitions and design discussions that motivate the approach.

The presentation is clear and well organized.

Overall, the paper presents interesting technical results, though I have some concerns about the real-time speed claim and applicability to real-world imagery.

Comments:
- Does the claimed 100 images/second include image resizing? If so, what is the throughput for larger inputs, e.g., 640x480 images from an iPhone 6s?
- The salient objects shown are often large. How does the method behave when the target object is small? Is 224x224 sufficient in that regime?
- In Table 3, can you report the corresponding metric for methods listed in Table 2, e.g., the approach in [2]?
