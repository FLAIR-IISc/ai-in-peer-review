

This paper proposes a fast neural network approach for learning saliency masks. While the concept of learning to explain models is compelling and the method demonstrates reasonable speed (processing multiple images per second), the paper presents significant limitations that prevent it from meeting NIPS standards. I find the submission borderline, but the identified flaws strongly lean toward rejection.

The core issue lies in the indirect and flawed measurement of saliency quality. The authors rely on two methods—weakly supervised localization and a novel saliency metric—both of which suffer from critical weaknesses that are inadequately addressed.

First, weakly supervised localization is unsuitable as a primary saliency quality metric. If an object’s class depends on contextual cues (e.g., a "cow" in a barn vs. a "cow" in a field), the object’s location may not correlate with saliency quality. The results using this method are interesting but cannot reliably validate the saliency masks, creating a major caveat that must be explicitly discussed.

Second, the proposed saliency metric introduces artifacts through its implementation. The salient region is cropped, rescaled to the original image size while preserving aspect ratio, and then re-classified. This process risks two key issues: (1) aspect ratio distortion may impair classifier performance, and (2) the metric favors small salient regions. Crucially, rescaling small regions significantly magnifies them, presenting objects at unnatural scales to the classifier. While CNNs are translation-invariant, scale invariance is not inherent and must be learned; the metric’s reliance on this property is problematic and unexamined.

The paper fails to address a critical dependency: how the masking model’s performance varies with the underlying classifier architecture. The authors never explore whether different network structures (e.g., ResNet vs. VGG) yield different saliency outputs or whether the masks primarily reflect the classifier’s behavior rather than the input data. This omission undermines the claim that the method visualizes "model saliency" and raises the question: are we interpreting the U-network’s behavior or the masking network’s?

The rebuttal confirms the most severe limitation: the saliency metric’s validity *entirely depends* on the classifier’s scale invariance. This has profound consequences:

1.  **Restricted applicability**: The method cannot be used during classifier training (when scale invariance is unlearned) or for classifiers lacking this property (e.g., specialized architectures for spectrograms or medical imaging).
2.  **Domain limitations**: It is not generally applicable to other domains (e.g., audio, time-series) where CNNs may lack scale invariance.
3.  **Misleading claim**: The title’s assertion of "general applicability to black-box classifiers" is invalid, as the method requires specific classifier properties.

Furthermore, the rebuttal suggests the masking model’s architecture heavily influences results, yet the paper does not discuss this. This raises a fundamental ambiguity: the output saliency may reflect the masking network’s biases rather than the target model’s reasoning. If the paper does not thoroughly address these dependencies and limitations, the method lacks scientific rigor.

Minor concerns require clarification:
- Table 1 results: Are they averaged across all classes or only correct predictions? (This impacts metric reliability.)
- LRP comparison: Specify which variant (epsilon, alpha-beta, etc.) and parameter settings were used, as these significantly affect results.

In summary, the paper’s core premise is undermined by its metric’s dependence on classifier-specific properties, which are neither acknowledged nor mitigated. The method is not "generally applicable" as claimed, and the rebuttal confirms its narrow scope. While the concept of learning explanations is valuable, the technical execution fails to deliver a robust, broadly useful tool. Without substantial revisions addressing these architectural dependencies, metric artifacts, and misaligned claims, the paper does not meet NIPS’s bar for publication. It should be rejected.
