The paper proposes an approach to learning saliency masks based on a neural network, which is capable of processing multiple images per second, making it a fast solution. While I find the concept of learning to explain a model compelling, I believe the paper is borderline and could be either rejected or accepted without strong objection.

The approach presented in the paper has some good ideas, but it also has clear limitations. One of the primary concerns is that saliency is only indirectly measured, either through weakly supervised localization or the proposed saliency metric. Both of these methods have limitations that should be discussed in the paper.

Weakly supervised localization is not a perfect measure, as it does not necessarily correlate with saliency quality, particularly when the context in which an object appears is essential to determining its class. Although the results on weakly supervised localization are interesting, they should be interpreted with caution when used as a quality metric for saliency.

The proposed saliency metric also has limitations due to its application. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size while maintaining the original aspect ratio. This could introduce two artifacts: the change in aspect ratio might impact classification accuracy, and the preference for small salient regions could lead to suboptimal scale presentation to the classifier. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are generally translation-invariant, but scaling invariance must be learned and may have limitations.

Another crucial aspect that is not discussed in the paper is the dependence of the masking model on the architecture used for learning the masks. Did the authors experiment with different architectures, and how did this influence the results?

Minor comments include:

* Clarify whether the results in Table 1 are obtained for all classes or only for the correct class.
* Specify the LRP variant and parameter setting used for comparison, as there are multiple variants with parameters (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta).

Post-rebuttal edit:

The paper's reliance on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier for the saliency metric to work is a significant limitation, restricting the method's applicability to networks with this invariance. This has important consequences, including:

* The method cannot be applied to models during the training phase or to models that do not exhibit this invariance.
* The method's applicability is limited to other domains, such as spectrogram analysis with CNNs.
* The method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers, contrary to the claim in the title.

Furthermore, the response suggests a strong dependence on the masking network, raising concerns about whether the method is visualizing the saliency of the U-network or the masking network.

If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, I believe it is balanced enough for publication. However, if these limitations are not addressed, the paper should not be published.
