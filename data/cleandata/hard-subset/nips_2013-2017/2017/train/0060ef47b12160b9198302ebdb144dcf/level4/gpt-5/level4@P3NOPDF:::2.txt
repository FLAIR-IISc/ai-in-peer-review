Summary:
The paper proposes a neural network that predicts the image regions most relevant to a separate object classification network, effectively learning where the classifier “looks” to make its decision. The approach is evaluated on challenging datasets and with state-of-the-art architectures, demonstrating clear performance advantages over prior methods. The manuscript is well written and easy to follow.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation: the goal of predicting classifier-relevant regions is well defined and practically meaningful for interpretability, efficiency, and potentially for guiding downstream tasks.
- Strong empirical results: the method consistently outperforms prior approaches across competitive benchmarks and with modern networks, which strengthens the case for general applicability.
- Sound experimental setup: the comparisons appear fair and the evaluation is conducted on realistic, challenging datasets, increasing confidence in the claimed improvements.

Areas for improvement and questions:
- Motivation for real-time constraints: The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of scenarios where real-time saliency prediction is critical, and how the proposed method fits those constraints (e.g., interactive systems, on-device perception, autonomous robotics, or large-scale retrieval where saliency is used repeatedly at inference).
- Analysis of learned saliency: While quantitative gains are compelling, the paper could provide deeper insights into what the model learns. For example:
  - Do the predicted salient regions align with human intuition and existing saliency or explanation methods?
  - Are there object categories that rely heavily on contextual cues or background correlations? A per-class analysis could reveal biases (e.g., boats-water, airplanes-sky) and whether the model exploits or mitigates them.
  - How consistent are the salient patterns across architectures and datasets? Do the maps generalize when the target classifier changes?
- Efficiency–accuracy trade-offs: If real-time performance is a goal, an explicit analysis of latency versus accuracy (e.g., varying resolution, model size, or computational budget) would clarify practical utility and deployment boundaries.
- Qualitative and failure-case studies: Additional visualizations showing both successful and failure cases would help readers understand limitations (e.g., cluttered scenes, occlusions, small objects) and guide future work.
- Robustness and bias: Brief experiments on distribution shifts (background changes, synthetic occlusions) or adversarial settings would further validate that the method captures semantically meaningful regions rather than spurious correlations.

Overall assessment:
This is a timely and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem at the intersection of interpretability and efficiency for deep visual recognition. The empirical results are strong and the paper is clearly written. Strengthening the motivation for real-time use cases and providing a deeper analysis of the learned salient regions—particularly regarding background biases and per-category behavior—would make the contribution more impactful. With these additions, the paper would offer both practical value and richer scientific insight into how modern classifiers use visual evidence. I lean positive.
