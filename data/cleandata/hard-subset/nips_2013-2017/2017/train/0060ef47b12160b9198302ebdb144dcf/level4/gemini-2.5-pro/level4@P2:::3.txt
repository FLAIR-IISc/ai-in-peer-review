### Summary

This paper proposes a novel method for generating image saliency maps for any differentiable black-box classifier. The core idea is to train a separate "masking model"—an encoder-decoder network based on a U-Net architecture—to produce a saliency mask in a single forward pass. This approach contrasts with previous iterative methods that optimize a mask for each image individually, which is often time-consuming. The masking model is trained to minimize a well-motivated objective function that encourages the mask to be small and smooth, while preserving the classifier's confidence on the masked region (Smallest Sufficient Region) and simultaneously destroying the classifier's confidence on the removed region (Smallest Destroying Region). The authors introduce a new saliency metric to evaluate the quality of the generated explanations. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet show that this method is significantly faster than iterative techniques and achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task.

### Quality and Significance

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of model interpretability. The primary strength is the shift from slow, per-instance optimization for saliency maps to a fast, amortized inference approach. By training a dedicated model, the method achieves real-time performance (claimed >100 images/sec), which opens up new applications such as video saliency analysis.

The work is technically sound. The design of the objective function (Equation 4) is thoughtful, balancing multiple desirable properties for a saliency map: smoothness (TV penalty), conciseness (AV penalty), sufficiency (preserving class score), and destructiveness (lowering class score when inverted). The architectural choice of a U-Net with a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder is sensible for leveraging multi-scale features to produce sharp masks. Furthermore, the introduction of a new saliency metric based on information concentration in a cropped region is an interesting and valuable contribution for evaluating interpretability.

The experimental results are strong. The method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task, even approaching the performance of some fully supervised methods. The ablation study on CIFAR-10, where the masking model is trained from scratch without a pre-trained encoder, effectively demonstrates the general applicability of the framework.

However, there are a few areas where the evaluation could be strengthened:

1.  **Real-Time Performance Claims:** The claim of processing over 100 images per second is impressive, but lacks context. It is based on 224x224 images. For the "real-time" claim to be fully convincing, it would be beneficial to understand the performance on larger, more realistic image sizes (e.g., 640x480 or HD resolutions). It is also unclear if the reported timing includes necessary pre-processing steps like image resizing.

2.  **Robustness to Object Scale:** The examples shown in the paper primarily feature large, centrally located objects. It is unclear how the model would perform on images where the salient object is very small. The fixed input size of 224x224 might be a limitation in such scenarios, as a small object could lose critical details after downsampling. An analysis of localization performance as a function of ground-truth bounding box size would be insightful.

3.  **Incomplete Comparison with the New Metric:** The new saliency metric introduced in Section 3.2 is a valuable contribution. However, Table 3, which presents results using this metric, does not include comparisons with all the other weakly supervised methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi [2]). Including these comparisons would provide a more complete picture of how the proposed method's interpretability stands against competing approaches under its own proposed metric.

### Originality

The idea of training a separate network to generate explanations for another model is novel and powerful. While related works like Fong & Vedaldi [2] also optimize a mask to perturb a black-box classifier, their approach is iterative and per-instance. This paper's main original contribution is to frame this as a learning problem, enabling the amortization of the optimization process over the training set and achieving fast inference. The paper is well-situated within the existing literature and clearly articulates its relationship to prior work.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of building intuition, particularly in Sections 2 and 3, where they discuss the concepts of Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR), Smallest Destroying Region (SDR), and the problem of "introduced evidence." The architecture and training process are described in sufficient detail.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Pro-acceptance):**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** Proposes a new paradigm for saliency map generation (learning a model vs. iterative optimization), enabling real-time performance.
*   **Strong Technical Contribution:** The objective function is well-designed, and the proposed saliency metric is a thoughtful addition to the field.
*   **Impressive Experimental Results:** Achieves state-of-the-art performance on the weakly supervised ImageNet localization task.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and provides excellent motivation and intuition for its design choices.

**Weaknesses (Con-acceptance):**
*   **Substantiation of Claims:** The "real-time" claim needs more detailed analysis regarding performance on larger images and the inclusion of pre-processing overhead.
*   **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The experiments do not sufficiently explore performance on challenging cases, such as images with very small objects.
*   **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The proposed saliency metric is not used to evaluate all competing methods, making a full comparison difficult.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and impactful idea. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and relate more to strengthening the evaluation rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. The work lays solid groundwork for a new class of fast saliency detectors and is likely to be influential. I recommend acceptance.

### Detailed Comments & Questions for Authors

*   Regarding the processing speed: Could you clarify if the 100 images/second figure includes the time for resizing input images to 224x224? What is the expected inference time for larger images, such as 640x480?
*   In Table 3, why were other methods from Table 2, particularly Fong & Vedaldi [2], not evaluated using your proposed saliency metric? A direct comparison would be very informative.
*   Have you analyzed the model's performance on images containing small salient objects? Does the initial downsampling to 224x224 pose a problem in such cases?
