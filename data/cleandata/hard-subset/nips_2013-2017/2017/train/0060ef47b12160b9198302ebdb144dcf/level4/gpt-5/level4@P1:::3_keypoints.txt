- Proposes a fast, model-agnostic single-pass saliency network (U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) that predicts masks conditioning on image and target class.  
- Uses class-embedding gated feature filtering and multi-scale upsampling to produce refined saliency masks.  
- Training objective combines total variation smoothness, small area penalty, high preserved-region probability (SSR-like) and low removed-region probability (SDR-like).  
- Mitigates adversarial masking artifacts by randomizing the alternative background (strong blur or noisy constant) during training and regularizing masks.  
- Introduces a saliency metric s(a,p)=log(ã)/log(p) based on area of the tightest enclosing rectangle and classifier probability on the resized crop.  
- Cropping (rather than masking) is used in the metric to discourage adversarial artifacts.  
- Experiments on ImageNet (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) show sharper, more interpretable maps than gradient/backprop and iterative perturbation methods, with improved weakly supervised localization.  
- CIFAR-10 experiments indicate the approach can be trained from scratch on low-resolution images.  
- Claims runtime >100 images/s at 224×224 on a standard GPU, enabling practical/real-time applications.  
- Implementation details (alternative image randomization, class-embedding gating, fake-label training) are sensible and well described.  
- Paper is well organized, clearly motivates SSR/SDR, and presents the method and discussion accessibly.  
- Main novelty is a learned single-pass masker approximating iterative perturbation outcomes for black-box classifiers, plus the combined SSR+SDR objective and new metric.  
- Significant for practicality and strong ImageNet localization, but real-time speed and applicability to higher-resolution, real-world images remain uncertain.  
- Pros: single-pass, model-agnostic, sharp interpretable masks; strong localization; thoughtful artifact handling; useful metric; demonstrates cross-architecture/dataset generality.  
- Cons: scalability untested beyond 224×224; unclear performance on small/rare objects occupying tiny image fractions; proposed metric not reported for all baseline methods in localization comparison.  
- Questions for authors: does throughput include resizing, and what are runtimes/images‑s for larger inputs (e.g., 640×480)?  
- Questions for authors: how does performance degrade for small target objects and would larger inputs materially help?  
- Questions for authors: can you report the proposed saliency metric for the same baselines used in Table 2 (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi) for direct comparison?  
- Overall: interesting, well-executed work with solid empirical evidence and a useful metric, but needs stronger validation of speed at higher resolutions and analysis on small-object cases.
