- Paper proposes a neural-network method for learning saliency masks that is computationally fast (several images/sec).
- Reviewer is neutral/borderline: supportive of the idea but not firmly for acceptance or rejection.
- No obvious technical errors but there are significant limitations that need discussion.
- Saliency is evaluated only indirectly via weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric, both of which have clear drawbacks.
- Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy because contextual cues can make localization disagree with true saliency.
- The proposed saliency metric has artifacts from cropping and resizing with aspect-ratio preservation, which can alter classification.
- The metric inherently favors small salient regions because aggressive upscaling changes scale presented to the classifier and exploits limited scale invariance of convnets.
- The paper does not address how dependent the mask-learning procedure is on the network architecture; experiments with alternate architectures are missing.
- Unclear whether results in Table 1 are computed over all classes or only the correct class.
- Please specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparisons.
- Post-rebuttal: the saliency metric’s reliance on classifier quality and scale invariance severely constrains applicability.
- Consequences: the method can’t be applied during model training, to models lacking scale invariance, to different domains (e.g., spectrograms), or to arbitrary black-box classifiers as implied.
- Strong dependence on the masking network raises doubt whether visualizations reflect the base (U-)network’s saliency or the masking network’s behavior.
- If these limitations are thoroughly discussed in the paper it could be acceptable; if not, the paper should not be published.
