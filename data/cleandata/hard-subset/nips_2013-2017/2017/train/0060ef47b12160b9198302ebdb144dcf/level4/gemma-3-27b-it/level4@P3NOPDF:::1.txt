## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

This paper proposes a neural network-based approach to learning saliency masks for visualizing the inner workings of deep neural networks. The method is notable for its speed, capable of processing multiple images per second. Overall, I find this work to be borderline; I would not strongly oppose either acceptance or rejection. The concept of learning to explain model behavior is valuable, and the paper presents several interesting ideas. However, significant limitations and caveats warrant careful consideration.

The primary concern lies in the indirect measurement of saliency. The paper relies on either weakly supervised localization or a proposed saliency metric, both of which suffer from inherent limitations that are not fully addressed. While the results demonstrating correlation with weakly supervised localization are intriguing, this approach is not a perfect proxy for saliency quality. Object localization alone doesn’t necessarily reflect which parts of the image are most salient for classification, particularly when contextual information is crucial for correct categorization. A localized object may be irrelevant without its surrounding context.

The proposed saliency metric also presents challenges. The cropping and rescaling procedure introduces potential artifacts. Altering the aspect ratio during rescaling could negatively impact classification performance. Moreover, the metric inherently favors smaller salient regions; the substantial upscaling required for re-classification may present the classifier with an object at a non-optimal scale. While convolutional networks exhibit translation invariance, scale invariance is a learned property and likely has limits. It’s unclear whether the observed performance is truly indicative of saliency or simply a consequence of the classifier’s scale invariance capabilities.

A critical omission is a discussion of the masking model’s sensitivity to architectural choices. Did the authors explore the impact of different network architectures on the learned saliency masks? Understanding this dependence is crucial for assessing the generalizability of the approach. 

**Specific Questions/Minor Comments:**

*   Table 1 presents results – are these scores aggregated across all classes, or computed only for the correctly classified instances? Clarification would be helpful.
*   When comparing against LRP, please specify the exact LRP variant and parameter settings used (e.g., epsilon value, alpha/beta parameters). LRP encompasses numerous variants and parameterizations, and precise details are essential for reproducibility and fair comparison.

**Post-Rebuttal Considerations:**

The authors’ response and further reflection highlight a fundamental limitation: the performance of the saliency metric is inextricably linked to the scale invariance and overall quality of the classifier being visualized. This significantly restricts the applicability of the proposed method. Specifically:

*   The technique cannot be applied during model training or to models that lack robust scale invariance.
*   This limitation extends to other domains where scale invariance might not be inherent in the underlying models (e.g., CNNs applied to spectrogram analysis).
*   The claim of general applicability to “black-box” classifiers, as stated in the title, is overstated and inaccurate given these constraints.

Furthermore, the rebuttal suggests a potentially strong dependence of the learned masks on the architecture of the masking network itself. This raises a fundamental question: are we visualizing the saliency of the original U-network or the masking network used to generate the masks? Without addressing this ambiguity, the interpretability of the results remains unclear.

In conclusion, if the paper is revised to thoroughly discuss these limitations – particularly the reliance on classifier scale invariance, the potential architectural dependence of the masking model, and the ambiguity in interpreting the visualized saliency – it could be considered balanced enough for publication. However, without a comprehensive acknowledgement and discussion of these issues, I believe publication is not warranted.
