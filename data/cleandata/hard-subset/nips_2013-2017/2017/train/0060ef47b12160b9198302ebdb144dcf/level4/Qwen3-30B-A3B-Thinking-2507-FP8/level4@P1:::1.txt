

This paper introduces a novel approach for real-time saliency detection using a trainable masking model that operates as a black-box interface to any differentiable classifier. The authors propose a single-forward-pass method that trains a U-Net-based network to predict saliency masks, bypassing iterative optimization. Key contributions include a new saliency metric based on tight crop localization and competitive results on ImageNet weakly supervised object localization (36.7% error vs. 34.3% for fully supervised VGG). The work builds on related efforts like Fong & Vedaldi (2017) and Zhou et al. (2016), but distinguishes itself through speed (100 masks/sec) and metric innovation.

**Strengths:**  
The paper demonstrates strong technical execution. The masking model achieves real-time performance (100+ images/sec on standard GPU), significantly outperforming iterative methods. The new saliency metric—evaluating tight-crop classification confidence—provides a meaningful alternative to standard localization accuracy, with results approaching ground-truth bounding boxes (Table 3). The architecture design (U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) is well-justified, and ablation studies (e.g., CIFAR-10 experiments without pre-training) validate generalizability. Results on ImageNet (Table 1) convincingly show superiority over weakly supervised baselines, including the "Center" and "Max Box" approaches.

**Weaknesses:**  
The core limitation lies in the saliency metric’s dependence on classifier scale invariance. As noted in the rebuttal, the metric resizes cropped regions to original dimensions *without* preserving aspect ratio, which risks misrepresenting classifier performance. This is particularly problematic for classifiers lacking strong scale invariance (e.g., those trained on non-square inputs or used during training). Consequently, the method’s applicability to "black-box classifiers" is overstated—its metric is only valid for models exhibiting the required invariance, excluding many practical scenarios (e.g., spectrogram analysis). The paper should explicitly acknowledge this constraint.

Furthermore, the masking model’s performance appears tightly coupled to the classifier architecture. The authors train a separate model per black-box classifier (AlexNet/GoogleNet/ResNet), yet fail to discuss whether the masking architecture itself introduces biases or whether its design (e.g., U-Net, ResNet encoder) is critical. The rebuttal hints at strong dependence on the masking network, raising the question: *Are we visualizing the classifier’s saliency or the masking model’s learned biases?* This ambiguity undermines the "black-box" claim. The paper should address this via ablation studies (e.g., varying masking architectures) or clearer discussion of model dependence.

**Balance and Recommendation:**  
While the approach is technically sound and delivers a useful real-time tool, the metric’s limitations and the unaddressed dependence on classifier properties significantly weaken the paper’s broader claims. The weakly supervised localization results are compelling *if* the metric is valid for the target classifiers, but the paper does not sufficiently justify this assumption. The rebuttal’s points about scale invariance and masking model bias are valid and must be resolved for the work to be broadly applicable. As written, the paper overstates its generality without adequate discussion of these constraints.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:*  
- Real-time saliency detection with strong empirical results (100+ fps, ImageNet localization error 36.7%).  
- Novel saliency metric that better assesses interpretability than localization accuracy alone.  
- Rigorous evaluation across multiple classifiers (AlexNet/GoogleNet/ResNet) and datasets (ImageNet/CIFAR-10).  

*Con:*  
- Saliency metric relies on classifier scale invariance, limiting applicability to specific architectures.  
- "Black-box" claim is undermined by masking model’s dependence on classifier architecture.  
- Critical limitations (metric constraints, model bias) are not adequately discussed.  

**Verdict:**  
The paper presents a valuable technical contribution to fast saliency detection but overreaches in its claims about general applicability. To merit acceptance, the authors must: (1) explicitly qualify the metric’s dependence on classifier properties, (2) address the masking model’s potential bias (e.g., via ablation studies), and (3) revise the "black-box" narrative to reflect these constraints. Without these revisions, the work’s significance is overstated. Given the technical merit and potential utility, I recommend *weak acceptance* contingent on addressing these points.
