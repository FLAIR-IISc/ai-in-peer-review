

This paper proposes a novel approach for fast saliency detection that trains a masking model to predict saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass, enabling real-time applications. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which evaluates saliency by cropping the most salient region and measuring classification confidence. The work builds on previous saliency methods such as gradient-based approaches [11, 12, 16] and iterative methods [1, 2, 17], addressing their limitations in speed and quality. Unlike previous optimization-based methods that require iterative computation [2], this model-agnostic approach achieves orders-of-magnitude speedup while producing sharper, more interpretable saliency maps.

Strengths:
- The paper presents a technically sound approach that achieves significant speed improvements (100+ saliency masks per second) compared to iterative methods while maintaining high-quality saliency maps.
- The proposed saliency metric provides a more meaningful evaluation of saliency quality than standard localization accuracy, as it directly assesses whether the salient region alone enables confident classification.
- The method outperforms existing weakly supervised approaches on ImageNet object localization (36.7% error rate for ResNet-50 trained model vs. 34.3% for fully supervised VGG).
- The architecture is well-designed, using a U-Net with ResNet-50 as encoder to produce precise masks, and the training procedure includes thoughtful optimizations to prevent overfitting.

Weaknesses:
- The saliency metric's effectiveness is heavily dependent on the classifier's scale invariance, which limits the method's applicability to classifiers that lack this property. This is a significant limitation since many classifiers (especially during training) do not exhibit strong scale invariance.
- The paper does not adequately discuss how the masking model's performance depends on the architecture of the black-box classifier, which is critical to understanding whether the saliency maps reflect the classifier's behavior or the masking model's biases.
- The weakly supervised localization metric has inherent limitations: when contextual information is essential for classification, object localization accuracy may not correlate with saliency quality.
- The metric's implementation (cropping and rescaling the salient region) introduces potential artifacts due to aspect ratio changes and scale distortion, which could impact classification confidence in ways not properly addressed.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper introduces a valuable real-time saliency detection method that addresses a practical need in computer vision applications.
- The technical contribution is significant in terms of speed and quality improvements over existing methods.
- The new saliency metric provides a meaningful alternative to standard localization accuracy for evaluating saliency quality.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper's claim of being applicable to "any differentiable image classifier" is overstated due to the dependence on scale invariance.
- The limitation regarding classifier scale invariance significantly restricts the method's applicability to real-world scenarios where this property may not hold.
- The paper does not sufficiently address the potential for the masking model to introduce its own biases rather than accurately reflecting the black-box classifier's behavior.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and interesting contribution to the field of model interpretability, with clear practical value for real-time applications. However, the method's dependence on specific properties of the classifier (scale invariance) and the lack of discussion regarding the masking model's relationship to the classifier architecture represent significant limitations that need to be addressed before the paper can be considered for publication. The authors should clarify these limitations and provide a more nuanced discussion of the method's applicability.
