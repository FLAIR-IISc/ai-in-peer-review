The paper proposes an approach to learn saliency masks, leveraging a neural network that can process multiple images per second – demonstrating a significant speed advantage.

I find this paper to be borderline; I would not strongly object to either rejection or acceptance. I strongly believe in the concept of learning to explain model behavior, and this paper presents some worthwhile ideas. 

While there are no obvious errors, the work does have clear limitations. 

– The measurement of saliency is indirect. Whether assessed through weakly supervised localization or the proposed saliency metric, both methods have inherent limitations that should be discussed more thoroughly in the paper.

The use of weakly supervised localization as a proxy measure is imperfect. If an object’s context is crucial for class determination, object localization may not accurately reflect saliency quality. The results obtained using weakly supervised localization are interesting, but there’s a significant caveat when interpreting them as a reliable metric for saliency.

The proposed saliency metric also has weaknesses due to its implementation. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size, maintaining the original aspect ratio. This could introduce two artifacts. First, altering the aspect ratio may negatively impact classification performance. Second, the metric implicitly favors smaller salient regions. Because these smaller regions are significantly magnified during rescaling for re-classification, the presented scale may not be optimal for the classifier (while ConvNets exhibit translation invariance, scale invariance is learned and likely has limits). 

The paper does not address how much the performance of the masking model depends on the underlying network architecture. Did the authors experiment with different architectures and, if so, how did those choices influence the results?

Minor comments:

*   Are the results in Table 1 obtained for all classes or only the correctly classified class?
*   Please specify the LRP variant and parameter settings used for comparison. LRP has numerous variants with parameters like epsilon and alpha-beta.

Post-rebuttal edit:

The strong dependence of the saliency metric on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier significantly limits the applicability of the proposed method. It can only be reliably applied to networks possessing these properties. 

This has important consequences:

*   The method cannot be used to analyze models during the training phase, nor can it be applied to models that lack this inherent invariance.
*   This restricts its applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs).
*   The method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers, contrary to the claim in the title.

Furthermore, the response suggests a strong dependence on the masking network itself. As a result, it’s unclear whether the visualizations represent the saliency of the underlying U-network or the masking network.

If these effects are properly discussed and contextualized within the paper, I believe it is balanced enough for publication. Otherwise, I would recommend against accepting it.
