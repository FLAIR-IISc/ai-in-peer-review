This paper presents an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing a hundred 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The approach trains a masking model to find the tightest rectangular crop containing the entire salient region of a specified class, guided by a black-box classifier such as AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet. The model architecture leverages image feature maps – specifically those generated by ResNet-50 – at multiple scales. The highest-resolution scale feature map is passed through a feature filter for initial localization, and subsequent upsampling blocks refine the resulting masks. Experiments demonstrate that this method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task. 

The paper includes sufficient references and a thorough discussion of related work (though a complete check was not performed). The technical aspects of the paper appear sound (again, without a complete verification). The authors present a number of insightful intuitions and justifications for their design choices. The paper’s presentation is clear and well-organized. 

Overall, this paper presents interesting technical results. However, I have some concerns regarding the claimed real-time speed and the generalizability to real-world images.

Comments:

- Does the reported processing time of 100 images per second include the image resizing operation? If so, what is the running time for larger images, such as 640x480 images typically captured by an iPhone 6s?
- The salient objects considered in this paper appear relatively large. How does the method perform when the requested class object is small within the image? Would a 224x224 input image size be sufficient in such cases?
- In Table 3, are there corresponding metrics available for other works listed in Table 2, such as Feed [2]?
