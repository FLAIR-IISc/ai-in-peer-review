This paper proposes a method for learning a silence mask. The proposed method is based on a neural network and can process multiple images per second (i.e., it is fast).

I find this paper to be of moderate quality; I would not object to its rejection or acceptance. I truly believe in the concept of learning to explain a model, and I think this paper contains some good ideas.

There are no obvious flaws here, but there are some limitations.

- Silence is only indirectly measured. With weak supervision, both the localization or proposed silence metric have clear limitations, and I think these limitations should be discussed in the paper.

Weak supervision is not perfect as a localization measure. If the context in which an object is displayed is essential for determining its class, there is no requirement for the quality of silence to be related to the object's localization. Weak supervision localization results are interesting, but there is a large catalog for using them as a silence metric.

The silence metric is not perfect because of how it is applied. An approximate salient region is cropped. This crop is then rescaled to the size of the original image, where the original shape ratio is maintained. This can create two flaws. Firstly, the change in shape ratio can affect how well the classification can be done. Secondly, the proposed metric prioritizes a small salient region. Since a small region is greatly enlarged for reclassification, the scale at which the object is presented to the classifier may not be ideal. (Convolutions are generally translation invariant, but scaling invariance must be learned and there are probably some limitations.)

What is not discussed here is how much the masking model depends on the architecture for learning the mask. Did the authors test different architectures at some point, and how did this affect the results?

Post-regent edit
How well the silence metric works depends on the quality of the classifier and the scaling invariance, which strongly limits the applicability of the proposed method. It can only be applied to networks with this invariance.

This has important consequences:
- This method cannot be used for models in the training phase, or for models that do not exhibit this invariance.
- It limits its applicability in other domains (such as spectrogram analysis with CNN).
- The title claims that this method is generally not applicable to black-box classifiers.

Furthermore, the response indicates a strong dependence on the masking network.
- Consequently, it is not clear to me whether we are imagining the silence of a U-network or the silence of a masking network.

If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, I think it is balanced enough for publication. If not, it should not be published.
