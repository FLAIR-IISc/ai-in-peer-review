This paper presents a method for learning saliency masks using a neural network. The proposed approach is computationally efficient, capable of processing multiple images per second.

My assessment of this paper is borderline; I could support either acceptance or rejection. The core concept of learning-based explainability is compelling, and the manuscript contains several promising ideas. While the paper is free of obvious errors, it has significant limitations that require attention.

- Saliency is evaluated indirectly, using either weakly supervised localization or a novel saliency metric. Both evaluation strategies have considerable limitations that the authors should discuss in the paper.
The weakly supervised localization task is an imperfect proxy for saliency quality. For instance, if contextual cues are essential for classification, the most salient regions may not perfectly align with the object's bounding box. While the localization results are noteworthy, their use as a saliency quality metric has this significant caveat.
The proposed saliency metric is also flawed due to its application. Cropping the estimated salient region and rescaling it to the original image dimensions can introduce artifacts. First, altering the aspect ratio could negatively affect classification performance. Second, the metric inherently favors smaller salient regions. A small crop that is significantly upscaled for re-classification may present the object at a scale for which the classifier is not robust, given that scale invariance in CNNs is learned and has its limits.

- The manuscript lacks a discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of architecture for the mask-generating network. It is unclear if the authors experimented with different architectures and how such choices would influence the final saliency maps.

**Minor Comments**
- In Table 1, please clarify whether the reported results are averaged over all classes or calculated only for the ground-truth class.
- For the LRP comparison, the specific variant (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) and its corresponding parameter settings should be specified.

**Post-Rebuttal Edit**
The rebuttal reinforces a critical concern: the proposed saliency metric's effectiveness is contingent upon the classifier's inherent scale invariance. This dependency severely restricts the method's applicability, as it is only reliable for networks that already possess this property.

This has several important consequences:
- The method is unsuitable for evaluating models during their training phase or any model that has not developed strong scale invariance.
- This limits its utility in other domains where CNNs are applied, such as spectrogram analysis, where scale may have a different physical meaning.
- Consequently, the claim in the title that the method is generally applicable to "black-box" classifiers is not supported, as it imposes a strong prerequisite on the model's properties.

Furthermore, the response suggests a significant dependency on the masking network's architecture. This raises a fundamental question: is the generated map visualizing the saliency of the original classifier, or is it merely reflecting the biases of the mask-generating network itself?

The paper could be considered for publication only if these limitations and their implications are thoroughly and transparently discussed. Without such a discussion, the paper is not ready for publication.
