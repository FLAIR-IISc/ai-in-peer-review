The paper proposes a novel approach to learn saliency masks for image classifiers. The core idea is to train a neural network to predict salient regions, enabling fast saliency detection – processing multiple images per second. This contrasts with iterative methods, which are computationally expensive.

The paper is borderline; I would not object to either acceptance or rejection. I believe in the concept of learning to explain a model and think the paper presents some good ideas. While there are no obvious technical mistakes, clear limitations exist.

The evaluation of saliency is indirect, relying on either weakly supervised localization or the proposed saliency metric. Both methods have limitations that should be discussed more thoroughly in the paper. The weakly supervised localization task isn’t a perfect proxy for saliency quality; if contextual information is crucial for classification, localization accuracy doesn’t necessarily correlate with the saliency of the object itself. The reported localization results are interesting, but this caveat should be explicitly acknowledged.

The proposed saliency metric also has potential issues due to its application. The estimated salient region is cropped and then resized to the original image dimensions, potentially distorting the aspect ratio. This resizing could introduce artifacts: changes in aspect ratio might affect classification performance, and the emphasis on small salient regions—while conceptually appealing—could be problematic. Enlarging a small region for re-classification presents the classifier with a scale that might not be ideal, even considering the translation invariance of CNNs, scaling invariance is learned and has limits.

An important aspect not discussed is the masking model's dependence on the underlying classifier’s architecture. Were different architectures experimented with, and how did this influence the results? Understanding this dependency is crucial.

Minor comments:
* Are the results in Table 1 obtained for all classes or only for the correct class?
* Please specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparison, as LRP has multiple variants with different parameters (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta).

Post-rebuttal edit:
The strong dependence of the saliency metric’s effectiveness on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier significantly limits the method’s applicability. It can only be reliably applied to networks exhibiting these properties. 

This limitation has important consequences:
* The method cannot be used to understand models *during* training, nor can it be used for models that lack scale or rotation invariance.
* This restricts the method’s applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs).
* The method is not as generally applicable to black-box classifiers as claimed in the title.

Furthermore, the rebuttal suggests a strong dependence of the masking model on the underlying classifier. This raises the question: are we visualizing the saliency of the original classifier, or the saliency *as learned by the masking network*?

If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, I believe it is balanced enough for publication. Otherwise, it should not be published.



---

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to fast saliency detection.
*   Achieves competitive results on weakly supervised localization.
*   The proposed saliency metric offers a new perspective on evaluating explanations.
*   Demonstrates applicability to different datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The reliance on classifier scale/rotation invariance is a significant limitation.
*   The indirect assessment of saliency quality (via localization or the proposed metric) is potentially flawed.
*   The potential for the masking network to learn its own biases, rather than accurately reflecting the underlying classifier’s behavior, is a concern.
*   Lack of discussion regarding the masking model’s architectural sensitivity.
