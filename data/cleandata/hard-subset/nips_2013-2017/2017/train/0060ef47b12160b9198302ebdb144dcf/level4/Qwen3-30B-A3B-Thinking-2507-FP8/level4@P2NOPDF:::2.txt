

This paper presents a novel neural network architecture for predicting salient image regions critical to another network's object categorization performance. The writing is clear and well-structured, with compelling results demonstrating superior performance over prior methods on challenging benchmarks using state-of-the-art networks. The experimental validation across diverse datasets underscores the method's robustness and practical relevance.

To further strengthen the contribution, the authors should more explicitly motivate real-time applications where this capability is essential. While the approach enables efficient inference, identifying specific scenarios—such as autonomous driving, robotic navigation, or real-time video analysis—would better justify the design choices and broaden the work's impact. A brief discussion of computational trade-offs (e.g., latency vs. accuracy) in these contexts would add significant value.

Additionally, the paper would benefit from deeper analysis of the learned salient regions. Beyond reporting accuracy metrics, examining whether the model leverages background biases (e.g., common contextual cues or foreground-background correlations) for object recognition could provide critical insights. For instance, do certain object categories consistently rely on specific background features, and how might this relate to known dataset biases? Such analysis would not only validate the model's interpretability but also inform future work on robustness to spurious correlations.

The technical foundation is sound, and the results are well-supported. However, the current presentation lacks sufficient discussion of these aspects. Addressing them would elevate the paper from a strong empirical contribution to a more insightful theoretical-practical advance. The authors should consider incorporating these elements to clarify the method's broader significance and limitations.
