- Paper proposes a neural-network method for learning saliency masks and achieves promising inference speed (multiple images/sec).
- Reviewer is borderline on acceptance—supportive of the core idea but notes clear limitations.
- Primary concern: saliency is measured indirectly via weakly supervised localization, which may not reflect true saliency when context matters.
- The proposed saliency metric has flaws: cropping/rescaling (aspect-ratio changes) can introduce artifacts that harm classification.
- The metric biases toward smaller salient regions; heavy upscaling can present objects at non-ideal scales given limited scale invariance of CNNs.
- Strong dependence of the metric on classifier quality and scale invariance limits applicability (unsuitable during training, for non-scale-invariant models, and some domains like spectrograms).
- Claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers is unsupported given the metric’s classifier-dependent requirements.
- Unclear whether visualizations reflect the underlying classifier’s saliency or artifacts of the masking network itself—dependence on the masking network needs investigation.
- Paper does not study the effect of different underlying network architectures on learned masks; this should be explored.
- Minor requests: clarify whether Table 1 reports results for all classes or only correctly classified classes.
- Minor requests: specify exact LRP variant and parameters used (epsilon, alpha-beta, etc.).
- If the authors thoroughly discuss and address these limitations, the paper could be acceptable; otherwise the reviewer recommends rejection.
