### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel method for generating saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier, which the authors treat as a black box. The core contribution is to train a separate, dedicated neural network—a "masking model" with a U-Net architecture—to produce these saliency maps. Unlike iterative optimization-based methods that generate a mask for each image individually, this approach learns a general function that can produce a mask in a single, fast forward pass.

The masking model is trained to minimize an objective function composed of four terms, designed to produce masks that are smooth, small (low area), and effective at both preserving the classifier's confidence on the masked-in region (a "smallest sufficient region" objective) and destroying it on the masked-out region (a "smallest destroying region" objective). The authors evaluate their method on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, using a variety of black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50). They demonstrate that their method achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task. Furthermore, they propose a new saliency metric to assess the interpretability of the maps, based on cropping the salient region and measuring the classifier's performance on the resized crop.

This work positions itself as a new class of saliency detectors that are significantly faster than iterative methods like Fong and Vedaldi (2017), while producing sharper and more accurate maps than gradient-based methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of training a separate generative model to produce explanations for a black-box classifier is a significant and novel contribution. This "learning to explain" paradigm is a departure from existing iterative or backpropagation-based approaches and could inspire a new line of research in model interpretability.

2.  **Performance and Speed:** A major advantage of the proposed method is its speed. By amortizing the optimization process into a single forward pass of a neural network, the method can generate saliency maps in real-time. This is a crucial practical benefit over iterative methods that require hundreds of optimization steps per image, making applications like video saliency analysis feasible.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method achieves excellent results on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, outperforming previous weakly supervised techniques. This provides strong quantitative evidence that the generated saliency maps effectively capture the location of key objects.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is well-established, the proposed method and its objective function are explained in detail, and the experimental setup is sound.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

Despite the promising direction, the paper has several significant limitations that should be addressed. The primary concerns relate to the evaluation of saliency and the unstated assumptions that challenge the method's claimed generality.

1.  **Limitations of the Evaluation Metrics:** Saliency is notoriously difficult to evaluate, and the proxies used in this paper have important caveats that are not discussed.
    *   **Weakly Supervised Localization:** While the localization results are strong, this metric is not a perfect proxy for saliency. A classifier might use crucial contextual information (e.g., water for a "ship") that lies outside the object's bounding box. A good saliency map should highlight such context, but this would be penalized by a localization metric. The potential mismatch between saliency and localization should be acknowledged.
    *   **Proposed Saliency Metric:** The new metric, `s(a, p)`, is an interesting concept but rests on a critical and strong assumption: that the black-box classifier is largely invariant to significant changes in object scale and aspect ratio. The process of taking a tight crop (which can be very small and have an arbitrary aspect ratio) and resizing it to the classifier's required input dimensions can drastically alter the input. This could introduce artifacts or present the object at a scale or distortion level that the classifier is not robust to. This dependence severely limits the claim of the method being generally applicable to any "black box classifier," as it is only suitable for models that happen to possess this specific invariance.

2.  **Confounding Inductive Biases of the Masking Model:** The paper does not address a crucial question: to what extent do the generated masks reflect the behavior of the target black-box classifier, versus the inductive biases of the powerful masking model itself? The masking model employs a pre-trained ResNet-50 within a U-Net architecture, which has its own strong, pre-existing knowledge of what constitutes an object. It is plausible that the masking network is a better object detector than the target classifier, and the generated masks reflect the masking network's priors more than they explain the target's decision-making process. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study showing how the choice of masking architecture affects the saliency maps for a fixed black-box model.

3.  **Overstated Generality:** Stemming from the points above, the claims of the method being "black box" and generally applicable are overstated. The strong, unstated requirement of scale/aspect-ratio invariance in the target classifier (for the proposed metric to be valid) and the potential for the masking model to introduce its own biases mean the method is not as universally applicable as the title and abstract suggest. These constraints should be clearly stated and discussed. For example, the method would likely be unsuitable for domains like spectrogram analysis where arbitrary rescaling is meaningless.

**Minor Comments**

*   In Table 1, it should be clarified whether the localization error is calculated only for images where the black-box classifier predicts the correct class.

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

This paper presents a novel, fast, and promising approach for generating saliency maps by training a dedicated explanation model. The idea is original and the performance on the object localization task is impressive.

However, the work is undermined by a lack of discussion around the significant limitations of its evaluation methodology and the unstated assumptions that challenge its generality. The reliance on the target classifier's scale/aspect-ratio invariance is a major constraint that is not acknowledged. Furthermore, the potential for the masking model's own biases to confound the explanation is a critical issue that is not explored.

My overall assessment is **borderline**. I am enthusiastic about the core idea, but the current manuscript overstates its claims of generality without adequately discussing the associated caveats. If the authors were to revise the paper to thoroughly discuss these limitations—specifically, the required invariances of the target classifier and the potential for confounding biases from the masking network's architecture—I believe it would represent a more balanced and valuable scientific contribution. Without such a discussion, the work feels incomplete and potentially misleading.
