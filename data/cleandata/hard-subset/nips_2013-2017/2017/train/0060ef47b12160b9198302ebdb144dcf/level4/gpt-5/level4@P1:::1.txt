Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a learned, feed-forward masking model that produces class-conditional saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier. Instead of optimizing a mask per image (as in perturbation-based methods), the authors train a U-Net–style network (with a ResNet-50 encoder) to output a saliency mask in a single pass, guided by a loss that balances total variation/sparsity with two fidelity terms: keeping the class score high on the preserved region and low when the salient region is removed. The approach targets both SDR and SSR notions of saliency. The method is evaluated on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, shows competitive weakly supervised localization, and is fast enough for real-time use. The paper also proposes a new saliency metric that crops to the salient region and evaluates the class probability on the resized crop.

The paper relates to earlier gradient/backprop-based visualization (e.g., Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop) and to optimization-based perturbation methods such as Fong & Vedaldi’s meaningful perturbations and Cao et al.’s activation-preservation approach. The main novelty is to train a dedicated masking model to amortize the perturbation search while targeting both SSR and SDR.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing: learning to explain a black-box classifier via a separate masking network, amortizing the costly per-image optimization.
- Fast: single-pass masks at >100 fps for 224×224 inputs, enabling real-time and video applications.
- Empirically strong: sharper, more interpretable masks than baselines; improved weakly supervised localization on ImageNet; reasonable performance on CIFAR-10 without pretraining.
- Thoughtful objective: addresses adversarial artifacts via TV regularization and randomized evidence replacement; combines preservation and deletion terms.
- New metric: attempts to operationalize SSR through a crop-based probability/area trade-off.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Evaluation is indirect. Both weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric are only proxies for “explanation quality.” Localization can correlate poorly with saliency when contextual cues are essential for recognition. The authors should discuss these limitations more explicitly.
- Saliency metric confounds scaling and aspect ratio. The metric resizes the salient crop to the classifier’s input size while ignoring aspect ratio. Two issues follow:
  1) Aspect ratio changes may systematically affect classification.
  2) The metric favors small regions; small crops are heavily upscaled, potentially placing the object at scales the classifier handles poorly. Convnets are not intrinsically scale-invariant; invariance must be learned and may be limited.
- Dependence on classifier invariances. The usefulness of the proposed metric (and arguably some conclusions) hinges on the classifier’s robustness to scale/aspect changes. This restricts applicability:
  - Not suitable for models during training or for architectures lacking sufficient scale invariance.
  - Limits extension to other domains (e.g., spectrogram-based CNNs) where invariances differ.
  - Undercuts the claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers.
- Dependence on masking network architecture. The approach appears sensitive to the U-Net design and encoder choice. It is unclear how much the results depend on the specific architecture and whether the learned masks reflect properties of the black-box classifier or biases of the masking model. More ablations would help.
- Clarity gaps. Some implementation details are under-specified (e.g., LRP variant/parameters if used as a baseline; details about class selection in evaluation).

Quality
The method is technically sound and thoughtfully engineered, with reasonable regularization against adversarial artifacts. However, the evaluation strongly relies on proxies (localization and the crop-based metric) that are known to be imperfect measures of saliency quality. The paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of failure modes and of how classifier invariances influence the metric.

Clarity
Overall readable and well-structured, but the limitations of the evaluation, the dependence on classifier invariance, and the architectural sensitivity of the masking model should be discussed. Please clarify baseline details (e.g., LRP variant/parameters) and evaluation protocols.

Originality
Training a feed-forward masking model that targets both preservation and deletion objectives is a meaningful extension of perturbation-based methods. The proposed crop-based metric is a novel attempt to quantify SSR, though it has the caveats noted above.

Significance
If the limitations are addressed and the method’s dependence on invariances and architecture is better characterized, this could be a useful practical tool for fast saliency, including video. As it stands, the claims of generality are stronger than what the evaluation supports.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Table 1: Are localization results computed for the ground-truth class only, or across all predicted classes?
- Which LRP variant and parameters were used? (epsilon, alpha-beta, etc.)
- How sensitive are results to the masking network architecture (encoder choice, depth, skip connections)? Any ablations?
- Can you quantify how the crop-based metric varies with controlled changes in object scale/aspect ratio for a fixed image/classifier?

Pros for acceptance
- Fast, practical approach with strong qualitative and quantitative results.
- Clear improvement over iterative perturbation baselines in speed and interpretability.
- Thoughtful objective mitigating adversarial artifacts.

Cons for acceptance
- Evaluation relies on proxies with known limitations; metric depends on classifier scale/aspect invariance.
- Generality claims (black-box applicability) are overstated given these dependencies.
- Limited analysis of architectural dependence and potential biases of the masking network.

Post-rebuttal update
The method’s reliance on classifier scale/aspect invariance substantially limits applicability: it cannot be reliably used during training or with models lacking such invariance, and generalization to other domains (e.g., spectrograms) is unclear. This also weakens the “generally applicable to black-box classifiers” claim. The rebuttal also suggests a strong dependence on the masking network, raising ambiguity about whether the masks reflect the black-box model’s saliency or biases of the masking network. If these limitations and dependencies are explicitly and thoroughly discussed in the paper, I would view it as borderline-accept; without that, I lean toward rejection.
