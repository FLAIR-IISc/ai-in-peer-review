The paper proposes a neural-network approach for learning saliency masks and achieves high throughput (processing multiple images per second). I view the paper as borderline; I would not object to either acceptance or rejection. I strongly support the goal of learning to explain models and find several ideas promising. I see no obvious technical errors, but there are important limitations that should be addressed.

Main concerns
- Saliency is evaluated only indirectly, via weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric; both have significant limitations that warrant deeper discussion in the paper.
  - Weakly supervised localization is not a faithful proxy for saliency. When contextual cues are essential for classification, object localization may not correlate with true saliency. The reported localization results are interesting, but using them as a quality metric for saliency comes with a major caveat.
  - The proposed saliency metric may be biased by its application procedure. The salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size (preserving the original aspect ratio). This introduces two issues: (1) aspect ratio changes can affect classification accuracy; (2) the metric implicitly favors small masks, which get heavily upscaled, potentially presenting objects at scales the classifier was not trained for. While CNNs can be translation-invariant, scale invariance must be learned and is limited, so the metric may reward degenerate solutions.
- Architectural dependence is not discussed. How sensitive is the masking model to architectural choices? Did the authors try alternative architectures for the masking network or the base classifier, and how did this affect mask quality and the metrics?

Minor comments
- Clarify whether Table 1 reports results for all classes or only the ground-truth class.
- Specify precisely which LRP variant and parameter settings were used (e.g., epsilon rule, alpha–beta, and the chosen parameter values).

Post-rebuttal
The utility of the saliency metric appears to depend strongly on the classifier’s quality and its scale invariance. This substantially limits applicability:
- The method cannot reliably be used to analyze models during training or models that lack sufficient scale invariance.
- Applicability to other domains (e.g., CNNs on spectrograms) is likely constrained.
- The approach is therefore not generally applicable to arbitrary black-box classifiers, contrary to the title’s claim.

Moreover, the rebuttal suggests a strong dependence on the masking network. It is therefore unclear whether the visualizations reflect the saliency of the U-network or the biases of the masking network itself.

Overall assessment
If the paper explicitly discusses these limitations, qualifies claims about black-box generality, and analyzes architectural sensitivity, I would lean toward acceptance. Without these clarifications and caveats, I would recommend rejection.
