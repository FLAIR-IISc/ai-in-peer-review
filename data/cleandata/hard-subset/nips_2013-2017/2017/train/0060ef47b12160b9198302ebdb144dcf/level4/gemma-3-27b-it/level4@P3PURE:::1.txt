The paper proposes an approach to learn saliency masks using a neural network, achieving a processing speed of multiple images per second. 

This paper is borderline; I would not object to either rejection or acceptance. I strongly believe in the concept of learning to explain model behavior, and the paper presents some good ideas. While there are no obvious errors, the work has clear limitations.

A primary concern is the indirect measurement of saliency. Whether assessed through weakly supervised localization or the proposed saliency metric, both methods have inherent limitations that should be thoroughly discussed. The weakly supervised localization approach is not a perfect proxy for saliency quality. If the context surrounding an object is crucial for class determination, object localization may not reliably correlate with its saliency. The results regarding weakly supervised localization are interesting, but their use as a definitive quality metric for saliency is questionable. 

The proposed saliency metric also has potential drawbacks due to its implementation. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size, maintaining the original aspect ratio. This process could introduce artifacts: altering the aspect ratio might negatively impact classification performance, and the metric inherently favors small salient regions. Because these small regions are significantly upscaled for re-classification, the resulting scale at which the object is presented to the classifier may be suboptimal. While convolutional neural networks generally exhibit translation invariance, scaling invariance must be learned, and likely has limitations.

The paper does not discuss how the performance of the masking model depends on the underlying network architecture. Did the authors explore different architectures and quantify their influence on the results? 

Minor comments:
*   Are the results in Table 1 obtained for all classes, or only for the correctly classified class?
*   Please specify the LRP variant and parameter settings used for comparison, as several exist with varying parameters (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta).

Post-rebuttal edit:
The strong dependence of the saliency metric’s effectiveness on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier severely limits the method’s applicability. It can only be reliably applied to networks possessing these properties. 

This limitation has important consequences:
*   The method cannot be used to analyze models during their training phase, or models that do not exhibit scale invariance.
*   This limits its applicability to other domains (e.g., spectrogram analysis with CNNs).
*   The method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers, contrary to the claim in the title.

Furthermore, the response suggests a considerable dependence on the masking network itself. As a result, it is unclear whether the visualizations represent the saliency of the original U-network or the masking network. 

If these effects are adequately addressed in the paper, I believe it would be balanced enough for publication. Otherwise, I recommend rejection.
