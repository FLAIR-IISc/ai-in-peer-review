This paper proposes a novel approach to learning saliency masks for image classification models. The method is based on a neural network that can process multiple images per second, making it suitable for real-time applications. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the CIFAR-10 and ImageNet datasets, achieving state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization.

The paper is well-motivated, and the idea of learning to explain a model is compelling. However, there are some limitations and potential issues that need to be addressed. Firstly, the saliency is only indirectly measured, either through weakly supervised localization or by the proposed saliency metric. Both of these methods have limitations, and it would be beneficial to discuss these limitations in the paper.

The weakly supervised localization metric is not perfect, as it may not always correlate with saliency quality. The context in which an object appears can be essential to determine its class, and object localization may not capture this. While the results on weakly supervised localization are interesting, it is crucial to acknowledge the potential caveats when using them as a quality metric for saliency.

The proposed saliency metric also has limitations. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size, which could introduce artifacts. The change in aspect ratio might impact how well the classifier can recognize the object, and the scaling invariance of the classifier may not be ideal. Since the method relies on the classifier being scale-invariant, it is essential to discuss the limitations of this assumption.

Another critical aspect that needs to be explored is the dependence of the masking model on the architecture for learning the masks. Did the authors experiment with different architectures, and how did this influence the results? It is crucial to understand the robustness of the method to different architectural choices.

Minor comments include specifying the results in Table 1, which are obtained for all classes or only for the correct class. Additionally, it would be beneficial to specify the LRP variant and parameter setting used for comparison.

In the post-rebuttal edit, it is mentioned that the saliency metric's effectiveness depends on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier. This has significant consequences, including limiting the applicability of the proposed method to networks with scale invariance. The method cannot be used for models during the training phase or for models that do not exhibit this invariance. This limitation also affects the applicability of the method to other domains, such as spectrogram analysis with CNNs. Furthermore, the method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers as claimed in the title.

The response also hints at a strong dependence on the masking network. As a result, it is unclear whether the method is visualizing the saliency of the U-network or the masking network. If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, it would be more balanced and suitable for publication.
