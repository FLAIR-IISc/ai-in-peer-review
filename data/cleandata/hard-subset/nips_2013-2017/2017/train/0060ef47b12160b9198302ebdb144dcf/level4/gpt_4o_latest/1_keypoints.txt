- Introduces a neural-network method for learning saliency masks that is computationally efficient (multiple images/sec).  
- Reviewer is borderline: neither strongly for acceptance nor rejection; finds the idea promising.  
- Main limitation: saliency is evaluated only indirectly (weakly supervised localization or proposed metric), both with clear shortcomings that must be addressed.  
- Weakly supervised localization is a flawed saliency proxy because contextual cues may be necessary for class prediction, so localization need not reflect saliency quality.  
- The proposed saliency metric has artifacts: cropping/rescaling can change aspect ratio and favor small regions, and CNNs are not perfectly scale-invariant.  
- Unclear dependence on masking-model architecture—no reported experiments showing how architecture choices affect results.  
- Minor: clarify whether Table 1 results are reported for all classes or only the true class.  
- Minor: state which LRP variant and parameter settings were used (e.g., epsilon vs. alpha-beta).  
- Post-rebuttal: metric effectiveness depends heavily on classifier quality and scale invariance, limiting applicability.  
- Post-rebuttal: method cannot be applied during model training, to models lacking scale invariance, or reliably to other domains (e.g., spectrograms), and is not truly applicable to black-box classifiers as claimed.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors appear to rely strongly on the masking network, leaving it unclear whether visualized saliency reflects the U-network or the masking network.  
- Overall: paper could be acceptable if these limitations are thoroughly discussed; otherwise it should not be accepted.
