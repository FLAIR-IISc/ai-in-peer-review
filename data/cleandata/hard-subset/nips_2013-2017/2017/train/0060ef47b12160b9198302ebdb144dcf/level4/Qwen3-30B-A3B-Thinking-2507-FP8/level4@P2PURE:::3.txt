

This paper proposes an image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing 100 images of size 224×224 per second on a standard GPU. The approach trains a masking model that identifies the tightest rectangular crop containing the entire salient region for a specified class, as determined by a black-box classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet). The model architecture utilizes image feature maps (such as those from ResNet-50) across multiple scales. The final scale feature is processed by a feature filter for initial localization, followed by upsampling blocks that refine the generated masks. Experiments demonstrate that the method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task.  

The paper includes sufficient references and related work. The technical correctness appears sound. The paper presents several intuitive design discussions and offers clear presentation.  

Overall, the paper presents interesting technical results, though I am concerned about the real-time speed claim and the applicability to real-world images.  

Comments:  
- Does the 100 images/second processing time include image resizing? If so, what is the runtime for larger images (e.g., 640×480 from an iPhone 6s)?  
- The salient objects in the paper are relatively large; how does the method handle cases where the requested class object is small in the image? Is 224×224 resolution sufficient?  
- In Table 3, are there corresponding metrics for other works cited in Table 2 (e.g., Feed [2])?
