

The paper presents a neural network-based approach for learning saliency masks with real-time processing capabilities (multiple images per second). The core idea—learning to explain model predictions via saliency—holds conceptual merit, and the method demonstrates technical competence. However, significant limitations in the evaluation framework and methodological scope prevent this work from meeting NeurIPS publication standards.  

The primary concern lies in the indirect and flawed measurement of saliency quality. The authors rely on two metrics: weakly supervised localization (WSL) and a proposed saliency metric involving cropped/rescaled regions. Both suffer from critical flaws that undermine their validity as quality indicators.  

First, WSL is inappropriate as a saliency metric when classification depends on contextual features. For instance, if an object’s class is determined by surrounding elements (e.g., a "zebra" in a savanna), the object’s location may not correlate with saliency relevance. The WSL results are interesting but misleading when used to evaluate saliency, as they conflate localization accuracy with model interpretability.  

Second, the saliency metric introduces artifacts via its rescaling procedure. The salient region is cropped, then rescaled to the original image dimensions while preserving aspect ratio. This creates two issues: (1) Aspect ratio distortion may alter the object’s visual representation, affecting classifier performance; (2) Small salient regions are significantly enlarged during rescaling, placing the object at unnatural scales for the classifier. While CNNs exhibit some translation invariance, scale invariance is not guaranteed and must be learned—making the metric’s preference for small regions potentially misleading. Crucially, the metric’s validity depends entirely on the classifier’s scale invariance properties, which is not discussed.  

The post-rebuttal clarifies a deeper limitation: the method’s applicability is fundamentally constrained by the target classifier’s architecture. The saliency metric’s efficacy hinges on the classifier’s scale/translation invariance, which:  
- Prevents application during model training (when invariance is unlearned),  
- Limits use to specific domains (e.g., excludes spectrogram analysis with non-invariant CNNs),  
- Invalidates the paper’s claim that the method works for "black-box classifiers."  

Furthermore, the rebuttal reveals a strong dependence on the masking network architecture. The authors do not address whether results are tied to the U-Net structure used, nor do they explore how alternative architectures might alter saliency outputs. This raises a critical ambiguity: Are the visualizations reflecting the *target model’s* saliency, or the *masking network’s* biases? Without this discussion, the method’s interpretability claims lack foundation.  

**Specific concerns requiring revision:**  
1. **Table 1**: Clarify whether results are averaged across all classes or restricted to correct classifications.  
2. **LRP comparison**: Specify the exact variant (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) and parameters used for all baselines.  
3. **Architecture dependence**: Address whether the masking network’s design critically impacts results. If so, the method’s generality is severely limited.  
4. **Metric limitations**: Explicitly discuss the scale-invariance dependency and its implications for the metric’s validity.  

The paper’s current formulation fails to acknowledge these dependencies, rendering its "black-box" claim unsupported. The proposed saliency metric is not universally applicable—it is contingent on the classifier’s architectural properties. Without a rigorous analysis of this constraint, the work risks misleading readers about the method’s scope.  

**Recommendation**:  
The paper’s conceptual contribution is promising but its technical execution lacks the necessary rigor for publication. The evaluation framework is flawed, and the method’s limitations are not adequately addressed. While the approach is technically sound, the absence of discussion on classifier dependency and metric validity undermines the core claims. The authors must either:  
- Substantially revise the paper to thoroughly analyze the method’s constraints (including classifier dependence and metric limitations), or  
- Acknowledge that the method is only applicable to specific architectures with known invariance properties.  

Given the current state, the work does not meet NeurIPS standards. It should be rejected unless the authors provide a comprehensive revision addressing these critical points. The current version overstates the method’s generality while obscuring its fundamental dependencies.
