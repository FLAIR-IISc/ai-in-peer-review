- Proposes a neural-network-based saliency mask method with high throughput; learning-to-explain concept is compelling.  
- Paper is borderline: presents promising ideas but also significant limitations that need addressing.  
- No obvious technical errors, but important shortcomings reduce confidence in results.  
- Evaluation relies on indirect metrics that have notable drawbacks.  
- Weakly supervised localization is a problematic saliency metric because contextual cues can make localization and saliency quality uncorrelated.  
- The proposed saliency metric’s implementation crops and rescales salient regions, introducing evaluation artifacts.  
- Rescaling preserves aspect ratio and can distort object representation, harming classification-based evaluation.  
- The metric disproportionately favors small salient regions that are heavily upscaled, creating non-ideal object scales for classifiers.  
- These scaling issues assume classifier scale invariance, an assumption CNNs may not satisfy.  
- The paper omits analysis of how masking performance depends on the underlying classifier architecture and alternative architectures.  
- Minor clarifications needed: whether Table 1 results are aggregated across classes or per correct class.  
- Minor clarifications needed: exact LRP variant and parameter settings (e.g., epsilon, alpha-beta) used for comparison.  
- Post-rebuttal concern: reliance on classifier scale invariance severely restricts the metric’s applicability.  
- Consequences: metric cannot be used during training or with classifiers lacking scale invariance, is not generalizable to other domains (e.g., spectrograms), and undermines the “black-box classifier” claim.  
- Ambiguity remains about whether the method visualizes the U-network or the masking network, with indications of strong architectural sensitivity.  
- Recommendation: accept only if metric dependencies, architectural sensitivity, and limited applicability are thoroughly discussed; otherwise recommend rejection.
