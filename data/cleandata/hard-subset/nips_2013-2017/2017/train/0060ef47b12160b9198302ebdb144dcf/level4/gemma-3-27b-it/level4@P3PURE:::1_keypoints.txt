- Paper proposes learning saliency masks with a neural network, running at multiple images per second; idea is promising but work is borderline.  
- No obvious errors, but the method has clear limitations that need addressing.  
- Primary concern: saliency is measured indirectly, which may not reflect true saliency.  
- Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy for saliency, especially when context drives classification.  
- The proposed saliency metric has implementation drawbacks (crop + rescale) that can introduce artifacts and bias.  
- Cropping/rescaling can alter aspect ratio, harm classification, and inherently favors small salient regions that are heavily upscaled.  
- CNNs may not be scale-invariant, so upscaling can produce suboptimal classifier behavior and skew the metric.  
- Paper lacks analysis of how masking performance depends on the underlying classifier architecture or comparisons across architectures.  
- Minor requests: clarify if Table 1 results are over all classes or only correctly classified ones, and state which LRP variant/parameters were used.  
- Post-rebuttal: metric’s reliance on classifier quality and scale invariance severely limits applicability (e.g., during training, other domains like spectrograms, and true black-box classifiers).  
- Post-rebuttal: dependence on the masking network raises ambiguity about whether visualizations reflect the original model’s saliency or the masker’s behavior; address these issues or risk rejection.
