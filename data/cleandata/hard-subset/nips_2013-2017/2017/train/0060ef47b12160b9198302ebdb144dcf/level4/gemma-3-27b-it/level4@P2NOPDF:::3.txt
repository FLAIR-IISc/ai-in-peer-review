This paper introduces a fast image saliency mask generation approach capable of processing approximately 100 224x224 images per second on a standard GPU. The core idea is to train a masking model that identifies the minimal bounding box enclosing the salient region of an object belonging to a specified class, as determined by a pre-trained “black box” classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet). The model leverages multi-scale feature maps, such as those generated by ResNet-50, with an initial localization performed via a feature filter on the final scale, followed by upsampling blocks to refine the resulting masks. Experiments demonstrate that this method surpasses other weakly supervised localization techniques on the ImageNet localization task.

The paper demonstrates a good understanding of related work, presenting a sufficient number of relevant references. The technical aspects appear sound, although a complete verification was not performed within the scope of this review. The authors provide clear intuition and a thoughtful discussion surrounding the design choices of their approach. The presentation is well-written and easy to follow. 

Overall, the paper presents interesting and technically strong results. However, I have some reservations regarding the claimed real-time speed and the method’s generalizability to real-world images beyond the ImageNet dataset. 

Here are some specific points and questions for the authors:

* **Runtime Clarification:** Does the reported processing time of 100 images per second include the time required for image resizing? If so, could the authors provide the runtime performance when processing larger images, such as 640x480 images typically captured by a smartphone camera (e.g., iPhone 6s)? This would provide a more practical understanding of the method’s performance in realistic scenarios.

* **Small Object Handling:** The approach appears to perform well with relatively large salient objects. However, how does the method handle cases where the object of the requested class is small within the image? Would a fixed input size of 224x224 be sufficient to accurately localize such objects, or would a more adaptive approach be necessary?

* **Comparative Metrics:** Table 3 presents results for the proposed method. It would be valuable to include corresponding metrics for the methods discussed in Table 2, such as Feed [2], to facilitate a more direct and comprehensive comparison of performance. This would strengthen the analysis and better position the contribution of this work.
