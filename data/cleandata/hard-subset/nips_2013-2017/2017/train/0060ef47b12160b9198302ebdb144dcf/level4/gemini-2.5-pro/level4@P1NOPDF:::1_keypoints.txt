- Proposes a neural-network method for saliency masks with strong computational efficiency (multiple images/sec).
- Reviewer views paper as borderline: promising ideas but substantial limitations, no obvious technical errors.
- Main concern: saliency is evaluated only indirectly via weakly supervised localization or a novel metric, both with clear limitations.
- Weakly supervised localization is an imperfect proxy because important contextual regions may lie outside object bounding boxes, so localization may not reflect true saliency.
- The proposed saliency metric is flawed: cropping/rescaling can alter aspect ratio and prefers smaller regions, introducing artifacts that harm classifier performance.
- The metric inherently magnifies small patches, relying on the classifier’s scale invariance—which is not guaranteed for ConvNets.
- Paper fails to discuss sensitivity of results to the masking-network architecture; unclear if different architectures were tested or how they affect maps.
- Minor: clarify whether Table 1 results are averaged over all classes or only the ground-truth class.
- Minor: specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparisons.
- Post-rebuttal: authors confirm the metric depends heavily on classifier scale invariance, a significant limitation on applicability.
- Consequence: method is unsuitable for models during training or models lacking scale invariance, questionable for other domains (e.g., spectrograms), and weakens the “black-box” generality claim.
- Rebuttal indicates strong dependence on masking-network architecture, raising whether maps reflect the target classifier or masking-network biases.
- If these limitations and consequences are thoroughly discussed, the paper could be acceptable; otherwise the reviewer recommends rejection.
