This paper proposes a novel approach to learning saliency masks using a neural network. The proposed method can process multiple images per second, making it suitable for real-time applications. The concept of learning to explain a model is compelling, and the paper presents some innovative ideas.

However, the paper is borderline, and I would not object to either rejection or acceptance. While there are no obvious mistakes, there are clear limitations that need to be addressed.

One limitation is that the saliency is only indirectly measured, either through weakly supervised localisation or the proposed saliency metric. Both methods have limitations that should be discussed in the paper. Weakly supervised localisation is not a perfect measure, as it may not correlate with saliency quality if the context in which an object appears is essential to determine its class. The results on weakly supervised localisation are interesting, but there is a big caveat when using them as a quality metric for saliency.

The proposed saliency metric also has limitations. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size, which could introduce artefacts. The change in aspect ratio might impact how well the image can be classified, and the proposed metric's preference for small salient regions may lead to suboptimal scaling. Since convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are generally translation-invariant but not scale-invariant, this could limit the applicability of the proposed method.

Another limitation is that the paper does not discuss how much the masking model depends on the architecture for learning the masks. Did the authors experiment with different architectures, and how did this influence the results? This is an essential consideration, as the choice of architecture may significantly impact the performance of the method.

Minor comments include specifying which LRP variant and parameter setting was used for comparison, as well as clarifying whether the results in Table 1 were obtained for all classes or only for the correct class.

In the rebuttal, the authors acknowledged that the saliency metric's performance depends on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier. This limitation has significant consequences, including that the method cannot be used for models during the training phase, nor for models that do not exhibit this invariance. This limits the applicability of the method to other domains, such as spectrogram analysis with CNNs. Furthermore, the method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers as claimed in the title.

The rebuttal also hinted at a strong dependence on the masking network. This raises concerns about whether the method is visualizing the saliency of the U-network or the masking network. If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, it would be more balanced and suitable for publication. Otherwise, it should not be published.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper proposes a novel approach to learning saliency masks using a neural network.
* The method can process multiple images per second, making it suitable for real-time applications.
* The concept of learning to explain a model is compelling, and the paper presents some innovative ideas.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* The saliency is only indirectly measured, either through weakly supervised localisation or the proposed saliency metric.
* The proposed saliency metric has limitations, including the potential introduction of artefacts due to rescaling.
* The paper does not discuss how much the masking model depends on the architecture for learning the masks.
* The method's applicability is limited to models with scale invariance, which may not be generally applicable to black-box classifiers.
* The strong dependence on the masking network raises concerns about whether the method is visualizing the saliency of the U-network or the masking network.
