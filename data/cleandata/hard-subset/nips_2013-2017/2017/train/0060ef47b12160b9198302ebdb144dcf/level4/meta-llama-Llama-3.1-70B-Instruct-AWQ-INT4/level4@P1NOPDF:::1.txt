The paper proposes an approach to learning saliency masks using a neural network, which can process multiple images per second, making it a fast method. I find this paper borderline, and I would not strongly object to either rejection or acceptance. I believe in the concept of learning to explain a model, and this paper has some good ideas. However, there are no obvious mistakes, but there are clear limitations that need to be addressed.

One of the primary limitations is that saliency is only indirectly measured, either through weakly supervised localization or by the proposed saliency metric. Both methods have clear limitations that should be discussed in the paper. Weakly supervised localization is not a perfect measure, as it may not correlate with saliency quality if the context in which an object appears is essential to determine its class. Although the results on weakly supervised localization are interesting, there is a significant caveat when using them as a quality metric for saliency.

The proposed saliency metric also has limitations due to its application. The estimated salient region is cropped and then rescaled to the original image size, maintaining the original aspect ratio. This could introduce two artifacts: the change in aspect ratio might impact the classification performance, and the preference for small salient regions in the proposed metric could lead to suboptimal scaling for re-classification. Since convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are generally translation-invariant but require learning scaling invariance, there are probably limits to this invariance.

Another critical aspect that is not discussed is the dependence of the masking model on the architecture used for learning the masks. Did the authors experiment with different architectures and analyze how this influenced the results?

Minor comments:
- Are the results in Table 1 obtained for all classes or only for the correct class?
- Please specify which LRP variant and parameter setting were used for comparison, as there are multiple variants with parameters, such as epsilon, alpha-beta, and more.

Post-rebuttal edit:

The fact that the effectiveness of the saliency metric depends on the quality and scale invariance of the classifier strongly limits the applicability of the proposed method. It can only be applied to networks with this invariance, which has important consequences:
- The method cannot be used for models during the training phase or for models that do not exhibit this invariance.
- This limits the applicability to other domains, such as spectrogram analysis with CNNs.
- The method is not generally applicable to black-box classifiers, as claimed in the title.

Furthermore, the response hints at a strong dependence on the masking network. As a result, it is unclear whether we are visualizing the saliency of the U-network or the masking network.

If these effects are properly discussed in the paper, I believe it is balanced enough for publication. If not, it should not be published.
