- Summary: Proposes a fast "masking model" (U-Net) that learns to generate saliency maps in one forward pass for any differentiable black-box image classifier.  
- Method: Trains the masking model with an objective combining smoothness, sparsity, smallest sufficient region, and smallest destroying region terms.  
- Evaluation: Tested on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet across multiple classifiers, showing strong weakly supervised object localization performance and a new crop-based saliency metric.  
- Strength — Originality: Introduces a novel "learning to explain" paradigm by amortizing per-image optimization into a learned generator.  
- Strength — Speed: Produces saliency maps in real-time, enabling applications like video saliency that iterative methods cannot.  
- Strength — Empirical results: Achieves state-of-the-art weakly supervised localization on ImageNet, supporting map quality.  
- Strength — Clarity: Paper is well-written with a clear motivation, method description, and experiments.  
- Weakness — Evaluation metric limits: Localization is an imperfect proxy for saliency because classifiers may rely on contextual cues outside object boxes.  
- Weakness — Proposed metric assumption: The crop-and-resize saliency metric assumes classifier invariance to large scale/aspect changes, which is a strong and often violated assumption.  
- Weakness — Masking model bias: The masking network’s own inductive biases (e.g., pre-trained ResNet-50 U-Net) may drive masks more than the target classifier’s behavior; no ablation on masking architecture is provided.  
- Weakness — Overstated generality: Claims of being a universal "black box" explainer are overstated given the metric and masking-model invariance/bias requirements and unsuitability for domains where rescaling is meaningless.  
- Minor: Clarify in Table 1 whether localization error is computed only on correctly classified images.  
- Recommendation: Overall borderline — promising and original but needs a thorough discussion and experiments on evaluation limitations and masking-model confounds to support generality claims.
