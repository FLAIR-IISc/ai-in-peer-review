This paper introduces a fast image saliency mask generation approach, achieving processing speeds of approximately 100 224x224 images per second on standard GPU hardware. The core idea is to train a masking model to identify the most salient regions of an image *with respect to a given class*, as determined by a differentiable “black box” classifier (e.g., AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet). The model learns to predict a mask highlighting these regions, effectively pinpointing what the classifier focuses on for its decision. The architecture leverages multi-scale feature maps (inspired by ResNet-50) with an initial localisation step followed by refinement via upsampling blocks. Experimental results on ImageNet demonstrate that this method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques in object localisation.

The paper is well-situated within the existing literature. It builds upon gradient-based saliency methods [11, 12, 16] and iterative approaches [17, 1, 2], addressing their limitations – specifically, the computational cost of iterative methods and the limited quality of gradient-based explanations. The authors appropriately reference relevant work and clearly position their contribution as a fast, model-agnostic alternative. 

**Strengths:**

* **Speed:** The reported real-time performance is a significant advantage, potentially enabling applications in areas like video analysis and interactive systems.
* **Model Agnostic:** The method’s ability to work with any differentiable classifier broadens its applicability.
* **Novel Objective Function:** The proposed objective function (combining smoothness, small region preservation, and classification probability) seems well-motivated and addresses the challenges of generating informative saliency maps.
* **New Saliency Metric:** Introducing a metric based on the “smallest sufficient region” (SSR) concept offers a more nuanced evaluation of saliency compared to standard localisation accuracy.
* **Strong Experimental Results:** The results on ImageNet demonstrate competitive performance against other weakly supervised methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Real-Time Speed Caveats:** The claim of 100fps requires clarification. Does this include image resizing? What is the performance on higher resolution images (e.g., 640x480) common in real-world applications?
* **Object Scale Sensitivity:** The method's performance with small objects is unclear. The use of 224x224 images might be limiting for scenes containing tiny instances of the target class.
* **Metric Comparison:**  Table 3 presents the new saliency metric for the proposed method. It would be more compelling to include corresponding values for comparison methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., Fong and Vedaldi [2]), to fully assess its benefits.
* **Adversarial Artifacts:** While the paper discusses mitigating adversarial artifacts, a more thorough analysis of their potential impact and the robustness of the model against them would be valuable.
* **Limited Generalization:** While the CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates transferability, it uses a different training paradigm (training the masking model from scratch). More extensive testing on diverse datasets and classifiers is needed.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The proposed approach is well-motivated and the experimental setup is reasonably rigorous. While a complete verification of all implementation details is beyond the scope of this review, the reported results and clear explanations suggest a solid contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organised. The motivation and key ideas are clearly presented.  Minor grammatical errors (e.g., "More over" should be "Moreover", "optimise" should be "optimises") are present but do not significantly detract from readability.

**Originality:** The combination of a trainable masking model with a carefully designed objective function for fast, model-agnostic saliency detection is novel. While building upon existing techniques, the paper presents a meaningful advancement in the field.

**Significance:**  The work addresses an important problem – understanding the decision-making processes of deep neural networks.  The potential for real-time saliency detection opens up new avenues for interpretability and application in areas such as visual debugging, human-computer interaction, and autonomous systems.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Solves a significant problem in deep learning interpretability.
* Presents a novel and effective approach to fast saliency detection.
* Demonstrates strong performance on a challenging benchmark (ImageNet).
* Introduces a new, well-motivated metric for evaluating saliency maps.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The real-time performance claims require further clarification.
* The method’s sensitivity to object scale and robustness against adversarial attacks need further investigation.
* A more comprehensive comparison of the proposed saliency metric against other methods is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend **accepting** this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the reviewer’s concerns regarding the real-time performance claims, object scale sensitivity, and metric comparison. The work presents a valuable contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning with promising potential for practical applications.
