This paper introduces a neural network to predict the salient regions of an image with respect to another neural network’s object categorization decisions. The core idea – training a masking model to identify important image regions in a single forward pass – is a compelling approach to accelerating saliency detection. The paper is well-written and presents a clear explanation of the method, its motivation, and experimental validation. It is noteworthy that the proposed approach demonstrates strong performance compared to existing techniques, particularly on challenging datasets utilizing state-of-the-art classifiers. 

The authors effectively contextualize their work within the broader field of interpretability, referencing prior research on adversarial perturbations [13] and unexpected classifier behaviors [6, 13]. They clearly articulate the limitations of iterative saliency methods [17, 1, 2] and position their contribution as a fast, model-agnostic alternative. The introduction of a new saliency metric, grounded in the concepts of Smallest Sufficient Region (SSR) and Smallest Destroying Region (SDR), is a valuable addition.

Strengths of the paper include its technical soundness, the compelling results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, and the clear presentation. The U-Net architecture [8] with a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder [3] appears well-justified, and the training optimizations described, such as the use of fake labels and randomized evidence removal [A], demonstrate a thoughtful approach to practical implementation. The experiments convincingly demonstrate the model’s ability to generate sharp, interpretable saliency maps and achieve competitive localization accuracy [1]. 

However, a more detailed discussion of potential real-world applications where real-time saliency detection is crucial would strengthen the paper’s impact. While the authors mention video-saliency, elaborating on specific use cases – such as autonomous driving, robotics, or human-computer interaction – would further highlight the significance of this work. Additionally, while the results are strong, a deeper analysis of the *types* of features the learned saliency maps emphasize for different object categories would be insightful. Investigating whether the model reveals known biases in the classifiers (e.g., relying on background features as highlighted in [6]) could provide valuable diagnostic information.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Technical novelty:** The proposed method offers a significant speed improvement over existing iterative saliency techniques while maintaining competitive accuracy.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, outperforming other weakly supervised methods [1, 2].
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to understand.
*   **Novel metric:** The proposed saliency metric provides a valuable tool for evaluating saliency maps and aligns with intuitive notions of interpretability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited discussion of applications:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed exploration of real-world scenarios where real-time saliency detection is essential.
*   **Superficial analysis of learned features:** A deeper investigation into the characteristics of the learned saliency maps, particularly in relation to potential biases in the underlying classifiers, is lacking.



Overall, I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of interpretability. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
