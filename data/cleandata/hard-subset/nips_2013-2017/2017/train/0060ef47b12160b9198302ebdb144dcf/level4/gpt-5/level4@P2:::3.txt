Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic method for generating saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier. Instead of iteratively optimizing a mask per image, the authors train a masking network that produces a saliency mask in a single forward pass, conditioned on an input image and a class selector. The loss encourages masks that are smooth and compact (TV and area penalties), preserve the class score when applied (SSR-like), and reduce the score when inverted (SDR-like). To reduce artifacts from masking, the method blends masked regions with either a strong blur of the image or randomized color+noise, selected stochastically during training. The network adopts a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder, uses a class-conditioned feature filter at the coarsest scale, and upsamples to a final mask at 1/4 resolution.

The paper evaluates on ImageNet with three black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50), reporting weakly supervised localization accuracy and a new saliency metric defined from the area of the tight crop enclosing the mask and the classifier’s confidence on that crop. The approach achieves sharper, more interpretable masks and better localization than prior weakly supervised or perturbation-based methods, and runs at >100 images/s for 224x224 inputs on a standard GPU. A CIFAR-10 variant trained from scratch further supports generality.

This work relates to gradient/backprop saliency (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), perturbation/erasure methods (Zeiler & Fergus; Zhou et al.; Cao et al.), and Meaningful Perturbations (Fong & Vedaldi), which optimize masks per image. The main novelty is amortizing the optimization via a trainable masking model with a combined SSR/SDR objective and artifact-aware training.

Strengths:
- Practical and fast: single-pass saliency suitable for real-time use.
- Thoughtful objective that balances SSR and SDR with explicit smoothness and area regularization.
- Careful treatment of “introduced evidence” via stochastic alternative images and TV regularization.
- Strong empirical results on weakly supervised localization; clear qualitative improvements (sharp, interpretable masks).
- New, simple saliency metric that aligns with SSR and appears discriminative across methods.
- Demonstrates generality across classifiers and a low-resolution setting (CIFAR-10).

Weaknesses and questions:
- “Black box” claim may be overstated: training appears to require differentiability and access to classifier gradients; clarify whether only logits/scores are needed or full backprop through the classifier is used.
- Real-time claim needs more detail (hardware, batch size, preprocessing costs, scaling to higher resolutions).
- Handling of small objects is unclear, given 224x224 resizing and a final mask at 1/4 input resolution; potential loss of fine details.
- Potential sensitivity to the chosen A (blur vs. noise) and to loss weights; limited ablation.
- The new metric involves cropping and aspect-ratio distortions; results may depend on classifier’s scale/aspect invariance.
- Comparisons could be more complete: some methods in Table 2 are not evaluated under the new metric in Table 3.
- Generalization across black boxes requires per-classifier training; cross-classifier transfer is not reported.
- Limited analysis of failure cases and robustness to adversarial artifacts at test time.

Quality:
The methodology is well-motivated and appears technically sound, with a clear objective and sensible architectural choices. Additional ablations (loss terms, A choices, mask resolution) and runtime profiling would further strengthen the claims.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized, with helpful figures and examples. A few minor typos are noted below, and some implementation details (e.g., whether gradients flow through the classifier, exact hardware for timing) should be clarified.

Originality:
Training a dedicated masking network to amortize per-image perturbation optimization, coupled with a joint SSR/SDR objective and artifact-aware blending, is a meaningful contribution beyond prior iterative perturbation approaches. The saliency metric is simple and useful.

Significance:
If the speed and quality claims hold broadly (including for higher-resolution inputs and small objects), the approach could be impactful for practical interpretability and weakly supervised localization, and potentially for video.

Pros for acceptance:
- Single-pass, high-quality, interpretable saliency with competitive localization.
- Solid engineering to mitigate masking artifacts.
- New evaluation metric and strong empirical evidence.

Cons for acceptance:
- Runtime and scalability details are incomplete.
- Limited ablations and small-object analysis.
- “Black box” positioning could be more precise.

Questions for the authors:
- Does the reported throughput of ~100 images/s include image resizing and mask upsampling? What GPU and batch size were used? How does runtime scale for larger inputs (e.g., 640x480 from an iPhone 6s)?
- How does performance change for small objects occupying <5% of the image? Is 224x224 sufficient, and does the 4× downsampling of the final mask hurt small-object precision?
- In Table 3, can you report the new saliency metric for the same methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi) to enable apples-to-apples comparison?
- Do you backpropagate through the black-box classifier during training, or only use its outputs? Could the method work with non-differentiable or API-only classifiers?
- Please provide ablations on the loss terms (λ1–λ4), the choice and sampling of A, and mask resolution.
- How well does a masking model trained on one classifier transfer to another without retraining?

Minor issues/typos:
- LN 273: “More over, because our model” → “Moreover, because our model”
- LN 151: “difference that [3] only optimise the mask” → “difference that [3] only optimises the mask” (confirm correct reference index)
- Consider clarifying the dependence on classifier differentiability and whether gradients are required during training.
- Specify hardware, preprocessing, and batch sizes for timing results.
