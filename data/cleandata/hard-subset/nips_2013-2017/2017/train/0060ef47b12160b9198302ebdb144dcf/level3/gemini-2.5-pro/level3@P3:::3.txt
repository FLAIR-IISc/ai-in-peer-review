This paper proposes a novel and efficient method for generating saliency masks for black-box image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate "masking model" to predict a saliency mask in a single forward pass, instead of using slow, iterative optimization techniques common in prior work (e.g., Fong and Vedaldi, 2017). The masking model, which uses a U-Net-like architecture with a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder, is trained to optimize a composite objective function. This objective encourages the generated mask to be smooth, cover a small area, preserve the classifier's confidence for the target class on the masked image (Smallest Sufficient Region), and destroy the confidence when the masked region is removed (Smallest Destroying Region). The authors also introduce a new saliency metric that evaluates the trade-off between the area of a tight bounding box around the mask and the classifier's score on that cropped region. Experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 demonstrate that the method can be applied to various classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) and achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Efficiency:** The primary contribution—amortizing the expensive optimization of saliency mask generation by training a predictive network—is a significant and original idea. This approach directly addresses the key limitation of previous optimization-based methods, which are often too slow for practical, real-time applications. The claimed speed of ~100 images/sec makes this work highly relevant.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method demonstrates excellent results on the ImageNet weakly supervised localization task, outperforming other weakly supervised techniques by a considerable margin (Table 2). Achieving a localization error of 36.7% with a ResNet-50 backbone is competitive and impressive for a method not explicitly trained with localization data.
*   **Generalizability:** The approach is model-agnostic, requiring only that the target classifier be differentiable. The authors successfully demonstrate this by training separate masking models for three different popular architectures, showing its broad applicability.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide strong motivation and intuition for their design choices, including the components of the objective function, the architecture, and the introduction of a new, well-justified saliency metric. The figures, particularly Figure 5, effectively illustrate the qualitative superiority of the generated masks compared to alternatives.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The main concerns relate to the practical aspects of the real-time claim and the method's robustness to input variations.

*   **Real-Time Performance Claims:** The claim of processing ~100 224x224 images per second is a central pillar of the paper's contribution. However, this figure needs further clarification to be fully convincing.
    *   Does the reported speed include the necessary pre-processing step of resizing an input image to 224x224? This can be a non-trivial bottleneck in a real-world pipeline.
    *   What are the expected runtimes on larger, more realistic image sizes, such as 640×480 or full HD? The applicability of this method to "video saliency" hinges on its ability to process frames at their native or near-native resolution efficiently. A performance analysis on larger inputs would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of real-world utility.
*   **Performance on Small Objects:** The methodology relies on a fixed 224x224 input to the masking network. This raises questions about its effectiveness in scenarios involving small objects. Resizing a high-resolution image containing a small object down to 224x224 could render the object nearly invisible or indistinguishable. How does the model perform in such cases? An analysis of localization error as a function of the ground-truth bounding box area would be a valuable addition to assess this potential limitation.
*   **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** In Section 5.2, the authors introduce a new and interesting saliency metric (Table 3). However, this metric is only reported for the proposed method and a few baselines. To provide a more comprehensive comparison, could the authors compute this metric for the other works listed in Table 2 (e.g., Fong and Vedaldi [2], Zhou et al. [17])? This would allow for a direct comparison of mask quality under the paper's own proposed metric and would make the results in Table 3 more impactful.

**Minor Typos and Wording:**

*   LN 151: The description of the work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] could be slightly more precise. Stating that they "only optimise the mask iteratively and for SDR" is a simplification. Their work proposes a general framework for meaningful perturbation.
*   LN 273: "More over" should be "Moreover".

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and significant contribution to the field of model interpretability and saliency detection. The core idea of training a predictive network for fast saliency generation is original and effectively addresses a major limitation of prior art. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and supported by strong experimental results. While there are some concerns about the specifics of the real-time claims and performance on small objects, these are points for clarification and potential future work rather than fundamental flaws. The work is likely to be influential and built upon by others in the community.
