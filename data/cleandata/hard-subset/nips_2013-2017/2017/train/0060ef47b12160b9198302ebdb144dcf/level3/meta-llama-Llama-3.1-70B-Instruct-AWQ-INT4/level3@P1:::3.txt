This paper proposes a fast image saliency mask generator that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The method is designed to find the tightest rectangular crop containing a requested class using black-box classifiers. The authors claim that their approach outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on ImageNet localization and achieves real-time performance.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method, including the model architecture and the training process. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of related work and provide sufficient references to support their claims. The presentation quality is good, and the paper is easy to follow.

The proposed method uses a multi-scale feature map approach, where the final scale passes through a feature filter for initial localization and upsampling blocks to refine masks. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric that can be used to assess the quality of explanations produced by saliency detectors.

The experiments claim that the method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on ImageNet localization. However, the reviewer is concerned about the real-time speed claim and real-world applicability. Specifically, the reviewer asks whether the 100 images/sec includes image resizing and requests runtimes for larger images (e.g., 640×480 from an iPhone 6s). The reviewer also questions performance on small objects and whether 224×224 input is sufficient for small requested-class objects.

The reviewer also requests whether Table 3 includes corresponding metrics for other works listed in Table 2 (e.g., Feed [2]). This is an important point, as it would provide a more comprehensive comparison of the proposed method with other state-of-the-art approaches.

Overall, the paper appears to be technically correct, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method. However, the reviewer's concerns about the real-time speed claim and real-world applicability need to be addressed.
