This paper proposes a trainable masking network that produces class-conditional saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass. The core idea is to learn a mask that both preserves the classifier’s confidence when applied (Smallest Sufficient Region, SSR) and suppresses it when inverted (Smallest Destroying Region, SDR), while discouraging adversarial artifacts via smoothness (TV) and randomized evidence-removal operators. The masking network uses a U-Net with a fixed ResNet-50 encoder, a class-embedding “feature filter,” and multi-scale upsampling to generate sharp masks. Training includes a “fake-label” regime to ensure the model uses the class selector and randomized alternative images (blur or noisy constant) to reduce overfitting to a single masking artifact. The authors introduce a new saliency metric grounded in SSR: the log-area vs. log-probability of the cropped tight rectangle enclosing the mask. Experiments on ImageNet (with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50 as black boxes) show improved weakly supervised localization and better scores under the proposed metric; a CIFAR-10 experiment trained from scratch shows generality. The method runs at >100 images/s, enabling real-time applications.

Relation to prior work: The method builds on and addresses limitations of backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al.; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), which are fast but often noisy or less faithful; and on optimization-based meaningful perturbations (Fong & Vedaldi), which produce interpretable masks but require per-image iterative optimization. The key novelty is amortizing the optimization into a learned masking model that captures a classifier’s behavior and delivers sharp, artifact-resistant maps in real time. The paper also relates to CAM/Weakly supervised localization (Zhou et al., 2016), but differs by not requiring architectural modifications or localization supervision.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized paper with a principled objective combining SSR/SDR, smoothness, and area regularization, and a thoughtful discussion of introduced evidence.
- Real-time, model-agnostic at test time; the approach amortizes an otherwise expensive optimization and empirically yields sharper, more precise masks than iterative alternatives.
- New SSR-inspired metric that operationalizes interpretability as “how much probability remains in how little area,” with a practical cropping protocol to avoid masking artifacts.
- Strong experiments on challenging datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10), across multiple black-box classifiers, with consistent gains in weakly supervised localization and the proposed metric.
- Practical training tricks (fake labels, randomized alternative images) and a simple, effective architecture design (U-Net with fixed encoder and class embedding).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- “Black-box” assumption: training requires differentiability and backpropagation through the classifier. This is closer to a gray-box setting; please clarify what information is required at train/test time and whether the method works when only class probabilities (no gradients) are available.
- Baselines: consider comparing to widely used attribution methods such as Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ and Integrated Gradients, in addition to Guided/Excitation Backprop and meaningful perturbations, to strengthen claims.
- Ablations: the paper would benefit from a systematic analysis of the objective’s components (λ1–λ4), the effect of the randomized alternative image A (blur vs. noisy constant), and the fake-label probability. A study of mask resolution/upsampling and TV weight vs. artifact formation would be useful.
- Metric sensitivity: the proposed metric depends on classifier calibration and on resizing crops without preserving aspect ratio. Please discuss sensitivity to calibration (e.g., temperature scaling) and to different cropping/resizing protocols.
- Transfer/generalization: masks are learned per black box. It would be informative to show cross-architecture transfer (train on ResNet-50, test on GoogLeNet) and cross-dataset robustness.
- Analysis of learned behavior: since a class embedding emerges, an analysis of which categories rely on background/context (e.g., “polar bear”/snow) versus object parts would be valuable. Aggregate statistics of background reliance and failure cases would strengthen the claim of interpretability.
- Applications: given the real-time speed, motivate specific scenarios (e.g., video explanation for autonomous driving, human-in-the-loop triage, streaming surveillance) and discuss runtime on CPU/mobile and latency/memory constraints.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective and training procedure are well-motivated, with good engineering to mitigate artifacts. Additional ablations and robustness tests (above) would further increase confidence.
- Empirical support: Results on ImageNet/CIFAR-10 with multiple classifiers are compelling; localization improvements over weakly supervised baselines are meaningful. Including more recent attribution baselines and ablations would round out the evidence.
- Reproducibility: Most architectural and training details are given; please specify training time, optimizer settings, exact subset of 250k ImageNet images, and release code/models if possible.

Clarity
- The manuscript is clearly written, with helpful figures and an accessible exposition of the introduced-evidence issue and SSR/SDR framing. Clarify the “black-box” terminology and the metric’s resizing choice.

Originality
- Training a dedicated, real-time masking network that jointly optimizes SSR and SDR with randomized evidence removal and a new SSR-inspired metric is novel and timely relative to optimization-based perturbation methods.

Significance
- The approach advances saliency quality and speed, making real-time explanation feasible and improving weakly supervised localization. It is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., to segmentation and video), with potential impact on transparency tools.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, well-executed idea that amortizes saliency optimization into a fast, accurate model.
- Clear gains on standard datasets and a principled new evaluation metric.
- Broad relevance to interpretability, weak supervision, and real-time applications.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited comparison to some widely used baselines; missing ablations and robustness analyses.
- Black-box assumption is looser than stated; clarity needed on gradient requirements.
- Proposed metric’s dependence on calibration/resizing warrants deeper analysis.

Recommendation: Accept. With additional baselines (e.g., Grad-CAM, Integrated Gradients), ablations, and an analysis of learned context biases, this work would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
