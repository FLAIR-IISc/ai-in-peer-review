This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection, leveraging a trainable masking model to learn saliency masks for any differentiable image classifier. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on various datasets, including CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, and introduce a new saliency metric to evaluate the quality of the produced saliency maps.

The proposed approach is built upon the idea of learning-to-explain, which is a promising direction in the field of explainable AI. The authors' use of a U-Net architecture and a pre-trained ResNet encoder enables their model to produce high-quality saliency masks in a single forward pass, making it suitable for real-time applications.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, highlighting the strengths and limitations of existing saliency detection methods. The experimental results are impressive, with the proposed method outperforming other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task.

However, there are several limitations and concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the saliency metric proposed by the authors is flawed, as it relies on cropping and rescaling the salient region, which can introduce classification artifacts. Additionally, the metric favors small salient regions, which may be excessively blown up, exploiting classifiers' limited scale invariance.

Furthermore, the masking model's dependence on architecture is not discussed, and it is unclear whether different architectures were tested and how they affect the results. The authors should provide more information on the robustness of their method to different architectural choices.

Another concern is the reliance on classifier quality and scale invariance, which severely limits the applicability of the method. The authors claim that their approach is generally applicable to black-box classifiers, but this is challenged by the fact that the method cannot be used during training, for models lacking invariance, or for some domains like spectrograms.

The authors' response to the rebuttal raises uncertainty about whether the visualizations reflect the target (U-)network or the masking network. This is a crucial point, as it affects the interpretability of the results.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a promising approach to image saliency detection, it has several limitations and dependencies that need to be properly discussed. If these concerns are addressed, the paper is acceptable for publication. However, if not, it should be rejected.
