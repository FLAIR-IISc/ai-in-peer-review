This paper proposes a fast image saliency mask generator that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The method uses a multi-scale feature map architecture, with a feature filter for initial localization and upsampling blocks to refine masks. The authors claim that their approach outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on ImageNet localization.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow. The authors provide a clear introduction to the problem of image saliency detection and the limitations of existing methods. They also provide a thorough explanation of their proposed method, including the architecture of the masking model and the training process.

The experimental results are impressive, with the authors demonstrating that their method can produce high-quality saliency masks at a rate of over 100 images per second on a standard GPU. The authors also provide a new metric for evaluating the quality of saliency maps, which they use to compare their method to other approaches.

However, I do have some concerns about the real-time speed claim and the real-world applicability of the method. The authors claim that their method can process over 100 images per second, but this is based on a relatively small image size of 224x224 pixels. I would like to see results for larger images, such as 640x480 pixels, to get a better sense of the method's performance in real-world applications.

Additionally, I am concerned about the performance of the method on small objects. The authors demonstrate that their method can produce high-quality saliency masks for objects that are relatively large in the image, but I would like to see results for smaller objects to get a better sense of the method's limitations.

Overall, I think that this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of image saliency detection. However, I do have some concerns about the real-time speed claim and the real-world applicability of the method that I would like to see addressed in future work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper proposes a novel and effective method for image saliency detection that outperforms other weakly supervised techniques.
* The authors provide a thorough explanation of their proposed method, including the architecture of the masking model and the training process.
* The experimental results are impressive, with the authors demonstrating that their method can produce high-quality saliency masks at a rate of over 100 images per second on a standard GPU.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The real-time speed claim may not be representative of real-world applications, particularly for larger images.
* The performance of the method on small objects is not well-documented and may be a limitation of the approach.
* The authors could provide more detail about the training process and the hyperparameters used to train the masking model.

**Rating:** 8/10

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.
