This paper proposes a fast neural-network-based method to learn saliency masks for any differentiable image classifier. The approach is based on training a masking model to manipulate the scores of the classifier by masking salient parts of the input image. The model is designed to be model-agnostic, allowing it to be applied to any black-box image classifier.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its advantages over existing methods. The proposed method is evaluated on the CIFAR-10 and ImageNet datasets, and the results show that it outperforms other weakly supervised methods in terms of localisation accuracy and saliency metric.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a fast and accurate saliency detection method that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The method is based on training a masking model to predict saliency masks for any input image in a single feed-forward pass. This approach is orders-of-magnitude faster than iterative methods and produces higher-quality saliency masks.

The authors also introduce a new saliency metric that can be used to assess the quality of explanations produced by saliency detectors. The metric is based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR) concepts, which are defined as the smallest region of the image that alone allows a confident classification and the smallest region of the image that when removed prevents a confident classification, respectively.

The paper's strengths include its clear explanation of the proposed method, its evaluation on multiple datasets, and its introduction of a new saliency metric. However, there are also some limitations and potential issues that need to be discussed.

One limitation is that saliency is measured only indirectly via weakly supervised localization and the proposed saliency metric, both of which have clear limitations. Weakly supervised localization can fail as a saliency quality metric when contextual cues (not object location) determine class. The proposed saliency metric is also flawed because cropping and rescaling the salient region can change aspect ratio and scale, introducing classification artefacts.

Another limitation is that the masking model's dependence on architecture is not discussed. It is unclear if different architectures were tested and how they affect results. The authors should provide more information on the architecture of the masking model and its impact on the results.

Additionally, the paper's claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers is challenged. The method's reliance on classifier quality and scale invariance severely limits its applicability. It cannot be used during training, for models lacking invariance, or for some domains like spectrograms.

The response to the rebuttal suggests a strong dependence on the masking network, raising uncertainty whether visualizations reflect the target (U-)network or the masking network. The authors should provide more information on the relationship between the masking network and the target network.

In conclusion, while the paper proposes a novel and promising approach to saliency detection, there are several limitations and potential issues that need to be addressed. If these limitations and dependencies are properly discussed in the paper, it is acceptable for publication. However, if not, it should be rejected.
