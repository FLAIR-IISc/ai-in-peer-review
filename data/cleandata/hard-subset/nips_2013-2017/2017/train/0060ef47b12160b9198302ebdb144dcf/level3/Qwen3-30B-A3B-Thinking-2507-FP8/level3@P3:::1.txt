

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a novel approach to fast saliency detection by training a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, rather than using iterative methods that require hundreds of iterations. The authors train a U-Net-based masking model that manipulates the scores of a differentiable image classifier by masking salient regions, enabling real-time saliency detection. They introduce a new saliency metric based on cropping the salient region and measuring the classifier's confidence on the cropped region, which they claim better evaluates interpretability than standard localization metrics.

The paper builds on recent work in saliency detection, including gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative patch removal [17], and optimization-based approaches [2]. While previous methods have focused on iterative techniques, this paper's key contribution is the shift to a trainable model that provides orders-of-magnitude speed improvements without sacrificing quality. The authors demonstrate their method on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, reporting better localization accuracy than other weakly supervised methods.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method is genuinely fast (100 saliency masks per second), enabling real-time applications like video analysis.
- The masking model architecture is well-motivated and properly implemented using a U-Net with ResNet-50 backbone.
- The new saliency metric (based on cropped regions) provides a more interpretable evaluation than standard localization metrics.
- The paper shows strong empirical results on ImageNet, with the ResNet-trained model achieving 36.7% localization error compared to 34.3% for a fully supervised VGG approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The saliency metric has serious flaws: cropping and rescaling the salient region can change aspect ratio and scale, introducing classification artifacts. Small salient regions are favored by the metric and may be excessively blown up, exploiting classifiers' limited scale invariance.
- The weakly supervised localization evaluation is insufficient for assessing saliency quality. When contextual cues (not object location) determine class (e.g., "polar bear" classification based on snow), localization accuracy fails as a saliency metric.
- The paper does not discuss how the masking model depends on the underlying classifier architecture. It's unclear if different architectures were tested or how they affect results.
- The claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers is questionable. The method relies heavily on the classifier's scale invariance and quality, making it unusable during training, for models lacking invariance, or for domains like spectrograms.
- The paper lacks crucial details: Table 1 results are unclear (over all classes or only correct classes?), and the specific LRP variant and parameter settings for comparisons are not specified.

**Critical Limitations:**
The most significant limitation is the paper's reliance on the classifier's scale invariance and quality. The saliency metric fundamentally depends on the classifier being scale-invariant, which is not true for many real-world classifiers. This severely limits the method's applicability, challenging the claim of "general applicability to black-box classifiers." The post-rebuttal concern about this limitation is well-founded—this method cannot be used for models that lack scale invariance, such as many CNNs trained on specific image sizes.

The masking model's dependence on the classifier architecture is also underdiscussed. The authors state their model "generalises well to unseen images" but don't address whether this generalization depends on the specific architecture of the black-box classifier. This raises uncertainty about whether visualizations reflect the target network or the masking network.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a promising approach with clear speed advantages over existing methods. However, the limitations in the saliency metric and the fundamental reliance on classifier properties are substantial. The paper should be accepted only if it properly discusses these limitations and dependencies in the final version. If these issues remain unaddressed, the paper should be rejected.

The authors have made a good technical contribution to the field of explainable AI, but the current version fails to adequately address the limitations of their approach. A revised version that thoroughly discusses the metric's flaws, the dependency on classifier properties, and the scope of applicability would make this a strong contribution. As it stands, the paper is borderline—acceptable if the limitations are properly acknowledged, but not acceptable if they remain unaddressed.
