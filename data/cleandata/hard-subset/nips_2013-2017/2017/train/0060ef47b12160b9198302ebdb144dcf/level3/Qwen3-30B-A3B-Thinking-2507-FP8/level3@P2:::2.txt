

# Review for "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper presents a novel approach to image saliency detection that trains a masking model to predict saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass, rather than relying on iterative methods. The authors address a significant limitation in existing saliency methods (which are either slow or produce low-quality explanations) by introducing a model-based approach that achieves both speed and quality. The work builds on prior research in saliency detection, including gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative patch removal [17], and optimization-based approaches [1, 2]. While [2] also proposes an optimization-based method for model-agnostic saliency, this paper's key innovation is training a neural network to predict saliency maps directly, enabling real-time performance without sacrificing quality.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a well-defined objective function that addresses key challenges in saliency detection, particularly the problem of adversarial artifacts in masks. The authors introduce a new metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which better evaluates the interpretability of saliency maps than traditional localization accuracy. The experiments are comprehensive, testing on both ImageNet and CIFAR-10 with multiple black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50). The ablation studies and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods provide strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. One limitation is that the new metric assumes classifier invariance to scale and aspect ratio, which might not hold for all classifiers.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of both the technical approach and the motivation behind it. The architecture of the masking model is well-described, and the figures (particularly Figures 1, 2, and 5) effectively illustrate key concepts and results. The new saliency metric is explained with sufficient detail to understand its rationale and application. The authors provide enough detail for an expert to reproduce their results.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by training a masking model to predict saliency maps directly, rather than using iterative optimization. This approach is fundamentally different from previous methods and enables real-time performance. The introduction of the new saliency metric based on SSR is also novel and addresses a gap in the evaluation of saliency methods. The paper distinguishes itself clearly from related work, particularly the concurrent work [2], by focusing on speed and model-agnosticity.

**Significance:** The real-time capability of this method opens up new applications that were previously infeasible with slower saliency techniques, such as video saliency for autonomous vehicles. The method achieves state-of-the-art results on weakly supervised object localization (36.7% error rate on ImageNet, comparable to fully supervised methods), demonstrating its practical utility. The high-quality saliency maps provide valuable interpretability for understanding black-box classifiers, which is increasingly important as these models are deployed in real-world applications.

**Strengths:**
- Real-time performance (100+ saliency maps per second) enables new applications
- High-quality saliency maps that outperform previous methods
- New metric that better evaluates saliency interpretability
- Comprehensive evaluation on challenging datasets with multiple classifiers
- Well-written and clearly organized paper

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could benefit from a deeper analysis of which categories the model exploits (e.g., whether it relies on background biases like "snow" for "polar bear" classification)
- The new metric assumes classifier invariance to scale and aspect ratio, which might limit its applicability
- The paper doesn't fully explore the potential biases introduced by the masking model itself

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper solves a real problem (slow saliency detection) with a novel solution
- The method is simple yet effective and generalizable to any differentiable classifier
- The evaluation is thorough and demonstrates clear improvements over prior work
- The real-time capability is a major practical contribution to the field

*Con:*
- The paper could provide more insight into what the saliency maps reveal about classifier behavior
- The theoretical guarantees for the masking model's generalization are limited
- The analysis of saliency maps in relation to classifier biases is not as deep as it could be

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of interpretability for deep learning models. The real-time capability combined with high-quality saliency maps addresses a critical gap in current methods. The new metric for evaluating saliency is particularly valuable, as it better aligns with the goal of producing interpretable explanations rather than just accurate localizations. While the paper could benefit from deeper analysis of the learned saliency patterns, the overall contribution is significant and well-supported by thorough experiments.

I recommend acceptance of this paper to NIPS 2017. It addresses an important problem in a novel and practical way, with strong experimental validation and clear potential for real-world impact. The method's ability to work with any differentiable classifier makes it broadly applicable, and the real-time performance opens up exciting new applications that were previously infeasible.
