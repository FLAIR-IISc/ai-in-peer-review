- Proposal: fast learned masking model (U-Net with frozen ResNet-50 encoder) that produces class-conditional saliency masks in a single forward pass using SSR+SDR objectives, TV regularization, and mixing with an alternative image A.
- Training/eval: trained on ImageNet for multiple classifiers and on CIFAR-10 from scratch; introduces a crop-based log-area/log-probability saliency metric and evaluates weakly supervised localization.
- Main contribution: learns to approximate iterative optimization-based attribution in real time, yielding sharper masks and improved localization versus several baselines.
- Strength: principled SSR/SDR objective that blends preservation and deletion with defenses against artifacts.
- Strength: single-pass inference is orders of magnitude faster than iterative methods, suitable for real-time/video.
- Strength: empirical gains on ImageNet localization and successful application to CIFAR-10 without pretraining.
- Strength: proposed metric attempts to formalize “concentration of evidence,” complementing localization.
- Weakness: evaluation depends heavily on weakly supervised localization and the new crop metric, which may not align with true saliency (object parts vs full objects; context-driven cues).
- Weakness: crop-based metric can be gamed by scale/aspect-ratio changes and conflates classifier invariances with saliency quality.
- Weakness: claims of applicability to “any” differentiable black-box are too strong given reliance on classifier scale/shape invariances and natural-image assumptions.
- Weakness: dependence on the masking network architecture is underexplored; no ablations on encoder choice, depth, skip connections, TV weight, or mixture of A.
- Weakness: some experimental details and baseline configurations are missing or underspecified.
- Quality: technically sound and well-motivated, but evaluation lacks faithfulness checks (randomization tests, cross-model transfer, deletion/insertion curves).
- Clarity: generally clear writing and figures, but missing exact training/eval splits, label usage in Table 1, and baseline parameter settings.
- Originality: meaningful extension—learning a masking model to emulate optimization-based saliency; crop-based metric is novel but limited.
- Significance: potentially valuable for real-time interpretability, but current evaluation leaves uncertainty about generality and faithfulness.
- Question: is Table 1 evaluated per-image using ground-truth class or top-1 predictions, and how are multi-object images handled?
- Question: specify exactly which backprop-based and perturbation baselines were used, with implementation details and parameters (including LRP variant if used).
- Question: how sensitive are results to resize protocol, aspect-ratio preservation, crop tightness, and classifier choice—provide ablations and discussion of metric gaming.
- Question: report ablations on masking architecture, TV weight, and the choice/mixing of alternative image A and their effects on sharpness/localization/metric.
- Question: does the trained masking model transfer across black-box architectures (e.g., train on ResNet-50, test on GoogLeNet) and what if the black box is updated during training?
- Question: provide analyses where context (e.g., snow) dominates classification to see whether masks highlight context instead of object.
- Suggestion: include additional faithfulness tests (deletion/insertion curves, pointing game, parameter randomization tests, causal metrics controlling scale).
- Suggestion: evaluate metric variants that control for scale/aspect ratio (fixed-size window, padding instead of resizing) and report robustness.
- Suggestion: soften universality claims and discuss applicability limits and domain assumptions.
- Suggestion: release code/models and provide runtime benchmarks across GPUs.
- Pros for acceptance: practical, fast saliency with sharp masks; well-motivated SSR+SDR objective; broad NIPS interest.
- Cons for acceptance: reliance on proxy evaluations with known failure modes; metric biases; limited architectural ablations; overstated generality.
- Post-rebuttal note: explicitly probe classifier scale invariance and masking inductive biases; acceptance likely if authors add analyses/ablations and temper claims.
- Overall recommendation: Borderline — acceptable if limitations are addressed and additional faithfulness/ablation experiments are provided; otherwise remain unconvinced.
