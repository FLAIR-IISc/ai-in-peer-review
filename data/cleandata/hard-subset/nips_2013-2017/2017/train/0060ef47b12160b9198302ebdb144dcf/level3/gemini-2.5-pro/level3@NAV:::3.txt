This paper proposes a novel and efficient method for generating image saliency masks for pre-trained, black-box image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate masking network—a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder—to produce a saliency mask in a single forward pass. This approach stands in contrast to prior work, such as iterative optimization-based methods [2], which are computationally expensive. The masking model is trained to minimize a well-motivated objective function that balances mask size, smoothness, and its ability to preserve or destroy the classifier's confidence. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the ImageNet weakly supervised localization task and introduce a new metric to evaluate saliency quality.

The paper’s primary strengths lie in its **originality** and potential **significance**. The concept of training a fast, feed-forward model to approximate the output of a slow, iterative saliency process is a valuable contribution that could enable real-time applications. The **clarity** of the paper is high; it is well-written, provides good intuition for the architectural design, and the proposed objective function is sound. The experimental **quality** is also strong, with the method outperforming other weakly supervised techniques on a standard benchmark.

Despite these strengths, several weaknesses should be addressed. The central claim of real-time performance (~100 images/sec) requires more substantiation. The authors should clarify whether this speed includes necessary preprocessing like image resizing from native resolutions. More importantly, providing runtimes for larger, real-world image sizes (e.g., 640×480) would make the performance claims more convincing. A second concern relates to the model’s performance on small objects; the reliance on 224×224 input may cause small but salient features to be lost, and this limitation warrants discussion. The experimental evaluation could also be improved. Table 3, which presents results using the proposed saliency metric, would be more impactful if it included corresponding metrics for the other methods listed in Table 2, such as [2], enabling a direct comparison. Finally, the manuscript would benefit from minor proofreading to correct typos (e.g., LN 273 "More over" → "Moreover") and clarify phrasing (e.g., LN 151 regarding [3] optimizing the mask). Addressing these points would significantly strengthen this promising work.
