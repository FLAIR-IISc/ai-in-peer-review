This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection, which can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The authors introduce a trainable masking model that can predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, making it suitable for real-time applications. The model is trained to minimize an objective function that encourages the mask to be smooth, small, and to preserve the classification probability.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on challenging datasets, including CIFAR-10 and ImageNet. The results show that the proposed method outperforms previous weakly supervised methods in terms of localisation accuracy and saliency metric.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The proposed method is model-agnostic, meaning it can be applied to any differentiable image classifier.
* The method is fast and suitable for real-time applications.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on challenging datasets.
* The paper introduces a new saliency metric that can be used to assess the quality of explanations produced by saliency detectors.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the learned salient regions, such as which categories exploit background biases.
* The paper does not discuss potential applications of the proposed method in detail, such as video saliency detection or autonomous vehicles.
* The authors do not provide a comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and efficient approach to image saliency detection.
* The method is model-agnostic and can be applied to any differentiable image classifier.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on challenging datasets.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the learned salient regions.
* The authors do not discuss potential applications of the proposed method in detail.
* The paper does not provide a comparison with other state-of-the-art methods in terms of computational complexity and memory requirements.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of image saliency detection and is worthy of acceptance. However, I would like to see the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above in the final version of the paper.
