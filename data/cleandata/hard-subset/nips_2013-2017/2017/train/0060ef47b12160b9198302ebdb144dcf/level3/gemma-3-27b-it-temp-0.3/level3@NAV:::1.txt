## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a fast, neural network-based approach to generate saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier. The core idea – training a masking model to directly predict salient regions – is a compelling alternative to iterative methods, offering significant speed advantages. The authors demonstrate promising results on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, introducing a new saliency metric and achieving competitive performance on weakly supervised localization. This work builds upon the growing field of explainable AI, specifically addressing the need for efficient saliency detection techniques, referencing relevant work like gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16], iterative removal approaches [1, 17], and recent optimization-based techniques [2].

The strengths of this paper lie in its practical focus and the potential for real-time applications, as highlighted in the conclusion. The proposed masking model, leveraging a U-Net architecture [8] with a ResNet-50 encoder [3], appears well-motivated. The introduction of a new saliency metric, attempting to capture both SSR and SDR principles [3], is a valuable contribution. The experimental results, particularly the competitive performance on ImageNet localization (Table 1) and the favorable results with the proposed metric (Table 3), are encouraging.

However, the paper suffers from several limitations. The reliance on weakly supervised localization as a primary evaluation metric is problematic, as the authors acknowledge. Contextual cues, rather than object location, can drive classification in certain scenarios, rendering this metric unreliable. More critically, the proposed saliency metric itself is flawed. Cropping and rescaling salient regions introduces potential artifacts and exploits classifiers’ limited scale invariance, potentially favoring small, artificially inflated regions. The dependence of the masking model on the chosen U-Net architecture is not adequately discussed; exploring different architectures and their impact on performance is crucial. 

Furthermore, the claim of general applicability to *any* black-box classifier is questionable. The post-rebuttal concern regarding reliance on classifier quality and scale invariance is valid – this method is unlikely to be effective during training, for models lacking invariance, or for domains like spectrograms. The response suggesting a strong dependence on the masking network raises concerns about whether the visualizations truly reflect the target network’s reasoning or merely the masking network’s learned biases. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The approach offers a significant speed improvement over existing methods and demonstrates promising results. The introduction of a new saliency metric and the exploration of a U-Net architecture are valuable contributions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The limitations of the saliency metric and the potential for artifacts raise concerns about the validity of the results. The dependence on classifier properties and the masking network’s potential biases limit the generalizability of the approach.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance *if* the authors thoroughly address the limitations outlined above in a revised manuscript. Specifically, a detailed discussion of the metric’s flaws, an exploration of alternative architectures, and a more nuanced discussion of the method’s applicability are essential. Without these revisions, I would recommend rejection.
