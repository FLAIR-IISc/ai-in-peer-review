This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection for black-box classifiers, moving away from iterative methods towards a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea – training a masking network to predict salient regions – is well-motivated by the limitations of existing techniques like gradient-based methods (Simonyan et al., 2013) and iterative patch removal (Zhou et al., 2015, 2016). These earlier works, while providing insights into classifier behavior, are computationally expensive. Fong & Vedaldi (2017), presented concurrently, also tackles this with an optimization-based approach, but differs in focusing solely on SDR and operating directly in image space. The authors’ contribution lies in training a U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) to directly predict saliency maps, offering significant speed advantages and, as demonstrated by their results, improved performance on both standard benchmarks and a weakly-supervised localization task.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed method is clearly described, and the experimental validation is thorough. The authors appropriately address the issue of introducing artifacts during masking (Section 3.1) and propose sensible solutions. The novel saliency metric (Equation 3) is well-justified and intuitively linked to the concepts of SSR and SDR. The ablation study with CIFAR-10 (Section 5.3) demonstrates the method’s generalizability and reduces concerns about reliance on a specific pre-trained model. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the potential for biases within the masking model itself. The experimental results convincingly support the claims made.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and motivates the proposed solution. Related work is appropriately contextualized. The mathematical notation is clear and consistent. Figures are informative and aid understanding. The authors provide enough detail for reproduction, including specifics on the network architecture and training parameters.

**Originality:** The approach of learning a saliency map predictor is novel, differentiating it from iterative and gradient-based methods. While Fong & Vedaldi (2017) concurrently explored a related optimization strategy, the learned masking model offers a different perspective and advantages in speed and potentially, generalization. The introduction of the new saliency metric is also a valuable contribution.

**Significance:** The results are significant. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on weakly-supervised localization, particularly with a fast, real-time method, is impactful. The potential applications highlighted in the conclusion – video saliency for autonomous vehicles – are compelling and demonstrate the practical relevance of this work. The ability to explain classifier decisions quickly and accurately is crucial for building trust and ensuring responsible AI. The work advances the state of the art in interpretability and offers a practical tool for understanding black-box models.

**Strengths:**

*   **Speed:** The method is significantly faster than iterative approaches.
*   **Accuracy:** Achieves state-of-the-art results on ImageNet localization.
*   **Novelty:** The learned masking model is a novel and effective approach.
*   **Clarity:** Exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   **New Metric:** The proposed saliency metric provides a useful evaluation tool.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Black-Box Dependence:** While model-agnostic in *application*, the masking model is trained *for* a specific black-box classifier. A masking model trained for AlexNet will not necessarily generalize well to ResNet-50 without retraining.
*   **Bias Analysis Limited:** The authors acknowledge potential biases within the masking model, but a more in-depth analysis of *what* those biases might be would be valuable.
*   **Dataset Bias:** The experiments primarily focus on ImageNet. Evaluating performance on more diverse datasets with different inherent biases would strengthen the findings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in interpretability.
*   The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and experimentally validated.
*   The results are compelling and demonstrate a clear advance over existing techniques.
*   The writing is excellent and makes the paper accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The dependency on training a masking model for each black-box classifier is a limitation, although a reasonable one given the model-agnostic goal.
*   Further investigation into potential biases within the masking model would strengthen the work.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of interpretability. The proposed method is novel, effective, and well-motivated. While minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I **strongly recommend acceptance**.

**Minor Suggestions:**

*   Expand the discussion on potential biases in the masking model. Perhaps analyze which categories the masking model consistently exploits background biases for.
*   Consider evaluating the method on datasets beyond ImageNet to assess generalizability.
*   In the conclusion, further elaborate on specific real-time applications beyond autonomous vehicles where this technique could be beneficial (e.g., medical image analysis, fraud detection).
