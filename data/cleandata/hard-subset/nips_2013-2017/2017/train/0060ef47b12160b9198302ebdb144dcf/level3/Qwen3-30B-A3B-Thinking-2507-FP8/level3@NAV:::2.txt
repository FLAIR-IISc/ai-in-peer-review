

This paper introduces a novel approach for real-time saliency detection that predicts important image regions for object classification tasks. The authors propose a masking model trained to generate saliency maps through a single forward pass, addressing the computational inefficiency of iterative methods. The work builds on prior saliency techniques like gradient-based approaches (e.g., [11, 12]) and iterative removal methods (e.g., [1, 2]), but offers a significant speed advantage while maintaining or improving accuracy.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with comprehensive experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, showing clear performance improvements over state-of-the-art weakly supervised methods. The proposed saliency metric (Equation 3) is well-motivated and provides a more nuanced evaluation than standard localization accuracy. The model achieves impressive results (36.7% localization error on ResNet-50), outperforming baselines like "center box" and iterative methods while being orders of magnitude faster.

Strengths include the clear problem formulation, thorough experimental validation, and well-organized presentation. The authors successfully address a meaningful gap in real-time explainable AI. The paper is well-written with a logical flow from motivation to evaluation.

However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing two key points: First, the authors should elaborate on specific real-time applications (e.g., autonomous vehicles, real-time video analysis) to better demonstrate the practical significance of their speed advantage. Second, a deeper analysis of learned salient regions would enhance the paper—particularly examining whether the model exploits background biases (e.g., detecting "snow" for "polar bear" classification as mentioned in the introduction) rather than the actual objects.

The work is highly original in its model-based approach to saliency detection, offering a significant advancement over previous iterative methods. Its significance is evident in the potential for real-time explainable AI systems.

Pro: Strong technical execution, clear contribution to real-time explainability, rigorous evaluation with new metric, well-motivated problem.
Con: Limited discussion of specific real-time applications, insufficient analysis of learned biases.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in explainable AI with practical implications. I recommend acceptance with the suggested minor revisions to strengthen the application discussion and bias analysis.
