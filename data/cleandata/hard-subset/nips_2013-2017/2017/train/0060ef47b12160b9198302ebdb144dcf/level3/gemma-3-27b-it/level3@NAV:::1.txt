## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper proposes a fast, neural network-based method for generating saliency maps for black-box classifiers. The core idea – training a masking model to directly predict saliency instead of iterative optimization – is a reasonable and potentially valuable approach, aligning with the growing interest in explainable AI and addressing the computational limitations of existing techniques like those based on iterative patch removal or gradient descent. The authors demonstrate results on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, showing competitive performance in weakly-supervised localization and introducing a new saliency metric. 

The strengths of this work lie in its practical focus and the potential for real-time applications. The U-Net architecture, leveraging pre-trained ResNet features, appears to be an effective design choice. The authors clearly articulate the challenges of defining and measuring saliency and attempt to address them with a novel metric, although its validity is questionable (see below).

However, I have significant concerns regarding the limitations and dependencies of the proposed method. While the paper doesn’t contain obvious technical errors, several aspects require more thorough discussion. The proposed saliency metric, while interesting, fundamentally relies on cropping and rescaling salient regions, which can introduce artifacts and exploit classifiers’ limited scale invariance, favoring small, potentially spurious, salient regions. This is a critical flaw. 

Furthermore, the dependence of the masking model on the underlying classifier architecture isn’t adequately explored. It's unclear whether the observed performance is generalizable or specific to the architectures tested. The authors mention training from scratch on CIFAR-10, but a more detailed comparison is needed. A major post-rebuttal concern remains: the demonstrated applicability is heavily reliant on the quality and scale invariance of the *target* classifier. This severely limits the method’s utility during training, for models lacking invariance, or in domains where scale is meaningful (e.g., spectrograms). The claim of general applicability to “black-box classifiers” is therefore challenged. The response highlighting the dependence on the masking network itself raises the question of whether the visualizations truly reflect the target network's reasoning or simply the masking network's learned associations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The method is novel and addresses a practical need for faster saliency detection. The experimental results are promising and the paper is well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The proposed saliency metric is flawed, the dependence on classifier characteristics is not adequately addressed, and the generalizability of the approach is questionable. 

**Minor Clarifications:** Table 1 results should clarify whether they represent performance across *all* classes or only the correct class. Specifying the LRP variant and parameter settings used for comparisons would be helpful. 

**Recommendation:** I currently lean towards **rejection** unless the authors significantly expand the discussion of the limitations described above. If they can clearly articulate these dependencies and convincingly demonstrate the robustness of their approach, I would reconsider. The paper, as it stands, overstates its general applicability.
