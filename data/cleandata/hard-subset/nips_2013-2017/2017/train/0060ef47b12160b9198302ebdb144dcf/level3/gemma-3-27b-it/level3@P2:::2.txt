This paper introduces a novel approach to real-time image saliency detection for black-box classifiers. The authors propose training a masking model to predict salient regions of an image with respect to a given classifier’s prediction, allowing for fast saliency map generation through a single forward pass. This contrasts with iterative methods which are computationally expensive. The work is well-motivated, addressing the need for interpretable explanations of deep learning models and tackles the limitations of existing techniques. It builds upon prior work in gradient-based saliency [11, 12, 16], iterative patch removal [17], and optimization-based approaches [1, 2], offering a significant improvement in speed and, in many cases, quality.

The paper’s strengths are numerous. First, the technical approach is sound and well-explained. The U-Net architecture, leveraging a pre-trained ResNet encoder, is a sensible choice for generating high-resolution, precise masks. The objective function, incorporating terms for smoothness, small region preservation, and classification score maintenance, appears thoughtfully designed. Second, the experimental validation is thorough. Testing on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet with multiple black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) demonstrates the generalizability of the method. The introduction of a new saliency metric, based on the smallest sufficient region concept, is valuable and the results support its validity. The paper demonstrates strong performance on object localization, outperforming other weakly supervised methods and achieving competitive results compared to fully supervised approaches. The ablation study with CIFAR-10 showing performance without pre-training further strengthens the work. Finally, the writing is clear and organized, making the paper accessible and easy to follow.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the potential for introduced evidence during masking, the mitigation strategies (Eq. 2, random A) feel somewhat ad-hoc. A more rigorous analysis of the types of artifacts generated and their impact on different classifiers would be beneficial. The analysis of the learned class embeddings (mentioned in the appendix) could be expanded and discussed in the main paper, as it offers insight into how the model learns to identify salient features. The authors also note potential biases within the masking model itself; exploring these biases would add further depth to the analysis. While real-time performance is highlighted, a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost (e.g., inference time for different image sizes) would be useful, particularly in relation to the target applications. The paper also lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity; a brief analysis of how the objective function weights (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4) impact performance would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of learning a saliency map predictor is a significant departure from iterative methods and offers a compelling solution to the speed-accuracy trade-off.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method consistently outperforms existing weakly supervised techniques on challenging datasets.
*   **Sound Technical Foundation:** The architecture and objective function are well-justified and demonstrate a clear understanding of the problem.
*   **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is easy to follow and the key ideas are presented concisely.
*   **Practical Significance:** The real-time performance unlocks potential applications in video analysis and interactive systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Mitigation of Introduced Evidence:** The approach to avoiding artifacts feels somewhat limited and could benefit from more rigorous analysis.
*   **Bias Analysis:** The potential for biases within the masking model is acknowledged but not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A lack of discussion regarding hyperparameter tuning.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh them significantly. The real-time performance and strong results on challenging datasets make this a promising approach with potential for wide-ranging applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding artifact mitigation and bias analysis.
