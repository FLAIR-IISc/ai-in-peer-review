This paper proposes a fast, neural-network-based method for generating saliency maps for black-box classifiers. The core idea is to train a “masking model” to predict which parts of an image are most salient based on their influence on the classifier’s output. This contrasts with iterative methods which are computationally expensive. The authors demonstrate the method on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, introducing a new saliency metric and achieving competitive results on weakly supervised localization.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in saliency detection and explainable AI. Gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] are fast but produce low-quality maps. Iterative methods [1, 17] are more accurate but slow. More recent approaches like Fong & Vedaldi [2] also aim for model-agnostic saliency but rely on iterative optimization. This work differentiates itself by learning a mapping *to* saliency, enabling single-pass prediction and real-time performance. The proposed metric attempts to address the disconnect between saliency and localization, acknowledging that identifying the minimal sufficient region doesn't necessarily equate to highlighting the entire object.

**Strengths:**

*   **Speed:** The primary strength is the method’s speed, offering a significant improvement over iterative approaches. This is crucial for real-time applications.
*   **Novel Approach:** Learning a saliency mapping is a reasonable and potentially powerful approach. The use of a U-Net architecture is sensible for this task.
*   **Competitive Results:** The method achieves comparable or superior results to existing weakly supervised localization techniques on ImageNet.
*   **New Saliency Metric:** The proposed metric attempts to address limitations of relying solely on localization accuracy and incorporates the concept of minimal sufficient regions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Indirect Saliency Measurement:** Saliency is assessed indirectly via weakly supervised localization and the new metric. Both have inherent limitations. Weakly supervised localization is susceptible to failure when contextual cues, rather than object presence, determine classification.
*   **Flawed Saliency Metric:** The metric’s reliance on cropping and rescaling introduces artifacts. Altering the aspect ratio and scale can impact classifier performance and artificially inflate the apparent saliency of small regions, exploiting limited scale invariance.
*   **Architectural Dependence:** The paper lacks discussion of architectural choices. It's unclear whether different masking model architectures were explored and how they affect performance.
*   **Dependence on Classifier Quality & Invariance:** A critical limitation is the method's reliance on the quality and scale invariance of the target classifier. The post-rebuttal concern regarding applicability to models lacking invariance (or domains like spectrograms where scale/aspect ratio is meaningful) is valid and substantially restricts the paper's scope. The claim of general applicability to *any* differentiable classifier is, therefore, questionable.
*   **Masking Network vs. Target Network:** The response to initial reviews raised concerns about whether the visualizations truly reflect the target network's reasoning or the masking network’s learned biases. This ambiguity undermines the interpretability of the saliency maps.

**Specific Points:**

*   **Table 1:** Clarification requested during the review regarding whether the results in Table 1 are averaged over all classes or only the correct class was not sufficiently addressed.
*   **LRP Comparison:** Specifying the exact LRP variant and parameter settings used for comparison would improve reproducibility and fairness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and efficient approach to saliency detection.
*   It achieves competitive results on standard benchmarks.
*   The proposed saliency metric is a thoughtful attempt to move beyond simple localization accuracy.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The inherent limitations in saliency measurement and the flaws in the proposed metric undermine the validity of the claims.
*   The strong dependence on classifier characteristics and the potential for the masking network to introduce its own biases severely restrict the method’s applicability.
*   The lack of thorough architectural exploration and analysis weakens the scientific rigor.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I initially found this paper borderline. After considering the rebuttal and the limitations raised, my assessment remains cautious. **Acceptance is contingent upon a significant revision that explicitly addresses the weaknesses outlined above.** Specifically, the paper *must* thoroughly discuss the architectural choices and their impact, acknowledge and elaborate on the limitations regarding classifier quality and invariance, and address the potential for the masking network to introduce its own biases. The discussion of the saliency metric should be revised to acknowledge its potential artifacts. Without these revisions, the paper’s claims of general applicability and interpretability are not sufficiently justified, and **I recommend rejection.** The potential of the approach is clear, but the current manuscript does not present a complete and rigorous scientific contribution.
