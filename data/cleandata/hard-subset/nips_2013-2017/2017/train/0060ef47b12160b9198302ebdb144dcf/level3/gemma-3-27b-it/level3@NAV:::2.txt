## Review of “Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers”

This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection, framing it as a learned masking problem applicable to any differentiable image classifier. The core idea – training a masking model to predict salient regions in a single forward pass – offers a significant speed advantage over iterative methods commonly found in the literature. The authors convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, achieving state-of-the-art results in weakly supervised object localization and introducing a new saliency metric.

The work builds upon the existing landscape of saliency detection techniques, acknowledging gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] and iterative approaches like those by Zhou et al. [17] and Cao et al. [1]. It importantly positions itself against recent optimization-based methods such as Fong and Vedaldi [2] by proposing a fully learned, feed-forward model, which addresses the computational bottleneck of iterative methods. The introduced saliency metric, grounded in the concepts of Smallest Sufficient/Destroying Regions [2], provides a useful lens for evaluating explanation quality beyond simple localization accuracy. 

A key strength of this work is its experimental validation. The authors test their method against strong baselines on challenging datasets, using established networks like AlexNet, GoogleNet, and ResNet-50.  Furthermore, the inclusion of the CIFAR-10 experiment demonstrates the generalizability of the proposed technique even at lower resolutions. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with detailed explanations of the proposed objective function and architecture. 

However, the paper could benefit from further discussion of potential applications driving the need for *real-time* saliency detection. While video-saliency is mentioned, elaborating on specific scenarios (e.g., explainable AI in autonomous driving, real-time visual debugging) would strengthen the motivation. Additionally, the authors could expand their analysis of the learned saliency maps. Specifically, exploring which categories consistently exploit background biases (as alluded to in the introduction referencing polar bear/snow examples [6]) would provide valuable insight into model behavior and potential vulnerabilities. Investigating adversarial artifacts in more detail, even with the mitigation strategies employed, would also be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and experimentally validated.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in explainable AI with a novel and efficient solution.
*   Outperforms existing methods on standard benchmarks.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Could benefit from a stronger articulation of real-time application scenarios.
*   Further analysis of learned saliency maps and potential biases would strengthen the work.



Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of explainable AI. The paper presents a compelling solution to a computationally challenging problem, supported by rigorous experimentation and clear writing. I recommend acceptance.
