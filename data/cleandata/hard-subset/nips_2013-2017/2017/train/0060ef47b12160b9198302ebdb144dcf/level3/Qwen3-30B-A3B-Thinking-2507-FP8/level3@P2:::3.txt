

This paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency detection method that generates interpretable saliency maps in a single forward pass, targeting real-time applications. The authors train a masking model (based on U-Net with ResNet-50 features) to predict saliency masks that minimize a novel objective combining smoothness (TV penalty), compactness (area penalty), and classification fidelity (SSR/SDR). The approach avoids iterative optimization (unlike Zhou et al. [17] or Fong & Vedaldi [2]) and introduces a new saliency metric based on the area/probability trade-off of the tightest rectangular crop containing the salient region. Experiments on ImageNet show competitive localization accuracy (36.7% error with ResNet-50) and superior interpretability metrics compared to baselines and ground-truth boxes. The method is evaluated across multiple black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) and extended to CIFAR-10.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The technical approach is sound. The objective function (Eq. 4) properly addresses key challenges: adversarial artifacts (via Eq. 3.1), smoothness (TV penalty), and dual SSR/SDR goals. The training strategy (30% fake labels, randomized evidence removal) effectively prevents overfitting to the classifier. Experiments are comprehensive (ImageNet, CIFAR-10), with ablation studies (Table 3) supporting the metric’s validity.  
*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written. The architecture (Fig. 4) and objective motivation (Sec. 3) are clear, and the new metric provides a meaningful alternative to standard localization accuracy. Minor typos (e.g., "More over" at LN 273) are easily corrected.  
*Originality*: The model-based saliency generation (vs. iterative methods) is novel and well-motivated. The work clearly distinguishes itself from Fong & Vedaldi [2] (which optimizes masks iteratively in image space) and Zhou et al. [17] (which uses patch removal). The metric (Sec. 3.2) is also a significant contribution.  
*Significance*: Real-time speed (~100 images/sec on 224×224) enables new applications (e.g., video saliency for autonomous systems). The weakly supervised localization results (36.7% error vs. 34.3% for supervised VGG [10]) demonstrate practical impact, and the method’s generalizability to diverse classifiers (AlexNet to ResNet) broadens applicability.

**Weaknesses**  
*Quality*: The real-time claim requires stronger validation. The speed (100 images/sec) is measured on 224×224 inputs; the reviewer requests runtime for larger resolutions (e.g., 640×480 from smartphone cameras), which is critical for real-world use. The paper also does not address performance on small objects (e.g., a bird in a landscape), where 224×224 inputs may discard key details.  
*Significance*: While the method is fast, the localization error (36.7%) lags behind fully supervised methods (34.3% for VGG [10]). The significance is slightly tempered by this gap and the lack of analysis on small-object performance.  
*Clarity*: Table 3 omits the saliency metric for Fong & Vedaldi [2] (cited in Table 2), making direct comparison incomplete. The authors should include this for fairness.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pros*: Novel model-based approach; strong real-time speed; new interpretability metric; robust on ImageNet; well-structured; addresses key pitfalls (adversarial artifacts).  
*Cons*: Speed claim lacks validation on non-224×224 inputs; small-object performance unaddressed; missing metric for Fong & Vedaldi [2] in Table 3; localization error slightly higher than supervised baselines.  

**Recommendation**  
This is a strong paper with significant contributions to interpretable AI. The core method is technically rigorous, well-motivated, and validated across multiple datasets. The minor weaknesses are addressable via minor revisions (e.g., adding runtime data for larger images, clarifying small-object limitations, and including Fong & Vedaldi [2]’s metric in Table 3). The real-time promise is compelling for applications like autonomous systems, and the new metric advances the field. The paper aligns with NIPS’ focus on high-quality, impactful work and distinguishes itself from prior art.  

**Decision**: Accept. The work is of high quality, original, and significant. The requested clarifications are straightforward to implement and would strengthen an already strong contribution.
