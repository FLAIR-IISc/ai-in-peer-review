This paper proposes a fast image saliency mask generator that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The method trains a masking model to manipulate the scores of the classifier by masking salient parts of the input image. The model generalizes well to unseen images and requires a single forward pass to perform saliency detection, making it suitable for use in real-time systems.

The authors relate their work to previous methods of image saliency detection, including gradient-based approaches and iterative optimization-based methods. They argue that their approach is more efficient and produces higher-quality saliency masks. The paper also introduces a new metric for saliency, which is based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR) concepts.

The proposed model architecture uses multi-scale feature maps, such as those from ResNet-50, and passes the final scale through a feature filter for initial localization and upsampling blocks to refine masks. The authors claim that their method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on ImageNet localization.

The paper is technically correct, and the authors provide intuition and discussion motivating their design choices. The presentation quality is good, and the references and related work appear sufficient.

However, there are some concerns about the real-time speed claim and real-world applicability. The reviewer asks whether the 100 images/sec includes image resizing and requests runtimes for larger images (e.g., 640×480 from an iPhone 6s). Additionally, the reviewer questions the performance on small objects and whether 224×224 input is sufficient for small requested-class objects.

Regarding the quality criterion, the paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their method and its advantages over previous approaches. However, there are some minor typos and wording issues that need to be addressed.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a new approach to image saliency detection that is different from previous methods. The use of a masking model and the introduction of a new saliency metric are novel contributions.

The significance of the paper is high, as it proposes a fast and accurate method for image saliency detection that can be applied to various applications, including real-time systems. The paper also provides a new perspective on the evaluation of saliency methods and introduces a new metric that can be used to assess the quality of explanations produced by saliency detectors.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection that is different from previous methods.
* The method is fast and accurate, making it suitable for use in real-time systems.
* The paper introduces a new metric for saliency that can be used to assess the quality of explanations produced by saliency detectors.
* The authors provide a thorough explanation of their method and its advantages over previous approaches.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the real-time speed claim.
* The performance on small objects and the sufficiency of 224×224 input for small requested-class objects are not thoroughly evaluated.
