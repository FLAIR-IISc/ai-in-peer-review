Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a fast, model-conditioned saliency detector that predicts, in a single forward pass, image regions important for a given classifier’s decision. The authors formalize saliency via smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR), and design an objective that encourages both: smooth and small masks that preserve class probability when kept and suppress it when removed. To mitigate artifacts from masking, they randomize the “alternative image” (blurred original or noisy constant) and regularize masks with total variation. The core contribution is a trainable masking network (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and a learned class embedding/filter) trained to minimize the saliency objective using gradients through the fixed “black-box” classifier. Training tricks include “fake labels” to force class-conditioning and randomization of the masking operator to reduce overfitting to a specific perturbation.

The method is compared to backpropagation-based explanations (e.g., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop) and iterative perturbation methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, “Meaningful Perturbations”; Cao et al.). It differs by learning a feed-forward saliency predictor per classifier, yielding sharper masks and orders-of-magnitude speedups. The paper also introduces a new saliency metric based on the area/probability trade-off measured on a tight crop, intended as a proxy for SSR while being robust to adversarial artifacts. Experiments on ImageNet (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) and CIFAR-10 demonstrate improved weakly supervised localization and better scores under the proposed metric, with real-time throughput (~100 images/s).

Quality
- Soundness: The objective is well-motivated by SSR/SDR and artifact mitigation concerns. The architecture and training scheme are standard and appropriate (U-Net with skip connections; class embedding filter; TV/area penalties). The paper evaluates on strong baselines and relevant datasets, and reports localization error and the proposed saliency metric. Runtime claims are plausible given the model size.
- Empirical evaluation: Results show clear improvements over iterative perturbation and backprop-based saliency on ImageNet localization, approaching fully supervised localization performance (e.g., 36.7% error vs 34.3% for VGG). The new metric further distinguishes methods, and a CIFAR-10 experiment from scratch supports generality.
- Gaps: (i) The “black-box” claim is somewhat overstated: training requires backpropagating through a differentiable classifier and a separate saliency model must be trained per classifier. (ii) Missing ablations for the loss components (e.g., role of the SSR vs SDR terms, TV strength, the randomized alternative image) and for the class-conditioning mechanism. (iii) The new metric is reasonable but could be stress-tested for biases introduced by aspect-ratio-insensitive resizing and multi-instance cases. (iv) Comparisons to contemporaneous Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ are not reported. (v) Limited analysis of failure modes and of whether masks reflect background biases (e.g., “snow” for polar bear).

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. The motivation for SSR/SDR and the artifact discussion are clear, and architectural/training details (lambdas, layer shapes, randomization scheme) support reproducibility. Minor clarity issues:
- Make precise the saliency metric formula (is it s = log(ã) − log(p)?), specify log base, and discuss stability when p is small; justify the 0.05 floor for a.
- Clarify “black-box”: emphasize that differentiability and gradient access are needed during training, and that a separate masking model is trained per classifier.
- Explicitly state whether test-time saliency uses the predicted class or the ground-truth label in each evaluation, and report both.

Originality
Training a dedicated saliency predictor with an SSR+SDR objective and randomized evidence removal is a novel and interesting direction compared to iterative optimization or gradient-based post-hoc explanations. The proposed metric is also a useful addition. Relation to Fong & Vedaldi (parallel work) and Cao et al. is well discussed. Adding comparisons to Grad-CAM (ICCV’17) would strengthen positioning.

Significance
The ability to produce sharp, interpretable masks in real time is valuable for interactive analysis and video-time applications, and the reported localization performance is competitive with fully supervised baselines, despite using only image-level labels. The masking model and metric are likely to be adopted or extended by others. The requirement to train per classifier limits plug-and-play deployment, but is acceptable in many practical settings.

Strengths
- Clear formulation tying SSR/SDR to a trainable objective; thoughtful treatment of artifact mitigation.
- Strong empirical results on ImageNet with state-of-the-art classifiers; consistent improvements over prior weakly supervised and saliency baselines.
- Real-time inference; simple, scalable architecture; additional CIFAR-10 experiment from scratch supports generality.
- New, principled saliency metric that correlates with interpretability.

Weaknesses
- Requires gradients through and training per classifier; “model-agnostic/black-box” framing is partially misleading.
- Limited ablations and failure analysis; little insight into when/why the approach fails.
- Missing comparison to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++.
- The proposed metric’s dependence on resizing and cropping may bias results; discussion is brief.
- No explicit applications demonstrating that real-time saliency changes outcomes (e.g., video or interactive monitoring).

Suggestions for improvement
- Motivate and, if possible, demonstrate concrete real-time applications (e.g., video saliency, online model monitoring, human-in-the-loop debugging), with qualitative/quantitative evidence.
- Add ablations: remove the SSR or SDR term; vary TV/area penalties; fix vs randomize the alternative image; analyze their effect on artifacts, sharpness, and metrics.
- Include comparisons to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++ and discuss complementarity.
- Provide per-category analysis of learned saliency to probe background biases (e.g., quantify overlap with context regions; show categories where the method highlights context vs object), as suggested in the introduction’s “polar bear/snow” narrative.
- Report results for both predicted and ground-truth labels; consider multi-instance scenarios.
- Clarify the saliency metric formula, stability, and resizing implications; consider a variant that preserves aspect ratio or uses adaptive warping.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance on standard benchmarks with clear gains over prior methods.
- Real-time, single-pass saliency with sharp, interpretable masks.
- Well-motivated objective and practical training scheme; broadly useful metric.

Arguments against acceptance
- Somewhat overstated “black-box” claim; needs per-classifier training with gradients.
- Missing ablations and comparisons to recent CAM-based methods.
- Limited analysis of biases and failure modes; applications not fully demonstrated despite “real-time” emphasis.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution that advances saliency mapping both methodologically and empirically. With added analysis and broader comparisons, it would be a strong fit for NIPS.
