This paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection, which is the task of identifying the most relevant regions of an image for a given object categorization model. The authors introduce a trainable masking model that can predict saliency maps in a single forward pass, making it suitable for real-time applications. The model is trained to minimize an objective function that encourages the mask to be smooth, to preserve the smallest region that allows for confident classification, and to remove the evidence for the selected class.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach on challenging datasets, including ImageNet and CIFAR-10, using state-of-the-art networks. The results show that the proposed method outperforms previous weakly supervised techniques in terms of object localization accuracy and a new saliency metric introduced by the authors.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide high-quality saliency maps that are easily interpretable and sharp, without the need for iterative optimization. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the conditions that a good saliency model should satisfy, including the need for smoothness, small region size, and removal of evidence for the selected class.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the architecture of the masking model and the training process. The use of a pre-trained ResNet-50 model as the encoder part of the U-Net architecture is a good choice, as it allows for fast training and provides a good initialization for the model.

The evaluation of the proposed method is thorough, and the authors provide a comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, including Guided Backpropagation, Excitation Backprop, and the approach proposed by Fong and Vedaldi. The results show that the proposed method outperforms these methods in terms of object localization accuracy and saliency metric.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of analysis of the learned salient regions. The authors could provide more insights into which categories exploit background biases and how the model is able to capture these biases. Additionally, the authors could provide more details on the training process, including the learning rate schedule and the batch size used.

Another potential weakness is the limited evaluation of the proposed method on specific applications that require real-time efficiency. While the authors mention the potential application of their method to video saliency detection, they could provide more details on how their method could be used in practice.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to image saliency detection that is different from previous methods. The use of a trainable masking model that can predict saliency maps in a single forward pass is a new contribution, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to impact the field of computer vision, particularly in applications that require real-time image saliency detection. The proposed method could be used in a variety of applications, including video saliency detection, object detection, and image segmentation.

In conclusion, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach. The proposed method is novel and has the potential to impact the field of computer vision. However, the authors could provide more insights into the learned salient regions and more details on the training process.
