

This paper introduces a novel, model-agnostic approach for real-time image saliency detection using a trainable masking model that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass. The core contribution is a masking model (based on U-Net architecture with ResNet-50 features) trained to minimize an objective function balancing mask smoothness, compactness, and classification fidelity (eq. 4), addressing key limitations of iterative methods [2, 17] and gradient-based approaches [11, 12]. The method achieves 100 saliency maps per second on 224x224 images, significantly faster than prior work, and introduces a new saliency metric (s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p)) that better aligns with the "smallest sufficient region" (SSR) concept. Experiments on ImageNet show competitive object localization accuracy (36.7% error with ResNet-50 black box, outperforming weakly supervised baselines and approaching fully supervised methods [10]), while the new metric demonstrates that the method’s explanations rival ground-truth bounding boxes in interpretability.

**Strengths:** The technical approach is well-motivated and addresses critical gaps in the literature. The objective function effectively combats adversarial artifacts via smoothness constraints (TV penalty) and randomized evidence removal (eq. 6), a significant improvement over [2]. The proposed s(a,p) metric is theoretically grounded and provides a more nuanced assessment of saliency quality than standard localization accuracy, which is particularly valuable for evaluating interpretability. The architecture design (multi-scale feature filtering, U-Net refinement) is sound, and the results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 (Fig. 6) convincingly demonstrate generalizability and sharp saliency maps. The paper also thoughtfully discusses limitations (e.g., mask overfitting in iterative methods) and future work (video saliency, bias analysis).

**Weaknesses:** Two key concerns undermine the real-world applicability claim. First, the "real-time" speed (100 images/sec) is demonstrated only for 224x224 inputs. For practical applications (e.g., smartphone video at 640x480), resizing overhead would reduce throughput significantly—this must be clarified with concrete runtime data. Second, the method’s performance on small objects is untested; 224x224 crops may exclude critical details for small classes (e.g., birds in natural scenes), which is a major limitation for localization tasks. While the paper claims strong results on ImageNet, Table 3 lacks the s(a,p) metric for methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., [2]), making it impossible to verify claims of superiority over competing weakly supervised techniques. Minor issues include a typo ("More over" → "Moreover" at LN 273) and slightly ambiguous phrasing about [3] optimizing masks (LN 151).

**Quality & Clarity:** The methodology is technically sound, with thorough ablation studies (e.g., blur/noise randomization in evidence removal, Table 3) and clear experimental design. The paper is well-organized, with sufficient detail for reproducibility (e.g., model architecture, hyperparameters). The new metric and artifact mitigation strategy represent strong theoretical contributions.

**Originality & Significance:** The work is novel in its model-agnostic, single-pass saliency framework, distinct from iterative [1, 2] or gradient-based [11, 12] methods. It advances the field by enabling real-time saliency (e.g., for autonomous vehicles), and the s(a,p) metric provides a more meaningful evaluation standard than localization accuracy alone. The significance is high for interpretable AI in safety-critical systems.

**Recommendation:** **Weak Accept.** The paper makes a valuable technical contribution with strong experimental results and a compelling new metric. However, the real-time speed claim requires validation at realistic resolutions (e.g., 640x480), and the small-object limitation must be addressed. The omission of s(a,p) metrics for competing methods (Table 2) in Table 3 is a critical gap that weakens the comparative claims. Addressing these points would strengthen the paper’s impact. The authors should clarify the speed benchmark (including resizing costs) and test on small objects (e.g., using ImageNet’s "small object" subsets [18]). The current version is technically solid but needs minor clarifications to fully realize its significance.
