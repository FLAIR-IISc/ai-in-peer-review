- Proposal: Train a fast, model-agnostic masking model to predict saliency maps directly, avoiding iterative optimization.  
- Method: Leverages multi-scale feature maps from a pre-trained CNN (e.g., ResNet-50).  
- Results: Strong ImageNet localization performance, surpassing other weakly supervised techniques.  
- Presentation: Clear, well-organized writing with helpful figures and good design intuition.  
- Technical soundness: Architecture and objective are well-described and well-motivated, though deeper verification of implementation/math would help.  
- Experiments: Comprehensive evaluation including a new saliency metric alongside standard localization accuracy.  
- Novelty: Directly learning a saliency mask generator and the new metric are meaningful contributions.  
- Impact: High potential for real-time applications (video, autonomous driving, HCI) and broad adoption due to model-agnostic nature.  
- Strength — Speed: Claimed ~100 images/sec on a standard GPU is a major advantage over iterative methods.  
- Strength — Accuracy: Competitive and superior performance among weakly supervised localization methods.  
- Strength — Model-agnostic: Applicable to any differentiable classifier.  
- Strength — Clarity & novelty: Well-written, novel approach, and useful new evaluation metric.  
- Weakness — Speed claim: 100 images/sec needs validation (does it include preprocessing/resizing; how does it scale to larger images like 640x480?).  
- Weakness — Small objects: Performance on small objects is not discussed and 224x224 inputs may limit localization accuracy for small targets.  
- Weakness — Comparisons: Table 3 lacks comparable saliency metric scores for other methods listed in Table 2, limiting direct comparison.  
- Acceptance — Pros: Novel, effective, strong results, clear presentation, and high potential impact.  
- Acceptance — Cons: Require validation of runtime claim, analysis of small-object performance, and more complete comparisons in Table 3.  
- Overall: Strong paper recommended for acceptance if authors address speed validation and provide fuller comparisons in Table 3.
