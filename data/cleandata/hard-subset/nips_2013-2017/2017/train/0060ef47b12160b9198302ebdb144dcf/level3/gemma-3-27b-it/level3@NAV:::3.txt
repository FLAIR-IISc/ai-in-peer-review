## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper presents a novel approach to generating image saliency masks in a fast and model-agnostic manner. The authors propose training a masking model, leveraging multi-scale feature maps from a pre-trained network (e.g., ResNet-50), to directly predict saliency masks for any given input and class. This contrasts with iterative methods, offering significant speed improvements – claimed to be ~100 224x224 images/sec on a standard GPU. The work is well-motivated, addressing the need for interpretable explanations of black-box classifier behavior, and offers a new saliency metric alongside promising experimental results on ImageNet, surpassing other weakly supervised localization methods.

The core strength of the paper lies in its practical approach and the potential for real-time applications. The U-Net architecture, incorporating features from multiple scales and a carefully designed objective function to combat adversarial artifacts, appears well-considered. The inclusion of a novel saliency metric, focusing on both smallest sufficient and destroying regions, is a positive contribution, and the experimental results on ImageNet demonstrate a clear improvement over existing techniques. The clarity of writing and organization are also commendable.

However, several concerns remain. The claimed real-time speed is a significant point requiring further scrutiny. Does the reported 100 images/sec figure include the overhead of image resizing? Runtime performance for larger image sizes (e.g., 640x480 as captured by a typical smartphone camera) would be crucial for assessing real-world applicability. Furthermore, the performance on small objects isn't explicitly addressed; a 224x224 input may be insufficient for accurately localizing small instances of the requested class. It would be valuable to see results on datasets with varying object sizes. 

Regarding the experimental evaluation, Table 3 would be more informative if it included the corresponding saliency metric scores for the methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., Feed [2]) to facilitate a direct comparison. Finally, minor typographical errors exist (e.g., LN 273 "More over" should be "Moreover", and LN 151 requires clarification regarding [3] optimizing the *mask*).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and practical approach to fast saliency mask generation.
*   Demonstrated improvement over existing weakly supervised methods.
*   Well-motivated, clearly written, and technically sound (based on initial assessment).
*   The introduced saliency metric shows promise.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Real-time speed claim needs further validation, especially concerning image resizing and larger image sizes.
*   Performance on small objects is not discussed.
*   Incomplete comparison in Table 3.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning. Addressing the concerns raised regarding speed, object scale, and completeness of experimental comparisons would significantly strengthen the manuscript.
