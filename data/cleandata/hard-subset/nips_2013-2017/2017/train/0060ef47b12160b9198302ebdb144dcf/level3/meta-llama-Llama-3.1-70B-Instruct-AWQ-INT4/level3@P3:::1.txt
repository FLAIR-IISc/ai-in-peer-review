This paper proposes a fast neural-network-based method to learn saliency masks for image classification models. The method is based on training a masking model to manipulate the scores of the classifier by masking salient parts of the input image. The proposed approach is model-agnostic and can be applied to any differentiable image classifier.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The authors provide a clear motivation for their work, discussing the limitations of current state-of-the-art image classifiers and the need for saliency detection methods that can provide insights into the decision-making process of these models.

The proposed method is based on a trainable masking model that can produce saliency masks in a single forward pass. The model is trained using a novel objective function that encourages the mask to be smooth, small, and informative. The authors also propose a new saliency metric that evaluates the quality of the produced saliency maps.

The paper presents an extensive evaluation of the proposed method on the ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets. The results show that the proposed method outperforms other weakly supervised methods in terms of object localization accuracy and saliency metric.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The proposed method is fast and efficient, making it suitable for real-time applications.
* The method is model-agnostic and can be applied to any differentiable image classifier.
* The authors propose a new saliency metric that evaluates the quality of the produced saliency maps.
* The paper presents an extensive evaluation of the proposed method on two datasets.

However, there are also some limitations and concerns:

* The proposed saliency metric has some clear limitations, such as the fact that it can be affected by the aspect ratio and scale of the object.
* The method relies on a classifier that is largely invariant to the scale and aspect ratio of the object, which may not always be the case.
* The masking model's dependence on the architecture is not discussed, and it is unclear whether different architectures were tested and how they affect the results.
* The paper does not provide a clear discussion of the limitations of the proposed method and how they can be addressed in future work.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a new method for saliency detection that is based on a trainable masking model. The method is novel and has the potential to contribute to the field of explainable AI.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of computer vision, namely the need for saliency detection methods that can provide insights into the decision-making process of image classification models. The proposed method has the potential to be widely applicable and to contribute to the development of more transparent and explainable AI systems.

Overall, I believe that the paper is well-written and well-organized, and that the proposed method is novel and has the potential to contribute to the field of explainable AI.
