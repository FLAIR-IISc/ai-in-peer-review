This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection, specifically designed for use with differentiable image classifiers. Instead of relying on iterative methods which are computationally expensive, the authors train a masking model to predict salient regions in a single forward pass, enabling real-time applications. This is a significant contribution as existing techniques, like gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] and iterative patch removal [17, 1], are either limited in quality or too slow for dynamic scenarios. The work builds upon the recent trend of interpretable machine learning, aiming to understand *why* a classifier makes a particular decision, as highlighted by papers exploring adversarial perturbations [13] and dataset biases [6].  The authors also introduce a new saliency metric, framing saliency in terms of smallest sufficient and destroying regions, which offers a more nuanced evaluation than traditional localization accuracy.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The use of a U-Net architecture [8] with a pre-trained ResNet encoder [3] provides a strong foundation, and the training process incorporates valuable optimizations to avoid overfitting and adversarial artifacts. The results presented on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet are compelling, demonstrably outperforming existing weakly supervised methods [1, 2] in object localization tasks. Furthermore, the introduced saliency metric, incorporating concepts of information concentration, is a thoughtful addition to the field.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, facilitating a thorough understanding of the proposed methodology and experimental results. The ablation studies, particularly the CIFAR-10 experiment trained from scratch, demonstrate the robustness of the approach. Finally, the authors acknowledge the potential for biases within the masking model itself, showcasing intellectual honesty.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the paper addresses the problem of adversarial artifacts, the discussion of mitigating them feels somewhat superficial. The introduction of alternative image 'A' seems somewhat ad-hoc, and a more systematic exploration of different artifact-reduction techniques could be beneficial.  The comparison to existing methods, while thorough, largely focuses on localization accuracy. A more detailed qualitative analysis comparing the visual fidelity and interpretability of the generated saliency maps – beyond the examples provided – would strengthen the claims. While the authors suggest future work in video saliency, expanding on the practical challenges of adapting the method to temporal data would be valuable. Finally, the suggestion in the paper to analyze the learned salient regions (e.g., which categories exploit background biases) is a strong one, and performing such an analysis would further demonstrate the model's capability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed method offers a fast, model-agnostic solution to image saliency detection, a significant improvement over existing iterative techniques.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks and introduces a new, well-motivated metric.
* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand, with clear explanations of the methodology and results.
* **Significance:** The real-time performance opens up new possibilities for applications like video saliency and dynamic visual explanations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Superficial artifact mitigation:** The discussion of addressing adversarial artifacts could be more in-depth.
* **Limited qualitative analysis:** A more comprehensive comparison of saliency map quality beyond localization accuracy is needed.
* **Potential for bias:** While acknowledged, a deeper investigation of biases within the learned masks would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and supported by compelling experimental results.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is a worthy addition to NIPS 2017 and recommend acceptance.
