- Proposes a fast, trainable masking network (U-Net w/ ResNet-50 encoder and class embedding) that predicts saliency masks in one forward pass using an SSR+SDR objective.  
- Formalizes saliency via smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR) and trains masks to be small, smooth, and class-preserving/ suppressing.  
- Mitigates masking artifacts by randomizing the alternative image (blur/noisy constant) and regularizing masks with total variation.  
- Trains the masking network by backpropagating through a fixed differentiable classifier, with tricks like fake labels and masking operator randomization.  
- Shows strong empirical improvements on ImageNet (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) and CIFAR-10, achieving sharper masks, better localization, and ~100 images/s throughput.  
- Introduces a new saliency metric based on area/probability trade-off on tight crops as a proxy for SSR and to be robust to adversarial artifacts.  
- Objective, architecture, and training scheme are well-motivated and appropriate; runtime claims are plausible.  
- Experimental results approach fully supervised localization performance and outperform iterative perturbation and backprop-based baselines.  
- Works from-scratch on CIFAR-10, supporting method generality beyond ImageNet.  
- Core limitation: calling the method “black-box” is misleading since training requires gradient access and a separate saliency model per classifier.  
- Missing important ablations (SSR vs SDR, TV strength, randomized alternative image) and analysis of the class-conditioning mechanism.  
- New saliency metric needs stress-testing for biases (aspect-ratio-insensitive resizing, multi-instance images) and stability when p is small.  
- Lacks comparisons to contemporaneous CAM-based methods (Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++).  
- Limited analysis of failure modes and potential background/context biases (e.g., highlighting snow for polar bear).  
- Clarity is generally good and reproducible; minor issues include precise metric formula, log base and stability, definition of “black-box,” and whether predicted or ground-truth labels are used at test time.  
- Originality: novel direction to train a dedicated saliency predictor with SSR+SDR and randomized evidence removal; metric is a useful contribution.  
- Significance: real-time, sharp, interpretable masks useful for interactive and video applications, though per-classifier training limits plug-and-play use.  
- Strengths: clear SSR/SDR formulation, artifact mitigation, strong ImageNet results, real-time inference, simple scalable architecture, and a principled new metric.  
- Weaknesses: per-classifier gradient requirement, limited ablations/failure analysis, missing Grad-CAM comparisons, metric resizing/cropping biases, and no demonstrated real-time applications.  
- Suggested improvements: add ablations (remove SSR/SDR, vary TV/area, fix vs random alternative), compare to Grad-CAM/Grad-CAM++, present per-category bias analysis, report results for predicted vs ground-truth labels, clarify metric formula and resizing implications, and demonstrate real-time applications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong empirical gains, practical real-time single-pass saliency, and a well-motivated objective with a broadly useful metric.  
- Arguments against acceptance: overstated black-box claim, missing ablations and CAM comparisons, limited bias/failure analysis, and under-demonstrated applications.
