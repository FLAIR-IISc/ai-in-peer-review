Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes a trainable masking network (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) that amortizes the “meaningful perturbation” idea (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017) to produce saliency masks in a single forward pass for any differentiable image classifier. The objective mixes SSR and SDR goals with TV and area penalties and uses randomized evidence-removal baselines to mitigate adversarial artifacts. The authors also introduce a saliency metric based on cropping the predicted salient region and measuring a trade-off between area and class probability. Experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 report sharp, interpretable masks, competitive weakly supervised localization, and high throughput. The work is positioned against gradient/backprop methods (Simonyan et al., GuidedBP, ExcitationBP), CAM-style localization, and iterative perturbation approaches.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for amortizing perturbation-based saliency; simple objective; strong runtime advantage.
- Empirical comparisons across multiple backbone classifiers; masks are visually compelling and sharper than baselines.
- Sensible engineering to reduce artifacts (TV, randomized A, class-selector training trick).
- The idea of learning a class embedding within the masker is interesting and potentially reusable.

Weaknesses and concerns:
Quality: While technically sound, saliency is only evaluated indirectly via weakly supervised localization and the proposed crop-resize metric, both of which have limitations. Localization can be misleading when context, not object location, determines class (e.g., “polar bear” via snow), so success there does not validate saliency quality. The proposed metric is problematic: cropping and aspect-ratio-agnostic resizing can change scale/geometry and introduce classification artifacts; it also favors tiny regions that, when blown up, exploit limited scale invariance. Post-rebuttal, the method’s reliance on classifier quality and scale invariance severely limits applicability (not suitable during training, for models lacking invariance, or for non-image domains like spectrograms). The “black-box” claim is overstated: training requires gradients, and a separate masker per classifier was needed. The response suggests strong dependence on the masking network, leaving uncertainty whether visualizations reflect the target model or the masker’s inductive bias.

Clarity: Generally well-written, but please clarify: (i) Are Table 1 results computed for the ground-truth class only, top-1 prediction, or averaged over classes? (ii) Which LRP variant and parameter settings were used in comparisons?

Originality: Moderate—novel in amortizing perturbation-based saliency with a dedicated network and in the training tricks; metric design is less convincing.

Significance: Practical speedup could impact applications, but evaluation weaknesses and dependence on classifier/architecture constrain generality.

Pros: fast; sharp masks; simple, reproducible framework; useful engineering insights.
Cons: weak validation of saliency; flawed metric; dependence on scale invariance and masker architecture; overstated black-box claim.

Recommendation: Borderline. Acceptable if the paper candidly discusses these limitations, tempers claims of generality, adds controls (aspect-ratio–preserving crops, scale-controlled tests, context-sensitive classes), and reports architecture/dependence/ablation results; otherwise, reject.
