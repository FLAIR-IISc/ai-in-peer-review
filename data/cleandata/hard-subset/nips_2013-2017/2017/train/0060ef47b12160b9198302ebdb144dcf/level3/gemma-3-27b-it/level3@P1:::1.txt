This paper presents a novel approach to generating saliency maps for image classifiers, moving away from iterative methods to a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea—training a masking model to predict salient regions—is a promising direction and addresses the computational cost of existing techniques like those based on backpropagation or iterative patch removal [11, 17]. The method demonstrates strong performance on ImageNet localization, outperforming other weakly supervised approaches, and introduces a new saliency metric. While the results are encouraging, several limitations and dependencies warrant careful consideration.

The work builds upon a rich history of saliency detection methods. Early approaches, like gradient-based methods [11], were computationally efficient but produced low-quality explanations. Iterative techniques [17, 1] improved explanation quality but suffered from significant computational cost. Recent methods like Fong and Vedaldi [2] also take a black-box approach but rely on iterative optimization. This paper distinguishes itself by learning a mapping from image to saliency map, enabling single-pass, real-time saliency detection. The introduction of a new saliency metric and the exploration of a U-Net architecture for the masking model are also valuable contributions.

A key strength of this work lies in its speed and potential for real-time applications. The learned model allows for rapid saliency map generation, opening possibilities for video-saliency analysis and other dynamic scenarios. The visual results presented (e.g., Figure 5) appear compelling, suggesting the model captures meaningful regions relevant to the classifier’s decision-making process. Furthermore, the strong performance on the weakly supervised localization task and the comparison to existing techniques demonstrate a clear advantage in this domain.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The proposed saliency metric, while novel, is fundamentally flawed. The cropping and resizing operation (Section 3.2) introduces artifacts and disregards aspect ratio, potentially impacting classification accuracy and biasing the metric towards small, scale-invariant regions. The reliance on a tightly cropped region to assess saliency also favors models with strong scale invariance, creating a dependency that is not adequately addressed. The metric thus measures something distinct from true saliency, potentially rewarding the model for exploiting classifier characteristics rather than identifying genuinely important image features.

Another concern is the potential for the masking model to overfit the specific classifier it is trained against. While the authors acknowledge this, the extent to which the learned masks reflect the behavior of the *target* classifier versus the *masking* network itself remains unclear. The architecture of the masking model (U-Net with a ResNet backbone) is largely fixed, and the authors do not explore the sensitivity of the results to different network architectures or hyperparameter settings. This raises questions about the generalizability of the approach.

The evaluation is primarily focused on weakly supervised localization. While this is a valuable benchmark, localization accuracy may not directly correlate with saliency, especially when contextual cues play a significant role in classification. The authors acknowledge this in their discussion of Smallest Sufficient/Destroying Regions, but this limitation should be more strongly emphasized. Furthermore, the results in Table 1 lack clarity regarding whether they are averaged across all classes or reported only for correctly classified images. Clarification on this point would be helpful. Similarly, more detail on the LRP variants and parameter settings used for comparison would enhance reproducibility.

The claim of broad applicability to *any* differentiable classifier is overstated. The performance of the masking model is inherently tied to the characteristics of the classifier it’s trained against, particularly its scale invariance. It’s unlikely to be effective for classifiers lacking such invariance or for domains where scale is a crucial factor (e.g., analyzing spectrograms). This dependency limits the generality of the proposed approach. The post-rebuttal concern regarding reliance on classifier quality and scale invariance is especially pertinent.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to fast saliency detection with promising results.
*   Outperforms existing weakly supervised methods on a relevant task.
*   Introduces a new (though flawed) saliency metric.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The proposed saliency metric is fundamentally flawed and biased.
*   Strong dependence on the target classifier’s characteristics (scale invariance).
*   Limited exploration of the masking model’s architecture and hyperparameters.
*   Overstated claim of general applicability.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of explainable AI. However, the limitations regarding the saliency metric, classifier dependencies, and limited architectural exploration must be addressed. If these issues are thoroughly discussed in the paper, acknowledging the constraints and potential biases of the approach, it would be acceptable for publication. Otherwise, I recommend rejection.
