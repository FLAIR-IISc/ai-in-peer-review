This paper introduces a novel approach to saliency detection for image classifiers, moving away from iterative methods towards a learned, feed-forward masking model. The core idea – training a network to predict salient regions for any differentiable classifier – is appealing and addresses a significant bottleneck in explainable AI: the computational cost of existing techniques. The authors demonstrate promising results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, achieving competitive performance on weakly supervised localization and proposing a new saliency metric. While the work is technically sound and the results are generally positive, several limitations and dependencies warrant careful consideration.

The paper builds on a rich history of saliency detection methods, starting with gradient-based approaches [11, 12, 16] and progressing to iterative techniques like those by Zhou et al. [17] and Cao et al. [1]. The authors correctly identify the speed limitations of iterative methods and position their work as a fast, model-agnostic alternative, similar in spirit to the black-box optimization approach of Fong and Vedaldi [2], but implemented via a learned model. The introduction effectively motivates the need for interpretable explanations and highlights the challenges of trusting high-performing but potentially brittle classifiers [6, 13].

The strengths of the paper lie in its novelty and potential for real-time applications. The proposed masking model offers a significant speed advantage over iterative methods. The experimental results, particularly the performance on weakly supervised localization (Table 1), are encouraging. The new saliency metric (Section 3.2) represents a thoughtful attempt to capture properties of good saliency maps beyond simple localization accuracy. The ablation studies on CIFAR-10 (Section 5.3) demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The reliance on weakly supervised localization as a primary evaluation metric is problematic. Localization accuracy can be driven by contextual cues rather than true object saliency; a classifier might correctly identify an object based on its surroundings, leading to seemingly accurate localization but failing to pinpoint the truly salient features. This is particularly relevant given the examples provided in the introduction regarding classifiers exploiting spurious correlations (e.g., snow and polar bears). 

The proposed saliency metric (Equation 3), while intuitively appealing, suffers from potential flaws. Cropping and resizing the salient region – as performed in the metric calculation – can introduce artifacts and alter the classifier’s performance due to its potential sensitivity to scale and aspect ratio changes.  The metric implicitly favors small salient regions, potentially leading to an overemphasis on localized features and exploiting classifiers with limited scale invariance.  The claim that this metric provides a good assessment of interpretability is therefore questionable.

The masking model’s architecture (U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) is largely taken as given. The paper doesn’t explore the sensitivity of the results to different architectural choices. A discussion of alternative architectures and their potential impact on performance would strengthen the work. Furthermore, the training details lack crucial elements; the choice of regularization parameters (λ1, λ2, λ3, λ4) seems somewhat arbitrary and requires further justification.

A few minor clarifications are needed. In Table 1, it is unclear whether the reported localization errors are averaged over all classes or only for instances where the correct class is predicted.  When comparing to LRP (presumably referring to Layer-wise Relevance Propagation), specifying the exact variant used (e.g., LRP-epsilon) and parameter settings is crucial for reproducibility and fair comparison.

Following the rebuttal, a major concern persists regarding the applicability of this method. The authors' response reinforces the strong dependence on the quality and scale invariance of the underlying classifier. This severely limits the method’s practical utility. It cannot be used *during* training to identify and mitigate biases, and it will likely perform poorly with models that lack scale invariance or in domains where scale is not a relevant feature (e.g., spectrograms in audio analysis). The claim of general applicability to *any* differentiable classifier appears overly ambitious.

The rebuttal also raises a subtle but important point: the visualizations produced by the method may reflect the biases and representations of the *masking network* itself, rather than the true decision-making process of the target classifier. This ambiguity undermines the goal of providing interpretable explanations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to fast saliency detection.
*   Promising experimental results on localization and a new saliency metric.
*   Technically sound and well-written.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in explainable AI.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Weak reliance on weakly supervised localization for evaluation.
*   Flawed saliency metric with potential for artifacts.
*   Limited discussion of architectural choices and sensitivity analysis.
*   Strong dependence on classifier quality and scale invariance severely restricts applicability.
*   Uncertainty regarding whose “interpretations” the masks reflect (target network vs. masking network).

**Overall Recommendation:**

I currently lean towards acceptance, *conditional* on significant revisions addressing the limitations outlined above. Specifically, the authors must thoroughly discuss the limitations of their evaluation metrics, acknowledge the strong dependence on classifier properties, and clarify the interpretability of the generated masks. If these issues are adequately addressed, the paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. Otherwise, I recommend rejection.
