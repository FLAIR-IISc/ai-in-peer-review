- Introduces a learned, feed-forward masking model for saliency detection as a fast, model-agnostic alternative to iterative methods.
- Training a network to predict salient regions for any differentiable classifier addresses computational bottlenecks in explainable AI.
- Demonstrates competitive results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, with promising weakly supervised localization performance and ablation studies showing generalizability.
- Proposes a new saliency metric intended to capture properties beyond simple localization accuracy.
- Major strength: novelty and potential for real-time applications due to substantial speed advantage over iterative approaches.
- Concern: heavy reliance on weakly supervised localization, which can reflect contextual cues rather than true object saliency.
- The proposed saliency metric may introduce artifacts via cropping/resizing, favor small regions, and exploit classifiers lacking scale invariance.
- The metric’s claim as a good measure of interpretability is questionable without addressing sensitivity to scale/aspect changes.
- Architecture choice (U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) is taken as given; sensitivity to alternative architectures is not explored.
- Training details lack justification for regularization parameters (λ1–λ4), making choices seem arbitrary.
- Reproducibility concerns: unclear whether localization errors in Table 1 are averaged over all instances or only correct predictions.
- Comparison to LRP lacks specificity about the variant and parameter settings used.
- Applicability is limited by strong dependence on the quality and scale invariance of the underlying classifier, restricting use cases.
- Method cannot be reliably used during training to detect or mitigate biases and may perform poorly in domains where scale is irrelevant (e.g., spectrograms).
- Visualizations may reflect biases of the masking network rather than the target classifier’s true decision process, undermining interpretability.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, fast approach; promising results; technically sound and well-written; addresses a relevant problem.
- Arguments for rejection: overreliance on weak localization metrics, flawed saliency metric, lack of architectural sensitivity analysis, strong classifier dependence, and ambiguity about whose representations are shown.
- Overall recommendation: lean toward conditional acceptance pending major revisions to address metric limitations, classifier-dependence, and interpretability/clarity issues.
