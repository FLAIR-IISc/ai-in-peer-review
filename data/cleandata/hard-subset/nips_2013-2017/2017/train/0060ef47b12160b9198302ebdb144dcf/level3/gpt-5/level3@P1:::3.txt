This paper proposes a fast, model-agnostic saliency method that learns to generate per-class masks for any differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass. The core idea is to train a masking network (a U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder) to produce smooth, compact masks that both preserve the target class when applied (SSR) and destroy it when inverted (SDR). To mitigate adversarial artifacts from masking, the authors apply the mask against an “alternative image” A chosen stochastically (strong blur or random color+noise) and regularize with TV and area penalties. A simple class-embedding “feature filter” gates high-level features to initialize localization, followed by multi-scale upsampling to sharpen masks. Training includes a “fake-label” strategy (30% probability) to force the model to use the class selector. The method is evaluated on ImageNet with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 as black-box classifiers and on CIFAR-10 with a FitNet black box. The authors also introduce a crop-based saliency metric s(a,p) designed to capture SSR: tighter crops with high class probability score better (lower is better). Empirically, the approach achieves competitive weakly supervised localization (e.g., 36.7% error with ResNet-50 black box) and favorable saliency metric values, while running at ~100 224×224 images/s on a single GPU.

Relation to prior work: The method is positioned between gradient/backprop saliency (e.g., Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop) and optimization-based perturbation methods (e.g., Zhou et al. iterative erasing; Cao et al.; Fong & Vedaldi’s Meaningful Perturbations). Compared to iterative perturbation approaches, this paper amortizes the optimization into a learned, feed-forward predictor, while explicitly balancing SSR and SDR objectives and addressing artifact introduction through randomized A and TV. The use of a learned masking network for real-time saliency with black-box classifiers (during training, differentiable access) appears novel and practically impactful.

Quality: The objective is well-motivated, addressing both preservation and deletion criteria with explicit regularization to reduce artifacts. The architectural choices (multi-scale U-Net, feature filter, two-channel mask output) are sensible and empirically justified. The training tricks (fake labels, random A) are pragmatic and likely important. Experimental evaluation on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 is reasonably broad; weakly supervised localization and the new saliency metric provide complementary views of quality. However, some important details are missing for reproducibility and for fully substantiating claims: throughput numbers beyond 224×224; behavior on small objects; ablations of loss terms and A choices; and clarity about the “black-box” assumption (gradients are needed during training).

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, with good intuition, figures, and a coherent training objective. The new metric is explained and motivated. Minor wording issues/typos noted below.

Originality: Training a dedicated masking model to approximate the solutions of perturbation-based saliency (jointly optimizing SSR and SDR) and to run in real-time is a fresh combination. The randomized evidence-removal mechanism and fake-label training are thoughtful contributions.

Significance: If the speed and robustness claims hold in realistic settings (higher resolutions, multi-object scenes), this could enable real-time explanation in applications like video and robotics, and may serve as a strong weakly supervised localization/segmentation prior. The saliency metric could be useful to the community, although further validation would strengthen its impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Single-pass, class-conditional, high-quality masks; sharp and interpretable outputs.
- Strong practical contribution: orders-of-magnitude faster than iterative perturbation methods; plausible for real-time use.
- Well-motivated objective that balances SSR and SDR; explicit measures to minimize artifacts.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization on ImageNet and good performance on the proposed saliency metric; evidence of generalization (CIFAR-10).
- Clear exposition and sensible architectural/training design choices.

Cons and questions:
- Real-time claim needs end-to-end clarity. Does ~100 imgs/s include resizing, mask generation, and the black-box forward pass? Please report wall-clock throughput and latency for 640×480 (e.g., iPhone 6s images) and for common video resolutions (e.g., 720p/1080p), including preprocessing.
- Small-object performance is unclear. With a 224×224 input and a mask produced at 1/4 resolution, how does the method handle small instances? Please report localization/saliency metrics stratified by object size and discuss potential improvements (e.g., higher-res inputs, multi-scale inference).
- “Black box” ambiguity. Training requires gradients through the classifier to optimize the masking model. Please clarify the exact assumptions: differentiable access during training, no access at test time. Consider rephrasing to “gradient-access during training; black-box at inference.”
- Ablations. Provide ablations for each loss term (SSR/SDR/TV/area) and for the choice of A (blur vs random+noise vs fixed color), including effects on artifacts and the saliency metric.
- Comparisons on the new metric. Table 3 should include the metric for other strong baselines reported in Table 2, especially Fong & Vedaldi (2017) and any recent CAM/Grad-CAM variants, to contextualize improvements.
- Metric design. The log-product form and area floor (0.05) are somewhat ad hoc. Please analyze sensitivity to the floor and discuss correlation with human judgments or with localization/segmentation quality.
- Generalization/robustness. How does the masking model transfer across classifiers or datasets? Any cross-model transfer results? What happens on multi-label images when the requested class is absent?

Suggestions:
- Report per-class and per-size breakdowns; provide visual failure cases, particularly for cluttered scenes and small objects.
- Include runtime and memory profiling across resolutions; discuss scaling strategies (tiling, pyramid).
- Consider a segmentation-oriented variant (mask at input resolution) and evaluate on weakly supervised segmentation benchmarks to support the future-work claim.
- Release code and pretrained models; include training details (optimizer, LR schedules, batch sizes) and exact throughput measurement setup.

Minor issues:
- LN 151: clarify wording regarding [3] and whether the mask is optimized directly or via the masking model.
- LN 273: “More over” → “Moreover.”
- Standardize notation for s(a,p) and confirm whether it is a product or sum of logs; define clearly in the main text and tables.

Overall: A well-motivated, practical, and empirically promising approach to fast, high-quality saliency for differentiable classifiers. With added clarity on runtime, small-object behavior, stronger comparisons on the proposed metric, and ablations, this would be a strong contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
