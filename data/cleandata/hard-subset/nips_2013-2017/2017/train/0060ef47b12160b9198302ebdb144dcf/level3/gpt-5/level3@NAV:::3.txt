This paper proposes a learned, real-time saliency mask generator that treats the classifier as a black box during training, and at test time predicts a class-conditional mask in one forward pass. The model uses a multi-scale U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder and a class-conditioned “feature filter” to localize, followed by upsampling to refine. A saliency objective combines smoothness, area, SSR- and SDR-inspired terms while randomizing evidence removal to reduce artifacts. The paper further introduces an SSR-aligned metric based on the tightest rectangular crop and reports improved weakly supervised localization on ImageNet across AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet black boxes. This builds on and contrasts well with iterative/optimization-based saliency (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi; CAM/Excitation Backprop), and relates to prior NIPS work on explanations and localization.

Quality: The approach is technically plausible and thoughtfully engineered (mask regularization, randomized A, class-embedding filter). Claims are supported by ImageNet localization and the new metric, though I cannot fully verify all results. Please clarify if Table 3 includes direct numbers for prior methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi [2]) and the exact evaluation protocol used to compute them.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized, with helpful intuition and design rationale. Minor typos: LN 273 “More over” → “Moreover”; LN 151: the wording around “[3] optimizing the mask” is confusing—ResNet-50 serves as encoder/backbone rather than performing the optimization.

Originality: The shift from iterative black-box optimization to a trainable, class-conditional masking network is a compelling and novel combination of ideas; the crop-based metric is also interesting, though sensitive to resizing assumptions.

Significance: Fast, sharp, black-box saliency with competitive weakly supervised localization is valuable. Real-time claims could enable video use cases.

Concerns and questions:
- Real-time claim: Does “~100 224×224 images/sec” include image resizing, mask upsampling, and bounding-box extraction? What GPU, batch size, and precision? Please report end-to-end throughput for higher-res inputs typical of mobile photos (e.g., 640×480 on an iPhone 6s).
- Small objects: How does performance vary with object size? Is 224×224 sufficient for small targets, and does the 32× downsampling harm recall? Provide size-stratified metrics or results at higher input resolution.
- Metric: Because crops are resized ignoring aspect ratio, how sensitive are scores to distortions? Consider an ablation.
- Table alignment: Please add Table 3 entries for all baselines in Table 2 (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated method; strong empirical results; practical speed; black-box applicability; novel metric.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incomplete runtime characterization; limited analysis of small-object and high-resolution settings; metric comparability across methods needs clearer reporting.
