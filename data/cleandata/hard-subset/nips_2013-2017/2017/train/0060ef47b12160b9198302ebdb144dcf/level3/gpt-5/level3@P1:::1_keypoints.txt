- Proposes a fast feed-forward masking model (U-Net with fixed ResNet-50 encoder) that predicts class-conditional saliency masks trained with SSR/SDR objectives, TV penalty, and randomized alternative images.

- Introduces a saliency metric based on the log-area of a tight crop and the log-probability of the class on the resized crop.

- Demonstrates speed and localization improvements on ImageNet (AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50) and feasibility on CIFAR-10.

- Strength: conceptually clean amortized “learn-to-explain” approach enabling single-pass saliency at >100 fps.

- Strength: thoughtful objective balancing area, smoothness, preserve/destroy scores with practical steps (randomized evidence removal, TV) to reduce adversarial artifacts.

- Strength: produces sharp, interpretable masks that localize discriminative parts rather than diffuse blobs.

- Strength: competitive weakly supervised localization without localization supervision; not strictly tied to pretraining (CIFAR-10).

- Weakness: evaluation is indirect—localization can be consistent with labels while failing to reveal true classifier evidence when context drives predictions.

- Weakness: the proposed metric is problematic—cropping/resizing can distort scale/aspect and favor tiny regions or scale-variant classifiers.

- Weakness: claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers is overstated—training requires gradients and stable score responses.

- Weakness: unclear architecture dependence—no ablations on encoder choice, skip connections, resolution, or output nonlinearity to separate architecture vs objective effects.

- Weakness: lack of quantitative ablations for evidence-removal choices, TV weight, and area penalty on artifact suppression and metric performance.

- Weakness: CIFAR-10 results lack quantitative comparisons and robustness checks (cross-model generalization, scale/rotation sensitivity).

- Quality: technically sound and well-motivated, but evaluation misses important controls (scale/aspect ablations, target-model invariance, architecture variants).

- Clarity requests: clarify Table 1 protocol, exact baseline implementations/parameters, sensitivity to λ1–λ4 and 0.5 binarization, A choices, and whether target-classifier gradients are used.

- Originality: novel amortization of meaningful-perturbation-style objectives and combined SSR/SDR terms; metric contribution limited by caveats.

- Significance: real-time single-pass saliency is practically valuable, but impact limited by reliance on classifier scale/aspect invariance and metric biases.

- Pros for acceptance: fast, practical method with clear qualitative improvements, solid objective design, competitive localization, and novel amortization.

- Cons for acceptance: evaluation does not convincingly validate faithfulness; metric may reward pathological solutions; overstated generality; missing ablations and analysis of context failures.

- Requested experiments/sanity checks: ablate A/TV/area penalty and mask nonlinearity; test different encoders; quantify scale/aspect sensitivity and context-biased classes; add deletion/insertion or occlusion checks without resizing.

- Post-rebuttal note: authors confirmed strong dependence on masking network and target classifier scale invariance—paper should explicitly temper “black-box” claims and limitations.

- Recommendation: borderline—lean accept if limitations are addressed and targeted ablations/sanity checks are added; otherwise lean reject.
