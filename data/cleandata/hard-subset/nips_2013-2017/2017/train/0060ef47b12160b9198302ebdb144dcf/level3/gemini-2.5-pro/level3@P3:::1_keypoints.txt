- Paper proposes a learnable saliency model (U-Net with ResNet encoder) that generates masks in one forward pass, trained to find Smallest Sufficient and Smallest Destroying Regions.  
- Contributions claimed: trainable saliency model, real-time inference, and strong results on CIFAR-10/ImageNet with a new saliency metric and weakly supervised localization.

Strengths:
- Novelty/significance: moving from per-instance optimization to amortized, learning-based explanations is a promising and important direction.  
- Performance: inference is orders of magnitude faster than optimization-based methods.  
- Objective: loss combines desirable properties (TV smoothness, area penalty, sufficiency/necessity) and is well-motivated.

Major weaknesses / concerns:
- Weakly supervised localization is a poor proxy for saliency because classifiers may use contextual cues; good saliency could reduce localization scores.  
- Proposed crop-and-resize saliency metric is flawed: resizing distorts scale/aspect ratio, favors tiny discriminative patches when upscaled, and may not reflect saliency in the original image.  
- Method depends on classifier invariances (scale/aspect-ratio robustness), limiting applicability to classifiers/domains lacking such invariance and preventing use during classifier training.  
- Heavy architectural priors (U-Net + pretrained ResNet encoder) may bias masks; no ablation to show whether masks reflect the target classifier or the masking network.  
- "Black-box" claim is misleading: training requires differentiability and gradient access to the classifier, so it is not truly black-box in practice.

Minor points / clarifications requested:
- Clarify whether Table 1 localization errors are computed over all validation images or only those with correct top-1 predictions.  
- Specify which variants and parameter settings were used for gradient-based baselines and LRP, as outputs depend on those choices.

Recommendation:
- Borderline accept conditional on adding a comprehensive discussion of the limitations (localization as proxy, crop-and-resize artifacts, reliance on classifier invariances) and reframing claims about generality.
