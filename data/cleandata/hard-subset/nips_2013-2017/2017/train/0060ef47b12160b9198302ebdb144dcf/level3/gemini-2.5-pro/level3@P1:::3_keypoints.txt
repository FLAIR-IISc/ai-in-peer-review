- Proposes a learned "masking model" (U-Net-like with ResNet-50 encoder) that predicts saliency masks in one forward pass for any differentiable black-box image classifier, avoiding costly per-image iterative optimization.  
- Training objective combines Total Variation (smoothness), mask size, Smallest Sufficient Region (classification on masked-in region), and Smallest Destroying Region (classification failure on masked-out region).  
- Introduces a new saliency evaluation metric measuring the "concentration of information" within a cropped salient region.  
- Experiments on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet show sharp, interpretable masks and state-of-the-art weakly supervised object localization (36.7% error on ImageNet).  
- Originality/significance: reframing saliency as a learned prediction problem shifts computation to training, enabling real-time performance and potential video applications.  
- Technical quality: well-motivated objective, appropriate architecture, and a clever training trick using "fake" class labels to enforce class conditioning.  
- Empirical strength: strong quantitative and qualitative results, including competitive localization versus fully supervised methods and successful training from scratch on CIFAR-10.  
- Clarity/presentation: well-written with clear intuition and a thoughtfully proposed new metric.  
- Runtime clarification needed: real-time claim (100+ images/sec) lacks details on preprocessing and runtimes for larger, real-world image sizes (e.g., 640x480, 1280x720).  
- Small-object performance concern: downsampling to 224x224 may hinder localization of small objects; evaluation on such cases is not reported.  
- Incomplete metric comparison: new saliency metric is not reported for other competing saliency methods, limiting direct comparisons.  
- Minor issues: wording should more sharply distinguish from iterative methods like Fong & Vedaldi, and fix typos (e.g., "More over" → "Moreover").
