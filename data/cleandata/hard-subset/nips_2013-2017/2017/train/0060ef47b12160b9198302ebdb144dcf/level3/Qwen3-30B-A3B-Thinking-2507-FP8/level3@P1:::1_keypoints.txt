- Proposes a fast saliency detection method for black-box classifiers using a U-Net masking model that predicts saliency maps in a single forward pass.  
- Targets the speed limitation of iterative saliency methods, achieving 100+ masks per second.  
- Trains with a composite loss encouraging smooth, small masks, high confidence on preserved regions, and low confidence after removal.  
- Introduces a new evaluation metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) rather than traditional localization.  
- Uses randomized evidence removal (blur or noise) to mitigate introduced-evidence/adversarial artifacts.  
- Shows strong results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10; ResNet model attains 36.7% localization error (near supervised 34.3%).  
- Strengths: well-motivated, genuinely fast, competitive benchmarks, and a clever randomized removal scheme.  
- Primary limitation: indirect evaluation via weakly supervised localization fails when classifiers rely on contextual cues instead of object pixels.  
- SSR metric problems: favors tiny regions and is vulnerable to artifacts from cropping/resizing (aspect-ratio and scale sensitivity).  
- Metric may reward exploitation of classifier scale invariance rather than true saliency.  
- Claim of "black-box" applicability is undermined by dependence on masking architecture and classifier scale invariance.  
- No analysis of different masking architectures or of performance on classifiers lacking scale invariance (e.g., spectrogram or medical models).  
- Missing experimental details: unclear averaging method for Table 1 localization errors and unspecified LRP variants used for comparison.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and explains technical/model details and experiments thoroughly.  
- Significance is constrained: valuable for scale-invariant, real-time settings but not broadly generalizable as claimed.  
- Recommendation: address evaluation flaws, clarify applicability limits, add architectural and classifier-robustness analyses; currently borderline.
