- Proposes a learned, real-time class-conditional saliency mask generator that treats the classifier as a black box and predicts masks in one forward pass using a multi-scale U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder and class-conditioned feature filter.  
- Uses a saliency objective combining smoothness, area, SSR-/SDR-inspired terms and randomized evidence removal to reduce artifacts.  
- Introduces an SSR-aligned metric based on the tightest rectangular crop.  
- Reports improved weakly supervised localization on ImageNet across AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet black boxes.  
- Builds on and contrasts with iterative/optimization-based saliency methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi; CAM/Excitation Backprop) and prior NIPS work on explanations/localization.  
- Approach is technically plausible and thoughtfully engineered (mask regularization, randomized A, class-embedding filter); results are supported though not fully verifiable by reviewer.  
- Request clarification whether Table 3 includes direct numbers for prior methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi) and exact evaluation protocol for those comparisons.  
- Paper is clearly written and well organized; minor typos: LN273 “More over” → “Moreover”; LN151 wording about “[3] optimizing the mask” is confusing (ResNet-50 is encoder/backbone).  
- Originality: shift from iterative black-box optimization to a trainable class-conditional masking network is novel; crop-based metric is interesting but may be sensitive to resizing.  
- Significance: fast, sharp, black-box saliency with competitive localization is valuable and could enable real-time/video use cases.  
- Real-time claim needs clarification: does “~100 224×224 images/sec” include resizing, mask upsampling, bounding-box extraction? Report GPU, batch size, precision and end-to-end throughput for higher-res inputs (e.g., 640×480).  
- Small-object concern: how does performance vary with object size, is 224×224 and 32× downsampling sufficient, and provide size-stratified metrics or higher-resolution results.  
- Metric concern: crop resizing ignores aspect ratio—how sensitive are scores to distortions; include an ablation.  
- Please add Table 3 entries for all baselines listed in Table 2 (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi).  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear, well-motivated method; strong empirical results; practical speed; black-box applicability; novel metric.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete runtime characterization; limited analysis of small-object and high-resolution settings; metric comparability across methods needs clearer reporting.
