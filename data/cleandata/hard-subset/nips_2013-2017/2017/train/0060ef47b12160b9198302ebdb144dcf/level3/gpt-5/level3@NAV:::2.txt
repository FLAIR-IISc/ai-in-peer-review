This paper proposes a learned saliency predictor that, given an image and a target class, outputs a mask in a single forward pass indicating regions most important for a separate classifier’s decision. The approach optimizes a principled objective that blends SSR (preserve minimal evidence to keep the class score high) and SDR (remove minimal evidence to drop the class score), while discouraging adversarial artifacts via TV regularization and randomized evidence-replacement schemes (blur or noise). A U-Net-style masking network with a frozen ResNet-50 encoder is trained against fixed black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50). The paper evaluates on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, reports strong weakly supervised localization, and introduces a new metric s(a,p) to quantify the compactness and sufficiency of salient content. The work relates clearly to gradient/backprop methods (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), perturbation-based explanations (Fong & Vedaldi), and weakly supervised localization (CAM-style methods), and aims to combine their interpretability with real-time speed.

Quality: Technically sound, with a well-motivated objective, careful discussion of introduced evidence, and practical training tricks (fake labels, randomized A). Results convincingly outperform prior weakly supervised baselines on challenging datasets using state-of-the-art networks, and masks are sharp and artifact-resistant. One limitation is that “black box” here still assumes differentiability and backprop access during training.

Clarity: The paper is well written and easy to follow; architectural and training details are sufficient for reproduction. The new metric is clearly defined and interpreted.

Originality: Combining SSR and SDR in a trainable saliency model that generalizes across images and runs in real time is novel relative to iterative perturbation methods and standard backprop saliency.

Significance: Advances the state of the art in weakly supervised localization and provides a practical, fast tool likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Motivate concrete real-time applications (e.g., on-device explainability, interactive debugging, video analytics, autonomous systems) and report end-to-end throughput/latency trade-offs.
- Provide analysis of learned saliency: per-category outside-GT-box ratios, categories relying on background context (e.g., “polar bear”→snow), and failure modes across classifiers.
- Ablate the evidence-replacement scheme and TV weight; report sensitivity to mask resolution and thresholds; evaluate targeted non-ground-truth classes.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong empirical gains with sharp, interpretable, real-time saliency maps.
- Clear, principled objective and thorough evaluation on challenging datasets.
- Useful new metric for saliency sufficiency/compactness.

Arguments against acceptance:
- “Black box” requires differentiable access during training; query-only applicability is not addressed.
- Limited analysis of biases in learned masks and application-oriented evaluation.

Overall, I lean positive; the contributions are substantial and well supported.
