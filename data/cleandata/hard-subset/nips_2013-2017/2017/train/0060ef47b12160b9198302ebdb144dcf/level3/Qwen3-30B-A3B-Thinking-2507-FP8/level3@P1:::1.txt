

This paper proposes a novel approach to fast image saliency detection for black-box classifiers by training a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. The authors address a significant limitation of existing methods, which require iterative optimization for each input image and are too slow for real-time applications. Their approach trains a U-Net-based masking model that learns to generate saliency masks by minimizing a composite objective function that encourages smooth masks, small preserved regions, high classification confidence on preserved regions, and low classification confidence after salient regions are removed. The method demonstrates impressive speed (100+ masks per second) and achieves competitive results on ImageNet object localization using a new saliency metric.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it introduces a new metric for evaluating saliency maps that focuses on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) rather than traditional localization accuracy. This metric directly measures the quality of the saliency map by assessing how small a region can be cropped while still maintaining high classification confidence. Second, the authors provide a principled solution to the "introduced evidence" problem through a randomized evidence removal scheme (blurred images or random noise), which helps prevent adversarial artifacts in the masks. Third, they demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both ImageNet and CIFAR-10 datasets, showing that the masking model can generalize well across different classifiers.

The paper's strengths are clear: the technical approach is well-motivated, the method is genuinely fast compared to existing alternatives, and the evaluation shows competitive results on standard benchmarks. The authors' use of a randomized evidence removal scheme is particularly clever and addresses an important limitation of prior work. The paper also demonstrates strong empirical results on the ImageNet weakly supervised localization task, with the ResNet-trained model achieving a 36.7% error rate, only slightly worse than fully supervised approaches (34.3%).

However, several critical limitations need to be addressed. The primary concern is the indirect evaluation of saliency quality through weakly supervised localization, which is problematic when contextual cues (rather than object location) determine the classifier's decision. For example, if a classifier identifies "polar bear" based on the presence of snow rather than the bear itself, the localization metric would incorrectly evaluate the saliency map as good. This undermines the paper's claim that the method reliably reveals what the classifier is "looking at."

The proposed saliency metric also has significant flaws. The metric (s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p)) favors small salient regions, but cropping and resizing these regions to classifier input dimensions can introduce artifacts due to aspect ratio changes and scale sensitivity. This means that the metric may be rewarding masks that exploit the classifier's scale invariance rather than truly capturing the salient regions. The paper does not adequately address how these artifacts affect the metric's validity.

Another critical limitation is the paper's claim of "black-box" applicability, which is contradicted by the method's strong dependence on the masking network architecture and the classifier's scale invariance. The paper does not explore how different masking architectures affect results, nor does it address whether the method works for classifiers that lack scale invariance (e.g., those used in spectrogram analysis or medical imaging). The post-rebuttal concern about the method's reliance on classifier quality and scale invariance is well-founded and significantly limits the approach's generalizability.

The paper also lacks important details: it's unclear whether the localization error results in Table 1 are averaged over all classes or only correct predictions, and the LRP variants used for comparison are not specified. These omissions make it difficult to fully evaluate the comparative results.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a good discussion of related work and a thoughtful analysis of the saliency problem. The technical details of the masking model and the objective function are well-explained, and the experiments are thorough.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a real need for fast saliency methods but the limitations in evaluation and applicability prevent it from making a truly transformative contribution. The method is valuable for specific applications where scale invariance holds and real-time performance is critical, but it cannot be broadly applied to all classifiers as claimed.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound and promising approach to fast saliency detection with clear advantages over iterative methods. However, the limitations in evaluation methodology and the lack of discussion about the method's applicability constraints significantly undermine its contribution. If these limitations were properly addressed in the final version, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As written, the paper is borderline - it has significant strengths but also important weaknesses that need to be resolved. The authors should provide a more nuanced discussion of the limitations and clarify the conditions under which their method is applicable.
