

The paper "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers" presents a novel approach to fast and accurate saliency detection that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The authors address a critical gap in the field: while existing saliency methods either produce low-quality explanations (gradient-based approaches [11, 12, 16]) or require computationally intensive iterative processes (e.g., [1, 17, 2]), their method trains a masking model to predict saliency maps in a single forward pass. This work builds upon NIPS contributions like Zhou et al. [17] and Fong and Vedaldi [2] but makes a significant advance by eliminating the need for iterative optimization while maintaining high-quality saliency maps.

The key innovation is the masking model, a U-Net architecture with ResNet-50 as the encoder, trained to minimize an objective function that balances mask smoothness, small region size, classification accuracy on preserved regions, and low classification probability after salient regions are removed. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which better evaluates interpretability than standard localization accuracy. This metric is well-motivated and provides a more principled way to assess saliency quality.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, with thorough explanations of the methodology and careful attention to potential pitfalls like adversarial artifacts. The authors demonstrate their method on both CIFAR-10 and ImageNet using state-of-the-art networks as black-box classifiers. Their approach outperforms previous weakly supervised methods on the ImageNet object localization task (36.7% error rate compared to 34.3% for fully supervised VGG [10]), and significantly surpasses baseline methods like the centrally placed rectangle (Table 2). The new saliency metric (Table 3) confirms their method produces saliency maps with interpretability comparable to ground truth bounding boxes.

The paper's strengths are substantial: the technical approach is sound, the experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, and the method's real-time capability (100 saliency masks per second) opens new application domains. The authors carefully address the problem of adversarial artifacts through their evidence removal function and mask smoothness constraints, which is a critical contribution often overlooked in related work.

The main weaknesses are minor but worth noting. The paper could better motivate specific real-time applications (as suggested in the key points), such as video analysis for autonomous vehicles where the authors briefly mention in their conclusion. Additionally, a deeper analysis of the learned salient regions would be valuable—particularly examining whether certain categories (like "polar bear" in the introduction) exploit background biases, which would provide more insight into the model's behavior and limitations.

The paper excels on all four review criteria. Quality is excellent: the method is technically sound, claims are well-supported by experimental results, and the authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses. Clarity is outstanding, with well-organized writing and sufficient detail for reproducibility. Originality is strong—the masking model approach is novel and clearly differentiates from related work. Significance is high, as the method enables new applications and provides a practical tool for understanding black-box classifiers, advancing the state of the art in interpretable machine learning.

The paper makes a significant contribution to NIPS 2017's focus on explainable AI. It provides a practical, fast, and accurate solution to a pressing problem in computer vision, with clear potential for real-world impact. The authors' attention to the practical constraints of real-time systems is particularly valuable.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's strengths—particularly its technical soundness, thorough evaluation, and practical contribution—significantly outweigh the minor weaknesses. The suggested improvements (better application motivation and deeper analysis of salient regions) are straightforward to address in the camera-ready version. This work represents an important step toward making black-box classifiers more interpretable and trustworthy, with clear relevance to both researchers and practitioners in the field.
