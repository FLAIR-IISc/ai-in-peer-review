This paper introduces a novel and efficient method for generating saliency maps for black-box image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate neural network, termed a "masking model," to directly predict a saliency mask for a given image and target class in a single forward pass. This stands in contrast to previous perturbation-based or optimization-based methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017) that require a slow, iterative process for each new image. The authors propose a comprehensive objective function for training this masking model, which encourages the generated masks to be small and smooth, while simultaneously functioning as both a "Smallest Sufficient Region" (preserving the classifier's confidence) and a "Smallest Destroying Region" (destroying the classifier's confidence when the masked region is removed). The masking model itself uses a U-Net architecture with a pre-trained ResNet encoder, allowing it to produce high-resolution, sharp masks. The method is validated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, where it is used to generate saliency maps for several state-of-the-art classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50). The paper also introduces a new metric for evaluating saliency based on the "concentration of information" in a cropped region.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The proposed objective function (Equation 4) is well-motivated, thoughtfully combining four distinct goals: mask smoothness (TV regularization), small mask area, high classifier score on the preserved region, and low classifier score on the removed region. The authors demonstrate a careful approach to training, introducing clever solutions to potential pitfalls, such as using "fake" class labels to ensure the model attends to the class input and randomizing the background fill to prevent the model from exploiting a specific evidence-removal scheme.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. Claims of state-of-the-art performance are well-supported by results on the challenging ImageNet weakly supervised object localization task. The method is shown to outperform several other weakly supervised and saliency-based approaches, establishing a new benchmark. The ablation of using different black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50) is also insightful, showing how the generated masks reflect the differing "needs" of each classifier.

**Clarity**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation for fast saliency detection is clearly articulated in the introduction, and the related work section effectively positions the contribution with respect to prior gradient-based and iterative optimization methods. The methodology is described in sufficient detail, including the model architecture and training procedure, to allow for reproducibility. The figures are clear and effectively illustrate the superior quality of the generated masks compared to alternatives. The introduction and evaluation of the new saliency metric in Section 3.2 is also a valuable and clearly explained contribution.

**Originality**
The primary contribution of this work is highly original. While other methods have sought to find saliency masks via optimization, the idea of training a separate, feed-forward network to *amortize* this optimization process is novel. This reframes the problem from a slow, per-instance search to a fast inference task, which is a significant conceptual shift. The specific architecture and the comprehensive loss function are also novel combinations of existing techniques tailored effectively to this new problem formulation. The work is well-situated within the literature and clearly distinguishes itself from parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi (2017), which remains an iterative, per-instance optimization approach.

**Significance**
The results presented are important and likely to have a significant impact on the field of model interpretability. The ability to generate high-quality saliency maps in real-time (over 100 FPS according to the authors) opens up a range of applications previously infeasible with slower methods. This includes real-time video saliency, interactive debugging of models, and potential use in safety-critical systems like autonomous driving. The strong performance on the weakly supervised localization task demonstrates that the generated explanations are not just visually plausible but are meaningfully tied to the object of interest, advancing the state of the art in that area. Researchers and practitioners seeking to understand and debug their vision models are likely to find this method highly useful.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty:** The core concept of a trainable, feed-forward model for amortized saliency generation is a significant and original contribution.
*   **Performance:** The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the challenging weakly supervised ImageNet object localization benchmark, outperforming previous methods by a clear margin.
*   **Efficiency:** The real-time speed is a major advantage over iterative techniques, enabling new applications in video and interactive systems.
*   **Quality of Masks:** The generated saliency maps are qualitatively sharp, precise, and more interpretable than those from competing methods, as shown in the figures.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, using multiple datasets and state-of-the-art black-box classifiers.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Motivation for Real-Time Application:** While the paper mentions real-time capability as a key advantage, it would be stronger if it provided more concrete motivation for specific applications. The future work section mentions autonomous vehicles, but elaborating on a use-case in the introduction—such as real-time monitoring of a classifier's decision-making process for safety verification—would better highlight the significance of the efficiency gains.
*   **Deeper Analysis of Classifier Behavior:** The paper successfully demonstrates that it can produce high-quality saliency maps. A missed opportunity is to use this powerful new tool for a deeper analysis of the black-box models themselves. For instance, an analysis of which ImageNet classes tend to rely on background or contextual cues (as per the "polar bear on snow" example) versus those that focus tightly on the object could provide valuable insights into the biases of well-known classifiers. Such an analysis would further strengthen the paper's contribution as not just a method, but also a tool for scientific discovery.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, effective, and highly efficient method for image saliency. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and experimental results far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It represents a clear advance in the field of model interpretability and is a strong candidate for acceptance.
