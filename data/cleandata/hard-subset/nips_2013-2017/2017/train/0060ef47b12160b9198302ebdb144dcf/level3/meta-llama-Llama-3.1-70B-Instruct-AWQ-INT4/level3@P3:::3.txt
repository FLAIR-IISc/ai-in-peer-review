This paper proposes a fast image saliency mask generator that can be applied to any differentiable image classifier. The method uses a trainable masking model to predict the saliency map for a given input image in a single feed-forward pass, making it suitable for real-time applications. The authors claim that their approach outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the ImageNet localization task and achieves state-of-the-art results.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its advantages. The introduction provides a good overview of the current state of image saliency detection and the need for a fast and accurate method. The related work section is comprehensive, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of existing methods and their limitations.

The proposed method uses a multi-scale feature map architecture, which is based on the U-Net architecture. The authors use a pre-trained ResNet-50 model as the encoder and train the masking model to predict the saliency map. The objective function is designed to encourage the model to produce sharp and precise masks that are free from adversarial artifacts.

The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on the ImageNet dataset. The authors show that their method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques on the localization task and achieves state-of-the-art results. The saliency maps produced by the model are visually appealing and provide a clear indication of the regions of the image that are most relevant to the classifier.

The authors also propose a new saliency metric that can be used to evaluate the quality of the produced saliency maps. The metric is based on the area of the rectangular crop that contains the salient region and the probability of the requested class returned by the classifier. The authors show that their method achieves a better saliency metric than other approaches.

The paper has several strengths. The proposed method is fast and accurate, making it suitable for real-time applications. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the related work and demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on a challenging dataset. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its advantages.

However, there are some concerns about the real-time speed claim. The authors claim that their method can process over 100 images per second on a standard GPU, but it is unclear whether this includes image resizing. The authors should provide more details about the computational requirements of their method and the hardware used to achieve the reported speed.

Additionally, the authors should provide more analysis on the performance of their method on small objects. The input size of 224x224 may not be sufficient for small objects, and the authors should investigate the effect of input size on the performance of their method.

The paper is technically correct, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its advantages. However, there are some minor typos and grammatical errors that should be corrected.
