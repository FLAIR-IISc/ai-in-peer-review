This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for any differentiable black-box image classifier. The core idea is to train a separate "masking model" that learns to produce a saliency mask for a given image and class label in a single forward pass. This approach stands in contrast to common iterative methods that optimize a mask for each specific image, which are often computationally expensive. The proposed masking model utilizes a U-Net-like architecture with a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder to leverage multi-scale features, enabling the generation of sharp and precise masks. The model is trained to minimize a well-motivated objective function that combines terms for mask smoothness (Total Variation), mask size, classification accuracy on the masked-in region (Smallest Sufficient Region), and classification failure on the masked-out region (Smallest Destroying Region). The authors also introduce a new metric for evaluating saliency based on the "concentration of information" within a cropped salient region. Experiments on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet demonstrate that the method produces high-quality, interpretable saliency maps and achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task, outperforming previous methods.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—framing saliency detection as a learning problem where a separate network is trained to predict masks—is both novel and significant. This shifts the computational burden of optimization from inference time to training time, enabling real-time performance. This is a substantial advantage over iterative perturbation or optimization-based methods (e.g., Fong and Vedaldi, 2017) and produces higher-quality maps than faster gradient-based methods (e.g., Simonyan et al., 2013). This approach opens up new possibilities for real-time applications like video saliency, as mentioned by the authors.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The design of the objective function (Eq. 4) is well-motivated, thoughtfully combining principles of sufficient and destroying regions with regularization to encourage smooth, interpretable masks. The architectural choice of a U-Net with a pre-trained encoder is appropriate for the task of generating high-resolution masks from image features. The training strategy, particularly the use of "fake" class labels to force the model to attend to the class selector, is a clever solution to a potential training pitfall.

3.  **Experimental Results:** The empirical evaluation is strong and convincing. The method's performance on the weakly supervised ImageNet localization task is impressive, achieving an error rate of 36.7%, which is competitive with some fully supervised methods and significantly better than other weakly supervised approaches. The qualitative results shown in Figure 5 clearly demonstrate that the generated masks are sharp and semantically meaningful, superior to those from competing methods. The CIFAR-10 experiment further validates the approach on a different dataset and shows it can be trained from scratch without a pre-trained encoder.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of providing intuition for their design choices, such as the discussion on "introduced evidence" and the motivation for the SSR and SDR concepts. The proposed new saliency metric is also a thoughtful contribution to the field, attempting to formalize the quality of an explanation beyond simple localization accuracy.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

1.  **Clarification on Real-Time Performance:** The claim of "real-time" performance (over 100 images/sec) is a key selling point. However, some practical details are missing.
    *   Does this figure include the time required for image pre-processing, specifically resizing the input image to 224x224?
    *   The proposed application for autonomous vehicles involves video streams, which often have higher resolutions than 224x224. It would strengthen the paper to report runtimes for larger, more realistic image sizes (e.g., 640x480 or 1280x720) to better substantiate the real-world applicability of the method.

2.  **Performance on Small Objects:** All experiments appear to be conducted on 224x224 input images. This raises a question about the model's ability to handle small objects. If a high-resolution image containing a small object of interest is downsampled to 224x224, the features of that object might become too coarse for the masking model to effectively localize. Could the authors comment on the limitations of the approach for localizing small objects and whether the model was evaluated on such cases?

3.  **Incomplete Comparison using the New Metric:** The introduction of a new saliency metric in Section 3.2 is a welcome contribution. However, in the evaluation (Table 3), this metric is only used to compare the proposed model against baselines and ground truth bounding boxes. For a more complete and compelling argument for the superiority of the proposed method's masks, it would be highly valuable to also report the performance of the other saliency methods from Table 2 (e.g., Zhou et al. [17], Fong et al. [2]) on this new metric. This would provide a direct comparison of mask interpretability under the authors' own proposed standard.

4.  **Minor Wording and Typos:**
    *   Line 151: The description of the parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] states they "only optimise the mask iteratively". While true, the key distinction is that the present work trains a *model* to *predict* the mask in one shot, whereas [2] performs iterative optimization per-image at test time. This distinction could be slightly sharpened to better highlight this paper's novelty.
    *   Line 273: "More over" should be corrected to "Moreover".

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a novel and impactful idea. The proposed method for learning a saliency generator is well-executed, technically sound, and supported by strong experimental results. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to requests for clarification or additional experimental details that would further strengthen the claims. The paper advances the state of the art in generating fast and accurate saliency explanations for black-box models.
