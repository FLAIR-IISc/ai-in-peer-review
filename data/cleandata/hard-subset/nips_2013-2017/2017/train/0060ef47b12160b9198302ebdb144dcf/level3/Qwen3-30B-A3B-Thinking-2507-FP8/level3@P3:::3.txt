

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper presents a novel approach to fast saliency detection for black-box classifiers, proposing a model that generates sharp saliency masks in a single forward pass. The authors address a critical need in model interpretability by developing a method that outperforms iterative approaches in both speed and quality. The core innovation lies in training a masking model (based on U-Net architecture with ResNet-50 feature extraction) to predict saliency masks directly, rather than iteratively optimizing them for each image. This approach enables real-time performance (100+ 224x224 images/sec) while producing high-quality masks that outperform existing weakly supervised methods on ImageNet object localization.

The paper makes significant contributions to the field of model interpretability. It builds upon prior work on saliency detection (e.g., [1, 2, 11, 12, 16, 17]) by addressing the critical limitation of speed in existing methods. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from recent related work [2] by emphasizing the model-based, non-iterative nature of their solution. They introduce a new saliency metric based on the smallest sufficient region (SSR) concept, which better evaluates the interpretability of saliency maps than standard localization accuracy metrics.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed method achieves remarkable speed (100+ 224x224 images/sec) while maintaining high quality, addressing a critical gap in the field.
- The architecture design (using multi-scale feature maps with feature filters and upsampling blocks) is well-motivated and effectively implemented.
- The new saliency metric provides a more meaningful evaluation of saliency map quality than standard localization accuracy.
- The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, outperforming other weakly supervised methods on ImageNet localization (36.7% error rate vs. 34.3% for fully supervised VGG).
- The architecture's ability to work on CIFAR-10 without pre-training shows generalization beyond ImageNet.
- The discussion on avoiding adversarial artifacts through mask smoothness and randomized evidence removal is insightful.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**
- The real-time speed claim requires clarification. The 100 images/sec figure appears to be for 224x224 inputs, but the paper doesn't specify whether this includes image resizing or preprocessing. For real-world applications (e.g., iPhone 6s images at 640×480), the runtime would be significantly slower. The authors should provide runtime measurements for larger input sizes.
- The paper doesn't address performance on small objects. For objects smaller than 224x224, the 224x224 input size may be insufficient to capture sufficient detail, potentially harming performance. The authors should discuss this limitation and provide examples of small object performance.
- Table 3 appears to report saliency metrics but doesn't clearly indicate whether these metrics correspond to the methods listed in Table 2. The authors should clarify if the metrics in Table 3 are comparable to those in Table 2.
- Minor typos exist (e.g., "More over" on LN 273 should be "Moreover"; LN 151 wording about [3] optimizing the mask).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The architecture and training procedure are clearly described, and the experimental results are comprehensive. The authors demonstrate careful attention to avoiding adversarial artifacts through their evidence removal strategy. The method appears to be well-implemented and rigorously evaluated.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear figures illustrating key concepts (e.g., Figure 1 showing saliency maps, Figure 5 comparing methods). The introduction of the new saliency metric is particularly well-explained. The only minor issues are the typos noted above.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel approach to fast saliency detection that significantly advances the state of the art in speed while maintaining quality. While related to recent work [2], the authors' model-based approach (as opposed to iterative optimization) represents a meaningful contribution. The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from previous work.

**Significance:** This work has substantial significance for both practical applications (real-time video saliency, autonomous systems) and research (model interpretability). The ability to generate sharp, interpretable saliency maps in real-time enables new applications that were previously impossible with slower iterative methods. The paper's success on weakly supervised object localization demonstrates clear practical value.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of model interpretability with a novel, well-executed approach that achieves both speed and quality. The minor concerns about real-world applicability (input size, small object performance) and table consistency can be addressed with a few clarifications in the revision.

The authors should:
1. Clarify the 100 images/sec claim by providing runtime measurements for larger input sizes (e.g., 640×480)
2. Discuss limitations on small object detection
3. Ensure Table 3 metrics correspond directly to methods in Table 2
4. Correct minor typos

This paper represents an important step toward practical model interpretability, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to NIPS 2017.
