This paper proposes a novel, learning-based approach for generating saliency maps for differentiable image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate neural network, a "masking model" with a U-Net architecture, to produce a saliency mask for a given image and class pair in a single forward pass. This stands in contrast to existing methods that are either gradient-based (e.g., Simonyan et al., 2013) and often produce noisy maps, or are optimization-based (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), which produce cleaner maps but are computationally expensive due to their iterative nature. The authors formulate a sophisticated objective function for training this masking model, which encourages the mask to identify the smallest sufficient region (SSR) and smallest destroying region (SDR), while also promoting smoothness via a total variation penalty. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, primarily through the lens of weakly supervised object localization and a newly proposed saliency metric.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The concept of training a dedicated model to learn to explain another model is a significant and promising direction for the field of interpretability. It elegantly trades the high per-image computational cost of iterative methods for a one-time, upfront training cost. The resulting speed (~100 images/sec) makes real-time applications feasible, which is a major advantage over optimization-based competitors.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, outperforming other saliency methods and weakly supervised techniques. The qualitative results shown in the figures are compelling; the generated masks are sharp, well-defined, and appear to accurately highlight the object of interest.

3.  **Well-Designed Methodology:** The objective function is thoughtfully constructed, combining terms for both sufficiency (SSR) and necessity (SDR) of a region, which is a more comprehensive view of saliency than many previous methods have taken. The training strategy, which includes presenting "fake" labels to force the model to attend to the class selector, is a clever solution to a potential failure mode.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Indirect and Potentially Flawed Evaluation Metrics:** The paper relies heavily on two metrics, both of which have significant limitations.
    *   **Weakly Supervised Localization:** While the results are strong, localization is an imperfect proxy for saliency. A classifier may rely on contextual cues rather than the object itself (e.g., detecting "snow" for a "polar bear"). In such cases, a saliency map correctly highlighting the context would be penalized by the localization metric, while a map that incorrectly highlights the object would be rewarded. This fundamental disconnect between "what the model looks at" and "where the object is" is a key challenge in saliency evaluation that is not addressed.
    *   **Proposed Saliency Metric:** The proposed metric, `s(a, p)`, is conceptually interesting but methodologically flawed. By cropping the salient region and resizing it to the classifier's required input size, the metric introduces significant artifacts. The aspect ratio and scale of the object are altered, potentially drastically. The metric favors very small salient regions, which are then blown up to the full image size. This procedure tests the classifier's invariance to scale and aspect ratio changes as much as, or perhaps more than, it tests the quality of the saliency map. A classifier that is not robust to such transformations would perform poorly on this metric, irrespective of the mask's quality.

2.  **Overstated Generality and Un-discussed Dependencies:** The paper claims the method is applicable to any "differentiable image classifier" and treats it as a "black box." However, this claim seems too strong.
    *   The method requires the classifier to be differentiable to train the masking model. While this is true for most modern networks, it is an important constraint.
    *   More critically, the evaluation and the underlying assumptions appear to rely heavily on the classifier having strong scale and aspect-ratio invariance. This may not hold for all models or for domains outside of natural images (e.g., spectrograms). This dependency severely limits the practical applicability and should be explicitly discussed.
    *   The performance of the method seems strongly tied to the architecture of the masking model itself (a powerful, pre-trained ResNet-50 within a U-Net). It is unclear how results would change with a different or simpler architecture. This raises the question of whether the visualizations reflect the target classifier or the learned priors and biases of the masking network.

3.  **Lack of Clarity on Minor Details:**
    *   In Table 1, it is unclear if the localization error is calculated over all images in the validation set, or only on images where the black-box classifier predicts the correct class. The latter would be a more faithful evaluation of the explanation for a *correct* decision.
    *   When comparing to other methods, specific variants and parameter settings should be provided for reproducibility. For instance, which variant of LRP was used for the comparison?

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a compelling and novel learning-based framework for generating saliency maps. The primary strengths are the speed of the method and the strong performance on the weakly supervised localization task. The idea of learning-to-explain is valuable and could inspire future work.

However, the paper is hampered by significant limitations in its evaluation protocol and an overstatement of its generality. Both the localization metric and the newly proposed saliency metric are indirect and have methodological flaws that are not discussed. Furthermore, the method's reliance on the target classifier's scale/aspect-ratio invariance and the powerful architecture of the masking network itself raises questions about what is actually being explained.

My recommendation is **Borderline**. I believe the core ideas are worthy of publication, but not without acknowledging the paper's limitations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
+ Novel and fast learning-based approach to saliency.
+ State-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark (weakly supervised localization).
+ Qualitatively sharp and convincing saliency maps.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- The primary evaluation metrics (localization and the new saliency metric) are indirect and have serious methodological flaws.
- The claim of general applicability is too strong; the method has clear dependencies on classifier properties (e.g., scale invariance) that are not discussed.
- It is unclear if the explanations reflect the target model or the biases of the explanation model itself.

If the authors can revise the manuscript to thoroughly discuss and acknowledge the limitations of their evaluation metrics and the dependencies of their method, I would lean towards acceptance. Without these revisions, the claims are not fully supported, and I would recommend rejection.
