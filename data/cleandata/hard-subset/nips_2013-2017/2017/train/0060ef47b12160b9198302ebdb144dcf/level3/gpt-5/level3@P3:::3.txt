Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a trainable, class-conditional masking network that produces saliency maps for arbitrary differentiable image classifiers in a single forward pass. The method is positioned between fast but noisy gradient/backprop-based explanations (e.g., Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop) and slow, high-quality optimization-based perturbation methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi’s Meaningful Perturbations; Cao et al.). The key idea is to train a U-Net–style model with a ResNet-50 encoder to predict masks that simultaneously satisfy SSR and SDR desiderata via a composite loss: TV smoothness, area penalty, classification confidence on preserved region, and suppression after removing the salient region. The model leverages multi-scale features; the coarsest scale is modulated by a learned class embedding (“feature filter”) for initial localization, then refined by upsampling blocks. To reduce adversarial artifacts and overfitting to a masking heuristic, training randomly mixes two evidence-removal baselines (heavy blur vs. random color+noise) and uses a “fake class” strategy to force attention to the class selector. The authors also propose a saliency metric based on the smallest rectangular crop that preserves classification confidence and report state-of-the-art weakly supervised ImageNet localization among contemporaries, while running at roughly 100 224×224 images/s on “a standard GPU.” The approach is related to CAM/weakly supervised localization but differs in providing sharp, per-pixel masks without architectural changes to the classifier and in using a learned amortized mapping rather than per-image optimization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective and training scheme are well-motivated (SSR/SDR terms, TV, area penalty, randomized A). Using a fixed, pretrained encoder and multi-scale refinement is a sensible architectural choice. Treating the classifier as differentiable during training but not needed at inference is practical; however, the paper repeatedly calls the classifier a “black box,” which in practice still requires gradient access for training. Please clarify this constraint.
- Empirical support: The ImageNet weakly supervised localization results are strong, and qualitative masks look clean, with sensible comparisons. The new saliency metric is reasonable and complements localization accuracy, though it depends on classifier invariance to scale/aspect ratio and on the resizing policy; some sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claim. Ablations (e.g., effect of TV, 3rd/4th loss terms, class-embedding filter, random A vs. fixed A, multi-scale vs. single-scale) would improve causal attribution of gains.
- Claims about speed: The real-time claim is compelling but underspecified (hardware, batch size, precision, inclusion of I/O and resizing). See questions below.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized, with intuitive motivation for design choices (adversarial artifacts, random A, fake labels).
- Figures support the narrative. The new metric is explained clearly, though the exact form s(a,p) should be unambiguous (is it log(ã) − log(p) or a ratio?). Also specify the classifier used to compute p in cross-method comparisons.
- Minor phrasing around “black-box” vs. “differentiable” could be tightened.

Originality
- Novel combination: amortized, class-conditional masking optimized for both SSR and SDR, with a multi-scale, class-filtered architecture and randomized evidence-removal during training. The proposed saliency metric via tightest crop is a useful addition.
- Relation to prior work is largely adequate; consider adding comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM if feasible (CAM is cited; Grad-CAM may be contemporaneous).

Significance
- Practical: Producing high-quality masks in a single pass is valuable for interactive and video settings.
- Scientific: Shows that saliency optimization can be amortized with improved quality over iterative perturbation on standard benchmarks.
- If the speed and robustness generalize to higher resolutions and small-object regimes, the impact would be high.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Real-time throughput: Does “~100 224×224 images/sec” include image resizing and data transfer, and is it measured end-to-end when generating saliency for the classifier’s top-1 class (i.e., including a classifier forward pass)? Please report:
  - GPU/CPU model, batch size, precision (FP32/FP16), framework.
  - Throughput and latency for larger inputs, e.g., 640×480 (iPhone 6s photo), and for common video resolutions (720p/1080p).
- Small objects: How does performance vary with object size? Please report localization error binned by object area (e.g., small/medium/large) and qualitative masks on small objects. Is 224×224 input sufficient, or does higher-resolution input yield meaningful gains?
- “Black-box” assumption: During training, do you require gradient access through the classifier? If so, please clarify that the method applies to differentiable classifiers with gradient access at training time (and no access needed at inference).
- Metric coverage: In Table 3, please include the proposed saliency metric for the other methods listed in Table 2, especially Fong & Vedaldi [2], CAM/Excitation Backprop, and the “Center” baseline to enable a consistent cross-metric comparison.
- Box generation details: How sensitive are results to the 0.5 threshold on the mask before boxing? Did you tune this threshold on a validation set?

Strengths
- Single-pass, class-conditional masks; sharp and interpretable.
- Strong weakly supervised localization on ImageNet with multiple classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50).
- Thoughtful training design to avoid artifacts; clear intuition and discussion.
- New, practical saliency metric aligning with SSR intuition.
- Good writing and presentation; related work largely adequate.

Weaknesses
- Real-time claim lacks hardware/throughput specifics and end-to-end accounting; unclear scalability to higher resolutions.
- Limited analysis of small-object performance and mask threshold sensitivity.
- “Black box” terminology is potentially misleading given training-time gradient needs.
- Missing ablations to isolate the contributions of architectural and loss components.
- New metric’s dependence on resizing/aspect ratio invariance could bias comparisons; more analysis warranted.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add detailed runtime benchmarks (incl. preprocessing) across resolutions and report memory/latency.
- Provide object-size–stratified results and a study on input resolution vs. quality/speed.
- Include ablations (loss terms, random A, class filter, multi-scale).
- Expand Table 3 to cover more baselines (e.g., [2]) and report correlation with human-judgment or pointing-game metrics.
- Clarify training-time gradient access to the classifier and scope of applicability (non-differentiable or proprietary models).

Minor issues/typos
- LN 273: “More over” → “Moreover.”
- LN 151: The wording implies [3] (ResNet-50) “optimizes the mask.” Please rephrase to avoid suggesting that [3] is about mask optimization (e.g., “We use ResNet-50 as the encoder; the masking model is trained to optimize the saliency objective.”)

Overall assessment
A strong and timely contribution: amortized saliency with compelling quality and speed. With clarified runtime claims, additional ablations, and small-object/resolution analyses, this paper would be a solid addition to NIPS. I lean positive.
