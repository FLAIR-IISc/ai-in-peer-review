Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a fast, neural-network-based approach to generate saliency masks for image classifiers. Instead of iterative perturbation on a per-image basis, the authors train a U-Net–style masking network (with a ResNet-50 encoder) to predict a saliency mask in a single forward pass, guided by an objective combining: (i) total variation regularization for smoothness, (ii) area penalty to encourage small masks, (iii) an SSR-like term to preserve class confidence under masking, and (iv) an SDR-like term to reduce class confidence when the salient region is removed. To mitigate adversarial artifacts, the method uses randomized evidence removal (blur or noisy/color fill). The authors also propose a new saliency metric based on the log product of crop area and class probability computed on a resized crop. They evaluate on ImageNet and CIFAR-10, report weakly supervised localization accuracy, and show speedups over iterative methods. The work is closely related to perturbation-based explanations (Fong & Vedaldi, 2017) and to CAM/excitation-backprop style localization (Zhou et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2016), but contributes the idea of learning a reusable masking model and an objective unifying SSR and SDR notions.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and motivation; learning-to-explain via a dedicated masking network is a compelling idea.
- Substantial speed advantage (real-time) while maintaining visually sharp masks; likely practical for video and interactive use.
- Thoughtful design to reduce masking artifacts (TV penalty, randomized alternative image A).
- Empirical performance on weakly supervised localization appears competitive, with sensible qualitative differences across black-box backbones (AlexNet vs GoogLeNet/ResNet).
- The SSR/SDR-inspired objective is intuitive and easy to optimize end-to-end.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Evaluation measures saliency only indirectly: (a) weakly supervised localization, and (b) the proposed saliency metric. Both have clear shortcomings.
  • Localization ≠ saliency. When contextual cues (e.g., snow for “polar bear”) determine the class, localization success/failure is not a reliable proxy for explanation quality.
  • The proposed metric crops the salient region and resizes to the classifier’s input without preserving aspect ratio. This can create scale/aspect distortions that introduce classification artifacts and conflate “good saliency” with “favorable re-scaling.”
  • The metric favors small regions: small masks that are blown up at test time can exploit limited scale invariance, yielding deceptively good scores even if they omit relevant evidence.
- Strong dependence on classifier properties. The method and metric assume the target classifier is differentiable and substantially invariant to scale/aspect changes. This limits applicability:
  • Difficult to use during training of the target model (the approach assumes a reasonably trained, fixed classifier with stable invariances).
  • Less suited to domains with rigid coordinate meaning (e.g., spectrograms), or models lacking scale/aspect invariance.
  • Undercuts the claim of general applicability to “black-box” classifiers: training requires backpropagation through the classifier and the metric relies on its invariances.
- Dependence on the masking network architecture is not systematically studied. It is unclear to what extent the saliency visualizations reflect the target classifier versus the inductive bias of the U-Net masking model. The response further suggests results are sensitive to the masking network design.
- Limited ablations. There is little analysis of the effect of objective weights, evidence removal schemes, resolution, or architectural choices on saliency quality and localization.
- Metric design choices (area floor at 0.05, log formulation) are somewhat ad hoc and lack sensitivity analysis.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the objective and training scheme are reasonable, and care is taken to mitigate artifacts. However, the evaluation is limited by the proxy nature of the metrics and by the potential for metric gaming via scale/aspect distortions. Claims of general black-box applicability overreach given the differentiability and invariance requirements.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with helpful figures. Two clarifications would improve reproducibility:
- Table 1: Are localization results computed over the ground-truth class only, or over all predicted classes?
- Please specify exactly which LRP variant and parameter settings were used in comparisons (if used), to ensure apples-to-apples baselines.

Originality
Learning a reusable saliency masking network in one pass, with an SSR+SDR objective, is a novel and useful combination relative to purely iterative perturbation approaches and backprop-based saliency maps.

Significance
If the limitations and dependencies are understood and communicated, the contribution is practically valuable (speed) and conceptually interesting (learning-to-explain). However, the current evaluation and metric design limit confidence that the learned masks faithfully reflect the target classifier across settings where invariances do not hold.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Architecture dependence: What other masking-network backbones were tried? How do results vary with depth, skip connections, and pretraining?
- Black-box claim: Please clarify the extent to which the approach requires gradient access and classifier properties (e.g., scale/aspect invariance). Can it work with non-differentiable or proprietary models?
- Metric robustness: Report results when preserving aspect ratio via padding instead of anisotropic resize; also report top-1 accuracy drop on the original image after masking, to assess SDR without cropping confounds. Can you include area–confidence tradeoff curves instead of a single scalar?
- Evidence removal: Provide ablations for the choice and mixing of A (blur vs color+noise).
- Table 1 computation details: exact class set used; confidence thresholds; multiple-instance handling.

Post-rebuttal comments
The rebuttal reinforced that performance depends strongly on the masking network and on the classifier’s scale invariance/quality. This further limits applicability (e.g., not usable during classifier training, fragile for domains lacking invariance) and challenges the “general black-box” claim. These dependencies should be explicitly acknowledged, with mitigations or scope statements.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, fast saliency with good qualitative quality.
- Novel learning-to-explain approach; unifies SSR and SDR.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization results.

Cons for acceptance
- Indirect and partly flawed evaluation; metric can be gamed by scale/aspect artifacts and favors tiny regions.
- Heavy dependence on classifier invariances and masking-network design; unclear generality.
- Overstated “black-box” claim given differentiability and invariance requirements.

Recommendation
Borderline. I see good ideas and practical value in learning a fast masking model, but I would not object to either acceptance or rejection. If the paper clearly and prominently discusses the above limitations (especially metric flaws, architecture dependence, and scope of applicability), I lean accept; otherwise, reject.

Reviewer confidence: moderate.
