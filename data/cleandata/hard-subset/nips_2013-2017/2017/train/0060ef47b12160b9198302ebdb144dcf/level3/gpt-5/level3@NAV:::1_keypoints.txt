- Proposes a trainable masking network (U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder) that amortizes Fong & Vedaldi’s “meaningful perturbation” to produce saliency masks in one forward pass.  
- Optimization mixes SSR and SDR objectives with TV and area penalties and uses randomized evidence-removal baselines to reduce adversarial artifacts.  
- Introduces a saliency metric based on cropping the predicted salient region and trading off area versus class probability.  
- Experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 report sharp, interpretable masks, competitive weakly supervised localization, and high throughput.  

- Clear motivation to amortize perturbation-based saliency and a simple objective yielding strong runtime advantages.  
- Empirical comparisons use multiple backbone classifiers; masks are visually sharper than baselines.  
- Sensible engineering (TV, randomized baselines, class-selector trick) to mitigate artifacts.  
- Interesting idea of learning a class embedding within the masker that could be reused.  

- Saliency evaluation is indirect (weakly supervised localization and the crop-resize metric), both of which have important limitations.  
- Localization can be misleading when context, not object location, determines class (e.g., “polar bear” inferred from snow).  
- The proposed crop-resize metric is problematic: aspect-ratio-agnostic resizing changes scale/geometry and can introduce classification artifacts.  
- The metric favors tiny regions that, when upsampled, exploit limited classifier scale invariance.  
- Post-rebuttal dependence on classifier quality and scale invariance limits applicability (unsuitable during training, for models without invariance, or non-image domains like spectrograms).  
- “Black-box” claim is overstated: training requires gradients and a separate masker per classifier was used.  
- Strong dependence on the masking network raises uncertainty whether visualizations reflect the target model or the masker’s inductive bias.  

- Clarity requests: clarify whether Table 1 uses ground-truth class only, top-1 prediction, or class-averaged results; specify which LRP variant and parameters were used.  
- Originality: moderate — novel in amortizing perturbation-based saliency and some training tricks; metric design is less convincing.  
- Significance: practical speedup could be useful, but evaluation weaknesses and dependence on classifier/architecture constrain generality.  

- Pros: fast, sharp masks; simple and reproducible framework; useful engineering insights.  
- Cons: weak validation of saliency, flawed metric, reliance on scale invariance and masker architecture, overstated black-box claim.  

- Recommendation: Borderline — acceptable only if the paper candidly discusses limitations, tempers generality claims, adds controls (aspect-ratio–preserving crops, scale-controlled tests, context-sensitive classes), and reports architecture dependence and ablations.
