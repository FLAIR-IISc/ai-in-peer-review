Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a fast, feed-forward “masking model” that, given an input image and a target class, predicts a saliency mask in a single pass. The mask is trained to both preserve evidence for the class in the retained region (SSR) and destroy it in the removed region (SDR) under a black-box classifier, while discouraging adversarial artifacts via a total-variation penalty and by applying the mask against randomized “alternative images” (blur or noisy color fills). The architecture is a U-Net with a fixed ResNet-50 encoder; the model conditions on a learned class embedding and uses multi-scale features to produce sharp masks. The authors also introduce a new saliency metric based on the log-area of a tight crop containing the salient region and the log probability of the class under the classifier on the resized crop. Experiments on ImageNet (with AlexNet/GoogLeNet/ResNet-50 as target black boxes) and CIFAR-10 show speed and localization improvements over several weakly supervised baselines.

Relative to prior work, this amortizes optimization-based perturbation methods such as Fong & Vedaldi (2017), which iteratively search masks per image. It also connects to gradient-based backpropagation methods (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop), but seeks higher-quality, artifact-resistant explanations while retaining real-time speed. The formulation explicitly addresses introduced-evidence pitfalls highlighted in meaningful perturbation work.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean “learn-to-explain” approach: trains a dedicated network to approximate solutions to a well-posed SSR/SDR objective, yielding single-pass saliency at >100 fps.
- Thoughtful objective that balances area, smoothness, preserve-score, and destroy-score terms; practical steps to reduce adversarial artifacts through randomized evidence removal and TV.
- Strong qualitative maps: sharp, interpretable masks that localize parts rather than diffuse blobs, aligning with how classifiers often latch onto most discriminative parts (e.g., heads).
- Competitive weakly supervised localization on ImageNet; CIFAR-10 results suggest the approach is not tied to pretraining and can work at low resolution.
- The concurrent positioning w.r.t. [2] is fair; this method’s amortization and dual SSR/SDR objective are meaningful additions.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Evaluation is indirect. Weakly supervised localization is only loosely coupled to saliency quality; when context drives predictions, a method can “localize” the object while failing to expose the true evidence (e.g., snow for polar bears). The paper discusses this risk but still relies heavily on localization.
- The proposed saliency metric is problematic: cropping and rescaling distort aspect ratio/scale, potentially boosting class scores via scale changes or magnification of small discriminative parts. This favors tiny regions and classifiers with limited scale invariance, and it can penalize methods that preserve global shape or multiple parts.
- The claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers is overstated. Training requires gradients and stable score responses; models with poor scale/aspect invariance or non-differentiable pipelines (common in APIs or non-image domains, e.g., spectrograms) will undermine the objective and the metric.
- Architecture dependence is unclear. The masking network is a substantial, fixed design (U-Net with ResNet-50 encoder); there is no ablation on encoder choice, skip connections, resolution, or the two-channel output nonlinearity. It remains unclear how much of the improvement is due to the architecture versus the objective, and whether the explanations reflect the black-box classifier or the masking network’s learned priors.
- The evidence-removal scheme A (blur vs noisy color) is randomized, which is sensible, but the paper lacks a quantitative ablation of A’s choices, TV weight, and area penalty on artifact suppression and metric performance.
- The CIFAR-10 experiment confirms feasibility but lacks quantitative comparisons and robustness checks (e.g., cross-model generalization, scale/rotation sensitivity).

Quality:
The method is technically sound and well-motivated; the training objective and architectural choices are reasonable, and the speed claims are credible. However, the evaluation falls short of decisively demonstrating that the produced maps faithfully capture the classifier’s evidence across diverse settings. Important controls (scale/aspect ablation, target-model invariance, architecture variants) are missing.

Clarity:
Generally clear and well organized. The SSR/SDR framing and introduced-evidence discussion are helpful. Some needed clarifications:
- Clarify whether Table 1 localization errors are computed with the ground-truth class provided to the masking model and evaluated against all ground-truth boxes, or restricted in any way.
- Specify which baselines and settings were used for comparisons, including which LRP variant (if used) and parameters; likewise for Guided Backprop/Excitation Backprop implementations and smoothing.
- Report sensitivity to λ1–λ4, the threshold 0.5 used to binarize masks for localization, and to the A choices; include ablations where possible.
- Explicitly state that gradients through the target classifier are used during training (if so), and discuss implications for “black-box” claims.

Originality:
Amortizing perturbation-based objectives with a feed-forward model and combining SSR and SDR terms is novel within contemporaneous work. The new metric is a contribution, albeit with caveats.

Significance:
Real-time, single-pass saliency is practically valuable. The method likely will be used and extended. That said, the reliance on classifier scale/aspect invariance and differentiability, and the metric’s biases, limit the breadth of conclusions. Significance is moderate unless the limitations are addressed.

Pros for acceptance:
- Fast, practical saliency with clear qualitative improvements.
- Solid objective design addressing artifact pitfalls.
- Competitive weakly supervised localization without localization supervision.
- Novel amortization of meaningful-perturbation-style objectives.

Cons for acceptance:
- Evaluation does not convincingly validate faithfulness; the proposed metric may reward pathological solutions.
- Overstated generality for black-box settings; requires differentiable, reasonably invariant classifiers.
- Lack of architectural and metric ablations; unclear dependence on masking network design.
- Limited analysis of context-induced failure modes.

Requested clarifications and suggestions:
- Provide ablations on A, TV, area penalty, and the two-channel mask nonlinearity; test different masking encoders.
- Quantify sensitivity to object scale/aspect ratio by controlled rescaling experiments; report performance on classes known to be context-biased.
- Add sanity checks for faithfulness, e.g., deletion/insertion curves without resizing, occlusion tests on the original image grid, or measure score drop as a function of revealed area.
- Clarify Table 1 protocol; specify the exact implementations and hyperparameters for all baseline saliency methods.

Post-rebuttal notes:
The authors’ responses suggest strong dependence on the masking network and on the target classifier’s scale invariance. This limits applicability (e.g., during classifier training, for non-invariant models, or outside natural images) and weakens the “general black-box” claim. Please incorporate these limitations explicitly and temper the scope claims.

Recommendation:
Borderline. If the paper clearly discusses these limitations, tempers the black-box/generalization claims, and adds targeted ablations or sanity checks to mitigate the metric and invariance concerns, I lean toward accept. Without these revisions, I would lean toward reject.
