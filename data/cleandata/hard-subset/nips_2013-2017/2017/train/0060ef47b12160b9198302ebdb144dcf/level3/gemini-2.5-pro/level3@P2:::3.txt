### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier, treating the classifier as a black box. The key idea is to train a separate generative model—a "masking model" with a U-Net-like architecture—to produce a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This contrasts with previous perturbation-based methods that iteratively optimize a mask for each input image, a process that is computationally expensive. The masking model is trained to minimize an objective function that encourages the generated mask to be smooth, small, and to identify regions that are both sufficient for classification (when kept) and destructive to it (when removed). The authors also introduce a new metric for evaluating saliency based on the classification confidence within the tightest rectangular crop around the salient region. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, where it is shown to produce sharp, interpretable maps and achieve state-of-the-art results on the weakly supervised object localization task, outperforming other saliency and weakly supervised methods. The claimed performance of over 100 images per second makes the approach suitable for real-time applications.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The motivation to replace slow, per-image optimization with a fast, amortized inference approach is compelling. The design of the objective function is thoughtful, incorporating terms for mask smoothness (TV), area minimization (AV), and the dual objectives of preserving and destroying classification evidence (SSR and SDR). The architectural choice of a U-Net with a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder is well-justified for producing high-resolution masks from multi-scale features.

The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem, and Section 3 provides an insightful discussion on the challenges of defining and measuring saliency, including the problem of "introduced evidence" from masking and the motivation for the new metric. The experimental section is thorough, with evaluations on multiple datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10) and against several black-box models (AlexNet, GoogleNet, ResNet-50).

### Originality and Significance

The primary contribution—training a separate model to generate saliency maps—is novel and significant. While the components (U-Nets, ResNets) are standard, their application to "learn" the saliency behavior of a black-box model is a clever way to move the computational burden from inference to training time. This fundamentally changes the trade-off between speed and quality in perturbation-based saliency methods, which have historically been slow.

The significance of this work lies in its potential to enable real-time applications for model interpretability, such as analyzing video streams or interactive debugging tools, which are infeasible with iterative methods. The strong performance on the ImageNet weakly supervised localization task demonstrates that the learned masks are not just fast but also meaningful, capturing object-relevant features. The introduction of a new, interpretable saliency metric is also a valuable contribution to the community.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel Approach:** The core idea of training a generative model for saliency prediction is an elegant solution to the speed limitations of iterative optimization-based methods.
2.  **Impressive Performance:** The method achieves state-of-the-art results on the challenging ImageNet weakly supervised localization task, demonstrating the high quality of the generated masks.
3.  **Real-Time Capability:** The reported speed of >100 fps is a significant practical advantage, opening up new application domains for saliency analysis.
4.  **Clarity and High-Quality Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-motivated, and provides valuable insights into the problem of saliency map generation and evaluation.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
1.  **Substantiation of Real-Time Claims:** The "100 images/sec" claim is a major selling point but requires more context. Does this figure include preprocessing steps like resizing images to 224x224? The practical utility for applications like autonomous driving depends on performance with higher-resolution inputs (e.g., 640x480 or higher). It would be helpful to report inference times for larger, more realistic image sizes.
2.  **Performance on Small Objects:** The experiments are conducted on 224x224 images. This resolution may cause very small objects to be lost or heavily distorted during downsampling, potentially limiting the method's effectiveness in such cases. A discussion on the method's limitations with respect to object scale would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Incomplete Comparison on the New Metric:** The proposed saliency metric in Section 3.2 is interesting. However, Table 3 only compares the proposed method against baselines and ground truth boxes. A much stronger validation of both the new metric and the proposed method would involve reporting the performance of the competing methods from Table 2 (e.g., [1], [2], [17]) on this new metric. This seems like a missed opportunity to demonstrate superiority more comprehensively.

### Summary of review (arguments pro and con acceptance)

**Arguments for acceptance:**
-   Presents a highly novel and practical approach to generating saliency maps.
-   The real-time performance is a significant leap forward compared to prior art in perturbation-based saliency.
-   Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark (weakly supervised ImageNet localization).
-   The paper is technically solid, clearly written, and provides insightful contributions, including a new evaluation metric.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
-   Key claims about real-time speed are not fully substantiated for practical, high-resolution inputs.
-   The evaluation using the newly proposed metric is incomplete, as it omits key competing methods.
-   The method's robustness to variations in object scale (especially small objects) is not analyzed.

**Recommendation:**
Despite the weaknesses, which are largely addressable, the core contribution is strong, novel, and significant. The idea of amortizing the search for saliency maps by training a predictive model is an important one that is likely to inspire follow-up work. The paper is well-executed and the results are impressive. I recommend acceptance.
