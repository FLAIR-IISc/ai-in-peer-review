

# Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper introduces a novel approach to image saliency detection that operates in real-time by training a masking model to predict saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier. The authors propose a masking model based on a U-Net architecture that generates saliency masks in a single forward pass, contrasting with iterative methods that require hundreds of steps. The key innovation is a new objective function that balances mask smoothness, region size, classifier confidence on preserved regions, and confidence after salient region removal. The authors introduce a new saliency metric based on the tightest rectangular crop that preserves classification confidence, and evaluate their method on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 using weakly supervised object localization.

The work builds upon previous saliency detection methods like gradient-based approaches (e.g., [11, 12, 16]), iterative patch removal methods (e.g., [17]), and optimization-based black-box methods (e.g., [2]). The authors position their contribution as a model-agnostic, real-time alternative that avoids the computational burden of iterative methods while producing higher quality saliency maps.

**Strengths:**
1. The proposed method is significantly faster than existing iterative approaches (100+ saliency masks per second), making it suitable for real-time applications like video saliency.
2. The masking model architecture is well-designed, leveraging U-Net's multi-scale feature extraction for precise saliency maps.
3. The weakly supervised object localization results are competitive with fully supervised methods (ResNet-trained model achieves 36.7% localization error vs. VGG's 34.3%).
4. The new saliency metric (s(a,p) = log(ã) - log(p)) provides a more direct assessment of saliency quality than standard localization metrics, as it measures the concentration of information in the salient region.
5. The method demonstrates generalization across different datasets (ImageNet and CIFAR-10) and classifier architectures.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The saliency metric has significant limitations: cropping and rescaling the salient region can alter the aspect ratio and scale, potentially introducing classification artifacts that the metric doesn't account for. This undermines the claim that the metric "provides a direct translation of the SSR."
2. The weakly supervised localization metric is problematic when contextual cues (rather than object location) determine classification. For instance, a model that classifies "polar bear" based on snow presence would produce accurate localizations that don't reflect the actual salient regions.
3. The paper doesn't adequately address the method's dependence on the masking network architecture. It's unclear whether different architectures were tested or how architectural choices affect results.
4. The claim of general applicability to "any differentiable image classifier" is questionable. The method relies heavily on the classifier's scale invariance, which isn't universal (e.g., models used for spectrograms or non-scale-invariant tasks would be problematic).
5. The paper lacks clarity on whether Table 1 results are averaged over all classes or only the correct class, and doesn't specify which LRP variant was used for comparisons.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, and the experimental results support the main claims. However, the evaluation methodology has significant limitations that weaken the conclusion that the method produces superior saliency maps.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The U-Net architecture and objective function are well-explained, though some details about the training process could be more precise.

**Originality:** The idea of training a masking model for real-time saliency detection is novel and represents a significant departure from iterative methods. The proposed saliency metric also introduces a new perspective on saliency evaluation.

**Significance:** The work has practical significance for real-time applications like autonomous vehicles, where immediate model explanations are needed. However, the limitations in the evaluation methodology and the method's dependence on classifier properties reduce its broader impact.

**Recommendation:**
This is a borderline paper. The core idea of learning-to-explain is compelling and the method shows promising results, but the evaluation methodology has significant flaws that undermine the main claims. If the authors address the weaknesses in the evaluation (particularly the saliency metric limitations and the contextual cues problem), this could be a strong contribution. As currently written, the paper overstates the generality of the approach and the robustness of the evaluation.

I recommend acceptance with the following conditions: the authors must thoroughly discuss the limitations of their saliency metric, clarify how contextual cues might affect the localization evaluation, and explicitly state the method's dependence on classifier scale invariance. If these issues are properly addressed in the final version, I would support acceptance. Otherwise, I would recommend rejection due to the significant limitations in the evaluation methodology.

The paper represents a meaningful contribution to the field of model interpretability, but needs to be more precise in its claims about evaluation and applicability.
