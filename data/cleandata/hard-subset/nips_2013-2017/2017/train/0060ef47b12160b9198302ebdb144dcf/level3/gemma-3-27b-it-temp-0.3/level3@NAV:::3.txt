## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper presents a novel approach to fast image saliency detection, aiming to identify the crucial image regions driving a classifier’s decision without requiring access to the classifier’s internal workings. The core idea is to train a masking model, leveraging a U-Net architecture with a ResNet-50 backbone, to directly predict saliency masks in a single forward pass – achieving impressive speed (claimed 100 images/sec on a standard GPU). The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, demonstrating competitive performance in weakly supervised object localization, even outperforming other techniques. The introduction of a new saliency metric, based on the smallest sufficient region concept, is a valuable contribution.

The strengths of this work lie in its speed, the clear motivation behind the design choices, and the good presentation quality. The authors effectively articulate the need for fast, interpretable saliency methods and convincingly demonstrate the potential of their model-based approach. The inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., training from scratch on CIFAR-10) and the detailed explanation of the objective function are commendable. The related work section appears comprehensive, placing the work within the context of existing saliency and explainable AI research.

However, several concerns remain. The claimed real-time speed is a significant point of scrutiny. While 100 images/sec for 224x224 images is promising, the paper lacks runtime information for larger, more realistic image sizes (e.g., 640x480 from a smartphone camera). It’s unclear whether this speed holds in practical applications. Furthermore, the performance on small objects is not explicitly addressed. A 224x224 input might be insufficient to accurately detect saliency for small objects within the image, potentially limiting the method’s applicability. 

Regarding the experimental results, Table 3 would benefit from including the corresponding metrics for other methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., Feed [2]) to facilitate a direct comparison. Finally, minor typos exist (LN 273 "More over" → "Moreover"; LN 151 wording about [3] optimizing the mask).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to fast saliency detection.
*   Competitive performance on ImageNet localization.
*   Clear motivation and well-explained methodology.
*   Introduction of a new saliency metric.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Real-time speed claim needs further validation with larger image sizes.
*   Performance on small objects is unclear.
*   Table 3 lacks complete comparative data.



Overall, this is a promising work with a clear contribution to the field. Addressing the concerns regarding speed, small object performance, and completeness of the experimental comparison would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
