

This paper proposes a fast neural-network-based method for generating saliency masks applicable to any differentiable image classifier. The authors train a masking model to predict salient regions by manipulating classifier scores through masking operations, enabling real-time saliency detection (100 masks/second) compared to iterative approaches. They introduce a novel saliency metric based on cropping the salient region and measuring classification confidence, and demonstrate strong results on ImageNet object localization.

The work builds on related literature in saliency detection (e.g., Zhou et al. [17], Cao et al. [1]) but offers a significant speed advantage. The masking model architecture (U-Net with ResNet encoder) is well-motivated and produces sharp, interpretable saliency maps that outperform weakly supervised baselines on localization accuracy.

However, several critical limitations require proper discussion. The saliency metric is fundamentally flawed: cropping/resizing salient regions alters aspect ratio and scale, introducing classification artifacts that the metric itself cannot detect. This directly exploits classifiers' limited scale invariance, as small salient regions are artificially favored. The paper's claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers is questionable, as results depend heavily on classifier properties (e.g., AlexNet vs. ResNet performance differences). The masking model's architecture dependence is not discussed—how different architectures affect results remains unclear. The weakly supervised localization metric also fails when contextual cues (not object location) determine classification, as noted in the paper's own polar bear example.

The authors should clarify whether Table 1 results are over all classes or only correct classes, and specify the LRP variant used for comparisons. Post-rebuttal concerns about reliance on classifier scale invariance and uncertainty whether visualizations reflect the target or masking network must be addressed.

Strengths: Fast method with real-time potential, novel metric, strong localization results. Weaknesses: Flawed metric, unaddressed limitations on classifier properties, insufficient discussion of architectural dependencies.

Pro: Novel approach to fast saliency detection with promising results. Con: Critical flaws in evaluation methodology severely limit applicability claims.

The paper has good technical merit but requires substantial revisions to properly discuss limitations and dependencies before acceptance. If these issues are adequately addressed, it would be a valuable contribution; otherwise, it should be rejected.
