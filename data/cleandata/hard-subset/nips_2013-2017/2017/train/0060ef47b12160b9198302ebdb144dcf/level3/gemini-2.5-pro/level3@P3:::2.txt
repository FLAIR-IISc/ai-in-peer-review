This paper introduces a novel, model-based approach for generating saliency maps for any differentiable image classifier. The core idea is to train a separate neural network (a U-Net-based "masking model") to predict a saliency mask for a given image-class pair in a single forward pass. This masking model is trained to optimize a well-defined objective function that encourages the mask to identify the smallest region that is both sufficient for classification (SSR) and whose removal is destructive to classification (SDR), while also promoting mask smoothness. This approach contrasts with prior work, such as gradient-based methods (Simonyan et al., 2013) which are fast but often noisy, and iterative optimization-based methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017) which produce high-quality maps but are computationally expensive. By amortizing the optimization process into the weights of a separate network, this work achieves both high-quality results and real-time performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The main contribution—training a dedicated network to generate saliency maps for another black-box classifier—is a novel and powerful idea. It effectively reframes the problem from a slow, per-instance optimization to a fast, amortized inference task. The resulting real-time capability (over 100 images/sec) is a significant practical advantage over iterative methods and opens up new applications.
*   **Technical Quality and Experimental Rigor:** The paper is technically sound. The authors perform a thorough experimental evaluation on challenging, large-scale datasets (ImageNet and CIFAR-10) using state-of-the-art classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) as the black-box targets. The proposed method demonstrates superior performance on the weakly-supervised object localization task, outperforming several recent methods and even coming close to a fully supervised baseline.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The objective function is well-justified, breaking down the desired properties of a saliency mask into distinct, understandable terms (smoothness, small area, high preserved score, low removed score). The introduction of a new saliency metric to better evaluate interpretability is also a thoughtful contribution.
*   **Generalizability:** The method is demonstrated to work on different datasets (ImageNet, CIFAR-10), different target architectures, and can be trained from scratch without relying on a pre-trained encoder, as shown in the CIFAR-10 experiment. This suggests the approach is robust and widely applicable.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Motivation for Real-Time Applications:** While the real-time performance is a key advantage, the paper only briefly mentions potential applications like video saliency in the conclusion. The impact of the work could be strengthened by motivating this capability more explicitly in the introduction. The authors could discuss specific use cases where real-time saliency is not just beneficial but enabling, such as in interactive debugging tools for model developers, providing immediate feedback in human-in-the-loop systems, or explaining the decisions of models in safety-critical systems like autonomous vehicles.
*   **Deeper Analysis of Saliency Maps:** The experiments convincingly show that the method finds salient regions that correlate with object locations. However, a significant opportunity is missed to use these maps to analyze and compare the behavior of the different black-box classifiers. The paper's introduction uses the excellent example of a classifier learning "snow" instead of "polar bear." The authors could include a qualitative analysis section that uses their tool to investigate such phenomena. For instance, do different architectures (e.g., AlexNet vs. ResNet-50) focus on different features for the same class? Can the method expose known dataset biases, where a model fixates on background context rather than the foreground object? Such an analysis would significantly elevate the paper's contribution from a strong methods paper to one that also provides new insights into the models it explains.
*   **Clarification of "Black Box":** The term "black box" is used throughout, but the training process requires backpropagation through the target classifier to compute the loss function. While the final masking model does not need access to the classifier's internals at inference time, the training phase requires full differentiability. It would be more precise to refer to the target as a "differentiable black box" to avoid any potential confusion with methods that require no gradient information at all.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper with a novel idea, excellent execution, and impressive results. The method represents a significant advance in saliency detection by achieving both the high quality of optimization-based methods and the speed of gradient-based ones. The weaknesses are primarily related to missed opportunities for deeper analysis and motivation, which can be addressed in a final version. The core contribution is solid and impactful. I strongly recommend acceptance.
