- Proposes a learned feed-forward saliency masking model that outputs class-conditional saliency maps in one pass.  
- Optimizes a loss combining TV smoothness, area penalty, SSR-like retention, and SDR-like suppression terms.  
- Reduces artifact-driven explanations by mixing input with alternative image A (blur/noise) and enforcing smoothness.  
- Uses a U-Net decoder with a fixed ResNet-50 encoder and a learned class embedding to filter features before upsampling.  
- Uses “fake labels” during training to enforce conditioning on the requested class.  
- Introduces a simple saliency metric s(a,p)=log(max(a,0.05))−log(p) evaluated on tight crops of salient regions.  
- Experiments on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 show sharp masks, improved weakly supervised localization, and competitive saliency scores.  
- Claims roughly 100 224×224 images/sec runtime on a standard GPU.  
- Positioned between gradient-based saliency and iterative perturbation methods, and related to CAM but class-conditional and classifier-agnostic.  
- Well-motivated objective addressing adversarial artifacts via A augmentation and TV regularization; fake-label conditioning is practical.  
- Architecture choice (U-Net with multi-scale features) is appropriate and plausible for the task.  
- Unclear whether training requires backpropagating through the classifier—if so, the classifier is not truly black-box at training time.  
- Needs ablation studies on loss component weights and choices of alternative image A to validate design choices.  
- Lacks analysis of robustness on small objects and multi-object images; performance stratified by object size is missing.  
- Empirical comparisons should include the new saliency metric for key baselines (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi) and report variability (std or percentiles).  
- Reproducibility would improve with full training details (optimizer, LR schedule, batch size, GPU) or code release.  
- Clarity issues: specify forward vs. backward interaction with classifier during training, detail the feature filter (dimensions/normalization), and state mask binarization threshold.  
- Significance: promising for real-time interpretability and weakly supervised localization, with potential video applications if claims hold.  
- Pros: unified SSR/SDR objective, fast single-pass class-conditional saliency, strong localization, simple metric, practical artifact-reduction tricks.  
- Cons: ambiguity about “black-box” training, limited small-object/multi-object analysis, runtime profiling needs standardization, missing metric comparisons to perturbation baselines, few ablations.  
- Key questions for authors: detailed runtime (including preprocessing and transfers), small-object performance vs. resolution, whether classifier gradients are used during training, add baseline saliency scores, sensitivity to A and loss weights, behavior in multi-label scenarios.  
- Suggestions: add ablations and small-object analysis, report metrics at multiple resolutions with throughput/latency tradeoffs, clarify black-box assumption, and release code/models.  
- Minor typos and wording issues noted (e.g., “More over” → “Moreover”, unclear phrasing at line 151).
