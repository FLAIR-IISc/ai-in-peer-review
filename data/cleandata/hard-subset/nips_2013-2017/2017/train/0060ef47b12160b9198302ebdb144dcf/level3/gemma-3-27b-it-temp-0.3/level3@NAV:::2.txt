## Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"

This paper introduces a novel approach to generating saliency maps for image classifiers, moving away from iterative methods towards a learned, feed-forward model. The core idea – training a masking model to predict salient regions – is a compelling solution to the computational bottleneck of existing techniques like iterative patch removal or optimization-based methods. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach achieves comparable or superior performance to state-of-the-art weakly supervised saliency detection methods, while offering significant speed advantages suitable for real-time applications. 

The work builds upon a well-established body of research in image saliency and interpretability, correctly citing key papers like Simonyan et al. (2014) on visualizing CNNs, Zhou et al. (2015, 2016) on iterative methods, and Fong & Vedaldi (2017) on optimization-based saliency. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of prior work, specifically the computational cost, and position their contribution as a practical solution. The introduction of a new saliency metric, based on the “smallest sufficient region” concept, is a valuable addition and provides a more nuanced evaluation than simple localization accuracy. 

The experimental results on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet are strong, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method across different datasets and network architectures. The ablation study and analysis of the masking model’s architecture are well-presented. The clarity of the paper is commendable; the writing is concise, and the technical details are explained thoroughly. 

However, while the paper excels in its technical execution, it could benefit from further discussion of potential applications beyond the brief mention of video saliency. Specifically, motivating scenarios where *real-time* efficiency is critical – such as human-robot interaction, autonomous driving (explaining decisions to passengers), or medical image analysis – would strengthen the significance of the work. 

Furthermore, a deeper analysis of the learned salient regions would be insightful. The authors mention potential biases, but exploring *which* categories tend to exploit background biases (as hinted at in the introduction with the polar bear/snow example) could reveal interesting insights into the behavior of the black-box classifiers and the limitations of the saliency detection method. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-supported by experimental results.
*   Addresses a significant problem (computational cost of saliency detection).
*   Outperforms existing methods on challenging datasets.
*   Clear and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of specific real-time applications.
*   Lack of in-depth analysis of learned biases in saliency maps.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance. This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning, offering a practical and efficient solution to a challenging problem. Addressing the minor suggestions regarding application motivation and bias analysis would further strengthen the work.
