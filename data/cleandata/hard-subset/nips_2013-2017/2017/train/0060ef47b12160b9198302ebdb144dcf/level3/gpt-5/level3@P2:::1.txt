Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a fast, learned masking model that produces saliency maps for any fixed differentiable image classifier in a single forward pass. Instead of per-image iterative optimization (e.g., Meaningful Perturbations, Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), the authors train a U-Net–style network (with a frozen ResNet-50 encoder) to output a class-conditional mask by minimizing an objective that jointly encourages: (i) smooth, small masks; (ii) preservation of class evidence in the kept region (SSR); and (iii) removal of class evidence in the complementary region (SDR). To mitigate introduced evidence, the masking applies a convex combination with an alternative image A (randomly chosen between heavy blur and random color/noise), and adds TV regularization. The model is trained on ImageNet for several black-box classifiers (AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50) and on CIFAR-10 (from scratch). The authors also introduce a saliency metric based on the log-area/log-probability trade-off of a tight crop around the salient region, and evaluate on weakly supervised localization.

Relative to prior work: backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al., Guided Backprop, Excitation Backprop) is fast but often noisy; optimization-based approaches (Zhou et al.; Fong & Vedaldi) can give interpretable maps but are slow. This paper’s main contribution is learning to approximate optimization-based attribution with a single pass, yielding real-time saliency and improved localization.

Strengths
- Clear problem framing (SSR/SDR) and a principled objective that blends preservation and deletion terms with practical defenses against artifacts.
- Learning-to-explain is compelling; single-pass saliency is orders of magnitude faster than iterative methods and suitable for real-time/video use.
- Empirical results show sharper masks and better weakly supervised localization vs. several baselines on ImageNet; the approach also works on CIFAR-10 without pretraining.
- The proposed metric attempts to formalize “concentration of evidence,” which complements localization.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evaluation relies heavily on weakly supervised localization and the proposed crop-based metric. Both can misalign with “true” saliency: localization rewards covering full objects, while saliency often focuses on diagnostic parts; contextual cues (e.g., snow for “polar bear”) can drive classification despite small or off-object masks.
- The proposed metric can be gamed by scale/aspect-ratio changes introduced by cropping and resizing. It favors very small regions that, when blown up, benefit from imperfect scale invariance, conflating classifier invariances with saliency quality.
- Claims of general applicability to “any” differentiable black-box are strong. The approach implicitly relies on classifier robustness to rescaling and aspect ratio (for the metric), and on the classifier’s capacity to be manipulated by smooth masks. Models with poor scale/shape invariance, or domains unlike natural images (e.g., spectrograms), may break assumptions.
- Dependence on the masking network architecture is under-explored. Results are shown with one U-Net configuration; it remains unclear how much the visualizations reflect the black-box vs. inductive biases of the masking network. No ablations on architecture, TV weight, or choice/mixing of A.
- Some experimental details and comparisons are missing or underspecified (see questions).

Quality
Technically sound and well-motivated. The objective and training heuristics are thoughtful, and the empirical improvements are plausible. However, the evaluation falls short of fully validating faithfulness: no sanity checks (e.g., model parameter randomization tests), cross-model generalization tests (train on one classifier, probe another), or robustness to scale/aspect ratio in the metric.

Clarity
Generally well-written and easy to follow. Figures and problem statement are clear. Missing details include: exact training/eval splits; whether Table 1 uses ground-truth labels at test time for all images; precise baseline configurations and parameter settings; additional ablations.

Originality
Learning a masking model to emulate optimization-based saliency with SSR+SDR is a meaningful addition beyond existing gradient and iterative perturbation methods. The crop-based metric is new, though its limitations should be acknowledged.

Significance
If robust, a single-pass, high-quality saliency model is valuable for real-time applications and for auditing classifiers. The current evaluation suggests promise but leaves uncertainty about generality and faithfulness due to the noted dependencies and metric issues.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Table 1: Are results computed per image using the ground-truth class only, or over all top-1 predictions? How are multi-object images handled?
- Baselines: Please specify exactly which backprop-based and perturbation methods are included, with implementation details and parameter settings. If LRP was used, indicate the variant and parameters.
- Metric sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the resize protocol (with/without aspect ratio preservation), the crop tightness, and classifier choice? Provide ablations and a discussion of potential gaming via scale/aspect ratio.
- Architecture dependence: Report ablations on the masking network (encoder choice, depth/width, skip connections), TV weight, and the mixture of A. How do these choices affect sharpness, localization, and the metric?
- Generality: Can the trained masking model transfer across black-box architectures (train on ResNet-50, test on GoogLeNet)? What happens during training time if the black box is simultaneously being updated?
- Contextual classes: Provide qualitative/quantitative analyses where context (e.g., snow, watermarks) dominates classification. Do masks highlight context rather than the object?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include faithfulness tests beyond localization: deletion/insertion curves; pointing game; model parameter randomization tests; causal metrics that measure score change vs. fraction of pixels modified while keeping scale fixed.
- Evaluate metric variants that control for scale/aspect ratio (e.g., fixed-size window, padding instead of resizing) and report robustness.
- Soften claims of universality; discuss applicability limits (scale invariance, domain assumptions) and guidance on when the metric/model is appropriate.
- Release code/models and provide runtime benchmarks across GPUs.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, fast saliency with sharp masks and improved localization.
- Well-motivated SSR+SDR objective with artifact mitigation.
- Broad interest to NIPS audience working on interpretability and vision.

Cons for acceptance
- Evaluation relies on proxies with known failure modes; proposed metric has intrinsic biases.
- Unclear dependence on masking architecture and black-box invariances; limited ablations.
- Overstated generality without thorough stress testing.

Post-rebuttal note
The method’s reliance on classifier quality and scale invariance, and the masking network’s inductive biases, should be explicitly acknowledged and probed. If the authors add analyses/ablations addressing these dependencies and temper claims of generality, I would lean toward acceptance; otherwise, I remain borderline.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. Acceptable if limitations and dependencies are clearly discussed and additional faithfulness/ablation experiments are added; otherwise, I would not object to rejection.
