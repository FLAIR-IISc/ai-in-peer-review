Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a learned, feed-forward saliency masking model that, given an image and a target class, produces a class-conditional saliency map in a single pass. The approach optimizes a loss that combines: (i) a total variation (TV) smoothness prior; (ii) an area penalty; (iii) an SSR-like term that encourages the preserved region to retain high class probability; and (iv) an SDR-like term that encourages the complement to suppress the class probability. To reduce artifact-driven explanations, masks are applied by mixing the input with an alternative image A (strong blur or noisy constant) and by enforcing smoothness. The model uses a U-Net-style decoder with a fixed ResNet-50 encoder and a learned class embedding to filter high-level features before upsampling. Training includes “fake labels” to enforce conditioning on the requested class.

The paper introduces a simple saliency metric s(a, p) = log(max(a,0.05)) − log(p) based on the SSR principle, evaluated by cropping to the tightest rectangle enclosing the salient region and measuring the class probability from the cropped image.

Experiments on ImageNet (with AlexNet, GoogLeNet, ResNet-50 as the black-box classifiers) and CIFAR-10 demonstrate sharp, interpretable masks, improved weakly supervised localization accuracy compared to prior methods, and competitive scores on the proposed saliency metric. The method runs at roughly 100 224×224 images per second on a standard GPU.

This work sits between gradient/backprop-based saliency [Simonyan et al. 2013; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop] and iterative perturbation approaches [Fong & Vedaldi 2017; Cao et al. 2015], aiming to retain the interpretability of image-space perturbations while achieving real-time speed via a learned masking network. It is also related to weakly supervised localization/CAM [Zhou et al. 2016], but is class-conditional, classifier-agnostic, and optimized for SSR/SDR-like objectives rather than solely for discriminative localization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective is well-motivated and addresses common pitfalls (adversarial artifacts) using both data augmentation in A and TV regularization. The class-conditioning trick with fake labels is clever and practical. The architecture choice (U-Net with multi-scale features) is appropriate. The claimed localization performance improvements over weakly supervised baselines appear plausible. However, several details need clarification to fully validate the claims:
  - “Black box” versus “differentiable classifier”: training the masking model seemingly requires backpropagation through the classifier to get gradients wrt the mask. Please clarify whether gradients through the classifier are used during training; if so, the classifier is not black-box during training (though it is frozen). If not, explain how gradients are obtained.
  - Ablations on the loss components (weights for TV/area/SSR/SDR) and on the alternative image A would strengthen the evidence for each design choice.
  - Robustness on small objects and multi-object images is not analyzed.
- Empirical evaluation: ImageNet localization and the proposed saliency metric are appropriate. Please include the saliency metric for the strongest baselines in Table 2 (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi [2]) in Table 3 for a like-for-like comparison. Also report standard deviations or percentiles if feasible.
- Reproducibility: Many hyperparameters are provided (loss weights, architecture sketch, training size). It would help to release code or, minimally, more training details (optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, GPU model).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear intuition (SSR/SDR) and practical guidance on avoiding artifacts. A few clarifications would improve readability:
- Precisely define the training-time interaction with the classifier (forward only vs. forward+backward; logits vs. probabilities).
- Explain the “feature filter” (Eq. 7) more explicitly, including dimensions and normalization of class embeddings and features.
- State the threshold used to binarize masks for localization and whether performance is sensitive to it.
- Minor typos noted at the end.

Originality
Training a dedicated masking network to emulate iterative perturbation saliency in a single pass, combining SSR and SDR in the loss, and using fake-label conditioning is, to my knowledge, novel relative to prior iterative perturbation methods [2] and to CAM/Excitation Backprop lines. The proposed metric is simple but useful. The work is a meaningful synthesis with practical impact rather than a purely theoretical advance.

Significance
If the speed and accuracy claims hold, this is a useful contribution for real-time interpretability and weakly supervised localization, potentially enabling video applications. The idea of learning to predict perturbation masks could become a standard approach. Demonstrating strong performance on small-object subsets and larger inputs would further increase practical significance.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated objective unifying SSR and SDR.
- Fast, single-pass, class-conditional saliency with sharp masks.
- Competitive or better weakly supervised localization on ImageNet.
- Simple, interpretable saliency metric; good empirical scores.
- Practical tricks (alternative image A, fake labels) that reduce artifacts and enforce conditioning.

Cons
- Ambiguity about “black-box” training and gradient requirements.
- Limited analysis of small-object performance and multi-object scenes.
- Real-time claim needs more detailed, standardized profiling.
- Missing direct comparison on the new metric to key perturbation baselines (e.g., [2]).
- Few ablations to quantify contributions of loss terms and A choices.

Questions for the authors
- Runtime: Does the ~100 images/sec include image resizing/preprocessing and host–device transfer? What GPU/CPU were used? Please report end-to-end throughput and latency for common resolutions (e.g., 640×480 from an iPhone 6s and 1280×720), including mask upsampling and crop extraction.
- Small objects: How does performance vary with object size? Is a 224×224 input sufficient to localize small instances, or does throughput degrade significantly at higher input resolutions needed for small objects?
- Training-time gradients: Do you backpropagate through the classifier during training? If yes, the method requires differentiable access at training time; please state this clearly. If no, how are gradients obtained?
- Table 3: Please add the saliency metric for Fong & Vedaldi [2], Excitation Backprop, CAM, and other methods in Table 2, computed with your crop-and-resize protocol.
- Robustness: How sensitive are results to the choice/probability of A (blur vs. noise) and to loss weights (especially λ3, λ4)? Provide ablations.
- Multi-label scenarios: How does the method behave when multiple instances of the same or different classes are present?

Suggestions
- Include ablations and small-object analysis (e.g., stratify ImageNet by object size).
- Report metrics at multiple input resolutions with throughput/latency trade-offs.
- Clarify the black-box assumption and release code/models if possible.

- Minor grammar/formatting throughout; a careful proofread would help.
