## Review of Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers

This paper presents a novel method for generating image saliency maps in real-time, applicable to any differentiable image classifier. The core idea is to train a masking model that predicts salient regions by manipulating classifier scores, instead of relying on iterative approaches. The authors demonstrate strong performance on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, achieving competitive results on object localization and proposing a new saliency metric that correlates well with interpretability.

The work builds upon a significant body of research in image saliency and interpretability of deep neural networks. Gradient-based methods [11, 12, 16] are noted as being fast but limited in quality, while iterative methods [1, 17] are accurate but computationally expensive.  The authors appropriately position their approach as bridging this gap, acknowledging recent parallel work by Fong and Vedaldi [2] which optimizes saliency directly in image space.  They clearly articulate the shortcomings of existing techniques and the need for a fast, accurate, and model-agnostic solution.  The introduction effectively motivates the problem by highlighting the often-opaque decision-making processes of even high-performing classifiers [6, 13]. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed approach of training a masking model for single-pass saliency detection is a significant contribution. It distinguishes itself from iterative optimization methods and offers a compelling trade-off between speed and accuracy.
* **Technical Soundness:** The architecture, utilizing a U-Net with a ResNet encoder, appears well-motivated and leverages established techniques effectively.  The objective function, combining smoothness, region size, and classification probability, demonstrates careful consideration of desired properties of a saliency map.
* **Experimental Validation:** The results on ImageNet and CIFAR-10 are promising. The method outperforms other weakly supervised techniques in object localization, demonstrating its practical applicability. The introduction of the new saliency metric and its correlation with ground truth provides further evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivations are clearly stated, and the technical details are presented in a comprehensible manner. The inclusion of examples and diagrams, such as Figure 1, aids understanding.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature, appropriately citing and discussing previous approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Real-Time Claim:** While the reported speed of 100 images/sec is impressive, the paper lacks clarity regarding overhead. Does this figure include image resizing/preprocessing? What are the runtimes for larger images commonly encountered in real-world applications (e.g., 640x480 from a smartphone camera)?  The practical implications of the "real-time" claim are therefore unclear.
* **Small Object Performance:** The discussion lacks a dedicated analysis of performance on small objects. Given the reliance on cropping and the potential for information loss, it’s crucial to understand how well the method performs when the requested class occupies a small portion of the image. Is a 224x224 input sufficient to accurately identify and highlight these small objects?
* **Metric Comparison:** Table 3 presents the proposed saliency metric for various methods. However, it’s unclear whether the metric has been calculated for *all* methods listed in Table 2 (e.g., Feed [2]).  A direct comparison across all methods would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Minor Presentation Issues:** There are minor typos such as “More over” (line 273) which should be "Moreover," and the phrasing in line 151 concerning [3] optimizing the mask could be clarified.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in the field of interpretability, offering a practical solution for generating saliency maps in real-time.
* The technical approach is novel and well-motivated, building upon existing research while making significant contributions.
* The experimental results are promising, demonstrating competitive performance on benchmark datasets.
* The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The "real-time" claim needs further justification and clarification regarding overhead and performance on larger images.
* The performance on small objects requires further investigation.
* A more comprehensive comparison of the proposed saliency metric with existing methods is needed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of image saliency and interpretability. The proposed method is innovative, technically sound, and experimentally validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding the real-time performance claim, small object handling, and metric comparison.  

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should clarify the runtime performance with varying input sizes, provide a more detailed analysis of performance on small objects, and include a complete comparison of the proposed saliency metric with all methods listed in Table 2.
