This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency maps for image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate neural network, termed a "masking model," to produce a saliency mask for a given image and class in a single feed-forward pass. This approach is contrasted with prior work, such as iterative optimization methods (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi, 2017), which are computationally expensive, and gradient-based methods (e.g., Simonyan et al., 2014), which can produce noisy maps. The authors train their U-Net-based masking model to optimize an objective function that encourages masks to be small and smooth, to preserve the classifier's score on the masked-in region, and to destroy the score on the masked-out region. The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, where it achieves state-of-the-art results on weakly supervised object localization. A new metric for saliency quality is also proposed.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The concept of "learning to explain" by training a dedicated model to generate explanations is a novel and significant departure from traditional per-instance explanation methods. Amortizing the optimization process into a single forward pass is a clever idea that makes saliency generation orders of magnitude faster, enabling real-time applications like video saliency, which is a major practical contribution.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates impressive performance on the weakly supervised object localization task on ImageNet, outperforming previous weakly supervised and saliency-based methods. This provides strong empirical evidence for the quality of the generated masks, at least for this particular proxy task.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation, methodology, and experiments are explained in sufficient detail to be understood.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Flawed Evaluation Metrics:** The paper's primary claims rest on two metrics, both of which have significant limitations.
    *   **Weakly Supervised Localization:** While the results are strong, localization accuracy is an imperfect proxy for saliency. As the authors themselves note in the introduction (the "polar bear" example), a classifier may rely on contextual cues (e.g., snow) rather than the object itself. In such cases, a *correct* saliency map highlighting the context would be penalized by the localization metric, which expects the map to cover the object. The evaluation thus potentially contradicts the paper's own motivation for saliency analysis.
    *   **Proposed Saliency Metric:** The new metric, which involves cropping the image to the salient region's bounding box and re-evaluating the classifier, is conceptually interesting but methodologically flawed. The crop-and-resize operation alters the object's scale and aspect ratio, introducing artifacts that can confound the classifier's prediction. The metric also inherently favors smaller regions (`log(a)` term). This could be exploited by finding a tiny, discriminative patch that, when blown up to the classifier's standard input size, is easily classified. This setup measures the classifier's scale invariance as much as it measures the quality of the saliency map.

2.  **Unaddressed Methodological Dependencies and Biases:** The work's central claim of being a general method for explaining "black box" classifiers is challenged by several unexamined dependencies.
    *   **Dependence on Target Classifier Properties:** The method's evaluation relies on the target classifier being robust to significant changes in scale and aspect ratio. This severely limits its applicability, as it cannot be reliably used for models that lack this invariance, for tasks where resizing is meaningless (e.g., spectrograms), or to analyze a model's behavior *during* training before such invariances are learned.
    *   **Dependence on Masking Model Architecture:** The saliency maps are generated by a powerful masking model (U-Net with a ResNet-50 encoder). It is unclear how much the final saliency map is a reflection of the target classifier's internal logic versus the architectural priors and learned biases of the masking model itself. For instance, a U-Net is predisposed to generate smooth, object-like shapes. The paper presents no ablation studies on the masking model's architecture, leaving open the critical question of whether we are visualizing the target network or the explanation network.

3.  **Minor Clarifications Needed:**
    *   In Table 1, are the reported localization error rates computed conditioned on the ground-truth class being provided to the model, or is it an average over all classes? Please clarify this point.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

I am borderline on this paper. The core idea of training a network to generate explanations is innovative and the promise of real-time saliency is compelling. However, the work is undermined by a significant disconnect between its motivation and its evaluation. The metrics used to validate the approach are either indirect proxies (localization) or methodologically flawed (the proposed metric), and they fail to address the core problem of whether the explanation truly reflects the classifier's reasoning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and promising "learning to explain" paradigm.
*   The real-time performance is a significant practical advantage over iterative methods.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark (weakly supervised localization).

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The evaluation relies on flawed or inappropriate proxy metrics for saliency.
*   The method's heavy dependence on the target classifier's scale/aspect-ratio invariance is a major limitation that is not discussed.
*   The paper fails to disentangle the properties of the explanation from the biases of the powerful explanation model itself.

Ultimately, my recommendation is conditional. If the authors can revise the paper to thoroughly acknowledge and discuss these limitations—framing the work as a promising but preliminary step in a new direction and being transparent about the flaws in the current evaluation protocol—then the paper could be a valuable contribution. However, if the claims of providing a general, high-quality explanation method are maintained without addressing these fundamental issues, the paper should be rejected.
