Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a trainable masking model that predicts, in a single forward pass, image regions that are salient for a given classifier and class. The model is trained end-to-end against a composite objective that encourages (i) smooth, compact masks, (ii) high classification confidence from the preserved region (SSR) and (iii) low confidence when the preserved region is removed (SDR), while mitigating adversarial artifacts by randomizing the “alternative image” and penalizing total variation. Architecturally, the masking model is a U-Net with a class-conditional feature filter on top of a frozen ResNet-50 encoder; it outputs a class-specific soft mask at 1/4 resolution, later upsampled. The method is evaluated on ImageNet (weakly supervised localization) and CIFAR-10, and the authors also introduce a new saliency metric that trades off crop area versus class probability on the cropped patch.

The paper positions itself between gradient/backprop-based saliency (Simonyan et al.; Zeiler & Fergus; Guided Backprop; Excitation Backprop), which are fast but often noisy, and optimization-based deletion/preservation methods (e.g., Meaningful Perturbations, Fong & Vedaldi 2017; Cao et al.), which are interpretable but slow and susceptible to artifacts. Compared to these, the proposed approach amortizes the optimization into a learned predictor, yielding sharper maps and real-time throughput. Results are reported for AlexNet, GoogLeNet, and ResNet-50 black-box classifiers on ImageNet, with separate masking models trained per classifier.

Quality
The technical formulation is clear and well-motivated. Combining SSR and SDR in a single objective is elegant, and the evidence-removal choices (randomized blur/noise backgrounds, TV regularization) demonstrate good awareness of introduced-evidence pitfalls. The new saliency metric is reasonable as a proxy for “information concentration,” though it depends on classifier calibration and the aspect-ratio-agnostic resizing could bias comparisons. Empirically, the method outperforms prior weakly supervised localization and iterative saliency baselines, and runs at >100 fps on 224×224 inputs, which is compelling.

That said, several analyses would strengthen the claims:
- Ablations on the loss terms (λ1–λ4), the form of A, mask resolution, and the two-channel mask nonlinearity.
- Sensitivity of the saliency metric to classifier calibration and confidence (e.g., temperature scaling or reporting p after calibration).
- Cross-model generalization: can a masking model trained for ResNet explain GoogLeNet, or a single model handle multiple backbones?
- Clarification of “black box”: training requires end-to-end gradients through the classifier (hence “differentiable”), so the term may be confusing; please state the access assumptions explicitly.
- More rigorous runtime profiling (latency, throughput, memory) across GPUs/CPUs and input resolutions.

Clarity
The paper is very well written and organized. The objective, architecture, and training details (including fake-label trick and randomized A) are described clearly. Figures are informative and support the “sharpness” claim. Minor suggestions:
- Move parts of Sec. 3.1 into a concise threat model for introduced evidence, with a brief visual ablation.
- Define the saliency metric’s intuition before the formula and discuss its limitations (calibration, resizing) explicitly.
- State clearly that a separate masking model is trained per classifier and that the classifier is kept fixed during training.

Originality
Amortizing iterative deletion/preservation into a learned, class-conditional U-Net is a novel and useful twist that bridges optimization-based saliency and fast feed-forward explanations. The combined SSR+SDR objective and randomized evidence-removal are thoughtful contributions. The metric is a modest but constructive addition. The work is distinct from gradient methods and from patch-removal optimization (e.g., Fong & Vedaldi), to which it is closely related.

Significance
The ability to produce high-quality saliency maps in real time is practically important and likely to be adopted. Results on ImageNet with state-of-the-art classifiers and a CIFAR-10 from-scratch variant support generality. The approach could catalyze real-time interpretability for video and interactive systems.

To further increase impact, I recommend:
- Motivating concrete real-time applications: on-device explainability for mobile AR, interactive model debugging, safety monitoring in autonomous driving (frame-by-frame explanations), human-in-the-loop decision support where latency matters.
- Adding dataset-level analysis of learned salient regions to expose contextual biases. For example:
  - Aggregate per-category overlap of masks with estimated foreground vs. background (using segmentation proxies) to quantify categories that rely on context (e.g., “polar bear” with snow, “gondola” with water).
  - Correlate mask extent with co-occurring scene attributes (grass/sky/snow) and report categories with highest background reliance.
  - Show failure cases where masks capture spurious context and how SSR/SDR terms trade off object vs. background focus.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, well-motivated objective combining SSR and SDR with explicit defenses against introduced evidence.
- Strong empirical results on ImageNet localization and a new saliency metric; competitive with fully supervised localization.
- Real-time throughput with a single forward pass; evaluations on challenging datasets and modern backbones.
- Method is broadly applicable to differentiable classifiers; architecture and training tricks are practical.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Requires training a separate masking model per classifier; no demonstration of cross-model generalization or multi-backbone support.
- “Black box” terminology is potentially misleading; gradients through the classifier are needed.
- Limited ablations and calibration analysis for the new metric; metric may reflect classifier confidence more than explanation quality in some regimes.
- Runtime reporting is limited to a single GPU/resolution; no CPU/embedded benchmarks.
- Limited analysis of learned biases and per-class qualitative/quantitative behavior.

Recommendation
This is a strong paper with a practical and novel approach to real-time saliency for differentiable classifiers. With additional ablations, clearer access assumptions, richer analysis of learned biases, and more concrete real-time use cases, it would be an excellent NIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
