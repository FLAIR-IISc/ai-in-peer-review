- Proposes a trainable, class-conditional U-Net–style masking network (ResNet-50 encoder) that produces saliency maps for arbitrary differentiable classifiers in a single forward pass.

- Positions method between fast gradient-based explanations and slow optimization-based perturbation methods by amortizing per-image mask optimization.

- Training objective enforces SSR/SDR via composite loss: TV smoothness, area penalty, preserved-region classification, and suppression after removal.

- Uses multi-scale features with a learned class embedding (“feature filter”) at the coarsest scale and upsampling refinement blocks.

- Reduces adversarial artifacts by randomly mixing evidence-removal baselines (heavy blur vs. random color+noise) and using a “fake class” strategy to force reliance on the class selector.

- Proposes a saliency metric based on the smallest rectangular crop that preserves classification confidence.

- Reports state-of-the-art weakly supervised ImageNet localization and clean qualitative masks; runtime roughly 100 224×224 images/s on “a standard GPU.”

- Clarifyability issue: paper calls the classifier a “black box” but training requires gradient access; this needs explicit clarification.

- Technical soundness: objective and training scheme are well-motivated and the fixed pretrained encoder plus multi-scale refinement is sensible.

- Empirical support: strong ImageNet localization and reasonable new metric, but needs sensitivity analysis for metric dependence on classifier scale/aspect invariance and resizing policy.

- Missing ablations: need experiments isolating effects of TV, loss terms, class-embedding filter, random A vs. fixed A, and multi-scale vs. single-scale.

- Speed claim underspecified: hardware, batch size, precision, framework, inclusion of I/O and resizing must be reported.

- Clarity: writing and figures are clear; metric formula s(a,p) and the classifier used for p should be unambiguously specified.

- Originality: novel combination of amortized class-conditional masking for SSR/SDR, multi-scale class-filtered architecture, randomized evidence-removal, and a tight-crop saliency metric.

- Suggest adding comparisons to CAM/Grad-CAM and including contemporaneous related work where feasible.

- Significance: practical for interactive/video settings and shows saliency optimization can be amortized with quality comparable or superior to iterative methods.

- Open questions on generalization: need analysis on higher resolutions, small-object performance, and scalability to common video resolutions.

- Requests for clarification: report end-to-end throughput/latency including resizing and classifier forward pass, plus details (GPU/CPU model, batch size, precision, framework).

- Requests: provide throughput/latency for larger inputs (e.g., 640×480, 720p, 1080p) and memory usage.

- Small-object analysis: report localization error binned by object area and show qualitative masks for small objects; assess if 224×224 suffices.

- Metric coverage: include the proposed saliency metric for other baseline methods (Fong & Vedaldi, CAM/Excitation Backprop, Center) for fair cross-method comparison.

- Box generation sensitivity: report sensitivity to the 0.5 mask threshold and whether it was tuned on a validation set.

- Strengths: single-pass class-conditional masks, sharp per-pixel outputs, strong ImageNet localization across classifiers, thoughtful training to avoid artifacts, and clear presentation.

- Weaknesses: underspecified runtime claim and scalability, limited small-object and threshold analyses, misleading “black-box” terminology, and missing ablations.

- Suggestions for improvement: add detailed runtime benchmarks (including preprocessing), object-size–stratified results, resolution vs. quality/speed study, comprehensive ablations, expanded baselines in Table 3, and clarify training-time gradient requirements.

- Minor issues: typos and phrasing fixes (e.g., “More over” → “Moreover”; clarify wording implying ResNet-50 “optimizes the mask”).

- Overall assessment: strong and timely contribution with compelling quality and speed; with clarified runtime claims, ablations, and small-object/resolution analyses the paper is a solid NIPS submission (reviewer leans positive).
