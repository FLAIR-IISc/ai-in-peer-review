This paper proposes a novel, learning-based approach for generating saliency masks for pre-trained image classifiers. Instead of iteratively optimizing a mask for each image, the authors train a separate neural network (a U-Net with a ResNet encoder) to produce a saliency mask in a single forward pass. The masking model is trained to minimize an objective function that encourages masks to be small and smooth, while also identifying image regions that are both sufficient for the classifier's prediction (Smallest Sufficient Region) and whose removal destroys the prediction (Smallest Destroying Region). The method is evaluated on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet, using weakly supervised object localization and a newly proposed saliency metric. The main contributions are the concept of a trainable saliency model, its real-time performance, and strong results on the chosen evaluation tasks compared to prior work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of training a separate network to learn to generate explanations is a promising and novel direction for the field of interpretability. Moving from slow, iterative, per-instance optimization methods to a fast, amortized inference model is a significant conceptual and practical step forward. The potential for real-time saliency opens up new application areas, such as video analysis.
*   **Performance:** The method is orders of magnitude faster at inference time than optimization-based approaches like Fong & Vedaldi (2017), which is a major practical advantage.
*   **Objective Function:** The proposed objective function (Eq. 4) is well-motivated, explicitly incorporating several desirable properties for a saliency mask: smoothness (TV penalty), conciseness (area penalty), and capturing both sufficient and necessary evidence for a prediction.

**Weaknesses and Major Concerns:**

The paper has several clear limitations that are not adequately discussed and which temper the enthusiasm for the proposed method. My primary concerns revolve around the evaluation methodology and the unstated assumptions and dependencies of the approach.

1.  **Indirect and Flawed Evaluation Metrics:** The quality of the saliency maps is measured only indirectly, and both chosen metrics have significant flaws.
    *   **Weakly Supervised Localization:** This task is a poor proxy for saliency. A classifier might correctly classify an image based on contextual cues that are correlated with an object but are not the object itself (e.g., classifying a "train" by looking at the tracks, not the train). In such cases, a faithful saliency method *should* highlight the tracks, which would lead to a low localization score. Therefore, high performance on this task does not necessarily imply high-quality saliency explanations. This limitation must be discussed.
    *   **Proposed Saliency Metric:** The proposed metric (Eq. 3) is based on cropping the salient region, resizing it to the classifier's required input size, and measuring the resulting class probability. This procedure is highly problematic. Resizing can drastically change the scale and aspect ratio of the cropped region, introducing significant artifacts that the classifier may be sensitive to. Furthermore, the metric inherently favors very small salient regions, which are then blown up to the full input size. This heavily exploits the classifier's (potentially limited) scale invariance and may not reflect what the classifier finds salient in the original, un-distorted image. A method could achieve a high score on this metric by finding a tiny, discriminative patch that the classifier happens to recognize well when upscaled, which is not a robust measure of explanation quality.

2.  **Un-discussed Dependencies and Limited Applicability:** The paper claims general applicability to "black-box" classifiers, but the method appears to have strong, unstated dependencies that severely limit its scope.
    *   **Reliance on Classifier Properties:** The method's success seems to hinge on the target classifier being robust to the significant scale and aspect-ratio distortions introduced by the evaluation metric and potentially by the masking process itself. This makes the method unsuitable for explaining classifiers that lack such invariance. It also means the method cannot be used during the training of a classifier (as its properties are not yet fixed) and may be inapplicable to domains like audio (spectrograms) where such geometric transformations are meaningless.
    *   **Dependence on Masking Model Architecture:** The paper uses a sophisticated U-Net with a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder as the masking model. There is no discussion or ablation on how this architectural choice influences the resulting saliency maps. Would a simpler model produce different, perhaps less plausible, masks? This raises a critical question: are the generated visualizations a reflection of the target classifier's internal logic, or are they heavily biased by the strong architectural priors of the masking network itself? Without this analysis, it is difficult to be sure which model is actually being explained.
    *   **"Black-Box" Claim:** While the classifier is treated as a black box at inference time, the training of the masking model requires full differentiability and gradient access to the classifier. This is an important distinction that should be made clear. The stronger claim of general applicability is challenged by the method's deep reliance on the specific properties of the target network.

**Minor Points and Questions for Clarification:**

*   In Table 1, are the localization error rates computed over all images in the validation set, or only for those images where the classifier's top-1 prediction was correct? This is a crucial detail for interpretation.
*   When comparing to gradient-based methods or LRP, please specify which variant and what parameter settings were used. The output of these methods can vary significantly with different settings.

**Recommendation:**

I am borderline on this paper. I believe the core concept of "learning to explain" is valuable and the paper presents a creative, well-engineered instance of this idea. However, the work in its current form oversells its generality and relies on flawed evaluation metrics that may paint an overly optimistic picture of the method's performance.

My recommendation is conditional: **The paper is acceptable for publication if, and only if, the authors add a comprehensive discussion of the limitations outlined above.** Specifically, they must acknowledge the issues with using localization as a saliency metric, the artifacts and dependencies introduced by their proposed crop-and-resize metric, and the method's strong reliance on the target classifier's scale/aspect-ratio invariance. The paper would be much stronger if it honestly framed the work as a promising but specific approach that works well for certain types of classifiers under certain assumptions, rather than a universally applicable solution. If these limitations are not adequately addressed, I would recommend rejection.
