- Proposes a fast neural masking model for saliency masks applicable to differentiable image classifiers, enabling real-time saliency (~100 masks/sec) vs. iterative methods.  
- Trains the masking model by manipulating classifier scores through masking operations to predict salient regions.  
- Introduces a novel saliency metric that crops the salient region and measures classification confidence.  
- Demonstrates strong ImageNet object localization results and outperforms weakly supervised baselines.  
- Uses a well-motivated U-Net with ResNet encoder architecture that produces sharp, interpretable saliency maps.  
- Builds on prior saliency literature (e.g., Zhou et al., Cao et al.) but offers a significant speed advantage.  
- Major flaw: the saliency metric is fundamentally flawed because cropping/resizing changes aspect ratio/scale and introduces classification artifacts.  
- The metric exploits classifiers’ limited scale invariance, artificially favoring small salient regions.  
- Claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers is questionable—results depend heavily on classifier properties (e.g., AlexNet vs. ResNet).  
- Dependence on masking model/architecture is not analyzed; unclear how different architectures affect results.  
- Weakly supervised localization metric fails when contextual cues, not object location, drive classification (polar bear example).  
- Authors should clarify whether Table 1 reports results over all classes or only correctly classified images and specify which LRP variant was used.  
- Post-rebuttal concerns remain about reliance on classifier scale invariance and whether visualizations reflect the target classifier or the masking network.  
- Strengths: fast method with real-time potential, novel metric idea, strong localization performance.  
- Weaknesses: flawed evaluation metric, unaddressed classifier and architecture dependencies, insufficient discussion of limitations—requires substantial revision or else rejection.
