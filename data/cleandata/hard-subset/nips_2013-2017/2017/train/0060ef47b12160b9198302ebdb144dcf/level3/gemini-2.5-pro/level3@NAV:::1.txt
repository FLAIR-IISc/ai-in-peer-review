**Review of "Real Time Image Saliency for Black Box Classifiers"**

This paper proposes a novel method for generating saliency masks for pre-trained, differentiable image classifiers. The core idea is to train a separate U-Net-based "masking model" to produce masks that optimize a saliency objective in a single forward pass. This "learning-to-explain" approach stands in contrast to prior iterative optimization methods, which are often computationally expensive. The primary contribution is a significant speed-up, enabling real-time saliency generation.

The paper’s strengths lie in its originality and potential significance. The concept of training a dedicated network to explain another is a promising research direction, and the achievement of real-time performance is a notable engineering feat that could enable new applications. The method appears technically sound, with no obvious methodological errors in its implementation.

However, the paper has several clear limitations that undermine its central claims of general applicability and robust evaluation. The primary weakness is the evaluation of saliency quality. Saliency is measured indirectly via two flawed metrics. First, weakly supervised localization can fail as a metric when classification relies on contextual cues (e.g., snow for a polar bear) rather than the object itself. Second, the proposed saliency metric is problematic: cropping and rescaling a salient region can introduce classification artifacts by altering the object's scale and aspect ratio. This exploits the target classifier’s limited scale invariance rather than genuinely measuring saliency quality. The metric also appears to favor small regions that, when blown up, might not constitute a faithful explanation.

Furthermore, the paper overstates its general applicability. The method's reliance on the target classifier's scale invariance severely limits its use. It is also unclear how the masking model's own architecture influences the results, raising a critical question: do the visualizations reflect the target classifier's reasoning or the learned biases of the masking network? This fundamental dependency is not discussed.

For clarity, the authors should specify if Table 1 results are over all classes or only the correct class and which LRP variant and parameters were used for comparison.

In conclusion, my recommendation is borderline. The paper presents a good idea but requires a more nuanced discussion of its limitations. If the authors revise the manuscript to thoroughly address the flaws in their evaluation metrics and acknowledge the strong dependencies on both the target and masking models, the paper would be acceptable. Without these critical revisions, I would recommend rejection.
