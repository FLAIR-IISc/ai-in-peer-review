- Paper proposes a fast neural-network-based method to learn saliency masks.  
- Reviewer is borderline: sees good ideas and believes in learning-to-explain concept, but would not object to either acceptance or rejection.  
- No obvious mistakes, but several clear limitations that need discussion.  
- Saliency is measured only indirectly via weakly supervised localization and a proposed saliency metric, both of which have clear limitations.  
- Weakly supervised localization can fail as a saliency quality metric when contextual cues (not object location) determine class.  
- The proposed saliency metric is flawed because cropping and rescaling the salient region can change aspect ratio and scale, introducing classification artefacts.  
- Small salient regions are favored by the metric and may be excessively blown up, exploiting classifiers’ limited scale invariance.  
- The masking model’s dependence on architecture is not discussed; unclear if different architectures were tested and how they affect results.  
- Minor clarifications requested: are Table 1 results over all classes or only the correct class?  
- Minor clarification requested: specify which LRP variant and parameter settings were used for comparisons.  
- Post-rebuttal concern: reliance on classifier quality and scale invariance severely limits applicability (cannot be used during training, for models lacking invariance, or for some domains like spectrograms).  
- Claim of general applicability to black-box classifiers is challenged.  
- Response suggests strong dependence on the masking network, raising uncertainty whether visualizations reflect the target (U-)network or the masking network.  
- Recommendation: if these limitations and dependencies are properly discussed in the paper, it is acceptable for publication; if not, it should be rejected.
