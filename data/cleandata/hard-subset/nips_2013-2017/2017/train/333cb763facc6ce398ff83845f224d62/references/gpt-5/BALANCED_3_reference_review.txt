Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Instead of constructing cross-covariances directly in the input domain or via latent process constructions (e.g., LMC/CONV), the authors design a complex-valued cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) and invoke Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s) to guarantee positive definiteness and obtain closed-form time-domain kernels via inverse Fourier transforms. Concretely, they factor S(ω) as R(ω)H R(ω) and parameterize each column Ri(ω) as a complex Gaussian (with weight, mean, diagonal covariance, delay, and phase). This yields pairwise cross-spectral Gaussians with interpretable parameters: pair-specific envelope (Σij), frequency (µij), delay (θij), and phase (φij). To ensure real-valued processes, they symmetrize the spectrum and obtain closed-form cross-covariances of the form exp(−½(τ+θij)TΣij(τ+θij)) cos((τ+θij)Tµij + φij). The framework subsumes SM-LMC and CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015) as special cases, while allowing per-output spectral parameters and explicit modeling of both delay and phase differences.

Empirically, MOSM is validated on: (i) a synthetic tri-variate setting (a signal, its derivative, and a delayed version), where MOSM best recovers auto- and cross-covariances and extrapolates both derivative and delay; (ii) a climate sensor missing-data task, where MOSM performs competitively with CSM/SM-LMC/CONV; and (iii) the Jura heavy-metals dataset, where MOSM improves MAE for Cadmium and is competitive for Copper.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean spectral-domain construction for MOGPs with solid grounding in Cramér’s theorem; positive definiteness is transparent via spectral factorization.
- Interpretable parameters (magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, delay, phase) at the pair level; explicit joint modeling of delay and phase goes beyond CSM/SM-LMC.
- Closed-form kernels enable straightforward inference with standard GP machinery.
- MOSM provably includes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases, clarifying relationships among prior models.
- Synthetic experiment is well-chosen to showcase capabilities beyond latent-process mixtures (simultaneous derivative and delay).
- Competitive performance on two real datasets; appropriate statistical testing is used where possible.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/technical presentation: (a) At one point Sij(ω) is written as Ri(ω)Rj(ω), but from S = RHR it should be Sij(ω) = conj(Ri(ω)) Rj(ω). (b) “Hermitian, i.e., Sij = Sji” is incorrect; Hermitian means Sij = conj(Sji). These should be corrected.
- Real-valuedness condition: the symmetrization Sij(ω) → ½(Sij(ω)+Sij(−ω)) works for the specific parameterization (yielding cosines), but the paper should more clearly connect this to the standard multivariate condition S(−ω) = S(ω)H to guarantee real-valued cross-covariances.
- Identifiability and optimization: many parameters per output per component (weights, means, diagonal covariances, delays, phases). No discussion of initialization (critical for SM kernels), parameter constraints (e.g., signs of weights), or regularization; risk of local optima and overfitting.
- Scalability: training remains O(N3) with respect to total observations; no sparse/structured approximations are provided (only mentioned as future work). No runtime comparisons.
- Experimental scope: limited to small m and Q; no ablations isolating the impact of delay/phase or per-output parameters vs CSM; results on climate are close and significance sometimes unclear.

Quality
- The main construction is sound and aligns with known spectral factorization results for MOGPs. The derivations for µij, Σij, and the closed-form cross-covariances follow standard product-of-Gaussians algebra and are plausible.
- Minor but important correctness/notation issues around conjugation and Hermitian properties need fixing.
- Empirical evaluation is reasonable but not exhaustive; optimization/initialization details are lacking.

Clarity
- Generally well-written with an informative background section and intuitive figures.
- Notational overload around Q (rank vs number of components) creates ambiguity; clarify the distinction or use different symbols.
- Please correct the conjugate/Hermitian statements, and explicitly state the real-valuedness condition for multivariate spectra.

Originality
- Extends SM kernels to the multi-output setting in a principled spectral way with pairwise phase and delay—beyond CSM’s constraints. While related ideas (LMC/CONV/CSM) exist, the per-output spectral factorization and resulting flexibility appear novel and well-motivated.

Significance
- Moderate. The approach offers a useful addition to the MOGP toolbox, with interpretable parameters and the ability to model rich cross-relationships. If adopted with scalable inference and robust initialization, it could be impactful.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Please correct the Sij(ω) expression to Sij(ω) = conj(Ri(ω)) Rj(ω) and the definition of Hermitian. Clarify whether S(−ω) = S(ω)H holds after symmetrization.
- Discuss identifiability and parameter constraints. Are wi allowed to be negative? For autocovariances αii ≥ 0, but cross-covariances can change sign—how do you regularize?
- Provide initialization strategies (e.g., per-channel periodograms/cross-periodograms, fitting initial µi, Σi, then θi, φi by cross-spectral phase).
- Clarify Q: is it rank of the factorization, number of spectral components, or both? If distinct, analyze the effect of increasing rank vs number of components.
- Include ablations: MOSM without delay/phase; MOSM with shared µ/Σ across channels; comparison to CSM when the ground truth has pair-specific µ/Σ.
- Report training time, sensitivity to Q, and robustness across random seeds.
- Consider non-diagonal Σi to capture anisotropy across dimensions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled and general spectral construction; interpretable and flexible cross-covariances.
- Subsumes prior multi-output SM variants; clear theoretical contribution.
- Demonstrated advantage on a synthetic task that stresses existing approaches; competitive real-data performance.

Arguments against acceptance
- Presentation issues around conjugates/Hermitian and real-valuedness conditions.
- Limited empirical scope and missing scalability/initialization details; potential over-parameterization risks not addressed.
- Improvements over strong baselines are sometimes modest.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6). The idea is solid and novel enough for NeurIPS; with corrected exposition and stronger experimental analysis, the paper would be a valuable contribution.
