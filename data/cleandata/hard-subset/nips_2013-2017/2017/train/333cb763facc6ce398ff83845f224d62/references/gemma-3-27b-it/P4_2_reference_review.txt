The paper proposes a novel multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernel, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the spectral mixture concept to multiple outputs. The core idea is to construct cross-spectral densities in the Fourier domain, leveraging Cramér’s theorem, and then transform them back to covariance functions. This allows for modeling complex relationships between outputs, including delays and phase shifts, with a clear spectral interpretation of the parameters. The approach builds upon existing work such as the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC), Convolution (CONV) models, Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernels, and the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel, addressing limitations of prior methods in flexibility and interpretability. Experimental validation is provided through synthetic data and two real-world datasets: climate signals and heavy metal concentrations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The MOSM kernel presents a fresh approach to MOGP kernel design, extending the successful spectral mixture concept to the multivariate case in a principled manner using Cramér’s theorem. It offers increased flexibility in modeling cross-covariances compared to existing methods like LMC and CONV.
* **Interpretability:** The spectral formulation provides a clear and intuitive interpretation of the kernel parameters in terms of frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay.
* **Generality:**  The MOSM kernel subsumes existing approaches like CSM as a special case, showcasing its broader applicability. The ability to model delays and phase shifts is a significant advantage.
* **Soundness:** The theoretical foundation based on Cramér’s theorem ensures the positive definiteness of the constructed kernel. The experiments demonstrate the kernel's ability to learn meaningful relationships in both synthetic and real-world data.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The paper includes a variety of experiments—synthetic data analysis, climate data imputation, and heavy metal concentration prediction—offering robust validation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While not explicitly discussed, the extension to higher dimensions or a larger number of outputs may lead to increased computational cost due to the matrix operations involved in the Fourier transform and covariance calculations.  A discussion of computational scalability would be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The kernel introduces a substantial number of parameters (especially with increased Q). The paper does not delve deeply into the sensitivity of performance to these parameters or strategies for effective hyperparameter optimization beyond standard methods.
* **Limited Statistical Significance:** While experiments show competitive performance, achieving statistically significant improvements is not consistent across all datasets. This suggests that the benefits of the MOSM kernel may be context-dependent.
* **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The comparison with other MOGP methods is somewhat limited. Including more recent and advanced techniques (e.g., deep kernel learning approaches for MOGPs) could strengthen the evaluation.
* **Clarity in Complex Components:** While the core ideas are explained well, the extension to arbitrary Q spectral components and the full implications of the complex-valued formulation could be further clarified with more detailed explanations or visual aids.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP kernel design.
* The proposed MOSM kernel offers increased flexibility and interpretability compared to existing methods.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the kernel on a variety of datasets.
* The work addresses an important problem in Gaussian process modeling—constructing flexible and interpretable kernels for multiple outputs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the proposed method is not fully addressed.
* Achieving statistically significant improvements over existing methods is inconsistent.
* The comparison with the current state-of-the-art in MOGP modeling is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a strong contribution to the field of Gaussian process modeling. The MOSM kernel is a novel and theoretically well-founded approach that addresses limitations of existing methods. While some aspects, such as computational complexity and statistical significance, could be further investigated, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should discuss the computational complexity, provide more insights into hyperparameter sensitivity, and consider including a more comprehensive comparison with recent advances in MOGP modeling.
