- Proposes MOSM kernel: extends Spectral Mixture to multi-output GPs using Cramér's theorem with a complex-valued cross-spectral density and interpretable frequency/magnitude/phase/delay parameters.  
- Generalizes CSM by allowing delays and independent channel frequency parameters (CSM is a special case with shared frequencies).  
- Theoretical soundness: rigorous derivation, guarantees positive definiteness via rank-1 factorization, and physically interpretable spectral parameters.  
- Synthetic experiments clearly validate MOSM’s ability to model derivatives and delays where LMC and CONV fail.  
- Paper is well-structured with clear definitions, theorems, derivations, and effective illustrative figures.  
- Experiments use appropriate baselines (SM-LMC, CONV, CSM) and include statistical validation (Kolmogorov–Smirnov test).  
- Limited practical differentiation from CSM: real-world results do not convincingly show MOSM’s advantages, partly due to highly correlated climate sensors.  
- Heavy metal dataset improvements are marginal/questionable in statistical significance and partly rely on comparisons to prior work.  
- No ablation studies to isolate the effects of delay, phase, and independent channel parameters, leaving the source of improvements unclear.  
- Scalability and computational complexity are not discussed; sparse implementations are only mentioned as future work.  
- Insufficient real-world validation: datasets are relatively simple and do not target scenarios where delays/phase differences are critical.  
- Overall assessment: technically solid and meaningful generalization with strong synthetic validation, but practical significance is limited by weak real-world evidence.  
- Recommendation: weak acceptance, contingent on additional experiments demonstrating unique MOSM advantages, clearer CSM comparisons, and addressing scalability concerns.
