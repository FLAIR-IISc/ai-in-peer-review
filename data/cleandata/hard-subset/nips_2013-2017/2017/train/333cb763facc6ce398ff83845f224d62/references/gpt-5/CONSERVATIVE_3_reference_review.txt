Summary
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a new family of kernels for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) constructed in the spectral domain via Cramér’s theorem. The authors factorize the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) as R(ω)^H R(ω), choose complex squared-exponential factors per output, and then enforce real-valuedness by symmetrizing the spectra. The resulting time-domain cross-covariances are closed-form spectral mixtures that admit per-pair frequency, bandwidth, magnitude, phase, and delay. MOSM subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases (with shared parameters and no delays). Experiments include: (i) a synthetic 3-output example involving a derivative and a delayed signal; (ii) a small climate imputation task with 4 stations; (iii) the Jura geostatistics dataset for metal concentration. MOSM is competitive across tasks and strongest on the synthetic example and Cadmium prediction.

Relation to prior work
The construction builds directly on: spectral mixture kernels for single outputs [Wilson & Adams, 2013], multi-output constructions via LMC and convolution processes [Álvarez & Lawrence], and the Cross-Spectral Mixture kernel [Ulrich et al., 2015]. The use of Cramér’s theorem to guarantee positive definiteness via a spectral factorization is natural and has appeared in related forms. The claimed generalization over CSM (allowing per-output frequency/bandwidth and explicit delays, with CSM/SM-LMC as special cases) is plausible and useful if validated.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity of designing MOGP kernels via spectral factorization; parameters have interpretable meaning (frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay).
- Closed-form time-domain kernels and straightforward training through standard GP marginal likelihood.
- MOSM unifies and generalizes SM-LMC/CSM; the delayed cross-covariances are not captured by standard LMC.
- Synthetic experiment demonstrates the ability to model phase and delay jointly, a setting tailored to the proposed construction.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical presentation has critical inaccuracies about complex Hermitian structure and conjugation that must be corrected. As written, some equations appear inconsistent with the stated positive-definiteness claims.
- Limited empirical validation: small-scale datasets, modest number of outputs, and evaluation limited to MAE. No assessment of uncertainty calibration, log likelihood, or runtime/scalability.
- Initialization, identifiability, and optimization issues (given many per-output, per-component parameters including phase and delay) are not addressed.
- Comparisons: reliance on reported CONV results for Jura (no statistical test), and lack of ablations (e.g., without delay/phase; rank Q effects) weaken the empirical case.

Quality
- Positive-definiteness: The factorization S(ω)=R(ω)^H R(ω) implies Sij(ω)=R_i(ω)^* R_j(ω); however, the paper writes Sij(ω)=R_i(ω)R_j(ω) (Eq. 7–9), omitting complex conjugation. Likewise, the Hermitian property is misstated as Sij(ω)=Sji(ω) instead of Sij(ω)=Sji(ω)^*. These are not minor typographical issues: without conjugation, S(ω) need not be Hermitian PSD. The real-valuedness condition is also misstated: for real processes, S(ω)=S(-ω)^H, but the paper enforces Sij(ω)←(Sij(ω)+Sij(-ω))/2, which does not include conjugation. The final real-valued kernel (Eq. 10) is plausible, but the spectral symmetrization step must be corrected to (S(ω)+S(-ω)^H)/2 or, better, construct conjugate-mirrored components explicitly.
- Parameter interpretation: The claim that αij can be negative conflicts with αij = w_ij (2π)^{n/2} |Σij|^{1/2} ≥ 0 as defined. Cross-covariances can be negative as a function of lag due to phase/shift, but αij itself should be nonnegative under the given construction; this needs clarification.
- Training: No discussion of initialization (critical for SM kernels), identifiability between phase and delay, or regularization. Complexity and scalability remain that of full GP inference; no sparse/structured approximation is provided (only mentioned as future work).

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but the mathematical exposition around complex Hermitian structure, conjugation, and symmetry conditions needs careful revision. There are several typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., squared vs. square exponential; stray footnote marker; missing 1/2 factors in exponents in some places). Reproducibility suffers from absent implementation details (initialization, priors/constraints on parameters, optimization settings) and no code link.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of designing cross-spectra via Cramér’s theorem and spectral mixtures is known; MOSM extends CSM by allowing per-output frequencies/bandwidths and explicit delays within a shared factorization. The extension is meaningful, but incremental relative to CSM and SM-LMC.

Significance
Currently limited. On real data, improvements are small or mixed; only the synthetic example shows a clear advantage (in a setting aligned with the method’s strengths). Without stronger empirical validation (larger m, higher-dimensional inputs, real tasks with known lags/phases, uncertainty metrics, runtime), it is hard to conclude practical impact. That said, a correct and clean spectral construction for multi-output kernels with delays could be useful to practitioners.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Fix the complex Hermitian and real-valuedness conditions rigorously: use Sij(ω)=R_i(ω)^* R_j(ω); enforce S(ω)=S(-ω)^H; update derivations accordingly and provide a short proof that the final real-valued kernel is valid.
- Clarify αij sign and the role of w_i, w_j in guaranteeing PSD.
- Discuss identifiability and initialization (e.g., spectral estimation of μ, Σ; priors/constraints on θ and φ).
- Provide ablations: effect of enabling/disabling delay and phase; effect of rank Q; sensitivity to initialization.
- Expand evaluation: larger multi-output problems, higher-dimensional inputs, predictive log likelihood and calibration; runtime and memory vs baselines; statistical tests for all comparisons; include your own CONV reruns for fairness.

Pros
- Interpretable, modular spectral construction with closed-form kernels.
- Generalizes prior MOGP spectral kernels; captures delay and phase jointly.
- Solid synthetic demonstration aligned with the method.

Cons
- Theoretical inaccuracies (conjugation/Hermitian symmetry) undermine the central PD claim as written.
- Empirical evidence is limited and mixed; no scalability story.
- Missing initialization/identifiability discussion and ablations.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and potentially useful, but the paper needs to correct the spectral construction rigorously and substantially strengthen the empirical validation before it can be endorsed over established MOGP approaches.
