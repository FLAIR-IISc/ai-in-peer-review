Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). It constructs cross-spectral densities in the Fourier domain and uses Cramér’s theorem to guarantee positive definiteness when mapping back to the time/space domain. The key idea is to factor the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) as R(ω)^H R(ω) and to parameterize each column Ri(ω) as a complex-valued squared-exponential (SE) with per-output parameters: magnitude, mean frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay. After ensuring the conjugate symmetry needed for real-valued processes (via a mirrored construction), the inverse Fourier transform yields closed-form cross-covariances that are products of SE envelopes and cosines with output-pair-specific shifts (delays) and phases. The model generalizes the scalar Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel [Wilson & Adams, 2013] to multiple outputs and strictly contains SM-LMC and CSM [Ulrich et al., 2015] as special cases. Experiments on synthetic data (derivatives and delays), climate sensor data, and the Jura geostatistical dataset illustrate that MOSM can capture inter-output structure (including delays and phase shifts) and performs competitively with or better than SM-LMC, CSM, and CONV [Álvarez & Lawrence, 2008].

Quality (technical soundness)
- The construction is theoretically grounded. The factorization S(ω) = R(ω)^H R(ω) enforces positive semidefiniteness for every ω, meeting Cramér’s theorem’s condition, and the mirrored spectral construction ensures real-valued covariances.
- The derivation of cross-spectral parameters from per-output parameters is consistent with products of Gaussian densities; the resulting kernel form (SE × cosine with pairwise delay and phase) is closed-form and interpretable.
- The training objective (marginal likelihood) and prediction follow standard GP methodology; implementation in GPflow is appropriate.

Concerns:
- A notational/technical inconsistency appears in Section 3.1: after stating S(ω) = R^H R, the text says Sij(ω) = Ri(ω) Rj(ω) for Q=1. This should involve a conjugate, Sij(ω) = Ri(ω)^* Rj(ω), to be consistent with Hermitian PSDs and to induce the “difference” of phases (φi − φj) that later appears. This looks like a fixable typo, but it should be corrected for clarity and correctness.
- The condition for real-valued processes requires S(−ω) = S(ω)^H for the multivariate case. The paper enforces symmetry by averaging Sij(ω) and Sij(−ω). It would be clearer to explicitly state and verify the conjugate-symmetry condition and to show that the proposed mirrored parameterization satisfies S(−ω) = S(ω)^H.
- Equation (10) appears to miss a factor 1/2 in the SE exponent; Definition 5 includes it. This should be made consistent.
- The paper focuses on stationary kernels; nonstationarity is not addressed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with clear motivation, derivation outline, and experimental setup. The spectral interpretation of parameters (magnitude, mean frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay) is helpful.
- A few typographical issues reduce clarity (e.g., “multiouput,” “wrt,” incomplete conference header, and the conjugation and 1/2-factor issues noted above). A brief appendix deriving the cross-spectral parameter formulas (Σij, µij, wij) from products of Gaussians would further improve reproducibility.
- Initialization and optimization details for MOSM (known to be important for SM kernels) are not discussed; adding these would aid practitioners.

Originality
- The paper extends spectral mixture ideas to MOGPs via a principled multivariate spectral construction, enabling per-output parameters and explicit modeling of both phase and delay across outputs. It strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM.
- The use of complex cross-spectral densities with per-output parameters in an R^H R factorization is a meaningful, nontrivial extension beyond prior LMC-based spectral models. While related ideas (e.g., CSM; real-valued cross-spectra in multivariate Matérn constructions) exist, the proposed formulation appears novel and more expressive.

Significance
- The method offers an interpretable and flexible MOGP kernel that can model delays and phase shifts—features relevant in multi-sensor time series and spatiotemporal applications.
- Empirical gains are strongest on the synthetic derivative/delay task and on one of the real datasets (Cadmium); on climate data, MOSM is competitive with CSM/SM-LMC but not consistently superior. Significance would be strengthened by broader benchmarks, ablations (e.g., turning off phase or delay), and scalability results.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the Sij(ω) conjugation and 1/2-factor inconsistencies; explicitly state and verify S(−ω) = S(ω)^H.
- Provide derivations for Σij, µij, and wij from the product of (possibly shifted) Gaussians and the complex exponential terms.
- Discuss identifiability and parameter coupling: only differences (θi − θj, φi − φj) appear in cross-terms; clarify how auto-terms anchor these parameters.
- Report initialization strategies for spectral means/variances, phases, and delays; include robustness analysis.
- Add ablations (no delay; no phase; shared per-output parameters) to isolate the contribution of each component.
- Consider larger-scale or higher-dimensional input experiments and discuss computational complexity and potential sparse approximations (e.g., variational Fourier features/inter-domain inducing variables).
- Include uncertainty calibration metrics, not only MAE.

Arguments for acceptance
- Theoretical soundness with a clean spectral construction guaranteeing positive definiteness.
- Clear interpretability (per-output frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay) and inclusion of prior models as special cases.
- Demonstrated capability to capture derivative and delay relationships that latent-process LMC-style models struggle with.
- Competitive to superior performance on multiple datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Minor but important clarity issues (conjugation, exponent factor, real-valued condition) should be corrected.
- Limited empirical breadth and only modest gains on some real datasets; no scalability experiments.
- Initialization, identifiability, and optimization considerations are not discussed, which can be critical for SM-type models.

Overall, the paper presents a technically well-founded and interpretable extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings, with meaningful modeling advantages. Addressing the noted clarity and empirical breadth issues would strengthen its impact.
