- Overall assessment: Proposes MOSM, a theoretically sound multivariate extension of spectral mixture kernels; recommend accept with minor revisions (leaning strong accept).
- Contribution: Extends SM kernels to MOGPs using Cramér’s theorem to construct flexible cross-spectral densities.
- Motivation vs prior work: MOSM offers spectral interpretability and flexibility not afforded by LMC, CONV, or CSM, and generalizes CSM by relaxing its constraints.
- Theoretical foundation: Rigorous derivation grounded in Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems, ensuring kernel validity.
- Interpretability & expressiveness: Allows independent control of auto- and cross-covariance parameters, modeling delays and phase differences.
- Generalization: MOSM strictly generalizes the CSM kernel, increasing modeling capability.
- Experiments: Evaluated on synthetic and two real-world datasets; synthetic results convincingly recover complex relationships (derivatives, delays).
- Experimental limitations: Real-world results show modest gains over CSM, CONV, and SM-LMC, without clear advantage in practice.
- Computational concerns: Paper lacks explicit analysis of computational complexity and scalability for inference and prediction.
- Parameter sensitivity: Introduces more parameters than simpler models but lacks investigation or guidelines on sensitivity and selection.
- Statistical rigor: Real-world result tables report only best runs without error bars or statistical significance testing.
- Q choice: Authors choose Q=1; a brief discussion of challenges with larger Q (cost, overfitting) is needed.
- Delay units: Clarify units of the delay parameter θij and whether they match input feature units.
- Visualizations: Recommend visualizing learned covariance functions alongside reconstructed signals for the synthetic experiment.
- Statistical tests: Add error bars and formal significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for real-world experiments to support performance claims.
- Scalability/future work: Suggest discussing combining MOSM with sparse GP techniques to address scalability.
- Pro acceptance arguments: Strong theory, novel and interpretable kernel, comprehensive evaluation, and generalization of state-of-the-art.
- Con acceptance arguments: Limited empirical gains on real data, missing discussion on complexity and parameter sensitivity, and absent statistical analysis.
- Final recommendation: Accept with minor revisions addressing experimental analysis, computational complexity, and parameter sensitivity.
