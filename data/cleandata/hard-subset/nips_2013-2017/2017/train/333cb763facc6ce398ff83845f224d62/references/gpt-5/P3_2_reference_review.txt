Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to design the matrix-valued power spectral density (PSD) S(ω) directly and recover the cross-covariances via Cramér’s theorem, the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem. The authors parameterize a rank-Q PSD factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), with each column Ri(ω) a complex Gaussian (SE) in frequency with output-specific parameters: magnitude wi, mean µi, diagonal covariance Σi, delay θi (linear phase), and phase φi (constant phase). This yields closed-form time-domain cross-covariances that are spectral mixtures with per-pair parameters and with explicit, interpretable delay and phase. The construction generalizes SM kernels [Wilson & Adams ’13] to multiple outputs and subsumes SM-LMC and CSM [Ulrich et al. ’15] as special cases. The paper presents synthetic experiments (jointly learning a derivative and a delayed signal) and two real data tasks (climate sensors and the Jura geostatistics benchmark), comparing against CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Using Cramér’s theorem and a factorized PSD is a principled way to guarantee positive definiteness while allowing rich cross-structure. The spectral-domain parameterization is physically interpretable (frequency, bandwidth, delay, phase).
- Expressiveness beyond LMC/CSM: Unlike LMC/CSM, MOSM permits per-output parameters that induce pair-specific cross-covariance means/bandwidths and simultaneously models delay and phase. The reduction to SM-LMC/CSM is clearly articulated.
- Closed-form kernels: Choosing complex SE factors leads to closed-form time-domain covariances (Gaussian envelope times cosine with a shift), enabling exact GP inference (modulo cubic scaling).
- Synthetic task demonstrates capability: MOSM is the only model among those compared that extrapolates both a derivative and a delayed channel simultaneously, which requires cross-covariances not representable as linear combinations of univariate kernels.
- Empirical competitiveness: On climate and Jura tasks, MOSM is competitive and sometimes best; statistical testing is reported where feasible.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Symmetrization and positive definiteness: To obtain real-valued processes, the paper symmetrizes each Sij(ω) as 0.5(Sij(ω)+Sij(−ω)). Entrywise symmetrization does not, in general, preserve positive semidefiniteness of the matrix S(ω). The text later states this is “equivalent to choosing Ri(ω) to be a vector of two mirrored complex SE functions,” which would preserve PSD if implemented at the factor level. Please provide a formal argument and a clear construction showing that the final, symmetrized S(ω) remains Hermitian PSD for every ω, e.g., by augmenting R with mirrored components (or by stacking factors) rather than averaging entries.
- Parameter identifiability and redundancy: The model introduces, per component and per output, wi, µi, Σi, θi, φi. There are likely non-identifiabilities (e.g., signs can be absorbed into phases, linear phase vs delay trade-offs, common shifts across outputs cancel in θij = θi − θj). A discussion of identifiability, parameter constraints (e.g., wi ≥ 0 w.l.o.g.), and regularization/priors would strengthen the work. Initialization is known to be critical for SM kernels; heuristics from (cross-)periodograms/coherences could be described.
- On “negative αij”: Eq. (10) defines αij = wij(2π)n/2|Σij|1/2, with wij derived from wi, wj. Since S(ω) = RH R is PSD, wi can be taken nonnegative without loss of generality by reparameterizing φi. The statement “αij ∈ R allows both negative and positive correlations” is potentially misleading: cross-covariances can of course take negative values in τ even with nonnegative amplitudes, due to the cosine and phase; explicit negative αij is not required nor generally compatible with S(ω) ≽ 0 if interpreted as a free parameter. Please clarify.
- Hermitian property: For complex cross-spectra, Hermitian PSD requires Sij(ω) = Sji(ω) (complex conjugate). The text at one point states Sij(ω) = Sji(ω) without conjugation—this should be corrected.
- Theoretical details: The derivation of µij, Σij, wij from products of Gaussians and phases is central. While plausible, explicit derivations and constants (including the 1/4 factor in wij) should be provided (e.g., in an appendix) to avoid ambiguities.
- Scalability: As with full GPs, training scales cubically in total observations N, and the experiments subsample (e.g., N=1000 for climate). The discussion mentions sparse/inter-domain routes; it would be valuable to (i) quantify current runtimes/memory, (ii) discuss whether MOSM interacts naturally with inducing Fourier features (e.g., variational Fourier features, inter-domain inducing variables), and (iii) outline whether the factorized S(ω) structure admits computational savings (e.g., Kronecker across tasks is not available here because parameters are pair-specific).
- Experiments and metrics: Beyond MAE, reporting test log-likelihoods, calibration metrics, and uncertainty visualizations would better assess a kernel’s quality. An ablation study (e.g., removing delay θ or phase φ, tying µ, Σ across outputs) could show where the gains come from. Runtime and sensitivity to Q would be informative. In the climate task, the signals are highly correlated; a dataset with known transport delays (e.g., hydrological or sensor arrays with advection) would better showcase the delay component. For Jura, the CONV results are taken from the literature—comparability may be imperfect.
- Related work coverage: The paper situates MOSM with LMC/CONV/CSM and multivariate Matérn. It would help to clarify more precisely how CSM is a special case (mapping of its shift/phase parameters to θ, φ here), and to discuss differences to other cross-covariance constructions (e.g., valid multivariate Matérn with phase/delay is generally nontrivial).

Quality
The construction is technically sound and well-motivated. With the caveat on symmetrization, the PSD-based factorization provides a clean PD guarantee. The experimental evidence supports the main claims but could be deeper in breadth, ablations, and uncertainty evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. However, key technical points (proof of PSD after symmetrization, hermitian conjugation, derivations of pairwise parameters, identifiability, and initialization) need clearer exposition. Figures help intuition.

Originality
Moderately high. Designing cross-spectra via a factorized complex Gaussian parameterization and deriving closed-form real-valued cross-covariances with explicit delay and phase appears novel relative to SM-LMC/CSM, and the framework neatly generalizes scalar SM to the multi-output case.

Significance
The approach is likely to be useful for practitioners modeling multi-channel signals with delays and phase offsets (e.g., spatiotemporal sensor networks, neuroscience, geostatistics). The spectral parameterization with interpretability is appealing. Adoption would be enhanced by scalable inference and robust initialization.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, elegant construction with interpretable parameters and PD guarantee.
- Extends SM to MOGPs in a way that strictly generalizes SM-LMC/CSM.
- Closed-form kernels; compelling synthetic demonstration.
- Competitive empirical results; some statistical testing.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity around symmetrization/PSD and some notation issues.
- Limited discussion of identifiability and initialization.
- Scalability not addressed beyond discussion; experiments relatively small.
- Empirical evaluation could be deeper and include uncertainty metrics and ablations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous construction ensuring real-valuedness that preserves PSD at the matrix level (augment R with mirrored factors) and correct Hermitian statements.
- Add derivations of µij, Σij, wij and discuss parameter constraints and identifiability; specify initialization strategy.
- Report test log-likelihoods and calibration; include ablations for θ and φ.
- Discuss computational complexity and a concrete scalable variant (e.g., variational inter-domain Fourier features tailored to MOSM).

Overall, this is a solid and promising contribution; addressing the theoretical clarifications and strengthening the experiments would significantly improve the paper.
