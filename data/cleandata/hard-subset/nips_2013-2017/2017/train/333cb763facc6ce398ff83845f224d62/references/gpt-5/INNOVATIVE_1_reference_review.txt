Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a principled extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output GPs via direct design in the Fourier domain using Cramér’s theorem. The key idea is to factor the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) as R(ω)^H R(ω), choose per-channel complex-valued squared-exponential factors Ri(ω) with frequency, bandwidth, delay, and phase, and then recover closed-form, real-valued cross-covariances by enforcing conjugate symmetry. This yields a stationary, positive-definite multi-output kernel whose cross-covariances can incorporate per-pair delays and phase shifts, while autocovariances reduce to standard SM components.

This sits in clear contrast to:
- LMC/CONV approaches [Álvarez & Lawrence] that enforce shared latent kernels and tend to produce centered, symmetric cross-covariances.
- SM-LMC and CSM [Ulrich et al., Wilson & Adams], where CSM introduces pairwise phase shifts via an ad hoc complex term but still ties many spectral parameters across outputs. The authors show MOSM subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases.

Experiments include: (i) a synthetic 3-output example (base signal, its derivative, and a delayed copy) where MOSM learns the known cross-covariances and extrapolates both derivative and delay better than baselines; (ii) a climate sensor imputation task with 4 outputs where MOSM is competitive with CSM; and (iii) the Jura geostatistics dataset, where MOSM is best on Cadmium and comparable on Copper.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: The spectral-domain factorization with complex SEs is a clean, general construction that guarantees positive definiteness while enabling delays and phase shifts per channel pair. This is a substantial conceptual step beyond CSM, which is a constrained special case.
- Interpretability: Parameters have direct spectral meaning (magnitude, mean frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay), making learned dependencies interpretable.
- Expressivity with parsimony: Although cross-covariances differ per pair, parameters are tied per channel via the factorization, preventing the m^2 blow-up and maintaining O(mQ) parameter growth.
- Theory-to-practice: The paper carefully enforces real-valuedness (via spectral symmetry) and provides closed-form time-domain kernels. The synthetic experiment convincingly demonstrates capabilities (derivative and delay) that LMC-style models struggle with.

Weaknesses and questions
- Notational/technical clarity: In Sec. 3.1, for the factorization S(ω) = R(ω)^H R(ω), the entry should be Sij(ω) = R_i(ω)^* R_j(ω) to ensure Hermitian PSD, but Eq. (9) omits the conjugate. Please fix this and make explicit the condition S(ω) = S(-ω)^H required for real-valued processes (the symmetrization step is reasonable, but the exact conjugate-symmetry should be stated precisely).
- Empirical scope: Real-world gains over CSM/SM-LMC are modest on climate and mixed on Jura (best on Cadmium, not on Copper). Given the strong conceptual advance, more challenging multi-sensor datasets with known lags/phase structure (e.g., EEG/MEG, speech array data, seismic arrays) would better showcase the unique capabilities.
- Initialization and identifiability: SM kernels are sensitive to initialization; here, phases and delays add additional symmetries. Please describe practical initialization heuristics (e.g., per-channel periodogram fits, unwrapping phases, priors/constraints) and discuss identifiability/equivalence (e.g., trade-offs between φ and θ across components).
- Rank and ablations: The construction generalizes to rank Q>1 via mixtures, but experiments use modest Q without ablations. It would help to show how increasing Q (or relaxing per-channel tying) trades off fit vs. overfitting, and whether delays/phases remain interpretable across components.
- Complexity and scalability: As with all exact MOGPs, training scales cubically in the number of observations across channels. The discussion mentions sparse extensions; a small-scale demonstration (e.g., inter-domain Fourier features) would strengthen practical significance.
- Fairness in baselines: On Jura, CONV results are taken from prior work, which may not be strictly comparable. Please note this in the main text and, if possible, rerun under your implementation.

Quality
- The theoretical construction is sound and well-motivated; minor corrections in the Hermitian/conjugate notation should be addressed. The paper is careful about guaranteeing positive definiteness and real-valuedness.
- Experiments support the central claims, especially on the synthetic task; real-data improvements are competitive but not uniformly superior.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Please correct the conjugation in Eq. (9), explicitly state the conjugate-symmetry condition for real processes, and briefly outline initialization and optimization practices.

Originality
- High. Designing multi-output kernels directly via complex cross-spectral densities with per-pair phase and delay, while keeping parameters per channel through a factorization, is a notable advance and subsumes prior SM-LMC/CSM constructions.

Significance
- Conceptually significant and likely to influence future MOGP kernel design, especially for applications where relative timing and phase are key. Even with modest empirical gains on some datasets, the modeling flexibility and interpretability are valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled, and novel kernel family that generalizes and unifies prior SM-based MOGP approaches.
- Enables delays and phase shifts with interpretable parameters; strong synthetic validation.
- Solid theoretical grounding via Cramér’s theorem and PSD factorization.

Cons against acceptance
- Real-world improvements are not yet decisive across the board.
- Minor theoretical notation issues; limited discussion on initialization/identifiability and scalability.

Overall recommendation
- Accept. This is a bold and elegant contribution that advances the design space for multi-output GPs. With minor corrections and expanded discussion, it will be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS program.

Confidence: 4/5
