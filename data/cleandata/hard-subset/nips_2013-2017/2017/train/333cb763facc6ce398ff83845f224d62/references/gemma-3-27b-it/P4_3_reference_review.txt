The paper proposes a novel multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernel, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the spectral mixture concept to multiple outputs. The core idea is to define complex-valued cross-spectral densities and utilize Cramér’s theorem to construct the covariance function. This approach aims to provide a flexible and interpretable kernel capable of modelling delays and phase differences between channels, overcoming limitations of existing methods like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernels. The paper includes a synthetic experiment and validation on two real-world datasets (climate data and heavy metal concentrations).

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the mathematical foundations of the proposed method. The derivation from Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems is concisely explained, and the connection to existing methods (SM, LMC, CSM) is appropriately highlighted. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the contribution. The formulation of the MOSM kernel is mathematically sound, and the discussion of parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay) enhances interpretability. The experimental setup is described adequately, and the results are presented in a clear and organized manner.

A significant strength lies in the theoretical grounding of the method. Leveraging Cramér’s theorem allows for a principled construction of the multi-output kernel directly in the Fourier domain, ensuring positive definiteness. The ability to model delays and phase differences, explicitly addressed in the design of the cross-spectral densities, represents a genuine extension over existing approaches. The synthetic example effectively demonstrates the model's ability to recover known covariance properties, including delays and derivatives.  The comparison against existing methods on real-world datasets provides a valuable assessment of the practical performance.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the mathematical formulation is rigorous, the explanation of the intuition behind the complex-valued cross-spectral densities could be improved. The link between the spectral parameters (magnitude, phase, delay) and their impact on the resulting covariance function isn’t fully elaborated, potentially hindering understanding for readers less familiar with signal processing concepts. The experiments, while well-executed, could be more comprehensive. The climate data results show little improvement over existing methods, and the statistical significance testing is limited. The heavy metal concentration dataset shows promising results, but the authors acknowledge that the simpler SM-LMC performs better on one task (Copper estimation). The discussion doesn’t fully explore potential reasons for these outcomes.  The scalability of the proposed method is not addressed. While GPflow is used, the computational complexity of the kernel, especially with a larger number of outputs and spectral components, is not discussed. Additionally, the connection to related work is not exhaustive. A brief discussion of other relevant spectral methods in multivariate statistics could be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting.
*   Strong theoretical foundation based on Cramér’s theorem.
*   Clear and interpretable formulation with physically meaningful parameters.
*   Demonstrated ability to model delays and phase differences.
*   Competitive performance on real-world datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited improvement over existing methods in some experiments.
*   Lack of detailed intuition behind complex-valued spectral densities.
*   Scalability and computational complexity not addressed.
*   Limited statistical significance testing.
*   Potential for more comprehensive related work discussion.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and original contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. The proposed MOSM kernel offers a principled and interpretable way to model dependencies between outputs, going beyond the capabilities of existing methods. While the experiments don't showcase overwhelming performance gains, the theoretical novelty and potential for further development warrant acceptance. The weaknesses, while present, do not fundamentally undermine the value of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve the clarity of the explanation, provide a discussion on scalability and computational complexity, and broaden the related work section.
