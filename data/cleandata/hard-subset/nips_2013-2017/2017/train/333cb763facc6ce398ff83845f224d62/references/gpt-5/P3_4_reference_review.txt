Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Starting from Cramér’s theorem, the authors parameterize the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) via a low-rank factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), with channel-specific complex Gaussian components Ri(ω). This yields closed-form, real-valued, stationary cross-covariances in the time domain after enforcing spectral symmetry. The resulting kernel generalizes the scalar Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output setting and strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM by allowing per-channel frequency means and variances, and crucially, both phase and delay differences between outputs. The parameters admit clear spectral interpretations (magnitudes, frequencies, bandwidths, phases, delays).

Compared to prior MOGP constructions (LMC/CONV), MOSM does not rely on shared latent GPs with fixed coregionalization but instead designs the cross-spectrum directly, ensuring positive definiteness at each frequency. Relative to CSM, MOSM allows different auto-spectral content per channel and adds explicit delays. The paper situates the work within classic spectral theory (Bochner/Cramér) and relevant multi-output covariance design literature (Genton & Kleiber, Gneiting et al.), and briefly mentions related spectral constructions with real t-student densities.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and principled construction: Designing S(ω) via per-channel complex Gaussian mixtures with Hermitian factorization is elegant and guarantees PSD in a simple way (zH S z = ||R z||^2).
- Interpretability: The decomposition yields intuitive channel-level parameters and pairwise cross-parameters (differences of per-channel phase/delay), enabling explicit modeling of delay and out-of-phase relations—a limitation of LMC/CSM.
- Closed-form kernels: The inverse Fourier transform gives tractable time-domain expressions combining SE and cosine with shifts.
- Clear generalization: MOSM reduces to SM-LMC and CSM under stated parameter tying, highlighting how those models are special cases.
- Empirical validation: Synthetic experiment demonstrates learning of derivative and delay relationships without supervision, where latent-process models struggle. On real data (climate, Jura), MOSM is competitive or best on several tasks.

Weaknesses and questions
- Real-valuedness condition: For real processes, the cross-spectrum must satisfy Sij(−ω) = Sij(ω)̄. The paper enforces symmetry by averaging S(ω) and S(−ω). Please clarify that the construction indeed ensures Sij(−ω) = Sij(ω)̄ (not merely evenness), ideally by defining Ri to include mirrored complex-conjugate pairs, as done in scalar SM.
- Parameterization and PD for Q > 1: Positive definiteness hinges on S(ω) = ∑q R(q)H R(q) with channel-specific parameters per component. Definition 5 reads as if {α(q)ij, μ(q)ij, Σ(q)ij, θ(q)ij, φ(q)ij} were free per pair. Please make explicit that for each q these pairwise parameters are induced (as in eq. 9–10) from per-channel parameters {w(q)i, μ(q)i, Σ(q)i, θ(q)i, φ(q)i}; otherwise PD is not guaranteed.
- Possible typos/factors of 2: The expression for Σij in the product of Gaussians is unusual: Σij = 2 Σi (Σi + Σj)−1 Σj (in 1D this yields 2ab/(a+b), whereas the standard product covariance is (a−1 + b−1)−1 = ab/(a+b)). Similarly, the magnitude term has a 1/4 factor in the quadratic form. Please double-check these constants; they can be reconciled by consistent placement of 1/2 factors in the Gaussian exponent but should be corrected/derived in an appendix.
- Identifiability and invariances: Only differences θij = θi − θj and φij = φi − φj appear; there are global shift/phase invariances and label-switching across mixture components. Discuss identifiability, initialization, and whether priors/constraints are needed.
- Scalability and parameter growth: The number of parameters scales as O(m Q (3n+2)) per component (w, μ, diag Σ, θ, φ per channel), which can be large. There is no sparse/variational treatment; cubic training is limiting. A complexity analysis and a practical sparse MOSM (e.g., inter-domain Fourier features or inducing spectral variables) would strengthen the contribution.
- Empirical rigor: 
  - Initialization is critical for SM kernels; please state how μ, Σ, θ, φ, w were initialized (periodogram per channel? random?).
  - Statistical testing: KS tests on MAE distributions are not ideal; paired tests across seeds/splits (Wilcoxon signed-rank or t-tests on per-run MAEs) are more appropriate. Clarify what distribution the KS was applied to.
  - Baselines: Consider multivariate Matérn cross-covariances (e.g., Gneiting–Kleiber–Schlather) and ablations (MOSM without delay; without phase; tied spectra across channels) to isolate the contribution of phase/delay.
  - Parameter recovery: In the synthetic study, report recovered θ and φ (and μ, Σ) against ground-truth to substantiate interpretability.
- Clarity issues: A few notational inconsistencies (missing 1/2 factors, αij said to be “in R” while αij ≥ 0 and the sign comes from cosine/phase), and an imprecise statement “non-positive-definite cross-covariances” (cross-covariances need not be PD but the full block kernel must be). Tighten language.

Quality
Technically solid and mostly correct, with a clean PSD proof via factorization. However, some derivations/constants need fixing and the PD guarantee for Q > 1 should be stated precisely. Experimental evidence is suggestive but modest on real data and could be made more rigorous and comprehensive.

Clarity
Generally well-written and well-organized, with clear intuition and figures. Needs fixes for minor typos, explicit real-valuedness condition, and precise parameterization to avoid misinterpretation. More implementation details (initialization, optimization, runtime) would improve reproducibility.

Originality
A meaningful and nontrivial extension of SM to multi-output via complex cross-spectra with per-channel parameters, enabling both phase and delay. The use of low-rank spectral factorization in this setting is novel relative to CSM/SM-LMC.

Significance
Potentially impactful for multi-task time series and spatial-temporal modeling where phase/delay are essential (e.g., sensor networks, neuroscience). While the real-data gains are moderate, the methodology is likely to be adopted and extended, especially with a scalable variant.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, interpretable, and general kernel construction with clear advantages over CSM/SM-LMC.
- New capability: joint modeling of phase and delay in cross-covariances.
- Closed-form kernels, solid theoretical foundation.

Cons for acceptance
- Some mathematical inconsistencies/typos; needs clarification for PD with Q > 1 and real-valuedness.
- Limited scalability and lack of sparse inference.
- Empirical evaluation could be stronger and more rigorous; modest gains on real datasets.

Suggestions
- Provide full derivations for eqs. (8–11), correct constants, and state the real-valuedness condition precisely.
- Explicitly parameterize via per-channel parameters for each mixture component in Def. 5 to ensure PD.
- Add ablations and parameter recovery on synthetic data; strengthen statistical testing; include multivariate Matérn baseline.
- Discuss/implement a sparse MOSM (inter-domain Fourier inducing features) and report complexity and runtimes.

Overall: A solid, principled contribution with clear novelty and good potential impact; I recommend acceptance after addressing the noted mathematical clarifications and strengthening empirical rigor.
