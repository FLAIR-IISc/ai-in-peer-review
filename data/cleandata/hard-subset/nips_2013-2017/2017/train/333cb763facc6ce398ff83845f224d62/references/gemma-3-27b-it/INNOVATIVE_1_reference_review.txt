This paper introduces a novel approach to constructing multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernels by extending the spectral mixture (SM) concept to the multivariate setting. The core idea – leveraging Cramér’s theorem to build covariance functions from complex-valued cross-spectral densities – is a compelling and potentially impactful contribution. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches (LMC, CSM) regarding interpretability and flexibility, and the proposed MOSM kernel offers a more general framework allowing for delays, phase shifts, and varying auto-covariances. The spectral interpretation of the parameters is a significant strength.

The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, building up the necessary background before presenting the main contribution. The mathematical derivations appear sound, and the connection to Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems provides a strong theoretical foundation. The inclusion of a synthetic example to validate the ability to recover known covariance structures is a good design choice. The experimental validation against existing methods on two real-world datasets is also commendable.

However, the experimental results, while demonstrating competitive performance, aren’t overwhelmingly convincing. The improvements over existing methods are often marginal, and statistical significance is only clearly established for one dataset and a specific failure scenario. The reliance on five latent components/spectral components across all experiments feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks justification. It would be beneficial to see a more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of the number of spectral components on performance.

A key weakness is the limited novelty of the experimental setup. The datasets and experimental protocols are borrowed directly from previous work, which diminishes the impact of the MOSM’s potential benefits. A more challenging and novel application would better demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach. Additionally, while the authors acknowledge the connection to CSM, they don't fully explore the implications of their more general framework.  Specifically, a deeper discussion of *when* the additional flexibility of MOSM is truly *needed* – beyond the synthetic example – would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output GPs via Cramér’s theorem is a genuinely novel idea. This moves beyond simply adapting existing techniques.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The work is built upon strong mathematical foundations (Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems).
* **Interpretability:** The spectral interpretation of the kernel parameters is a significant advantage over black-box kernel approaches.
* **Generality:** MOSM generalizes existing methods like CSM, offering increased flexibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Incremental Improvement:** The experimental results are not a dramatic improvement over existing methods.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments largely replicate existing work and lack a compelling, novel application.
* **Lack of Ablation Study:** The choice of the number of spectral components isn't justified or systematically explored.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Benefits:** The paper doesn’t adequately explain when the added flexibility of MOSM is crucial in real-world scenarios.



Overall, I believe this paper represents a significant conceptual advance in the field of MOGPs. While the experimental validation is not entirely convincing, the novelty and theoretical soundness of the approach are strong enough to warrant acceptance, even with the current limitations.  I would encourage the authors to address the concerns raised above in future work, particularly by exploring more challenging applications and conducting a thorough ablation study. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should consider adding a discussion section explicitly outlining when the additional complexity of the MOSM kernel is justified.
