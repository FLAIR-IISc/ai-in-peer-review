Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to construct cross-spectral densities directly in the Fourier domain and then obtain cross-covariances by inverse Fourier transform, leveraging Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem). The authors parameterize a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)^H R(ω) of the Hermitian cross-spectrum, choose each column function Ri(ω) to be a complex Gaussian (with weight, mean, covariance, phase, and delay), symmetrize to ensure real-valued processes, and derive a closed-form time-domain kernel:
kij(τ) = Σ_q α(q)ij exp(-1/2 (τ+θ(q)ij)^T Σ(q)ij (τ+θ(q)ij)) cos((τ+θ(q)ij)^T μ(q)ij + φ(q)ij).
This generalizes Wilson & Adams’ spectral mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output setting and strictly contains Ulrich et al.’s Cross Spectral Mixture (CSM) and SM-LMC as special cases. Compared to LMC and convolutional models, MOSM provides interpretable per-pair phase and delay, and allows different auto-spectral parameters per channel.

Experiments include: (i) a synthetic 3-output problem (a function, its derivative, and a delayed version) where MOSM best recovers auto- and cross-covariances and extrapolates held-out regions; (ii) a climate sensor imputation task (4 stations, missing half of one sensor), where MOSM is competitive with CSM and SM-LMC; and (iii) the Jura geostatistics dataset for metal concentration prediction, where MOSM improves MAE on Cadmium and is comparable on Copper.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Designing MOGP kernels via cross-spectral densities with an explicit factorization guarantees positive semidefiniteness and yields closed-form time-domain kernels with interpretable parameters (magnitude, frequency, phase, and delay).
- Generality: MOSM strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM; it supports pair-specific cross-covariances with phase shifts and delays, and need not enforce shared auto-spectral parameters across outputs.
- Technical soundness of the construction: The S(ω) = R^H R prescription ensures S(ω) ≽ 0 for each ω, and the symmetricization step produces real-valued processes.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic results convincingly show MOSM can capture derivative and delayed relationships that are challenging for LMC/CONV. Real-data results are competitive or better, with reasonable hyperparameterization parity (same number of spectral components).
- Relevance to NeurIPS: Builds on well-cited lines (SM kernels; LMC; cross-spectral methods) and contributes a principled, interpretable MOGP kernel.

Weaknesses
- Clarity and correctness issues in notation: Several places appear to omit complex conjugation or misstate Hermitian symmetry (e.g., Sij(ω) = Sji(ω) rather than Sij(ω) = Sji(ω)̄; and for Q=1, Sij(ω) should be R_i(ω)̄ R_j(ω), not R_i(ω) R_j(ω)). The condition ensuring real-valued processes should be stated as S(-ω) = S(ω)^H; replacing S(ω) by (S(ω)+S(-ω))/2 lacks conjugation and may be insufficient as written.
- Limited empirical scope and analysis: Real-data gains are modest; statistical testing choices (Kolmogorov–Smirnov) are nonstandard for paired predictive errors. No ablations (e.g., impact of delay/phase; parameter tying) or parameter count comparisons are provided.
- Scalability: As with full GPs, training is O(N^3). While the discussion mentions sparse avenues, no scalable approximation or runtime/memory analysis is provided; yet MOGP utility often hinges on scalability.
- Overparameterization and identifiability: Each channel has separate μ, Σ, θ, φ per mixture component, which increases parameter count relative to CSM/SM-LMC. The paper does not discuss identifiability (global phase/delay gauges, weight-scale trade-offs) or regularization/priors, nor initialization strategies, which are crucial for stable training.
- Relation to CSM could be sharpened: The mapping of CSM constraints to MOSM parameter restrictions is only sketched; a more precise equivalence (including when MOSM reduces exactly to CSM) would help.

Quality
The spectral construction is principled, and the derived kernel appears correct modulo the conjugation/symmetry notational issues. Experiments are well set up, and the synthetic task is appropriate. However, stronger empirical validation (larger-scale, additional datasets, and robust statistical tests) and a discussion of optimization/initialization would improve the assessment of robustness.

Clarity
Overall well written and motivated, but clarity suffers in key complex-valued details. Hermitian and conjugate symmetry properties should be stated precisely, and the symmetricization step should be corrected/explained to guarantee S(-ω) = S(ω)^H. Distinguish clearly between the “rank” Q in the R^H R factorization and the number of spectral mixture components to avoid confusion.

Originality
The use of a frequency-domain factorization with complex Gaussian Ri(ω) to derive pairwise phase and delay is an elegant generalization beyond CSM. While conceptually incremental relative to CSM/LMC, MOSM’s combined per-pair spectral parameters and explicit delay modeling offer added flexibility and interpretability.

Significance
Moderate. The method is likely to be of interest to practitioners working with MOGPs, time series, and structured sensors. However, the empirical gains on real data are not decisive, and lack of scalability limits immediate impact.

Reasons to accept
- Principled, interpretable, closed-form MOGP kernel generalizing SM and CSM with phase and delay.
- Solid synthetic validation for challenging cross-relationships (derivatives, delays).
- Clear spectral-domain construction with potential for further extensions and sparse implementations.

Reasons to reject
- Notational/technical issues around Hermitian symmetry and conjugation need correction.
- Empirical gains are limited; statistical analysis is not fully appropriate; no ablations or parameter-count fairness checks.
- No scalable inference; unclear training stability/initialization under many parameters.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and clarify the complex-valued derivations: ensure Sij(ω) = R_i(ω)̄ R_j(ω) for Q=1, state Hermitian as S(ω) = S(ω)^H (i.e., Sij = Sjī), and enforce real-valuedness by S(-ω) = S(ω)^H (explain how the proposed symmetricization achieves this).
- Report parameter counts per model and include ablations: (i) without delay and/or phase; (ii) tying μ, Σ across outputs; (iii) varying Q. Analyze learned parameters to demonstrate interpretability (e.g., recovered delays).
- Use appropriate paired tests (paired t-test, Wilcoxon signed-rank, or bootstrap) over test points/runs; report confidence intervals. Include RMSE/NLL and calibration metrics where appropriate.
- Discuss identifiability (global phase/delay gauges), constraints, and initialization (e.g., per-channel periodograms to initialize μ/Σ, softplus for positive params). Provide training curves and robustness to random seeds.
- Add a scalability section: a sparse variational version (e.g., inter-domain inducing features or variational Fourier features) tailored to MOSM, with complexity/runtime comparisons.
- Minor: fix typos (“multiouput”, “Gaussian noised”, Hermitian definition), and clarify the distinction between factorization rank and the number of spectral mixture components.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is sound and promising, but the paper needs corrections to complex-valued notation, stronger empirical analysis, and clearer treatment of parameterization and scalability to meet the NeurIPS bar.
