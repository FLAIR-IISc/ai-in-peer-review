

# Peer Review: Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The authors leverage Cramér's theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner's theorem) to design a complex-valued matrix of spectral densities, which they transform to the space domain to obtain a valid MOGP covariance function. Unlike previous approaches like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, MOSM explicitly models delays and phase shifts between channels through complex-valued cross-spectral densities. The paper builds on the SM kernel [6] and CSM [5], which were themselves extensions of the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) [3].

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for MOSM, correctly applying Cramér's theorem to ensure positive definiteness of the resulting covariance matrix. The derivation of the kernel from complex-valued spectral densities is mathematically sound and clearly presented.

2. **Physical Interpretability**: A major contribution is the clear spectral interpretation of all parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay), which provides valuable insights for practitioners. This is a significant improvement over LMC and CSM, which lack this interpretability.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The synthetic example (derivative and delayed signals) effectively demonstrates MOSM's ability to model complex dependencies that other methods cannot capture. The real-world experiments on climate data and heavy metal concentration show competitive results, with MOSM significantly outperforming competitors on the Cadmium prediction task.

4. **Clear Differentiation from Related Work**: The paper clearly articulates how MOSM generalizes CSM (which is shown to be a special case of MOSM with constrained parameters), addressing a key limitation in previous work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification for Complexity**: While the paper provides a theoretically sound construction, it doesn't convincingly argue why the complex-valued approach is necessary or superior to other methods. The choice of Q=1 (rank of the decomposition) is not justified, and the authors don't explore whether higher ranks would be beneficial. The paper should clarify whether the complex-valued formulation is truly necessary or if the same flexibility could be achieved with real-valued approaches.

2. **Inconsistent Performance Claims**: The paper claims MOSM outperforms competitors on the heavy metal dataset (Cadmium), but the performance gains on the climate data are marginal and not statistically significant in most cases. The paper should more carefully qualify its claims about performance improvements.

3. **Lack of Computational Analysis**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of MOSM compared to other methods. Training times, memory requirements, and scalability to larger output dimensions are not discussed, which is crucial for practical adoption.

4. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges that performance gains aren't always significant, it doesn't thoroughly explore *why* MOSM works better on some datasets (e.g., heavy metal concentration) but not others (e.g., climate data). A deeper analysis of when the delay and phase parameters are most beneficial would strengthen the paper.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes by providing a theoretically grounded, interpretable kernel that models delays and phase shifts between channels. The MOSM kernel fills an important gap in the existing literature, as previous methods like CSM couldn't model these properties.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides compelling experimental evidence for its claims on specific datasets. However, the theoretical justification for the complex-valued approach is somewhat weak, and the paper overstates the generality of the performance improvements.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in multi-output GPs with a novel solution that has clear theoretical and practical advantages over previous methods. The experimental results are solid, and the paper is well-structured. To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more thorough justification for the complex-valued spectral densities (e.g., theoretical guarantees about expressiveness)
2. Discuss computational complexity and scalability
3. Qualify the performance claims with more precise statements about when MOSM is most beneficial
4. Include a more detailed analysis of why MOSM performs better on certain datasets than others

These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper and make it an even more valuable contribution to the field of Gaussian processes.

The paper's focus on interpretable modeling of complex dependencies across multiple outputs aligns well with NeurIPS' interest in principled machine learning approaches. The MOSM kernel has clear potential for applications in sensor networks, climate modeling, and signal processing where channel dependencies are important.
