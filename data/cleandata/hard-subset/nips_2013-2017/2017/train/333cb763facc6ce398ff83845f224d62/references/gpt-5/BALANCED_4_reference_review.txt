Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Building on the spectral view of kernels, it designs a matrix-valued cross-spectral density S(ω) and uses Cramér’s theorem to obtain valid multi-output stationary kernels via inverse Fourier transform. Concretely, the authors factor S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), where columns of R are complex-valued squared-exponential (SE) functions with output-specific means, variances, delays, and phases. After enforcing the symmetry condition S(−ω) = S(ω)∗ to ensure real-valued processes, the resulting cross-covariances are closed-form “spectral mixtures” with interpretable parameters: frequency means, bandwidths, magnitudes, output-pair-specific delays, and phase shifts. The construction generalizes SM-LMC (Wilson & Adams, 2013; Alvarez & Lawrence, 2008) and the Cross Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel (Ulrich et al., 2015), and is more expressive by allowing output-dependent spectral means, variances, delays, and phases.

Empirically, MOSM is validated on (i) a synthetic 3-output task (base signal, its derivative, and a delayed copy), where MOSM best recovers the known auto- and cross-covariances and extrapolates missing regions; (ii) a climate imputation task with four stations, where MOSM is competitive with CSM/SM-LMC/CONV; and (iii) heavy metals in the Jura dataset, where MOSM significantly outperforms baselines for cadmium and is competitive for copper. The method is implemented in GPflow/TensorFlow and trained by marginal likelihood.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The factorization S = RHR ensures PSD pointwise in ω, and using complex SE functions yields closed-form kernels after enforcing the spectral symmetry required for real GPs. The derivations are plausible and consistent with Bochner/Cramér. One notational issue: eq. (9) appears to omit complex conjugation on Ri when writing Sij(ω); the earlier S = RHR implies Sij = RHi Rj = conj(Ri) Rj. Clarifying this would avoid confusion about Hermitian structure.
- Correctness/subtle points: (i) The claim that “αij ∈ R allows positive/negative correlations” is misleading—αij as defined is nonnegative, while sign changes arise from the cosine factor; please correct. (ii) The imposed symmetry Ssym(ω) = 1/2(S(ω)+S(−ω)) maintains PSD (convex combination of PSD matrices pointwise in ω), but a brief proof or citation would help. (iii) Identifiability: since cos((τ+θ)⊤μ+φ) = cos(τ⊤μ + (μ⊤θ + φ)), φ and μ⊤θ are not separately identifiable; practical implications for optimization and uncertainty should be discussed.
- Experiments: The synthetic study is convincing and highlights capabilities (delay + derivative). Real-data evaluations are reasonable but modest: (a) only MAE is reported—negative log likelihood and calibration would strengthen claims; (b) climate results show small gaps and partial significance claims; please report number of random splits/runs and show dispersion (mean±std) for all; (c) for Jura, using results for CONV from prior work may confound fair comparison; try to re-run under a consistent pipeline. Ablations on the number of components Q, rank vs. mixture trade-offs, and parameter-tying across outputs would be valuable.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written with solid background on Bochner/Cramér and MOGPs. Suggestions:
- Fix the conjugation in eq. (9) and explicitly restate Sij(ω) = conj(Ri(ω)) Rj(ω).
- State the realness condition for multivariate spectra: S(−ω) = S(ω)∗, and show that your symmetrization enforces it while preserving PSD.
- Provide a concise derivation (or appendix reference) for the expressions of Σij, μij, and wij via Gaussian identities.
- Document initialization: spectral mixtures are sensitive to initialization; describe how output-specific μi, Σi, θi, φi are initialized (e.g., from per-output periodograms/cross-periodograms).
- Discuss computational complexity and memory; current method scales cubically in total observations. Even a short note on typical runtimes and prospects for sparse/structured approximations would help.

Originality
The construction is a principled and elegant generalization of the scalar SM kernel to the multi-output setting via Cramér’s theorem with complex-valued factors, enabling interpretable delays and phases. While related to CSM and LMC, MOSM strictly generalizes both by allowing output-specific spectral parameters and explicit delays, and by deriving cross-covariances in closed form from a PSD factorization. To my knowledge, this is novel and incrementally significant relative to prior NIPS/ML work on SM-LMC/CSM.

Significance
The ability to model frequency structure with output-dependent delays/phases is useful for multi-sensor time series and spatial fields with transport or wave phenomena. The synthetic and real-data results suggest practical utility, though stronger empirical evidence (larger-scale datasets, uncertainty metrics, and runtime comparisons) would bolster the case. The interpretability of parameters is a plus.

Pros
- Principled spectral-domain construction via Cramér’s theorem with clear PSD guarantees.
- Interpretable parameters enabling output-specific frequency content, delays, and phases.
- Closed-form cross-covariances; contains SM-LMC and CSM as special cases.
- Strong synthetic demonstration (simultaneous delay and derivative).
- Competitive performance on real datasets.

Cons
- Minor technical clarity issues (conjugation in eq. (9); sign interpretation of αij).
- Limited empirical breadth: small improvements on some real tasks; reliance on MAE only; partial significance analysis; one baseline taken from prior work.
- No discussion of identifiability and optimization challenges (phase vs. delay).
- Scalability and initialization strategies not detailed; no sparse/structured inference.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct eq. (9) and clarify Hermitian structure and realness conditions; add a short appendix proof that the symmetrization preserves PSD and yields real-valued processes (Kay, 1988).
- Discuss identifiability of θ and φ and consider regularization or constraints (e.g., fix φi=0 and absorb into θ, or vice versa) to stabilize optimization.
- Provide initialization details (e.g., estimate per-output μi/Σi from periodograms; initialize θ via cross-correlation lags; initialize φ from cross-spectral phase).
- Add metrics beyond MAE (test NLL, CRPS, calibration plots) and report mean±std over multiple splits for all datasets; avoid relying on external baseline numbers when possible.
- Include ablations: effect of Q and rank; compare to tying μi,Σi across outputs to emulate CSM/SM-LMC; show runtime scaling.
- Consider additional baselines (e.g., ICM/Matérn cross-covariances; separable multi-task GP) to contextualize gains.
- Briefly outline a sparse MOSM (e.g., inter-domain inducing Fourier features, Hensman et al., 2016; Tobar et al., 2015) to address scalability.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a clean, theoretically grounded, and practically useful extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings, with interpretability and expressive cross-structure. While the empirical section could be stronger and some clarifications are needed, the core contribution is solid and of interest to the NeurIPS community.
