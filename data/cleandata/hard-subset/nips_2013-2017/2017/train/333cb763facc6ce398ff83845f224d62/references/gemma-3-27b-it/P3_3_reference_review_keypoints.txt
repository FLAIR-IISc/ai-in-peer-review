- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian Processes using Cramér’s theorem.
- MOSM constructs complex-valued cross-spectral densities to produce flexible, interpretable covariance functions.
- Generalizes existing methods (CSM, LMC) by allowing independent auto-covariance parameters and modeling delays and phase shifts between outputs.
- Provides clear theoretical derivation guaranteeing positive-definiteness and a spectral interpretation of parameters.
- Empirical validation includes a synthetic example and real-world datasets showing MOSM captures relationships other methods miss.
- Positions work appropriately within GP and spectral-kernel literature, referencing Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems.
- Strength: solid mathematical foundation and clear derivation of spectral density and covariance function.
- Strength: greater flexibility and expressiveness via independent auto-covariances and explicit delay/phase modeling.
- Strength: interpretability of parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay) enables domain knowledge incorporation.
- Strength: experiments demonstrate effectiveness and the synthetic example is particularly illustrative.
- Strength: paper is well-written with clear notation and explanations.
- Weakness: likely higher computational cost due to increased number of parameters, not fully discussed.
- Weakness: increased parameter count raises concerns about sensitivity and the need for careful hyperparameter tuning.
- Weakness: limited statistical significance of improvements on real-world datasets; more rigorous testing needed.
- Weakness: initial focus on rank-one (Q=1) decomposition; implications of larger Q on cost and expressiveness underexplored.
- Weakness: brief treatment of connections to existing complex-valued GP methods; could elaborate benefits of spectral-domain formulation.
- For acceptance: MOSM is a significant, well-motivated advance with strong theory, interpretability, and empirical support.
- Against acceptance: computational and parameter-sensitivity issues plus inconclusive statistical gains constrain the claim of superiority.
- Overall assessment: strong paper that merits acceptance with minor revisions to address computational and statistical concerns.
- Suggested revisions: discuss computational complexity and mitigations (e.g., sparse methods), provide hyperparameter guidance, perform more robust statistical testing, expand discussion of complex-valued GP connections, and clarify trade-offs when choosing Q.
