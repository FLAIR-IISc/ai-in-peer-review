- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel that builds PSD cross-spectral density matrices S(ω)=R(ω)* H R(ω) with per-output complex Gaussian factors and yields closed-form, interpretable time-domain cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform.

- Generalizes Wilson & Adams’ SM to multi-output, strictly generalizes CSM and SM-LMC by allowing output-specific spectral means/covariances and explicit delays/phases.

- Works natively in the spectral domain where positive definiteness is easier to enforce and interpret, relating conceptually to LMC/CONV but differing in spectral construction.

- Experiments: synthetic tri-variate (base, derivative, delayed), climate signals with simulated missing data, and Jura heavy metal dataset; MOSM is best on synthetic and Cadmium, competitive otherwise.

- Technical soundness: PSD factorization, symmetry for real-valuedness, and inverse FT to closed-form kernels are standard and valid; PSD is guaranteed across frequencies.

- Notational error: Sij(ω) is written without complex conjugate but should be Sij(ω)=Ri(ω)* Rj(ω); this must be clarified/fixed.

- Derivation clarity: mapping to cross-parameters (Σij, μij, wij, θij, φij) is stated and interpretable, but a short derivation or appendix is needed to aid reproducibility.

- Evaluation: synthetic experiment well designed and shows MOSM’s strengths; real-data results are competitive but not dominant.

- Statistical testing: choice of Kolmogorov–Smirnov for MAE differences is unusual; paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank would be more standard.

- Optimization and scalability: paper omits discussion of O(N^3) scaling, initialization sensitivity for SM-style kernels, number of restarts, and practical runtime; addressing these would strengthen empirical claims.

- Identifiability: only relative phase/delay (θij, φij) matter, so gauge freedoms exist and can affect optimization; this should be discussed or mitigated.

- Originality: natural but nontrivial extension of spectral mixture ideas to multi-output with novel parameterization that yields interpretable delays and phases.

- Conceptual significance: provides a flexible, interpretable multi-output kernel able to capture delays and out-of-phase relations that LMC/CONV struggle with.

- Empirical significance: clear advantage on targeted synthetic task and one real task, but broader benchmarks and ablations (e.g., without delay/phase) are needed to validate practical impact.

- Strength: sound spectral construction with guaranteed PSD and closed-form kernels.

- Strength: strictly generalizes existing CSM/SM-LMC approaches while providing interpretable frequency, bandwidth, amplitude, delay, and phase parameters.

- Strength: demonstrates recovery of derivative and delay structure in synthetic data and competitive real-data performance.

- Weakness: notation/technical clarity issues (missing conjugate, omitted derivations).

- Weakness: limited discussion of optimization, initialization sensitivity, and parameter non-identifiability.

- Weakness: scalability and experiments limited to modest 1D datasets; sparse/large-scale extensions not evaluated.

- Weakness: unusual statistical test choice and limited experimental runs; one baseline result taken from prior work may hamper fair comparison.

- Questions for authors: confirm conjugation in Sij(ω), describe initialization and sensitivity of μ, Σ, φ, θ and Q, suggest mitigation for non-identifiability (e.g., fix one channel’s phase/delay), provide computational cost comparisons and convergence behavior versus CSM/SM-LMC, and include ablations turning off delay/phase.

- Minor requests: clarify whether Σij in Eq. (10) matches spectral Gaussian notation, use appropriate paired tests for MAE, and note extensions to multidimensional inputs with anisotropic delays.

- Overall assessment: solid, meaningful contribution with clear interpretability and targeted empirical gains; accept with minor revisions to fix conjugation and expand training/initialization/runtime details — recommendation: Weak accept.
