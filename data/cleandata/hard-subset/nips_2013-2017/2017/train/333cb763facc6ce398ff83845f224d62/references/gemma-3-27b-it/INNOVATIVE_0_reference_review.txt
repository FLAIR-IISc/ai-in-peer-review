This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output Gaussian process setting. The core idea – leveraging Cramér’s theorem to construct a complex-valued kernel in the Fourier domain – is clever and represents a distinct departure from existing approaches like LMC and CSM. The authors correctly identify a key limitation of previous methods – their lack of flexibility in modeling cross-covariances and the forced similarity of auto-covariances – and address this with a framework allowing for channel-specific parameters, delays, and phase shifts. This is a bold approach that attempts to provide a more general and interpretable model for multi-output dependencies.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The use of Cramér’s theorem to construct multi-output kernels directly from the spectral domain is a genuinely novel contribution. While spectral methods are known in GP literature, extending this rigorously to the multivariate case with complex-valued kernels and explicit phase/delay parameters is a significant step.
*   **Interpretability:** The parameters of the MOSM kernel are clearly linked to spectral properties (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay), which provides a strong interpretative advantage over black-box kernel methods.
*   **Generality:** The model can represent a wide range of covariance structures, encompassing previous approaches (like CSM) as special cases and introducing new flexibility.
*   **Synthetic Validation:** The illustrative synthetic example convincingly demonstrates the model’s ability to recover known relationships (derivative, delayed signals), showcasing its expressive power.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, with a good background section and clear explanations of the mathematical concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Advantage:** While the results are competitive, the performance gain over existing methods on the real-world datasets is not overwhelming. The improvement on the Jura dataset is statistically significant, but the lack of clear advantage on the climate dataset is a concern.
*   **Computational Complexity:** Although not explicitly discussed, working in the Fourier domain and potentially dealing with complex-valued kernels could introduce significant computational overhead, especially for large datasets. This is not a fatal flaw but warrants further discussion.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The number of parameters in the MOSM kernel is substantial. While the interpretability is a benefit, it also raises the question of whether careful parameter tuning is critical for achieving good performance, potentially making the model less accessible to non-experts.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive. While CSM is adequately addressed, further comparison to other multi-output GP models beyond those directly tested could strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The core idea is highly original and has the potential to significantly advance the field of multi-output GP modeling.
*   The mathematical framework is sound and provides a principled way to design flexible and interpretable kernels.
*   The synthetic validation provides strong evidence for the model’s correctness and ability to capture complex relationships.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical gains over existing methods are modest, raising questions about the practical impact of the proposed approach.
*   The computational complexity and parameter sensitivity could limit the scalability and usability of the model.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a compelling and novel contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. While the empirical results are not dramatically superior to existing methods, the originality, theoretical soundness, and interpretability of the MOSM kernel are significant strengths. The authors successfully extend the powerful spectral mixture concept to the multivariate setting in a rigorous and insightful way. I believe the potential impact of this work outweighs the limitations, and I **recommend acceptance** with the caveat that the authors should address the computational complexity and parameter sensitivity concerns in the final version. I encourage future work exploring scalable implementations and methods for automatic parameter tuning.
