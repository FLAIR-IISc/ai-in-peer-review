### Summary

This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel and expressive covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key challenge in designing MOGP kernels is to flexibly model the auto- and cross-covariances between different output channels while ensuring the resulting kernel matrix remains positive definite. The authors propose a principled approach by working in the spectral domain. Building upon Cramér's theorem, the multivariate generalization of Bochner's theorem, they design a valid matrix-valued cross-spectral density `S(ω)` and then obtain the covariance kernel via the inverse Fourier transform.

The core technical contribution is the parameterization of `S(ω)` as a sum of rank-one components, where each component is formed by the outer product of complex-valued vectors (`S(ω) = R(ω)H * R(ω)`). This construction elegantly guarantees that `S(ω)` is Hermitian positive definite. By modeling the elements of `R(ω)` as complex-valued squared exponential functions, the authors derive a closed-form expression for the MOSM kernel. This resulting kernel is a generalization of the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel and the recently proposed Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. Notably, it can capture complex inter-channel dependencies, including channel-specific spectral properties, time delays, and phase shifts, with interpretable parameters. The model's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic task involving derivatives and delays, where it significantly outperforms baselines, and on two real-world datasets, where it achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is theoretically sound, resting on the well-established foundation of Cramér's theorem for stationary multivariate processes. The parameterization of the cross-spectral density matrix via a low-rank decomposition is a standard and effective technique for ensuring positive definiteness, and its application here is both clever and appropriate. The derivation of the final kernel form from this spectral density appears correct.

The claims are well-supported by the experiments. The synthetic experiment is particularly strong; it is specifically designed to create a scenario (learning relationships between a function, its derivative, and a delayed version) where simpler MOGP models like LMC-based approaches are known to fail. The success of MOSM in this setting provides compelling evidence for its superior expressiveness. The real-world experiments are thorough and benchmarked against relevant state-of-the-art methods. The authors are honest about the results, noting where performance gains are not statistically significant and offering plausible explanations for these outcomes. The work is presented as a complete contribution, from theory and model derivation to implementation and empirical validation.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors provide the necessary background on scalar SM kernels and MOGPs before introducing their method, making the paper accessible. The progression from Bochner's theorem to Cramér's theorem provides a clear motivation for the spectral design approach. The derivation of the MOSM kernel in Section 3.1 is detailed and easy to follow. The figures are illustrative, especially Figure 1, which clearly visualizes the effect of the delay and phase parameters on the cross-covariance function. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

A minor suggestion for improvement: In Section 3.2, it is stated that the NLL is minimized with respect to Θ, which contains the parameters `{w, μ, Σ, θ, φ}` for each channel `i` and component `q`. This is the correct set of parameters to optimize. It might be helpful to explicitly reiterate here that these are the parameters of the factor functions `R_i(ω)` from the decomposition, and that optimizing these parameters implicitly defines the valid cross-covariance parameters (e.g., `α_ij`, `μ_ij`) and guarantees the positive definiteness of the full MOGP kernel. This is already implied but making it explicit could further enhance clarity.

### Originality

The work is highly original. While spectral methods for GPs and MOGPs exist (e.g., Wilson & Adams, 2013; Ulrich et al., 2015), this paper is the first to propose a fully general spectral mixture kernel for the multi-output case based directly on Cramér's theorem. Previous work, such as CSM, extended the SM kernel using an LMC-like framework and then added a phase parameter. In contrast, this paper's approach of designing the entire cross-spectral density matrix from first principles is more fundamental and results in a more general and flexible model. The MOSM kernel subsumes CSM as a special case where certain parameters are shared across channels. The clear connection to Cramér's theorem and the novel parameterization represent a significant and novel contribution to the literature on kernels for MOGPs. Related work is adequately cited and distinguished from the proposed contribution.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be impactful. MOGPs are a crucial tool for many applications in science and engineering, from geostatistics to neuroscience and sensor networks. The development of expressive, interpretable, and principled kernels is a key research direction in this field. The proposed MOSM kernel represents a substantial step forward. Its ability to automatically discover and model complex relationships such as time lags and phase differences between signals is a powerful feature that is highly relevant for time-series and spatio-temporal modeling.

Practitioners who currently use MOGPs are likely to find this new kernel useful, as it broadens the class of dependency structures that can be modeled effectively. Researchers are likely to build upon this work, for example, by developing sparse approximations for MOSM (as noted by the authors), exploring non-stationary extensions, or investigating Bayesian non-parametric approaches to select the number of spectral components. The paper clearly advances the state of the art and addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The paper provides an elegant and theoretically sound generalization of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting based on Cramér's theorem.
*   **High Expressiveness and Interpretability:** The MOSM kernel is more flexible than prior work, capable of modeling channel-specific spectral content, delays, and phase shifts, with parameters that have a clear spectral interpretation.
*   **Convincing Empirical Evidence:** The synthetic experiment masterfully highlights the unique advantages of the proposed model in a setting where existing methods are known to be deficient.
*   **Strong Performance:** The model demonstrates competitive or superior performance against relevant baselines on real-world data, establishing its practical utility.
*   **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is well-written, making a sophisticated technical contribution easy to understand.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   **Optimization Challenges:** The paper does not discuss the potential challenges of optimizing the large number of hyperparameters, which scales with the number of outputs and spectral components. The optimization surface is likely highly multi-modal, and performance may be sensitive to initialization. A brief discussion of this practical aspect would be beneficial.
*   **Mixed Real-World Results:** While strong, the performance on real-world datasets is not uniformly dominant over the simpler CSM model. This is not a major weakness, as the authors provide good reasons, but it indicates that the additional flexibility of MOSM is not always necessary.

### Suggestions for Improvement

*   Consider adding a short paragraph in the discussion or an appendix about the practicalities of optimization, such as initialization strategies or the computational cost as a function of the number of outputs (`m`) and components (`Q`).
*   Briefly mention how the number of components `Q` was selected or could be selected in practice (e.g., model selection via cross-validation).

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of Gaussian processes. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, and I strongly recommend acceptance.
