

# Peer Review: Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes

## Summary
This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel [6] to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The authors leverage Cramér's theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner's theorem) to construct a complex-valued matrix of spectral densities that ensures positive definiteness while providing interpretable parameters for modeling delays, phase shifts, and cross-channel relationships. This generalizes the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5], which could not model delays or phase differences across channels. The paper demonstrates MOSM's effectiveness on synthetic data (learning derivatives and delayed signals) and two real-world datasets (climate signals and heavy metal concentrations), showing competitive performance against existing MOGP approaches.

## Strengths

**Technical Quality:** The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution by extending the SM kernel framework to MOGPs using complex-valued spectral densities. The derivation is mathematically rigorous, with clear connections to Bochner's and Cramér's theorems. The proposed MOSM kernel is well-motivated and provides a clear interpretation of all parameters in spectral terms (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay). The experimental design is thorough, with a synthetic example that demonstrates MOSM's unique ability to model derivatives and delays—capabilities that latent process-based models (LMC, CONV) cannot capture.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The theoretical background (Sections 2.1-2.2) provides an excellent foundation for readers unfamiliar with spectral approaches to GPs. The derivation of the MOSM kernel (Section 3.1) is logically presented with clear notation. Figures 1-3 effectively illustrate key concepts, and the experimental results are presented with appropriate metrics (MAE) and statistical validation (Kolmogorov-Smirnov test).

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the MOGP literature by addressing a significant limitation of existing approaches. While CSM [5] introduced phase differences, it could not model delays, and the authors correctly identify this as a key gap. The complex-valued spectral density approach is novel and directly enables the modeling of both phase shifts and delays, which is not possible with real-valued cross-spectral densities. The paper properly situates its contribution relative to prior work (Section 3.3).

**Significance:** The ability to model delays and phase shifts is practically significant for many applications (e.g., sensor networks, time series analysis with lagged relationships). The MOSM kernel provides a more expressive covariance function for MOGPs while maintaining positive definiteness—a critical requirement often overlooked in previous approaches. The experimental results on heavy metal concentration data (Table 3) show statistically significant improvements for Cadmium prediction, demonstrating real-world applicability.

## Weaknesses

**Marginal Performance Gains:** While MOSM outperforms existing methods on the Cadmium prediction task (Table 3), the performance gains on the climate data (Table 2) are modest and statistically insignificant for most sensors. This suggests MOSM may not be universally superior to simpler models like CSM, raising questions about when MOSM should be preferred. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of scenarios where MOSM's unique capabilities (delays, phase shifts) provide tangible advantages.

**Lack of Computational Analysis:** The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of MOSM compared to existing MOGP methods. Given that the kernel involves complex arithmetic and multiple spectral components, a discussion of training and inference time would be valuable for practitioners.

**Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, it lacks analysis of how the number of spectral components (Q) affects model expressiveness and generalization. A discussion of the bias-variance tradeoff with respect to Q would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the MOGP literature by providing a more expressive kernel that can model delays and phase shifts—capabilities missing in prior approaches. The theoretical development is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. While the performance gains on real-world datasets are modest, the paper demonstrates clear advantages in specific synthetic scenarios and provides a theoretically grounded approach that addresses a gap in the literature.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Strong theoretical foundation, clear novelty in modeling delays/phase shifts, comprehensive experimental validation, well-written and organized, significant contribution to MOGP literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Marginal performance gains on some real-world datasets, no computational complexity analysis, limited theoretical analysis of model capacity.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper's theoretical contribution is significant, and the experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage in specific scenarios where delays and phase shifts matter. The modest performance gains on some datasets do not detract from the paper's core contribution, which is the development of a more expressive kernel that can model previously unaccounted-for relationships.

The authors should consider adding a brief discussion of when MOSM is likely to be particularly advantageous (e.g., when delays/phase shifts are present in the data) and addressing the computational complexity of their approach in the revision. This would further strengthen the paper's significance and practical value for the ML community.
