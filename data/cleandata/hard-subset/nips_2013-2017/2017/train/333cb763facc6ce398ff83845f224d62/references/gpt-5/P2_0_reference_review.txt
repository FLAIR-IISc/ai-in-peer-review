Summary
This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). It designs cross-spectral densities in the Fourier domain using Cramér’s theorem: the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) is constructed via a low-rank factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), where each column function Ri(ω) is a complex-valued squared-exponential (SE) with channel-specific mean, covariance, delay, and phase parameters. Enforcing real-valued processes is achieved by symmetrizing the spectrum. The inverse Fourier transform yields closed-form time-domain cross-covariances that (i) allow channel-specific auto-covariances, (ii) capture phase shifts, (iii) capture delays, and (iv) permit non-symmetric cross-covariances. The authors show that CSM and SM-LMC are special cases of MOSM. Experiments include a synthetic 3-output task (recovering a derivative and a delayed signal simultaneously) and two real-world datasets (climate sensors and heavy metal concentrations), with MOSM competitive or superior in MAE, and statistically significant gains in some cases.

Relation to prior work
- Extends spectral mixture kernels (Wilson & Adams, 2013) to the multi-output setting using the multivariate spectral view (Cramér/Yaglom). In contrast to SM-LMC and CONV (Álvarez & Lawrence, 2008), MOSM directly designs cross-spectra and does not force shared auto-covariance structure across outputs.
- Generalizes CSM (Ulrich et al., 2015): MOSM reduces to CSM when per-channel spectral means/covariances/delays are tied; MOSM adds per-output parameterization and an explicit delay term.
- Related to multivariate Matérn cross-covariances (Gneiting et al., 2010), but MOSM focuses on spectral mixtures, interpretability, and expressive non-separable cross-covariances with phase/delay.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria

Quality
- The construction is principled: positive semidefiniteness follows from the spectral factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), and closed-form k(τ) follows from the Fourier transform of complex SEs. Summing Q components preserves PSD.
- Real-valuedness is enforced by symmetry in ω. However, some notational/technical details need tightening:
  - Hermitian condition: the text states Sij(ω) = Sji(ω), but for complex-valued cross-spectra the standard requirement is Sij(ω) = Sji(ω)̄. Please clarify and correct.
  - When making S(ω) symmetric for real-valued processes, the necessary conjugate symmetry is Sij(−ω) = Sji(ω)̄. The paper averages Sij(ω) and Sij(−ω) without explicit conjugation. Explain why PSD and real-valuedness are preserved under the proposed symmetrization (or adjust the construction to the usual conjugate symmetry).
  - The derivation of pairwise parameters (Σij, μij, wij, θij, φij) from per-channel parameters is useful and interpretable; a brief derivation (or pointer to an appendix) would improve rigor.
- Experiments are reasonable but limited in scope (1D inputs, small m). The synthetic task is compelling: MOSM uniquely captures derivative and delay simultaneously, where LMC-based models struggle. Real-data improvements are modest; nonetheless, statistical tests are provided in some cases. Using only MAE (no predictive log-likelihood) underutilizes the GP’s probabilistic nature.

Clarity
- Overall well-organized and readable, with a clear story from theory to practice. The spectral interpretation of parameters is a strong point.
- Notational issues (Hermitian vs. conjugate symmetry) should be fixed. A brief discussion of optimization/initialization and complexity would help practitioners reproduce results.
- Suggest adding a short algorithmic box or pseudo-code for training with the channel-wise parameterization and how cross-pair parameters are derived on the fly.

Originality
- The use of complex-valued SE factors per output within a Cramér-theorem-based factorization to yield closed-form cross-covariances with both phase and delay is a meaningful extension over CSM and SM-LMC. The generalization to per-output parameters (μ, Σ, θ, φ) is the key advance.
- While Fourier-domain MOGP constructions are known, the specific combination—rank-Q factorization with interpretable per-output parameters and explicit phase-and-delay modeling—appears novel.

Significance
- Conceptually important: provides an expressive, interpretable, and principled construction that subsumes prior spectral multi-output kernels and can model realistic inter-channel relationships (delays, phase shifts, asymmetric cross-covariances).
- Practical impact is promising but currently tempered by:
  - Scalability: training remains O(N^3); no sparse or inducing-point variant is implemented (acknowledged as future work).
  - Limited empirical breadth and modest gains on real data; still, the synthetic result demonstrates a capability absent in competing models.
- Likely to be used and extended by the GP community, especially for multi-sensor time series with phase/delay structure.

Pros
- Principled Fourier-domain construction with PSD guaranteed.
- More general than CSM/SM-LMC; interpretable parameters (frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay).
- Closed-form time-domain kernel; ability to model non-symmetric cross-covariances.
- Synthetic task convincingly demonstrates unique modeling capability.
- Statistical testing reported for some real-data comparisons.

Cons
- Notational inaccuracies around Hermitian/conjugate symmetry; real-valuedness argument needs clarification.
- Empirical evaluation limited in scale and variety; improvements on real data are modest.
- No runtime/complexity discussion; no sparse approximation.
- Only MAE reported; no calibration/log-likelihood metrics.
- Initialization and identifiability considerations for many parameters are not discussed.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Clarify the Hermitian and conjugate symmetry conditions and prove that the symmetrized S(ω) remains a valid cross-spectral density for real-valued MOGPs.
- Provide derivations (appendix) for the pairwise parameter formulas and the inverse Fourier transform to Eq. (10)/(11).
- Discuss parameter count, identifiability, and initialization heuristics; report optimization stability.
- Add predictive log-likelihood and calibration metrics; include ablations (e.g., removing delay/phase) to quantify their contribution.
- Provide a complexity analysis and, if possible, a small-scale sparse MOSM prototype using inducing Fourier features.
- Explore additional datasets and higher-dimensional inputs; visualize learned phase/delay on real data.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is technically sound and offers a principled, interpretable, and strictly more general spectral construction for MOGPs. While empirical gains on real data are modest and some theoretical details (conjugate symmetry) need clarification, the contribution is novel and likely to influence subsequent work on expressive multi-output kernels.
