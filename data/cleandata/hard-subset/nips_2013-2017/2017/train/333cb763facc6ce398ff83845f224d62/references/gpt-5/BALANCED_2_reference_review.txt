Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to design complex-valued cross-spectral densities via a low-rank factorization in the frequency domain and then invoke Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s) to obtain a valid, real-valued, stationary multi-output kernel after inverse Fourier transform. Concretely, each output i has its own spectral parameters (mean µi, diagonal covariance Σi), amplitude wi, phase φi, and delay θi. Pairwise cross-spectral terms Sij(ω) then follow from the factorization, yielding closed-form cross-covariances with interpretable parameters: magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, relative phase, and relative delay. Summing over Q rank-1 components gives an expressive family. This generalizes single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernels (Wilson & Adams, ICML’13) to MOGPs in a principled way and subsumes SM-LMC and CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS’15) as special cases while enabling per-output spectral parameters and explicit relative delays. Empirically, MOSM is evaluated on (i) a synthetic three-output setting (reference, derivative, delayed signal), (ii) temperature data from four climate stations (missing-half imputation), and (iii) the Jura heavy-metal dataset. MOSM is competitive overall and notably stronger in the synthetic task and on Cadmium in Jura.

Quality
Strengths:
- The construction is grounded in Cramér’s theorem, with a clear PSD factorization argument to ensure positive semidefiniteness. The rank-Q decomposition is natural and interpretable.
- The resulting kernel has a closed form, retains the SM family’s flexibility, and adds practical modeling capacity (delays + phases + differing auto-spectra across outputs) that LMC/CSM cannot fully capture.
- The synthetic experiment convincingly demonstrates MOSM’s ability to model derivative and delayed relations simultaneously, which are known to induce asymmetric/odd cross-covariances.

Weaknesses / points needing clarification:
- Hermitian PSD details: In Section 3.1, S(ω) = R(ω)^H R(ω) implies Sij(ω) = R_i(ω)* R_j(ω). The text/equations (e.g., eqs. (8)-(10)) appear to omit the complex conjugate at times, and the symmetrization step uses Sij(ω) → 1/2(Sij(ω) + Sij(−ω)) rather than the usual conjugate symmetry S(ω) = S(−ω)* required for real processes. Please clarify the exact conjugation and symmetry used, and prove that the final S(ω) remains Hermitian PSD for all ω.
- Identifiability: Relative delay θij and phase φij are partially confounded (a delay induces a phase shift proportional to frequency). Some discussion of identifiability, constraints (e.g., φ in (−π, π]), and practical regularization would be valuable.
- Parameterization and overfitting: Each output and component has many parameters (wi, µi, Σi, θi, φi). This is powerful but risks overfitting. There is little guidance on initialization (critical for SM kernels), priors/regularization, or parameter tying when signals are similar.
- Scalability: Training follows O(N^3) GP inference on all observations across outputs. No runtime or memory comparisons are reported; no sparse/structured approximation is used. Given the motivation for real applications, a scalability discussion or a small runtime table would help.
- Evaluation breadth: Real-data improvements are modest and mixed; metrics are limited to MAE. Please add log-marginal likelihood (or test NLL), calibration (e.g., coverage), and runtime. An ablation isolating delay/phase benefits (e.g., MOSM with θ=0 or φ=0) would demonstrate where the added flexibility matters. A comparison to multivariate Matérn (Gneiting et al., 2010) would situate MOSM against a strong classical baseline.
- Statistical testing: Using a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test on MAE samples across runs without detailing sample sizes and independence assumptions is questionable. Consider paired tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank) with proper reporting of effect sizes and confidence intervals.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, with useful background. The interpretability of parameters is clear.
- Specific notational issues (complex conjugation in Sij, the real-valuedness condition, the definition of αij and its sign) should be corrected for precision. It would help to explicitly show the Fourier pair leading to eq. (10) with all constants and conjugations, and to state conditions ensuring kij(τ) ∈ R and kij(τ) = kji(−τ).
- Please describe initialization, constraints (bounds on Σ, θ, φ), optimization details (learning rates, early stopping), and the selection of Q.

Originality
- The paper offers a principled and more general spectral-domain construction for MOGP kernels than SM-LMC/CSM, enabling per-output spectra and explicit relative delays. While closely related to prior spectral methods for MOGPs, the particular complex SE factorization with interpretable delay and phase is a novel and meaningful extension.

Significance
- The kernel is flexible, interpretable, and likely to be adopted by practitioners working with multi-sensor and multi-task time series where delays/phases matter (e.g., audio, neuroscience, climate).
- Current empirical gains on real data are modest; stronger evidence on larger, more diverse datasets or a compelling application (with learned delays/phases analyzed) would enhance impact. Providing an open-source implementation and guidance on initialization would further increase practical significance.

Pros
- Solid theoretical construction via Cramér’s theorem and PSD factorization.
- Interpretable parameters (per-output spectra, delay, phase); subsumes SM-LMC/CSM.
- Compelling synthetic result demonstrating capabilities beyond LMC/CSM/CONV.

Cons
- Some technical ambiguities in the complex/Hermitian PSD derivation and real-valuedness condition.
- Many parameters; limited guidance on initialization/regularization and scalability.
- Modest real-data improvements; limited metrics and ablations; missing multivariate Matérn baseline.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify complex conjugation, Hermitian PSD, and the exact symmetrization needed for real-valued processes; provide a short proof or appendix.
- Discuss identifiability of θ vs φ and any constraints/priors you use; provide ablations fixing θ or φ.
- Add test NLL, calibration metrics, and runtime/memory; include a multivariate Matérn baseline.
- Report initialization strategy (e.g., per-output spectral estimates), parameter constraints, and guidance for selecting Q.
- Consider a more challenging multi-sensor dataset where learned delays/phases are interpretable and validated externally.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is principled and extends spectral MOGP kernels in a useful, interpretable way. With clarified derivations and stronger empirical evidence (metrics, baselines, and ablations), this could be a solid addition to the NIPS literature on MOGPs.
