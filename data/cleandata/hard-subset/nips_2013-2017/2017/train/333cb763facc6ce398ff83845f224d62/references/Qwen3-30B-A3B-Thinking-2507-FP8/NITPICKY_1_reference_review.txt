

# Review: Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes

This paper proposes a "Multi-Output Spectral Mixture kernel" (MOSM) as an extension of the Spectral Mixture kernel to multi-output Gaussian processes. The authors build on Cramér's theorem to construct a complex-valued spectral density matrix that ensures positive definiteness, claiming to provide more flexibility than previous approaches like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The paper includes synthetic and real-world experiments comparing MOSM to several baseline methods.

## Major Technical Flaws

The most serious issue is a fundamental mathematical error that appears repeatedly throughout the paper: the square exponential terms consistently omit the factor of 1/2 in the exponent. For example, in equation (8), the authors write "exp(-1 (ω-µi)>Σi^{-1}(ω-µi))" which should be "exp(-1/2 (ω-µi)>Σi^{-1}(ω-µi))". This error appears in equations (9), (10), and (11) as well, where they write "-1" instead of "-1/2" in the exponents. This is not a typo but a consistent mathematical error that fundamentally changes the kernel definition. The Spectral Mixture kernel in [6] correctly includes the 1/2 factor, and the omission in this paper would make the proposed kernel mathematically incorrect and would significantly impact its behavior.

## Other Significant Issues

1. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses Q for the rank of the decomposition but later uses q as the index for the spectral components (e.g., equation 11: "qth component"), creating confusion.

2. **Missing Technical Details**: The paper claims to model "delayed signals" but fails to properly explain how the derivative signal f'(x) is generated from the reference signal f(x). This is critical for the synthetic experiment.

3. **Contradictory Claims**: In the related work section, the authors claim that SM-LMC is a special case of MOSM with "µi = µj, Σi = Σj, θi = θj", but earlier they state that the CSM kernel is defined with "µi = µj, Σi = Σj, θi = θj ∀i, j ∈ {1, . . . ,m} and φi = µ>i ψi". These are contradictory statements.

4. **Incomplete Citations**: Several references have incomplete or incorrectly formatted citations (e.g., "Gaussian Processes for Machine Learning, C.E. Rasmussen, C.K.I. Williams, , , " which should include the publication year and publisher).

5. **Data Description Inconsistency**: In the climate data section, the paper states "N = 1000 samples for training" but earlier describes using "N1 = 500 samples from the reference function" and "N2 = 400 samples from the derivative signal", totaling 1400 samples, not 1000.

6. **Missing Figure**: The paper references "Fig. 2" showing synthetic results but does not include the figure in the provided text.

7. **Lack of Parameter Interpretation**: The paper claims "clear interpretation of all the parameters in spectral terms" but fails to explicitly state what each parameter represents in terms of spectral properties.

## Strengths

The paper identifies a genuine challenge in multi-output GP kernel design and attempts to address it through spectral methods. The theoretical approach using Cramér's theorem is conceptually sound, and the experimental results on synthetic data are promising (despite the mathematical errors).

## Weaknesses

The most significant weakness is the mathematical error that fundamentally undermines the proposed kernel. Without the 1/2 factor in the exponents, the kernel does not match the Spectral Mixture kernel from [6] and would not have the intended properties. This error appears in every equation involving the square exponential term, making the entire theoretical development questionable.

The paper also has multiple minor but persistent formatting inconsistencies, incomplete citations, and unexplained technical details that would make it difficult for other researchers to reproduce the work.

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses an interesting problem in multi-output GPs and has promising experimental results, the fundamental mathematical error in the kernel definition is too severe to overlook. This error would invalidate the proposed kernel's behavior and would likely cause the experimental results to be misleading. The paper should be rejected due to these technical flaws, as they represent a failure in the core contribution of the work.

The authors should correct this fundamental error before considering publication, as it renders the main contribution mathematically incorrect. The other minor issues (inconsistent notation, missing details, formatting problems) are secondary to this critical error. The paper's significance is undermined by this basic mistake in the kernel definition.
