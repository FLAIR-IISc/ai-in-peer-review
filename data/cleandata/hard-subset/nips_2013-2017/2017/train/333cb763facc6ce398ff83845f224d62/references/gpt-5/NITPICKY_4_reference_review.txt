Summary
This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes. The key idea is to construct cross-spectral densities using Cramér’s theorem: model S(ω) = R(ω)^H R(ω), with each per-output factor Ri(ω) a complex-valued squared-exponential term carrying magnitude, frequency, delay, and phase; then symmetrize in frequency to obtain real-valued processes and inverse Fourier transform to get a closed-form kernel. The resulting cross-covariances are mixtures of SE envelopes times cosines with pair-specific delays and phases, while auto-covariances reduce to (real) SM kernels. MOSM subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases (CSM via phase-only differences; SM-LMC with shared parameters), and aims to be more interpretable (frequency, delay, phase) and flexible (non-symmetric cross-covariances). Experiments include a synthetic 3-output example (derivative and delayed channels), climate data imputation for 4 sensors, and the Jura heavy-metal dataset.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant construction grounded in Cramér’s theorem; positivity is guaranteed via S = R^H R and preserved under symmetrization.
- Interpretability: per-output parameters induce pairwise phase and delay differences; auto-covariances are standard SM.
- Closed-form time-domain kernel enabling exact GP inference (modulo cubic complexity).
- The synthetic example convincingly shows the advantage when cross-covariances are not linear combinations of univariate kernels (derivative + delay).
- The relationship to SM-LMC and CSM is discussed; MOSM indeed generalizes them by allowing per-output delay and phase.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and clarity:
- Inconsistent Gaussian exponents. Ri(ω) is defined with exp(−(ω−μ)^T Σ^{-1}(ω−μ)) (missing 1/2), but later the symmetrized density and kernel use 1/2. This propagates to the derived Σij and μij. Please fix and keep a single convention consistent with Theorem 1.
- Derived Gaussian parameters: you state Σij = 2 Σi (Σi + Σj)^{-1} Σj. For the product of Gaussians with the standard −1/2 exponent, the correct covariance is (Σi^{-1} + Σj^{-1})^{-1} = Σi (Σi + Σj)^{-1} Σj (no factor 2). The given factor 2 appears to be a mistake under the usual convention. Similarly, ensure μij is consistent under the same convention (with diagonal Σi these identities commute but the factor matters).
- Hermitian property and complex symmetry are misstated. The paper says S(ω) is Hermitian with Sij(ω) = Sji(ω); for complex-valued spectra one needs Sij(ω) = conj(Sji(ω)) and Sij(−ω) = conj(Sji(ω)) for real processes. Also S(ω) ∈ C^{m×m}, not R^{m×m}. Please correct these statements (Eq. 5–6 and surrounding text).
- Positive-definiteness condition in Eq. (6) uses “z denotes the complex conjugate of z.” The notation is confusing and appears incorrect; the condition should read z^H S(ω) z ≥ 0 for all z ∈ C^m.
- Kronecker delta in the noise term: you write σ^2_{ir,noise} δ_{iris}. This indexing is unclear; usually σ^2_{i_r} δ_{rs} if noise is iid per observation with channel-specific variance.
- Symmetry/validity of cross-covariance: please explicitly state and verify that kij(τ) = kji(−τ), which your parameterization should satisfy via θji = −θij and φji = −φij.
- Identifiability and redundancy: wi and φi are not identifiable simultaneously (negative wi can be absorbed as a π shift in φi). Consider constraining wi ≥ 0 and φi ∈ (−π, π] or similar, and discuss how you handle this in optimization.
- Rank and expressivity: S(ω) has rank ≤ Q. This imposes structure across outputs (additive decomposition across Q components). The paper should discuss the implications of this low-rank cross-spectral structure and how it compares to full coregionalization in terms of flexibility and parameter growth.
- Additive constraints across pairs: θij = θi − θj and φij = φi − φj impose cycle-consistency across triples of outputs. This is beneficial for PSD but restricts arbitrary pairwise specifications. Please discuss this modeling trade-off.

Fourier conventions and constants:
- The Fourier transform conventions and constants are not fully specified. The appearance of αij = wij (2π)^{n/2} |Σij|^{1/2} depends on the chosen convention. Earlier, Eq. (2) uses a normalized Gaussian density; later sections do not. Please define the convention once and ensure all constants align.
- The statement in Theorem 1 that S(ω) is “non-negative bounded” is stronger than needed; Bochner’s theorem involves a finite nonnegative measure. Since you assume integrability, clarify the exact assumptions.

Experiments and evaluation:
- Metrics: only MAE is reported. For GPs, negative log predictive density (NLPD) or calibration metrics are important to assess uncertainty quality.
- Statistical testing: the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test with n=10 runs on MAE distributions is dubious (low power, distributional assumptions not matched). A paired test (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank) on per-trial differences is more appropriate. No multiple-comparison corrections are mentioned.
- Text–table inconsistencies: you state MOSM is best for Sotonmet, but in Table 2 MOSM (0.137±0.007) is worse than CSM (0.129±0.004) in the second column. Please rectify and clearly map columns to sensors.
- Initialization and optimization: SM-type models are sensitive to initialization. No details on how μ, Σ, θ, φ, and w were initialized, constraints (e.g., positivity of Σ diagonals), or optimization settings are provided. This hinders reproducibility.
- Runtime/complexity: no discussion of computational cost or stability (e.g., condition numbers, Cholesky failures), despite the large number of parameters (O(mQn) per group). Reporting timing and convergence behavior would help.
- Ablations: no study on the importance of delay vs phase vs frequency differences, the effect of Q, or comparisons to a tied-parameter MOSM matching CSM capacity.
- The climate experiment uses only N=1000 training points out of 5692 and a single Q (5). It would be useful to test robustness to Q and training set size.

Writing, formatting, and references:
- Numerous typos/grammar: e.g., “multiouput,” “south on England,” “noised was added,” “derivate,” “joint model delays,” “wrt,” “st Conference …” header artifact. Please proofread carefully.
- Notation inconsistencies: use of ι vs i; bold vs non-bold vectors; inconsistent use of 1/2 in exponents; misuse of R vs C for matrix spaces.
- References are incomplete or incorrect (many missing years/venues; [17] is cited as “t-Student cross-spectral densities” but the reference given is Gneiting et al. (2010) on Matérn cross-covariances). Please correct and complete all citations.

Pros for acceptance
- Sound high-level idea with interpretable parameters and a principled PSD-based construction.
- Clear generalization of SM kernels to multi-output with delays and phases.
- Strong synthetic demonstration; competitive real-data performance.

Cons for acceptance
- Several mathematical inconsistencies (exponents, Σij factor, Hermitian and PSD conditions) and unclear Fourier conventions.
- Evaluation limited to MAE with questionable statistical testing and some text–table mismatches.
- Insufficient detail on optimization/initialization and no uncertainty evaluation.
- Numerous presentation and referencing errors.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The core idea is promising and likely impactful, but the accumulation of technical inconsistencies, evaluation limitations, and presentation issues obscure the contribution. With corrected derivations, clarified conventions, stronger and more rigorous experiments (including uncertainty metrics and appropriate statistical tests), and careful proofreading, this work could be a strong submission.
