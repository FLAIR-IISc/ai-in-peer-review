- Overall assessment: Novel, technically sound extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output GPs using Cramér’s theorem; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
- Main idea: Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel by designing complex-valued cross-spectral densities to model multi-output covariance with guaranteed positive definiteness.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on spectral mixture kernels and GP foundations, generalizes CSM/LMC/CONV approaches, and offers more flexible cross-covariance control.
- Theoretical foundation: Uses Cramér’s theorem to ensure positive definiteness of the proposed kernel.
- Interpretability: MOSM parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay) are spectrally interpretable, aiding understanding of learned dependencies.
- Flexibility: Allows independent auto-covariances and explicit modeling of phase shifts and delays across outputs.
- Experimental validation: Includes synthetic recovery experiment and two real-world datasets showing competitive or improved performance.
- Clarity and presentation: Well-written, well-structured, with appropriate mathematical detail and reproducible implementation (TensorFlow/GPflow).
- Implementation details: Code/implementation details provided to enhance reproducibility.
- Computational concerns: Increased flexibility implies more parameters and potential computational/training cost not fully discussed.
- Statistical significance: Real-world result improvements lack sufficiently rigorous statistical significance analysis.
- Parameter tuning: High parameter count; paper lacks detailed guidance on tuning, optimization, and choosing number of spectral components Q.
- Physical interpretation: Could better relate spectral parameters to underlying physical processes in application domains.
- Quality/Clarity/Originality/Significance ratings: Quality—Excellent; Clarity—Excellent; Originality—Good; Significance—Good.
- Arguments for acceptance: Theoretically sound, interpretable, flexible, empirically validated, and clearly presented.
- Arguments against acceptance: Practical challenges from computational complexity and insufficient statistical robustness.
- Suggested revisions: Discuss computational cost and complexity-reduction strategies (e.g., sparse approximations), perform stronger statistical tests, detail parameter tuning/search and optimization, give guidance on selecting Q, and expand discussion linking spectral parameters to application physics.
