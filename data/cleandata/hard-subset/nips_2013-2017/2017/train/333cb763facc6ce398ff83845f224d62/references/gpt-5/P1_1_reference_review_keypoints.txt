- Proposal: Introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes built via cross-spectral design and inverse Fourier transform using Cramér’s theorem.
- Parameterization: Spectral density matrix S(ω) factorized as R(ω)H R(ω) with complex, output-specific Gaussian factors yielding closed-form time-domain cross-covariances.
- Capabilities: MOSM supports distinct per-output auto-covariances, positive/negative cross-correlations, delays, and phase shifts.
- Generalization: Strictly subsumes SM-LMC and CSM by allowing per-output means, scales, delays, and phases.
- Inference: Enables exact GP inference in closed form (O(N^3) complexity) trained via marginal likelihood and gradient-based optimization.
- Experiments: Evaluated on a synthetic 3-output task, a climate sensor-failure imputation, and Jura heavy metals interpolation.
- Empirical results: Synthetic experiment convincingly recovers delay/derivative structure; real-data performance is competitive and sometimes significantly better than baselines.
- Conceptual strength: Elegant factorization ensures positive definiteness and real-valuedness via spectral symmetrization.
- Interpretability: Parameters map directly to frequency, bandwidth, magnitude, delay, and phase across outputs.
- Closed-form advantage: Exact analytic kernels avoid numerical Fourier transforms.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on spectral mixture kernels and extends LMC/convolution constructions; closely related to CSM but adds delays, phases, and decoupled auto-parameters.
- Novelty: Using complex Gaussian spectral factors with per-output parameters to induce delays/phases is a novel extension.
- Clarity issues: Notational inconsistency—Eq. (7) suggests conjugation but Eq. (9) omits it; need explicit proof S(ω) is Hermitian.
- Symmetrization issue: Elementwise symmetrization description is ambiguous—should state matrix-level symmetrization preserving PSD: 1/2(R(ω)H R(ω)+R(−ω)H R(−ω)).
- Identifiability concerns: Many per-output/component parameters create potential redundancy and multimodality; tradeoffs between parameters (θ, φ, μ) need discussion and regularization guidance.
- Initialization missing: Paper lacks details on parameter initialization despite nonconvexity and many hyperparameters; recommend heuristics (per-output SM fits, periodogram-based init).
- Scalability concerns: Exact inference is O(N^3); authors should detail per-iteration cost, dependence on Q and m, runtimes, and relation to CONV/SM-LMC/CSM; sparse extensions suggested for future work.
- Empirical scope: Real-data gains are modest and sometimes not significant; additional datasets with delays/phases (e.g., physiological, seismic) and predictive log-likelihood reporting recommended.
- Rank-Q clarification: Need explicit statement S(ω)=Σq Rq(ω)H Rq(ω) (no cross-terms) and note how parameters are coupled across components for reproducibility.
- Reproducibility: Suggest adding algorithmic description of training, initialization, and figures visualizing learned delays/phases.
- Quality judgment: Core construction is correct and principled; closed-form inverses are strong, but minor technical/notation fixes needed.
- Significance: Kernel design for MOGPs is impactful and likely useful to practitioners, though wider evidence and scalable inference would increase impact.
- Pros summary: Principled PSD-guaranteed construction, interpretable parameters, strict generalization of prior work, closed-form kernels, strong synthetic demo, GPflow implementation.
- Cons summary: Notational/PSD clarity issues, parameter identifiability/initialization challenges, limited empirical breadth and scalability discussion.
- Overall assessment: Solid, well-motivated contribution; reviewer leans toward acceptance pending fixes to Hermitian/real-valuedness details and expanded discussion of initialization and computational aspects.
