Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new family of kernels for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) built in the spectral domain. Leveraging Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s), the authors construct matrix-valued power spectral densities (PSDs) by factorizing S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), where each column Ri(ω) is a complex Gaussian (square-exponential) in frequency with per-output parameters for mean, covariance, delay, and phase. Enforcing real-valued processes via spectral symmetrization yields closed-form time-domain cross-covariances that are mixtures of exp×cos terms with explicit pairwise delay and phase. The resulting Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel generalizes Wilson & Adams’ SM kernel (single-output) and prior multi-output constructions based on LMC and convolution models. Compared to Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernels (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015), MOSM allows per-output frequency means and covariances and introduces explicit delays across outputs in addition to phase shifts, while retaining positive definiteness by construction in the spectral domain.

Empirically, the paper evaluates MOSM on: (1) a synthetic 3-output task (a reference SM GP, its derivative, and a delayed version), and (2) two real datasets: climate sensor temperatures (4 outputs) with simulated missing data, and the Jura geostatistics dataset for predicting Cd and Cu using correlated metals. Baselines include CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM, with MAE as the main metric and a KS test for limited statistical comparisons.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The spectral construction is principled. Using S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) ensures PSD for each ω. Symmetrizing S(ω) with S(−ω) to obtain real-valued processes preserves PSD since convex combinations of PSD matrices remain PSD. The inverse Fourier transform yields a closed-form kernel with interpretable parameters (amplitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay). The derivations appear correct, although some steps (e.g., the precise amplitude term and the PSD preservation under the specific element-wise symmetrization) would benefit from an explicit proof in the appendix.
- Completeness: The method, training via marginal likelihood, and comparisons are presented. However, scalability and complexity are not addressed beyond a brief outlook; no runtime or memory analysis is provided. There is limited discussion of identifiability (e.g., trade-offs between phase φ and delay θ given nonzero frequencies) and initialization, both important in spectral kernels.
- Empirical support: The synthetic task is well chosen, demonstrating capabilities (simultaneous delay and derivative-like cross-covariances) that LMC/CONV cannot express easily. On real data, MOSM is competitive, with improvements in some cases (notably Cadmium) but small gains or parity elsewhere. Statistical testing is limited (KS tests on some comparisons) and NLL or calibration metrics are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized, with a coherent progression from theory to kernel construction and experiments. The spectral-to-time-domain derivations are concise and interpretable. Minor clarity issues:
- Clarify that αij is nonnegative (it comes from a Gaussian integral), and that negative cross-covariances arise via the cosine factor rather than αij itself.
- Make explicit that symmetrization is applied to the full spectral matrix S(ω) (not just element-wise) and that it preserves Hermitian PSD and yields real-valued processes (S(−ω)=S(ω)H condition).
- Provide more detail on parameter constraints (e.g., wi ≥ 0, Σi diagonal vs. full), initialization (e.g., per-channel spectral estimates), and optimization (restarts, regularization).

Originality
Extending SM kernels to multi-output via a complex spectral factorization with per-output parameters and explicit delays is a meaningful advance beyond SM-LMC and CSM. The use of Cramér’s theorem is known, but the particular parameterization Ri(ω) leading to closed-form, interpretable pairwise cross-covariances with both phase and delay is novel to my knowledge. The work is an incremental but nontrivial generalization that improves expressiveness and interpretability.

Significance
The kernel is potentially impactful for multi-sensor time series and spatial-temporal tasks where phase and delays matter. The empirical gains are modest on real data but the synthetic study convincingly showcases capabilities missing in prior models. Lack of a scalable inference scheme limits immediate applicability to large m and N; still, the construction can be combined with sparse inter-domain or Fourier feature methods in future work.

Pros
- Principled spectral-domain construction guaranteeing positive definiteness.
- Expressive, interpretable parameters enabling both phase and delay, with closed-form time-domain kernels.
- Clear generalization of SM and CSM; relates well to prior MOGP literature.
- Synthetic results highlight modeling advantages for nontrivial cross-structure.

Cons
- Limited scalability discussion and no runtime comparisons; method remains cubic in total observations.
- Experimental gains on real data are modest; statistical testing is partial and uses only MAE.
- Potential identifiability and optimization challenges are not analyzed; initialization and priors not discussed.
- Parameter count is larger than baselines; fairness of comparisons could be addressed via ablations matching capacity to CSM.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a formal proof that the chosen symmetrization yields a real-valued MOGP and preserves PSD for all ω (matrix-level argument).
- Discuss identifiability between θ and φ when μ ≠ 0 (e.g., how much adjustment in φ can offset small changes in θ) and any regularization or priors used.
- Detail initialization (e.g., fitting per-output SMs to get μi, Σi, then initializing θi, φi) and optimization heuristics (restarts, constraints).
- Report negative log-likelihood and calibration metrics, not just MAE; include runtime and parameter counts to contextualize capacity vs. performance.
- Add ablations: (i) MOSM restricted to CSM parameterization, (ii) effect of Q and of rank (Q > 1), (iii) using full Σi vs. diagonal.
- Consider larger-scale or higher-m tasks, or include a preliminary sparse variant (e.g., inter-domain inducing variables or variational Fourier features).
- Clarify that αij ≥ 0; rephrase the statement about negative correlations to attribute sign changes to the cosine term.
- Minor: fix small typos and formatting artifacts; ensure all references are complete.

Overall, the paper provides a technically sound, interpretable, and more expressive MOGP kernel with clear theoretical grounding. The contribution is original and potentially useful, though empirical evidence is moderate on real data and scalability remains to be addressed.
