

# Review: Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes

## Summary
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output case using Cramér's theorem. The key idea is to construct a complex-valued spectral density matrix that satisfies positive definiteness requirements, then transform it to the spatial domain via inverse Fourier transform. The MOSM kernel allows for modeling delays, phase differences, and cross-covariances with clear spectral interpretations, offering advantages over existing methods like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel and Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC).

## Strengths
- The paper provides a theoretically sound extension of SM to MOGPs using Cramér's theorem, with clear mathematical derivation.
- The proposed MOSM kernel has clear physical interpretation of parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay), which is valuable for practitioners.
- The experimental validation on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates competitive performance, particularly on the Jura dataset for Cadmium prediction.
- The paper properly addresses the positive definiteness challenge in MOGP kernel design, which is a significant contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses
1. **Technical Inconsistencies in Formulation**: The paper consistently omits the 1/2 factor in the exponent of Gaussian terms (e.g., equations 8, 9, 10). For example, equation (8) should have "exp(-1/2(ω-µi)>Σi⁻¹(ω-µi))" but omits the 1/2. This is a critical error in the mathematical formulation that affects the interpretation of the parameters.

2. **Notation and Terminology Issues**: 
   - The use of "ι" to denote the imaginary unit is unconventional (standard is "i" or "j"), potentially causing confusion.
   - The paper states "we choose Q = 1" (Section 3.1) but later refers to "rank of the decomposition" as Q. When Q=1, the rank can be at most 1, not Q, creating conceptual confusion.

3. **Ambiguous Claims**: 
   - The paper claims "MOSM was the only model that successfully extrapolated the derivative signal and the delayed signal simultaneously" (Section 4.1), but provides no quantitative evidence beyond Figure 2 (which isn't included in the text).
   - The statement "the MOSM can also collapse to models that share parameters across pairs of outputs" (Section 4.2) is vague and lacks explanation of how this parameter sharing occurs.

4. **Formatting and Reference Issues**: 
   - References are inconsistently formatted (e.g., "Gaussian Processes for Machine Learning, C.E. Rasmussen, C.K.I. Williams, , , " has unnecessary commas).
   - The paper references "NIPS 2017" in the header, but NIPS 2017 has already occurred, suggesting a copy-paste error.
   - Equation (10) states "kij(τ) = αij exp (-1 (τ + θij)>Σij(τ + θij))" without the 1/2 factor, which is inconsistent with standard Gaussian form.

5. **Experimental Clarity**: 
   - The synthetic example (Section 4.1) doesn't specify how boundary conditions were handled for the derivative computation (finite differences), which could affect results.
   - The claim that "all models were able to capture the behaviour of the signal in the missing range" (Section 4.2) is vague without quantitative measures of signal similarity.

6. **Related Work Clarification**: The paper claims CSM is a special case of MOSM with "µi = µj, Σi = Σj, θi = θj" but doesn't explicitly show how the CSM parameters map to the MOSM parameters, making it difficult to verify this relationship.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to MOGPs with clear spectral interpretation, the numerous minor technical inconsistencies and ambiguities in the formulation significantly undermine the paper's technical rigor. The omission of the 1/2 factor in multiple critical equations is particularly problematic as it affects the fundamental mathematical foundation of the proposed kernel. The paper's claims about performance advantages lack sufficient quantitative support in the experimental section.

The authors have made a valuable contribution to the MOGP literature, but the technical flaws require substantial correction before the paper can be considered for publication. The current version would benefit from a thorough technical review to ensure all mathematical formulations are precise and consistent.

Given the paper's strong theoretical foundation but numerous technical inconsistencies, I recommend rejection. The minor flaws, while individually small, collectively represent a significant issue with the paper's technical quality that would require substantial revision to address.
