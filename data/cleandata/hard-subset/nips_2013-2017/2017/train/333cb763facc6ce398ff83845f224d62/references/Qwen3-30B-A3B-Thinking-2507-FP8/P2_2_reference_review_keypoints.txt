- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel extending SM kernels to multi-output GPs using complex-valued spectral densities.  
- Builds on Cramér’s theorem to model delays and phase differences across channels, addressing CSM’s inability to capture these.  
- Theoretical construction is rigorous; positive definiteness ensured via a rank-1 factorization approach.  
- Experimental validation includes a synthetic task (learning derivatives and delays) and two real datasets (climate, heavy metals), with KS testing for significance.  
- MOSM uniquely extrapolates derivative and delayed signals in the synthetic example and shows statistically significant improvement (Cadmium) in the heavy metal data.  
- Climate dataset yields only marginal gains over CSM, attributed to high inter-signal correlation.  
- Paper is very well written, with clear derivations, effective figures, and well-structured experiments; minor clarity issue on motivation for rank-1 (Q=1).  
- Original contribution: novel use of complex spectral densities to capture frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay, differentiating MOSM from prior work (SM-LMC, CONV, CSM).  
- Significance: enables modeling of delays and phase relationships important in sensor networks and multi-channel signal processing.  
- Strengths: solid theory, interpretable parameters, comprehensive experiments, appropriate baselines and statistical analysis.  
- Weaknesses: limited discussion of computational complexity/scalability, no guidance on selecting number of spectral components (Q), and modest climate results.  
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, original, well-presented, and practically valuable for multi-output GP modeling.
