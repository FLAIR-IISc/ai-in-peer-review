

# Review of "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the spectral mixture approach for scalar GPs to multi-output settings using Cramér's theorem. The key innovation is modeling cross-spectral densities as complex-valued functions to capture delays and phase differences across channels. The authors demonstrate their approach on synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing against existing MOGP models.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem in multi-output GP modeling. The spectral domain approach is theoretically sound, and the proposed MOSM kernel offers clear physical interpretation of parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay). The experimental results on synthetic data demonstrate the model's ability to capture complex relationships like derivatives and delayed signals that simpler models fail to learn.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical Notational Errors:** The paper contains numerous inconsistent and incorrect mathematical notations, particularly with the factor of 1/2. In equations (2)-(3), (9)-(11), the authors repeatedly write "-1" instead of "-1/2" for the quadratic terms. This is a fundamental error that undermines the mathematical correctness of the proposed kernel. For instance, in equation (3), it should be "exp(-1/2 τ^T Σ_q τ)" not "exp(-1 2 τ^T Σ_q τ)". Such errors appear throughout the paper and suggest insufficient mathematical review.

2. **Technical Inaccuracy in Rank Explanation:** In Section 3.1, the authors state "We refer to Q as the rank of the decomposition, since by choosing Q < m the rank of S(ω) = R^H(ω)R(ω) can be at most Q." This is mathematically incorrect. The rank of S(ω) = R^H(ω)R(ω) is at most min(Q, m), not Q. When Q > m, the rank can still be limited to m. This error in a fundamental theoretical claim is concerning.

3. **Missing Derivation:** The paper claims that "the resulting (symmetric with respect to ω) cross-spectral density... are the following Fourier pairs" (equation 10) but provides no derivation of the inverse Fourier transform. This omission is significant as it's central to the paper's claim of closed-form covariance functions.

4. **Ambiguous Parameter Definitions:** The relationship between the parameters in Section 3.1 and Definition 5 is unclear. For example, it's not specified how w^(q)_ij in Definition 5 relates to the parameters in equation (9). The paper also fails to define what "Q" represents in the general case (beyond the rank-1 case).

5. **Formatting Inconsistencies:** The paper contains multiple typos and formatting issues: "multiouput" (introduction), "st Conference" (title), inconsistent use of "µ" vs "μ" for mean parameters, and inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions (e.g., "−1 2" instead of "−1/2").

6. **Missing Figure References:** The paper references "Fig. 1", "Fig. 2", and "Fig. 3" but none are provided in the text, making it impossible to evaluate the experimental results as presented.

7. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper switches between "Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM)" and "Cross Spectral Mixture" without consistency, and fails to clearly state how the proposed MOSM generalizes CSM beyond the parameter constraints mentioned.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting extension to multi-output GPs with clear physical interpretation of parameters, the accumulation of minor but fundamental mathematical errors, inconsistencies in notation, and unclear derivations significantly undermines the paper's technical credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues too numerous to overlook, especially since they affect the core mathematical contribution of the paper.

The paper's contribution is not entirely novel - it builds directly on the CSM kernel [5] and LMC framework, and the generalization to complex-valued spectral densities is a logical extension. The experimental results are promising but cannot compensate for the mathematical inconsistencies.

The paper would benefit from a complete mathematical revision to correct the notation errors, provide the missing derivation, and clarify the parameter relationships. Without these corrections, the paper fails to meet the technical standards expected for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's technical content is compromised by multiple fundamental mathematical errors and inconsistent notations that would mislead readers and undermine the contribution. While the idea has potential, the current presentation is insufficient for publication.
