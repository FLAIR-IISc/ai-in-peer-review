This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to build a covariance function by designing its power spectral density in the Fourier domain, leveraging Cramér’s theorem. This approach allows for flexible modeling of cross-covariances, including delays and phase differences between channels, with a clear spectral interpretation of the parameters. The paper provides a theoretical foundation, a detailed description of the MOSM kernel, and experimental validation on synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in Gaussian Processes and MOGPs. It correctly positions itself within the existing literature, referencing key papers like Rasmussen & Williams (2006) on GPs, Álvarez et al. (2008) on sparse GPs, and previous work on spectral mixture kernels (Wilson & Adams, 2013; Genton & Kleiber, 2015; Ulrich et al., 2015). The connection to Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems is accurately presented and forms the theoretical basis for the proposed method. The authors acknowledge and compare their work to related approaches like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, highlighting the advantages of MOSM in terms of flexibility and interpretability.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is built on a solid theoretical foundation, leveraging established theorems in spectral analysis (Bochner’s and Cramér’s) to justify the proposed approach.
* **Flexibility & Expressiveness:** The MOSM kernel offers greater flexibility compared to existing MOGP kernels (LMC, CSM) by allowing for independent control over auto- and cross-covariance parameters, including delays and phase shifts.
* **Interpretability:** The spectral interpretation of the kernel parameters is a significant strength. It provides insights into the underlying processes and facilitates model understanding.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The paper includes experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the performance of MOSM in different scenarios. The synthetic example clearly shows the ability to recover known relationships.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it relatively easy to follow the complex mathematical details. The derivation of the kernel and its connection to the spectral density are presented logically.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While not explicitly discussed, the computational cost of evaluating and training MOGPs, especially with a complex kernel like MOSM, can be significant.  The paper doesn't address scalability concerns or potential optimization strategies.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The MOSM kernel has a large number of hyperparameters, potentially making optimization challenging. The paper does not delve into the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings or provide practical guidance on tuning them.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with other methods on the real-world datasets is somewhat limited. While MOSM performs competitively, the statistical significance of the improvements is not consistently established.  Further investigation with more datasets and statistical tests is needed.
* **Lack of Discussion on Complex-Valued GPs:** The paper briefly mentions the connection to complex-valued GPs but doesn't fully explore the implications or benefits of this aspect. Exploring the interpretability or practical advantages of this formulation could strengthen the contribution.
* **Dependence on Gaussian Mixtures:** The performance is tied to the ability of Gaussian mixtures to represent the spectral density accurately. The authors could discuss the limitations of this assumption and potential alternatives.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound method for constructing MOGP kernels with increased flexibility and interpretability.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of MOSM in various settings, including a challenging synthetic example.
* The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Gaussian Processes and provides a useful tool for modeling complex multi-output relationships.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational complexity and hyperparameter tuning challenges of MOSM are not adequately addressed.
* The statistical significance of the improvements over existing methods is not consistently demonstrated.
* The potential benefits of the complex-valued GP formulation are not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of MOGPs. The proposed MOSM kernel offers a compelling combination of flexibility, interpretability, and theoretical foundation. While the computational challenges and the need for further statistical validation are legitimate concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

1. **Address Computational Complexity:** Discuss the computational cost of MOSM and potential strategies for improving scalability (e.g., sparse approximations, parallelization).
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Include a discussion on the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings. Consider providing guidelines for hyperparameter tuning.
3. **Statistical Significance:**  Perform more rigorous statistical tests to assess the significance of the improvements over existing methods.
4. **Explore Complex-Valued GPs:** Expand the discussion on the implications and potential benefits of the complex-valued GP formulation.
5. **Limitations of Gaussian Mixtures:** Briefly discuss the potential limitations of relying on Gaussian mixtures for representing the spectral density.
