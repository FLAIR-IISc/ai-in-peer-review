Summary
This paper proposes a Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for stationary multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The construction works in the spectral domain using Cramér’s theorem: for each output i, a complex Gaussian “square-exponential” factor Ri(ω) with channel-specific amplitude, center, bandwidth, phase φi, and delay θi is defined; the cross-spectrum matrix is then built via a low-rank factorization and symmetrized to ensure a real-valued process. The inverse Fourier transform yields closed-form cross-covariances of the form exp(−0.5(τ+θij)⊤Σij(τ+θij)) cos((τ+θij)⊤μij+φij), enabling inter-channel phase differences and delays. Experiments on a synthetic three-output signal (original, derivative, delayed) and two real datasets (climate sensors, Jura metals) compare MOSM to LMC/SM-LMC, CONV, and CSM; MOSM is competitive and sometimes best.

Relation to prior work
- Extends Wilson & Adams (SM) to the multi-output case via Cramér’s theorem rather than LMC/CONV. Related to CSM (Ulrich et al.), which models phase; authors argue MOSM generalizes CSM by allowing channel-dependent spectral parameters, phase and delay.
- Uses complex cross-spectra as in prior complex GP work (Tobar & Turner).
- Related to multivariate Matérn (Gneiting et al.) but with richer structured cross-spectra.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing spectral-domain construction with interpretable parameters (frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay) per output pair.
- Closed-form stationary cross-covariances including both phase shifts and delays; the shift in τ induced by exp(iθ⊤ω) is elegant.
- Low-rank spectral factorization gives a simple route to positive semidefiniteness (if correctly implemented).
- Demonstrates nontrivial behavior in synthetic example (simultaneous derivative and delay).
- Implementation in GPflow; empirical results are competitive.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Major technical issues
- Hermitian/positivity inconsistencies:
  - The paper states “for any ω, S(ω) = [Sij(ω)] ∈ Rm×m is Hermitian, i.e., Sij(ω) = Sji(ω)” (Sec. 3). For complex cross-spectra, Hermitian means S(ω) = S(ω)H and thus Sij(ω) = Sji(ω)̄, not equality unless entries are real. Also S(ω) is complex-valued, not real-valued as written.
  - Positivity condition (6) is stated with “∑i,j zizj Sij(ω) ≥ 0, where z denotes the complex conjugate of z.” This is confusing/wrong notation; it should be zH S(ω) z = ∑i,j z̄i Sij(ω) zj ≥ 0. Using the same symbol z for a number and its conjugate is an error.
- Factorization mismatch:
  - The proof in (7) correctly shows that S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω) is PSD with Sij(ω) = R:i(ω)H R:j(ω), implying complex conjugation on the left index. However, later the paper states Sij(ω) = Ri(ω) Rj(ω) (Sec. 3.1), dropping conjugation. This breaks Hermitian structure and contradicts (7). The expressions for θij = θi − θj and φij = φi − φj only follow if Sij = R̄i Rj. This needs correction and a consistent derivation.
- Real-valuedness and symmetry:
  - For real-valued multi-output processes, the spectral density must satisfy S(−ω) = S(ω)H (matrix-level condition), implying kij(τ) = kji(−τ). The proposed “symmetrization” Sij(ω) ← 0.5(Sij(ω) + Sij(−ω)) is insufficient to guarantee the required matrix-level constraint unless applied to the whole matrix as S(ω) ← 0.5(S(ω) + S(−ω)H). The current text is ambiguous and likely incorrect.
- Gaussian product constants:
  - The product of Gaussians implies Σij = (Σi−1 + Σj−1)−1 and μij = Σij(Σi−1μi + Σj−1μj). The paper instead states Σij = 2 Σi (Σi + Σj)−1 Σj and other constants; the extraneous factor 2 and certain 1/2 factors around exponents appear inconsistent across (8)–(11). A careful, consistent derivation with the chosen Fourier convention (including (2π)n factors) is needed. As written, dimensions and constants don’t match standard identities, and αij/wij definitions are confusing (e.g., αij is called “magnitude” but later used as possibly negative).
- Cross-covariance symmetry:
  - The paper claims cross-covariances can be “non-symmetric” for φij ≠ 0. For real-valued vector processes, kij(τ) need not be symmetric in τ, but it must satisfy kij(τ) = kji(−τ). Please prove MOSM satisfies this with the final, symmetrized S(ω). The current text does not verify this.

Modeling/identifiability concerns
- Redundant parameterization: Only differences θij = θi − θj and φij = φi − φj matter; there is a gauge invariance (global shift) that is not acknowledged. Similarly, scale ambiguities among wi may exist since cross magnitudes depend on products. Without constraints or priors, optimization may be ill-posed.
- Parameter count/scaling: For m outputs and Q components, MOSM introduces O(mQ(n + n + 1 + 1 + 1)) parameters (μ, diag Σ, w, θ, φ) per channel per component. This is large relative to CSM/SM-LMC. No discussion of computational complexity, regularization, or initialization is provided.
- Noise term: The covariance definition uses “σ2ir,noiseδiris,” which is unclear. Observation noise should add σ2i(r),noise δrs; the notation suggests dependence on both indices and is ambiguous.

Experimental issues
- Fairness and details: The number of components is set to five for all models, but MOSM has many more free parameters per component than SM-LMC/CSM. No parameter counts, CPU time, or convergence diagnostics are reported; no initialization strategy is described (critical for spectral kernels).
- Metrics: Only MAE is reported; no log-marginal likelihood or uncertainty calibration. Confidence intervals in figures are absent/unclear.
- Statistical testing: The KS test on absolute errors from time series violates independence assumptions; multiple comparisons are not corrected; number of random splits not clearly stated (especially for climate). Claims of significance are weak under these conditions.
- Baselines: No comparison to multivariate Matérn (Gneiting et al.) or other cross-spectral constructions beyond CSM; no ablation to quantify the value of per-channel μ/Σ vs. shared ones, or of delay vs. phase.
- Reproducibility: Code/seed/initialization details and hyperparameter priors are missing.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and formatting issues: “multiouput,” “wrt,” broken footnotes (integrability note inline), malformed headers, inconsistent equation punctuation, incomplete references (many missing years/pages), inconsistent bold/transpose notation, misuse of Rm×m for complex matrices, and incorrect Hermitian definition. Equation numbering and 1/2 factors are inconsistent. These hinder reproducibility and raise concerns about mathematical correctness.

Pros
- Conceptually interesting generalization of SM to MOGPs in the spectral domain.
- Interpretability (frequency, phase, delay) per pair of outputs; potentially useful in applications requiring delays.
- Closed-form covariance and straightforward composition over Q components.
- Promising empirical performance on synthetic and some real tasks.

Cons
- Fundamental mathematical inconsistencies (Hermitian condition, conjugation, real-valuedness constraints, Gaussian product constants).
- Ambiguous/incorrect notation and several derivational errors.
- Under-specified training details, lack of complexity/parameter-count analysis, and questionable statistical testing.
- Many presentation and formatting errors; references incomplete.

Questions for the authors
1) Please correct the PSD matrix construction: is S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω)? If so, all formulas should use Sij = R̄i Rj. Provide a rigorous derivation for μij, Σij, wij under your Fourier convention.
2) How do you ensure S(−ω) = S(ω)H and consequently kij(τ) = kji(−τ)?
3) What constraints are used to resolve gauge invariances in θ and φ, and to avoid parameter redundancies in w?
4) Provide parameter counts, wall-clock times, and initialization strategies; how sensitive are results to initialization and Q?
5) Revisit statistical testing using appropriate paired tests, accounting for autocorrelation or using multiple independent splits.

Recommendation
Given the technical inconsistencies in the core construction (conjugation/Hermitian/realness) and the presentation issues, I recommend rejection in the current form. The idea is promising, but the paper needs a substantial rewrite with corrected derivations, proofs, and more rigorous experiments.
