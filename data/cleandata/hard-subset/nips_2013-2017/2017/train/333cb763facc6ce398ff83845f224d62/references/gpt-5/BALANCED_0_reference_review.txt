Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new family of kernels for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. It extends Wilson & Adams’ spectral mixture (SM) kernel for single-output GPs to the multi-output setting by working in the frequency domain. Using Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analog of Bochner’s), the authors construct a complex, Hermitian, positive semidefinite cross-spectral density S(ω) via a low-rank factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), where each column Ri(ω) is a complex-valued Gaussian with parameters that are output-specific. Enforcing spectral symmetry yields real-valued processes; the inverse Fourier transform gives closed-form cross-covariances with interpretable parameters (amplitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay). The resulting kernel generalizes LMC-based spectral approaches (SM-LMC) and the Cross Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel by allowing per-output spectral parameters and, crucially, a linear-in-frequency phase term (θ) that corresponds to time delay—something CSM’s constant phase does not capture.

Experiments include: (i) a three-output synthetic example with a reference signal, its derivative, and a delayed version, demonstrating MOSM’s ability to recover known cross-covariances and extrapolate; (ii) climate time series with missing data imputation across four sensors, where MOSM is competitive with CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM; and (iii) the Jura geostatistics dataset predicting heavy metal concentrations, where MOSM outperforms alternatives on Cadmium and is competitive on Copper. Implementation uses GPflow/TensorFlow; model selection uses MAE and some KS tests for significance.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: A clean, principled construction in the spectral domain using Cramér’s theorem and a PSD factorization. The derivation is technically sound and elegant.
- Interpretability: The time-domain kernel has intuitive parameters per output pair: amplitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase offset, and explicit delay.
- Generality: MOSM subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases (shared parameters; no delay; constant phase), while enabling richer cross-output structure.
- Closed-form covariances: The choice of Gaussian cross-spectra yields tractable, closed-form time-domain kernels and straightforward gradients.
- Synthetic validation: The derivative+delay example is compelling and clearly favors MOSM over latent-process constructions, as the required cross-covariances are not linear combinations of univariate kernels.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope: Real-data evaluations are limited in diversity (1D time series; one spatial dataset) and show modest gains over strong baselines, with significance established only in some cases. No runtime or scalability comparisons.
- Scalability: The approach inherits O(N^3) inference; no sparse or structured approximations are provided (left to future work), which limits practical impact on larger multi-output problems.
- Model selection and initialization: The paper does not discuss how Q (number of components/rank) is chosen, how parameters are initialized, or sensitivity/identifiability issues (e.g., phase–delay trade-offs, component permutations), which are known to be critical for SM kernels.
- Stationarity: MOSM is stationary. Many multi-output applications demand nonstationarity; no extension or discussion is provided.
- Rank limitation: The PSD factorization imposes rank Q at each frequency. While reasonable, the implications of low Q for modeling complex cross-spectra are not explored.
- Statistical testing: The KS test on MAE distributions is unusual and insufficiently described (number of runs, independence, alternative tests), and there is no uncertainty quantification on predictions.

Quality
- The core construction is technically sound. The PSD proof via S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω) is correct; symmetrization ensures real-valuedness; the inverse Fourier transform derivations are consistent (shift in frequency domain ↔ delay in time domain).
- Experiments substantiate the claims qualitatively, especially the synthetic case. However, empirical evidence for clear state-of-the-art improvements on real data is limited.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The spectral–time duality and parameter interpretations are explained clearly.
- Minor issues: a few typos/formatting glitches; incomplete references; the training objective and optimization details could benefit from more guidance (initialization, constraints, regularization).

Originality
- The method provides a meaningful and nontrivial generalization of CSM by introducing a linear-in-frequency phase term (delay) and per-output spectral parameters within a PSD-guaranteed construction. While grounded in known theorems, the specific design and derivations seem novel and useful.

Significance
- For practitioners modeling multi-channel time series where delays and phase relationships matter (e.g., climate, neuroscience, communications), MOSM is an appealing, interpretable tool. That said, without scalability and broader evaluations, the near-term impact may be limited.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you initialize μ, Σ, θ, φ and weights? Any heuristics from periodograms/cross-spectra? How sensitive are results to initialization?
2) How do you choose Q? Did you observe overfitting with larger Q? Any Bayesian treatment (e.g., ARD priors) considered?
3) Can you report runtimes and memory vs. baselines, and the number of optimization restarts?
4) Could CSM be extended with a linear phase term to approximate delays? If so, how would MOSM differ empirically?
5) Any insights on identifiability (e.g., θ vs. φ) and regularization?
6) Can you comment on extending MOSM to nonstationary settings (e.g., input warping, locally stationary mixtures)?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: MOSM without delay (θ=0), without phase (φ=0), or with shared μ/Σ across outputs to quantify contributions.
- Provide sensitivity to Q and initialization; include uncertainty calibration metrics.
- Compare on more diverse datasets (e.g., multichannel physiological/neural signals) where delays are known to be present.
- Report computational costs and consider a sparse/structured variant (e.g., variational Fourier features; inter-domain inducing variables) to bolster practical applicability.
- Clarify the statistical testing protocol and consider alternatives (paired t/Wilcoxon across multiple runs).

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, principled kernel construction with clear novelty (delays + per-pair parameters) and interpretability.
- Subsumes prior SM-based multi-output kernels; demonstrably superior on a carefully designed synthetic task.
- Clean, closed-form expressions; easy to implement with automatic differentiation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical evidence of substantial gains on real datasets; modest performance differences.
- No scalability solution; unclear guidance on model selection and initialization.
- Stationarity restriction may limit applicability; identifiability and robustness not thoroughly explored.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The technical contribution is sound and nontrivially advances spectral MOGP kernels with interpretable delay and phase. While experiments could be stronger and scalability is pending, the idea is elegant and likely to be used and extended by the community.
