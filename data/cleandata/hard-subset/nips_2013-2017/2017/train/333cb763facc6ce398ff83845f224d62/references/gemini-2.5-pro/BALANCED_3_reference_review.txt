This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel and expressive family of covariance functions for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to design the kernel in the spectral domain by leveraging Cramér's theorem, the multivariate generalization of Bochner's theorem. Instead of directly constructing a positive-definite matrix-valued kernel, the authors propose a model for the matrix of cross-spectral densities, S(ω). To ensure the necessary Hermitian positive-definite property of S(ω), they propose a factor decomposition S(ω) = R(ω)<sup>H</sup>R(ω), where the components of R(ω) are modeled as complex-valued squared exponential functions. This principled construction guarantees a valid MOGP kernel and, due to the choice of spectral components, results in a closed-form expression for the kernel in the time/space domain via the inverse Fourier transform.

This work builds directly upon the spectral mixture (SM) kernel for single-output GPs (Wilson & Adams, 2013) and provides a more general and flexible alternative to the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel (Ulrich et al., 2015). While CSM is based on the restrictive Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) framework, forcing certain parameters to be shared across outputs, the proposed MOSM kernel allows each output to have its own characteristic spectral properties (frequencies, lengthscales), from which interpretable cross-channel properties like delays and phase shifts are derived.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** The main strength of the paper lies in its theoretically sound approach. Using Cramér's theorem to move the design problem to the spectral domain and then employing a factor decomposition `S(ω) = R(ω)^H R(ω)` to guarantee positive definiteness is an elegant solution to the core challenge in MOGP kernel design. This approach is more fundamental and flexible than previous methods like LMC or convolution-based models.

2.  **High Expressivity and Interpretability:** The resulting MOSM kernel is highly expressive. It can capture complex inter-channel dependencies, including variable delays (via `θ_ij`), phase shifts (via `φ_ij`), and channel-specific periodicities and lengthscales (via `μ_i`, `Σ_i`). These parameters have clear physical interpretations, which is a significant advantage for applications where understanding the underlying process is as important as prediction.

3.  **Strong Experimental Validation on Synthetic Data:** The experiment involving a signal, its derivative, and a delayed version is very well-conceived. This task creates known, non-trivial cross-covariance structures (e.g., non-symmetric, phase-shifted) that are challenging for existing models. The results in Figure 2 and Table 1 compellingly demonstrate the superior flexibility of MOSM and clearly highlight the limitations of CSM and LMC-based approaches in a setting where their underlying assumptions are violated.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The theoretical background on Bochner's and Cramér's theorems is presented clearly, and the derivation of the MOSM kernel from the spectral density model is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Optimization and Scalability:** The MOSM kernel introduces a large number of hyperparameters: for `m` outputs, `Q` components, and `d` input dimensions, the number of parameters scales as O(`Qmd`). The paper states that the negative log-likelihood is minimized with respect to these parameters but does not discuss the practical challenges of this optimization. High-dimensional, non-convex optimization problems are often plagued by local minima and require careful initialization. A discussion of these practical aspects would significantly strengthen the paper.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should add a discussion on the optimization landscape. Do they have any recommendations for hyperparameter initialization? For instance, one could initialize the frequencies (`μ_q,i`) using periodograms of the individual output series. A comment on the practical scalability of the model with respect to the number of outputs (`m`) and spectral components (`Q`) would also be valuable for practitioners.

2.  **Model Selection:** The number of spectral components, `Q`, is a critical hyperparameter that controls the complexity and flexibility of the model. In the experiments, `Q` is fixed to 5. However, there is no discussion of how to choose `Q` in a data-driven way. This is a crucial step for applying the model in practice.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should briefly discuss potential strategies for selecting `Q`, such as cross-validation or model selection criteria like BIC or AIC. This would make the proposed method a more complete and readily usable tool.

3.  **Mixed Results on Real-World Data:** While the synthetic results are excellent, the performance on the two real-world datasets is less decisive. On both the climate and Jura datasets, MOSM performs competitively but does not consistently or significantly outperform the simpler CSM model. The authors correctly note that this may be because the signals in the climate dataset are highly correlated and similar, a scenario where the extra flexibility of MOSM may not be necessary. However, this does weaken the empirical case for the universal superiority of MOSM.
    *   **Suggestion:** While I do not believe new experiments are essential for acceptance, the discussion could be strengthened. The authors could better characterize the types of real-world problems (e.g., those involving propagation delays in sensor networks, or analyzing signals from spatially distributed arrays) where the unique capabilities of MOSM are most likely to provide a significant advantage over prior art.

**Summary and Decision**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the literature on multi-output Gaussian processes. The proposed MOSM kernel is a novel, principled, and highly expressive tool for modeling complex dependencies between multiple time series or spatial processes. The theoretical formulation is elegant, and the synthetic experiment provides a clear and convincing demonstration of its advantages over existing methods. While the real-world experimental results are not as striking and some practical aspects like optimization and model selection could be further discussed, the overall quality, originality, and significance of the work are high. The MOSM kernel is likely to be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners and will inspire further work in this area. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
