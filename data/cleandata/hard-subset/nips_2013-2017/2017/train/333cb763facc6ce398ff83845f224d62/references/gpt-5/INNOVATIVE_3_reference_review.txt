Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to design the matrix-valued cross-spectral density S(ω) directly in the Fourier domain via a low-rank factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), where each column corresponds to a complex-valued squared-exponential (SE) “feature” per output. Using Cramér’s theorem, these complex Gaussian cross-spectra admit closed-form inverse Fourier transforms, yielding real-valued cross-covariances that jointly capture: (i) different auto-covariances per channel, (ii) inter-output phase shifts, and (iii) explicit delays via linear phase terms. The authors mirror the spectrum to ensure real-valued processes, derive the form of the cross-kernels, and show that their construction strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM: by allowing per-output means, covariances, delays, and phases, MOSM subsumes LMC-style shared parameters and the hand-engineered shifts used in CSM.

Empirically, MOSM is tested on: (1) a synthetic tri-variate example (base SM process, its derivative, and a delayed copy), where the ground-truth cross-covariances are known and MOSM extrapolates both derivative and delay best; (2) a climate sensor network dataset with simulated sensor dropouts, where MOSM performs comparably to CSM and better than SM-LMC/CONV in some cases; and (3) the Jura geostatistics benchmark predicting Cadmium and Copper, where MOSM is best on Cadmium and competitive on Copper.

Quality
- Theory: The construction is principled and leverages Cramér’s theorem appropriately; the low-rank spectral factorization guarantees positive semidefiniteness at each ω. The mirroring step ensures real-valued outputs. The resulting kernel has a clean closed form and interpretable parameters (frequency, bandwidth, magnitude, delay, phase) per output pair.
- Minor technical issues to fix/clarify:
  - Conjugation: From S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), the (i,j) entry should be Sij(ω) = R̄i(ω) Rj(ω) (complex conjugate on the first factor). In Section 3.1 it is written as Sij(ω) = Ri(ω) Rj(ω), which would not, in general, be Hermitian. This likely is a notational oversight—please correct.
  - Sign of αij: With αij = wij (2π)^{n/2} |Σij|^{1/2} and wij > 0 by construction, αij is nonnegative. Anti-correlation is captured by φij ≈ π, not negative αij. The text currently states “αij ∈ R” (possibly negative); please reconcile.
  - Rank constraint: The spectrum has rank at most Q uniformly over ω. This is a modeling choice but could limit expressivity if inter-output dependencies vary strongly across frequency. A short discussion would help.
- Empirics: The synthetic task convincingly demonstrates capabilities (derivative + delay) that LMC-based models struggle with. The real-data results are competitive; MOSM matches or slightly exceeds CSM and SM-LMC/CONV depending on the case. The evaluation is fair but somewhat limited in scale. Practical aspects (initialization, sensitivity to Q, optimization stability, run-time) are not discussed; given the larger parameterization (O(mQ) parameters per spectral component), guidance on priors/regularization and initialization would be valuable. No sparse inference is implemented; the authors acknowledge this as future work.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and mathematically clear. The spectral-to-time-domain mapping and parameter interpretations are a strength.
- Please fix typographical issues (e.g., “multiouput”, “wrt”), equation indexing, and the above technical clarifications. Briefly summarizing the parameter count and constraints per component would aid reproducibility.
- It would help to explicitly state the computational complexity and the number of free parameters as a function of m, Q, and n, and to provide training details (optimizer, iterations, initialization schemes).

Originality
- The paper takes a bold and elegant step beyond SM-LMC and CSM by directly factorizing the cross-spectrum with per-output complex SE features and by explicitly modeling both phase and delay while allowing per-channel spectral parameters. This unifies and generalizes prior approaches under a clean spectral factorization, leading to interpretable cross-covariances with closed-form kernels. While spectral constructions for MOGPs exist (e.g., CSM; real-valued constructions like multivariate Matérn), the proposed parameterization and its combination of delay+phase with per-output spectra is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: provides a flexible, interpretable MOGP kernel family with closed-form expressions, likely to be used by practitioners dealing with delayed or out-of-phase multi-sensor time series.
- Empirically, gains over CSM are modest on real data but clear on the synthetic task where the ground truth violates LMC assumptions. Given the novelty and potential impact, I view the contribution as meaningful even with limited empirical margins.
- A sparse/variational implementation (as suggested) could greatly increase practical impact.

Strengths
- Clear, principled spectral construction with guaranteed PSD and real-valued outputs.
- Simultaneous modeling of per-channel auto-spectra, inter-output delay, and phase.
- Interpretable parameters and closed-form kernels.
- Subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases.
- Strong synthetic demonstration aligned with the motivation.

Weaknesses
- Minor technical inconsistencies (conjugation, sign of αij) to fix.
- Scalability and optimization details are thin; potential over-parameterization and identifiability issues are not discussed.
- Real-world improvements over CSM are modest; more diverse datasets or ablations (e.g., turning off delay/phase) would strengthen the case.
- Uniform low-rank spectral assumption may be restrictive.

Questions and suggestions
- How sensitive is performance to Q? Any heuristic or BIC-style selection?
- How do you initialize µi, Σi, θi, φi? Any priors to avoid degeneracies (e.g., permutation/symmetry, scaling of wi)?
- Can you provide wall-clock comparisons and parameter counts vs. CSM?
- Ablate delay and phase to show their empirical contribution.
- Consider releasing code and initializations to enhance reproducibility.

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept to accept). The paper introduces a novel and elegant spectral construction for MOGPs that unifies and extends prior work and offers practically useful flexibility (delay + phase + per-output spectra) with closed-form kernels. Despite some minor technical fixes and modest empirical margins on real data, the conceptual advance is significant and likely to spur follow-up work (e.g., sparse inference). Confidence: high.
