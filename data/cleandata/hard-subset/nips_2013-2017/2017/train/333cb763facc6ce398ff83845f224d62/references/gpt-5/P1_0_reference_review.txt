Summary
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to design the cross-spectral density matrix directly in the Fourier domain using Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem). Specifically, the authors construct a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω) with per-output complex-valued Gaussian components Ri(ω). This guarantees positive semidefiniteness at each frequency and yields closed-form, real-valued time-domain kernels after symmetrization. The resulting cross-covariances are spectral-mixture functions that can express output-specific frequency content, inter-output phase shifts, and delays. The authors show that SM-LMC and CSM are special cases of MOSM, with MOSM adding delays and per-output spectral parameters. Experiments include a synthetic 3-output task (original, derivative, delayed signal), climate sensor imputation, and the Jura heavy-metal dataset. MOSM is clearly superior on the synthetic task, competitive and sometimes best on the two real datasets.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Wilson & Adams’ Spectral Mixture kernel (ICML’13), generalizes the SM-LMC and CSM kernels (Ulrich et al., NIPS’15) by allowing complex cross-spectra with delays and per-output parameters, and is conceptually related to multivariate covariance construction via LMC and convolution processes (Álvarez & Lawrence). The use of Cramér’s theorem to ensure valid multivariate spectra aligns with recent theory, and the paper correctly contrasts with real-valued cross-spectra (e.g., Matérn multivariate families). The positioning is appropriate and related work is largely cited.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω) ensures PSD for each ω. Symmetrization S(ω) ← (S(ω)+S(−ω))/2 preserves PSD (average of PSD matrices), ensuring real-valued processes. The inverse Fourier transform is handled in closed form, yielding interpretable time-domain parameters (lengthscales, mean frequencies, phase, delay). The kernel symmetry requirement kij(τ) = kji(−τ) appears to be satisfied due to θji = −θij and φji = −φij induced by the factorization; a brief explicit statement/proof would improve completeness.
- Claims vs. results: The synthetic experiment is compelling and demonstrates MOSM’s unique ability to jointly capture derivative and delay cross-structure where latent-process models underperform. On real data, MOSM is competitive; improvements are modest except in one climate case and for Cadmium in Jura.
- Missing details: Optimization/initialization are critical for SM-style models; the paper does not describe initialization (e.g., per-output periodograms/EM) or sensitivity to Q. No runtime or scalability analysis is provided. Identifiability and multimodality (φ vs θ vs μ) are not discussed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear derivation from spectral design to time-domain kernels and a nice parameter interpretation.
- Some notational/typing issues: factors of 1/2 in Gaussian exponents are inconsistently typeset; clarify the sign/constraints on weights (w_i) and whether αij is necessarily nonnegative (it should be, given the construction; negative cross-covariance values arise from the cosine, not from αij<0).
- Suggest adding: (i) a short proof or proposition on kernel symmetry kij(τ) = kji(−τ), (ii) details on initialization and hyperparameter constraints, and (iii) a brief complexity discussion (parameter count, training O(N^3), and how Q, m, n affect cost).

Originality
- The approach is a principled and elegant extension of SM to multi-output via complex spectral factorization with delays, subsuming SM-LMC and CSM. While conceptually close to CSM, the addition of per-output spectral parameters and explicit delays is nontrivial and yields a more expressive family. This is a meaningful methodological contribution.

Significance
- The contribution is likely to be useful to practitioners needing interpretable cross-output structure (phase/delay) and richer cross-covariances than LMC/CSM. The synthetic experiment shows a clear advantage. Real-data gains are modest; stronger empirical evidence (broader datasets, ablations, scalability) would strengthen claims. The method’s interpretability is a plus.

Pros
- Principled construction via Cramér’s theorem; PSD guaranteed.
- Clear interpretability of frequency, lengthscale, phase, and delay; subsumes SM-LMC and CSM.
- Closed-form kernels; no numerical Fourier transforms needed.
- Strong synthetic demonstration of capabilities beyond latent-process baselines.
- Competitive performance on real datasets.

Cons
- Limited discussion of optimization/initialization; SM kernels are known to be sensitive.
- No runtime/scalability analysis; no sparse approximation implemented.
- Real-data improvements are limited; significance established only in some cases.
- Minor clarity issues (typos, sign/normalization ambiguities).
- Potential identifiability/multimodality not addressed.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you initialize μi, Σi, θi, φi, wi per component/output? How sensitive are results to initialization and Q?
2) Can you provide an explicit proof that kij(τ) = kji(−τ) holds under your parameter tying?
3) Do φ and θ trade off in practice (identifiability)? Any constraints/priors to mitigate?
4) What is the computational overhead compared to CSM/SM-LMC for similar Q and m? Any evidence on convergence behavior?
5) Could you show ablations (no delay, no phase) and per-component interpretability on real data?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an initialization strategy (e.g., fit per-output Gaussian mixtures to periodograms; then set delays from cross-spectral phase slopes).
- Include an ablation study (phase-only, delay-only, shared μ/Σ across outputs) and discuss identifiability.
- Provide runtime and scaling comparisons; consider including a sparse/variational version or at least an experiment with larger N.
- Clarify the sign/constraints on wi and αij and fix minor typos.
- Visualize learned cross-spectra/coherence and delays on real data to emphasize interpretability.

Overall assessment
A solid, principled, and interpretable generalization of spectral mixture kernels to MOGPs that subsumes prior SM-LMC/CSM and adds delays. The theory is sound and the synthetic results are compelling; real-data improvements are moderate but competitive. With added clarity on initialization/identifiability and some scalability discussion, this would be a strong contribution.
