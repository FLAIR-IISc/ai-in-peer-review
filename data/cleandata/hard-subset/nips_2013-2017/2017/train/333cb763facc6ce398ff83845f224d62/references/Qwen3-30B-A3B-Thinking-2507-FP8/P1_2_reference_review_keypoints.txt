- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel extending the SM kernel to multi-output GPs via complex-valued cross-spectral densities.  
- Uses Cramér's theorem to derive MOSM and enable modeling of delays and phase differences across channels.  
- MOSM generalizes CSM and provides interpretable spectral parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay).  
- Demonstrated on a synthetic example recovering derivatives and delayed signals without prior knowledge.  
- Evaluated on two real-world datasets (climate signals, Jura heavy metals) and compared to LMC, CONV, and CSM.  
- Shows competitive performance with statistically significant improvement for Cadmium on the Jura dataset; marginal gains elsewhere.  
- Technically sound with rigorous mathematical foundations and clear theoretical justifications.  
- Lacks a formal proof of positive definiteness beyond the factor decomposition argument.  
- Computational complexity relative to baselines is not discussed and is an important omission.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and includes clear mathematical derivations and illustrative figures.  
- Experimental setup is thoroughly described (preprocessing, training, MAE metrics).  
- Would benefit from reporting parameter counts to clarify model complexity versus baselines.  
- Original contribution: first multi-output application of complex-valued spectral densities with interpretable parameters and delay/phase modeling.  
- Significance: provides a more flexible way to model cross-covariances and temporal relationships useful in signal processing and sensor networks.  
- Strengths: solid theory (Cramér’s theorem), novel complex cross-spectra, comprehensive experiments, clear interpretation, good baseline comparisons.  
- Weaknesses: no PD proof, missing complexity analysis, simple synthetic example (3 outputs), marginal real-data improvements in some cases, limited guidance on when to prefer MOSM.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address computational complexity and compare parameter counts.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel extension, clear theory and interpretation, ability to model delays/phase, competitive results, well-written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: marginal improvements over CSM in places, missing computational discussion, simple synthetic test, no formal PD proof.
