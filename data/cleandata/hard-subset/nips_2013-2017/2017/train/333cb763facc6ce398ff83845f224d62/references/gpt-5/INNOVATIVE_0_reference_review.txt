Summary
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a principled and expressive construction of stationary multi-output GP kernels in the Fourier domain. Leveraging Cramér’s theorem, the authors design a complex-valued, Hermitian positive semidefinite cross-spectral density S(ω) via a low-rank factorization S(ω) = R(ω)^H R(ω), where each channel-specific factor Ri(ω) is a complex squared-exponential with its own mean, diagonal covariance, phase, and delay. Symmetrizing the PSD yields real-valued processes, and the inverse Fourier transform gives closed-form cross-covariances that simultaneously capture different auto-covariances per channel, cross-channel phase shifts, and delays. The model generalizes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases, while offering clearer spectral interpretability. Experiments on a synthetic task (derivatives and delays) and two real datasets (climate sensors and Jura heavy metals) show that MOSM is competitive with, and sometimes superior to, SM-LMC, CONV, and CSM.

Relation to prior work: MOSM extends Wilson & Adams’ SM kernel to multi-output through a spectral matrix construction (Cramér), rather than via LMC/CONV latent-process combinations. Compared to CSM (Ulrich et al.), MOSM introduces explicit delays and channel-specific spectral means/covariances, and recovers CSM/SM-LMC under parameter tying. This connects neatly to classic multivariate covariance design (e.g., Gneiting et al.) but via a more expressive spectral route.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and elegance: Using a PSD matrix factorization in the Fourier domain to guarantee positive definiteness, with complex SE factors that encode per-channel frequency content, phase, and delay, is a clean and powerful idea. It unifies and strictly generalizes SM-LMC/CSM.
- Interpretability: Parameters map transparently to spectral magnitude, mean frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay at the pairwise level; auto-covariances reduce to standard SM.
- Closed-form kernels: The choice of Gaussian spectra yields tractable inverse Fourier transforms and thus efficient evaluation/training akin to standard GPs.
- Theoretical grounding: Correct use of Cramér’s theorem; symmetry construction for real-valued outputs; explicit characterization of cross-spectral parameters and their induced time-domain forms.
- Empirical evidence: Clear synthetic demonstration where MOSM uniquely recovers delayed and derivative relationships (non-LMC-type cross-covariances). Competitive or best results on climate and Jura datasets, with statistical testing reported.

Weaknesses
- Clarity on conjugation and signs: In Section 3.1 the factorization suggests Sij(ω) = RH:i(ω)R:j(ω) = conj(Ri(ω))Rj(ω), but some equations appear to drop the conjugation and flip delay/phase signs. Please correct and keep consistent; it matters for Hermitian symmetry and parameter interpretation (i.e., θij should align with conj/phase conventions).
- Parameter growth and identifiability: For m outputs, Q components, and n-dimensional inputs, MOSM introduces mQ(3n + 2) parameters (μ, diagonal Σ, θ, φ, w). This is powerful but raises overfitting/identifiability concerns. The paper would benefit from discussing initialization, regularization, and parameter tying strategies, as well as sensitivity to Q.
- Scalability: Inference is O(N^3). No sparse/structured approximation is provided, and datasets are relatively small. Given the spectral structure, an inducing-variable scheme in the Fourier domain (as suggested in the discussion) seems natural; a preliminary scalable variant would strengthen the work.
- Experimental breadth: Real-data gains are modest; MOSM outperforms clearly on synthetic and one Jura target (Cadmium) but is similar to CSM on climate and not best on Copper. An ablation isolating the contributions of delay and phase (and channel-specific μ/Σ) would substantiate the claimed advantages. Reporting runtimes and robustness across random seeds/initializations would also help.
- Restrictions: Diagonal Σ per channel simplifies derivations but limits cross-frequency correlations; brief discussion of non-diagonal Σ or spectral rotations would be useful.

Quality
Generally sound and well-supported. The key construction is valid and elegant, though the conjugation/sign inconsistency needs fixing. Experiments are reasonable for a first demonstration; however, more diagnostics (e.g., learned delays/phases on real data, or recovery of known offsets) would improve the case.

Clarity
Well-written and organized, with helpful background. Please:
- Fix conjugation/sign notation in Sec. 3.1 and align θij, φij definitions with Sij = conj(Ri)Rj.
- Explicitly state computational complexity and number of parameters.
- Add a short paragraph on initialization and optimization details (learning rates, priors, constraints).

Originality
High. The PSD matrix factorization with complex SE factors to capture delay and phase in multi-output GPs, yielding a closed-form kernel and strictly generalizing CSM/SM-LMC, is a substantial conceptual advance over existing LMC/CONV-based constructions.

Significance
Promising. This provides a new design paradigm for multi-output kernels that is both expressive and interpretable. With a scalable inference scheme, MOSM could become a practical default for multi-sensor time series and spatial-temporal tasks.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, principled, and novel kernel construction with clear advantages (delay + phase + channel-specific spectra).
- Theoretical correctness via Cramér’s theorem and PSD factorization.
- Clean generalization of widely used SM kernels to MOGPs; clear relation to prior art.
- Convincing synthetic demonstration; competitive real-world performance.

Cons for acceptance
- Some notation/derivation clarity issues (conjugation/signs).
- Parameter proliferation and lack of scalability; limited discussion of identifiability/initialization.
- Modest empirical gains on real data; lack of ablations and runtime analysis.

Requested clarifications/suggestions
- Correct and clarify Sij(ω) = conj(Ri)Rj throughout; ensure θij/φij definitions reflect this and Hermitian symmetry.
- Provide details on initialization (e.g., spectral means from periodograms), constraints, and regularization.
- Add ablations disabling θ and/or φ, and plots of recovered delays/phases on real data.
- Comment on extending Σ beyond diagonal and on scalable variational inference with spectral inducing variables.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). The paper introduces a valuable, general, and interpretable kernel family for multi-output GPs, with a principled spectral construction that expands modeling capabilities beyond existing approaches. Despite some empirical and clarity limitations, the conceptual advance justifies acceptance.

Confidence
High.
