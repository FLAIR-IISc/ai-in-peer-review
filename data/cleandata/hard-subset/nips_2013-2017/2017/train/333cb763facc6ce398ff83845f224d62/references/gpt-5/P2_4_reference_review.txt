Summary
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to design complex cross-spectral densities directly in the Fourier domain using Cramér’s theorem, ensuring positive definiteness by factorizing the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) = R(ω)^H R(ω). Each column Ri(ω) is parameterized as a complex Gaussian (square-exponential) with output-specific mean µi, covariance Σi, magnitude wi, phase φi, and delay θi. After symmetrization to ensure real-valued processes, the inverse Fourier transform yields closed-form stationary cross-covariances that are Gaussian times cosine with channel-pair-specific amplitude, frequency, phase, and delay. The construction strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM: it allows different auto-covariance parameters across channels, explicit inter-channel delays, and flexible phase differences. Experiments include (i) a synthetic three-output example (original signal, derivative, and delayed copy) where MOSM uniquely recovers both derivative and delay structure, (ii) climate sensor failure imputation, and (iii) multi-metal prediction on the Jura dataset, where MOSM is competitive and often best.

Relation to prior work
- Extends Wilson & Adams’ spectral mixture (SM) kernel to MOGPs via Cramér’s theorem (multivariate Bochner).
- Generalizes LMC/CONV approaches by avoiding latent-process coupling that forces similar auto-covariances and symmetric cross-covariances.
- Subsumes SM-LMC (shared spectral parameters, no phase/delay) and CSM (phase but limited parameter sharing), adding interpretable delays and per-output spectral parameters.
- Related to multivariate Matérn cross-covariances and prior PSD-based constructions; provides a distinct, interpretable, closed-form kernel family.

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The PSD factorization guarantees positive semidefiniteness (pointwise in ω). The symmetrization to enforce real-valued processes is justified by interpreting it as duplicating mirrored components; making this equivalence explicit (e.g., constructing R with mirrored rows scaled by 1/√2) would fully close the loop but is standard and plausible.
- The inverse Fourier transform derivation to Gaussian×cosine with phase and delay is correct and yields interpretable parameters. The reduction to SM for i=j and to CSM/SM-LMC under parameter tying is clearly argued.
- Some identifiability aspects are unaddressed: θij = θi − θj and φij = φi − φj imply invariances (global shifts/phases), suggesting one output should be fixed as a reference. The sign of wi can be absorbed into φi; constraining wi ≥ 0 could avoid redundancy. These do not invalidate correctness but are practical concerns for training.
- Empirics: Synthetic case convincingly demonstrates capacity (derivative + delay). Real-data results are competitive; statistical testing is mentioned (KS test), but the choice of test for MAE distributions and multiple-comparisons control could be clarified.

Clarity
- Overall well organized with clear definitions and derivations, including explicit parameter mappings (µij, Σij, θij, φij, αij). Figures help interpret delay/phase effects.
- Minor typos and notational hiccups (e.g., occasionally missing 1/2 terms in exponents in the text; clarity on rank-Q construction could be tightened by explicitly writing S(ω) = Σq Rq^H Rq).
- Training details are adequate but could benefit from a brief note on parameter constraints/priors and initialization (important for nonconvex hyperparameter learning).

Originality
- The core idea—constructing multivariate kernels via complex cross-PSDs and Cramér’s theorem—is known, but the particular factorized complex SE parameterization with per-output parameters yielding closed-form cross-covariances with interpretable delay and phase is a meaningful and nontrivial generalization of SM-LMC/CSM. The demonstration that both delay and phase can be modeled jointly and in closed form is novel relative to CSM.

Significance
- Practical impact: Provides a flexible, interpretable MOGP kernel that can capture realistic inter-channel relationships (delays, phase shifts, negative correlations) with closed form, which is attractive in time series, geostatistics, and sensor networks.
- Empirical gains: Strong on synthetic; competitive and sometimes best on real data. The lack of consistent superiority on real benchmarks tempers the impact but is understandable given dataset similarity and CSM’s proximity.
- Scalability: The current implementation is O(N^3) and not sparse; this limits immediate applicability to larger datasets. The discussion points to plausible sparse extensions.

Pros
- Solid theoretical construction with guaranteed positive definiteness and closed-form kernels.
- Clear parameter interpretability (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay) and inclusion of SM-LMC and CSM as special cases.
- Demonstrates modeling capabilities (derivative + delay) that latent-process models struggle with.
- Competitive empirical performance; appropriate baselines; some statistical testing.

Cons
- No sparse/structured inference; limited scalability.
- Parameter redundancy/identifiability (global phase/delay shifts, wi sign) not discussed; could affect optimization.
- The empirical advantage over CSM is modest on real datasets; more varied benchmarks and ablations (e.g., effect of allowing delays) would strengthen the case.
- Symmetrization preserving PSD is asserted but not proved explicitly; a short constructive proof would improve rigor.
- Limited guidance on initialization, constraints, and hyperparameter priors.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a brief identifiability discussion and fix one channel’s phase/delay as reference; constrain wi ≥ 0 and absorb sign into φi.
- Provide an explicit construction showing symmetrization corresponds to an expanded R with mirrored components, ensuring PSD.
- Report computational cost and convergence behavior; include ablations isolating the effect of delay and phase.
- Outline or prototype a sparse/structured variant (e.g., variational Fourier features/inter-domain inducing variables).
- Use more suitable significance tests for paired errors (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank) and note multiple-comparison corrections.

Overall assessment
- Quality: good
- Clarity: good
- Originality: moderate-to-high (clear generalization of prior spectral MOGP kernels)
- Significance: moderate

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a clean, theoretically grounded, and interpretable extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings, with convincing synthetic evidence and competitive real-world results. Addressing the noted issues would further strengthen the contribution.
