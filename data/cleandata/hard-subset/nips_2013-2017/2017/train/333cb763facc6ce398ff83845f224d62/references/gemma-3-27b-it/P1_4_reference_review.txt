## Peer Review: Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to designing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs) by extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel concept to the multivariate setting. The authors leverage Cramér’s theorem to construct a “Multi-Output Spectral Mixture” (MOSM) kernel based on complex-valued cross-spectral densities.  This allows for greater flexibility in modelling dependencies between outputs, explicitly incorporating parameters for delays and varying auto-covariances, while maintaining positive definiteness. The paper includes a synthetic experiment and validation on two real-world datasets (climate signals and metal concentrations) comparing the MOSM kernel against existing MOGP methods like LMC, CONV, and CSM.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon the well-established SM kernel for scalar GPs [6] and the earlier attempts to adapt it for MOGPs, specifically the CSM kernel [5]. The authors correctly position their work as addressing limitations of these prior approaches, namely the lack of interpretability in LMC/CONV and the manual parameterization of phase shifts in CSM.  The paper draws heavily on Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems as foundational theoretical tools for kernel construction.  It acknowledges relevant work in multivariate geostatistics and spectral estimation.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding in Cramér’s theorem provides a strong theoretical justification for the proposed kernel and guarantees its positive definiteness. This is a crucial strength, as ensuring positive definiteness is a significant challenge in MOGP kernel design.
* **Interpretability:** The MOSM kernel offers clear spectral interpretation of its parameters – frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay.  This improves the ability to understand the learned dependencies between outputs and facilitates more informed model building.
* **Flexibility:** The proposed approach offers a higher degree of flexibility compared to existing MOGP kernels, allowing for independent parameterization of auto- and cross-covariances and the inclusion of delay components.
* **Synthetic Validation:** The synthetic experiment successfully demonstrates the MOSM kernel’s ability to learn and model derivatives and delayed signals, showcasing its advantages in scenarios where dependencies are known.
* **Competitive Performance:** The results on real-world datasets demonstrate competitive performance, especially in the Jura dataset where MOSM outperforms other methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While providing flexibility, the increased number of parameters in the MOSM kernel introduces potential challenges for hyperparameter optimization and computational cost, particularly for high-dimensional outputs. This is not thoroughly discussed.
* **Limited Real-World Advantage:** The performance gains on the climate data are not statistically significant and are relatively modest. The paper acknowledges this, but it raises questions about the practical benefits of MOSM over simpler alternatives in certain scenarios.
* **Connection to CSM:** The relationship to the CSM kernel, while acknowledged, could be more thoroughly explored. The authors state that CSM is a special case of MOSM, but a more explicit derivation would solidify this connection and highlight the specific advantages of the proposed approach more clearly.
* **Scalability:** The computational scalability of the method is not addressed in sufficient detail.  The paper mentions implementation in TensorFlow/GPflow, but doesn't discuss scaling to very large datasets or high-dimensional outputs.
* **Lack of Discussion on Rank (Q):** The choice of Q=1 for the rank of the decomposition is motivated by simplicity but the impact of increasing Q (and its implications for computational cost and expressiveness) is not explored.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound contribution to the field of MOGPs. The MOSM kernel offers a compelling extension of the spectral mixture approach, providing greater flexibility, interpretability, and a strong theoretical foundation. While the practical benefits on real-world datasets are not always dramatic, the method demonstrates competitive performance and shows promise for applications where modelling complex, delayed relationships between outputs is crucial.  The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation (Cramér's theorem).
* Novel and flexible kernel design with clear spectral interpretation.
* Successful validation on a synthetic example demonstrating key capabilities.
* Competitive performance on real-world datasets.
* Addresses a significant challenge in MOGP design (positive definiteness and flexible cross-covariances).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Modest performance gains on some datasets.
* Lack of detailed discussion on computational complexity and scalability.
* Limited exploration of the impact of the rank parameter (Q).
* Could benefit from a more thorough comparison and discussion of the connection to the CSM kernel.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for publication with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Discuss computational complexity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the MOSM kernel, especially in relation to the number of outputs and the rank parameter Q.
2.  **Elaborate on the CSM connection:**  Provide a more explicit derivation showing how the CSM kernel can be seen as a special case of MOSM.
3.  **Investigate the impact of Q:** Briefly explore the effect of increasing the rank parameter Q on model performance and computational cost.
4.  **Clarify scalability:** Discuss potential strategies for scaling the method to larger datasets and higher dimensional outputs.
