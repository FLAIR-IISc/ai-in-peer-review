

# Review of "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by leveraging Cramér's theorem. The authors construct a complex-valued spectral density matrix that satisfies positive definiteness through a rank-one decomposition, enabling the modeling of delays and phase differences across channels - features not captured by existing approaches like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The paper builds on the foundational work of Wilson et al. (2013) on SM kernels and the CSM approach of Ulrich et al. (2015), extending these concepts to a more flexible multi-output framework.

**Strengths:**

The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant theoretical extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting. The derivation using Cramér's theorem is sound and well-motivated, with clear explanations of how the complex-valued spectral densities translate to meaningful covariance functions. The proposed MOSM kernel provides a clear physical interpretation of all parameters in spectral terms, which is a significant improvement over previous approaches like LMC or CSM that lack such interpretability.

The synthetic experiment in Section 4.1 is particularly compelling, demonstrating MOSM's ability to learn derivative relationships and delayed signals without prior knowledge of these relationships - a capability that existing MOGP models cannot achieve. This showcases the practical value of the model's flexibility. The experimental design is thorough, with appropriate comparison against relevant baselines (CONV, SM-LMC, CSM) on two real-world datasets (climate data and heavy metal concentrations).

The paper also provides a clear, step-by-step explanation of the model's implementation, including the necessary modifications to handle complex-valued spectral densities and ensure real-valued outputs. The experimental results, particularly the MAE comparisons in Tables 1-3, are well-presented and support the main claims.

**Weaknesses:**

The most significant weakness is the lack of strong empirical evidence demonstrating the superiority of MOSM over CSM in real-world scenarios. While MOSM shows statistical significance in the Cadmium prediction task (Table 3), the climate data results (Table 2) show only marginal improvements over CSM, with no statistically significant difference for three of the four sensors. Given that the climate signals are highly correlated, the close performance between MOSM and CSM is expected, but the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why MOSM's theoretical advantages don't translate to clear empirical gains in this setting.

The paper claims MOSM can model "delays and phase differences," but the only experimental evidence for this capability comes from the synthetic example. The real-world experiments don't explicitly demonstrate or quantify the delay/phase modeling capability that distinguishes MOSM from previous approaches. This is a missed opportunity to showcase the model's unique advantages.

The paper also lacks discussion of computational complexity and scalability, which is crucial for practical applications. The use of complex-valued spectral densities may increase computational requirements, but this isn't addressed. Additionally, the paper doesn't discuss how the rank of the spectral density matrix (Q in Definition 5) affects performance or whether there are principled ways to select Q.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the MOGP literature by extending spectral mixture kernels to a more flexible framework that can model delays and phase differences. The synthetic example provides strong evidence for the model's capabilities, and the experimental results on real-world data are competitive with existing methods.

However, the lack of clear empirical advantages over the closely related CSM model in the real-world experiments weakens the paper's significance claim. The authors should provide more thorough analysis of when MOSM's additional flexibility leads to tangible improvements, particularly in scenarios where delays and phase differences are present.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a technically sound and theoretically interesting extension to multi-output Gaussian processes. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the empirical evidence supporting the model's practical advantages is somewhat limited. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the following points:

1. Add a more detailed discussion of the scenarios where MOSM's delay/phase modeling capability should provide clear advantages, and provide evidence of these advantages in the real-world experiments.

2. Include computational complexity analysis and discuss potential scalability limitations.

3. Strengthen the discussion of when the MOSM model should be preferred over CSM or other simpler models, particularly in the climate data experiment where the signals are highly correlated.

4. Consider adding a brief analysis of how the rank parameter Q affects performance to help guide practical application of the model.

The paper has significant potential to advance the field of multi-output Gaussian processes, and with these revisions, it would make a strong contribution to the NeurIPS community.
