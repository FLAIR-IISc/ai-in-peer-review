- Introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the SM kernel to multi-output GPs via Cramér's theorem and complex-valued spectral densities.  
- MOSM introduces delay and phase parameters to flexibly model cross-covariances and generalizes the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel.  
- Theoretical soundness: rigorous derivation from Cramér's theorem with guaranteed positive definiteness through complex factorization.  
- Clear physical interpretation: parameters correspond to frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay in the spectral domain.  
- Comprehensive experiments: validated on synthetic data (with known ground truth) and two real-world datasets, compared to SM-LMC, CONV, and CSM.  
- Demonstrated ability to learn derivatives and delayed signals in synthetic experiments.  
- Novel extension: captures delays and phase shifts across outputs that previous MOGP approaches could not.  
- Well-structured presentation with clear definitions, theorems, and helpful figures.  
- Limited comparison of computational complexity and no analysis of runtime or cost relative to other MOGP methods.  
- Scalability concerns: experiments limited to small output dimensions (3, 4, 7) with no discussion of scaling to many outputs.  
- No guidance on selecting the number of spectral components (Q), a key hyperparameter affecting performance and complexity.  
- Limited discussion of when MOSM would outperform CSM in practice beyond a brief climate-data note.  
- No discussion of handling outputs with different scales or physical units in multi-output problems.  
- Quality: technically sound with strong theoretical and experimental support (4/5).  
- Clarity: clearly written and reproducible, with consistent mathematical notation (5/5).  
- Originality: meaningful and novel extension enabling delays/phase modeling in MOGPs (4/5).  
- Significance: valuable for applications requiring modeling of delays/phase shifts; shows practical improvements on a real dataset (4/5).  
- Recommendation: accept — contribution is strong and practical despite minor concerns about complexity and scalability.  
- Pro arguments: novel, theoretically grounded, practically validated, differentiates from prior work, and shows real-world improvement (Cadmium task).  
- Con arguments: lacks complexity/scalability analysis, no Q-selection guidance, limited exploration of limitations, and small synthetic example.
