Summary
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a new family of kernels for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) constructed directly in the frequency domain via Cramér’s theorem. The key idea is to parameterize the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) through a low-rank Hermitian factorization S(ω)=∑q Rq(ω)H Rq(ω), where each column function Ri(ω) is a complex-valued squared-exponential (SE) with channel-specific mean, covariance, magnitude, delay, and phase. Enforcing real-valued processes is achieved by symmetrizing S(ω) with respect to ω. The inverse Fourier transform yields a closed-form stationary kernel with interpretable per-pair parameters: αij, Σij, μij, delay θij, and phase φij. This construction strictly generalizes SM-LMC and the CSM kernel: it retains SM autocovariances while allowing cross-covariances to differ across output pairs, include both delay and phase differences, and need not be positive definite or symmetric individually (while the full kernel is PD). Experiments include (i) a synthetic 3-output setting (a function, its derivative, and a delayed copy), where MOSM uniquely recovers both relationships and extrapolates well, and (ii) two real datasets (climate sensors and heavy metal concentrations), where MOSM is competitive or best in MAE.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on Wilson & Adams’ spectral mixture kernels by extending from scalar to multivariate via Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate Bochner).
- Generalizes LMC- and convolution-based MOGP constructions by moving design to the cross-spectrum and providing per-channel spectral parameters.
- Extends CSM (Ulrich et al.) by introducing explicit pairwise delays and allowing channel-specific spectral means/covariances; SM-LMC and CSM are special cases of MOSM.
- Related to complex GP literature and multivariate Matérn kernels, but offers a different spectral parameterization with stronger interpretability and flexibility.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual elegance: a principled spectral-domain construction that guarantees PD via a low-rank Hermitian factorization, with closed-form time-domain kernels.
- Interpretability: explicit frequency, phase, and delay parameters per channel pair—rare among MOGP kernels and highly valuable in real applications.
- Strict generalization: contains SM-LMC and CSM as special cases; relaxes tying of auto-covariances across outputs.
- Empirical evidence: synthetic experiment is compelling—MOSM recovers derivative and delay relationships that latent-process models miss; competitive on real data; significance testing reported.
- Practicality: implemented in GPflow/TensorFlow; gradients via autodiff.

Weaknesses
- Clarity/notation: There is a potential confusion about conjugation in Sij(ω). If S=R^H R, then Sij=conj(Ri)Rj, which ensures Hermitian PSDs; Eq. (9) omits the conjugation explicitly. The paper later symmetrizes for real-valued processes, but the Hermitian structure should be made explicit to avoid ambiguity.
- Scope of experiments: Real datasets do not strongly showcase the delay/phase advantage (climate signals are very similar and nearly synchronous). It would strengthen the case to demonstrate learned delays/phases that align with known physical offsets and to compare performance when θ/φ are disabled.
- Scaling: The method inherits cubic complexity in total observations; no sparse or inter-domain approximation is provided (though discussed as future work). Runtime and memory comparisons are missing.
- Ablations/sensitivity: No analysis of the number of components Q, identifiability, initialization strategies (crucial for spectral kernels), or robustness to local minima. The distinction between rank (per-ω factor rank) and mixture components could be clearer.
- Comparisons: While main baselines are included, comparisons to other flexible multivariate kernels (e.g., multivariate Matérn variants) and to convolutional models with explicit time-lags would add context.

Quality
The theoretical construction is sound and well-motivated; PD is guaranteed by factorization, and real-valuedness by spectral symmetry. The inverse transform is handled cleanly, yielding interpretable kernels. Minor notation issues aside, claims match derivations. Experiments are modest in scale but adequate to validate the key modeling claim (delay+phase). Statistical tests are a nice addition.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with a solid background section. However:
- Clarify the conjugation in Sij(ω) derived from S=R^H R.
- Be explicit about the real-process symmetry condition S(−ω)=S(ω)^T and how your symmetrization enforces it.
- Distinguish clearly between Q as mixture components versus the spectral factor rank; explain how higher rank beyond summing rank-1 factors would differ.

Originality
High. The spectral factorization approach with complex SE functions enabling per-pair delay and phase is a notable and non-incremental step beyond SM-LMC/CSM.

Significance
Potentially high. The interpretability and flexibility (delay+phase per pair) are valuable in multi-sensor/time series settings. With a scalable variant, this could become a standard MOGP kernel family.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Please correct/clarify the complex conjugation in Eq. (9) and explicitly show Hermitian PSDs before symmetrization for real-valued processes.
- Provide details on initialization (μ, Σ, θ, φ, w) and sensitivity; spectral kernels are notorious for local optima.
- Report runtimes and memory vs. baselines; discuss the impact of m and N on complexity.
- Show that learned delays/phases on the real datasets correspond to plausible physical offsets (even qualitatively).
- Add ablations: Q components, turning off θ and/or φ, and rank vs mixture.
- Consider a small demonstration with known non-zero delays between sensors to better showcase the unique capabilities.

Minor comments/typos
- Several minor typos (e.g., “multiouput,” “derivate”) and occasional missing 1/2 factors in exponents; please proofread.
- Be consistent about αij sign: αij as defined is nonnegative; negative correlations arise via the cosine/phase term.

Pros
- Principled, elegant spectral construction with PD guarantee.
- Clear interpretability and ability to model delay and phase per pair.
- Strictly generalizes prior SM-based MOGP kernels.
- Strong synthetic evidence; competitive real-data results.

Cons
- Notational clarity issues around complex conjugation and symmetry.
- Limited scale and breadth of experiments; limited demonstration of learned delay/phase on real data.
- No scalable inference implementation; no runtime comparisons.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses.

Recommendation: Accept (leaning strong). Despite some clarity and experimental breadth limitations, the core idea is novel, bold, and likely to influence future MOGP kernel design. I would be satisfied with a camera-ready that addresses the noted clarity issues and adds brief ablations.
