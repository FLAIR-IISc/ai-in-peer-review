Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to design the matrix-valued cross-spectral density S(ω) directly, using Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s), rather than specifying covariance functions in the time/space domain. The authors posit a low-rank factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω) with complex-valued, per-output, squared-exponential (SE) components Ri(ω) that include magnitude, mean frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay. Enforcing conjugate symmetry yields real-valued processes and closed-form inverse Fourier transforms give the kernel (eq. 11). This construction generalizes SM-LMC and CSM: it allows different auto-spectral parameters per output (µi, Σi), and explicitly models both inter-output phase φij and delay θij, while guaranteeing positive semidefiniteness via the factorization.

Empirically, a synthetic example (reference, derivative, delayed signals) highlights the ability to capture both phase and delay in cross-covariances, and two real datasets (climate sensors; Jura geochemistry) show competitive accuracy versus CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM, with statistically significant gains in some cases.

Quality
- Theory: The construction is technically sound. The PSD factorization (eq. 7) guarantees S(ω) is Hermitian PSD pointwise, and the symmetrization S(ω) ← (S(ω)+S(−ω))/2 ensures real-valued processes while preserving PSD. The inverse Fourier transform is tractable because Ri(ω) are SEs, yielding eq. (10)/(11) with interpretable parameters (magnitude αij, frequency µij, bandwidth Σij, delay θij, phase φij). The reduction to SM-LMC/CSM under parameter tying is explained (Sec. 3.3).
- A minor technical clarity issue: since wij = wi wj exp(−·) > 0, αij in eq. (10) is nonnegative; negative cross-covariances arise via the cosine term and phase, not from αij being negative. The claim “αij ∈ R” as allowing negative correlations is potentially misleading.
- Scope/expressivity: Rank-Q mixtures allow universal approximation of stationary matrix-valued kernels in principle (by analogy to SM); using diagonal Σi is standard but somewhat restrictive; the low-rank constraint is explicit and well-motivated.
- Optimization/practicality: The paper optimizes the full marginal likelihood with dense O(N3) costs; no sparsity is proposed (noted as future work). The parameterization is large: for m outputs, Q components, and input dimension n, the model introduces per-output-per-component parameters wi, µi (n), Σi (diag, n), θi (n), φi, which raises identifiability risks (e.g., scaling/phase ambiguities in the rank-1 factorization) and potential overfitting. The paper would benefit from a discussion of identifiability constraints, parameter tying, or priors, and more details on initialization (which is crucial for SM-like models).
- Experiments: The synthetic example is well chosen to stress delay and phase; MOSM’s advantage over LMC/CONV is convincing there. On real data, gains are modest and sometimes MOSM ties with CSM/SM-LMC; statistical testing is performed only in part. Evaluation uses MAE only; calibration or uncertainty quality is not assessed. Ablations isolating the contribution of θ and φ, or comparing different ranks Q and numbers of components, would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with helpful background (Bochner/Cramér). The mapping from per-output Ri to cross-parameters (µij, Σij, θij, φij, wij) is explicit and interpretable.
- Minor typos and grammatical issues (e.g., “multiouput”, “joint model delays”), and the αij remark above.
- Reproducibility: Implementation platform (GPflow) is stated; however, hyperparameter initialization and practical training details (learning rates, restarts, constraints) are sparse; code availability is not indicated.

Originality
- The work is an incremental but nontrivial extension of SM to the multi-output setting via complex cross-spectral densities with per-output parameters and explicit delays, generalizing CSM and SM-LMC. Using a factorized, complex SE cross-spectrum with conjugate symmetry to achieve real-valued, closed-form cross-covariances that include both delay and phase is, to my knowledge, novel in the GP literature, and aligned with classical multivariate spectral analysis.
- Related work is adequately cited (LMC, CONV, SM, CSM, Matérn cross-covariances), and the relationship to CSM/SM-LMC is clarified.

Significance
- The proposed kernel is flexible and interpretable, offering a principled way to encode and learn delay and phase relationships across channels—important in temporal/spatiotemporal sensor networks and signal processing.
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines on real tasks are modest; however, the model’s interpretability and generality are valuable, and the synthetic study demonstrates capabilities not easily matched by LMC/CONV without bespoke operators.
- The lack of scalability and limited empirical breadth temper impact; a sparse/variational version would substantially increase practical significance.

Pros
- Sound, principled construction of a flexible MOGP kernel with clear spectral interpretation.
- Generalizes and subsumes SM-LMC/CSM; supports both phase and delay across outputs.
- Closed-form covariance; straightforward GP training/inference.
- Synthetic experiment convincingly demonstrates unique capabilities.

Cons
- Many parameters; potential identifiability/overfitting; limited guidance on initialization/constraints.
- Scalability not addressed beyond dense GP; experiments are relatively small.
- Real-data gains are modest; limited statistical testing and no calibration analysis.
- Minor clarity issues (αij sign interpretation; typos).

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss identifiability and impose/justify constraints or priors; provide initialization strategies (e.g., spectral estimates per output).
- Provide ablations isolating delay/phase benefits and sensitivity to Q and number of components.
- Add sparse/variational approximation leveraging inter-domain/variational Fourier features (as noted).
- Report uncertainty quality (e.g., NLL/RMSE/coverage) and perform broader significance testing.
- Release code for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper is technically solid, extends a widely used kernel family in a principled, interpretable way, and shows clear advantages on a designed task with competitive real-data performance. Strengthening empirical evidence and addressing practical training/scalability would further enhance impact.
