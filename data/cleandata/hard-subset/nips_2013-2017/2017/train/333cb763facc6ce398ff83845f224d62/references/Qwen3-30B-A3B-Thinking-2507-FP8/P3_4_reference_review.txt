

# Peer Review: "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

## Summary

This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output setting by leveraging Cramér's theorem. The authors construct complex-valued spectral densities that allow for modeling delays and phase differences across channels, addressing limitations of existing approaches like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The paper builds on the SM kernel's spectral mixture approach, but extends it to the multi-output case by using complex-valued spectral densities and ensuring positive definiteness through a rank-1 factorization. The authors validate their approach on synthetic data (showing ability to model derivatives and delayed signals) and two real-world datasets (climate signals and heavy metal concentrations).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation using Cramér's theorem, which is the natural extension of Bochner's theorem to the multi-output case. The derivation of the MOSM kernel from complex-valued spectral densities is mathematically sound and well-motivated.

2. **Clear Interpretability**: The authors successfully provide a spectral interpretation for all parameters, which is valuable for understanding the learned relationships between channels. The ability to interpret parameters as frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay is a significant contribution.

3. **Effective Synthetic Validation**: The synthetic example (Fig. 2) clearly demonstrates MOSM's ability to model derivative relationships and delayed signals, which existing approaches (like CSM) cannot handle. This is a strong validation of the proposed model's flexibility.

4. **Good Experimental Design**: The paper compares MOSM against relevant baselines (SM-LMC, CONV, CSM) on two real-world datasets with appropriate metrics (MAE). The experimental setup is reasonable and well-documented.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Differentiation from CSM**: The paper claims MOSM can model delays and phase differences while CSM cannot, but this is not entirely accurate. The CSM paper (Ulrich et al., 2015) explicitly includes a phase shift parameter that can model delays. The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how the MOSM's delay parameter differs from CSM's phase shift parameter or why MOSM is fundamentally more expressive. This weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Limited Practical Evaluation**: The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity or scalability, which is crucial for real-world applications. For instance, how does MOSM scale with the number of outputs or data points compared to LMC or CONV? The lack of timing experiments or memory usage analysis is a significant omission.

3. **Marginal Real-World Performance Gains**: On the climate data, MOSM's performance is only marginally better than CSM (0.129 vs 0.130 MAE), and the difference is not statistically significant. On the heavy metal dataset, MOSM only outperforms others for Cadmium (statistically significantly) but not for Copper. This limits the perceived significance of the contribution.

4. **Incomplete Limitation Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss when MOSM might not be the best choice (e.g., for highly correlated outputs where simpler models like LMC suffice). The claim that MOSM "can collapse to models that share parameters across pairs of outputs" is vague and doesn't address when this would be beneficial.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the multi-output GP literature, but the novelty claim is overstated relative to existing work. The key theoretical contribution—using complex-valued spectral densities to model delays and phase differences—is valid, but the paper fails to clearly articulate how this differs from or improves upon CSM. The CSM kernel (Ulrich et al., 2015) already includes a phase shift parameter that could model similar effects, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that MOSM's approach leads to better performance or more interpretable models in practice.

The experimental results, while promising for the synthetic example, are not sufficiently compelling for the real-world applications. The marginal improvements on real datasets and lack of statistical significance in most cases weaken the argument for the model's practical utility. The paper would be stronger if it included ablation studies showing how individual components (delays, phase shifts) contribute to performance.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings, but the novelty is somewhat overstated, and the practical significance is limited by the marginal performance gains on real-world datasets. I recommend *weak acceptance* (borderline) with the following conditions:

1. The authors must clarify the theoretical and practical distinction between MOSM and CSM, particularly regarding the modeling of delays and phase shifts.

2. The authors should include a discussion of computational complexity and scalability, with timing experiments showing how MOSM compares to existing approaches.

3. The authors should provide more thorough statistical analysis of the real-world results to better establish significance.

4. The authors should better articulate when MOSM is likely to be advantageous compared to simpler alternatives like LMC or CONV.

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution is somewhat incremental. However, the theoretical foundation is strong, and the synthetic validation is compelling. With the suggested revisions, this paper could make a meaningful contribution to the multi-output GP literature. As it stands, it's a good paper that needs to better justify its novelty and practical significance.
