The paper proposes a Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the spectral mixture concept to multiple outputs via Cramér’s theorem. The core idea is to define cross-spectral densities and then construct the covariance function using the inverse Fourier transform. The authors claim this approach offers flexibility, interpretability, and accounts for delays and varying auto-covariances across channels, addressing limitations of existing MOGP methods like LMC, CONV, and CSM.

The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical derivations appear correct. The connection to Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems is clearly explained, providing a solid theoretical foundation. However, despite the apparent soundness and novelty of the proposed approach, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and a lack of compelling empirical evidence to justify the complexity of the model. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The grounding in spectral analysis and the use of Cramér’s theorem provide a rigorous framework.
*   **Novelty:** Extending the spectral mixture concept to multi-output GPs is a novel contribution. The ability to model delays and phase differences is a potential advantage.
*   **Interpretability:** The parameters of the MOSM kernel have clear spectral interpretations.
*   **Clear presentation of background**: The background section thoroughly covers the necessary theoretical foundation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overly Complex Parameterization:** The MOSM kernel introduces a significant number of parameters (wi, µi, Σi, θi, φi for each channel and spectral component). While flexibility is claimed, the practical challenges of estimating and interpreting these parameters are not adequately addressed. The benefit of this complexity is not clearly demonstrated.
*   **Synthetic Experiment Limitations:** The synthetic example, while demonstrating the ability to recover known covariances, is somewhat contrived. The signals are explicitly designed to be recoverable, and the experiment doesn't test the model's ability to learn complex relationships from noisy or less structured data. The choice of numerical differentiation for the derivative signal introduces potential inaccuracies.
*   **Limited Empirical Gains:** The results on the climate and metal concentration datasets are not particularly impressive. The performance of MOSM is often comparable to or slightly worse than simpler models (CONV, CSM). The statistical significance analysis is weak, concluding significance only for one sensor in the climate dataset. The authors attribute the lack of improvement on the metal concentration dataset to data non-Gaussianity but do not explore potential solutions (e.g., non-Gaussian likelihoods).
*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:**
    *   Equation (1) refers to an integrable function *k*, but *k* is later defined as a covariance function. This should be clarified.
    *   The notation in eq. (9) introduces “wij” without clear relation to prior parameters.
    *   Equation (10) includes an unnecessary constant factor which is explained to be absorbed into αij. This complicates readability.
    *   The phrasing “all models considered five latent signals/spectral components” appears throughout but a justification for this choice is missing.
*   **Missing Ablation Study:** An ablation study to evaluate the contribution of the delay and phase parameters would strengthen the paper.
*   **Lack of Scalability Discussion:** The computational cost of the proposed kernel (especially with a large number of spectral components) is not discussed. 

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP kernel design.
*   Clear explanation of the underlying mathematical principles.
*   Potential for modeling complex relationships between outputs.

**Con:**

*   Overly complex model with a large number of parameters.
*   Limited empirical evidence of significant performance gains.
*   Minor technical inconsistencies and lack of clarity in some sections.
*   Missing ablation study and scalability discussion.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty and theoretical appeal of the MOSM kernel, the lack of compelling empirical results and the model’s complexity outweigh its strengths. While the paper demonstrates a sound understanding of Gaussian processes and spectral analysis, it falls short of presenting a practical and impactful contribution. The paper feels incomplete, as if the authors haven’t fully explored the potential and limitations of their approach. 

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. The paper requires substantial revision, including a more thorough empirical evaluation, a justification for the chosen parameterization, a discussion of computational complexity, and an ablation study to demonstrate the value of the proposed extensions. A clearer articulation of the benefits of MOSM over existing methods is also crucial.
