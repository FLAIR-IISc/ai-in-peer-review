This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the Spectral Mixture kernel to multivariate Gaussian processes by leveraging Cramér’s theorem. The core idea—designing spectral densities in the Fourier domain to ensure positive definiteness—is a compelling and elegant approach to constructing MOGP kernels, offering interpretability through spectral parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay). The authors correctly identify limitations in existing approaches like LMC and CSM, particularly their lack of flexibility in modeling cross-covariances and the ad-hoc nature of parameter tuning (e.g., the phase shift in CSM). The connection to Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems provides a strong theoretical foundation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The MOSM kernel represents a significant advance in MOGP kernel design. The shift to spectral density design via Cramér’s theorem, allowing for complex-valued cross-spectral densities and explicit modeling of delays and phase shifts, is a genuinely novel contribution. It’s not simply an incremental improvement, but a fundamentally different way to think about MOGP kernels.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The grounding in Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems is solid, ensuring the mathematical validity of the proposed approach.
* **Interpretability:** The spectral parameters offer clear physical interpretations, making the model more transparent and potentially easier to apply in specific domains.
* **Generality:** The MOSM kernel generalizes previous approaches (like CSM) by offering more flexibility in modeling cross-covariances.
* **Illustrative Example:** The synthetic experiment effectively demonstrates the ability of MOSM to learn and extrapolate derivatives and delayed signals, a capability absent in the compared baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Validation:** While the synthetic example is convincing, the real-world experiments (climate data and metal concentrations) are somewhat underwhelming. The results aren’t overwhelmingly superior to existing methods, and the statistical significance analysis is limited. The climate data experiment, in particular, shows little differentiation between the models.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't adequately address the computational challenges associated with the MOSM kernel, particularly concerning the inversion of the covariance matrix during inference. A discussion of scalability and potential approximations would strengthen the work.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** The authors briefly mention the relationship to CSM but don't offer a comprehensive ablation study demonstrating the specific benefits of the added complexity (phase and delay parameters) in the real-world datasets. Do these parameters consistently improve performance, or are they only crucial in specific scenarios?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The performance of kernel methods is often highly sensitive to hyperparameter tuning. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter optimization process and sensitivity analysis.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **High Novelty:** The core idea is significantly different from existing approaches to MOGP kernel design. The use of complex-valued spectral densities and explicit modeling of delays represent a genuine advance.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding in Cramér’s theorem and the clear mathematical development of the kernel are impressive.
* **Potential Impact:** The MOSM kernel could have a significant impact on applications requiring modeling of complex dependencies between multiple outputs, particularly in areas where phase shifts or delays are important.
* **Illustrative Synthetic Example:** The synthetic experiment demonstrates a clear advantage of MOSM over existing methods in a controlled setting.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Empirical Superiority:** The results on the real-world datasets aren’t compelling enough to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* **Lack of Scalability Discussion:** The paper doesn’t address the potential computational challenges associated with using the MOSM kernel in large-scale applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations in empirical validation and the lack of a scalability discussion, the novelty and theoretical strength of this paper are substantial. The authors present a genuinely new and promising approach to MOGP kernel design. I believe the potential impact of this work outweighs its current weaknesses. The paper introduces a significant new concept and direction, even if further work is needed to fully realize its potential. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions). 

I recommend the authors address the following points in a revision:

1. **Expand the experimental validation:** Explore additional real-world datasets or more challenging scenarios within the existing datasets to demonstrate a more convincing advantage of the MOSM kernel.
2. **Discuss computational complexity:**  Address the computational challenges associated with the MOSM kernel and explore potential approximations or sparse implementations.
3. **Conduct an ablation study:**  Systematically evaluate the impact of the phase and delay parameters on performance.
4. **Provide more details on hyperparameter optimization:**  Discuss the hyperparameter optimization process and sensitivity analysis.
