The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the spectral mixture concept to multi-output Gaussian processes. The core idea is to leverage Cramér's theorem to construct a covariance function from complex-valued cross-spectral densities, offering a flexible framework for modeling dependencies between outputs, including delays and phase shifts. The authors demonstrate the applicability of their approach through a synthetic example and comparisons with existing MOGP models on climate and metal concentration datasets.

While the concept is interesting and builds upon established techniques like the SM kernel and MOGPs, the presentation suffers from several shortcomings. The mathematical notation, while largely correct, is occasionally imprecise and could benefit from more rigorous definitions (e.g., the jump between equation 5 and 6 regarding the positive definiteness condition feels abrupt). The introduction, while outlining the motivation, is somewhat verbose and could be more concise. The connection between the proposed method and existing approaches, particularly CSM, is superficially addressed; a deeper dive into *why* MOSM provides benefits beyond CSM—aside from the generalization to arbitrary delays and phase shifts—would strengthen the argument. 

A major concern is the level of experimental validation. The synthetic example, though demonstrating the ability to learn the desired parameters, feels contrived. The real-world datasets, while standard benchmarks, do not consistently show a clear advantage for MOSM. The claim of statistical significance in the Jura dataset (Cadmium) is qualified as “cannot guarantee” in the Copper case, and the justification for the varying performance across datasets is weak. The discussion of the experimental results lacks critical analysis – for instance, why does MOSM perform similarly to CSM on the climate dataset, despite its broader flexibility?  The choice of five latent components for all models is somewhat arbitrary and not properly justified; a sensitivity analysis regarding this parameter would be beneficial.

The writing quality, while generally good, contains several minor issues. The statement "This can be understood through an analogy..." feels hand-wavy and lacks precision. The phrase "without prior information about the delayed or the derivative relationship" in the synthetic example is misleading, as the experiment is *designed* with this relationship known. There’s inconsistent use of terminology – “integrable” is defined, yet used later without explicit justification for its relevance. Formatting is also inconsistent. For instance, equations are not always numbered sequentially.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel extension of the SM kernel to multi-output GPs.
*   Clear theoretical foundation based on Cramér’s theorem.
*   Potential for modeling complex dependencies between outputs (delays, phase shifts).
*   Demonstrated applicability on standard benchmark datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited experimental validation and inconsistent performance gains.
*   Insufficient justification for model choices (e.g., number of latent components).
*   Minor notational imprecisions and occasional hand-waving arguments.
*   Superficial comparison to related work (CSM).
*   Inconsistent formatting and minor writing issues.



**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The idea of extending spectral methods to MOGPs is potentially valuable.
*   The theoretical grounding is sound.

**Con:**

*   The experimental results are not compelling enough to justify acceptance.
*   The presentation is marred by minor flaws in clarity and rigor.
*   The lack of a convincing advantage over existing methods, particularly CSM, weakens the contribution.
*   The analysis of results is superficial.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While the paper presents a technically sound idea, the limited experimental validation, minor flaws in presentation, and lack of a compelling advantage over existing methods outweigh the novelty of the approach. The authors would need to significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation—perhaps by exploring more challenging datasets or conducting a more thorough sensitivity analysis—and address the concerns regarding clarity and rigor before this work would be suitable for NIPS. Specifically, a detailed ablation study showing the impact of the added flexibility (delay and phase shift) would be critical.
