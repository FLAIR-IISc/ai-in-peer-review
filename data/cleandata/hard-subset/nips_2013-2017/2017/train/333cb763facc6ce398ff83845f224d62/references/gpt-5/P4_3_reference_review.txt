Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a new family of kernels for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The key idea is to design the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) directly in the Fourier domain, leveraging Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem) to guarantee that the resulting matrix-valued covariance function is positive definite. Specifically, the authors parameterize S(ω) via a low-rank factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), with each column function Ri(ω) chosen as a complex-valued squared exponential (SE) with per-output parameters controlling mean frequency μi, bandwidth Σi, amplitude wi, delay θi, and phase φi. Enforcing real-valued processes is achieved by symmetrizing S(ω). The inverse Fourier transform yields a closed-form cross-covariance kij(τ) as a sum of components of the form exp(−1/2(τ+θij)T Σij (τ+θij)) cos((τ+θij)T μij + φij), which naturally encodes pairwise phase and delay between outputs while preserving positive definiteness.

This builds on Wilson & Adams’ spectral mixture (SM) kernel for single outputs, and extends multi-output constructions such as LMC and convolution processes (Álvarez & Lawrence), as well as the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel (Ulrich et al.). The authors argue MOSM subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases by allowing per-output component parameters and complex cross-spectral terms that induce both phase and delay. They also relate to real-valued cross-spectral constructions (e.g., Matérn cross-covariances).

Experiments include: (i) a synthetic 3-output task involving a reference GP, its derivative, and a delayed version, where MOSM captures known cross-covariances and extrapolates both derivative and delayed channels; (ii) imputation on multi-station climate temperature data (sensor dropouts); and (iii) the Jura heavy metals dataset, predicting Cadmium and Copper from correlated metals. MOSM performs best on the synthetic task and Cadmium, and is competitive on the others, with statistical testing reported where applicable.

Quality:
- Theory: The construction is principled. Using S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) ensures S(ω) is Hermitian PSD for each ω, satisfying Cramér’s condition. Choosing complex SE Ri and symmetrizing S(ω) yields real-valued kernels. The derived closed forms for kij(τ) are consistent, and the required symmetry kji(τ)=kij(−τ) follows from θji=−θij and φji=−φij. The mapping from per-output parameters (μi, Σi, θi, φi, wi) to per-pair parameters (μij, Σij, θij, φij, αij) is coherent (product of Gaussians in Fourier domain).
- Completeness and care: Training and prediction use standard GP machinery with NLL optimization. However, some important practical details are missing: initialization strategy for spectral components and phase/delay, parameter constraints and reparameterizations (e.g., enforcing Σi ≻ 0, handling phase wrap), and computational complexity/runtime. The model is cubic in the number of observations and does not exploit structure; scalability is only discussed as future work.
- Empirics: The synthetic task is well chosen and clearly demonstrates the benefits of modeling pairwise delay and phase. Real-data evaluations are limited in scope (two datasets) and metrics (MAE only), and show modest improvements except in one case. Reporting negative log marginal likelihoods, uncertainty calibration, and ablations (e.g., with/without phase/delay) would strengthen the claims.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with useful background on Bochner/Cramér and SM. The parameter interpretations (frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay) are clear and appealing. Minor issues: a few typos; notational overload (e.g., weights wq vs wi); and it would help to explicitly state and prove the Hermitian symmetry in time domain (kij(τ)=kji(−τ)) and derive the per-pair parameter formulas (μij, Σij) in an appendix. Providing a complexity discussion and practical guidance for initialization would improve readability.

Originality:
The core novelty is a multivariate spectral mixture constructed via complex cross-spectral densities with per-output parameters, enabling joint modeling of phase and delay and yielding closed-form kernels. While closely related to SM-LMC and CSM, the additional flexibility (delay, per-output parameterization) and the clean factorization perspective are substantive and, to my knowledge, not previously presented in this form. The connection to and generalization of prior kernels are well articulated.

Significance:
The approach is potentially impactful for multi-output time series and spatial modeling where inter-channel phase and delay matter (e.g., sensor networks, neuro, geophysics). The interpretability of parameters is a practical strength. That said, empirical gains on real datasets are modest, and the lack of scalability may limit immediate applicability. With stronger experiments and a sparse/structured implementation, the impact could be higher.

Pros:
- Principled, elegant construction via Cramér’s theorem and spectral factorization.
- Closed-form, interpretable cross-covariances with controllable phase and delay.
- Subsumes SM-LMC/CSM as special cases; clarifies relations to prior work.
- Compelling synthetic results demonstrating capabilities unavailable to latent-process mixtures.

Cons:
- Limited empirical evaluation: few datasets, single metric, modest real-world gains.
- Missing practical details: initialization, constraints, runtime, sensitivity analyses.
- No scalable inference; complexity is cubic with dense covariance.
- Potential identifiability/coupling between phase and delay not discussed.
- Some derivations and symmetry properties relegated to text without formal proofs.

Suggestions and questions for authors:
- Provide derivations for the per-pair parameters (μij, Σij, αij) and an explicit statement/proof that kij(τ)=kji(−τ) holds under your parameterization.
- Discuss identifiability and optimization: how do φ and θ interact, and how are they initialized? Any constraints or priors used?
- Report NLL and calibration metrics, and include ablations removing phase and/or delay to quantify their contributions.
- Add computational details: training time, scaling with Q and m, and any numerical stabilization (e.g., reparameterizations for Σi, phase wrapping).
- Consider comparing with additional baselines (e.g., separable/ICM Matérn) and reporting results on more diverse datasets.
- Outline or prototype a sparse/variational version leveraging spectral inducing variables.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Sound, principled kernel construction with clear novelty over CSM/SM-LMC.
- Interpretability and flexibility (phase and delay) are valuable in practice.
- Solid synthetic demonstration and competitive real-data performance.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical support is limited and improvements on real data are small.
- Lack of scalability and missing practical training details.
- Some theoretical details (proofs/derivations) and clarity could be improved.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and novel extension of spectral mixture ideas to the multi-output setting with interpretable parameters. Strengthening the empirical section and adding practical guidance would improve its significance.
