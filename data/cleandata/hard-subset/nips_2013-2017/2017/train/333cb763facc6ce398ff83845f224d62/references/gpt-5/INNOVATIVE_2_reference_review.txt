Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a new class of stationary multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernels constructed in the Fourier domain via Cramér’s theorem. The key idea is to parameterize a Hermitian positive semidefinite cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) by a low-rank factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), where each column function Ri(ω) is a complex Gaussian (square-exponential) with output-specific magnitude, mean, covariance, linear phase and delay. Enforcing real-valued processes is achieved by symmetrization in frequency, yielding closed-form time-domain kernels that include per-pair delays and phase shifts:
kij(τ) = ∑q α(q)ij exp(−1/2 (τ + θ(q)ij)⊤ Σ(q)ij (τ + θ(q)ij)) cos((τ + θ(q)ij)⊤ μ(q)ij + φ(q)ij).
This generalizes prior spectral MOGP constructions: SM-LMC (real, shared parameters across outputs) and CSM (adds cross-channel phase), by additionally allowing per-pair delays and decoupled auto-covariances across channels, while maintaining interpretability and positive definiteness. Experiments include (i) a synthetic tri-variate example (base function, derivative, delayed version), and (ii) two real datasets (climate sensors and Jura metals), comparing against CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A neat and principled extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting using Cramér’s theorem and a factorized complex cross-spectral density. The addition of learnable per-pair delays and phases is both elegant and practically meaningful, going beyond CSM/SM-LMC.
- Guarantees and interpretability: Positive semidefiniteness is ensured by construction. Parameters have clear spectral meaning (frequency content, bandwidth, magnitude, phase, delay) at the pairwise level.
- Closed-form kernels: The choice of complex SE factors yields closed-form inverse Fourier transforms, making the approach usable without numerical integration.
- Experiments: Synthetic study convincingly demonstrates MOSM’s ability to learn derivative and delayed relationships that are not linear combinations of univariate kernels—where latent-process models struggle. Real-data comparisons show competitive performance and some statistically significant wins.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Empirical depth: On real-world datasets, improvements over strong baselines (CSM, CONV) are modest, with significance established only in some cases. The synthetic example is strong but controlled; broader evaluations (e.g., more heterogeneous multi-sensor/time-series, varying output counts, irregular sampling) would better evidence the practical gains of delays/phases.
- Scalability: Training uses dense GP inference; no sparse or structured approximations are provided, nor runtime/memory comparisons. This limits applicability to larger N, m. Given the spectral framing, connections to scalable inducing Fourier features or inter-domain approaches (cited) would be natural and strengthen the work.
- Optimization/initialization: SM-style models are notoriously sensitive to initialization; the paper does not discuss strategies for initializing μ, Σ, phases, delays, or the number of components Q, nor ablations on Q. This could affect robustness.
- Minor technical clarity: αij is defined positive via wij ≥ 0, yet the text later suggests αij ∈ R to allow negative correlations; in practice the sign comes from the cosine with phase, not αij. Please clarify to avoid confusion. Also, briefly justify that symmetrization S(ω) ← (S(ω)+S(−ω))/2 preserves PSD for each ω (it does, as a sum of PSD matrices), to reassure readers.

Clarity
- Generally well organized with clear derivations and useful intuition. A few typos and phrasing issues (“multiouput”, “derivate”, “wrt”), and a dangling footnote marker, but nothing major.
- Consider a compact diagram contrasting SM-LMC, CSM, and MOSM parameterizations and what phenomena they can express (phase, delay, per-output autos, asymmetry).

Originality
- High. While CSM introduced complex cross-spectra for phase, MOSM’s factorized complex Gaussian design with per-output parameters and explicit delays is a meaningful generalization with a clean proof path to PSD and closed-form kernels. The spectral-domain construction for multi-output with delays is, to my knowledge, novel in the GP literature.

Significance
- The approach is likely to be adopted by researchers/practitioners who use MOGPs for multi-sensor and multi-channel time series where delays and phase offsets are common (e.g., geophysics, climate, neuroscience). The spectral interpretability is a strong plus. The current empirical gains are moderate but the conceptual advance is solid and opens avenues for scalable variants.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide initialization heuristics (e.g., periodogram-based μ, bandwidths from local spectral peaks, phase/delay from cross-spectral phase) and sensitivity analyses.
- Report training time, parameter counts, and convergence behavior vs baselines.
- Add ablations isolating the contributions of delay and phase (e.g., MOSM w/o delay, w/o phase).
- Explore a sparse/structured variant (variational Fourier features or inter-domain inducing variables) to demonstrate scalability.
- Visualize learned delays and phases on real datasets to show interpretability.

Pros
- Elegant, general, and interpretable construction; positive definiteness guaranteed.
- Captures phenomena (delays + phase) beyond prior spectral MOGP kernels.
- Closed-form expressions; strong synthetic validation.
- Clear relationship to and generalization of SM-LMC and CSM.

Cons
- Limited empirical advantage on real datasets; small-scale experiments.
- No scalability story or runtime analysis.
- Missing discussion on initialization and robustness.
- Minor notational inconsistencies.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning accept). Novel and bold kernel design with clear theory and practical promise, even if empirical gains are modest so far. The paper is likely to inspire follow-on work on scalable spectral MOGPs with delays/phases.

Confidence
High in assessment of novelty and theory; moderate in empirical significance due to limited experimental scope.
