

# NIPS Peer Review: "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) called the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The authors extend the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel [6] to multi-output settings by designing complex-valued cross-spectral densities and leveraging Cramér's theorem to ensure positive definiteness. The key innovation is the ability to model delays and phase differences across channels through a complex-valued spectral density, which generalizes the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5] and provides clear physical interpretations of all parameters. The authors demonstrate their approach on synthetic data where they successfully recover known derivatives and delayed signals, and on two real-world datasets (climate signals and heavy metal concentrations), showing competitive performance with state-of-the-art MOGP models.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on MOGPs, particularly the LMC framework [3], the Convolution Model [4], and the CSM kernel [5]. Unlike these approaches that rely on linear combinations of latent GPs, MOSM directly models the spectral properties of the cross-covariances, providing greater flexibility in capturing channel relationships.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous mathematical foundations. The authors properly derive the MOSM kernel from Cramér's theorem and provide clear theoretical justifications for their design choices. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with a synthetic example that demonstrates the model's ability to capture derivative and delayed relationships without prior knowledge, and two real-world applications where they compare against relevant baselines (LMC, CONV, CSM).

The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, noting that MOSM and CSM perform similarly on highly correlated climate data, and that the improvements on the Jura dataset for Cadmium are statistically significant while those for Copper are not. The authors also properly discuss the relationship between MOSM and CSM as a special case.

However, the paper could strengthen its theoretical claims by providing a formal proof of positive definiteness for the resulting kernel (beyond the factor decomposition argument in equation 7). Additionally, the computational complexity of the MOSM kernel compared to baselines is not discussed, which is an important consideration for practical applications.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear and well-motivated, with careful definitions of key concepts (e.g., Definitions 1-5). The figures (particularly Fig. 1) effectively illustrate the spectral interpretation of the parameters. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, including data preprocessing, training procedures, and evaluation metrics (MAE).

The paper could benefit from a brief comparison of the parameter counts between MOSM and baseline models, as this would help readers understand the relative complexity of the proposed approach. The connection between the spectral parameters and their physical interpretations (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay) is particularly well-explained.

## Originality

The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of MOGPs. The key novelty lies in the complex-valued cross-spectral density design that enables modeling of delays and phase differences, which is not possible with previous approaches like CSM or LMC. The authors explicitly identify how their work differs from previous contributions, noting that CSM is a special case of MOSM with restricted parameters.

The approach represents a meaningful extension of the SM kernel [6] to the multi-output setting, moving beyond the linear combination of latent GPs used in most existing MOGP frameworks. While some related work has used complex-valued kernels for GPs [14, 15], this is the first to apply this concept to MOGPs with a focus on interpretable spectral parameters.

The paper's originality is well-supported by the thorough comparison to related work in Section 3.3, which clearly establishes the relationship between MOSM and previous approaches.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important challenge in MOGPs: designing expressive cross-covariances while maintaining positive definiteness. The proposed MOSM kernel provides a more flexible modeling approach that can capture complex channel relationships (delays, phase shifts) that are not possible with existing methods. The experimental results demonstrate that MOSM can outperform baselines on specific datasets (Jura dataset for Cadmium), with statistically significant improvements.

The clear spectral interpretation of parameters is particularly valuable for practitioners, as it allows for intuitive understanding of the channel relationships. The ability to model delays and phase differences has important implications for applications like signal processing, sensor networks, and time series analysis where temporal relationships between channels are critical.

However, the paper could better emphasize the broader significance of the work. While the experiments show competitive performance, it would be helpful to discuss how the approach might be applied in other domains beyond the specific examples presented.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Clear theoretical foundation using Cramér's theorem
2. Novel complex-valued cross-spectral density design enabling delay and phase modeling
3. Comprehensive experimental validation across synthetic and real-world data
4. Excellent clarity in explaining the spectral interpretation of parameters
5. Proper comparison to relevant baselines and clear relationship to prior work

**Weaknesses:**
1. Limited discussion of computational complexity compared to baselines
2. No formal proof of positive definiteness (beyond the factor decomposition argument)
3. Synthetic example is relatively simple (3 outputs only)
4. Marginal improvements on some real-world datasets without strong justification
5. Limited discussion of when MOSM would be preferred over simpler alternatives

## Recommendation

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes by extending the spectral mixture framework to model delays and phase differences across channels. The theoretical foundation is sound, the experimental results are comprehensive, and the approach provides clear interpretability of model parameters. While the improvements over existing methods on some datasets are modest, the ability to model delays and phase differences represents a meaningful advance in the expressive power of MOGP kernels.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the computational complexity discussion and provide a more thorough comparison of parameter counts between models to help readers assess the trade-offs between model complexity and performance.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Novel extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings with complex-valued cross-spectral densities
- Clear theoretical foundation and physical interpretation of parameters
- Demonstrated ability to model delays and phase differences, which previous methods cannot
- Competitive performance on real-world datasets, with statistically significant improvements on one dataset
- Well-written, clear, and properly contextualized within related work

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Improvements over CSM on real-world datasets are marginal in some cases
- Limited discussion of computational considerations
- Synthetic example is relatively simple (only 3 outputs)
- No formal proof of positive definiteness of the resulting kernel

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field that advances the state-of-the-art in MOGP kernel design. The proposed MOSM kernel provides a more expressive framework for modeling complex channel relationships that will be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with multi-output Gaussian processes.
