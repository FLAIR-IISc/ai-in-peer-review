Summary
The paper proposes a new multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernel, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. It extends the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel by constructing complex-valued cross-spectral densities via Cramér’s theorem. The key design is to factor the cross-spectrum as S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), with per-output complex SE functions Ri(ω) whose parameters (mean, covariance, magnitude, phase, and delay) induce pairwise cross-spectral Gaussians with analytically tractable inverse Fourier transforms. After symmetrization, the resulting cross-covariance in the time/space domain has an SE envelope and cosine term, with per-pair phase and delay shifts. The authors argue MOSM generalizes SM-LMC and CSM, enabling both phase and delay across channels with interpretable parameters. Experiments include a tailored synthetic task (a process, its derivative, and a delayed version), a small climate dataset with missing data, and the Jura geostatistics dataset.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean spectral construction: the use of Cramér’s theorem and a spectral factorization guarantees positive semidefiniteness while yielding closed-form kernels.
- Interpretability: per-pair phase and delay are explicit; auto-covariances reduce to standard SM components.
- Relationship to prior work is well positioned: SM-LMC and CSM emerge as special/restricted cases.
- Synthetic example convincingly shows MOSM can represent delayed and derivative relationships that LMC-style latent constructions struggle with.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation: on real datasets, gains are small or inconsistent. Climate results show no statistically significant advantage except for one sensor; in Jura, MOSM beats baselines for Cadmium but loses to SM-LMC for Copper. This does not establish clear superiority over established baselines.
- Missing ablations: no analysis of the contribution of delay vs. phase vs. channel-specific spectral parameters; no comparison to the same model with θ=0 and/or φ=0; no sensitivity to the number of components Q or to initialization (critical for SM-type models).
- Baselines: comparisons omit widely used multivariate kernels such as multivariate Matérn (Gneiting et al., 2010) or full-rank coregionalization with SM components; CONV results for Jura are borrowed from prior work and cannot be statistically tested.
- Scalability and identifiability: the parameter count scales with m×Q and input dimension; no discussion of identifiability (multiple parameter sets can induce similar cross-spectra), regularization, or priors. Training details (constraints on wi, φi; how Σ is parameterized; initialization) are sparse. No sparse/inducing or structure-exploiting inference is used; experiments remain small.
- Interpretation in spatial settings: the “delay” vector becomes a spatial shift, which is not always physically meaningful when outputs are co-located variables; the paper does not discuss whether θij tends to zero on Jura, or how to constrain it if such shifts are undesirable.

Quality
The theoretical construction is sound and standard within spectral GP methodology. The positive semidefiniteness argument via S = RHR is correct; the symmetrization to ensure real-valuedness is reasonable (a brief proof that convex combinations S(ω) and S(−ω) preserve PSD would improve rigor). The closed-form time-domain expressions look consistent with Gaussian-product identities. The empirical section is less compelling: datasets are small; improvements are modest; and important training details and ablations are missing. Claims about interpretability are not validated on real data.

Clarity
Overall clearly written and well organized. The mapping from per-output Ri parameters to pairwise (µij, Σij, θij, φij, wij) is useful; however, a derivation or appendix justification would help, especially for Σij. Some typos and minor notation issues exist. Implementation details (hyperparameter constraints, initialization strategy) should be clarified to aid reproducibility.

Originality
The paper’s main novelty is a principled, complex-valued spectral factorization for multi-output SM kernels that allows per-pair delay and phase while maintaining closed forms. The idea builds on well-known spectral GP theory (Bochner/Cramér, SM kernels) and on prior multi-output kernels (LMC, CSM). The specific parametrization and generalization beyond CSM appear novel.

Significance
Potentially useful for domains where phase and delay across channels matter. However, the current empirical evidence does not convincingly demonstrate consistent advantages over established baselines on realistic tasks, and practical issues (scalability, initialization, identifiability) are not addressed. As such, near-term impact is uncertain.

Questions/requests for authors
1) How are wi, µi, Σi, θi, φi initialized? How sensitive are results to initialization and Q?
2) Please provide ablations: MOSM without θ, without φ, and with shared µ/Σ across channels (recovering CSM/SM-LMC) to quantify each design choice.
3) For Jura, what values do θij learn? Are they close to zero (as one might expect for co-located outputs)? If not, how should one interpret them?
4) Why restrict to rank-1 per component? Did you try higher-rank per component vs. more components?
5) Can you add comparisons to multivariate Matérn and full-rank coregionalization baselines?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, principled extension of SM to multi-output with interpretable delay and phase.
- Clean theory with closed-form kernels; generalizes existing methods.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical gains over strong, established baselines are limited and inconsistent.
- Missing ablations and training details; no scalability story.
- Applicability/interpretability of “delay” in spatial settings not discussed.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical construction is sound and interesting, but the experimental evidence and analysis are not yet sufficient to justify acceptance over established methods. Strengthening the empirical evaluation (broader datasets, ablations, stronger baselines, and a scalability plan) would significantly improve the paper.
