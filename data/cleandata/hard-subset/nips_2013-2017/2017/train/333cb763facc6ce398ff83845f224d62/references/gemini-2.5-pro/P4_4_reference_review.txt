### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel and flexible covariance function for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The key challenge in MOGP modeling is designing a matrix-valued kernel that is both expressive in modeling cross-channel dependencies and guaranteed to be positive definite. The authors tackle this by working in the spectral domain. Building on Cramér's theorem—the multivariate extension of Bochner's theorem—they propose designing the matrix of cross-spectral densities directly, rather than the covariance functions. To ensure the positive definiteness of this spectral density matrix, S(ω), they propose a low-rank factor decomposition S(ω) = R(ω)ᴴR(ω). They model the components of the factor R(ω) as complex-valued squared exponential functions.

By taking the inverse Fourier transform of the resulting cross-spectral densities, they derive a closed-form expression for the MOSM kernel. The resulting kernel is a sum of components, each of which models relationships between any pair of output channels with interpretable parameters corresponding to magnitude, delay, and phase. This construction is a principled generalization of the scalar Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel [6] to the multi-output case. It is more general than previous approaches like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5] (NIPS 2015), which is shown to be a special case of MOSM. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on a synthetic task involving derivatives and delayed signals, and show competitive or superior performance against established MOGP models on two real-world datasets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality: Is the paper technically sound? Are claims well-supported?**

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method rests on a solid theoretical foundation, namely Cramér's theorem for stationary multivariate processes. The use of a factor decomposition `S(ω) = R(ω)ᴴR(ω)` is a standard and correct method for guaranteeing that the spectral density matrix is Hermitian positive semi-definite, which in turn guarantees a valid covariance function. The mathematical derivations from the parametric form of `R(ω)` to the final kernel `k(τ)` are clear and appear correct.

The claims are well-supported by a strong set of experiments. The synthetic experiment is particularly insightful. By constructing a problem with a function, its derivative, and a delayed version, the authors create a scenario where the true cross-covariances are known to be non-symmetric and exhibit delays. The results convincingly show that MOSM can successfully identify these complex relationships where models based on the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) or Convolution frameworks fail. The real-world experiments on climate and geological data demonstrate that the model performs competitively with or better than state-of-the-art methods, with statistical tests used to support claims of significance where applicable.

**Clarity: Is the paper clearly written and well-organized?**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The authors provide a concise but sufficient background on scalar SM kernels and MOGPs before introducing their method. The core contribution is developed logically, stepping from Cramér's theorem to the factor decomposition, the specific parametric form, and the final kernel expression. The relationship to prior work is explicitly and clearly stated, correctly identifying the CSM kernel [5] as a constrained version of the proposed MOSM kernel. The figures are illustrative, especially Figure 1 which helps build intuition for the delay and phase parameters, and Figure 2 which powerfully visualizes the model's success on the synthetic task.

A minor point for improvement would be a brief discussion on the practicalities of model fitting. The MOSM kernel introduces a significant number of hyperparameters. Given that optimization of the scalar SM kernel is known to be challenging due to local minima, a comment on initialization strategies or the optimization landscape for MOSM would be beneficial for practitioners seeking to apply this model.

**Originality: Are the problems or approaches new?**

The approach is highly original. While using Cramér's theorem to construct MOGP kernels has been explored before (e.g., Gneiting et al. [17]), this work's main novelty lies in the specific parametric form proposed for the spectral densities. By modeling the factors `R(ω)` as *complex-valued* functions, the authors provide a principled mechanism to generate cross-covariance functions that naturally incorporate phase and delay parameters. This is a significant conceptual advance over the more ad-hoc construction in the CSM kernel [5], which added a phase term to an LMC-based model. The MOSM framework provides a more fundamental and general recipe for designing expressive stationary MOGP kernels from the spectral domain.

**Significance: Are the results important? Are others likely to use or build on these ideas?**

The work is highly significant. It provides the field with a new, powerful, and interpretable family of kernels for MOGPs. The ability to flexibly model channel-specific spectral properties, as well as delays and phase shifts between channels, is critical for many applications in signal processing, geostatistics, finance, and climate science. The presented model demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art in its ability to capture these complex dependencies.

Given its clear theoretical grounding and strong empirical performance, researchers and practitioners are likely to use this model for challenging multivariate time-series and spatial modeling tasks. Furthermore, the paper's framework for designing kernels via spectral density factorization may inspire further research into developing other novel families of MOGP kernels. The availability of an implementation in a popular framework like GPflow also enhances its potential impact by lowering the barrier to adoption.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** The method is derived from first principles of multivariate spectral theory, resulting in a theoretically sound and elegant model.
2.  **High Expressivity and Interpretability:** The MOSM kernel is highly flexible, capable of modeling complex relationships like delays and phase shifts that are crucial in many real-world applications. Its parameters have a clear spectral interpretation.
3.  **Demonstrably Superior to Prior Art:** The synthetic experiment provides clear, unambiguous evidence that MOSM can model dependencies that previous state-of-the-art MOGP kernels cannot.
4.  **Clear and Well-Written Paper:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, making a sophisticated topic accessible and its contribution easy to appreciate.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Optimization Not Discussed:** The paper does not address the potential challenges in optimizing the large number of hyperparameters, a known difficulty for related spectral mixture models.
2.  **Scalability:** The proposed method inherits the standard `O(N³)` complexity of GPs. While this is not a flaw of the kernel itself, it limits immediate applicability to large datasets without sparse approximations (which are mentioned as future work).

The arguments in favor of acceptance substantially outweigh the minor points against it. This paper presents a significant and well-executed contribution to the literature on Gaussian processes.
