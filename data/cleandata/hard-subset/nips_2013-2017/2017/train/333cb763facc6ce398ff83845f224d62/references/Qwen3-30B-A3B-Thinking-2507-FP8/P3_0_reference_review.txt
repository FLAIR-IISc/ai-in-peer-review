

# Peer Review: Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel approach to the multi-output setting using Cramér's theorem. The authors construct a complex-valued cross-spectral density matrix that guarantees positive definiteness, then derive the corresponding covariance function with interpretable parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay). This generalizes the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel by allowing delays and independent channel parameters, whereas CSM constrains all channels to share the same frequency parameters.

The paper builds on the SM kernel [6], CSM [5], and the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) [3]. It correctly identifies CSM as a special case of MOSM (with identical frequency parameters across channels), but the novelty lies in the complex-valued spectral density enabling explicit modeling of delays and phase differences.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The derivation using Cramér's theorem is mathematically rigorous and correctly guarantees positive definiteness through the rank-1 factorization of the spectral density matrix. The connection between spectral parameters and physical interpretations (frequency, delay, phase) is well-motivated.

2. **Clear Experimental Validation**: The synthetic example (learning derivatives and delayed signals) effectively demonstrates MOSM's ability to capture relationships that linear latent approaches (LMC, CONV) cannot model, as evidenced by successful extrapolation where other models fail.

3. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, theorems, and derivations. The figures (particularly Fig. 1 and Fig. 2) effectively illustrate the model's capabilities.

4. **Appropriate Baselines**: The experiments compare against relevant MOGP baselines (SM-LMC, CONV, CSM) on two real-world datasets, with appropriate statistical validation (Kolmogorov-Smirnov test).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Differentiation from CSM**: While the paper correctly identifies CSM as a special case, the experimental results don't sufficiently demonstrate MOSM's advantages over CSM in practical scenarios. The climate dataset shows high correlation between sensors, making it difficult to distinguish the benefits of the more flexible model. The heavy metal dataset shows MOSM's superiority for Cadmium, but the statistical significance claim is questionable since the CONV results are taken from prior work.

2. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each key component (delay, phase, independent channel parameters). Without this, it's unclear whether the observed improvements are due to the delay parameter specifically or the general increased flexibility.

3. **Scalability Concerns**: The computational complexity of MOSM isn't discussed, which is critical for real-world applications. The paper mentions sparse implementations as future work but doesn't address how MOSM compares to existing sparse MOGP methods in terms of computational efficiency.

4. **Insufficient Real-World Validation**: The two real-world datasets are relatively simple (climate signals with high correlation, heavy metals with some non-Gaussianity). The paper misses an opportunity to demonstrate MOSM's advantages in more challenging scenarios where delays and phase differences are critical (e.g., multi-sensor time series with known time lags).

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound extension of the spectral mixture approach to multi-output GPs with clear theoretical foundations. The model's ability to explicitly model delays and phase differences is a meaningful generalization over CSM. The synthetic example convincingly demonstrates this capability, but the real-world experiments don't fully leverage the model's potential.

The primary weakness is the insufficient demonstration of MOSM's advantages over CSM in practical settings. The climate data results show only marginal improvements, and the heavy metal results for Copper don't show statistical significance. Without a compelling real-world case where delays/phase differences are critical, the practical significance remains somewhat limited.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a valid theoretical contribution to MOGP modeling, and the synthetic validation is strong. However, the real-world results don't sufficiently demonstrate the model's advantages over existing methods, and the paper would benefit from additional experiments showcasing the unique capabilities of MOSM in scenarios where delays and phase differences are important. The authors should also provide a clearer comparison with CSM and address scalability concerns in the revision.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the significance claim is slightly overstated given the modest performance improvements on the real-world datasets. With additional experiments demonstrating the model's unique advantages in practical scenarios, this could be a strong contribution to the field.

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Rigorous theoretical extension of SM to MOGPs using Cramér's theorem
- Clear interpretation of spectral parameters (frequency, delay, phase)
- Strong synthetic validation showing ability to model derivatives and delays
- Well-structured presentation with clear figures

*Con:*
- Limited practical demonstration of advantages over CSM in real-world scenarios
- No ablation study to isolate the contribution of key components
- Insufficient discussion of computational complexity and scalability
- Real-world datasets are relatively simple (highly correlated signals)

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the MOGP literature, but would be significantly strengthened with more compelling real-world validation of its unique capabilities.
