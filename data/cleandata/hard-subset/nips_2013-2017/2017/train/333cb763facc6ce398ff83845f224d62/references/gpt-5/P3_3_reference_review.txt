Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Building on Cramér’s theorem, the authors model the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) via a low-rank Hermitian factorization S(ω) = ∑q Rq(ω)H Rq(ω), where each column function Ri(ω) is a complex Gaussian (square-exponential) with per-output parameters (amplitude wi, mean μi, covariance Σi, delay θi, phase φi). Enforcing symmetry S(ω) = S(−ω) yields real-valued GPs. The inverse Fourier transform leads to closed-form cross-covariances:
kij(τ) = ∑q α(q)ij exp(−0.5(τ+θ(q)ij)T Σ(q)ij (τ+θ(q)ij)) cos((τ+θ(q)ij)T μ(q)ij + φ(q)ij),
with interpretable per-pair parameters derived from the per-output ones. Compared to LMC/CONV and CSM, MOSM allows (i) different spectral means/covariances per output, (ii) explicit time delays and phase offsets across outputs, and (iii) both positive and negative cross-correlations, while guaranteeing positive semidefiniteness via the spectral factorization. Experiments on a synthetic tri-variate signal (reference, derivative, delayed) and two real datasets (UK climate sensors; Jura heavy metals) show competitive or improved MAE versus CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM.

Quality
- Theory: The construction is principled. Using Cramér’s theorem and S(ω)=∑q RqH Rq ensures S(ω) is PSD for all ω, and real-valuedness is enforced by symmetry S(ω)=S(−ω). The closed-form inverse Fourier pairs are correctly derived for Gaussian spectra with linear phase. The interpretation of parameters (frequency content μ, bandwidth Σ, delay θ, phase φ) is clear and valuable.
- A potential ambiguity: Definition 5 presents kij with pairwise parameters αij, μij, Σij, θij, φij per component. Positivity is guaranteed only if these are induced from a per-output factorization Rq (as in Section 3.1). This is implied by the training section (Θ comprises per-output parameters), but the definition could be misread as allowing free pairwise parameters, which would not guarantee PSD. This should be clarified explicitly.
- Realness via symmetrization: Averaging S(ω) and S(−ω) preserves PSD if each is constructed via a factorization; the text alludes to this by “choosing Ri to be a vector of two mirrored SEs.” A brief proof or construction would strengthen the exposition.
- Experiments: The synthetic task convincingly demonstrates the ability to represent antisymmetric cross-covariances (function–derivative) and delays—cases where standard LMC without operators struggles. Real-data results show MOSM is competitive; significance testing is appreciated, though the climate task is too easy (highly correlated sensors), leading to small differences. Evaluation is limited to MAE; predictive log-likelihood and calibration would better assess GP benefits.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The spectral construction and parameter mapping (from per-output to per-pair) are explained clearly.
- Minor notational issues: some places denote S(ω) ∈ Rm×m while discussing Hermitian matrices; strictly, S(ω) ∈ Cm×m. Also, a footnote marker “2” appears without the footnote. Several typos (e.g., “multiouput”, “wrt”, missing articles) should be corrected.
- Please make explicit in Definition 5 and throughout Section 3.2 that the trainable parameters are per-output (wi(q), μi(q), Σi(q), θi(q), φi(q)), and all per-pair parameters are deterministic functions thereof; this is crucial for PSD.

Originality
- The idea to parameterize multi-output cross-spectra with complex Gaussian factors and to carry through the inverse Fourier transform to get closed-form kernels with delay and phase is novel and elegant. While CSM introduced phases within an LMC/SM construction, MOSM broadens flexibility by allowing distinct μi, Σi, θi per output, leading to richer cross-covariances and explicit delays. The use of spectral factorization for PSD control is conceptually clean.

Significance
- The approach provides a practical, interpretable family of MOGP kernels that can capture realistic inter-channel relationships (phase lags, delays, different bandwidths/frequencies) with guaranteed PSD. This is likely to be adopted by practitioners dealing with multi-sensor time series and spatial-temporal tasks where lags matter.
- Current experimental evidence is promising but not definitive. Stronger empirical validation (harder datasets, more baselines, uncertainty metrics) and an analysis of learned delays/phases versus ground truth would strengthen impact.

Strengths
- Principled construction with PSD guarantee via spectral factorization.
- Expressive yet interpretable parameterization (frequency, bandwidth, delay, phase) per pair of channels.
- Closed-form time-domain kernels enabling exact GP inference (modulo cubic cost).
- Clear relation to and generalization of SM-LMC and CSM; includes them as special cases.
- Synthetic example demonstrating capabilities (derivative and delay) that are difficult for latent linear mixing.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Ambiguity in Definition 5 about whether per-pair parameters are free; must be constrained via per-output factorization to ensure PSD.
- Identifiability and optimization: θ and φ can trade off along μ; optimization may be ill-conditioned. No discussion of initialization, regularization, or constraints.
- Evaluation metrics limited to MAE; lacks NLPP/RMSE and uncertainty calibration.
- Baseline fairness: LMC can incorporate linear differential operators; stating that latent-process models “fail” may be contingent on the chosen operator family. Please discuss/clarify.
- Scalability: No sparse or structured implementation; complexity O((mN)3). Practical guidance for larger m, N is missing.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify Definition 5: are α(q)ij, μ(q)ij, Σ(q)ij, θ(q)ij, φ(q)ij derived deterministically from per-output parameters via the rank-1 construction, for each q? If so, state the mapping explicitly and emphasize that per-pair parameters are not free.
2. How do you initialize μi(q), Σi(q), θi(q), φi(q)? Did you use per-channel periodograms or cross-periodograms? How sensitive are results to initialization and Q?
3. Can you report NLPP or log-marginal likelihood on the test sets, and calibration metrics?
4. In the synthetic experiment, can you report the recovered delays and phases, and compare to ground truth?
5. Does the symmetrization S(ω) → 0.5(S(ω)+S(−ω)) always preserve PSD in your construction? A short argument would be helpful.
6. Can LMC with known differential operators (or process convolutions with derivative kernels) recover the derivative cross-covariance? If tested, please report.

Minor comments/typos
- “multiouput” → “multioutput”; “wrt” → “w.r.t.”; several small grammatical fixes needed.
- Be consistent about complex vs real matrix notation for S(ω).
- Provide the missing footnote referenced by “choose2 Q=1.”
- Fig. 1/2 captions should explicitly state parameter settings to improve reproducibility.

Reproducibility
- Using GPflow is a plus. Please release code and scripts, including initialization procedures and seeds, and document the exact hyperparameter priors/constraints.

Overall assessment
A technically sound and elegant contribution that generalizes spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings with interpretable parameters and a solid PSD guarantee. The theoretical construction is compelling; empirical validation is good but could be more comprehensive. With clarifications about the parameterization/PSD guarantee and stronger experiments/metrics, this work would make a valuable addition to NeurIPS. I lean towards acceptance.
