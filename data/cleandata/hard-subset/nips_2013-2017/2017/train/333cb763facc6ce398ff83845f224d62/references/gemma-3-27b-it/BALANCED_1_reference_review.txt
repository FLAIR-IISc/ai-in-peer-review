This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel approach to defining covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The authors extend the spectral mixture concept from scalar GPs to the multivariate case, leveraging Cramér’s theorem to construct a kernel with interpretable spectral parameters, including delay and phase shifts between channels. The key idea is to work in the Fourier domain, defining cross-spectral densities and then inverting to obtain the covariance function, ensuring positive definiteness. The paper includes a synthetic experiment demonstrating the model’s ability to recover known relationships and comparisons to existing MOGP methods on two real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The MOSM kernel presents a novel extension of the spectral mixture approach to the multi-output setting. The use of complex-valued cross-spectral densities to model delays and phase shifts is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is grounded in solid mathematical theory, properly invoking Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems to justify the construction of the kernel.
* **Interpretability:** The spectral parameterization of the kernel offers increased interpretability compared to purely data-driven approaches or latent variable models.  The connection between parameters and frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay is clearly articulated.
* **Completeness:** The paper provides a comprehensive derivation of the MOSM kernel, including the training procedure and predictive posterior.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of both a synthetic experiment to verify the model’s capabilities and comparisons on real-world datasets demonstrates the practical relevance of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Improvement in Real-World Experiments:** While the MOSM kernel performs competitively on the real-world datasets, the improvements over existing methods (especially CSM) are not consistently significant. The authors acknowledge this and attribute it to the high correlation between signals in the climate dataset and the non-Gaussianity of the metal concentration data.  More substantial demonstrations of improvement in challenging scenarios would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Complexity:** The authors do not explicitly discuss the computational complexity of the MOSM kernel, particularly in comparison to simpler MOGP models. The inversion of the covariance matrix during inference could be a bottleneck for large datasets.
* **Rank-One Assumption:** The initial formulation focuses on a rank-one decomposition of the spectral density matrix (Q=1). While the authors mention the possibility of extending to higher ranks, the implications and trade-offs of increasing Q are not fully explored.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the relationship between the MOSM kernel and other approaches for modeling phase shifts and delays in time series data, beyond the existing references to CSM.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of GP models is known to be sensitive to hyperparameter settings. The paper doesn't provide much detail on the hyperparameter optimization process or sensitivity analysis.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP kernel design.
* The spectral parameterization of the MOSM kernel offers increased interpretability.
* The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly conclusive, demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and logically organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The improvements over existing methods on real-world datasets are not consistently significant.
* The computational complexity and scalability of the MOSM kernel are not thoroughly addressed.
* The assumption of a rank-one spectral density matrix may limit the model's expressiveness.


**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Gaussian process modeling, particularly for multi-output scenarios. The MOSM kernel offers a principled and interpretable way to model complex dependencies between channels, including delays and phase shifts. While the experimental results do not demonstrate overwhelming improvements over existing methods, the theoretical novelty and potential for future development warrant consideration. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by:

*   Conducting more comprehensive experiments on datasets where the MOSM kernel is expected to offer a significant advantage (e.g., datasets with strong phase differences or non-stationary behavior).
*   Analyzing the computational complexity of the MOSM kernel and exploring potential optimizations.
*   Investigating the impact of increasing the rank (Q) of the spectral density matrix.
*   Providing a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter optimization process.
*   Comparing the performance against other relevant approaches for modeling phase shifts and delays.
