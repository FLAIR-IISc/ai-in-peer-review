The paper proposes a novel multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernel, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the spectral mixture concept to multiple outputs. The core idea is to define cross-spectral densities in the Fourier domain, leveraging Cramér’s theorem, and then transform them to the covariance function using the inverse Fourier transform. This approach allows for flexible modeling of dependencies between outputs, including delays and phase differences, while ensuring positive definiteness. The paper relates to existing work on MOGPs like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC), Convolution models, Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, and spectral mixture kernels. It aims to address limitations of previous approaches by offering more interpretability and flexibility in modeling cross-covariances.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The MOSM kernel presents a novel extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting. The use of complex-valued spectral densities to model phase differences and delays is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation is grounded in Cramér’s theorem, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed kernel. The explanation of the relationship between spectral densities and covariance functions is clear and well-supported.
*   **Interpretability:** The parameters of the MOSM kernel have a clear spectral interpretation (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay), enhancing the understanding of the learned dependencies.
*   **Generality:** The MOSM kernel generalizes existing MOGP kernels like CSM, offering greater flexibility.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The paper includes experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the performance of the MOSM kernel in comparison to existing methods. The synthetic example highlights the ability of the kernel to learn derivatives and delays.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and the explanations are clear.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While not explicitly discussed, extending the SM kernel to the multi-output setting could potentially lead to increased computational costs, particularly for large datasets and high-dimensional outputs. This should be addressed with a discussion of scalability.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The proposed kernel introduces additional hyperparameters (delay, phase) that require careful tuning. The paper does not provide detailed guidance on how to optimize these parameters effectively.
*   **Limited Statistical Significance:** The statistical significance of the results on the climate data is not fully established for all sensor failure cases. While improvements are observed, stronger statistical evidence would strengthen the claims.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** While related work is mentioned, the connection to other spectral methods in multivariate statistics beyond GPs could be expanded.
*   **Redundancy in Presentation:** There’s some repetition of background material (e.g., Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems) which slightly detracts from the flow.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The MOSM kernel is a genuinely novel contribution to the field of MOGPs, offering a flexible and interpretable approach to modeling cross-covariances.
*   The theoretical foundation is strong and the derivation is well-justified.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the kernel on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   The paper addresses an important challenge in MOGP design—the trade-off between expressiveness and positive definiteness.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the MOSM kernel is not discussed.
*   The statistical significance of the results is not consistently demonstrated across all experiments.
*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work in multivariate spectral analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of Gaussian process modeling. The proposed MOSM kernel offers a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP design, with potential for a wide range of applications. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding computational complexity, hyperparameter tuning guidance, and statistical significance. Specifically, a brief discussion of scalability, and a more rigorous statistical analysis of the real-world experiment results, would strengthen the paper considerably.
