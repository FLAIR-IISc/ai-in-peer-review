Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to construct a matrix-valued cross-spectral density S(ω) via a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), where each column Ri(ω) is a complex Gaussian (square-exponential) function with output-specific parameters controlling magnitude, mean frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay. By Cramér’s theorem, this guarantees a valid multivariate stationary covariance after inverse Fourier transform. To ensure real-valued processes, S(ω) is symmetrized, yielding closed-form cross-covariances: a Gaussian envelope centered at a relative delay θij, modulating a cosine with frequency μij and phase φij, and with output-pair-specific bandwidth Σij and amplitude αij. The model naturally extends to multiple components (rank Q) by summing independent factors.

This approach generalizes prior spectral methods: the scalar spectral mixture (SM) kernel (Wilson & Adams, 2013) and its multi-output versions under the Linear Model of Coregionalization (SM-LMC), and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015). Compared to SM-LMC, MOSM allows distinct auto-spectral parameters per output and explicit cross-output delays and phase shifts. Compared to CSM, MOSM derives these effects systematically via complex cross-spectra and includes delays in addition to phase, while recovering SM-LMC/CSM as special cases.

Experiments include: (i) a tri-variate synthetic example (a function, its numerical derivative, and a delayed version), where MOSM learns the correct auto- and cross-covariances and outperforms baselines; (ii) a climate sensor imputation task with half-sensor failures, where MOSM is competitive with CSM and better on one station; and (iii) heavy metal concentration prediction (Jura), where MOSM outperforms baselines on Cadmium and is competitive on Copper.

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The construction via Cramér’s theorem and per-frequency PSD positive semidefiniteness through S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) is sound. Symmetrization of S(ω) to ensure real-valued processes is appropriate. The resulting Fourier pairs are plausible and consistent with products-of-Gaussians identities. The inclusion of delay and phase in cross-covariances is well-justified.
- Some derivational details are omitted, and there are notational inconsistencies (e.g., missing 1/2 factors in Gaussian exponents in eqs. (8–9), stray footnote markers, and occasionally unclear normalization). Providing a full derivation in the supplement would enhance reproducibility.
- Training is by exact GP marginal likelihood. However, computational complexity, parameter counts, initialization strategies, and identifiability considerations (e.g., interplay between θ and φ, and per-output parameters across Q) are not discussed. Given the expanded parameterization, overfitting risks and optimization sensitivity should be addressed.
- Statistical testing: Using the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test on MAE distributions over 10 runs is unconventional; a paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test would better match the comparison of paired errors.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, with a clear narrative from spectral design to closed-form kernels and experiments. The spectral interpretation of parameters (frequency, bandwidth, delay, phase) is helpful.
- Notational issues and typographical errors can impede precise understanding (e.g., inconsistent Gaussian exponents, minor grammar/formatting artifacts). A careful pass to fix these would improve clarity.
- Details on hyperparameter initialization, constraints (e.g., positivity of bandwidths), and practical tips (e.g., reparameterizations to ensure identifiability) are limited.

Originality
- The core contribution—factorizing cross-spectral densities with complex Gaussian Ri(ω) to yield multi-output kernels with output-pair-specific frequencies, bandwidths, phases, and explicit delays—is a substantive and principled extension of SM/CSM/LMC. The inclusion of delay via θij and per-output spectral parameters is novel relative to CSM (which focused on phase).
- The connection to prior MOGP constructions (LMC/CONV) is well-drawn, and the special-cases relationships are clarified.

Significance
- The ability to model cross-output phase and delay in a coherent, positive-definite, and interpretable framework is valuable for applications with asynchronous, shifted, or oscillatory multi-channel signals (e.g., sensing, neuroscience, geostatistics).
- Empirical gains are clear on the synthetic task designed to stress delays/derivatives and on Cadmium in Jura; on climate and Copper the improvements are modest or parity with CSM. Overall, the method appears competitive and sometimes superior, with strong potential impact given its interpretability and generality.
- Practical scalability is not addressed; exact inference with large N and m will be costly. A discussion or demonstration of sparse/inducing approaches would strengthen significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, general construction of multi-output spectral kernels via Cramér’s theorem and complex cross-spectra.
- Clear parameter interpretability (frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay) and inclusion of delays beyond prior CSM.
- Recovers SM-LMC/CSM as special cases; more expressive cross-covariances.
- Competitive to superior performance on synthetic and one real dataset; appropriate baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Notational inconsistencies and missing derivation details hinder reproducibility.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity, scalability, and optimization/initialization.
- Risk of overparameterization not empirically analyzed; no ablation on the role of delays/phases or Q.
- Statistical testing methodology could be improved.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full derivations of eqs. (8–11), including consistent Gaussian constants and exponents; clarify constraints and reparameterizations.
- Discuss parameter counts, identifiability, and initialization; include ablations (e.g., removing θ or φ) to quantify their contributions.
- Report training time and complexity; outline or demonstrate sparse MOSM with inducing variables.
- Use appropriate paired statistical tests for comparative evaluations; include variance across runs and seeds.

Overall, this work makes a technically sound and interpretable extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting, with meaningful novelty and promising empirical results. Addressing clarity and practical concerns would further strengthen the contribution.
