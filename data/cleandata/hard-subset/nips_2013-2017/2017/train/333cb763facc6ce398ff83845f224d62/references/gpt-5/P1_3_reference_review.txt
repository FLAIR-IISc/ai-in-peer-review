Summary
The paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Leveraging Cramér’s theorem—the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem—the authors design a complex-valued cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) via a low-rank factorization S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω), with per-output components Ri(ω) modeled as complex Gaussian bumps with linear phase. After enforcing evenness in ω to yield real-valued processes, the inverse Fourier transform produces closed-form, stationary cross-covariances that are mixtures of cosine-modulated exponentials with: (i) output-pair-specific amplitudes, (ii) means and covariances, (iii) phase shifts, and (iv) explicit delays. The construction guarantees positive definiteness via the spectral factorization. The authors argue that MOSM strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM, while offering interpretable spectral parameters (frequency, bandwidth, magnitude, phase, delay) per output pair.

Empirically, MOSM is evaluated on: (1) a 3-output synthetic example (reference signal, its derivative, and a delayed channel), where MOSM best recovers known auto- and cross-covariances and extrapolates both derivative and delay structure; (2) a climate sensor missing-data task across four stations, where MOSM is comparable to or slightly better than SM-LMC/CSM/CONV; (3) the Jura geostatistical dataset for heavy metal concentrations, where MOSM performs best for Cadmium (statistically significant) and competitive for Copper. Implementations use GPflow/TensorFlow with MAE as metric.

Relation to prior work: SM kernels for single-output GPs (Wilson & Adams), LMC and process convolutions for MOGPs (Álvarez & Lawrence), and CSM (Ulrich et al.) are discussed. MOSM can be seen as a spectral-domain generalization that subsumes CSM/SM-LMC by allowing per-output spectral parameters and explicit delays.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The spectral construction is principled; positive definiteness of S(ω) is ensured by factorization. The real-valuedness condition via symmetrization Sij(ω) → 0.5[Sij(ω)+Sij(−ω)] is reasonable; however, a short proof or lemma confirming that the full matrix S(ω) remains Hermitian PSD for all ω after symmetrization would strengthen the theory (especially for complex phases and pairwise parameters). The closed-form inverse FT is well motivated.
- Claims vs. evidence: The synthetic study compellingly demonstrates learning of derivative and delay structure. Real-data results are competitive but not uniformly superior; statistical testing is used in some cases. Scalability, sensitivity to initialization, and robustness are not assessed.
- Completeness: The method, training objective (NLL), and predictive inference are standard. Important practical aspects (initialization of spectral parameters, choice of Q, computational complexity, and runtime) are missing, though crucial for SM-type models.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear motivation and derivation. The spectral parameters’ interpretations are helpful. Minor notational/typographical issues should be fixed (e.g., “multiouput”, “wrt”, “st Conference…”, “joint model”→“jointly model”). Clarify whether Σ in time-domain exponent is a precision or covariance (consistency with earlier SM notation).
- It would help to explicitly state the Hermitian property Sij(ω)=Sji(ω) and show how it follows from the chosen Ri(ω) and symmetrization.

Originality
- The use of complex cross-spectral densities with rank-one (and Q-component) factorization to yield interpretable, output-pair-specific delays and phases is a novel and elegant extension of SM to MOGPs. While related to CSM, the generalization to per-output spectral parameters and explicit delays is substantive. The spectral-domain construction for guaranteeing PD is conceptually clean.

Significance
- The approach offers a flexible, interpretable family of MOGP kernels that can capture rich cross-channel structure (phase and delay) with closed-form kernels. This is likely useful to practitioners modeling multivariate temporal/spatial signals. The empirical gains are moderate on real data but promising; the synthetic example highlights capability beyond prior baselines. Broader adoption would be aided by addressing scalability and initialization.

Strengths
- Principled construction via Cramér’s theorem; positive definiteness guaranteed.
- Interpretable spectral parameters per output pair; can model phase shifts and delays.
- Closed-form stationary cross-covariances; subsumes SM-LMC/CSM as special cases.
- Solid synthetic demonstration; competitive real-data results; statistical testing reported.
- Clear relation to prior work; implementation on standard GP frameworks.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scalability: Cubic complexity in total observations; no sparse/inducing solution or runtime analysis. Suggest adding complexity discussion and a sketch of a sparse MOSM (e.g., spectral inducing variables).
- Initialization: SM kernels are sensitive to initialization. Please detail initialization heuristics (e.g., periodogram fits per output, cross-spectral estimates) and report sensitivity/ablation.
- Identifiability and over-parameterization: Many per-output(-pair) parameters can cause overfitting. Discuss identifiability, regularization, and constraints (e.g., tying or priors).
- Hermitian PSD after symmetrization: Provide a brief proof or reference that the matrix S(ω) remains Hermitian PSD for all ω after the evenization step.
- Baselines: Consider adding multivariate Matérn cross-covariances (Gneiting et al., 2010) and more advanced process convolution variants; also discuss why LMC with linear operators could not capture derivative structure (the current statement may be too strong, as LMC with derivative operators can, in principle, produce f–f′ cross-covariances).
- Evaluation: Report uncertainty (calibration), hyperparameter counts, training time, and learned delays/phases to support interpretability claims. For climate data, the KS test on MAE distributions is acceptable but limited; consider paired tests or confidence intervals over repeated splits.

Minor comments
- Clarify the role of the magnitude term wij and its dependence on (µi,µj,Σi,Σj); a short derivation in the appendix would help.
- State dimensionality assumptions (n-D inputs) and show a 2D example if possible.
- Fix typos and notation consistency (Σ vs Σ−1 in time-domain kernel).

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, principled, and interpretable extension of SM to multi-output with new capabilities (delays and phases).
- Theoretical construction is sound and general; closed-form kernels are practical.
- Competitive empirical performance; compelling synthetic validation.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth and scalability analysis; modest real-data gains.
- Missing details on initialization, identifiability, and complexity.
- Some theoretical details (Hermitian PSD after symmetrization) and notation need tightening.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The methodological contribution is solid and of interest to the GP and time-series communities; addressing the noted clarity and experimental breadth issues would strengthen the final version.
