### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel and highly expressive covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The authors' approach moves the design of the MOGP kernel from the spatio-temporal domain to the frequency domain. Building upon Cramér's theorem (the multivariate generalization of Bochner's theorem), they construct a flexible family of matrix-valued cross-spectral densities. Specifically, they model the cross-spectral density matrix as a sum of rank-one components, where each component is formed from complex-valued squared exponential functions. The inverse Fourier transform of this construction yields a closed-form, interpretable, and provably positive-definite matrix-valued kernel. The resulting MOSM kernel can model channel-specific spectral properties as well as inter-channel delays and phase shifts, generalizing previous spectral approaches for MOGPs. The authors validate their model on a synthetic task involving derivatives and time-delays and demonstrate competitive or superior performance against state-of-the-art MOGP kernels on two real-world datasets.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** The core contribution is the principled derivation of a flexible MOGP kernel from first principles in the spectral domain. The use of Cramér's theorem is appropriate, and the method of ensuring the positive-definiteness of the spectral density matrix `S(ω)` via the `R^H R` decomposition is both standard and elegant. The specific choice of complex-valued squared exponential functions for the factors `R_i(ω)` is clever, as it leads to an interpretable and closed-form kernel after the inverse Fourier transform.

2.  **High Expressivity and Interpretability:** The MOSM kernel is significantly more flexible than its predecessors. It generalizes the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel by allowing each output channel to have its own spectral mean (`µ_i`) and variance (`Σ_i`). Crucially, it introduces an explicit delay parameter (`θ_ij`) which is distinct from the phase (`φ_ij`), allowing it to model a richer set of relationships between outputs. This is a key advantage over prior work and is convincingly demonstrated in the synthetic experiment. All hyperparameters have a clear physical interpretation in the frequency domain, which is a desirable property for model building and analysis.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively showcase the model's capabilities. The synthetic example—learning the relationships between a signal, its derivative, and a delayed version—is particularly compelling. It constructs a scenario where LMC-based models are known to fail and demonstrates that MOSM can correctly identify these complex, non-symmetric cross-covariances without prior knowledge. The performance on the real-world climate and Jura datasets shows that MOSM is competitive with or superior to established MOGP models like CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM, demonstrating its practical utility.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide the necessary background on spectral methods and MOGPs before clearly detailing their own derivation step-by-step. The connection between the parameters of the spectral density and the final kernel is clearly laid out. The overall structure is logical and guides the reader effectively.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Hyperparameter Optimization and Scalability:** The flexibility of the MOSM kernel comes at the cost of a large number of hyperparameters, scaling with the number of outputs `m`, input dimensions `n`, and spectral components `Q`. Optimizing this high-dimensional, non-convex log-likelihood surface can be challenging and sensitive to initialization. The paper does not discuss the optimization strategy (e.g., initialization, optimizer used) in detail. Given that this is a practical barrier to using such models, a brief discussion of these challenges and the methods used would strengthen the paper and aid reproducibility.

2.  **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not explicitly discuss the computational complexity. While it is standard for GP models, it is worth noting that computation scales as O(N³) where N is the total number of observations across all outputs. The paper rightly points to sparse approximations as future work, but a brief acknowledgment of the computational cost in the main text would be appropriate.

3.  **Generalization to Q > 1:** The derivation focuses on a single component (`Q=1`) and then presents the multi-component kernel in Definition 5 as a simple sum. This is valid, as the sum of positive-definite kernels is positive-definite. However, the initial formulation `S(ω) = R^H(ω)R(ω)` could allow for a full rank-Q matrix `R(ω) ∈ C^(Q×m)`, which is not necessarily equivalent to a sum of Q rank-one matrices. A brief discussion clarifying why the sum-of-rank-one-models approach was chosen and its relationship to the more general rank-Q model would add theoretical depth.

4.  **Statistical Significance in Experiments:** In the real-world experiments, the performance gains over CSM are sometimes marginal and not always statistically significant. While the authors are honest about this (using the KS test), it suggests that for some well-behaved datasets, the extra flexibility of MOSM may not be necessary. It would be beneficial to frame this not as a weakness, but as a feature: MOSM can gracefully collapse to a simpler model when the data demands it, while retaining the power to model more complex relationships when they exist (as shown in the synthetic task).

### **Decision**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on multi-output Gaussian processes. The proposed MOSM kernel is a well-motivated, technically sound, and powerful generalization of previous spectral mixture approaches. The increased expressivity is clearly demonstrated through a strong set of experiments. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and common to this class of models, and they do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is well-written and will likely be influential for researchers and practitioners working with complex multi-output time-series or spatial data. I strongly recommend acceptance.
