Summary
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output GPs by constructing complex cross-spectral densities and invoking Cramér’s theorem to guarantee positive-definiteness. The core idea is to parameterize a factor R(ω) per output (weight, mean, diagonal covariance, delay θ, and phase φ) so that S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω) is PSD for all ω; then invert the Fourier transform to obtain closed-form cross-covariances. Compared to SM-LMC and CSM, MOSM allows per-output spectral parameters and jointly models both delays and phase shifts; SM-LMC and CSM are shown as special cases. Experiments include: (i) a synthetic tri-variate setting with a reference signal, its derivative, and a delayed channel; (ii) climate sensor imputation; and (iii) the Jura metals dataset. MOSM performs best on the synthetic and Cadmium tasks and is competitive elsewhere.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Wilson & Adams’ SM kernel, LMC/CONV for MOGPs, and CSM’s cross-spectral parameterization. Using Cramér’s theorem to design valid multi-output stationary kernels in the spectral domain is well-known in statistics/geostatistics (e.g., multivariate Matérn; Gneiting et al., 2010), though here the contribution is a concrete, interpretable, closed-form MOSM with delays and phases via complex spectra. The paper appropriately cites SM-LMC, CSM, CONV, and multivariate kernel surveys, but would benefit from a deeper comparison to multivariate Matérn families and other cross-spectrum constructions common in time-series and signal processing.

Strengths
- Principled construction: PSD is ensured via a spectral factorization; inverse Fourier yields closed-form kernels.
- Interpretability: parameters map to magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, delay, and phase for each output pair.
- Generality: recovers SM auto-covariances and includes SM-LMC/CSM as special cases; allows different per-output spectra.
- Empirical evidence: clear synthetic example demonstrating the ability to simultaneously learn derivative- and delay-type relationships; competitive performance on two real datasets.
- Usable implementation: fits into the GPflow/TF stack.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/clarity:
- Conjugation inconsistency: The paper derives S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), implying Sij(ω) = R:i(ω)H R:j(ω). However, when specializing to Q=1, the text states Sij(ω) = Ri(ω)Rj(ω) (no conjugate), yet later derives θij = θi − θj and φij = φi − φj, which only follows with the conjugate on Ri. This needs correction and consistent notation.
- Real-valuedness: For real processes, one typically requires S(−ω) = S(ω)∗ (and S(ω) Hermitian). The proposed symmetrization Sij(ω) ← 0.5(Sij(ω)+Sij(−ω)) is ad hoc; please clarify whether the construction guarantees the necessary conjugate symmetry and preserves PSD pointwise (it likely does as a sum of PSD matrices, but the real-valuedness condition should be explicitly proven for the multivariate case).

Modeling/identifiability:
- Identifiability between θ and φ: in kij(τ) = αij exp(−0.5(τ+θij)T Σij (τ+θij)) cos((τ+θij)T µij + φij), some combinations of θij and φij can trade off (e.g., shifts along µij). The paper does not discuss identifiability, constraints, or regularization to avoid degenerate solutions.
- Overparameterization risk: per-component, per-output parameters (w, µ, Σ diag, θ, φ) can be numerous. There is no sensitivity analysis, regularization, or prior structure to mitigate overfitting.

Empirics:
- Limited and mixed gains: Beyond the synthetic case designed to showcase delays/derivatives, gains on climate and Jura are modest. In several cases MOSM is roughly tied with CSM, suggesting the added flexibility does not consistently translate to better performance.
- Baselines and fairness: All models are given 5 components, but there is no validation that this is optimal per method; no comparison to multivariate Matérn/parsimonious geostatistical models; no ablations on Q, rank > 1, or the value of adding delays/phases.
- Scalability and practicality: No discussion of computational complexity, wall-clock time, or sparse approximations beyond future work. For realistic MOGP sizes, O((Nm)^3) is prohibitive.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameter initialization (critical for SM-type kernels), optimization details, and code availability are not fully described; statistical testing is used selectively and with unclear repetition counts.

Scope:
- The method is stationary; many multi-output problems exhibit nonstationarity or input-dependent cross-relations. This limitation should be acknowledged more clearly.

Quality
The mathematical idea is solid and largely correct, but the conjugation/symmetry points should be fixed and real-valuedness conditions made rigorous. Empirical support is limited and does not convincingly establish superiority beyond the synthetic setting.

Clarity
Generally well written but notation is heavy. Please correct the conjugation inconsistency, explicitly state the real-valuedness conditions, and streamline Section 3.1 to avoid confusion. A figure explaining parameter roles would help.

Originality
Moderate. The use of a spectral factor R(ω) to induce valid cross-spectra is standard; the novelty is a specific, interpretable parameterization that yields closed-form kernels with delay and phase and encompasses SM-LMC/CSM. This is an incremental advance.

Significance
Moderate to low. The contribution is conceptually neat but empirical gains are small on standard tasks, and the paper lacks scalability and robustness evidence. Without stronger validation, it is hard to foresee broad adoption beyond niche use cases.

Questions for the authors
1) Please fix and clarify the definition of Sij(ω) (with conjugation) and provide a formal argument that the final symmetrized spectrum yields real-valued cross-covariances and maintains PSD for all ω.
2) How do you address identifiability between θij and φij? Any constraints or priors used?
3) How sensitive are results to initialization and Q? Do you have results with higher rank (Q > 1) factorizations across outputs?
4) Can you report runtime and memory vs. CSM/SM-LMC/CONV, and provide a sparse variant or at least a discussion of computational feasibility?
5) Could you include a multivariate Matérn baseline and ablations removing delay/phase to quantify their contribution on real data?

Pros
- Principled spectral construction with closed-form kernels and interpretable parameters.
- Generalizes SM-LMC and CSM; can model delays and asymmetric phases.
- Strong synthetic demonstration; competitive results on real datasets.

Cons
- Technical ambiguities (conjugation/symmetry) require correction.
- Overparameterization and identifiability not addressed.
- Limited and mixed empirical improvements; narrow set of baselines; no scalability results.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the current version lacks rigorous clarification of key spectral properties, stronger empirical validation, and evidence of practical robustness/scalability. Strengthening these areas could make this a solid contribution.
