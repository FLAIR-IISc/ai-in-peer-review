Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to design a matrix-valued spectral density S(ω) in the Fourier domain using Cramér’s theorem, analogously to how the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel is constructed for single outputs via Bochner’s theorem. The authors factorize the cross-spectral density matrix as S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), where each output i is assigned a complex-valued squared-exponential “spectral factor” Ri(ω) whose magnitude, center, bandwidth, delay θi, and phase φi are learned. This yields, in closed form, a family of stationary cross-covariances
kij(τ) = Σq α(q)ij exp(-1/2 (τ + θ(q)ij)T Σ(q)ij (τ + θ(q)ij)) cos((τ + θ(q)ij)T μ(q)ij + φ(q)ij),
with parameters for each pair (i, j) induced from per-output parameters. The construction guarantees positive definiteness (by factorization) and produces real-valued kernels by symmetrizing the spectrum. Compared to existing approaches:
- SM-LMC (Wilson & Adams 2013 within LMC) is recovered when spectral parameters are tied across outputs, with no phase or delay.
- CSM (Ulrich et al. 2015) is a restricted special case; MOSM additionally supports explicit inter-output delays and allows per-output spectral parameters, yielding richer, interpretable cross-structures.
- CONV (Álvarez & Lawrence 2008) and classical LMC provide symmetric, centered cross-covariances and generally tie behavior across outputs; MOSM aims for flexibility and interpretability via spectral parameters.

Experiments include: (1) a synthetic 3-output example (a reference GP, its derivative, and a delayed version) showing MOSM can learn derivative and delay relationships and extrapolate; (2) a climate sensor imputation task (four stations) where MOSM is competitive; and (3) the Jura heavy metals dataset, where MOSM outperforms competitors on Cadmium and is competitive on Copper. Implementation uses GPflow/TF and maximum likelihood.

Quality:
- Theory: The positive-definiteness argument via S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) is sound. The per-pair parameter formulas (for μij, Σij, θij, φij, weights) are consistent with Gaussian product identities and the complex-exponential factors for phase/delay. One point to clarify is the conjugation in Sij(ω)=R:i(ω)H R:j(ω): later equations sometimes drop the explicit conjugate; ensuring the correct conjugation is important for Hermitian S(ω) and for the expressions of θij and φij. The step used to ensure real-valued processes (symmetrizing Sij(ω) via 0.5(Sij(ω)+Sij(-ω))) is plausible and preserves PSD (as an average of PSD matrices), and the resulting closed-form kernel is real; still, it would help to explicitly state the standard Hermitian symmetry condition for real processes (S(-ω)=S(ω)*) and show how the proposed mirrored construction of Ri(ω) satisfies it.
- Empirics: The synthetic task is compelling and shows a clear advantage over latent-process baselines when cross-covariances are not linear combinations of univariate kernels. On real data, results are competitive rather than uniformly superior; significance testing is provided for some cases. However, only MAE is reported; predictive log-likelihood or calibration metrics would strengthen the claims. Details on initialization (important for SM-type models) and sensitivity to Q are limited.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear derivation from Cramér’s theorem to the time-domain kernel and a helpful interpretation of parameters (magnitude, frequency μ, bandwidth Σ, delay θ, phase φ).
- A few notational/typographical issues: (i) explicitly include the complex conjugate in Sij(ω) = R:i(ω)H R:j(ω); (ii) clean up factors of 1/2 in Gaussian exponents and constants to avoid confusion; (iii) clarify the real-valuedness condition and the “two mirrored components” construction; (iv) fix minor typos (e.g., “multiouput,” “derivate,” footnote markers).

Originality:
- The work meaningfully extends SM kernels to the multi-output setting via a principled spectral construction, allowing per-output spectral parameters and explicit phase and delay between channels. While it builds on known tools (Bochner/Cramér, LMC-inspired factorization, SM/CSM), the combination and the specific parameterization enabling delays and pair-specific cross-covariances is novel and well-motivated.

Significance:
- The contribution is useful for multi-channel time series and spatial-temporal modeling where phase lags and delays are common and interpretable. The synthetic demo highlights scenarios where MOSM’s flexibility is necessary. Real-data gains are dataset-dependent but competitive, suggesting practical utility. A path to sparse/variational approximations is discussed and would increase impact.

Pros:
- Principled, constructive way to design expressive, interpretable MOGP kernels via the spectrum.
- Closed-form time-domain kernels supporting phase shifts and delays across outputs.
- Includes SM-LMC/CSM as special cases, clarifying relationships between models.
- Synthetic example convincingly shows benefits in scenarios with derivative and delay relations.

Cons:
- Empirical evaluation on real data is modest: only MAE, limited datasets, mixed wins; no likelihood-based metrics or uncertainty assessment.
- Scalability and parameterization: the number of per-output per-component parameters is large; discussion of identifiability, regularization, and initialization is limited.
- Some theoretical details (conjugation, real-valuedness conditions) could be made more precise.
- No comparison to alternative multivariate kernels such as multivariate Matérn (Gneiting et al. 2010) in the experiments.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Initialization: How are μi, Σi, θi, φi initialized? Do you use spectral estimates (per-channel periodograms) as in SM? How sensitive are results to initialization and Q?
- Identifiability: Are there redundancies between θ and φ (delay vs. phase) or between μ and φ in 1D? Any constraints/priors used to stabilize learning?
- Scalability: What is the training time vs. baselines? Any evidence of overfitting given the large parameter count? A sparse/variational version (as suggested) would be valuable.
- Metrics: Please report log-marginal likelihood or test log-likelihood, and maybe calibration metrics, to complement MAE.
- Baselines: Consider including multivariate Matérn cross-covariances as a strong classical baseline.
- Interpretation: For the real datasets, can you report the learned delays/phases to illustrate interpretability?

Recommendation:
This is a solid, technically sound and interpretable kernel construction that generalizes prior spectral MOGP work and can capture delays and phases. While the empirical evaluation could be stronger, the contribution is methodologically valuable and relevant to NeurIPS. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the theoretical points (conjugation/symmetry), expanding experimental metrics/details, and addressing initialization/scalability.
