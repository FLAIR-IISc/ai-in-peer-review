Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to design the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) directly, leveraging Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s), by factorizing S(ω) = R(ω)^H R(ω) with complex-valued, component-wise Gaussian functions for the columns of R(ω). This guarantees positive semidefiniteness in the spectral domain and yields closed-form, real-valued cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform after symmetrization, with interpretable parameters per output pair: frequency mean/bandwidth, magnitude, delay, and phase. The construction generalizes Wilson & Adams’ spectral mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output case, and generalizes prior MOGP approaches based on latent processes: the SM-LMC and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. Compared to CSM (Ulrich et al.), MOSM permits per-channel spectral parameters and explicit inter-channel delays in addition to phase, thus increasing flexibility and interpretability.

Experiments include: (i) a synthetic 3-output setting involving a base process, its derivative, and a delayed version—where MOSM recovers the correct auto- and cross-covariances and extrapolates both derivative and delay better than CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM; (ii) climate station imputation with four sensors, where MOSM is competitive with CSM and better on one sensor; and (iii) the Jura heavy metals dataset, where MOSM improves on competitors for Cadmium prediction and is competitive for Copper. The model is implemented in GPflow.

Quality:
- Theory: The factorization S(ω)=R^H(ω)R(ω) ensures spectral-domain PSD and hence a valid matrix-valued kernel after inverse Fourier transform. Modeling each column of R(ω) as a complex Gaussian in frequency leads to closed-form time-domain cross-covariances with delay and phase. The need to produce real-valued processes is handled by symmetrizing S(ω) with S(ω)+S(−ω); as each term is PSD, their average remains PSD. One minor notational inconsistency is that, after stating S(ω)=R^H R (which implies Sij=overline{Ri} Rj), the text briefly writes Sij=Ri Rj; the subsequent formulas for phases/delays are consistent with the conjugate and the Hermitian structure, but this should be corrected. Another minor confusion is the statement that αij∈R allows negative correlations; in fact αij>0 but the cosine yields sign changes in the covariance—worth clarifying.
- Empirics: The synthetic experiment cleanly demonstrates the added expressiveness (derivatives and delays simultaneously), which is unlikely to be captured by LMC-style models. Real-world gains are modest but present in places. However, the statistical testing is not well justified: a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test on error distributions across single held-out segments is not the right tool for paired predictive comparisons; a paired t-test, Wilcoxon signed-rank, or bootstrap on per-point absolute errors would be more appropriate. The datasets are small/modest, and there is no stress test on higher-dimensional inputs or larger m.
- Practicality: The parameterization scales linearly with m and Q but can grow large (per-channel weights, means, bandwidths, phases, delays), and the paper does not discuss identifiability, initialization, or regularization beyond standard maximum likelihood. Computational complexity remains cubic in N; sparsity/inducing strategies are suggested as future work.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written, with clear motivation, a clean derivation, and sensible interpretation of parameters. Figures help. A few notational/typographical issues (noted above) should be fixed. It would help to make explicit the real-valuedness condition S(−ω)=S(ω)^H and to state that the symmetrization preserves PSD at each ω. A brief discussion of parameter count, initialization, and sensitivity to Q would improve reproducibility. Code availability is not mentioned.

Originality:
The work is an incremental but meaningful generalization of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting via a principled spectral factorization with complex-valued components. It clearly subsumes SM-LMC and CSM and adds the ability to model inter-channel delays alongside phase differences with per-channel spectral parameters. Prior uses of Cramér’s theorem in MOGPs (e.g., real-valued t processes) are acknowledged; the complex cross-spectra with delays appears novel in this context.

Significance:
The contribution is practically useful: an interpretable, expressive, closed-form MOGP kernel that can model realistic inter-channel structure (phase, delay) beyond latent linear mixing. While the empirical gains on real datasets are not dramatic, the synthetic study convincingly demonstrates capability that latent-process models lack. Given the popularity of SM kernels and multi-output modeling, this kernel is likely to be used and extended (e.g., with sparse approximations).

Pros:
- Sound spectral-domain construction guaranteeing valid MOGP kernels.
- Interpretable parameters per output pair (frequency, bandwidth, delay, phase).
- Generalizes SM-LMC and CSM; adds modeling flexibility.
- Closed-form kernels; straightforward training with standard GP machinery.
- Synthetic example convincingly shows unique strengths.

Cons:
- Limited empirical scope and modest real-world gains; no large-scale experiments.
- Statistical testing choice (KS test) is inappropriate for paired prediction comparison.
- Parameter proliferation and potential identifiability/optimization issues are not discussed.
- Minor notational inconsistencies (conjugation) and clarity points.
- No scalability solution beyond future work; no ablations on Q, initialization, or regularization.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Clarify Sij=overline{Ri}Rj and the real-valuedness condition explicitly; ensure notation is consistent.
- Use paired tests or bootstrap for significance; report multiple random splits where possible.
- Discuss initialization strategies, identifiability, and sensitivity to Q and m.
- Provide guidance on regularization and priors to mitigate overfitting.
- Consider releasing code to aid reproducibility.

Overall: A technically solid, interpretable, and useful kernel construction with a convincing synthetic demonstration and competitive real-data performance. I lean toward accept, with the caveat that empirical validation and clarity on a few technical points could be strengthened.
