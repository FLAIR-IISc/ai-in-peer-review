Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a new family of stationary covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) constructed in the spectral domain using Cramér’s theorem. The key idea is to parameterize a matrix-valued power spectral density (PSD) S(ω) via a low-rank factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), where each per-output factor Ri(ω) is a complex Gaussian (square-exponential) with output-specific amplitude, mean, covariance, delay, and phase. Enforcing real-valued processes is handled by symmetrizing the spectral density. The inverse Fourier transform yields a closed-form time/space-domain kernel whose autocovariances are standard spectral mixture (SM) kernels while cross-covariances admit delays and phase differences and can be asymmetric in τ. The construction guarantees positive definiteness by design.

This approach extends Wilson & Adams’ SM kernel to the multi-output setting and generalizes LMC-based spectral mixtures (SM-LMC) and the Cross Spectral Mixture kernel (CSM; Ulrich et al., NeurIPS 2015). Compared to CSM, MOSM allows output-specific spectral means and covariances, explicit delays, and phases, yielding richer and more interpretable cross-covariances. The paper relates to classical geostatistical multi-output kernels (e.g., Matérn cross-covariances; Gneiting et al., 2010) and to convolution/LMC constructions (Álvarez & Lawrence, 2008), but differs by designing cross-spectra directly. It also connects to complex/analytic GP formulations and inter-domain/spectral inducing-variable methods.

Quality:
- Theory: The use of Cramér’s theorem is appropriate, and the PSD factorization S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) ensures positive semidefiniteness for each ω. The Fourier pairs are derived to yield a closed-form kernel with interpretable parameters (magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, delay, phase). However, there appears to be a notational/technical inconsistency: after introducing S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω), the paper states Sij(ω)=Ri(ω)Rj(ω) (Eq. 9), whereas the Hermitian construction implies Sij(ω)=conj(Ri(ω)) Rj(ω). The final expressions for θij=θi−θj and φij=φi−φj match the conjugate form, so Eq. 9 (and surrounding text) should be corrected for consistency and to avoid confusion about Hermitian PSDs. The symmetrization step to ensure real-valued processes is reasonable; averaging S(ω) and S(−ω) preserves PSD.
- Empirics: The synthetic tri-variate task (GP, derivative, and delayed channel) convincingly demonstrates MOSM’s ability to model nontrivial cross-covariances (delays and derivative relations) that LMC-style models cannot represent. Real-data experiments (climate sensors and Jura metals) show competitive performance; MOSM is best on some tasks (notably Cadmium) and comparable to CSM/SM-LMC on others. The empirical section would benefit from more detail on initialization, number of components Q selection, sensitivity analyses, and runtime.
- Completeness/care: The paper is careful in derivations and relates to prior work. It lacks a scalable inference variant (all methods are dense O(N^3)) despite referencing sparse approaches as future work.

Clarity:
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. The parameter interpretation is a strong point. There are several typos and a few places where terminology could be tightened (e.g., “symmetric w.r.t. ω” vs the usual conjugate-symmetry requirement for real processes; see above). Clarify the Hermitian structure, conjugation in Sij, and the exact real-process condition S(−ω)=S(ω)H. Explicitly list the per-output per-component parameters and discuss identifiability and invariances.

Originality:
Moderately high. The idea of designing multi-output kernels via spectral densities is known, and CSM already moved beyond SM-LMC by allowing phases. MOSM’s contribution is a principled, more general complex Gaussian factorization per output that yields interpretable, closed-form cross-covariances with delays and output-specific spectral characteristics. This generalization is conceptually simple but nontrivial, and the derivation is useful.

Significance:
Potentially impactful for practitioners modeling multi-sensor time series/spatial fields where delays, phase shifts, and channel-specific spectra are important. While real-world improvements over strong baselines are mixed, the expressivity and interpretability are valuable. The lack of scalability limits immediate applicability to large datasets; however, the construction seems compatible with variational spectral inducing-variable methods.

Pros:
- Principled, PSD-guaranteed construction via Cramér’s theorem with closed-form kernels.
- Clear, physical interpretation of all parameters (frequency, bandwidth, delay, phase).
- Strict generalization of SM-LMC and CSM; allows asymmetric cross-covariances and delays.
- Strong synthetic demonstration and competitive real-data performance.
- Good linkage to prior literature.

Cons:
- Notational/technical inconsistency around Sij(ω) and conjugation; needs correction.
- No scalable inference; experiments are small/medium scale and 1D inputs only.
- Many hyperparameters (per-output per-component), raising identifiability/overfitting concerns; no discussion of priors/regularization or invariance handling.
- Limited empirical analysis: few ablations, no uncertainty calibration, sparse detail on initialization/runtimes, and mixed gains on real data.
- Extensions to higher-dimensional inputs are claimed but not demonstrated empirically.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix the conjugation issue explicitly: use Sij(ω)=conj(Ri(ω)) Rj(ω) and state the Hermitian property Sji(ω)=conj(Sij(ω)); clarify the real-process condition (S(−ω)=S(ω)H).
- Discuss identifiability (e.g., phase/delay ambiguities, scaling/permutation symmetries across components) and practical initialization (e.g., per-channel spectral estimation).
- Provide sensitivity to Q and an ablation showing the impact of enabling delays/phases/output-specific µ, Σ.
- Report computational cost and optimization details; consider a sparse/variational variant or at least a discussion with small-scale experiments using inducing variables.
- Demonstrate a 2D spatial example to substantiate the Rn claim.
- Evaluate predictive uncertainty (e.g., calibration) and include additional metrics (e.g., log-likelihood).

Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
The paper presents a sound, interpretable, and useful generalization of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings, with a principled PSD construction and closed-form kernels. Despite some clarity issues and limited scalability/empirics, the contribution is novel enough and likely to influence follow-on work (including scalable implementations).
