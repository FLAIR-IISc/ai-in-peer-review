Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new family of multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernels, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The construction works in the frequency domain via Cramér’s theorem: define a matrix-valued cross-spectral density S(ω) that is Hermitian positive semidefinite for each ω, then obtain the time-domain cross-covariances by inverse Fourier transform. Concretely, the authors factor S(ω) = R(ω)^H R(ω) and choose rank-Q factors whose columns are complex Gaussian (SE) functions with per-output frequency mean μi, spectral covariance Σi, delay θi, phase φi, and weight wi. This yields cross-spectral densities Sij(ω) proportional to complex Gaussians with induced parameters (μij, Σij, θij, φij), and closed-form time-domain cross-covariances kij(τ) as exponentially-damped cosines with delay and phase. The model strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015), adding per-output spectral parameters and explicit delays. Experiments include (i) a synthetic tri-variate task (base function, its derivative, and a delayed version), (ii) data imputation for 4 climate stations, and (iii) the Jura heavy-metal dataset. MOSM is competitive; it clearly wins on the synthetic and Cadmium tasks, while showing parity with strong baselines elsewhere.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: constructive use of Cramér’s theorem with a low-rank factorization is elegant, with interpretable parameters (magnitude, frequency, delay, phase).
- Expressivity: allows pairwise-specific cross-covariances with delay and phase while ensuring PSD via factorization; strictly contains SM-LMC/CSM as special cases.
- Closed-form kernels: choosing complex SE factors yields analytic inverse Fourier transforms.
- Empirical results: synthetic example nicely demonstrates joint extrapolation of derivative and delayed signals; real-data results show competitive performance.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Major correctness issues:
1) Gaussian-product algebra for Sij parameters appears incorrect. If Ri(ω) ∝ exp(−1/2 (ω−μi)^T Σi^{-1} (ω−μi)), then Sij(ω) = Ri(ω)^* Rj(ω) is proportional to the product of two Gaussians. The product covariance should be (Σi^{-1} + Σj^{-1})^{-1} (in the scalar case, σ^2 = (σi^{-2}+σj^{-2})^{-1}). The paper states Σij = 2 Σi (Σi + Σj)^{-1} Σj, which equals 2 times the harmonic-mean form and disagrees by a factor of 2 even in one dimension. Likewise the stated μij = (Σi + Σj)^{-1}(Σi μj + Σj μi) swaps the usual precision-weighted averaging. These look like derivation errors (or a nonstandard Gaussian parameterization that is not explained). Given that these parameters feed directly into the time-domain kernels, this is a fundamental flaw that must be resolved.

2) Hermitian condition misstated. The paper asserts “S(ω) is Hermitian, i.e., Sij(ω) = Sji(ω)”. For complex cross-spectra, Hermitian means S(ω) = S(ω)^H, i.e., Sij(ω) = Sji(ω)*. This is important because it underlies positive semidefiniteness and reality of the time-domain process.

3) Realness/symmetry condition. For real-valued processes, one needs S(−ω) = S(ω)^T (equivalently, entrywise conjugate symmetry). The paper enforces “symmetry w.r.t. ω” via Sij(ω) ← 1/2 (Sij(ω) + Sij(−ω)) without conjugation or transpose. While averaging two PSD matrices preserves PSD, this operation does not in general guarantee the proper conjugate symmetry needed for real-valued outputs unless justified. The resulting derivation to the cosine-form kernel suggests a conjugate pair is implicitly assumed; please clarify and correct.

Other issues:
- Sign of αij: Eq. (10) defines αij ≥ 0, yet the text claims “both negatively- and positively-correlated signals (αij ∈ R)”. The sign is governed by the cosine term (phase), not αij; this inconsistency should be fixed.
- Notational inconsistencies and typos (many): missing fractions (−1/2 often rendered as “−1 2”), “st Conference…”, “multiouput”, ambiguous δ-indexing in the noise term (δiris), missing conjugates in Sij = RH:i R:j vs. later Sij = Ri Rj, etc. These hinder reproducibility.
- Statistical testing: Using a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test on pointwise absolute errors over time ignores temporal dependence and multiple-comparison issues; if only one train/test split is used, per-point errors are not i.i.d. Prefer repeated runs with different splits and a paired test on per-run MAEs (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank), or block-bootstrap respecting temporal structure.
- Experimental scope: Results are competitive but modest; climate task shows no clear improvement vs. CSM. No ablation teasing apart the contributions of delay vs. per-output μ/Σ vs. phase. Initialization, identifiability, and optimization stability (given many parameters) are not discussed.
- Complexity: Full GP O(N^3) is used; no discussion of runtime or scalar/matrix parameter scaling with m and Q; sparse/structured approximations are deferred to future work but would be important for practicality.

Quality
The central construction is promising, but the apparent errors in the Gaussian-product derivation (μij, Σij) and Hermitian/conjugate symmetry are serious. Empirically, improvements are limited beyond the synthetic case; statistical analysis is inadequate.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but marred by mathematical inaccuracies, notation glitches, and formatting issues. A careful rewrite is needed, with corrected derivations and explicit statements of the symmetry/PSD conditions.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of designing MOGP kernels in the spectral domain via Cramér’s theorem and a low-rank factorization is natural and related to LMC/CSM; the main novelty is allowing per-output spectral parameters plus explicit delays with an analytic construction.

Significance
Potentially useful if the derivation is corrected and the method is shown to materially outperform CSM/SM-LMC on harder tasks. As is, significance is limited.

Pros
- Elegant, interpretable construction with closed-form kernels
- Strict generalization of SM-LMC/CSM; includes delays and phases
- Solid synthetic demonstration

Cons
- Likely incorrect formulas for μij, Σij (core to the method)
- Misstatements of Hermitian and realness conditions
- Weak statistical testing and modest empirical gains
- Numerous notation/formatting errors; reproducibility concerns

Recommendation: Reject
Rationale: The technical inaccuracies in the spectral parameter derivation and Hermitian/conjugate symmetry are substantial and undermine correctness. Even if rectified, the empirical evidence is not yet compelling. I encourage revision with corrected derivations, stronger experiments (with proper statistical methodology and ablations), and a thorough cleanup of notation and presentation.

Confidence: High (familiar with GP spectral methods, Cramér/Bochner constructions, and MOGPs).
