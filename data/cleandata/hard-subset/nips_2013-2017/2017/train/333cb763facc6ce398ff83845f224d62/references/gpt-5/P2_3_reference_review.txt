Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to construct cross-spectral density matrices S(ω) that are positive semidefinite for each frequency via a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), with each column Ri(ω) modeled as a complex Gaussian (SE) whose parameters are output-specific. By Cramér’s theorem, inverse Fourier transforming these cross-spectral densities yields a valid matrix-valued stationary kernel. The resulting time-domain cross-covariances have closed form with interpretable parameters: per-output frequency mean and bandwidth (μi, Σi), amplitude wi, and output-specific phase and delay (φi, θi); cross pairs inherit differences (φij, θij), enabling both phase shifts and delays across channels. Extending from rank-1 to Q components gives a mixture.

This generalizes Wilson & Adams’ spectral mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output case and strictly generalizes the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel of Ulrich et al. (2015) and SM-LMC by allowing different spectral means and covariances per output and explicit delays. It relates conceptually to LMC/CONV constructions, but works natively in the spectral domain where positive definiteness is easier to enforce and interpret. Experiments include: (i) a synthetic tri-variate example with a base process, its derivative, and a delayed version; (ii) real climate signals with simulated missing sensor segments; and (iii) the Jura heavy metal dataset. MOSM is competitive overall and best in the synthetic and Cadmium tasks.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The core construction—PSD factorization via R(ω), symmetry to ensure a real-valued process, and inverse Fourier transform to closed-form kernels—is standard and sound. The positive semidefiniteness is guaranteed because S(ω) is PSD for each ω and sums of PSDs preserve PSD. The symmetry S(ω) → 0.5(S(ω)+S(−ω)) preserves PSD and enforces real-valuedness.
- Derivation details: The mapping from per-output parameters to cross-pair parameters (Σij, μij, wij, θij, φij) is clearly stated and physically interpretable. However, there appears to be a notational inconsistency: after stating S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), the text later writes Sij(ω) = Ri(ω)Rj(ω) (without a complex conjugate). Given the subsequent phase subtraction φij = φi − φj, the correct expression should be Sij(ω) = Ri(ω)* Rj(ω). This should be clarified/fixed.
- Completeness and evaluation: The synthetic experiment is well designed to stress delays and derivatives; only MOSM recovers both, consistent with the model’s flexibility. Real-data evaluations show competitive performance; significance testing is attempted, though the choice of Kolmogorov–Smirnov for MAE differences is unusual (a paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank would be more standard). Computational aspects (O(N^3) scaling) and initialization sensitivity—important for SM-style kernels—are not discussed in depth; acknowledging this and giving practical initialization details would strengthen the empirical section. The authors note sparse extensions as future work.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear conceptual flow from Cramér’s theorem to model construction and interpretation. The closed-form kernel is stated explicitly.
- Areas to improve:
  - Fix the conjugation issue in Sij(ω).
  - Provide a short derivation or appendix for the cross-parameter formulas (Σij, μij, wij), to aid reproducibility.
  - Discuss identifiability/gauge freedoms: only differences θij and φij enter cross-covariances, so multiple parameterizations are equivalent; this can affect optimization.
  - Expand on training details: initialization of μ, Σ, φ, θ; constraints; number of restarts; and runtime.

Originality
- The contribution is a natural but nontrivial generalization: per-output complex Gaussian factors in the spectrum yield cross-covariances with both phase and delay, strictly generalizing CSM and SM-LMC. The factorization approach and spectral-domain design for multivariate kernels are known, but the specific parameterization that yields an interpretable, closed-form multi-output SM with delays is novel to my knowledge and clearly differentiates from prior work (LMC/CONV/CSM). The paper positions itself well with respect to related work, including Matérn cross-covariances and CONV.

Significance
- Conceptual significance: Provides a flexible, interpretable multi-output kernel that can capture phenomena (delays+phase) that are difficult for LMC/CONV with shared auto-covariances. The spectral perspective and parameter interpretation are likely to be useful for practitioners analyzing multi-channel temporal data (sensors, neuroscience, etc.).
- Empirical significance: Demonstrates clear advantage on a synthetic task designed to stress the new capabilities; competitive on real data, with a win on one real task (Cadmium). While not a sweeping empirical win, the method’s flexibility and interpretability are valuable; broader/stronger benchmarks and ablations (e.g., with/without delay/phase) would further substantiate practical impact.

Strengths
- Sound spectral construction with guaranteed PSD and closed-form time-domain kernels.
- Strict generalization of CSM/SM-LMC with interpretable parameters (frequency, bandwidth, amplitude, delay, phase).
- Demonstrates modeling of delayed and out-of-phase relationships; recovers derivative and delay structure in synthetic data.
- Competitive real-data performance; provides statistical tests (albeit with caveats).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Notational/technical clarity: missing complex conjugate in Sij expression; derivations omitted.
- Limited discussion of optimization, initialization, and identifiability; SM kernels are sensitive in practice.
- Scalability not addressed beyond a brief future-work note; experiments are modest scale and 1D.
- Choice of significance test and limited runs; one baseline result taken from prior work (CONV on Jura) hinders fair testing.

Questions for the authors
- Please confirm Sij(ω) = Ri(ω)* Rj(ω) and correct Eq. (9) if needed.
- How do you initialize μ, Σ, φ, θ and weights? How sensitive are results to initialization and Q?
- Any strategies to mitigate parameter non-identifiability (e.g., fixing one channel’s φ, θ to zero)?
- What is the computational cost vs. CSM/SM-LMC for the same Q and number of observations? Any observed differences in convergence?
- Could you show an ablation demonstrating the contribution of delay and phase (turning them off) on the synthetic and climate tasks?

Minor comments
- Clarify whether Σij in Eq. (10) is the same as in the spectral Gaussian; the notation aligns with standard SM, but an explicit note helps.
- Consider more appropriate paired tests for MAE differences.
- A brief note on extending to multidimensional inputs with anisotropic delays would broaden applicability.

Overall assessment
This is a solid, technically sound extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting, with clear interpretability and demonstrated advantages on tasks requiring phase and delay modeling. While empirical gains on real data are modest and some clarity/implementation details should be improved, the contribution is meaningful and relevant to the NeurIPS audience. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the conjugation issue and adding training/initialization details.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
