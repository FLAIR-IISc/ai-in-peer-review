Summary
The paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Building on Cramér’s theorem, the authors design a complex-valued cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) via a low-rank factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), with per-output factors Ri(ω) parameterized as complex Gaussian (SE) functions whose phases and linear terms in ω encode phase shifts and time delays. Enforcing conjugate symmetry by mirroring in frequency yields real-valued covariance functions with closed-form expressions: each cross-covariance is a spectral mixture whose envelope and cosine argument are shifted by a learned delay and phase. The construction generalizes SM-LMC and CSM: MOSM allows channel-specific spectral means/covariances and explicit delays, while guaranteeing positive definiteness by design. Experiments include: (i) a synthetic task with a base process, its derivative, and a delayed copy; (ii) imputation on a multi-station climate dataset; (iii) the Jura heavy-metal spatial dataset. MOSM is competitive overall and best on the synthetic task and Cadmium prediction.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a principled frequency-domain route to multi-output kernels with explicit phase and delay, grounded in Cramér’s theorem.
- Positive-definiteness by construction: S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) with frequency-dependent “coregionalization” is elegant and tractable; the inverse FT yields closed-form cross-kernels.
- Expressivity: MOSM captures non-symmetric, delayed cross-covariances, and permits negative correlations; SM-LMC and CSM are recovered as special cases.
- Interpretability: parameters have clear spectral meaning (magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay) per output pair.
- Empirical demonstration: synthetic derivative/delay example is well matched to the method’s strengths and shows clear gains; real-data results are competitive, with some statistically significant improvements.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Notational inconsistency/possible errors:
  - With S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω), one should have Sij(ω) = conj(Ri(ω)) Rj(ω). The text in Sec. 3.1 states Sij(ω) = Ri(ω) Rj(ω), which drops the conjugate and contradicts the Hermitian property discussed earlier. This affects the sign definition for θij and φij in eq. (9). Please correct and make the conjugation explicit throughout.
  - There is an inconsistency in the SE exponent: eq. (8) seems to miss the 1/2 factor present in eqs. (2) and (9).
  - The discussion of “symmetrizing” Sij(ω) via Sij(ω) ← 1/2(Sij(ω)+Sij(−ω)) should clarify that (i) both S(ω) and S(−ω) are PSD; (ii) the average preserves PSD; and (iii) the real-valuedness condition for multivariate real processes is S(ω) = S(−ω)H. As written, it enforces evenness but the Hermitian symmetry should be stated precisely.
- Identifiability: θ and φ can be partially confounded via μ (since cos((τ+θ)T μ + φ) = cos(τT μ + (φ+μT θ))), and the Gaussian envelope is also shifted by θ. A discussion on identifiability, priors or constraints (e.g., centering θ around zero, bounding φ) would improve robustness.
- Parameter explosion and optimization: Per-output, per-component parameters wi, μi, Σi (diagonal), θi, φi lead to many degrees of freedom for moderate m and Q, with a highly multimodal likelihood. Practical issues (initialization strategies, priors/regularization, constraints to avoid degenerate solutions, sensitivity analyses) are not discussed.
- Experimental scope:
  - Synthetic baseline fairness: The derivative relationship can be modeled exactly with linear operator MOGPs; a baseline using known differential operators (e.g., Alvarez & Lawrence process convolutions or linear model with derivative operators) would be informative. The claim that “latent-process models fail” is too strong without operator-aware baselines.
  - Climate data: Differences are small. The use of a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test on 10 runs of MAE may not be ideal; a paired t-test or Wilcoxon test with matched splits would be more appropriate. Also report time/memory and convergence behavior.
  - Jura data: While MOSM improves Cadmium, SM-LMC wins on Copper. Please provide variability across random seeds/splits and runtime. It would help to analyze when allowing channel-specific μ/Σ (vs. shared, as in CSM/SM-LMC) is beneficial.
- Expressivity limitations: With rank Q, the PSD matrix rank is at most Q for each ω. While mixtures increase rank, the structure μij, Σij being precision-weighted combinations of per-output parameters restricts the set of admissible cross-spectra. A short discussion on approximation limits vs. arbitrary Hermitian PSDs would be valuable.

Relation to prior work
- The relation to SM-LMC and CSM is well drawn; emphasizing that MOSM effectively introduces a frequency-dependent coregionalization is useful. Consider citing multivariate Matérn cross-covariances (Gneiting et al., 2010) as an alternative family with interpretable cross-structure, and clarifying how MOSM differs in modeling phase/delay.
- Process convolutions can encode delays via shifted smoothing kernels; a direct comparison on a delay-heavy task would strengthen the empirical case.
- There is related work on multi-output SM variants that could be acknowledged if contemporary.

Quality
The core construction is sound and, barring the conjugation/symmetry notational issues, technically correct and well supported. The inverse FT derivations to eq. (10) are plausible and interpretable. Empirical support is mixed but sufficient to demonstrate feasibility.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with clear motivation and background. The noted inconsistencies should be fixed. Adding a brief derivation (or appendix) proving PSD preservation after symmetrization and detailing the product-of-Gaussians formulas for μij, Σij would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Moderately high. The idea of designing frequency-dependent coregionalization via complex SE factors and obtaining closed-form time-domain cross-kernels with joint phase and delay is a meaningful extension beyond CSM/SM-LMC.

Significance
Potentially impactful for practitioners needing interpretable cross-channel structure (delays, phases). While gains on real data are modest, the kernel family broadens the MOGP toolbox and is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., sparse/variational implementations).

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, interpretable generalization of SM to multi-output with phase and delay
- Positive-definiteness guaranteed; closed-form kernels
- Solid synthetic demonstration; competitive real-data results
- Clear connections to and generalization of CSM/SM-LMC

Cons for acceptance
- Notational/technical clarity issues (conjugation, symmetry, factors)
- Limited empirical advantage on real data; baselines could be stronger
- No scalability solution; many parameters without identifiability discussion

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Clarify the conjugation in Sij, the symmetry condition for real processes, and fix exponent factors.
- Discuss identifiability of θ vs φ and practical constraints/priors.
- Provide initialization and optimization details; report runtimes and convergence.
- Include an operator-aware derivative baseline; add ablations isolating the gains from per-channel μ/Σ vs shared.
- Comment on approximation limits of the low-rank spectral factorization and potential sparse/variational extensions.

Overall rating: Weak accept (borderline). The kernel construction is novel and sound, with clear practical appeal, though empirical gains are modest and clarity issues should be addressed. Confidence: High.
