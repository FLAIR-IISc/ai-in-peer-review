Summary
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes. Using Cramér’s theorem, the authors construct a matrix-valued cross-spectral density S(ω) by factorizing it as R(ω)H R(ω), with channel-specific complex SE components. After symmetrization in frequency to ensure real-valued processes, the inverse Fourier transform yields closed-form cross-covariances with interpretable parameters: per-channel frequency means and bandwidths, as well as pairwise phase and a linear phase (delay) term. The kernel strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM: it allows channel-dependent auto-covariance parameters and explicit delays across channels. Experiments include (i) a synthetic three-output example (base signal, derivative, and delayed version) and (ii) two real datasets (climate sensors and Swiss Jura heavy metals), compared against CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The construction via Cramér’s theorem and PSD factorization is standard and guarantees positive definiteness at each frequency. The symmetrization step to enforce real-valued processes is principled.
- Interpretability: The inclusion of explicit delay (linear phase) and phase offset parameters per pair of outputs is attractive and physically meaningful.
- Closed-form kernels: The inverse Fourier transform gives tractable expressions resembling SM kernels augmented with delay and phase, aiding implementation.
- Synthetic validation: The derivative-and-delay example is appropriate; MOSM appears to capture nontrivial cross-relationships that LMC-style latent models cannot.

Weaknesses
- Incremental novelty: CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015) already introduced complex cross-spectra and phase. MOSM’s main additions are (i) explicit delay (linear phase) and (ii) relaxing shared spectral parameters across channels. Both are natural generalizations of cross-spectral constructions and the R(ω)H R(ω) PSD factorization is standard. The novelty feels modest relative to CSM/SM-LMC.
- Experimental evidence is limited and mixed: On climate, MOSM provides only marginal improvements over CSM and not consistently significant; on Jura, MOSM wins for Cadmium but not Copper. The synthetic result is convincing but not sufficient to demonstrate broad practical superiority.
- Incomplete evaluation: 
  - Only MAE is reported; no negative log-likelihood or calibration metrics. For GPs, predictive likelihood is important.
  - No comparison to multivariate Matérn (e.g., Gneiting et al., 2010) or other widely used multi-output baselines beyond LMC/CONV/CSM.
  - No ablation to isolate the gains from delay vs phase vs channel-specific spectral parameters.
  - No sensitivity to Q (number of components), nor selection criteria or model evidence.
- Optimization details under-specified: SM kernels are known to be sensitive to initialization, and MOSM introduces many more parameters (per-channel µ, Σ, θ, φ, per-component). The paper does not describe initialization strategies, restarts, priors/regularization, or hyperparameter constraints (e.g., positivity of wi). Risk of overfitting is nontrivial.
- Scalability: The naive O((Nm)^3) complexity and reliance on subsampling (N=1000 for climate) are limiting; no sparse/inducing implementation is provided (only mentioned as future work), and no timing results are reported.
- Theoretical details: While PD is guaranteed by construction, the paper elides a short proof that the symmetrization S(ω) → 0.5(S(ω)+S(−ω)) preserves pointwise PSD-ness and yields real symmetric time-domain kernels; this is true, but a brief argument would help. Also, clarity on constraints ensuring αii ≥ 0 and how αij may be negative without violating PD would improve rigor.

Quality
Technically sound in the core construction; the kernel is valid and interpretable. However, empirical validation is not sufficiently thorough to support strong claims of superiority, and optimization/scalability practicalities are under-discussed. Some notation and typos could be improved.

Clarity
Generally clear, with useful background on Bochner/Cramér. Some notation is dense; a compact parameter count and a comparison table vs CSM/SM-LMC would help. Minor typos and formatting issues exist.

Originality
Moderate. The idea is a natural extension of CSM/SM-LMC via PSD factorization with per-channel parameters and explicit delay. The step is incremental rather than a fundamentally new modeling principle.

Significance
Potentially useful to practitioners needing delays and asymmetric cross-covariances with spectral interpretability. However, given the mixed empirical wins and incremental novelty, the current impact appears limited without stronger evidence.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide predictive negative log-likelihood and calibration metrics in addition to MAE.
- Report parameter counts, initialization strategies, restart protocol, and training time; discuss overfitting and regularization (e.g., priors on µ, Σ, θ).
- Add ablations: no-delay, no-phase, shared µ/Σ vs per-channel, to quantify contributions.
- Compare against multivariate Matérn cross-covariances and other multi-output baselines beyond those already included.
- Discuss selection of Q; attempt model selection (e.g., marginal likelihood, BIC) or sensitivity analysis.
- Clarify and briefly prove PD preservation under symmetrization; detail constraints on wi ensuring αii ≥ 0.
- Provide code for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled construction with clear interpretability.
- Valid kernel that strictly generalizes prior SM-based multi-output kernels.
- Demonstrated on synthetic and real datasets; competitive performance.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty over CSM; delay and per-channel parameters are expected extensions.
- Empirical gains over strong baselines are small/inconsistent; evaluation lacks key GP metrics and ablations.
- Practical issues (optimization, scalability) not sufficiently addressed.

Overall rating: 5 (marginally below acceptance)
Confidence: 4/5
