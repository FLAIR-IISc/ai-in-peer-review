The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, an extension of the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to multi-output Gaussian processes. The core idea is to construct a complex-valued cross-spectral density using a factorized form, leveraging Cramér’s theorem to guarantee positive definiteness. This allows for modeling delays and phase differences between outputs, offering greater flexibility than existing methods like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) or the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The authors provide a theoretical justification, a clear derivation of the kernel, and empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

This work builds upon a solid foundation of existing research in Gaussian processes, spectral methods, and multi-output modeling. The SM kernel [6] is effectively extended to the multivariate case, addressing the limitations of previous approaches like LMC [3, 4] and CSM [5] which lack the ability to model independent delays and phases across channels. The use of Cramér's theorem is a principled way to ensure positive definiteness, and the spectral interpretation of the parameters provides valuable insight. The experimental setup, comparing against several baselines including CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM, is appropriate and helps demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The MOSM kernel introduces a novel approach to multi-output GP modeling by leveraging complex-valued cross-spectral densities and Cramér’s theorem. The ability to model independent delays and phase differences is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear and mathematically rigorous derivation of the MOSM kernel, grounded in Bochner's and Cramér's theorems. The explanation of the spectral interpretation of the parameters is also well-done.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and highlights the limitations of existing methods. The mathematical notation is generally clear and consistent.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, using both synthetic and real-world datasets, provide evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison against relevant baselines is comprehensive.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the authors mention a potential sparse implementation, the computational complexity of the MOSM kernel, especially with a large number of outputs or spectral components, is not thoroughly discussed. This is a critical consideration for real-world applications.
*   **Synthetic Data Limitations:** The synthetic example, while illustrative, is relatively simple. Exploring more complex synthetic scenarios with varying degrees of correlation and noise could further strengthen the validation.
*   **Statistical Significance:** The statistical significance of the results is not consistently reported. While the authors mention a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test for one dataset, it would be beneficial to provide statistical tests for all comparisons to provide a more rigorous assessment of the improvements achieved by MOSM.
*   **Connection to Existing Complex GPs:** The paper briefly touches upon complex GPs [14, 15], but does not elaborate on how MOSM relates to or differs from existing work in that area. A more in-depth discussion of this connection would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The MOSM kernel offers a novel and theoretically sound approach to multi-output GP modeling, addressing limitations of existing methods.
*   The ability to model independent delays and phase differences between outputs is a significant contribution, potentially enabling applications in areas where temporal relationships are critical.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides comprehensive empirical validation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the MOSM kernel could limit its scalability to large-scale problems.
*   The statistical significance of the improvements is not consistently demonstrated across all experiments.
*   The connection to existing work on complex Gaussian processes could be further explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. The MOSM kernel is a novel and theoretically well-grounded method that addresses limitations of existing approaches. The empirical validation is encouraging. While some concerns remain regarding scalability and statistical significance, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly a discussion of computational complexity and more thorough statistical analysis.
