Summary
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to construct cross-spectral densities using Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner’s theorem), ensuring positive definiteness by factorizing the spectral density matrix S(ω) as a sum of rank-one Hermitian outer products, S(ω) = Σq Rq(ω)H Rq(ω). Each channel-specific factor Ri(ω) is a complex-valued squared-exponential (SE) in frequency with its own mean, covariance, magnitude, phase, and a linear phase term encoding delay. Symmetrizing S(ω) to enforce real-valued processes and taking inverse Fourier transforms yields closed-form cross-covariances of the form exp(−(τ+θij)TΣij(τ+θij)/2) cos((τ+θij)Tμij + φij), with interpretable parameters capturing magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase shifts, and delays. The construction subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases and allows distinct auto-covariance parameters per channel, non-symmetric cross-covariances, and delays.

Empirically, MOSM is tested on: (i) a synthetic 3-output task (a process, its derivative, and a delayed version), where MOSM best matches known cross-covariances and achieves the lowest MAE; (ii) climate temperature data from four sensors with simulated missing segments, where MOSM is competitive with CSM/SM-LMC/CONV and statistically best on one sensor; (iii) the Jura heavy metals dataset, where MOSM significantly outperforms baselines for Cadmium, while SM-LMC performs best for Copper.

Relation to prior work
- Scalar SM kernels (Wilson & Adams 2013) design stationary kernels via Gaussian mixtures in the spectral domain and are recovered on the diagonal of MOSM.
- MOGP constructions via LMC and convolution processes (Álvarez & Lawrence 2008; Goovaerts 1997) yield symmetric, centered cross-covariances tied to latent kernels.
- CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015) adds phase to SM-LMC cross-spectra. MOSM generalizes CSM by allowing channel-specific spectral means/covariances and explicit delays, while ensuring multivariate positive definiteness through spectral factorization.
- Cramér-based constructions have appeared with real-valued spectral families (e.g., Matérn cross-covariances), but without the complex parametrization that simultaneously handles phase and delay.

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The use of Cramér’s theorem with S(ω) built as Σq RqH Rq ensures positive definiteness for each ω. The symmetrization S(ω) ← (S(ω)+S(−ω))/2 preserves positive definiteness and guarantees real-valued processes. The inverse Fourier transforms to closed-form cross-covariances are consistent with Gaussian and linear phase properties.
- The derivation that yields μij, Σij, αij, θij, φij from per-channel parameters is coherent; however, some equations contain typographical inconsistencies (e.g., missing factors of 1/2 in exponents) that should be corrected for precision.
- Empirical evaluation is sensible and includes both synthetic and real datasets with standard baselines (SM-LMC, CSM, CONV, and IGP in Jura). Statistical testing is reported for some comparisons; however, the choice of Kolmogorov–Smirnov on MAE distributions may be less appropriate than paired tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank or paired t-test), depending on experimental design.
- Practical aspects such as initialization strategies for spectral parameters, identifiability, and sensitivity to Q are not discussed and are important for robust training.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized, with a clear progression from scalar SM to multivariate spectral construction via Cramér’s theorem. The interpretability of parameters is well articulated.
- Several typographical errors and minor notation inconsistencies hinder readability (e.g., “multiouput”, “derivate”, inconsistent exponential constants, stray conference header text). A careful proofreading is advised.
- More detail on training (initialization of spectral means/covariances/phases/delays, optimization settings, constraints on parameters) would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The method is a principled and nontrivial generalization of SM-based kernels to MOGPs via complex cross-spectral factorization, enabling joint modeling of delays and phase differences with channel-specific spectra. While related ideas (SM-LMC, CSM, convolution models, Cramér’s theorem) are known, the specific combination, parameterization, and closed-form spatial-domain kernel appear novel and provide additional flexibility and interpretability.

Significance
- MOSM offers a flexible, interpretable kernel family for multi-output regression that can capture phenomena (phase shifts, delays, different per-channel spectra) difficult for existing LMC/CONV-based kernels. This can be valuable for time series and spatial-temporal applications involving sensor networks or multi-channel signals.
- Empirical gains over strong baselines are clear in the synthetic setting and on one real dataset (Cadmium). On climate and Copper tasks, improvements are modest or dataset-dependent. The practical impact would be strengthened by broader experiments, ablations, and scalability demonstrations.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Sound spectral-domain construction guaranteeing positive definiteness and yielding closed-form kernels.
- Novel ability to jointly model phase shifts and explicit delays in cross-covariances with interpretable parameters.
- Subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases, clarifying relationships among MOGP kernels.
- Competitive to superior empirical performance; clear win on synthetic and one real task.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited discussion of training details (initialization, parameter constraints), identifiability, and hyperparameter sensitivity; potential over-parameterization for large m and Q.
- Scalability not addressed; experiments are relatively small and do not explore sparse/inducing approximations.
- Statistical analysis is limited and may use suboptimal tests for paired comparisons.
- Minor clarity/typo issues in equations and text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide initialization procedures for μ, Σ, φ, θ (e.g., from empirical spectra/cross-spectra) and discuss parameter constraints to aid optimization stability.
- Include ablations that remove phase (φ) and/or delay (θ) to quantify their contributions, and sensitivity analyses over Q and rank.
- Report computational complexity, wall-clock times, and memory; consider and evaluate a sparse/inducing version leveraging spectral structure.
- Use paired statistical tests across repeated splits where applicable.
- Correct typographical/notation issues and clearly state all constants in Fourier pairs.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and interpretable extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting, with evidence of utility. The contribution is incremental relative to CSM/SM-LMC but meaningfully extends flexibility by incorporating delays and channel-specific spectra.
