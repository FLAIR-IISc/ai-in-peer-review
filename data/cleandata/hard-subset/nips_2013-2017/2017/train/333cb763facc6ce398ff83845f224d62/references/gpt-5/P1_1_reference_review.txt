Summary
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to design cross-spectral densities in the Fourier domain and obtain matrix-valued stationary covariance functions via the inverse Fourier transform, using Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem). The authors parameterize the spectral density matrix S(ω) through a low-rank Hermitian factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω) with complex-valued, output-specific Gaussian factors. This yields, in closed form, time/space-domain cross-covariances that are spectral mixtures with: (i) distinct auto-covariances per channel; (ii) cross-covariances that support positive or negative correlation, (iii) delays, and (iv) phase shifts. The model strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM by allowing per-output means, scales, delays, and phases. Training uses standard GP marginal likelihood with gradient-based optimization.

Experiments include: (1) a synthetic 3-output task (a base process, its derivative, and a delayed copy), where MOSM recovers known auto- and cross-covariances and extrapolates both derivative and delay; (2) a climate sensor-failure imputation task; and (3) Jura heavy metals interpolation. MOSM is competitive with, and sometimes significantly better than, CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on spectral mixture kernels (Wilson & Adams, 2013) and extends them to multi-output via Cramér’s theorem, echoing the LMC and convolution process constructions for MOGPs (Álvarez & Lawrence).
- Closely related to CSM (Ulrich et al., 2015), but adds explicit, interpretable delay and phase, and decouples auto-covariance parameters across outputs.
- Contrasts with earlier multivariate spectral constructions (e.g., real-valued t-Student cross-spectra), by leveraging complex spectra to enable delays/phases and maintaining analytic time-domain forms.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: elegant factorization of S(ω) ensures positive definiteness; real-valuedness enforced by spectral symmetrization.
- Interpretability: parameters map cleanly to frequency, bandwidth, magnitude, delay, and phase for each output pair.
- Generality: MOSM strictly subsumes SM-LMC and CSM; auto-covariances reduce to standard SM; cross-covariances gain flexibility (delays and asymmetry).
- Closed-form kernels enable exact GP inference (modulo O(N^3)) without numerical Fourier transforms.
- Synthetic experiment convincingly demonstrates capabilities (simultaneous delay and derivative relationships are hard for LMC/CONV).
- Competitive results on real data; some statistical testing is provided.

Weaknesses and questions
- Technical clarity: In Sec. 3.1, Eq. (7) defines Sij(ω) = RH:i(ω)R:j(ω), implying Sij(ω) = conj(Ri) Rj for rank-1, but Eq. (9) drops the complex conjugate. Please fix this inconsistency and explicitly show S(ω) is Hermitian (Sij = conj(Sji)) under the chosen parameterization.
- Real-valuedness and PSD after symmetrization: The text says Sij(ω) is replaced by 1/2(Sij(ω) + Sij(−ω)). To avoid any doubt, it would help to present this as S(ω) → 1/2(R(ω)H R(ω) + R(−ω)H R(−ω)), which preserves PSD for each ω. As written, elementwise symmetrization could be misread as decoupled from matrix structure.
- Parameter identifiability and redundancy: With per-output per-component parameters (μi, Σi, θi, φi, wi), many different settings can induce very similar cross-covariances (e.g., tradeoffs between θ and φ; μ and θ inside the cosine and envelope). Please discuss identifiability, regularization, and practical constraints (e.g., wi ≥ 0), and whether you observed multimodality or degeneracy.
- Initialization: The paper does not explain how parameters are initialized, which is critical given the nonconvex landscape and the large number of hyperparameters. Simple heuristics (e.g., fitting per-output univariate SM first; initializing θ, φ from empirical cross-periodograms) would be valuable.
- Scalability: Exact MOGP inference is O(N^3) in the total number of observations. While you mention sparse extensions as future work, some discussion of computational cost (per-iteration complexity, dependence on Q and m) and runtime would be helpful, especially relative to CONV/SM-LMC/CSM.
- Empirical scope: Real-data gains are modest and sometimes not statistically significant. Additional tasks where delays/phases matter (e.g., multichannel physiological signals, seismic arrays) could better showcase MOSM’s advantages. Reporting predictive log-likelihood alongside MAE would present a fuller picture.
- Rank-Q construction: The text moves from rank-1 to Q components by summation. Clarify that S(ω) = Σq Rq(ω)H Rq(ω) (no cross-terms across q), and that the kernel is a sum of Q MOSM components. A brief note on how parameter coupling across q is handled (or not) would aid reproducibility.

Quality
Generally sound and well-supported. The core construction is correct and principled; closed-form inverse Fourier transforms are a strong plus. However, the conjugation/Hermitian notational slip should be corrected, and the PSD-preserving symmetrization made explicit at the matrix level. Experimental evaluation is solid but could be broader.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and provides adequate background. Some equations are cramped or have minor typos (see above), and a short algorithmic description (training, initialization) would improve reproducibility. Figures illustrating learned delays/phases would reinforce interpretability claims.

Originality
The use of complex Gaussian factors in the spectral domain with per-output parameters to induce delays and phases, under a PSD-preserving factorization, is a novel and meaningful extension beyond CSM and SM-LMC.

Significance
Kernel design for MOGPs is impactful, and the proposed construction is likely to be adopted by practitioners interested in interpretable multi-sensor modeling. Demonstrated gains are situational; wider evidence and scalable inference would increase impact.

Pros
- Principled, PSD-guaranteed multi-output spectral construction with clear interpretability.
- Strict generalization of SM-LMC/CSM; closed-form kernels with delays and phases.
- Strong synthetic demonstration; competitive real-data results; GPflow implementation.

Cons
- Minor but important technical clarity issues (conjugation, PSD after symmetrization).
- Many parameters with potential identifiability/initialization challenges.
- Limited empirical breadth and scalability discussion.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-motivated contribution to MOGP kernel design with clear theoretical grounding and practical appeal. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the Hermitian/real-valuedness details and expanding discussion of initialization and computational aspects.
