**Summary**

This paper proposes a new multi-output kernel for Gaussian Processes, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The core idea is to generalize the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel by working directly in the frequency domain. Instead of designing a valid scalar power spectral density (PSD) and using Bochner's theorem, the authors design a valid matrix-valued cross-spectral density (CSD) and use Cramér's theorem, the multivariate extension of Bochner's theorem. To ensure the resulting matrix-valued kernel is positive definite, they construct the CSD matrix `S(ω)` via a factor decomposition `S(ω) = R^H(ω)R(ω)`. Each factor `R_i(ω)` is modeled as a mixture of complex-valued Gaussian functions. The inverse Fourier transform of this CSD yields the MOSM kernel. This construction provides an expressive kernel that can capture channel-specific spectral properties (frequencies, lengthscales), as well as inter-channel delays and phase shifts, all with interpretable parameters. The paper shows that previous work, such as the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, is a special case of their more general formulation. The authors validate their model on a synthetic task designed to highlight its strengths and on two real-world datasets, comparing it to established MOGP models.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation (Originality & Quality):** The paper's main strength is its clean and theoretically sound derivation. Grounding the kernel design in Cramér's theorem and using a factor decomposition to guarantee positive definiteness is an elegant approach. It represents a natural, yet non-trivial, generalization of the highly successful SM kernel to the multi-output setting, moving beyond the limitations of methods based on the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC).

2.  **High Expressiveness and Interpretability (Significance):** The resulting MOSM kernel is very flexible. Unlike many MOGP kernels, it does not force auto-covariances to share properties (e.g., lengthscales). Crucially, it introduces dedicated parameters for delay (`θij`) and phase (`φij`) between channels, which are often critical in real-world multivariate time series (e.g., signals propagating across sensors). The clear connection between these parameters and their spectral interpretation is a significant advantage over more black-box approaches.

3.  **Excellent Synthetic Experiment (Quality):** The experiment involving a signal, its derivative, and its delayed version is perfectly chosen. It creates a scenario where the cross-covariances have known, non-trivial forms (e.g., asymmetry, shifts) that cannot be captured well by simpler models like SM-LMC or CONV. The superior performance of MOSM in this setting provides compelling evidence for the practical value of its increased flexibility and validates the theoretical claims of the paper.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The background material is concise and relevant, the derivation in Section 3 is logical and easy to follow, and the connection to prior work (especially CSM) is clearly articulated. The mathematical notation is consistent and the motivation is clear throughout.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Scalability and Optimization Challenges:** The primary weakness of the proposed model is the large number of hyperparameters. For `m` outputs and `Q` mixture components, the number of parameters to optimize is on the order of `O(Q * m * d)`, where `d` is the input dimensionality. The authors use `Q=5` in their experiments, which already presents a difficult, high-dimensional, and likely multi-modal optimization problem. While standard GP inference is `O(N^3)`, the optimization of the marginal likelihood here is a significant practical hurdle that is not discussed in detail. This could limit the model's applicability to problems with many output channels or those requiring many spectral components.

2.  **Mixed Real-World Results:** While the synthetic results are impressive, the performance on the two real-world datasets is less conclusive. On the climate data, MOSM is often on par with the simpler CSM, and on the Jura dataset, it is not the best-performing model for the Copper prediction task. The authors are honest about this and provide plausible explanations (high correlation in climate data, potential non-Gaussianity in Jura data). However, these results suggest that the additional flexibility of MOSM does not always translate into superior predictive performance on real tasks, potentially due to the aforementioned optimization challenges or model misspecification.

3.  **The Role of the Number of Components (Q):** Similar to the single-output SM kernel, choosing the number of mixture components `Q` is a critical modeling decision. The paper fixes `Q=5` for the experiments without discussing how this choice was made or how sensitive the results are to it. A brief discussion on model selection for `Q` (e.g., using Bayesian Information Criterion or cross-validation) would strengthen the paper's practical utility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a significant and elegant theoretical contribution to the MOGP kernel literature.
*   The proposed MOSM kernel is highly expressive, principled, and has interpretable parameters.
*   It generalizes and subsumes previous state-of-the-art spectral MOGP kernels.
*   The paper is extremely clear and well-written.
*   The synthetic experiment provides strong, convincing evidence for the model's capabilities.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The model introduces a very large number of hyperparameters, raising serious concerns about optimization and scalability which are not addressed.
*   The empirical gains on real-world datasets are modest and not consistent, which might temper the practical significance of the model's theoretical advantages.

**Recommendation:**
This is a high-quality paper that presents a solid, well-motivated, and theoretically sound contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. The clarity of the exposition and the strength of the synthetic validation are particularly commendable. While the practical concerns regarding hyperparameter optimization and mixed real-world results are valid, they do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. This work provides a new, powerful tool for MOGP modeling and lays a clear foundation for future research (e.g., sparse approximations, more robust optimization schemes). It is a definite advance on previous work and will be of high interest to the GP community. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Minor Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   In Section 3.1, the transition from `Q=1` to the general mixture model in Definition 5 is very brief. It would be helpful to explicitly state that the positive definiteness of the CSD matrix is preserved because the sum of positive definite matrices is also positive definite.
*   A short discussion in the main text or appendix on the optimization strategy would be valuable. Were multiple random restarts used? How challenging was it to train the model compared to CSM?
*   Adding the number of hyperparameters for each model to the experimental tables would make the performance-vs-complexity trade-off more explicit.
