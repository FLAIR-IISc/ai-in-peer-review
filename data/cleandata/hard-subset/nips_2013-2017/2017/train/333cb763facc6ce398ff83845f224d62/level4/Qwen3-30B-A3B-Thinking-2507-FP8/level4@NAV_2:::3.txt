

The paper introduces a novel Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes, extending the spectral mixture approach from single-output to multi-output settings. This work builds upon previous contributions including the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5] and the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) [3], while providing a more principled construction through Cramér's Theorem. The MOSM kernel offers clear parametric interpretation of relationships across channels, including delays and phase differences, which are not fully captured by prior methods. The authors validate their approach through synthetic experiments and comparisons with existing MOGP methods on real-world climate and heavy metal concentration datasets.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations, with a well-constructed kernel based on complex-valued cross-spectral densities that ensure positive definiteness. The experimental results show competitive performance, particularly on the heavy metal concentration dataset where MOSM significantly outperforms other methods for Cadmium prediction. The writing is clear and well-organized, with appropriate technical details provided for reproducibility.

Strengths of the paper include:
- A principled theoretical extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings
- Clear parametric interpretation of model parameters (delays, phase differences, etc.)
- Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets
- Effective comparison with relevant baselines (SM-LMC, CONV, CSM)

Weaknesses include:
- Limited discussion of parameter learning challenges and computational considerations
- Insufficient information about performance with small training datasets
- The synthetic example lacks specification of the number of training points

The authors should address the following questions:
* How does the proposed method perform when training data is scarce?
* How does the model handle cases where channels have significantly different length scales, particularly regarding correlation limits?

Minor comments:
* The synthetic example (Section 4.1) should specify the number of training points.
* There appears to be a missing word in lines 74 and 140 of the manuscript.

Overall, this is a well-executed extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output Gaussian processes with clear theoretical foundations and competitive empirical results. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes and would be of interest to the NIPS community.
