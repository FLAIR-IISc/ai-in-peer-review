The paper "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes" introduces a novel covariance structure for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs) by generalizing the spectral approach to build kernels. This method is grounded in Cramér's Theorem, allowing for the design of complex-valued cross-spectral densities that provide a clear interpretation of the relationships between different channels. The proposed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel is capable of modeling delays and phase differences among channels, making it more expressive than existing methods.

The authors provide a thorough background on Gaussian Processes, multi-output Gaussian Processes, and the spectral mixture approach, making the paper self-contained and easy to follow. The method is clearly described, and the illustrations are relevant and well-chosen.

The effectiveness of the MOSM kernel is demonstrated on several datasets, including synthetic and real-world examples. The experimental results show that the proposed method performs competitively with existing MOGP models, such as the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel.

The paper's strengths include:

1. **Quality**: The paper is technically sound, and the method is well-motivated and clearly described.
2. **Originality**: The proposed MOSM kernel is a novel contribution to the field of MOGPs, and its ability to model delays and phase differences among channels is a significant improvement over existing methods.
3. **Significance**: The method has the potential to become a standard for multi-output GPs, as it provides a clear interpretation of the relationships between different channels.

However, there are some minor remarks and questions that need to be addressed:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the parameter learning process, as this is a crucial aspect of any GP model.
* In the synthetic example (Section 4.1), the number of training points should be specified.
* The paper assumes that the reader is familiar with the spectral mixture approach and Cramér's Theorem. While the background section provides a good introduction, some readers may still find it challenging to follow.
* A word is missing in lines 74 and 140.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper introduces a novel and technically sound method for MOGPs.
* The method is well-motivated and clearly described.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the parameter learning process.
* The method may not be suitable for datasets with few training points.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, as it makes a significant contribution to the field of MOGPs. However, the authors should address the minor remarks and questions mentioned above to improve the paper's clarity and completeness.
