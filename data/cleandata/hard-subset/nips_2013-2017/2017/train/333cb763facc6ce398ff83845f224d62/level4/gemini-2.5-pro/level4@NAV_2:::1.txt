This paper introduces a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The central idea is to design the matrix-valued kernel in the spectral domain, which is a powerful and arguably underutilized approach in the Gaussian process literature. This work extends the well-known Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel for single-output GPs by leveraging Cramér's theorem, the multivariate generalization of Bochner's theorem. The main technical challenge in the multi-output setting is to construct valid cross-covariance functions, which need not be positive definite, while ensuring the overall kernel matrix remains so. The authors present a principled and elegant solution by parameterizing the matrix of cross-spectral densities via a factor decomposition, which naturally guarantees the necessary positive definiteness condition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by providing a principled generalization of the spectral mixture kernel to the multi-output setting. The proposed construction is more flexible than prior work like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, as it can model delays between channels in addition to phase differences. The method provides a highly expressive family of MOGP kernels whose parameters have a direct and valuable interpretation in terms of frequencies, magnitudes, phases, and delays.
*   **Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound, with its construction firmly rooted in the theory of stationary random processes via Cramér's Theorem. The derivation is clear and logical. Claims are appropriately supported by theoretical reasoning and validated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides a concise but sufficient background on spectral methods for GPs and the challenges of MOGPs. The motivation for the proposed approach and its derivation are presented with exceptional clarity, and the relationship to previous work is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The experimental evaluation is sufficient to demonstrate the model's viability and superior performance in the synthetic case. However, the analysis of the real-world datasets could be enhanced to provide deeper intuition. For instance, while the model achieves competitive results on the Jura and climate datasets, it would be more insightful if the authors discussed the learned spectral parameters (e.g., delays, frequencies) and related them back to the physical properties of the data. This would more strongly showcase the interpretability advantages claimed.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method for constructing expressive kernels for multi-output Gaussian processes. It is clearly written, thoroughly discusses related work, and significantly extends an important line of research. The resulting kernel is more flexible and interpretable than previous approaches.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The experimental section, while adequate, misses an opportunity to fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the kernel's interpretability on the real-world problems.

Overall, the strengths of this paper in originality, technical quality, and clarity are substantial. It presents a valuable and elegant contribution to the literature on multi-output Gaussian processes that is likely to be built upon by other researchers.
