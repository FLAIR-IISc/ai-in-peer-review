This paper introduces a novel approach to defining covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (GPs). Rather than directly specifying cross-covariance functions, the authors propose defining cross-power spectral densities and then employing the inverse Fourier transform to derive the corresponding cross-covariances. This methodology draws inspiration from work by [6], which leveraged power spectral density representation with a Gaussian mixture to construct a powerful kernel function with extrapolation capabilities for single-output GPs. The proposed covariance function is demonstrated on a synthetic dataset, as well as two real-world datasets: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset concerning metal concentrations.

The extension of the power spectral density approach from [6] to the multi-output setting represents a conceptually elegant and potentially valuable contribution. A key motivation behind the original work [6] was the development of kernels capable of extrapolation; it would have strengthened the current paper to explicitly investigate and demonstrate similar extrapolation properties for this new multi-output covariance function. 

The experimental evaluation, while clearly presented, relies on commonly used benchmark datasets. The reported performance gains are modest, and largely comparable to those achieved by existing multi-output GP covariance functions. To better establish the distinct advantages of this approach, the authors should consider evaluating it on datasets where current multi-output GP methods demonstrably struggle, or exhibit limitations.  Demonstrating superior performance – or even unique suitability – on such challenging datasets would significantly enhance the paper's impact.

Specifically regarding the quantitative results, it is unclear whether the observed differences in Tables 1 and 2 are statistically significant. The reported standard deviations suggest considerable overlap in performance between the various models. A statistical significance test (e.g., t-test, ANOVA with post-hoc analysis) would be crucial to substantiate claims of improvement and provide more confidence in the conclusions drawn from the experimental results. Without such analysis, it is difficult to determine whether the observed differences represent a genuine advantage of the proposed method, or simply arise from random variation.

In summary, this paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. While the idea is promising, the limited scope of the experimental evaluation and the lack of statistical rigor weaken the overall impact. Addressing these points—specifically by exploring extrapolation capabilities and providing statistical analysis—would significantly improve the paper's contribution and increase its appeal to the NIPS community.
