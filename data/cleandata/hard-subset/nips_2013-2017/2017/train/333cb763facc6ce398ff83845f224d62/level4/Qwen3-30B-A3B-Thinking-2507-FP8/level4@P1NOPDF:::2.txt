

The paper introduces a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes, proposing to specify cross-power spectral densities instead of directly defining cross-covariance functions. This approach extends the methodology of [6], which used a mixture of Gaussians to represent the power spectral density for single-output Gaussian processes, enabling kernel construction with strong extrapolation capabilities via inverse Fourier transformation. The authors apply their covariance function to a synthetic dataset and two real-world examples: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentration prediction.  

While extending [6]’s framework to multi-output settings is conceptually sound and potentially valuable, the paper fails to address a key motivation from the original work: the ability to extrapolate beyond training data. The authors should explicitly demonstrate whether their proposed covariance function inherits this extrapolation capability, as this was a primary contribution of [6]. Without this, the novelty appears incremental rather than transformative.  

The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and impact. The chosen datasets—weather and Swiss Jura—are standard benchmarks for multi-output Gaussian processes, and the results reported (Tables 1–2) show performance comparable to established methods. The paper does not include experiments on datasets where existing multi-output covariance functions struggle, which would better highlight the proposed method’s advantages. For instance, including applications in domains with complex output dependencies or sparse multi-task relationships would strengthen the case for the new covariance.  

Furthermore, the statistical significance of the results is unclear. Table 1 reports mean performance metrics with standard deviations, but the differences between models fall within the reported error margins. The authors should explicitly test whether these differences are statistically significant (e.g., via paired t-tests or confidence intervals) rather than implying equivalence based solely on overlapping standard deviations. Without this, the conclusion that the proposed method performs "comparably" to alternatives lacks rigorous support.  

In summary, while the technical approach is reasonable, the paper’s contributions are underdeveloped. The failure to demonstrate extrapolation (a core feature of [6]), the lack of challenging benchmark experiments, and the absence of statistical validation weaken the overall impact. The authors should address these gaps to justify the method’s novelty and practical utility.
