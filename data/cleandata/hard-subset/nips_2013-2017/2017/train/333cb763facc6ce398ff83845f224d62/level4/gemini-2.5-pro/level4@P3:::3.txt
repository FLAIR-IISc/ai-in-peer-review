This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel and expressive covariance function for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to generalize the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel, which builds a flexible kernel by designing its power spectral density (PSD) as a mixture of Gaussians and applying Bochner's theorem. This work extends that principle to the multi-output case by leveraging Cramér's theorem, the multivariate generalization of Bochner's theorem.

The authors propose constructing the matrix of cross-spectral densities, S(ω), by designing a family of complex-valued functions. To ensure the resulting covariance matrix is valid (i.e., positive definite), they parameterize S(ω) using a factor decomposition, S(ω) = R(ω)ᴴR(ω). By modeling the components of R(ω) as complex-valued squared exponential functions, they derive a closed-form expression for the MOSM kernel via the inverse Fourier transform. This principled construction results in a highly flexible kernel whose parameters have a clear physical interpretation, corresponding to magnitudes, frequencies, phase shifts, and, crucially, time delays between different output channels. This ability to model delays is a key contribution that distinguishes MOSM from prior work like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic task designed to highlight its unique capabilities and on two real-world datasets, where it performs competitively against or better than existing MOGP models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is theoretically sound, resting on a principled application of Cramér's theorem. The derivation of the MOSM kernel from the designed cross-spectral densities is clear and correct. The claims of increased expressiveness are well-supported by both the mathematical formulation and the empirical results. The synthetic experiment, involving a function, its derivative, and a delayed version, is particularly compelling as it presents a scenario where modeling delays and phase shifts is essential, and where the MOSM model demonstrably succeeds while other methods fail. The real-world experiments further validate the model's utility.

One potential weakness is the lack of discussion regarding the practical challenges of hyperparameter optimization. The MOSM kernel introduces a significant number of parameters, which scales with the number of outputs, input dimensions, and mixture components. While the paper mentions optimizing the negative log-likelihood using standard methods, it does not address the complexity of the resulting optimization landscape, potential issues with local minima, or sensitivity to initialization. A brief discussion of these practical considerations would make the work more complete.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors provide the necessary background on single-output SM kernels and MOGPs before clearly motivating and deriving their proposed MOSM kernel. The connection between the factor decomposition of the spectral density matrix and the guaranteed positive definiteness of the final kernel is explained well. The figures and tables are informative, and the experimental setup is clearly described, enhancing reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the idea of designing kernels in the spectral domain is not new, its generalization to the multi-output setting via Cramér's theorem and the proposed factorized parameterization of the cross-spectral density matrix is a novel and significant contribution. This approach provides a more flexible and principled way to construct MOGP kernels than previous methods. In particular, it cleanly generalizes the CSM kernel and introduces the novel capability of modeling inter-channel delays, which was not explicitly possible with prior spectral MOGP methods.

**Significance:** The proposed MOSM kernel is a significant contribution to the MOGP literature and is likely to be influential. The challenge of designing expressive and valid cross-covariance functions has long been a central problem in this field. This paper offers an elegant and powerful solution. The interpretability of its parameters (delay, phase, etc.) makes it particularly valuable for scientific and engineering applications where understanding the relationships between different signals is as important as prediction. Given its strong performance and clear theoretical grounding, the MOSM kernel could become a standard choice for MOGP modeling, especially for problems involving time series or spatio-temporal data from multiple sensors.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and principled method for constructing expressive MOGP covariance functions based on Cramér's theorem.
*   The resulting MOSM kernel is highly flexible and, uniquely among spectral MOGP methods, can explicitly model delays between output channels in addition to phase shifts.
*   The parameters of the model are interpretable, which is a major asset for many applications.
*   A strong synthetic experiment clearly demonstrates the advantages of the proposed model over existing methods in a scenario where they are expected to fail.
*   The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on real-world benchmark datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The paper does not address the practical challenges of optimizing the large number of hyperparameters, such as the potential for poor local minima or the need for careful initialization.
*   An analysis of the model's performance in low-data regimes is absent; given its complexity, it could be prone to overfitting.
*   The experiments are conducted on datasets of modest size, and the inherent cubic scalability of the GP model is not addressed beyond a brief mention of future work.

### Questions and Comments for the Authors

1.  The optimization of the MOSM hyperparameters seems challenging due to their large number and the non-convex nature of the GP log-likelihood. Could you comment on the practical difficulty of the optimization? Did you encounter issues with local minima, and how sensitive is the final performance to parameter initialization?

2.  Given the model's high flexibility, how does it behave when only a small number of training points are available? Is it particularly susceptible to overfitting compared to simpler MOGP models like SM-LMC?

3.  The cross-spectral magnitude `wij` depends on the squared distance between the component means `µi` and `µj`. This seems to imply that two components can only be strongly correlated if their characteristic frequencies are similar. This is intuitive, but could you elaborate on this property and its implications? For instance, does this prevent the model from capturing correlations between a very smooth process (low frequency) and a rapidly oscillating one (high frequency)?

4.  In the synthetic example (Section 4.1), please specify the exact number of training points used for each of the three signals (N1, N2, N3). This information is important for reproducibility.
