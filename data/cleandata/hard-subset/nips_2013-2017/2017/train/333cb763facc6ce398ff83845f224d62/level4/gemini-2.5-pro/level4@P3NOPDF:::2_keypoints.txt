- Proposes modeling multi-output GP cross-covariances via cross-power spectral densities in the frequency domain, recovered by inverse Fourier transform.  
- Extends the spectral mixture kernel from single-output to multi-output by representing cross-PSDs as mixtures of complex-valued Gaussians.  
- Validated on a synthetic example and two real-world benchmarks (weather prediction and Swiss Jura heavy metal data).  
- Strength: elegant, principled approach grounded in Fourier theory.  
- Strength: provides a flexible family of non-stationary, non-separable MOGP covariance functions.  
- Strength: natural and clever extension of spectral mixture kernels offering an alternative to LMC/ICM.  
- Weakness: misses experiments demonstrating long-range extrapolation, a key advantage of the original spectral mixture kernel.  
- Weakness: experimental validation is limited—benchmarks are standard and results are roughly on par with existing methods.  
- Weakness: lacks demonstrations on tasks where spectral-domain flexibility yields clear advantages (e.g., complex periodic/quasi-periodic inter-output structure).  
- Weakness: reported performance gains are marginal and need more rigorous statistical significance analysis.  
- Questions for authors: Were statistical significance tests performed for Tables 1–2, how interpretable are the learned spectral components relative to real phenomena, and was extrapolation ability investigated?
