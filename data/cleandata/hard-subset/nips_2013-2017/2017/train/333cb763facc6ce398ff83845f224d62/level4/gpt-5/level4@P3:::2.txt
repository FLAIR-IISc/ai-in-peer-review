Summary
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a new family of covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) constructed in the spectral domain. Building on Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner’s theorem), the authors specify complex-valued cross-spectral densities, parameterized as mixtures of Gaussian components, and obtain closed-form cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform. The resulting kernel affords an interpretable parameterization of cross-channel relationships in terms of magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay. Importantly, MOSM generalizes prior spectral approaches for MOGPs, including the Spectral Mixture Linear Model of Coregionalization (SM-LMC) and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, by allowing per-pair parameters and explicit time delays in addition to phase shifts. The method is validated on a synthetic three-output example (including derivatives and delays) and two real datasets: a four-station climate dataset and the Jura heavy metal concentration dataset.

Relation to prior work
The work extends the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel of Wilson and Adams (ICML’13) by moving to multi-output settings using Cramér’s theorem. In contrast to LMC/CONV approaches (Álvarez and Lawrence; Goovaerts), which define cross-covariances via shared latent processes, MOSM directly specifies cross-spectral densities and then guarantees positive definiteness through a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω). Compared to CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS’15), MOSM is strictly more general: it allows channel-specific means and covariances in the spectral domain and introduces explicit cross-channel delays, while CSM essentially corresponds to a restricted instance of MOSM with tied parameters. The paper also connects to work on multivariate Matérn cross-covariances and real-valued spectral parameterizations (e.g., Gneiting et al.; Genton and Kleiber), but offers a more flexible, interpretable spectral construction with closed-form kernels.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The spectral-domain construction with Cramér’s theorem is principled, and the factorization ensures positive definiteness in a simple, transparent manner.
- Interpretability: Parameters map directly to frequency content, bandwidth, phase, and delay per output pair; autocovariances reduce to SM kernels.
- Expressivity: By allowing complex-valued cross-spectra, MOSM captures asymmetric cross-covariances, negative correlations, and delays—capabilities that are limited or absent in LMC/CSM variants.
- Theoretical soundness: The Hermitian PSD construction and symmetrization to ensure real-valued processes are clearly motivated, and the closed-form time-domain kernels follow from Gaussian Fourier pairs.
- Synthetic validation: The derivative-and-delay example convincingly demonstrates cases where latent-process models struggle and MOSM succeeds.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope: Real-data experiments are small-scale and differences are modest; only the synthetic case shows a clear advantage. Datasets with known inter-sensor delays or misalignment would better showcase MOSM’s unique features.
- Statistical testing: For the climate data, a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test is reported only for one sensor. For paired prediction errors, a paired t-test or Wilcoxon signed-rank test would be more appropriate; similar tests should be reported for all datasets (including the synthetic case across runs).
- Clarity/notation: The Hermitian structure Sij(ω) = R:i(ω)H R:j(ω) should be kept explicit (conjugation signs occasionally disappear), and the symmetrization step to ensure real-valued processes could be clarified (i.e., the condition Sij(−ω) = Sji(ω) overline).
- Practical guidance: Initialization of spectral parameters (e.g., via per-channel periodograms), constraints (positivity, bandwidth), and identifiability when Q > 1 are not discussed in depth.
- Scalability: All experiments use exact GP inference; complexity and parameter counts may grow quickly with m and Q. A concrete sparse/inter-domain approximation and computational analysis would strengthen the paper.

Quality
The technical development appears sound: the PSD factorization yields positive definiteness pointwise in ω; enforcing symmetry in ω ensures real-valued processes; and the inverse transform yields closed-form kernels. The experimental methodology is standard, but significance analyses are incomplete and metrics are limited to MAE. Including negative log predictive density or calibration metrics would better assess probabilistic performance. Some derivational details (e.g., the exact forms of wij and Hermitian symmetry) would benefit from a more careful presentation to avoid confusion.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written but dense. Suggested improvements:
- Make the conjugation and Hermitian properties explicit throughout (Sij = R:iH R:j).
- Clearly state all assumptions (stationarity, diagonal Σ) and the consequences for real-valuedness.
- Summarize training details: initialization, constraints, priors, and parameter counts per m and Q.
- Minor wording/typos: “squared exponential” (not “square exponential”); several grammatical slips (e.g., “south on England,” “Gaussian noised,” “reconstruction the”).

Originality
Using complex cross-spectral densities with per-pair parameters to jointly model phase and delay is a meaningful extension beyond CSM/SM-LMC. While spectral-domain MOGPs are not new, the proposed parameterization and its interpretability are novel and useful.

Significance
The approach is promising: interpretable cross-channel structure is valuable in many applications. However, the current empirical evidence is moderate; stronger demonstrations (e.g., real tasks with known delays, larger m, or clear extrapolation benefits) are needed to establish a compelling advantage over existing methods.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please report statistical significance across all datasets with paired tests suited to MAE (e.g., paired t-test or Wilcoxon), not only KS and not only for one sensor.
- Provide ablations: MOSM vs CSM with matched number of components and parameter tying; MOSM with/without delay and phase terms; rank Q = 1 vs higher Q.
- Demonstrate extrapolation explicitly (as in Wilson & Adams) on multi-output data, where phase/delay matter.
- Discuss parameter identifiability and initialization strategies; visualize learned delays/phases to support interpretability claims.
- Clarify the Hermitian PSD notation and the real-valuedness conditions.
- Comment on computational scaling and outline a concrete sparse MOSM variant.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, interpretable, and more expressive kernel construction for MOGPs.
- Clear theoretical grounding with closed-form kernels.
- Demonstrated advantage on a challenging synthetic task.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical gains on real data; incomplete statistical testing.
- Some notational ambiguities and missing training details.
- No scalable inference demonstrated.

Overall evaluation
A solid and well-motivated contribution to kernel design for MOGPs, with clear interpretability and theoretical underpinnings. I recommend acceptance if the authors strengthen the empirical section with more decisive, statistically sound comparisons and clarify the noted technical and presentation issues.
