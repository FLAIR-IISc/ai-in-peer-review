

The paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The authors extend the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel from [6] to the multi-output setting by working in the spectral domain. Instead of directly specifying cross-covariance functions, they construct complex-valued cross-power spectral densities and use Cramér's theorem to derive the corresponding covariance functions. This approach provides clear parametric interpretation of relationships between channels, including delays and phase differences, which previous methods like Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) [5] cannot capture.

The paper's main strengths include a rigorous theoretical foundation, well-executed mathematical derivations, and a clear presentation that builds logically on previous work. The synthetic experiment demonstrates MOSM's ability to successfully learn the covariance structure of derivative and delayed signals, where it outperforms other methods in extrapolating these relationships. The real-world experiments on climate data and the Swiss Jura metal concentration dataset are well-designed, with clear descriptions of the experimental setup and proper statistical evaluation.

However, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the modest improvements over existing methods in the real-world experiments. While MOSM shows slightly better performance in some cases (e.g., Table 3 for Cadmium predictions), the differences are small and not consistently statistically significant (as noted in the climate data experiment, the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test only confirms MOSM's superiority for one sensor). The authors do not provide experiments on datasets where MOSM's unique capabilities (delays and phase differences) are clearly essential, which would better demonstrate the method's practical value.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a good balance of mathematical detail and intuitive explanation. The authors properly contextualize their work with respect to previous literature on MOGPs, including LMC [3], CONV [4], and CSM [5]. The technical quality is strong: the derivation of the MOSM kernel is sound, and the theoretical foundation using Cramér's theorem is well-justified. The implementation in GPflow [19] is standard practice for Gaussian process models.

The main weakness is that the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method. While the synthetic example is compelling, the real-world experiments show only marginal improvements over existing approaches. The authors claim that MOSM "is the only model that successfully extrapolated the derivative signal and the delayed signal simultaneously" in the synthetic example (Table 1), but this advantage isn't clearly translated to the real-world applications where the improvements are modest.

The paper's contribution is technically sound and represents a meaningful extension of previous work. The ability to model delays and phase differences between channels is a theoretically interesting advancement, but the practical impact could be better demonstrated. The authors might have strengthened their case by including experiments on datasets where these capabilities are essential for good performance.

In summary, the paper presents a well-executed technical extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output Gaussian processes with clear theoretical justification. While the real-world results don't fully demonstrate the method's practical advantages, the theoretical contribution is solid and the synthetic example is compelling. The paper would benefit from more compelling real-world demonstrations of MOSM's unique capabilities. Given the sound technical contribution and clear theoretical foundation, I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the practical impact could be better demonstrated in future work.
