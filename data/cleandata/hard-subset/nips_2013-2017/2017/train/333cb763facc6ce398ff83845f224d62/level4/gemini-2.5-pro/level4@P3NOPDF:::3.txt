### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel and elegant covariance structure for multi-output Gaussian Processes (GPs). The proposed method is a generalization of the spectral mixture approach, which leverages Bochner's theorem to construct flexible kernels for single-output GPs. A key advantage of this new "cross-spectral mixture" kernel is the interpretability of its hyperparameters, which directly model physically meaningful properties such as delays and phase differences between output channels. The authors present a theoretically sound framework and demonstrate its effectiveness on several synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Contribution:** The development of flexible and interpretable covariance structures for multi-output GPs is a challenging and important problem. This paper provides a principled and powerful contribution to the field, addressing a clear need for methods that go beyond simple intrinsic coregionalization models.

2.  **Interpretability:** A major strength of the proposed kernel is the physical interpretability of its parameters. The ability to infer properties like channel-specific spectral content, inter-channel delays, and phase shifts is a significant advantage over more "black-box" kernel constructions and could provide valuable insights in many scientific and engineering applications.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and the methodology is described with great clarity. The theoretical development is easy to follow, and the chosen illustrations are highly effective at building intuition and demonstrating the model's capabilities.

4.  **Potential Impact:** The proposed method is flexible, general, and theoretically grounded. If the practical challenges of parameter learning can be managed effectively, this approach has the potential to become a standard and widely-adopted tool for multi-output GP modeling.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

The primary concern is the practical aspect of hyperparameter optimization, which is not discussed in detail. The cross-spectral mixture kernel introduces a significant number of parameters, particularly when the number of mixture components or output dimensions is large. This complexity suggests that the marginal log-likelihood surface is likely to be highly multi-modal, making robust optimization challenging and highly sensitive to initialization. A more detailed discussion of the optimization strategy, initialization techniques used, and potential convergence issues would greatly strengthen the paper and improve its practical utility for other researchers.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  How does the model's performance and the reliability of the hyperparameter estimation degrade as the amount of training data becomes sparse? Given the model's complexity, it seems susceptible to overfitting in low-data regimes.

2.  The model couples outputs through shared spectral densities. Intuitively, a process with a very long length-scale (dominated by low frequencies) might be difficult to correlate strongly with a process with a very short length-scale (dominated by high frequencies). How does your model capture or reflect this phenomenon? Is it possible for the model to learn a strong correlation between a very smooth process and a rapidly oscillating one?
