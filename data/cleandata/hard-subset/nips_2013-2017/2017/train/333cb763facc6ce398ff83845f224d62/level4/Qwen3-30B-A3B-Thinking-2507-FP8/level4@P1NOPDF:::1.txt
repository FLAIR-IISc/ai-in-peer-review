

This paper introduces a multi-output Gaussian process covariance function defined in the spectral domain. Designing covariances in the spectral domain represents an important, yet underappreciated, contribution to Gaussian process methodology. The authors extend this approach to the multi-output setting, addressing a significant challenge: modeling cross-covariances, which need not be positive-definite. The paper successfully proposes an elegant solution that generalizes the spectral mixture covariance to multi-output GPs.

The writing is clear and concise, with a well-structured introduction that efficiently contextualizes the problem and foundational GP concepts. The explanation of the spectral mixture kernel is particularly lucid, and the proposed extensions appear novel to my knowledge. The relationship to prior work is thoroughly and accurately articulated, providing sufficient justification for the novelty of this contribution.

The experimental evaluation is adequate for the scope of this work. While the results demonstrate the method's efficacy, the analysis—particularly regarding the Jura dataset—lacks sufficient interpretability to provide strong intuitive insights. That said, given the paper's primary focus on theoretical and methodological advancement rather than empirical exploration, the experimental validation is sufficiently rigorous for publication.

This work builds upon Wilson and Adams (2013), extending their spectral mixture framework to multi-output scenarios. The core technical contribution—handling non-positive-definite cross-covariances via spectral domain modeling—is compelling and well-executed. The paper makes a solid case for its novelty and significance within the GP literature.
