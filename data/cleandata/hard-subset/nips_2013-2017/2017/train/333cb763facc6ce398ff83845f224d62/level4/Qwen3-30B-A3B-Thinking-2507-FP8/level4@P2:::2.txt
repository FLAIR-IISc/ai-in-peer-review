

This paper proposes a novel multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) covariance function called the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The authors extend the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel [6] to the multi-output setting by leveraging Cramér's Theorem as the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem. Instead of directly specifying cross-covariance functions, they construct complex-valued cross-spectral densities that are then transformed via inverse Fourier transform to yield valid covariance functions. This approach provides clear parametric interpretation of relationships across channels, including delays and phase shifts—capabilities absent in previous methods like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5] and the Spectral Mixture Linear Model of Coregionalization (SM-LMC).

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding with careful derivation of the MOSM kernel, properly citing relevant mathematical foundations (Cramér's Theorem) and establishing the conditions for positive definiteness. The authors correctly identify the limitations of prior approaches: LMC and CONV methods lack interpretability and force similar auto-covariance behaviors across channels, while CSM introduces phase differences but cannot model delays. The MOSM kernel successfully addresses these limitations by incorporating both delay and phase parameters, with the theoretical framework well-motivated and rigorously developed.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and carefully written, with a thorough background section that properly introduces necessary concepts (Gaussian processes, spectral mixture kernels, multi-output GPs). The mathematical derivations are presented logically, with appropriate notation and explanations. The authors effectively use Figures 1 and 2 to illustrate key concepts, and the experimental section clearly describes the synthetic and real-world validation procedures.

The originality of the work is solid. The extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings with the ability to model delays and phase differences represents a meaningful advancement beyond existing approaches. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior literature (LMC, CONV, CSM, SM-LMC), demonstrating a clear understanding of how their method differs from previous contributions. The connection to [6] is appropriately acknowledged, and the paper successfully builds upon this foundation with a novel extension.

Regarding significance, the proposed MOSM kernel has clear potential applications in multi-output regression tasks where channel relationships involve delays and phase shifts. The synthetic experiment (Section 4.1) convincingly demonstrates the model's ability to learn derivative and delayed relationships without prior knowledge of these relationships—something latent-variable approaches fail to achieve. However, the real-world experiments (Section 4.2 and 4.3) show only modest improvements over existing methods. The MOSM outperforms others on the Cadmium prediction in the Jura dataset (with statistical significance), but on the climate data, the differences are marginal and not consistently significant across all sensors. The paper would have benefited from experiments on datasets where previous methods fail to capture delays or phase shifts, which would better demonstrate the unique value of the proposed approach.

The quality of the paper is strong technically, with proper theoretical justification and reasonable experimental validation. The implementation details are clear, and the use of GPflow with TensorFlow for implementation is appropriate. However, the paper does not fully demonstrate the extrapolation capability that was a key motivation in [6] for single-output GPs. The synthetic example shows some extrapolation ability for the derivative and delayed signals, but the real-world experiments do not explicitly test this important aspect.

The experimental results in Tables 1-3 show only marginal performance differences between MOSM and existing methods, with the authors noting that the differences are not statistically significant in many cases (particularly for the climate data). The Kolmogorov-Smirnov test was applied to determine statistical significance, but the review of the results suggests that the performance gains are often within the standard deviation of the results. The authors should have provided more explicit statistical analysis to support their claims about the superiority of MOSM in specific cases.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical foundation using Cramér's Theorem
- Clear parametric interpretation of all model parameters
- Successful modeling of delays and phase differences in synthetic data
- Well-organized and clearly written with proper mathematical derivations
- Appropriate experimental validation on multiple datasets

Weaknesses:
- Limited demonstration of the extrapolation capability that motivated [6]
- Real-world results show only marginal improvements over existing methods
- Statistical significance of results is not consistently established
- Lacks experiments on datasets where previous methods fail to capture delays
- The relationship to the single-output SM kernel is well-established but doesn't fully leverage the extrapolation advantages

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes, with a clear theoretical extension that addresses limitations of previous approaches. While the experimental validation is appropriate, the significance of the contribution could be strengthened by demonstrating more substantial improvements in real-world scenarios where delays and phase shifts are critical. The paper would benefit from additional experiments on datasets where these features are essential to model correctly.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, clear parametric interpretation, successful modeling of delays and phase differences in synthetic data.
Con: Limited demonstration of extrapolation capability, marginal real-world performance improvements, statistical significance not consistently established.

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound extension to multi-output Gaussian processes. While the contribution is significant and the theoretical development is strong, the experimental results do not fully demonstrate the practical advantages that would make this method compelling for broader adoption. With stronger empirical evidence of the unique capabilities, particularly in terms of extrapolation and performance on datasets where delays are critical, the significance of the work would be substantially enhanced.
