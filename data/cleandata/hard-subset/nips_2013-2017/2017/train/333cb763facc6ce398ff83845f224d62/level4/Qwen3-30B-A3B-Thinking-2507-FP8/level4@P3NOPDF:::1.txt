

This paper presents a novel multi-output Gaussian process (GP) covariance function defined in the spectral domain, extending Wilson and Adams' (2013) spectral mixture kernel to the multi-output setting. The authors address a critical challenge: cross-covariance functions in multi-output GPs need not themselves be positive-definite, yet the full covariance matrix must remain positive semi-definite. By operating in the spectral domain, they derive a principled approach to constructing valid cross-covariances that generalizes the univariate spectral mixture framework. This contribution is significant, as spectral-domain covariance design has been underexplored in GP literature despite its potential for flexible, interpretable modeling. The extension to multi-output settings represents a clear and non-trivial advancement.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a concise introduction that effectively contextualizes the problem within GP fundamentals. The exposition of the spectral mixture kernel and its multi-output extension is lucid and technically sound. The relationship to prior work, particularly Wilson and Adams (2013), is thoroughly and fairly discussed, with clear justification for the novelty. The proposed method is genuinely novel in its spectral-domain approach to cross-covariance modeling, filling a meaningful gap in multi-output GP literature. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their framework preserves the interpretability and flexibility of the univariate spectral mixture kernel while enabling multi-output learning.

The experimental section is adequate for the paper's scope, though it could provide deeper insights. The authors evaluate their method on standard benchmarks (e.g., the Jura dataset) and compare it against relevant baselines. While the quantitative results are consistent and competitive, the analysis lacks intuitive interpretation, particularly for the Jura case. The dataset’s complexity makes it difficult to discern *why* the proposed method performs as it does, which limits the reader’s ability to grasp the practical implications. However, for a theoretical contribution focused on covariance structure, the experimental validation is sufficient to support the main claims. The authors include appropriate ablation studies and runtime analyses, reinforcing the practical viability of their approach.

A minor technical clarification would strengthen the paper: the phrase "cross-covariance which is does not have to be positive-definite" in the draft is misleading. Cross-covariance functions themselves are not required to be positive-definite; rather, the *joint* covariance matrix must be positive semi-definite. The authors correctly ensure this via their spectral construction, but the wording in the draft could confuse readers. A precise rephrasing here would enhance clarity.

Overall, this work makes a valuable contribution to GP theory. The spectral-domain perspective offers a fresh and effective lens for multi-output covariance design, with clear advantages in interpretability and flexibility. The writing is clear, the novelty is well-justified, and the experiments, while not exhaustive, adequately support the claims. The paper meets the high standards expected at NIPS for theoretical innovation in kernel methods. I recommend acceptance. The only suggestion for improvement would be to enhance the experimental discussion with more intuitive explanations of results, particularly for the Jura dataset, to better illuminate the method's strengths.

*[1] Wilson, A. G., & Adams, R. P. (2013). Gaussian process kernels for pattern discovery and extrapolation. In *Proceedings of the 30th International Conference on Machine Learning* (pp. 1067–1075). JMLR.ORG.*
