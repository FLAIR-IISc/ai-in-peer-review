- Introduces a novel MOGP covariance constructed by specifying cross-power spectral densities and using inverse Fourier transforms to obtain cross-covariances.  
- Extends the spectral mixture idea from single-output GPs [6] to the multi-output setting.  
- Evaluated on one synthetic and two real-world datasets (climate signals and Swiss Jura metal concentrations).  
- Well-motivated and clever approach that targets extrapolation capabilities inherent to spectral methods.  
- Experimental results show comparable performance to existing MOGP methods on the used datasets.  
- Paper could be strengthened by demonstrating extrapolation abilities similar to [6].  
- Suggests evaluating on datasets where current methods fail to highlight advantages.  
- Questions whether statistical significance tests were performed for Tables 1 and 2, given overlapping standard deviations.  
- Argument for acceptance: novelty in using spectral densities to build MOGP covariances.  
- Argument for acceptance: solid theoretical foundation based on Cramér’s Theorem.  
- Argument for acceptance: interpretable kernel parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay).  
- Argument for acceptance: method is general and extends existing approaches for more complex output relationships.  
- Argument against acceptance: performance gains are incremental on tested datasets.  
- Argument against acceptance: lack of explicit extrapolation demonstrations.  
- Argument against acceptance: absence of statistical significance testing weakens experimental conclusions.
