The paper introduces a novel type of covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes. Rather than directly specifying the cross-covariance functions, the authors propose specifying the cross-power spectral densities and utilizing the inverse Fourier transform to derive the corresponding cross-covariances. This approach follows a similar idea to the one presented in [6] for single-output Gaussian processes, where the power spectral density was represented through a mixture of Gaussians and then back-transformed to obtain a powerful kernel function with extrapolation abilities. The authors apply the newly established covariance to a synthetic data example, as well as two real-world datasets: the weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations.

Extending the idea from [6] appears to be a clever way to construct new types of covariance functions for multiple outputs. One of the primary motivations in [6] was to develop a kernel function capable of performing extrapolation. It would have been interesting to provide a covariance function for multiple outputs with the same ability. The experimental section only presents results using very common datasets, and the results obtained are similar to those achieved with other covariance functions for multiple outputs. The impact of the paper could be increased by including experiments on datasets for which none of the other models are suitable.

It is not clear whether the authors tested for the statistical significance of the results presented in Tables 1 and 2. Within the standard deviation, almost all models appear to provide similar performance, which raises questions about the significance of the observed differences.
