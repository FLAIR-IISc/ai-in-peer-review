- Introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for interpretable cross-covariances in MOGPs.  
- Extends scalar spectral mixture framework to multivariate case using Cramér’s Theorem and complex-valued cross-spectral densities.  
- Models both phase differences and delays between channels—limitations of prior methods like CSM.  
- Defines a rank-one factorization of the spectral density matrix that guarantees positive definiteness.  
- Provides closed-form covariance functions with parameters directly interpretable as physical quantities (delay, phase, magnitude).  
- Validated on synthetic data (recovering derivatives and delayed signals) and two real-world datasets (climate monitoring, Jura heavy metals).  
- Technical approach is sound and rigorously justified; correctly addresses cross-covariance positive-definiteness.  
- Closed-form derivations (Eq. 10–11) are clearly derived and mathematically rigorous.  
- Experimental design is appropriate; evaluation uses standard metrics (MAE) and statistical tests (Kolmogorov–Smirnov).  
- Minor experimental gap: no ablation studies isolating delay/phase impact in real-world settings.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized; background and innovations are explained with intuitive interpretation.  
- MOSM is novel: generalizes and subsumes SM-LMC and CSM by allowing independent spectral parameters per channel.  
- Advances significance by enabling interpretable modeling of delays and phase shifts useful in sensor fusion and geostatistics.  
- Strengths: rigorous theory, interpretable parameters, strong synthetic validation, competitive real-world performance, excellent exposition.  
- Weaknesses: limited discussion of computational scalability for large numbers of outputs; some real-data significance claims (Copper) rely on prior results.  
- Recommendation: Accept — technically sound, novel, clear, and practically impactful.
