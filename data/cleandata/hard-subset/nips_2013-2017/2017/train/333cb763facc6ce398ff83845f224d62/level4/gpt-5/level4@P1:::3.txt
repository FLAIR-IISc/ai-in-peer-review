Summary
This paper proposes a new covariance construction for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by extending the spectral mixture approach to the multivariate setting via Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s). The authors define a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities whose inverse Fourier transforms yield a Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The construction ensures positive definiteness by factorizing the spectral density matrix S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), and enforces real-valued processes by symmetrizing the spectrum. The resulting kernel provides interpretable parameters for each output pair: frequency content, magnitude, phase shift, and—importantly—delay. This generalizes prior MOGP kernels based on the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC), convolution processes, and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, offering increased flexibility (e.g., different auto-covariances per channel and explicit delays). Empirically, the method is validated on a synthetic three-output example (original signal, derivative, and delayed version) and on two real-world datasets (climate sensors and heavy metal concentrations), comparing against SM-LMC, CONV, and CSM. Implementation is in TensorFlow/GPflow; performance is assessed using MAE and, for some comparisons, Kolmogorov–Smirnov tests for statistical significance.

Evaluation
- Quality: The theoretical construction is sound. Using Cramér’s theorem and the R(ω) factorization neatly guarantees positive definiteness, and the symmetrization yields real-valued processes. The model admits closed-form kernels due to the Gaussian form of the spectral components, and the interpretation of parameters (including phase and delay) is clear. The empirical results are coherent with the theory: MOSM uniquely handles simultaneous derivative and delay relationships in the synthetic task, and performs competitively on real data, with statistically significant gains in some cases. One weakness is the limited discussion of parameter learning: initialization, identifiability, and sensitivity to local optima are not detailed, despite the large number of hyperparameters.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and clearly organized. The motivation, construction, and Fourier-domain perspective are well explained. The experimental setups are relevant and easy to follow, though a few details could be clarified (see Comments/Minor remarks).
- Originality: The work provides a principled and more expressive generalization of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output case, explicitly capturing phase and delay in cross-covariances and allowing per-output auto-covariance parameters. It subsumes SM-LMC and is more flexible than CSM by handling delays in addition to phase shifts.
- Significance: Modeling interpretable cross-channel structure (including delays) is important in many applications. If training is reliable, MOSM could become a standard tool for MOGPs. While gains over strong baselines are dataset-dependent, the synthetic study highlights clear advantages in settings where cross-covariances are not linear combinations of univariate kernels.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled construction grounded in Cramér’s theorem with guaranteed positive definiteness.
- Interpretable parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay) for each output pair.
- Greater expressiveness than LMC/CONV/CSM; generalizes prior spectral approaches.
- Closed-form kernels enable straightforward GP training and prediction.
- Strong synthetic results and competitive real-data performance; some statistically significant improvements.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited discussion of optimization: initialization strategy, identifiability, and robustness with many hyperparameters.
- Scalability and sample efficiency are not deeply analyzed; behavior with very few observations is unclear.
- On real datasets, improvements over CSM/SM-LMC are modest in some cases.

Questions
- How does the method behave when few training points are available? Is it robust to limited data given the expanded parameterization?
- More as a curiosity: a channel with a very large length-scale should not be highly correlated with a channel with a very small length-scale. How does this manifest in your model?

Comments
- In the synthetic example (Section 4.1), please specify the number of training points used per channel and per interval directly in the main text for clarity.
- A brief discussion of initialization (e.g., spectral heuristics) and practical tips to avoid local optima would be very useful to practitioners.
