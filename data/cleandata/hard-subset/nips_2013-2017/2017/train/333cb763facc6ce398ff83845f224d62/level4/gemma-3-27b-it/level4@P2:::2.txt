This paper introduces a novel approach to constructing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to define cross-spectral densities and leverage Cramer’s Theorem to transform them into corresponding cross-covariance functions. This builds upon the successful spectral mixture approach for single-output GPs [6], which allows for flexible kernel construction and extrapolation capabilities. The proposed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel is validated on a synthetic dataset and two real-world examples: climate data and heavy metal concentration measurements.

The extension of the spectral mixture concept [6] to the multi-output setting is a logical and promising direction. The motivation – to design expressive kernels with interpretable parameters – is well-articulated. The approach provides a principled way to construct MOGP kernels, ensuring positive definiteness through the use of Cramér’s Theorem. The ability to model delays and phase differences between channels, beyond simple phase shifts as in previous work [5], is a notable strength. The mathematical formulation appears sound and the connection to existing literature (Bochner’s and Cramér’s Theorems, spectral mixture kernels) is clearly established.

However, while the theoretical foundation is strong, the experimental evaluation feels somewhat limited. The chosen datasets are standard benchmarks in the MOGP literature, which is positive for comparability, but the results do not demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing methods like CSM [5] or CONV [4]. The authors acknowledge this and mention potential statistical insignificance in some cases. A more compelling demonstration of the MOSM kernel’s benefits would involve applying it to problems where existing methods struggle, or where the unique properties of MOSM – particularly its ability to model delays – are crucial. The synthetic example successfully demonstrates the modeling of derivatives and delays, but a more challenging synthetic scenario could further highlight the kernel’s capabilities.

Regarding the experimental results, it's important to assess statistical significance. The reviewer is correct to question whether the observed differences in performance (Tables 1 and 2) are meaningful, given the overlap in standard deviations. Performing statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests or ANOVA with post-hoc analysis) would strengthen the conclusions. It would be valuable to know if the improvements observed in the Jura dataset for Cadmium estimation are statistically significant.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of defining cross-spectral densities and utilizing Cramer’s Theorem for MOGP kernel construction is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is built on a solid mathematical foundation, leveraging established results in spectral analysis and Gaussian processes.
*   **Interpretability:** The MOSM kernel offers a clear spectral interpretation of its parameters, facilitating understanding and potential application-specific design.
*   **Generality:** The framework is flexible and allows for modeling complex relationships between outputs, including delays and phase differences.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Demonstration:** The experimental results, while sound, do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods on standard datasets.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The paper lacks a formal assessment of the statistical significance of the observed performance differences.
*   **Incremental Improvement:** While novel, the improvement over existing methods, particularly CSM, feels incremental in the presented experiments.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. The novelty lies in the spectral approach to kernel design, offering increased flexibility and interpretability. However, the limited empirical validation and lack of statistical significance testing weaken the overall impact. 

I recommend **weak acceptance** contingent upon the authors:

1.  Performing and reporting statistical significance tests on the results presented in Tables 1 and 2.
2.  Considering the inclusion of a more challenging dataset or a modified experiment that specifically leverages the unique capabilities of the MOSM kernel (e.g., modeling systems with significant channel delays).
3.  Providing a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed method and potential areas for future research.
