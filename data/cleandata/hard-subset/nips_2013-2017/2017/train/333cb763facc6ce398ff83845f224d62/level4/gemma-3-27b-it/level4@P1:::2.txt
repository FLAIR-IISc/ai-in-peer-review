The paper introduces a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Rather than directly specifying cross-covariance functions, the authors propose to define cross-power spectral densities and then use the inverse Fourier transform to obtain the corresponding cross-covariances. This approach extends the idea presented in [6] for single-output GPs, which represented the power spectral density as a mixture of Gaussians to create a powerful kernel with extrapolation capabilities. The proposed covariance function is evaluated on a synthetic dataset and two real-world datasets: climate signals and metal concentrations from the Swiss Jura region.

Extending the spectral mixture concept from [6] to the multi-output setting is a well-motivated and clever approach to constructing new covariance functions. A key motivation behind [6] was to develop a kernel capable of extrapolation, and it would have been valuable to demonstrate similar extrapolation abilities with the proposed multi-output covariance function. The experimental evaluation employs commonly used datasets, and the results generally show comparable performance to existing MOGP methods. Enhancing the impact of the paper could involve demonstrating superior performance on datasets where current methods struggle.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach of utilizing spectral densities to construct MOGP covariance functions is a novel contribution.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The work is grounded in established mathematical principles, specifically Cramér’s Theorem, providing a solid theoretical basis.
*   **Interpretability:** The resulting kernel provides a clear spectral interpretation of its parameters, relating them to frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay.
*   **Generality:** The proposed method extends existing approaches and offers potential for modeling more complex relationships between outputs.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** While novel, the performance gains on the tested datasets are not substantial compared to existing methods.
*   **Limited Extrapolation Demonstration:** The paper does not explicitly demonstrate extrapolation capabilities, a key benefit of the approach in the single-output case [6].
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The absence of statistical significance testing weakens the conclusions drawn from the experimental results.
