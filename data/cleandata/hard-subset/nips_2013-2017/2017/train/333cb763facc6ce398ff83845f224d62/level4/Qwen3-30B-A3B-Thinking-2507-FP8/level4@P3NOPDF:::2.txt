

The paper proposes a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes by specifying cross-power spectral densities (CPSDs) and deriving cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform, extending the spectral mixture framework of [6] from single-output to multi-output settings. This approach is conceptually sound and represents a technically competent extension of prior work. The authors demonstrate the method on three experiments: a synthetic dataset, a weather dataset, and the Swiss Jura metal concentration dataset. While the technical contribution is valid, the paper’s impact is limited by insufficient experimental justification and failure to address a key motivation from the foundational work [6].

The most significant weakness lies in the omission of extrapolation capability—a core motivation in [6] for spectral mixture kernels. The authors cite [6]’s success in enabling extrapolation via spectral mixtures, yet their multi-output extension does not demonstrate this critical property. The experimental section lacks any analysis of extrapolation performance (e.g., out-of-distribution prediction, long-term trend modeling), leaving the primary advantage of the spectral approach unverified for multi-output scenarios. This omission is particularly striking given the explicit connection to [6] and undermines the paper’s central claim about leveraging spectral representations for enhanced flexibility.

Furthermore, the experimental validation is underwhelming. The chosen datasets (weather, Swiss Jura) are standard benchmarks widely used in multi-output GP literature, where existing methods (e.g., linear model of coregionalization, separable kernels) typically achieve strong performance. Tables 1 and 2 show results comparable to baselines, with no clear evidence that the proposed method consistently outperforms competitors. The paper fails to identify a domain where conventional multi-output kernels fail and the proposed CPSD-based approach succeeds—a critical requirement for establishing novelty. Without such an example, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. For instance, the authors could have tested on datasets with complex output dependencies (e.g., multi-sensor environmental monitoring with non-stationary cross-correlations) where spectral mixtures might provide a distinct advantage.

The statistical significance of results also requires clarification. Table 1 reports mean test errors with standard deviations, but no statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests, Bayesian model comparison) are provided to confirm whether observed differences are meaningful. Within the reported standard deviations, performance across methods overlaps substantially, suggesting that any apparent gains may be negligible or non-robust. The authors should explicitly address whether differences in Table 1 (e.g., 0.12 vs. 0.13 MSE) are statistically significant, as this is a standard expectation for empirical evaluations in machine learning.

Minor technical concerns include: (1) The paper does not discuss computational complexity relative to existing multi-output kernels; given the spectral approach involves Fourier transforms, scalability to large outputs or data should be addressed. (2) The synthetic experiment lacks detail on how the ground-truth CPSD was constructed, making it difficult to assess whether the method recovers the intended structure. (3) The Swiss Jura dataset description omits key details (e.g., number of output variables, spatial resolution), hindering reproducibility.

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a technically valid extension of [6] to multi-output settings. The spectral representation framework is well-motivated and theoretically sound, and the implementation appears correct. However, the lack of demonstration of extrapolation, absence of novel experimental validation, and insufficient statistical rigor prevent the work from making a compelling case for its novelty or utility. The contribution would be substantially strengthened by: (1) Adding experiments that explicitly test extrapolation capabilities; (2) Including a dataset where standard multi-output kernels fail (e.g., highly non-stationary cross-correlations); and (3) Reporting statistical significance tests for all results.

In summary, the paper presents a technically correct but incremental extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings. While the method is sound, the experimental evaluation fails to justify its necessity or superiority over existing approaches. The omission of extrapolation analysis—a key innovation in [6]—is particularly damaging to the paper’s argument. Without addressing these gaps, the work remains a minor technical refinement rather than a meaningful advancement for multi-output GP modeling. The authors should either provide stronger empirical evidence or clarify the scope of their contribution to avoid overclaiming.
