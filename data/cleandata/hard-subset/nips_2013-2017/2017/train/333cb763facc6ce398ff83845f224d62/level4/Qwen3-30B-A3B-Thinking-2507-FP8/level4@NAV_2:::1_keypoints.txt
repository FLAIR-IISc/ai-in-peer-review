- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel: a multi-output GP covariance constructed in the spectral domain.  
- Extends Wilson & Adams (2013) spectral mixture framework to multi-output, addressing cross-covariances that need not be positive definite.  
- Employs complex-valued cross-spectral densities and Cramér’s theorem for a principled covariance design.  
- Models delays and phase differences between channels while retaining full parametric spectral interpretability.  
- Well-structured paper with a concise introduction and clear explanation of the spectral mixture kernel.  
- Theoretical development is rigorous, novel, and advances prior approaches (e.g., CSM, LMC).  
- Relations to previous work are clearly articulated, showing the MOSM’s advancement over existing methods.  
- Experimental validation includes synthetic data (recovery of known covariances) and two real-world datasets (climate signals, heavy metal concentrations).  
- Jura dataset results are difficult to intuitively interpret due to high inter-signal correlation.  
- MOSM achieves competitive performance and statistically significant improvement on Cadmium (MAE).  
- Strengths: novel extension, theoretical soundness, parametric interpretability, ability to model delays/phase, and comprehensive validation.  
- Weaknesses: limited intuitive explanation of some experimental results (Jura), some spectral-domain technical details need clarification, and the practical importance of modeling delays could be emphasized more.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution to GP literature; technical quality, clarity, and experiments support acceptance.
