The paper introduces a new covariance structure for multi-output Gaussian processes (GPs), which corresponds to the generalization of the spectral approach (Bochner Theorem) to build kernels. One particular asset of the proposed method is that the parameters of the model can be interpreted, such as delay between outputs or phase difference.

The definition of covariance structures for multi-output GPs is a challenging topic, and relatively few methods are currently available to do so. The method proposed by the authors is theoretically sound, and its effectiveness is demonstrated on several datasets. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field by providing a principled approach to designing multivariate covariance functions.

The method is clearly described in a well-written paper, and the choices for the illustrations appear relevant. If the parameter learning isn't too troublesome (the paper does not contain much information regarding this), I would expect this method to become a standard for multi-output GPs.

The paper has some strengths that are worth highlighting. Firstly, the authors have provided a thorough review of the existing literature on multi-output GPs, which helps to situate their contribution in the context of the field. Secondly, the proposed method is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, which demonstrates its effectiveness in a variety of settings.

However, there are a few questions and comments that I would like to raise. Firstly, how does the method behave when few training points are available? This is an important consideration, as many real-world applications involve limited data. Secondly, a channel with a large length-scale cannot be highly correlated with a channel with a small length-scale. How does this appear in the proposed model?

In terms of minor remarks, I noticed that a word is missing in lines 74 and 140. Additionally, in the synthetic example (Section 4.1), the number of training points should be specified.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-output GPs, and I would recommend it for publication.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper introduces a new and theoretically sound method for defining covariance structures for multi-output GPs.
* The proposed method is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in a variety of settings.
* The authors have provided a thorough review of the existing literature on multi-output GPs, which helps to situate their contribution in the context of the field.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper does not provide much information regarding the parameter learning process, which could be a potential limitation of the method.
* The method may not perform well when few training points are available, which could limit its applicability in certain real-world settings.
