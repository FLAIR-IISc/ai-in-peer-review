This paper introduces a new covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Rather than defining the cross-covariance functions directly, the proposed approach specifies the cross-power spectral densities and then recovers the corresponding cross-covariances via the inverse Fourier transform. This method extends the approach of [6] for single-output GPs, where the power spectral density was modeled as a mixture of Gaussians. The inverse Fourier transform of this spectral density yielded a flexible kernel with strong extrapolation capabilities. The authors demonstrate their method on a synthetic dataset and two real-world benchmarks: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations.

Extending the spectral mixture approach from [6] to the multi-output setting is a clever and promising direction for constructing new covariance functions. However, a key motivation of the original work in [6] was to create a kernel with strong extrapolation properties. It is therefore a missed opportunity that the authors do not demonstrate or even discuss whether their multi-output extension retains this important capability.

The experimental evaluation is limited to common benchmark datasets where the proposed model achieves performance comparable to existing MOGP methods. The paper's impact would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating its advantages on a problem where existing models are known to be unsuitable.

Regarding the results in Tables 1 and 2, have the authors performed any statistical significance tests? Given the reported standard deviations, the performance of most models appears to be statistically indistinguishable.
