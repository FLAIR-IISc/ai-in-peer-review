This paper introduces a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The core idea is to design the kernel in the spectral domain by first defining a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities. By leveraging Cramér's theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner's theorem), the authors ensure that the resulting matrix-valued covariance function is positive definite. This work is a principled extension of the single-output Spectral Mixture kernel (Wilson & Adams, 2013) to the MOGP setting. The key challenge addressed is the construction of expressive cross-covariance functions—which need not be positive definite on their own—in a way that preserves the positive definiteness of the overall MOGP kernel. The proposed MOSM kernel is notable for its ability to model complex relationships between output channels, including delays and phase shifts, offering greater flexibility and interpretability than previous methods.

**Strengths**

*   **Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a concise but thorough introduction to the necessary background on Gaussian processes, spectral mixture kernels, and the challenges of MOGP modeling. The derivation of the MOSM kernel from first principles is logical and easy to follow. The theoretical foundation of the work is solid, resting on Cramér's theorem.

*   **Originality and Significance:** The proposed approach represents a novel and important contribution to the MOGP literature. While designing kernels in the spectral domain is an important concept for single-output GPs, its extension to the multi-output case, as presented here, is non-trivial and well-executed. The method provides a more flexible and interpretable framework than existing models like CSM, particularly through its ability to explicitly model delays between channels. The paper does a good job of positioning this work relative to prior art and clearly explaining its advantages. This is a significant advancement as it could enable more sophisticated modeling in applications where inter-channel dynamics like delays and phase shifts are critical.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

The main weakness of the paper lies in the experimental evaluation. While the experiments on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate that the proposed MOSM kernel performs well and is competitive with existing methods, they offer limited intuition into the model's behavior. The synthetic experiment is effective in validating the kernel's ability to capture derivatives and delays, but the real-world experiments are less insightful. For instance, the results on the Jura dataset are presented primarily as performance tables (MAE scores), which makes it difficult for the reader to appreciate *why* the model performs well or what specific cross-channel structures it has learned.

To strengthen the paper, the authors could expand the analysis of the real-world experiments. A key claimed benefit of the MOSM kernel is its interpretability (e.g., learning delays, phases, and spectral content). It would be highly beneficial to visualize some of these learned parameters for the climate or Jura datasets. This would provide concrete evidence of the model's capabilities and offer valuable insights into the data itself, thereby better justifying the use of this more complex kernel over simpler alternatives.

**Conclusion**

Overall, despite the room for improvement in the experimental section, the paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and novel contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. The proposed MOSM kernel is an elegant and powerful tool, and the work is of sufficient quality and significance for publication. The clarity of the presentation and the importance of the problem being addressed are major strengths.
