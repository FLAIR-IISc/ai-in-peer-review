This paper introduces a novel type of covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes. Instead of directly specifying the cross-covariance functions, the authors propose a method that involves specifying the cross-power spectral densities and then using the inverse Fourier transform to obtain the corresponding cross-covariances. This approach builds upon a similar idea presented in [6] for single-output Gaussian processes, where the power spectral density was represented as a mixture of Gaussians and then back-transformed to obtain a powerful kernel function with extrapolation capabilities.

The authors apply the newly established covariance function to a synthetic data example, as well as two real-world data examples: the weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations. The idea of extending the concept from [6] to build new types of covariance functions for multiple outputs is indeed a clever one. However, one of the primary motivations in [6] was to create a kernel function with the ability to perform extrapolation. It would be interesting to see if the proposed covariance function for multiple outputs can also be designed to possess this ability.

The experimental section is somewhat limited, as it only provides results on very common datasets, and the results obtained are similar to those achieved with other covariance functions for multiple outputs. To increase the impact of the paper, it would be beneficial to include experiments on datasets for which existing models are not suitable. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.

Additionally, it is not clear if the authors tested for the statistical significance of the results presented in Tables 1 and 2. Upon inspection, it appears that almost all models provide similar performance within the standard deviation. A discussion on the statistical significance of the results would strengthen the conclusions drawn from the experiments.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to building covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes. However, with some additional experiments and analysis, the authors can further demonstrate the potential of their proposed method and increase its impact on the field.
