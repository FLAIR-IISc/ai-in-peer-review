This paper introduces a novel approach to constructing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian Processes (GPs). Rather than directly defining cross-covariance functions, the authors propose specifying cross-power spectral densities and then leveraging the inverse Fourier transform to derive the corresponding covariance functions. This strategy is inspired by the work in [6] for single-output GPs, which utilized a Gaussian mixture representation of the power spectral density to create a kernel with strong extrapolation capabilities. The proposed covariance function is evaluated on a synthetic dataset, as well as two real-world datasets: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura metal concentration dataset. 

The extension of the spectral density approach from single-output GPs to the multi-output setting represents a potentially valuable contribution. A key motivation behind the work in [6] was the development of kernels capable of extrapolation; exploring whether the proposed multi-output covariance function inherits similar properties would be a compelling direction for future work. 

Currently, the experimental evaluation relies on benchmark datasets commonly used in the multi-output GP literature. While this allows for a basic comparison, the reported results are comparable to those achieved by existing covariance functions. To strengthen the paper's impact, the authors should consider including experiments on datasets where current multi-output GP models demonstrably struggle, or where the unique characteristics of the proposed covariance function could offer a distinct advantage. 

Finally, the statistical significance of the results presented in Tables 1 and 2 is unclear. The observed performance differences between the models appear marginal, often falling within the reported standard deviations. A statistical significance test would help to determine whether the observed differences are meaningful, and would greatly enhance the robustness of the conclusions drawn from the experimental results.
