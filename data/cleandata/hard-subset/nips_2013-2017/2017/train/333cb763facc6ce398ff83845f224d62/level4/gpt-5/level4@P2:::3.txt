Summary
This paper proposes a new multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernel, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The authors construct a complex-valued family of cross-spectral densities parameterized by mixtures of Gaussians and use Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner’s theorem) to guarantee positive definiteness via a factorization in the Fourier domain. The resulting cross-covariances have interpretable parameters—magnitude, frequency, lengthscale, phase, and delay—allowing the model to capture phase shifts and time delays between channels while keeping auto-covariances as standard spectral mixture (SM) kernels. The method generalizes prior MOGP kernels: SM-LMC and CSM appear as special cases when parameters are tied across channels and delays are removed. Empirically, the authors validate MOSM on a synthetic tri-variate example (learning derivatives and delays) and two real datasets (climate and heavy metal concentrations), comparing against CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM. MOSM performs best on the synthetic task, is competitive on climate, and shows clear gains on the Cadmium task in the Jura data.

Relation to prior work
Early MOGP models such as LMC and CONV build cross-covariances via linear combinations of latent processes, which can limit interpretability and flexibility. Wilson & Adams (ICML 2013) introduced spectral mixture kernels for single outputs by designing the power spectral density (PSD). Ulrich et al. (NIPS 2015) extended SMs to multi-output via CSM to model phase differences. The present work generalizes these ideas by directly specifying complex cross-spectral densities that also encode inter-channel delays. This places MOSM as a principled and more expressive spectral construction for MOGPs.

Quality
- Theory: The construction follows Cramér’s theorem and ensures positive definiteness by modeling S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω). The symmetry needed for real-valued processes is enforced via spectral mirroring. The derivation of closed-form kernels from Gaussian spectral components is sound.
- Experiments: The synthetic study convincingly demonstrates learning of both derivative and delayed relationships. Real-data evaluations are reasonable and include significance testing where possible. However, training and model selection details (e.g., initialization, choice of Q, sensitivity to local optima) are limited.
- Implementation: Training via marginal likelihood with GPflow/TensorFlow is standard, but scalability and computational cost are not discussed in depth.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The spectral-factor construction and the interpretability of parameters are explained well. A few details (see Minor comments) would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The work is a meaningful advance over SM-LMC and CSM by introducing a parametrized family of complex cross-spectra that capture both phase and delay, with explicit parameter interpretation, and by framing a general construction for multivariate kernels in the spectral domain.

Significance
The ability to model delays and phase differences with interpretable parameters in a principled, positive-definite MOGP kernel is valuable. The approach should be broadly useful for multi-sensor, multi-channel time series. With robust training procedures and scalable inference, MOSM could become a standard tool.

Strengths
- Principled Fourier-domain construction with guaranteed positive definiteness.
- Clear parametric interpretability (frequency, lengthscale, magnitude, phase, delay).
- Generalization that subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases.
- Strong synthetic results on challenging derivative-and-delay extrapolation.
- Competitive performance on real-world tasks, with statistical testing where feasible.

Weaknesses / concerns
- Limited discussion of training stability: initialization, identifiability, and sensitivity to local optima (especially with many components and channels).
- No guidance on selecting the number of spectral components Q or on regularization.
- Scalability and computational complexity (in N, m, Q, and input dimension) are not analyzed; no sparse/structured approximation is provided.
- Limited ablations: unclear how much each parameter (delay vs phase vs independent lengthscales) contributes.

Questions for the authors
- How does MOSM perform with few training points per channel? Is the PSD parameterization robust in low-data regimes compared to latent-process models?
- More a curiosity: in practice, can a channel with a very large lengthscale be highly correlated with a channel with a very small lengthscale under MOSM? How does the model reconcile such mismatched Σi across outputs (e.g., via the induced Σij and wij)?
- How sensitive is training to initialization of µi, Σi, θi, and φi? Do you use periodogram-based or heuristic initializations?
- What is the computational complexity as a function of m and Q, and are there straightforward sparse/inducing or Kronecker/Toeplitz variants for scalability?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Solid and elegant theoretical construction with interpretable parameters.
- Demonstrated ability to model both phase and delay, beyond prior spectral MOGP kernels.
- Strong synthetic validation; competitive real-data results.

Cons:
- Insufficient training and scalability details; no sparse approximation.
- Limited guidance on model selection and initialization.
- Real-data gains are mixed; advantages are clearest on the synthetic and Cadmium tasks.

Comments on experiments
- Please specify the exact number of training points used in the synthetic experiment (Sec. 4.1) and include initialization details for all methods for fair comparison.
- Consider adding an ablation isolating the effect of delay and phase components.

Minor comments and typos
- In the synthetic example (Section 4.1), state the training set sizes explicitly in the main text.
- A word seems to be missing around lines 74 and 140.
- Clarify the symmetry enforcement step for real-valued processes (the S(ω) → 0.5[S(ω)+S(−ω)] transformation) and whether it impacts parameter interpretability.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated, theoretically sound, and interpretable MOGP kernel with promising empirical results. With more detail on training, model selection, and scalability, the method has strong potential to become a standard tool for multi-output GP modeling.
