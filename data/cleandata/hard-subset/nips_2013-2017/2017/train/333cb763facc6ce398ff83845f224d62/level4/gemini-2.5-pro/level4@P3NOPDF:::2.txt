This paper introduces a novel method for constructing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Instead of directly specifying the cross-covariance functions in the spatial or temporal domain, the authors propose to model the cross-power spectral densities (cross-PSDs) in the frequency domain. The corresponding cross-covariance functions are then recovered via the inverse Fourier transform. This approach extends the spectral mixture kernel, previously proposed for single-output GPs in [6], to the multi-output setting by representing the cross-PSDs as a mixture of complex-valued Gaussians. The authors validate their proposed covariance function on a synthetic data example and two real-world benchmarks: a weather prediction dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for heavy metal concentrations.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work is its elegant and principled approach to designing MOGP covariance functions. Extending the spectral mixture kernel framework from [6] to the multi-output case is a clever and natural idea. By working in the frequency domain, the authors provide a flexible new family of non-stationary and non-separable covariance functions. The formulation is well-grounded in Fourier theory and offers a compelling alternative to existing MOGP construction methods, such as the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC).

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While the core idea is promising, the paper could be significantly strengthened in its experimental validation and motivation.

1.  **Missed Opportunity for Demonstrating Extrapolation:** A key motivation and a celebrated feature of the original spectral mixture kernel in [6] was its impressive ability to perform long-range extrapolation. While the proposed multi-output extension is theoretically sound, the paper misses a significant opportunity to demonstrate whether this valuable property is preserved or enhanced. It would be highly compelling to see experiments specifically designed to test the model's extrapolation capabilities, as this would constitute a major advantage over many existing MOGP models.

2.  **Limited Experimental Impact:** The experimental validation feels preliminary. The chosen datasets (weather, Jura) are standard benchmarks where many MOGP models perform reasonably well. The results presented in Tables 1 and 2 show that the proposed model's performance is largely on par with, but not clearly superior to, established methods like LMC and ICM. To truly showcase the value of this new covariance family, the authors should apply their model to problems where existing MOGP kernels are known to be unsuitable or perform poorly. Demonstrating a scenario where the flexibility of modeling in the spectral domain provides a tangible benefit—for instance, in modeling complex periodic or quasi-periodic relationships between outputs that other models cannot capture—would substantially increase the impact of this work.

3.  **Statistical Significance of Results:** The performance gains reported in the experimental section appear marginal. A more rigorous analysis is needed to support the claim of improved performance.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Have you performed statistical significance tests on the results presented in Tables 1 and 2? Given the overlapping standard deviations, it is difficult to conclude that the proposed model offers a significant performance improvement over the baselines on these specific tasks.
*   Could you comment on the interpretability of the learned spectral densities? For instance, do the learned frequency components and their cross-correlations correspond to any known underlying phenomena in the real-world datasets? This could be another unique advantage of your spectral approach.
*   Was the model's ability to extrapolate beyond the range of the training data investigated? This was a key strength of the single-output spectral mixture kernel and would be a powerful motivation for its multi-output extension.
