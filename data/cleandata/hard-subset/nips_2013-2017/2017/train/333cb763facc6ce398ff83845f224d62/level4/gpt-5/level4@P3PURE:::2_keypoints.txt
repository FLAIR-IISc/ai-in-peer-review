- Introduces a new family of multi-output covariance functions by specifying cross-power spectral densities and using inverse Fourier transforms to obtain cross-covariances.

- Approach parallels spectral mixture kernels ([6]) for single-output GPs, modeling frequency content with Gaussian mixtures.

- Evaluated on a synthetic example, a weather dataset, and the Swiss Jura metal concentration dataset.

- Strength: Natural, technically sound extension—defining cross-PSDs and deriving kernels via inverse FT is a principled construction.

- Strength: Connection to spectral mixture kernels suggests potential to capture complex correlations and periodicities.

- Weakness: Paper does not demonstrate whether the multi-output model inherits the extrapolation capabilities central to [6].

- Weakness: Experimental evaluation uses common benchmarks and shows broadly similar performance to existing multi-output kernels; lacks challenging cases where the method clearly outperforms.

- Weakness: Statistical significance of results is unclear—means and standard deviations overlap and no significance tests are reported.

- Suggestion: Add experiments that explicitly test extrapolation across outputs (e.g., training on truncated time ranges or sparse frequencies, evaluating future horizons).

- Suggestion: Include tasks/datasets where spectral structure and cross-output dependencies are crucial and competing models underperform.

- Suggestion: Report formal statistical significance tests (preferably paired tests across splits) for results in Tables 1 and 2.
