- Proposes MOSM: extend spectral mixture to multi-output via Cramér’s theorem, using complex cross-spectral densities and inverse Fourier transform to obtain a kernel.  
- Ensures positive definiteness by factorizing S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) and enforces real-valued processes by symmetrizing the spectrum.  
- Provides interpretable per-output-pair parameters: frequency content, magnitude, phase shift, and delay.  
- Generalizes prior MOGP kernels (LMC, convolutional, CSM) by allowing per-output auto-covariances and explicit delays.  
- Uses Gaussian spectral components to obtain closed-form kernels, enabling straightforward GP training and prediction.  
- Implementation provided in TensorFlow/GPflow and evaluated on a synthetic three-output task plus two real datasets (climate sensors, heavy metals).  
- Empirically, uniquely handles simultaneous derivative and delay relationships in synthetic data and is competitive on real data with some statistically significant improvements.  
- Theoretical construction is sound: Cramér’s theorem and the R(ω) factorization guarantee positive definiteness and real-valuedness.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of parameter learning—initialization, identifiability, and sensitivity to local optima are not detailed.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well written and clearly organized; motivation and Fourier-domain perspective are well explained.  
- Originality: principled and expressive multivariate generalization of spectral mixture kernels that captures phase and delay.  
- Significance: modeling interpretable cross-channel structure (including delays) is important and MOSM could become a standard tool if training is reliable.  
- Pros: principled construction, interpretable parameters, greater expressiveness than prior methods, closed-form kernels, strong synthetic and competitive real-data performance.  
- Cons: sparse optimization details, unclear scalability/sample efficiency, and only modest gains over baselines on some real datasets.  
- Question: how does the method behave with very few training points given the expanded parameterization?  
- Question: how does the model handle channels with very different length-scales that intuitively should be weakly correlated?  
- Comment: specify number of training points per channel/interval in the synthetic example in the main text for clarity.  
- Comment: add a brief discussion of initialization (e.g., spectral heuristics) and practical tips to avoid local optima.
