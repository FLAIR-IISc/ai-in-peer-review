This paper proposes a novel, flexible covariance function for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs), termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The core contribution is to design the MOGP kernel in the spectral domain by first specifying a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities. By leveraging Cramér’s Theorem, the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem, the authors derive the corresponding matrix-valued covariance function via the inverse Fourier transform. This approach is a principled extension of the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel [6] and is related to the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5]. The authors demonstrate the model's capabilities on a synthetic task involving derivatives and delayed signals, and compare its performance against established MOGP methods on two real-world datasets: climate data and the Swiss Jura heavy metal dataset.

**Strengths:**

*   **Principled Derivation and Originality:** The method of constructing a valid MOGP kernel by designing a Hermitian positive-definite matrix of cross-spectral densities is elegant and theoretically sound. It provides a principled way to generalize the spectral mixture approach to the multi-output setting, going beyond previous works like CSM by explicitly modeling delays in addition to phase shifts.
*   **Expressiveness and Interpretability:** The resulting MOSM kernel is highly expressive and its parameters have a clear physical interpretation in terms of magnitudes, frequencies, phase shifts, and delays between pairs of output channels. The synthetic experiment effectively showcases this unique ability to jointly model delays and derivatives, a setting where simpler latent variable models would likely fail.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The background on Bochner's and Cramér's theorems provides the necessary context, and the derivation of the MOSM kernel from a rank-one decomposition of the spectral density matrix is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Experimental Validation of Significance:** While the model is theoretically appealing, its empirical performance on the real-world datasets is not substantially better than existing methods. In many cases, the performance of MOSM is very close to that of CSM, and the reported error bars often overlap. The impact of the paper would be significantly strengthened by including an application or dataset where the ability to model delays is crucial and where MOSM demonstrates a clear, practical advantage over other MOGP kernels.
*   **Missed Opportunity to Demonstrate Extrapolation:** A key motivation for the original SM kernel [6] was its ability to perform long-range extrapolation by discovering periodic patterns. It is not clear from the current experiments whether this powerful feature is retained by the MOSM kernel in the multi-output context. Demonstrating this capability would be a compelling argument for the proposed model.

**Questions and Suggestions:**

*   The results presented in Tables 1 and 2 show considerable overlap in the standard deviations across different models. While statistical significance is discussed for some results in the Jura dataset, was a similar analysis performed for the synthetic and climate data experiments? Clarifying the statistical significance of the reported improvements would strengthen the paper's claims.
*   Could the authors provide more intuition on the initialization of the hyperparameters? Given the complexity and number of parameters in the MOSM kernel, the model could be sensitive to initialization and prone to local optima during optimization.
