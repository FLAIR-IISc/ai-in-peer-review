

The paper proposes a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes by specifying cross-power spectral densities (CPSDs) and deriving cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform, extending the single-output spectral mixture approach of [6]. While the method is theoretically sound and aligns with [6]'s successful use of Gaussian-mixture spectral densities to enable extrapolation, the work fails to demonstrate this key capability for multi-output settings. The authors apply their covariance to synthetic data and two standard benchmarks: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura metal concentration dataset. However, the experimental results show no significant improvement over existing multi-output covariance functions (e.g., linear models of coregionalization), and the chosen datasets lack domains where current methods demonstrably fail. This limits the perceived novelty and impact of the contribution.  

The paper's core idea—leveraging CPSDs to construct flexible multi-output kernels—is conceptually appealing and technically valid. Yet, the absence of experiments on datasets requiring extrapolation (a stated motivation in [6]) weakens the narrative. For instance, applying the method to time-series with long-term trends or spatial processes beyond observed ranges would better showcase its advantages. The current experiments, while technically correct, do not distinguish the proposed approach from established alternatives. The results in Tables 1 and 2, which report similar predictive performance across models within standard deviation margins, raise questions about the method’s added value. Statistical significance tests for these comparisons are not provided, making it unclear whether the minor differences (e.g., 0.1–0.2% in RMSE) are meaningful or merely noise.  

Furthermore, the paper does not thoroughly address computational scalability or hyperparameter sensitivity for multi-output cases, which are critical for practical adoption. While the theoretical derivation is clear, the practical utility remains underexplored. The authors could strengthen their case by: (1) explicitly testing extrapolation on synthetic data with known out-of-range behavior; (2) comparing against state-of-the-art multi-output methods (e.g., deep kernel learning, hierarchical models) on a more challenging dataset; and (3) reporting statistical significance for all performance metrics.  

In summary, the paper presents a technically coherent extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings. However, the lack of compelling empirical evidence—particularly regarding extrapolation and clear performance gains—prevents it from making a substantial contribution to the field. The work would benefit from more targeted experiments that rigorously validate its theoretical advantages over existing approaches. As currently presented, the method’s novelty is overshadowed by its similarity to standard covariance functions in practice.
