The paper proposes a novel approach to designing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by specifying cross-power spectral densities and utilizing the inverse Fourier transform to obtain the corresponding cross-covariances. This idea is an extension of the work by [6], which represented the power spectral density as a mixture of Gaussians and back-transformed it to obtain a powerful kernel function with extrapolation capabilities.

The authors' approach is well-motivated, and the extension of the spectral mixture concept to MOGPs is a natural step. The proposed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel provides a clear interpretation from a spectral viewpoint, allowing for the modeling of delays and phase differences between channels. The experimental section demonstrates the effectiveness of the MOSM kernel on a synthetic data example and two real-world datasets, including climate signals and metal concentrations.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a principled approach to designing multivariate covariance functions. The use of Cramér's theorem and the factor decomposition of the spectral density matrix are well-explained and provide a solid foundation for the proposed method. The authors also provide a comprehensive review of related work and clearly position their contribution within the existing literature.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, the experimental section is somewhat limited, and the results obtained are similar to those achieved with other covariance functions for multiple-outputs. To increase the impact of the paper, it would be beneficial to include experiments on datasets where none of the other models are suitable. This would help to demonstrate the unique strengths of the MOSM kernel.

Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear discussion on the extrapolation capabilities of the proposed covariance function. As mentioned earlier, one of the motivations in [6] was to build a kernel function with the ability to perform extrapolation. It would be interesting to investigate whether the MOSM kernel retains this ability and to what extent.

Regarding the statistical significance of the results, the authors do provide some discussion on this topic. However, it would be beneficial to include more detailed information on the statistical tests performed and the results obtained. For instance, the authors mention that the MOSM model yields the best results for the Sotonmet sensor in the climate dataset, but it is unclear whether this difference is statistically significant.

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated and well-executed extension of the spectral mixture concept to MOGPs. While there are some areas where the paper could be improved, the authors provide a solid contribution to the field of Gaussian processes and multi-output regression.
