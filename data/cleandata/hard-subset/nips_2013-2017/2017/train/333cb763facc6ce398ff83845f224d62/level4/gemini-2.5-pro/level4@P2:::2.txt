This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a new family of covariance functions for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to generalize the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel proposed by Wilson and Adams (2013). Instead of directly specifying the matrix of cross-covariance functions in the time/space domain, the authors design a matrix of cross-spectral densities in the frequency domain. By leveraging Cramér's Theorem, the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem, they use the inverse Fourier transform to obtain a valid, positive-definite matrix-valued kernel. The cross-spectral densities are modeled as a mixture of complex-valued rank-one components, which provides a principled way to ensure the positive-definiteness of the resulting kernel. This construction allows the model to capture not only frequency and magnitude but also inter-channel phase shifts and time delays, making it more expressive than previous spectral methods like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The authors validate their proposed kernel on a synthetic task designed to highlight its unique properties and compare it against existing MOGP models on two real-world benchmark datasets: climate data and the Swiss Jura dataset for heavy metal concentrations.

**Strengths (Pro-acceptance arguments):**

1.  **Originality and Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a novel and elegant approach to designing expressive MOGP kernels. The generalization of the spectral mixture idea from the single-output to the multi-output setting via Cramér's Theorem is a principled and well-founded contribution. The construction of the positive-definite spectral density matrix through a rank-one decomposition is clever and ensures the validity of the resulting kernel.

2.  **Expressiveness and Interpretability:** The proposed MOSM kernel is highly expressive. Its parameters have a clear physical interpretation in the spectral domain, corresponding to the magnitude, mean frequency, spectral width, phase, and delay of each component for any pair of output channels. The ability to explicitly model delays between channels is a significant advantage over previous methods like CSM, which are special cases of the proposed MOSM framework.

3.  **Compelling Synthetic Experiment:** The synthetic experiment, which involves learning a signal, its derivative, and a delayed version, is very well-chosen. It effectively demonstrates the unique capabilities of the MOSM kernel in a scenario where simpler models based on latent processes (like LMC and CONV) fail. This example clearly showcases the value of the added flexibility in the cross-covariance structure.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The theoretical background is presented logically, leading from the single-output case to the proposed multi-output construction. The derivation of the kernel from the spectral density is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses (Con-acceptance arguments):**

1.  **Modest Empirical Gains on Real-World Data:** While the model is theoretically appealing, its empirical performance on the two real-world datasets is not consistently superior to existing methods. In many of the experimental settings (e.g., several of the climate sensor failures and the Copper prediction task), the performance of MOSM is statistically indistinguishable from the simpler CSM model. This raises the question of whether the additional complexity and number of parameters in MOSM provide a practical benefit on these standard benchmark problems.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experimental Validation:** A key motivation for the original SM kernel was its ability to perform long-range extrapolation by discovering periodic patterns. The current experiments focus on interpolation (missing data imputation) tasks on well-known datasets. The paper would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating the advantages of MOSM on a real-world problem where the additional expressiveness (especially the ability to model delays and distinct spectral properties per channel) is critical. An experiment focusing on extrapolation or a dataset with known, complex inter-channel dynamics would make a more compelling case for the model's practical significance.

3.  **Potential Optimization Challenges:** The MOSM model introduces a large number of hyperparameters, scaling with the number of outputs, input dimensions, and spectral components. The paper mentions minimizing the negative log-likelihood but does not discuss potential optimization difficulties, such as sensitivity to initialization or the presence of local minima. A brief discussion of these practical aspects would be valuable for potential users of the method.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

*   The paper reports statistical significance tests for the real-world experiments, which is commendable. However, for the climate dataset, the results for MOSM are often not statistically different from CSM. Could the authors comment on why the additional flexibility of MOSM (e.g., delays and channel-specific means/variances) might not be beneficial for these specific signals? Is it possible that the climate signals are so highly correlated that the simpler CSM model, which shares these parameters, is sufficient?

*   Regarding the synthetic experiment in Table 1, the standard deviations are quite high relative to the mean for some models. While MOSM appears to perform best, statistical significance is not discussed for this experiment. A significance test here would strengthen the claims made based on this compelling example.

*   To further improve the paper's impact, I would encourage the authors to consider an application that better highlights the unique properties of their kernel. For example, a problem in signal processing, geophysics, or econometrics where signals are known to have delays or phase shifts relative to one another could provide a stronger showcase for MOSM over existing MOGP kernels.

In summary, this paper presents a technically sound and theoretically elegant contribution to the literature on multi-output Gaussian processes. The proposed MOSM kernel is a principled generalization of spectral mixture kernels. The main weakness lies in the empirical evaluation, where the performance gains on standard benchmarks are marginal. Despite this, the novelty of the approach and its potential for modeling complex inter-domain relationships make it a valuable contribution to the field.
