- Introduces Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel by defining cross-spectral densities and using Cramér’s Theorem to obtain cross-covariances.  
- Builds on the spectral mixture approach for single-output GPs, extending it logically to multi-output settings.  
- Motivation is clear: design expressive, interpretable MOGP kernels with principled positive definiteness.  
- MOSM can model delays and phase differences between channels beyond simple phase shifts in prior work.  
- Mathematical formulation is sound and well-connected to Bochner’s and Cramér’s Theorems and related literature.  
- Validated on one synthetic dataset and two real-world datasets (climate and heavy metal concentrations).  
- Experimental evaluation uses standard MOGP benchmarks, aiding comparability but limiting novelty demonstration.  
- Results do not show a substantial or consistently significant advantage over existing methods like CSM and CONV.  
- Synthetic experiment demonstrates modeling of derivatives and delays, but more challenging scenarios are needed to highlight advantages.  
- Lacks formal statistical significance testing to confirm observed performance differences (e.g., paired t-tests or ANOVA).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty in cross-spectral construction, theoretical soundness, interpretability, and modeling generality.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical gains, absence of statistical significance analysis, and perceived incremental improvement.  
- Overall recommendation: weak acceptance contingent on adding statistical tests, a more challenging/delay-focused experiment, and a discussion of limitations and future work.
