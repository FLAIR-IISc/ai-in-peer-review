This paper proposes a new multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernel, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, constructed in the spectral domain. Building on Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem), the authors define a family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities via a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω). By choosing R’s columns as complex squared-exponential terms and enforcing spectral symmetry, they obtain closed-form, real-valued cross-covariances that combine a Gaussian envelope with a cosine term and include explicit magnitude, frequency, lengthscale, phase, and delay parameters for each pair of outputs. This extends the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel of Wilson and Adams (2013) to the multi-output setting in a way that (i) preserves positive definiteness of the full matrix-valued kernel, (ii) allows non-symmetric cross-covariances, and (iii) provides interpretable spectral parameters per output pair. The proposed formulation subsumes SM-LMC and closely relates to the CSM kernel (Ulrich et al., 2015), but additionally models inter-channel delays and relaxes parameter tying across outputs.

Relation to prior work: Earlier MOGP constructions (LMC; convolved/latent process models) express cross-covariances through linear combinations of shared latent GPs. These approaches tend to yield centered, symmetric cross-covariances with limited interpretability and often tie auto-covariances across outputs. CSM introduced phase shifts in the spectral domain within the LMC framework. MOSM advances this line by directly parameterizing the cross-spectrum with complex Gaussians, enabling both phase and delay while preserving positive definiteness via the spectral factorization.

Technical quality:
- The construction is principled: positive definiteness follows from the spectral factor model and Cramér’s theorem, and the symmetrization ensures real-valued processes. The resulting kernel family is closed-form and straightforward to use with standard GP machinery (log-marginal likelihood and predictive posterior).
- The paper clearly delineates how per-output parameters (means, covariances, phases, delays) map to pairwise cross-spectral parameters, and shows that SM-LMC and CSM arise as special/restricted cases.
- Empirically, MOSM outperforms baselines on a challenging synthetic task (joint recovery of a signal, its derivative, and a delayed version) and is competitive on two real datasets (climate sensors; Jura metals), with statistically significant gains in some settings.

Limitations and questions:
- Rank: While the derivation focuses on rank-1 spectral factors with a sum over Q components, the benefits and costs of higher-rank S(ω) are not explored empirically. A discussion of identifiability and practical initialization for many parameters (per output, per component) would be helpful.
- Complexity: There is little analysis of computational overhead versus SM-LMC/CSM, nor runtime comparisons. Guidance on choosing Q and regularization to avoid overfitting would strengthen the practical contribution.
- Scope: All experiments involve stationary settings; the approach inherits stationarity from the spectral construction. Nonstationary extensions (e.g., varying spectra) are not discussed.
- Evaluation depth: Real-data gains are modest in some cases; ablations isolating the impact of phase versus delay, and sensitivity to parameter tying across outputs, would add insight.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and well organized. The exposition of spectral design, the role of Cramér’s theorem, and the resulting kernel form is clear. Minor suggestions: summarize notation early; provide brief intuition on how delay and phase manifest in the time-domain cross-covariance; and describe initialization (e.g., via empirical spectra) to aid reproducibility.

Originality:
- The direct complex cross-spectral parameterization with explicit delay and phase, combined with a constructive PD guarantee via spectral factorization, is novel relative to LMC/CSM and provides a broader, interpretable family of multi-output kernels.

Significance:
- The method offers a useful and interpretable tool for modeling multi-sensor/time-series data where phase and delay matter. While the empirical improvements are strongest on synthetic data and mixed on real data, the kernel design principle is broadly applicable and likely to influence future MOGP work and applications.

Pros:
- Principled, elegant construction using Cramér’s theorem with closed-form kernels.
- Interpretable parameters (frequency, lengthscale, magnitude, phase, delay) at the pairwise level.
- Generalizes and subsumes prior spectral MOGP kernels (SM-LMC, CSM).
- Strong synthetic results; competitive real-data performance; statistical testing reported.

Cons:
- Limited exploration of model rank, parameter identifiability, and initialization.
- Sparse or scalable approximations not developed; no runtime comparisons.
- Modest improvements on some real datasets; few ablations to tease out where gains come from.
- Stationarity assumption; no discussion of nonstationary extensions.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide initialization heuristics (e.g., from cross-periodograms) and discuss identifiability/priors.
- Include runtime/complexity comparisons and an ablation on phase/delay components.
- Explore higher-rank spectral factors and report whether they improve fit.
- Discuss potential sparse/inducing-variable variants and nonstationary extensions.

Overall assessment: A solid, well-motivated and technically sound contribution to MOGP kernel design with clear interpretability and a principled foundation. I recommend acceptance.
