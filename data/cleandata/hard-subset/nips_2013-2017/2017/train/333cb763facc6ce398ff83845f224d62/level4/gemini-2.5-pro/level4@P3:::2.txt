### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel and principled method for constructing expressive covariance functions for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The proposed kernel, termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, is derived by first defining a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities in the frequency domain. By leveraging Cramér’s Theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner’s Theorem), these spectral densities are then transformed via the inverse Fourier transform into a valid, positive-definite matrix-valued covariance function.

This work extends the core idea of the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel [6] to the multi-output setting. The key contribution is a principled construction that ensures the positive-definiteness of the spectral density matrix through a factor decomposition. This allows the resulting MOSM kernel to model not only phase differences between output channels, similar to the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5], but also time delays and channel-specific spectral properties (frequencies and lengthscales). The authors validate their model on a synthetic dataset designed to highlight its unique capabilities, as well as on two common real-world MOGP benchmark datasets (climate data and the Jura dataset).

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the MOSM kernel is principled, starting from the foundational Cramér's Theorem and using a valid factorization (`S(ω) = R^H(ω)R(ω)`) to ensure the positive-definiteness of the cross-spectral density matrix. This provides a solid theoretical underpinning for the proposed kernel family. The synthetic experiment is well-conceived and effectively demonstrates the model's unique ability to jointly learn derivative and delay relationships between channels, something that simpler models like SM-LMC cannot do. This provides strong evidence for the expressiveness of the kernel.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The authors provide the necessary background on Bochner's and Cramér's theorems, making the subsequent derivation in Section 3 easy to follow. The connection between the kernel's hyperparameters and their physical interpretation (magnitude, delay, phase) is clearly explained and is one of the main strengths of this approach.

**Originality:** The work is novel and represents a significant step forward in the design of interpretable MOGP kernels. While it builds upon the ideas of SM [6] and CSM [5], the proposed formulation is more general and theoretically grounded. Specifically, a key distinction from CSM is that MOSM does not require all channels to share the same spectral means (`μi`) or covariances (`Σi`), and it introduces an explicit delay parameter (`θij`). The use of Cramér’s Theorem as a constructive principle for a flexible parametric kernel is an elegant and original contribution.

**Significance:** The proposed kernel has high potential significance due to its expressiveness and interpretability. The ability to parametrically model delays and phase shifts could be highly valuable in fields like geostatistics, econometrics, and multi-sensor signal processing, where such relationships are common.

However, the demonstrated significance in the paper is somewhat limited by the experimental results on real-world data. While the synthetic experiment is a strong proof of concept, the results on the climate and Jura datasets are less compelling. In these experiments, the performance of the more flexible MOSM model is often statistically on par with the simpler CSM model. The paper would have a much greater impact if it included a real-world application where the unique features of MOSM—particularly its ability to model delays or distinct spectral content across channels—lead to a demonstrably superior result that could not be achieved by prior methods.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The paper presents a theoretically sound and elegant method for constructing a new family of MOGP kernels based on Cramér's Theorem.
2.  **High Expressiveness and Interpretability:** The resulting MOSM kernel is a clear generalization of previous spectral MOGP kernels and can model complex inter-channel dependencies, including delays, which is a novel feature.
3.  **Strong Proof of Concept:** The synthetic experiment convincingly demonstrates the unique capabilities of the proposed kernel in a setting where simpler models are known to fail.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Empirical Gains on Real-World Benchmarks:** The experimental evaluation on the two real-world datasets does not show a clear and consistent advantage over the existing CSM model. The performance gains are often marginal and not statistically significant, which raises questions about the practical utility of the added model complexity for these types of problems.
2.  **Missed Opportunity to Demonstrate Significance:** The paper fails to showcase a real-world problem where the unique capabilities of MOSM are necessary for achieving state-of-the-art performance. Finding and analyzing such a dataset would have made a much stronger case for the paper's impact.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Justifying Model Complexity vs. Performance:** In Tables 2 and 3, you commendably perform statistical significance tests. However, these tests often show that the performance of MOSM is indistinguishable from that of CSM. Given that MOSM has a larger number of parameters and thus higher complexity, this suggests that for these particular datasets, the simpler CSM model might be preferred. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on this trade-off between model flexibility and empirical performance. Is it possible that the chosen datasets are too simple to require the full expressiveness of MOSM?

2.  **Extrapolation Capabilities:** One of the celebrated features of the original single-output SM kernel [6] was its ability to perform long-range extrapolation by discovering periodic patterns. It would be very interesting to see if this powerful property translates to the multi-output case. An experiment on a time-series dataset requiring extrapolation could significantly bolster the claims of the paper and highlight another key advantage of this spectral approach.

3.  **Optimization Challenges:** The MOSM kernel introduces a significant number of hyperparameters, particularly as the number of outputs (`m`) and spectral components (`Q`) grows. Could you comment on the practical challenges of optimizing the negative log-likelihood in this high-dimensional parameter space? For instance, is the optimization sensitive to initialization, and did you employ any specific strategies to avoid poor local minima?

Overall, this is a very good paper with a strong theoretical contribution. My main reservation lies in the gap between the model's theoretical potential, so clearly demonstrated in the synthetic example, and its realized performance on the chosen real-world benchmarks. Strengthening the experimental section with a more compelling real-world application would elevate this work from a good contribution to an excellent one.
