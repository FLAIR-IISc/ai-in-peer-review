The paper proposes a novel type of covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes, deviating from the conventional approach of directly specifying cross-covariance functions. Instead, the authors suggest specifying cross-power spectral densities and utilizing the inverse Fourier transform to derive the corresponding cross-covariances. This approach bears resemblance to the idea presented in [6] for single-output Gaussian processes, where the power spectral density was represented as a mixture of Gaussians and subsequently back-transformed to obtain a powerful kernel function with extrapolation capabilities.

The authors apply the newly established covariance function to a synthetic data example, as well as two real-world datasets: the weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations. Extending the idea from [6] appears to be a clever method for constructing new types of covariance functions for multiple outputs. One of the primary motivations in [6] was to develop a kernel function capable of performing extrapolation. It would have been intriguing to provide this covariance function for multiple outputs with the same capability.

The experimental section, however, only presents results using relatively common datasets, and the obtained results are comparable to those achieved with other covariance functions for multiple outputs. The paper's impact could be increased by incorporating experiments on datasets for which existing models are not suitable. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed covariance function's strengths and limitations.

Furthermore, it is unclear whether the authors tested for the statistical significance of the results presented in Tables 1 and 2. Upon examination, it appears that, within the standard deviation, almost all models exhibit similar performance.
