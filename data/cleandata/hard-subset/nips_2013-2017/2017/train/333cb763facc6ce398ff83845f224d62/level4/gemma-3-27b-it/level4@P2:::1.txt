This paper introduces a novel multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) covariance function constructed in the spectral domain. Designing covariances in the frequency domain, rather than directly in the spatial or temporal domain, is a valuable approach within the GP literature that, in my opinion, has been historically underappreciated. This work extends this concept to the multi-output setting, addressing the challenge of defining cross-covariances that aren’t necessarily positive-definite. The authors present a principled method for constructing a spectral mixture covariance function for MOGPs, building upon Cramér’s Theorem and offering a flexible way to model relationships between outputs.

The paper is well-written and clearly introduces the problem and relevant GP fundamentals. The explanation of the spectral mixture kernel and its extensions is lucid, and to the best of my knowledge, the proposed approach is novel. The authors effectively contextualize their work within the existing literature, and I believe the contribution demonstrates sufficient originality to warrant publication. The connection to previous work, notably [1], is clearly established, and the authors successfully build upon it.

The experimental evaluation is adequate for this type of theoretical contribution. While the results don't necessarily offer strong intuitive insights, especially regarding the Jura dataset, the experiments provide a solid validation of the method. The synthetic experiment effectively demonstrates the model’s ability to capture known covariance structures, including delays and derivatives. The real-world experiments offer a comparison to existing MOGP methods, establishing competitive performance. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach represents a novel extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output Gaussian process framework.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is grounded in Cramér’s Theorem, providing a principled approach to covariance function design.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed method.
*   **Completeness:** The paper includes a sufficient experimental evaluation, including both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   **Potential Impact:** This work could encourage further research into spectral domain methods for MOGPs, potentially leading to more expressive and interpretable models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Intuition from Experiments:** The experimental results, particularly on the Jura dataset, don’t offer strong qualitative insights into the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   **Complexity:** The mathematical framework, while sound, might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with spectral analysis and Bochner’s/Cramér’s Theorems.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of Gaussian processes. The approach is novel, theoretically sound, and clearly presented. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more in-depth analysis, it is sufficient to demonstrate the validity of the proposed method. I recommend acceptance.
