This paper introduces a novel method for constructing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Instead of directly modeling the cross-covariance functions, the authors propose a spectral approach. They define the cross-power spectral densities and then recover the corresponding cross-covariance functions via the inverse Fourier transform. This approach extends the spectral mixture kernel proposed by [6] for single-output GPs, where the power spectral density was modeled as a Gaussian mixture, resulting in a flexible kernel with strong extrapolation capabilities. The proposed covariance function is evaluated on a synthetic dataset and two real-world benchmarks: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations.

The extension of the spectral mixture kernel from [6] to the multi-output setting is a clever and promising direction for developing new MOGP covariance functions. However, the evaluation could be strengthened. A key motivation of the original work in [6] was the kernel's ability to extrapolate beyond the training data. The current paper does not explore or demonstrate whether this significant advantage is retained in the proposed multi-output formulation. It would be compelling to see if the new covariance function also possesses these extrapolation properties, as this would be a major selling point.

Furthermore, the experimental section relies on common benchmark datasets where the proposed model achieves performance that is comparable to, but not clearly superior to, existing MOGP methods. The paper's impact would be significantly increased by including experiments on datasets where traditional MOGP models are known to struggle or are unsuitable. This would more clearly demonstrate the unique value and necessity of this new class of covariance functions.

Regarding the results presented in Tables 1 and 2, it is unclear whether the performance differences between the models are statistically significant. Considering the reported standard deviations, the performance of most models appears to be very similar. Have any significance tests been performed? Clarification on this point would help in accurately interpreting the comparative results.
