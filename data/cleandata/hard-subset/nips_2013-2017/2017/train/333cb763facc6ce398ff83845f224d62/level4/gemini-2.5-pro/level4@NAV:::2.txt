This paper introduces a novel method for constructing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes. The proposed approach defines these functions in the spectral domain by parameterizing the cross-power spectral densities, rather than the cross-covariances directly. The corresponding covariance functions are then recovered via the inverse Fourier transform. This methodology extends the work of [6], which applied a similar principle to single-output GPs by modeling the power spectral density as a Gaussian mixture to derive a flexible kernel with strong extrapolation capabilities. The efficacy of the proposed covariance is demonstrated on a synthetic dataset and two real-world benchmarks: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for heavy metal concentrations.

The generalization of the spectral approach from [6] to the multi-output setting is a conceptually elegant and promising direction for developing new covariance functions. However, a key advantage highlighted in [6] was the resulting kernel's capacity for extrapolation. It is surprising that this paper does not investigate whether their multi-output extension inherits this valuable property, which would have been a compelling demonstration of its utility. The empirical evaluation is limited by its reliance on standard benchmark datasets, on which the proposed model achieves performance that is comparable, but not superior, to existing multi-output covariance functions. To significantly strengthen the paper's contribution, the authors should consider applying their model to more challenging problems where conventional models are known to perform poorly, as this would better showcase the unique benefits of the proposed approach.

Regarding the results presented in Tables 1 and 2, have the authors conducted statistical significance tests? The reported performance metrics for the different models appear to overlap considerably, especially when accounting for the standard deviations, suggesting that the observed differences may not be statistically significant.
