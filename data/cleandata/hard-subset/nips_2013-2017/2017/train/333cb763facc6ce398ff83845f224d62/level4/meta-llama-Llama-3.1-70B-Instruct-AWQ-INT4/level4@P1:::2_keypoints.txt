- Proposes a new multi-output GP covariance by specifying cross-power spectral densities and inverse Fourier transforming to get cross-covariances.
- Extends Wilson et al. (2013) spectral mixture idea from single-output to multi-output GPs.
- Applied to synthetic data, a weather dataset, and the Swiss Jura metal concentration dataset.
- Experimental results show the method is competitive with existing multi-output GP approaches.
- Results on common datasets are similar to other methods and do not show a clear advantage.
- The paper does not evaluate whether the multi-output spectral mixture kernel retains the extrapolation ability of single-output spectral mixtures.
- Experiments are limited to standard datasets; need datasets where other models perform poorly to demonstrate unique strengths.
- Authors should test statistical significance of results (Tables 1 and 2) since performance differences may be within standard deviation.
- Strength: novel covariance formulation grounded in a clear, well-established single-output idea.
- Strength: technically sound, well-executed, and clearly written with thorough experimental presentation.
- Weakness: lacks demonstration of unique benefits (e.g., extrapolation) and broader empirical validation.
- Weakness: absence of statistical significance testing undermines claims of improvement.
- Originality: new multi-output kernel but conceptually based on existing single-output spectral mixtures.
- Significance: competitive method but no clear evidence of significant improvement over existing methods.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, solid technical work, competitive empirical performance.
- Arguments for rejection: missing extrapolation analysis, limited/didactic datasets, and no significance testing.
