- Introduces MOSM: a principled multi-output spectral mixture kernel derived from parametric complex cross-spectral densities and Cramér’s Theorem.
- MOSM ensures positive-definite spectral density via factorization S(ω)=R^H(ω)R(ω) and converts it to a covariance by inverse Fourier transform.
- MOSM generalizes single-output SM and prior CSM by modeling phase differences, explicit delays, and channel-specific spectral properties (frequencies, lengthscales).
- Theoretical derivation is sound and well-grounded in Bochner/Cramér background, with clear interpretation of hyperparameters (magnitude, delay, phase).
- Synthetic experiment convincingly shows MOSM can jointly learn derivative and delay relationships that simpler models (e.g., SM-LMC) cannot.
- Novelty: MOSM is more general and theoretically principled than CSM, allowing per-channel spectral means/covariances and explicit delay parameters.
- Potential significance: expressive, interpretable modeling of delays and phase shifts is valuable for domains like geostatistics and multi-sensor signal processing.
- Real-world results are mixed: MOSM often performs on par with simpler CSM on climate and Jura datasets, limiting demonstrated practical impact.
- Empirical gains are frequently marginal and not statistically significant, raising questions about the benefit versus added model complexity.
- Paper lacks a real-world application where MOSM’s unique features (delays/distinct spectra) yield clear, superior performance.
- Need discussion on trade-off between MOSM complexity (more parameters) and empirical performance; simpler models might be preferred for some datasets.
- Recommend experiments on extrapolation tasks to test whether SM’s long-range extrapolation benefits extend to the multi-output case.
- Ask authors to address optimization challenges: sensitivity to initialization, high-dimensional hyperparameter optimization, and strategies to avoid poor local minima.
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical contribution and proof-of-concept, but limited empirical evidence on real-world benchmarks weakens the case for broad impact.
