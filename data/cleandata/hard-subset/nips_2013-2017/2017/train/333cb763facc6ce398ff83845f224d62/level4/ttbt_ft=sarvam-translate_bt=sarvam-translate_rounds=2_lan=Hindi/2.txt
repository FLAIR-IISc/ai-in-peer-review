This paper proposes a new type of covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes. Instead of directly specifying the cross-covariance function, the authors propose using an inverse Fourier transform to specify the cross-power spectral density and find the corresponding cross-covariance. This paper is similar to the idea proposed by [6] for single-output Gaussian processes, where the power spectral density was represented through Gaussian blending and then transformed back to obtain a powerful kernel function with extrapolation capabilities. The authors applied the newly established covariance to a synthetic data example, and also applied it to two real-world data examples, the weather dataset for metal concentration and the Swiss Jura dataset.

Creating a new type of covariance function for multiple outputs seems like a clever way to extend the idea from [6]. One of the motivations in [6] was to create a kernel function with the ability of extrapolation. It would be interesting to provide this covariance function with the same ability for multiple outputs. The experimental section provides results with only very general datasets, and the results obtained are similar to those obtained with other covariance functions for multiple outputs. The impact of this paper can be enhanced by including experiments in datasets for which no other model is suitable.
