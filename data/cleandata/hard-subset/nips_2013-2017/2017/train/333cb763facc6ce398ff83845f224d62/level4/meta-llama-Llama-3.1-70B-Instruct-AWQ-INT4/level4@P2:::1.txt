This paper presents a multi-output Gaussian process covariance function that is defined in the spectral domain. The approach of designing covariances in the spectral domain is an important contribution to GPs that has not received sufficient attention. This paper extends this to the multi-output scenario, which is a challenging task due to the need to describe cross-covariance that does not have to be positive-definite.

The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and GP fundamentals. The explanation of the spectral mixture kernel is well-written, and the proposed extensions are novel. The relationship to previous work is well-explained, and there is sufficient novelty in the work to justify publication.

The paper's main contribution is the proposal of a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and the construction of a multivariate covariance function using Cramér's Theorem. This approach allows for the modeling of delays among channels in addition to phase differences, making it more expressive than previous methods. The authors also provide a clear interpretation of the parameters in spectral terms.

The experiments are sufficient for a paper of this type, although they do not provide a lot of intuition, especially with the Jura dataset. However, the experimental evaluation is sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors have also provided sufficient background information and references to related work.

Regarding originality, the paper presents a novel approach to designing multi-output Gaussian process covariance functions in the spectral domain. The use of Cramér's Theorem and the construction of a multivariate covariance function using complex-valued cross-spectral densities are new contributions.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the modeling of complex relationships between multiple outputs in Gaussian process regression models. The proposed method can be used in various applications, such as climate modeling, signal processing, and geostatistics.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors have provided sufficient explanations and examples to facilitate understanding. The notation is clear, and the equations are well-formatted.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper for publication. The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to designing multi-output Gaussian process covariance functions in the spectral domain. The experiments are sufficient, and the writing is clear and concise.
