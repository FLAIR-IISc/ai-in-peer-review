

The paper introduces a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes by specifying cross-power spectral densities rather than direct cross-covariance functions, then deriving the corresponding cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform. This approach extends the methodology of [6], which used a mixture of Gaussians in the power spectral density for single-output Gaussian processes to obtain a kernel with extrapolation capabilities. The authors apply their covariance function to a synthetic dataset and two real-world datasets: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations.  

While extending [6]'s idea to multi-output settings is conceptually sound, the paper fails to address a key motivation from [6]: the ability to perform extrapolation. It would have been valuable to demonstrate that the proposed covariance function inherits this extrapolation property for multi-output scenarios. Furthermore, the experimental section relies on standard benchmark datasets, yielding results comparable to existing multi-output covariance functions. The paper's impact would be significantly strengthened by including experiments on datasets where current models are unsuitable.  

Additionally, the statistical significance of the results in Tables 1 and 2 is unclear. Within the reported standard deviations, all models exhibit comparable performance, raising questions about whether the observed differences are meaningful.
