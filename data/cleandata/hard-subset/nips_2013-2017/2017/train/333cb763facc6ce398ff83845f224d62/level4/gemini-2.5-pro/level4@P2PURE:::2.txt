This paper proposes a novel method for constructing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Rather than specifying the cross-covariance functions directly, the proposed approach models the cross-power spectral densities and recovers the corresponding cross-covariances via the inverse Fourier transform. This method extends the work of [6] for single-output GPs, which represented the power spectral density as a mixture of Gaussians. The inverse Fourier transform of this spectral representation yielded a flexible kernel with strong extrapolation capabilities. The authors evaluate their proposed covariance function on one synthetic dataset and two real-world datasets: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations.

The extension of the spectral mixture approach from [6] to the multi-output setting is a clever and promising direction. However, a key motivation of [6] was to create a kernel with the ability to extrapolate. It is a missed opportunity that this paper does not investigate whether the proposed multi-output kernel retains this valuable property. The experimental evaluation is limited to common benchmark datasets where the proposed model achieves performance comparable to existing MOGP methods. The paper's impact could be significantly strengthened by demonstrating its effectiveness on tasks where conventional models are known to perform poorly.

Regarding the experiments, were statistical significance tests performed for the results in Tables 1 and 2? Considering the reported standard deviations, the performance differences between most models appear to be marginal.
