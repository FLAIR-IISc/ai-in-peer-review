Summary
The paper proposes a multi-output Gaussian process (GP) covariance function defined in the spectral domain, extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel of Wilson and Adams [1] to vector-valued processes. The key challenge in the multi-output setting is to specify cross-covariances that, while not individually required to be positive-definite, still yield a jointly positive semidefinite kernel. The authors address this by constructing cross-spectral densities that ensure global positive semidefiniteness and lead to a flexible, interpretable multi-output SM kernel. The manuscript is clearly written, provides a concise introduction to GPs and spectral methods, and situates the contribution well within the literature.

Strengths
- Clear and well-structured presentation with an accessible overview of GP fundamentals and the SM kernel.
- A principled and, to my knowledge, novel extension of spectral mixture ideas to the multi-output setting.
- The construction of cross-spectral densities appears sound and addresses the central difficulty of ensuring joint positive semidefiniteness.
- Good discussion of related work and of how the proposed kernel relates to existing approaches.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The experimental section, while adequate for a methodological paper, offers limited intuition. In particular, the Jura dataset is hard to interpret, which makes it difficult to understand what the model is learning across outputs.
- Scalability and parameterization complexity are not fully discussed. Multi-output SM kernels can involve many parameters (per component and per output pair), raising concerns about optimization difficulty, identifiability, and computational cost as the number of outputs or mixture components grows.
- It is not entirely clear from the current draft what conditions are enforced to guarantee that the cross-spectral density matrix is positive semidefinite for all frequencies, and how these conditions translate to constraints on the parameters in practice (e.g., weight matrices, phase terms, and bandwidths).
- A more systematic comparison against strong multi-output baselines would strengthen the empirical case, especially standardized kernels such as ICM/LMC with Matérn or SE components, and related spectral approaches (e.g., generalized spectral mixture variants).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add qualitative analyses to complement the quantitative results:
  - Visualizations of the learned cross-spectral density matrices or cross-covariances, including inferred frequencies, bandwidths, and phases/delays between outputs.
  - Simple synthetic experiments that demonstrate the model’s ability to recover known inter-output delays, negative correlations, and shared/unique frequency structure.
- Clarify theoretical guarantees:
  - State explicitly the sufficient conditions used to ensure positive semidefiniteness of the cross-spectral matrix at each frequency and summarize the proof idea (or provide an appendix reference). Make clear how Hermitian symmetry and parameter constraints are enforced so that time-domain covariances are real-valued.
  - Discuss identifiability issues (e.g., permutation and scaling symmetries between mixture components) and any priors or constraints used to mitigate them.
- Expand on computation and scalability:
  - Provide the computational complexity in terms of N (per-output data), P (number of outputs), and Q (number of mixture components), and discuss memory requirements. Indicate whether the kernel admits structure (e.g., Kronecker, block Toeplitz) that can be exploited, or whether inducing-point/sparse approximations are used.
  - Report the number of restarts, initialization strategies for frequencies and bandwidths, and sensitivity analyses for Q. An ablation varying Q and regularization would help readers trade off interpretability and performance.
- Strengthen baselines and reporting:
  - Include comparisons with ICM/LMC (with Matérn/SE), separable kernels, and existing multi-output spectral or quasi-periodic kernels.
  - Report uncertainty calibration (e.g., negative log predictive density) in addition to RMSE/MAE, since extrapolation and uncertainty quantification are central to SM kernels.

Minor comments
- Ensure consistent Fourier transform conventions and notation for real versus complex spectra; clarify how conjugate symmetry is enforced so that the time-domain covariances are real.
- Briefly discuss whether the construction is strictly stationary; if so, comment on possible extensions to nonstationary multi-output kernels (e.g., generalized spectral mixtures).
- A small schematic of the parameterization (per component: frequencies, bandwidths, output coregionalization/phase parameters) would improve clarity.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written and timely contribution that advances spectral methods for Gaussian processes to the multi-output setting. The theoretical construction appears principled, and the paper is grounded in prior work with a clear exposition. While the experiments could provide more intuition and broader comparisons, they are adequate for a methodology-focused submission. With additional clarification on constraints, scalability, and qualitative insights, this work would be a strong addition to the literature.

Recommendation: Accept.

Reference
[1] Wilson, A. G., and Adams, R. P. (2013). Gaussian process kernels for pattern discovery and extrapolation. Proceedings of ICML 2013, 1067–1075.
