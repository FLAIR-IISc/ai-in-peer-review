This paper presents a novel multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) covariance function constructed in the spectral domain. Designing covariances in the spectral domain is a valuable, though often underappreciated, approach to GP modeling. This work extends this concept to the multi-output setting, addressing the challenge of defining cross-covariances without the positive-definiteness constraints of single-output kernels. The authors propose a principled method leveraging Cramér’s Theorem to build a spectral mixture kernel for MOGPs, enabling the modeling of delays and phase differences between channels.

The paper is well-written and clearly introduces the problem, providing a concise review of GP fundamentals. The explanation of the spectral mixture kernel and its extensions is particularly lucid, and to the best of my knowledge, the proposed approach is novel. The connection to prior work, including [1], is well-established, and the authors convincingly argue for the originality and significance of their contribution. 

The experimental evaluation is adequate for this type of theoretical work. While the results don’t offer deep intuitive insights – particularly the Jura dataset, which is inherently complex – the authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world data. The comparison to existing MOGP methods (SM-LMC, CONV, and CSM) is appropriate. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed MOSM kernel represents a novel extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting, offering increased expressiveness through the explicit modeling of delays and phase differences.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The construction of the kernel is rigorously grounded in Cramér’s Theorem, ensuring its theoretical validity.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with spectral methods.
* **Clear Parameter Interpretation:** The spectral formulation provides a clear and intuitive interpretation of the kernel’s parameters.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Depth:** While sufficient, the experimental evaluation could benefit from a more in-depth analysis and more intuitive examples. The Jura dataset’s complexity makes it difficult to draw definitive conclusions.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational implications of using the proposed kernel, particularly compared to other MOGP methods. A discussion of scalability would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons are made to existing methods, a more thorough evaluation against state-of-the-art MOGP techniques would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP covariance function design.
* The proposed method extends existing spectral mixture techniques in a meaningful way, addressing a key limitation of previous approaches.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation lacks depth and could be strengthened with more intuitive examples and a more thorough analysis.
* The computational cost of the proposed method is not discussed.
* The comparison to other MOGP methods is not exhaustive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Gaussian processes. The proposed MOSM kernel is a significant advancement in MOGP modeling, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. Despite some minor weaknesses in the experimental evaluation and computational discussion, the strengths of the work outweigh its limitations. I recommend acceptance.
