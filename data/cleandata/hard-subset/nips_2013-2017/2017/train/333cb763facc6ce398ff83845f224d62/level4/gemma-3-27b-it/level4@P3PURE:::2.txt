The paper introduces a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes. Rather than directly specifying cross-covariance functions, the authors propose to define cross-power spectral densities and then utilize the inverse Fourier transform to recover the corresponding cross-covariances. This approach mirrors the work in [6] for single-output Gaussian processes, where a mixture of Gaussians was employed to represent the power spectral density, ultimately leading to a kernel function with strong extrapolation capabilities. The proposed covariance function is demonstrated on a synthetic dataset, as well as two real-world datasets: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations.

Extending the idea from [6] to the multi-output setting is a clever strategy for constructing new covariance functions. A key motivation of [6] was the development of a kernel function capable of extrapolation; it would have been valuable to explore whether the proposed multi-output covariance function inherits this property. The experimental evaluation relies on commonly used datasets, and the resulting performance is comparable to that achieved by existing multi-output covariance functions. The impact of this work could be strengthened by including experiments on datasets where current models struggle or perform poorly. 

Furthermore, the statistical significance of the results presented in Tables 1 and 2 remains unclear. The observed performance appears largely comparable across all models, within the reported standard deviations. It would be beneficial to include a statistical test to determine if the observed differences are significant.
