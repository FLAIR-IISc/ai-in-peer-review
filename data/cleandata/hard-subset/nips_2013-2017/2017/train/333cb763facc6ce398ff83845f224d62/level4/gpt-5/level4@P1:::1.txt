This paper presents a multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) covariance function defined in the spectral domain. The approach of designing covariances spectrally is important and, in my view, underappreciated in the GP literature. The work extends the spectral mixture (SM) kernel of Wilson and Adams [1] from the single-output to the multi-output setting. The core challenge in the multi-output case is specifying cross-covariances, which need not be positive-definite individually. The authors propose precisely such a construction, leading to a spectral mixture covariance for MOGPs.

The paper is well written, with a concise and clear introduction to the problem and to GP fundamentals. The explanation of the SM kernel is clear, and the proposed extensions to the multi-output case appear, to my knowledge, novel. The relation to previous work is well explained, and I believe there is sufficient novelty to justify publication.

Regarding empirical evaluation, the experiments are adequate for a methodological contribution of this type. They do not provide much intuition, however; in particular, the Jura dataset is hard to reason about qualitatively. Nonetheless, for this paper, I find the experimental evaluation sufficient.

Quality: The paper is technically sound. The authors address the key issue of constructing cross-covariances by working in the Fourier domain and using complex-valued cross-spectral densities, with positive definiteness ensured via Cramér’s theorem. The resulting multi-output spectral mixture (MOSM) kernel retains interpretability in terms of lengthscales/frequencies, magnitudes, phases, and delays across channels. The methodological development is clearly presented, and the experiments, while not exhaustive, are appropriate and support the claims.

Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well organized. The background on GPs and the SM kernel is succinct and sufficient to follow the main contribution. The connection to prior work is articulated well.

Originality: Extending the SM construction to multi-output GPs by explicitly designing complex-valued cross-spectral densities, and thereby modeling both phase shifts and delays with a parametric, interpretable kernel, is novel to the best of my knowledge. The paper goes beyond earlier SM-based multi-output formulations by enabling richer cross-channel structure while maintaining a principled spectral interpretation.

Significance: Spectral-domain kernel design is a powerful idea that facilitates interpretability and flexible modeling of periodic and long-range structure. Bringing this perspective to MOGPs is valuable and likely to influence future work. While the empirical gains are not uniformly large, the method’s expressiveness and interpretability make it a meaningful contribution.

Minor limitations: The experiments, particularly on the Jura dataset, are hard to interpret intuitively, and the empirical section could do more to build intuition about when the added flexibility (e.g., delays and phase shifts) is critical. Nevertheless, for this submission, the current evaluation is acceptable.

Overall, I recommend acceptance.

Reference:
[1] Wilson, A. G., & Adams, R. P. (2013). Gaussian process kernels for pattern discovery and extrapolation. In Proceedings of the 30th International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML), pp. 1067–1075. JMLR.org.
