This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel approach to defining covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to specify cross-power spectral densities and utilize the inverse Fourier transform (via Cramér’s Theorem) to construct corresponding cross-covariance functions. This builds upon the successful spectral mixture approach for single-output GPs [6], extending it to the multi-output setting and aiming to provide a more flexible and interpretable framework for modeling dependencies between outputs. The method is validated on a synthetic dataset and two real-world datasets: climate signals and heavy metal concentrations.

The paper effectively addresses a key challenge in MOGP design – constructing expressive yet interpretable cross-covariance functions while guaranteeing positive-definiteness. Leveraging the spectral domain offers a principled way to achieve this, as highlighted by the authors. The connection to Bochner’s and Cramér’s Theorems is clearly explained, grounding the approach in solid mathematical foundations. The proposed MOSM kernel offers advantages over existing methods, specifically allowing for modeling delays and phase differences between channels, which is not readily achievable with simpler approaches like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) [3] or the Convolution Model [4]. The extension to arbitrary rank mixtures (Q) is a valuable addition, providing further flexibility.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical soundness, clear motivation, and potential for increased expressiveness in MOGP modeling. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature, appropriately referencing prior work in spectral methods [6] and MOGP design [3, 4, 5]. The illustrative example with the synthetic trivariate signal convincingly demonstrates the model’s ability to capture known dependencies, including derivatives and delays.

However, the experimental results, while demonstrating competitive performance, do not strongly differentiate MOSM from existing methods. The choice of datasets – climate signals and metal concentrations – are standard benchmarks in the MOGP literature, but their inherent similarities may limit the ability to showcase the unique advantages of the proposed kernel. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion section. A more compelling evaluation would involve datasets where the ability to model delays or phase differences is crucial and where existing methods struggle. The discussion of statistical significance in the results (Tables 1 & 2) is a valid point, and the lack of statistically significant improvements across all experiments weakens the claim of superiority. While the authors address this by mentioning the Sotonmet dataset, a more comprehensive statistical analysis would strengthen the conclusions.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though the mathematical notation can be dense at times. Providing more intuitive explanations alongside the equations could enhance readability, particularly for readers less familiar with signal processing concepts. While the paper details the parameter interpretation in spectral terms, briefly discussing how these parameters relate to physical or practical interpretations in the context of the real-world datasets would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical novelty:** The proposed approach offers a principled and mathematically sound way to construct MOGP kernels.
*   **Increased expressiveness:** The MOSM kernel is more flexible than existing methods, allowing for modeling delays and phase differences.
*   **Solid experimental validation:** The results demonstrate competitive performance on standard benchmarks and validate the model’s ability to capture known dependencies.
*   **Clear connections to existing work:** The paper builds upon established concepts in spectral methods and MOGP design.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited experimental differentiation:** The performance gains over existing methods are not consistently statistically significant.
*   **Standard benchmark datasets:** The choice of datasets may not fully showcase the unique advantages of the proposed kernel.
*   **Mathematical density:** Some sections are mathematically dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. While the experimental results do not demonstrate overwhelming superiority over existing methods, the theoretical novelty, increased expressiveness, and solid mathematical foundations of the proposed MOSM kernel warrant consideration for acceptance. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Perform a more rigorous statistical analysis of the experimental results, providing p-values or confidence intervals to support their claims.
2.  Consider including experiments on datasets where the ability to model delays or phase differences is particularly important.
3.  Improve the clarity of the mathematical notation by providing more intuitive explanations.
4. Briefly discuss the practical interpretation of the spectral parameters in the context of the real-world datasets.
