Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a principled construction of covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) in the Fourier domain. Building on Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem), the authors define a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities, symmetrize them to ensure real-valued processes, and obtain closed-form cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform. The resulting kernel generalizes the scalar spectral mixture (SM) kernel and subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases. Importantly, MOSM offers interpretable parameters per output pair (magnitude, mean frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay), allowing the model to capture both phase differences and explicit time delays—capabilities not afforded by earlier LMC/CONV approaches and only partially addressed by CSM.

The paper places MOSM within the well-established literature on MOGPs, including LMC and convolved GPs, and connects to multivariate Matérn and other spectral constructions. The experimental section compares MOSM to SM-LMC, CSM, and CONV on synthetic data (derivatives and delays) and two real datasets (climate sensors and heavy-metal concentrations), demonstrating competitive or superior performance, especially where delays and phase differences matter.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and principled construction: The factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω) plus symmetrization provides a clean route to positive semidefinite, interpretable multi-output kernels.
- Expressiveness with interpretability: Per-pair delay and phase parameters, along with frequency and bandwidth, allow rich cross-channel structure with clear physical meaning.
- Theoretical grounding: Careful use of Cramér’s theorem, Hermitian PSDs, and closed-form inverse Fourier transforms ensures technical soundness.
- Empirical validation: MOSM excels on the synthetic task involving simultaneous derivative and delay extrapolation where latent-process models struggle. On real-world tasks, MOSM is competitive and sometimes best.
- Relation to prior work: The paper clarifies how SM-LMC and CSM arise as constrained instances of MOSM.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Optimization and identifiability: The parameterization is rich (per-output and per-pair parameters across Q components), which can make maximum likelihood training sensitive to initialization, local optima, and component permutation/phase wrapping. Practical guidance is limited.
- Scalability: The approach inherits the O(N^3) training cost for dense GPs. Although the discussion notes potential sparse extensions, no empirical scalability study (e.g., inducing features/variables) is provided.
- Statistical testing: Reported significance uses the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test; paired tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank or paired t-tests on per-instance errors) may be more appropriate. Multiple comparisons are not discussed.
- Limited ablations: It would help to quantify the contribution of delays and phases (e.g., MOSM without delays, or with shared parameters across outputs) and to report sensitivity to Q.
- Practical details: More detail on hyperparameter constraints (e.g., how negative αij coexist with global PSD), initialization, and training times would improve reproducibility.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: The derivation is sound, with correct PSD factorization, Hermitian symmetry, and closed-form kernels. The synthetic experiment convincingly targets a setting tailored to MOSM’s inductive biases. Real-data improvements are modest but credible. More training/initialization details and stronger significance analysis would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Clarity: The paper is well written and well organized, with clear motivation and connections to prior work. Some implementation details (initialization, constraints, training budgets) are sparse. Figures and tables are informative.
- Originality: Extending spectral mixture ideas to multivariate settings via complex cross-spectra that capture both delay and phase is novel and extends prior CSM/SM-LMC constructions.
- Significance: The method is broadly applicable, interpretable, and likely to be adopted for multi-sensor and spatiotemporal tasks. Even where performance gains are small, the interpretability and generality are valuable.

Questions for the authors
- Data efficiency: How does MOSM behave with few training points per channel? Are delays/phases robustly learned in low-data regimes? Can you share learning curves or a subsampling study?
- Length-scale mismatch: As I understand, Σij = 2Σi(Σi + Σj)−1Σj implies cross-covariances become narrower when length-scales differ substantially, reducing correlation. Could you elaborate on this behavior and any practical implications?
- Initialization and identifiability: How do you initialize µi, Σi, θi, φi and weights to avoid poor local optima and label switching across Q components? Any constraints on φ (e.g., wrapping) and θ to stabilize training?
- Rank Q choice: How sensitive is performance to Q? Do you use model selection (e.g., BIC) or a heuristic? Are there practical benefits to low-rank cross-spectra (Q < m)?
- PSD guarantees: After symmetrization of Sij(ω), negative αij can arise in the time domain. Could you clarify how PSD of the full block kernel K is preserved and whether additional constraints are enforced in optimization?
- Computational cost: What are typical training times and memory usage versus CSM/SM-LMC on your datasets? Any preliminary results with inducing features/variables?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add practical training guidance: initialization scheme, parameter constraints, optimizer settings, and convergence diagnostics; report training time and sensitivity to Q.
- Include ablations to quantify the value of delays and phases and to examine robustness to mis-specified Q.
- Use paired statistical tests for per-instance errors and consider multiple-comparison correction.
- Provide a brief discussion on identifiability and parameter coupling (e.g., phase–delay trade-offs), and any regularization used.
- Clarify the noise model (per-output noise levels) and whether any cross-noise terms were considered.
- Release code and scripts to reproduce experiments.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, general, and interpretable construction of MOGP kernels.
- Captures delays and phase differences; subsumes existing spectral MOGP kernels.
- Strong synthetic demonstration; competitive real-world performance.
- Clear contribution likely of interest to the NIPS community.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited discussion of optimization, identifiability, and scalability.
- Modest gains on some real datasets; statistical testing could be stronger.
- Few ablations to isolate the contribution of new components.

Minor remarks
- Please ensure the number of training points and noise levels are clearly stated in Section 4.1 (and/or the supplement).
- There appear to be missing words around lines 74 and 140.
- Consider clarifying whether KS tests were one- or two-sided and whether multiple comparisons were corrected.

Overall recommendation
A solid, well-motivated, and technically sound contribution that generalizes spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting with interpretable delay and phase parameters. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the practical training details and strengthening the empirical analysis as outlined above.
