This paper proposes a new covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by specifying cross-power spectral densities (PSDs) and then obtaining the corresponding cross-covariances via the inverse Fourier transform. The approach mirrors the single-output spectral mixture (SM) construction of Wilson and Adams [6], where the PSD is modeled as a mixture of Gaussians and then transformed back to yield a flexible, extrapolative kernel. Here, the idea is extended to the multi-output setting by designing cross-spectral densities directly, and the method is evaluated on a synthetic example and two real datasets (a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura metal concentration dataset).

Strengths:
- The spectral-domain construction is principled and closely aligned with the successful single-output SM framework. Defining cross-PSDs and invoking the inverse Fourier transform is a natural and elegant way to ensure positive definiteness while enabling interpretable parameterization.
- Extending the SM idea to multiple outputs is a clever way to build richer multi-output kernels with potentially strong extrapolation capabilities.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- A key motivation in [6] was extrapolation. It would be compelling to demonstrate explicitly that the proposed multi-output kernel inherits similar extrapolation ability in challenging multi-output scenarios. As it stands, the experiments focus on very common datasets, and the observed performance is largely comparable to that of existing multi-output kernels.
- The empirical section would be stronger if it included datasets and tasks where existing models are known to struggle or are not well suited (e.g., settings requiring pronounced delays or phase relationships across channels), thereby highlighting the unique advantages of the proposed design.
- Please clarify whether you tested for statistical significance in Tables 1 and 2. Based on the reported means and standard deviations, most models appear to perform similarly. Formal significance tests would help quantify whether any observed improvements are meaningful.
