

The paper introduces a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes by specifying cross-power spectral densities and deriving corresponding cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform, rather than directly defining cross-covariance functions. This approach extends the spectral mixture kernel framework from [6] for single-output processes, where power spectral densities were modeled as mixtures of Gaussians to yield kernels with extrapolation capabilities. The authors apply their proposed covariance to synthetic data and two standard benchmark datasets: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentration prediction.  

While extending [6]'s spectral mixture idea to multi-output settings is conceptually clever, the paper fails to demonstrate the extrapolation capability that motivated the single-output variant in [6]. The experimental section relies exclusively on conventional datasets where performance is comparable to existing multi-output covariance functions, without showcasing scenarios where alternative models fail. This limits the perceived impact; inclusion of challenging datasets where current methods are unsuitable would strengthen the contribution. Additionally, the results in Tables 1 and 2 lack statistical significance testing. Given that all models' performance metrics fall within overlapping standard deviation ranges, the paper does not establish whether observed differences are meaningful.
