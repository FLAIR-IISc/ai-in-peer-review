This paper introduces a new family of covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) constructed in the spectral domain. Instead of specifying cross-covariances directly, the authors design complex-valued cross-spectral densities that are guaranteed to be positive-definite (via a factorization) and then obtain the corresponding cross-covariances by inverse Fourier transform, using Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem). The resulting Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel generalizes the Spectral Mixture (SM) approach of Wilson and Adams [6] from single-output to multi-output settings, and it subsumes prior multi-output constructions such as SM-LMC and CSM by allowing channel-specific spectral means, scales, phases, and, importantly, delays. The model provides a parametric, interpretable description of inter-channel relationships in terms of frequency content, magnitude, phase, and delay.

Relation to prior work: Classical multi-output kernels such as the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and convolution processes (CONV) build cross-covariances by linearly combining latent processes, which can limit interpretability and flexibility. The Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5] extended SM to MOGPs within LMC and introduced phase shifts, but used restricted (real-valued) cross-spectral forms with shared parameters across channels. By designing the full matrix-valued spectral density and enforcing positive definiteness through a factor decomposition, MOSM yields closed-form kernels that (i) recover standard SM on the diagonal, (ii) include phase shifts as in CSM, and (iii) additionally capture inter-channel delays—something not handled by SM-LMC/CSM under their shared-parameter constraints.

Technical quality:
- The construction is principled: the authors propose a family of complex-valued Gaussian spectral factors per channel and derive cross-spectral densities and their inverse Fourier transforms in closed form. Symmetrization in frequency ensures real-valued processes.
- Positive definiteness follows from the RH(ω)R(ω) factorization at each frequency, mirroring LMC-like guarantees but in the spectral domain; the rank-Q extension corresponds to additive kernel components.
- The parameters (magnitude, mean frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay) are interpretable and differ across output pairs, enhancing expressivity beyond prior models.
- Training and inference reduce to standard GP marginal likelihood optimization, with implementation in GPflow.

Empirical evaluation:
- Synthetic data: MOSM learns auto- and cross-covariances among a base signal, its derivative, and a delayed version. It outperforms LMC/CONV and CSM in MAE and uniquely handles the simultaneous derivative and delay structure, which is not a linear combination of univariate kernels.
- Climate data: In a sensor-failure setting across four stations, all methods perform similarly; MOSM is competitive but not consistently superior. The authors report a Kolmogorov–Smirnov test indicating MOSM’s advantage on one station; differences elsewhere are not statistically significant.
- Swiss Jura (metals): MOSM shows a statistically significant improvement for Cadmium; for Copper, MOSM and CSM are comparable and SM-LMC is competitive, possibly due to higher variability/non-Gaussianity.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The spectral construction, symmetry requirement for real-valuedness, and parameter interpretations are well explained. Some derivations are dense; a brief intuitive discussion of how delay and phase separately shape the time-domain cross-covariance would aid readers. A short complexity section (time/memory vs. number of outputs, data size, and Q) and guidance on hyperparameter initialization would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
- The core idea—designing multi-output kernels via Cramér’s theorem with complex Gaussian spectral factors and explicit delay—is a novel and natural extension of SM. The factorization-based positive definiteness in the spectral domain, combined with closed-form inverse transforms, provides a flexible family that strictly generalizes SM-LMC/CSM.

Significance:
- The construction is broadly applicable to multi-sensor time series and spatial-temporal settings where phase and delay are meaningful. While real-data gains are modest on the chosen benchmarks, the method’s interpretability and ability to model delays make it an attractive alternative to latent-process models. Demonstrating clear practical wins in settings where delays matter would substantially strengthen the impact.

Strengths:
- Principled, general, and interpretable spectral construction for MOGPs.
- Captures both phase shifts and inter-channel delays; subsumes SM-LMC/CSM as special cases.
- Closed-form kernels with standard GP training; competitive and sometimes superior empirical performance.
- Synthetic study convincingly highlights capabilities beyond latent-process mixtures.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Evaluation is limited to common benchmarks where methods perform similarly. Add tasks where delays/phase are essential (e.g., multi-sensor physiological data, seismology, lead–lag financial series) to showcase unique benefits.
- Report statistical significance uniformly across all tables (including exact tests and p-values), and consider multiple random train/test splits for the real datasets.
- Provide ablations: (i) no-delay vs. full MOSM; (ii) shared vs. per-channel spectral parameters; (iii) effect of rank Q and number of components. This would clarify when MOSM reduces to CSM/SM-LMC and when extra flexibility helps.
- Discuss computational complexity and scaling (O(N^3) cost, block-structured kernels), and consider a sparse/inducing version leveraging spectral structure as suggested in the discussion.
- Comment on identifiability/sensitivity between phase and delay and on initialization strategies.

Pro acceptance:
- Novel, principled extension of SM to the multi-output setting with interpretable parameters.
- Strictly more expressive than prior MOGP SM variants; clear theoretical foundation.
- Strong synthetic results demonstrating capabilities not matched by LMC/CONV/CSM.

Con acceptance:
- Modest empirical gains on standard benchmarks; limited evidence that the added expressivity yields substantial practical improvements.
- Incomplete statistical analysis across experiments; limited ablation and scalability discussion.

Overall assessment:
- Quality: sound and well-supported; more comprehensive significance testing and ablations recommended.
- Clarity: good, with minor suggestions for added intuition and complexity details.
- Originality: high in the multi-output spectral construction with delays.
- Significance: potentially high, contingent on demonstrating advantages on tasks where delays/phase are crucial.

Questions for the authors:
- Please report statistical tests (and p-values) for all comparisons in Tables 1–3. For the climate data, did you test all stations and account for multiple comparisons?
- How sensitive is performance to initialization of phase and delay? Are there identifiability issues between them?
- What is the computational and parameter scaling with m and Q, and can you provide results with a sparse/inducing approximation?
