This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The authors extend the spectral mixture approach, leveraging Cramér’s Theorem to construct a flexible and interpretable kernel capable of modeling delays and phase differences between output channels. This work addresses a challenging problem in MOGP design – balancing expressiveness with parametric interpretability – and builds upon previous approaches like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the method is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating promising results.

The core idea of designing covariance functions in the Fourier domain, guided by Cramér’s Theorem, is well-motivated and presented clearly. The MOSM kernel offers several advantages over existing methods, most notably its ability to explicitly model delays between channels, a feature not readily available in previous formulations. The authors effectively connect their work to the broader literature on GP kernels and spectral methods, providing valuable context for the reader. The experimental validation, including a synthetic example designed to highlight the method's capabilities and comparisons on climate and metal concentration datasets, strengthens the paper’s contribution. The writing is generally excellent, and the paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow the key concepts and experimental setup. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the computational challenges associated with parameter learning, particularly for high-dimensional output spaces or large datasets. While the authors mention using GPflow for efficient implementation, details regarding scalability and optimization strategies are lacking. It would be helpful to understand how the number of spectral components (Q) influences computational cost and model performance. Additionally, the experiments, while well-chosen, could be augmented with a more systematic investigation of the sensitivity of the MOSM kernel to different parameter settings and noise levels. 

Regarding specific questions: the authors should address how the method performs with limited training data, as this is a common scenario in many applications. The question about the interplay between different lengthscales – specifically, whether channels with vastly different lengthscales can exhibit strong correlation – is insightful and deserves a discussion within the paper. Exploring this limitation or providing insights into how the kernel handles such cases would enhance the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The approach is well-grounded in Bochner’s and Cramér’s Theorems, providing a principled framework for kernel design.
*   **Interpretability:** The parameters of the MOSM kernel have clear spectral interpretations, allowing for better understanding of the relationships between output channels.
*   **Expressiveness:** The ability to model delays and phase differences extends the capabilities of existing MOGP methods.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the MOSM kernel on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:**  The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the computational challenges associated with parameter learning and scalability.
*   **Limited Scalability Analysis:** The impact of the number of spectral components (Q) on performance and computational cost is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** A more systematic analysis of the kernel's sensitivity to parameter settings and noise levels would be beneficial.
*   **Lengthscale Interaction:** The potential limitations arising from differences in lengthscales between channels are not addressed.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP kernel design.
*   The MOSM kernel offers a significant improvement in expressiveness and interpretability compared to existing methods.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The work addresses a challenging and important problem in Gaussian process modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks a detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, including a sensitivity analysis and investigation of performance with limited data.
*   The potential limitations arising from differences in lengthscales between channels are not addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the work – particularly its theoretical foundation, interpretability, and expressiveness – outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above regarding computational complexity, sensitivity analysis, and lengthscale interactions.
