The paper formulates a new type of covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes, building on the idea proposed by Wilson et al. (2013) for single-output Gaussian processes. Instead of directly specifying the cross-covariance functions, the authors propose to specify the cross-power spectral densities and use the inverse Fourier transform to find the corresponding cross-covariances. This approach allows for a more flexible and interpretable model of the relationships between different channels.

The paper provides a clear and thorough explanation of the theoretical background and the construction of the proposed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the MOSM kernel on a synthetic data example and two real-world datasets, including climate signals and metal concentrations. The experimental results show that the MOSM kernel performs competitively with other state-of-the-art models, including the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC), the Convolution Model (CONV), and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to model delays and phase differences between channels, which is a unique feature of the MOSM kernel. The authors provide a clear interpretation of the parameters of the MOSM kernel from a spectral viewpoint, which is useful for understanding the relationships between different channels.

However, the experimental section could be improved by including more challenging datasets or scenarios where the MOSM kernel can demonstrate its unique strengths. The results obtained on the climate signals and metal concentrations datasets are similar to those obtained with other covariance functions for multiple-outputs, which may limit the impact of the paper.

Regarding the statistical significance of the results, the authors do report the standard deviation of the mean absolute error (MAE) in Tables 1 and 2. However, it would be useful to provide a more detailed analysis of the statistical significance of the results, such as using a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test or a paired t-test to compare the performance of different models.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their ideas. The writing is good, and the paper is easy to follow. However, there are some minor errors and typos throughout the paper that should be corrected.

Overall, the paper presents a new and interesting approach to modeling multi-output Gaussian processes, and the results demonstrate the potential of the MOSM kernel. With some additional experiments and analysis, the paper could have a stronger impact in the field.
