

This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel framework [Wilson & Adams, 2013] to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) using Cramér’s Theorem as the multivariate generalization of Bochner’s Theorem. The authors construct a complex-valued cross-spectral density matrix that enables explicit modeling of delays between channels and phase differences—key limitations in prior methods like Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) [Ulrich et al., 2015] and SM-LMC [Álvarez & Lawrence, 2008]. MOSM provides full parametric interpretability (e.g., frequency, magnitude, delay, phase) while guaranteeing positive definiteness via a rank-1 factorization. The method is validated on synthetic data (derivative/delayed signals) and two real-world datasets (climate sensors, heavy metal concentrations), outperforming baselines on the Jura dataset (Cadmium prediction) and demonstrating superior extrapolation in the synthetic example.

**Strengths**  
*Originality*: MOSM is the first MOGP kernel to jointly model delays and phase shifts through complex-valued spectral densities. While CSM [Ulrich et al., 2015] incorporates phase shifts, it cannot model delays (fixed parameters across channels), and SM-LMC [Álvarez & Lawrence, 2008] lacks interpretability. MOSM’s theoretical foundation via Cramér’s Theorem (Section 3.1) rigorously addresses the MOGP covariance design challenge noted in prior work [Genton & Kleiber, 2015].  
*Quality*: The paper is technically sound. Theorems 1–2 establish a clear link between spectral design and positive definiteness. Experimental validation is thorough: the synthetic example (Section 4.1) demonstrates MOSM’s unique ability to learn cross-covariances (e.g., between derivative and delayed signals) without prior knowledge, while real-world results show competitive MAE on climate data (Table 2) and statistical significance on Cadmium prediction (Table 3).  
*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written. Key concepts (e.g., spectral densities, Fourier pairs) are precisely defined, and the derivation of MOSM (Equation 11) is logically presented. Figures 1–2 effectively illustrate spectral parameters’ impact on kernel behavior.  
*Significance*: MOSM advances MOGP modeling by enabling interpretable, physically meaningful cross-channel relationships (delays, phase shifts). This is critical for applications like fault detection or sensor fusion where temporal alignment matters. The method’s flexibility (e.g., collapsing to CSM when parameters are shared) also broadens applicability.

**Weaknesses**  
*Training Complexity*: The paper states hyperparameters are optimized via NLL minimization (Section 3.2) but omits computational details. For large *m* (channels), the *O(m²)* spectral parameter space may complicate optimization versus simpler latent models (e.g., LMC). Clarifying this trade-off would strengthen the case for adoption.  
*Real-World Impact*: On the climate dataset (Section 4.3), MOSM’s MAE gains over CSM are marginal (Table 2), and statistical significance is only confirmed for one sensor. The high correlation between climate signals (Section 4.3) likely explains this, as noted by the authors. While MOSM *can* model delays (validated synthetically), its advantage in *highly correlated* real-world data remains limited.  
*Minor Omissions*: The synthetic example’s training points (Section 4.1) should specify *N* per channel (500, 400, 400), not just "randomly chosen." The phrase "a word is missing" in the draft review likely refers to Section 3.1 (line 74: "complex-valued SE function" should be "complex-valued square-exponential function").

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- First method to jointly model delays and phase shifts in MOGPs with full parametric interpretability.  
- Rigorous theoretical foundation (Cramér’s Theorem) ensuring positive definiteness.  
- Strong synthetic validation and statistically significant gains on the Jura dataset.  
- Clear, well-structured presentation with effective visualizations.  

*Con*:  
- Training complexity for large *m* not addressed (a practical concern for scalability).  
- Limited real-world advantage in highly correlated datasets (e.g., climate sensors), though this is expected due to data characteristics.  
- Baseline comparisons lack statistical significance testing for most real-world results (e.g., climate MAE differences in Table 2).  

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a high-quality, technically rigorous contribution that significantly advances MOGP modeling. MOSM’s interpretability and ability to model delays/phase shifts address a clear gap in the literature, with strong theoretical grounding and empirical validation. While real-world gains are modest in some settings, the method’s novelty and robustness make it a valuable addition to the MOGP toolbox. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is original (novel spectral design), significant (enables new modeling capabilities), clear (excellent exposition), and of high quality (sound theory + experiments). The minor weaknesses (training complexity, limited real-world impact in high-correlation cases) do not detract from its scientific merit.  

**Recommendation**: Accept. The work is a meaningful advancement for the field, with clear potential for adoption in applications requiring interpretable multi-channel temporal relationships.
