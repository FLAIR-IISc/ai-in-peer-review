This paper introduces a novel covariance structure for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs), extending the spectral approach based on Bochner’s Theorem to kernel construction. A key strength of the proposed method is the interpretability of its parameters, allowing for modeling of phenomena like delays and phase differences between outputs.

Defining covariance structures for MOGPs remains a challenging problem, and relatively few methods are available. This work provides a theoretically sound contribution, building upon established results like Cramér’s Theorem, and demonstrates its effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with relevant illustrative examples. If parameter learning proves computationally feasible – the paper lacks detailed discussion on this aspect – this method has the potential to become a standard approach for MOGPs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The method is firmly grounded in mathematical theory, specifically leveraging Cramér’s Theorem to ensure positive-definiteness.
*   **Interpretability:** The parameters of the proposed kernel have clear physical interpretations (delay, phase, lengthscale), enhancing understanding and potentially facilitating model selection.
*   **Generality:** The MOSM kernel generalizes existing approaches, such as the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, by relaxing restrictions on parameter sharing between outputs.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The method is validated on both synthetic data, demonstrating its ability to recover known relationships, and real-world datasets, showing competitive performance.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making the concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The paper does not provide a detailed discussion of the computational challenges associated with parameter learning, particularly for high-dimensional datasets or a large number of outputs. Scalability remains an open question.
*   **Limited Discussion of Lengthscale Interaction:** The paper does not explicitly address potential issues arising when outputs exhibit significantly different lengthscales (as highlighted in the "Questions" section of the original review).  Further investigation into this interaction would be valuable.
* **Synthetic Data Details:** The number of training points used in the synthetic example (Section 4.1) is not explicitly stated, hindering complete reproducibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of MOGPs.
*   The proposed method addresses a significant challenge in MOGP modeling – defining expressive and interpretable covariance structures.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   The clear and well-organized presentation makes the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of detailed discussion on computational complexity raises concerns about the practical applicability of the method to large-scale problems.
*   The paper does not fully explore the interaction between different lengthscales across outputs.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of multi-output Gaussian Processes. The theoretical contribution is solid, the method is well-motivated and clearly presented, and the experimental results are promising. While the computational complexity and lengthscale interaction deserve further attention, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.
