Summary
This paper introduces a new covariance construction for multi-output Gaussian processes by specifying cross-power spectral densities (PSDs) and obtaining the corresponding cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform. The approach extends ideas from prior single-output work (e.g., spectral mixture kernels) that model PSDs as mixtures of Gaussians to yield flexible stationary kernels with strong extrapolation capabilities. The authors evaluate the proposed multi-output kernel on synthetic data and two standard real datasets (weather and Swiss Jura metals).

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing extension: Building multi-output kernels via cross-PSDs is principled and leverages well-understood Fourier arguments (Bochner’s theorem) for stationary processes.
- Potentially high expressivity: Mixtures in the spectral domain can capture multiple characteristic frequencies, smoothness levels, and time/space scales. In the multi-output case, cross-PSDs can also encode relative phase and delay, enabling richer cross-task structure than linear model of coregionalization (LMC) alone.
- Empirical results are competitive: On the presented datasets, the method performs on par with standard multi-output GP kernels.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited empirical scope: The experiments use common benchmark datasets and the results are largely similar to existing methods. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate situations where the proposed kernel has a clear advantage (e.g., long-range extrapolation, pronounced periodicity with cross-task phase shifts, or structured missingness).
- Missing evidence for extrapolation: A key appeal of spectral mixture kernels is extrapolation. The paper does not show whether the multi-output extension inherits this property (e.g., forecasting beyond the training window across correlated outputs).
- Statistical significance: Tables 1 and 2 report means and standard deviations, but it is unclear whether differences are statistically significant. Many results appear overlapping within one standard deviation.
- Validity conditions and parametrization: Constructing a valid multi-output kernel via cross-PSDs requires that the cross-spectral matrix is Hermitian and positive semidefinite for all frequencies, and that real-valued covariances are recovered. The paper should clearly specify the parameterization that guarantees these properties (e.g., mixture components with frequency-dependent PSD matrices constrained via Cholesky factors and symmetry S_ij(ω) = S_ji(−ω)).
- Identifiability and interpretability: Mixture models in the spectral domain can suffer from component redundancy and label switching, which can complicate learning and interpretation of cross-task structure (e.g., phase delays). Guidance on initialization and regularization would be valuable.
- Computational considerations: Multi-output GPs can be computationally demanding. The paper should more explicitly discuss training complexity, memory footprint, and any structure exploited (e.g., shared inducing points, Kronecker structure, Toeplitz/circulant approximations) to scale with number of outputs, mixture components, and data size.

Questions for the authors
- How do you ensure that the cross-PSD matrix is valid for all frequencies (Hermitian, positive semidefinite) and that the resulting cross-covariances are real-valued? Please detail the exact parametrization and constraints per mixture component.
- Do you model phase offsets or time/space delays across outputs explicitly? If so, how are these identified and learned in practice?
- Did you assess extrapolation performance (e.g., long-horizon forecasting) where spectral mixture–based methods often shine? If not, can you include such experiments or discuss limitations?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of spectral components and to initialization? Any heuristics (e.g., periodograms) for robust initialization?
- What is the computational complexity in terms of number of outputs (P), total training points (N), and mixture components (Q)? How does runtime compare to LMC/ICM and convolved-process baselines?
- Did you conduct statistical significance tests for Tables 1 and 2 (e.g., paired t-tests or Wilcoxon tests across folds)? Several differences appear within one standard deviation.
- Are code and hyperparameter settings available to facilitate reproducibility?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments that stress the advantages of spectral modeling:
  - Extrapolation on time series with multiple periodicities.
  - Cross-task phase shifts or delays (e.g., meteorological stations with lead–lag).
  - Structured missingness where transfer across outputs is critical.
- Include stronger and diversified baselines (if not already present): ICM/LMC with multiple latent functions, semiparametric latent factor models, convolved processes, and single-output spectral mixture kernels as a reference.
- Provide ablations:
  - Vary Q (number of components) and show performance/runtime trade-offs.
  - Analyze the effect of enabling/disabling cross-task phase/delay terms.
  - Study sensitivity to initialization and regularization.
- Clarify the theoretical guarantees and implementation details that ensure kernel validity, and add a brief discussion on identifiability.
- Report training time, memory, and convergence behavior to contextualize practicality.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a natural and potentially powerful extension of spectral mixture ideas to the multi-output setting. The contribution is promising, but the current empirical evidence does not yet showcase a clear advantage over existing multi-output kernels, and several important details about validity, scalability, and statistical significance are missing or underdeveloped. With additional experiments targeted at extrapolation and cross-task structure, clearer theoretical and implementation details, and significance testing, the paper’s impact and clarity would be substantially strengthened. I lean toward a borderline evaluation, with the potential to become strong with the suggested revisions.
