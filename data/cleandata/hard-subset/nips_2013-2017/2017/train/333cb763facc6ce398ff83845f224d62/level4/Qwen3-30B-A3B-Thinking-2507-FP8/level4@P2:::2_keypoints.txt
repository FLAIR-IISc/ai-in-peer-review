- Proposes the MOSM kernel: a novel multi-output spectral mixture covariance derived via Cramér’s Theorem using complex cross-spectral densities to model delays and phase shifts.
- Strong theoretical grounding with careful derivations and conditions for positive definiteness.
- Provides clear parametric interpretation of relationships across channels, explicitly modeling delays and phase differences.
- Correctly identifies limitations of prior methods (LMC/CONV lack interpretability and force similar autocovariances; CSM cannot model delays).
- Well-organized and clearly written with thorough background, logical mathematical presentation, and helpful figures.
- Original and meaningful extension of single-output SM to multi-output settings with added modeling capabilities.
- Synthetic experiments convincingly show MOSM can recover derivative and delayed relationships without prior knowledge.
- Real-world experiments yield only modest improvements; statistically significant gains observed mainly on the Jura cadmium task.
- Statistical significance is not consistently established (KS test used, but many differences lie within standard deviations).
- The paper does not fully demonstrate the extrapolation capability that motivated the single-output SM work.
- Implementation details are clear and appropriate (GPflow with TensorFlow).
- Lacks experiments on datasets specifically requiring delay/phase modeling, which would better showcase MOSM’s unique advantages.
- Overall: technically sound and well-motivated, but practical significance would be strengthened by stronger empirical evidence in delay/phase-critical real-world tasks.
