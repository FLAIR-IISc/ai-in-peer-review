This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel covariance structure for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The work provides a principled generalization of the popular Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output setting by leveraging Cramér's Theorem, the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem. A key advantage of the proposed approach is that it models the cross-spectral densities directly, resulting in a flexible kernel whose hyperparameters have a clear physical interpretation, such as delays and phase differences between output channels.

The problem of defining valid and expressive covariance structures for MOGPs is challenging, and relatively few general-purpose methods are available. The method proposed by the authors is theoretically sound, and its effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated on several datasets. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with relevant and effective illustrations. If the hyperparameter learning is not overly complex (the paper does not contain extensive information on this practical aspect), I expect this method could become a standard for MOGP modeling.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the important and difficult problem of designing covariance functions for multi-output GPs.
*   The proposed method is theoretically sound and principled, offering a clear and interpretable construction based on spectral theory.
*   The experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world data effectively demonstrates the model's capabilities and advantages over existing methods.
*   The work is presented with excellent clarity.

**Questions and Areas for Improvement:**
The model is very compelling. My main questions concern its practical behavior and properties under different conditions.
*   How does the method behave when only a small number of training points are available? The model has a rich parameterization, and its performance in low-data regimes is not explored.
*   It is generally understood that a channel with a large length-scale is unlikely to be highly correlated with a channel possessing a small length-scale. How is this property reflected or constrained within your model?
*   In the synthetic example (Section 4.1), the number of training points for each of the three signals should be specified more clearly to improve reproducibility.
