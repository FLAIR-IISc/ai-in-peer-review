The paper introduces a novel covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes. Rather than directly specifying cross-covariance functions, the authors propose defining cross-power spectral densities and then utilizing the inverse Fourier transform to derive the corresponding cross-covariances. This approach mirrors the work in [6] for single-output Gaussian processes, which represented the power spectral density as a mixture of Gaussians to create a powerful kernel function with extrapolation capabilities. The proposed covariance function is evaluated on a synthetic dataset, as well as two real-world datasets: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations.

Extending the ideas from [6] to the multi-output setting represents a clever approach to constructing new covariance functions. A key motivation behind [6] was the development of a kernel function capable of extrapolation, and it would have been valuable to demonstrate a similar ability in this multi-output extension. The experimental evaluation currently relies on commonly used datasets, and the reported results are comparable to those achieved with existing multi-output covariance functions. The paper’s impact could be strengthened by including experiments on datasets where existing models struggle or perform poorly.

Furthermore, the statistical significance of the results presented in Tables 1 and 2 was not explicitly tested. The observed performance across models appears largely similar, given the reported standard deviations. Investigating statistical significance would help to better understand whether the observed differences are meaningful.
