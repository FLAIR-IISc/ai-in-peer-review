This paper presents a novel and principled framework for constructing expressive covariance kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The core contribution is the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, which generalizes the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel. The authors achieve this by first defining a flexible, parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and then applying Cramér's Theorem—the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem—to derive the corresponding matrix-valued covariance function. This approach stands in contrast to previous methods like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) or the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, offering greater expressiveness. A key advantage of the MOSM kernel is its ability to directly model and interpret physically meaningful relationships between output channels, such as delays and phase differences, in addition to shared spectral content.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The problem of designing valid, flexible, and interpretable cross-covariance functions for MOGPs is highly relevant and challenging. This work provides an elegant and theoretically sound solution by building upon the spectral representation of covariance functions. The direct construction of the cross-spectral density matrix based on Cramér's theorem is a novel and powerful approach in this domain.
*   **Quality:** The proposed method is technically robust and well-founded. The effectiveness of the MOSM kernel is convincingly demonstrated through experiments on both a synthetic dataset, cleverly designed to highlight the model's unique ability to capture derivative and delay relationships, and two real-world datasets where it performs competitively or superiorly to existing state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The theoretical background is introduced clearly, and the derivation of the MOSM kernel is presented in a logical and understandable manner. The choice of illustrations and experiments effectively showcases the properties and advantages of the proposed model.
*   **Potential Impact:** Given its interpretability and superior flexibility, the MOSM kernel has the potential to become a standard and widely used tool for MOGP modeling, particularly in applications where complex inter-channel dynamics are present.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The paper does not elaborate on the practical aspects of hyperparameter optimization. The MOSM kernel introduces a significant number of parameters. A discussion on the empirical behavior of the optimization, such as potential issues with local minima, the choice of initialization strategies, and the model's overall sensitivity to these parameters, would be a valuable addition.
*   How does the model perform in low-data regimes? The kernel's high degree of expressiveness might require a substantial amount of data to reliably estimate all its parameters. An analysis of its data efficiency would be useful.
*   The model allows each output channel to possess distinct spectral characteristics (e.g., via channel-specific means `µ_i` and variances `Σ_i`). Could you elaborate on how the cross-covariance structure `S_ij` captures the interaction between two channels with very different spectral properties (e.g., a very smooth process and a high-frequency process)?
*   For improved reproducibility, the number of training points used for each of the three signals in the synthetic experiment (Section 4.1) should be explicitly stated in the main text.
