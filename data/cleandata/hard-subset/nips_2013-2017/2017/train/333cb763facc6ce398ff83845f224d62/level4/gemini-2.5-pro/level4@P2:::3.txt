### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel covariance function for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The work extends the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel, proposed by Wilson and Adams (2013), to the multivariate case. The key idea is to move from Bochner's theorem, which relates a single-output stationary covariance function to its power spectral density (PSD), to its multivariate counterpart, Cramér's Theorem. The authors propose a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities which are guaranteed to form a positive-definite matrix at any frequency. Applying the inverse Fourier transform, as prescribed by Cramér's Theorem, yields a valid matrix-valued covariance function. A significant advantage of this principled construction is that the resulting kernel's hyperparameters have a clear physical interpretation, modeling properties such as delays, phase shifts, and frequencies across different output channels. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on a synthetic task designed to highlight its unique capabilities and show competitive or superior performance against existing MOGP methods on two real-world datasets.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

The problem of designing expressive and valid covariance structures for MOGPs is a well-known challenge in the field. This paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and significant contribution to addressing this problem.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposed approach of designing MOGP kernels directly in the spectral domain via Cramér's Theorem is both elegant and novel. It provides a principled way to construct a rich family of cross-covariance functions that go beyond the limitations of earlier methods based on the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) or convolution processes. The resulting MOSM kernel is more expressive than the related Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, notably by explicitly modeling time delays between channels. Given its flexibility and interpretability, this method has the potential to become a widely used tool for MOGP modeling.

2.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the MOSM kernel from a rank-Q decomposition of the cross-spectral density matrix is clear and ensures the positive-definiteness of the resulting covariance matrix by construction. The claims of the paper are well-supported by the experiments. The synthetic example, involving a function, its derivative, and a delayed version, is particularly insightful as it creates a scenario where methods based on linear combinations of latent processes are expected to fail, whereas MOSM succeeds by correctly identifying the underlying non-trivial cross-covariances. The performance on real-world data further validates its practical utility.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors provide the necessary background on spectral methods for GPs before methodically building up their contribution from Cramér's theorem. The connection between the parameters of the proposed cross-spectral density and the final kernel properties (delay, phase, etc.) is explained well. The experiments are well-described and the results are presented clearly.

4.  **Interpretability:** A major strength of the MOSM kernel is the direct interpretability of its parameters in spectral terms. This contrasts sharply with many MOGP models where the relationship between hyperparameters and the learned cross-channel dependencies is often opaque. This feature makes the model not just a predictive tool, but also an analytical one for understanding complex multi-output systems.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where additional clarification would be beneficial.

1.  **Hyperparameter Optimization:** The MOSM kernel introduces a significant number of hyperparameters, scaling with the number of outputs (`m`), input dimensions (`n`), and spectral components (`Q`). The paper mentions that training is done by minimizing the negative log-likelihood but provides little discussion on the practical challenges of this optimization. Could the authors comment on the difficulty of the optimization? For instance, is the model sensitive to initialization? Are there strategies to mitigate issues with local minima in this high-dimensional parameter space? A brief discussion of the computational complexity and practical training considerations would strengthen the paper's utility for practitioners.

2.  **Performance in Low-Data Regimes:** The experiments are conducted with a reasonable number of training points (e.g., N=1000 for the climate data). How does the model's performance degrade as the number of available training points becomes small? The model's high flexibility might lead to overfitting in such scenarios, and it would be valuable to understand its behavior and limitations in sparse data settings.

3.  **Model Constraints:** (This is more a point of curiosity) The model parameterizes the cross-spectral densities (`Sij`) through shared underlying components (`Ri`, `Rj`). How does this formulation constrain the types of relationships that can be learned? For example, it seems intuitive that a channel with a very short length-scale (high-frequency characteristics) would have limited correlation with a channel possessing a very long length-scale (low-frequency characteristics). How is this intuition reflected in the model's parameters, specifically in the interaction between `Σi`, `µi`, `Σj`, and `µj` when forming `Σij` and `µij`?

**Minor Remarks:**

*   In Section 4.1, the phrase "Gaussian noised was added" should be "Gaussian noise was added".
*   There may be a typo in Equation (1), where `ω >τ` should likely be `ω^T τ` to denote the dot product.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant and elegant contribution to the literature on multi-output Gaussian processes. The proposed MOSM kernel is theoretically principled, highly expressive, and offers interpretable parameters. The empirical validation is convincing and thoughtfully designed. Despite minor open questions regarding the practicalities of optimization, the work is a complete and valuable piece of research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
