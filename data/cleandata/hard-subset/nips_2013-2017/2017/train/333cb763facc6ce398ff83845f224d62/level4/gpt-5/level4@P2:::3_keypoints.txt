- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a complex-valued cross-spectral mixture construction for MOGPs.  
- Uses Cramér’s theorem and spectral-factorization to guarantee positive definiteness of the multi-output kernel.  
- Cross-covariances have interpretable parameters: magnitude, frequency, lengthscale, phase, and delay.  
- Auto-covariances reduce to standard spectral mixture (SM) kernels.  
- MOSM generalizes prior MOGP kernels: SM-LMC and CSM are recovered as special cases when parameters are tied and delays removed.  
- Empirical results: best on synthetic derivative-and-delay task, competitive on climate data, clear gains on Cadmium (Jura) task.  
- Positions MOSM as a principled and more expressive spectral construction compared to LMC, CONV, and prior SM-based MOGPs.  
- Theoretical derivation is sound: closed-form kernels from Gaussian spectral components and spectral mirroring enforce real-valued symmetry.  
- Synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate learning of derivatives and delays.  
- Real-data evaluations are reasonable and include statistical testing where possible.  
- Training and model-selection details (initialization, choice of Q, sensitivity to local optima) are limited in the manuscript.  
- Scalability and computational complexity (in N, m, Q, input dimension) are not analyzed and no sparse/structured approximations are provided.  
- Implementation uses standard marginal likelihood optimization with GPflow/TensorFlow, but cost and scalability trade-offs are not discussed.  
- Limited ablations leave unclear the individual contributions of delay vs. phase vs. independent lengthscales.  
- Questions for authors: performance with few training points per channel and robustness of PSD parameterization in low-data regimes.  
- Questions for authors: how mismatched per-channel lengthscales are reconciled (induced Σij and wij behavior).  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity of training to initialization and whether periodogram-based heuristics are used.  
- Questions for authors: computational complexity as function of m and Q and availability of sparse/inducing or Kronecker/Toeplitz variants.  
- Strengths: principled Fourier-domain construction, clear parameter interpretability, generalization of prior kernels, and strong synthetic validation.  
- Weaknesses: insufficient training/stability guidance, no model-selection heuristics for Q, no scalability analysis, and limited ablation studies.  
- Pros for acceptance: elegant theory, interpretable parameters capturing phase and delay, and demonstrated empirical advantages on key tasks.  
- Cons for acceptance: missing scalability and training details, limited guidance on initialization/model selection, and mixed real-data improvements.  
- Experiment requests: specify exact training set sizes for synthetic study and include initialization details for all compared methods.  
- Minor comments: fix missing words around lines 74 and 140, clarify the S(ω) → 0.5[S(ω)+S(−ω)] symmetry enforcement and its impact on interpretability.  
- Overall: well-motivated, theoretically sound, and interpretable MOGP kernel with strong potential if training, model selection, and scalability details are strengthened.
