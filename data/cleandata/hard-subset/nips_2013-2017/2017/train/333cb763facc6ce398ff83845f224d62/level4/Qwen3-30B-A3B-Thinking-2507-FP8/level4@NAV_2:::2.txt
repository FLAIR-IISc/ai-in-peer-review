

This paper extends the spectral mixture kernel framework to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by constructing a principled family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities. Building on Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate generalization of Bochner’s theorem), the authors derive the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, which explicitly models delays across channels alongside phase shifts and provides full parametric interpretation of cross-covariances. This generalizes prior work such as the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5], which only models phase differences without delays. The approach is validated on synthetic data (learning derivative and delayed signals) and two real-world datasets (climate signals and heavy metal concentrations), demonstrating improved performance on Cadmium prediction in the Jura dataset (statistically significant at α=0.05) while matching or exceeding competitors on other tasks.

Strengths: The paper provides a theoretically grounded extension of spectral mixture kernels to MOGPs, with clear parametric interpretation of delays (θ_ij) and phases (φ_ij) that previous methods lacked. The synthetic experiment successfully demonstrates MOSM’s ability to learn derivative and delayed relationships without prior knowledge—something latent-process-based models (e.g., SM-LMC, CONV) fail to capture. Statistical significance testing (Kolmogorov-Smirnov) is appropriately applied to real-world results, confirming MOSM’s superiority on the Sotonmet climate sensor and Cadmium metal concentration.

Weaknesses: The climate dataset results show only marginal improvements over CSM (Table 2), likely due to high inter-sensor correlation, which obscures MOSM’s full potential. While the synthetic example validates extrapolation capability, the real-world experiments do not explicitly test this strength (e.g., extrapolating beyond observed climate ranges). The paper could better emphasize how MOSM’s delay modeling specifically enables the derivative/delay learning in the synthetic case.

Pro: Principled extension of spectral methods to MOGPs; explicit delay/phase parameters; statistically significant gains on Jura Cadmium; synthetic validation of latent relationship learning.  
Con: Limited demonstration of extrapolation in real-world settings; climate data results show minimal gains due to high correlation; real-world datasets do not fully leverage MOSM’s unique capabilities.
