This paper proposes a novel family of covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), named the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The core idea extends the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel proposed by Wilson & Adams [6]. Rather than defining cross-covariance functions directly in the time or space domain, the authors construct them in the spectral domain by modeling the cross-power spectral densities. They define a flexible parametric form for the matrix of cross-spectral densities, ensuring it is Hermitian positive-definite, and then use the inverse Fourier transform (via Cramér's Theorem) to obtain the corresponding valid MOGP covariance functions. The authors validate their model on a synthetic task involving derivatives and time-delayed signals, as well as on two real-world benchmarks: a climate dataset and the Jura heavy metal concentration dataset.

**Strengths**

The extension of the spectral mixture approach to the multi-output setting is a principled and clever way to construct expressive covariance functions. The formulation, grounded in Cramér’s Theorem, provides an elegant method for ensuring the positive-definiteness of the resulting kernel matrix while allowing for rich interactions between outputs, such as delays and phase shifts, which are explicitly parameterized and interpretable. The synthetic experiment is particularly effective at demonstrating the model's unique ability to capture complex relationships (like derivatives and delays) where simpler models based on latent processes fail.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  A key motivation for the original SM kernel [6] was its powerful extrapolation capability. The current work would be significantly stronger if it demonstrated that the proposed MOSM kernel retains this ability in the multi-output setting. An experiment focused specifically on extrapolation would better showcase the benefits inherited from the original SM formulation.

2.  The experimental evaluation on real-world data is somewhat limited. The paper uses standard MOGP benchmark datasets where the proposed model achieves performance that is largely comparable to existing methods like CSM. The paper's impact could be greater if the authors included a real-world application where the unique properties of MOSM provide a clear and significant advantage over other models. This would better justify the model's increased parametric complexity and more convincingly demonstrate its practical value.

**Questions for the Authors**

Please clarify if statistical significance tests were performed for the results presented in Tables 1 and 2. The performance differences between several of the compared models often appear small, especially when considering the reported standard deviations.
