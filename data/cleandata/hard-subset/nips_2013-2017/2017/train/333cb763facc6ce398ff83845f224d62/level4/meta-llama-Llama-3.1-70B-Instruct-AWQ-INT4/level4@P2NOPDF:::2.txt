The paper proposes a novel approach to formulating covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes. Instead of directly specifying cross-covariance functions, the authors suggest specifying cross-power spectral densities and utilizing the inverse Fourier transform to obtain the corresponding cross-covariances. This approach is an extension of the idea presented in [6] for single-output Gaussian processes, where the power spectral density was represented as a mixture of Gaussians and then back-transformed to yield a powerful kernel function with extrapolation capabilities.

The authors apply their newly established covariance function to a synthetic data example and two real-world datasets: the weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations. Building upon the idea from [6] to construct new types of covariance functions for multiple outputs is a clever approach. One of the primary motivations in [6] was to create a kernel function capable of extrapolation. It would be interesting to see if the proposed covariance function for multiple outputs possesses similar extrapolation abilities.

The experimental section presents results using common datasets, with performance comparable to that of other covariance functions for multiple outputs. To increase the paper's impact, it would be beneficial to include experiments with datasets for which existing models are not suitable. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed covariance function's strengths and limitations.

Regarding the results presented in Tables 1 and 2, it is unclear whether the authors performed statistical significance tests. A cursory examination of the tables suggests that, within the standard deviation, almost all models exhibit similar performance. Providing statistical significance tests would help to better understand the differences in performance between the proposed covariance function and existing methods.
