- Introduces MOSM: constructs multi-output kernels by designing complex-valued cross-spectral densities, enforcing positive-definiteness via factorization, and applying inverse Fourier transform (Cramér’s theorem).  
- MOSM generalizes spectral mixture (SM) kernels to multi-output settings and subsumes SM-LMC and CSM while allowing channel-specific spectral means, scales, phases, and delays.  
- The spectral construction is principled: complex Gaussian spectral factors, frequency symmetrization for real-valued processes, and closed-form inverse transforms.  
- Positive definiteness is guaranteed by R(ω)H R(ω) factorization at each frequency; rank-Q extension yields additive kernel components.  
- Model parameters (magnitude, mean frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay) are interpretable and can differ across outputs/pairs, increasing expressivity.  
- Training and inference use standard GP marginal likelihood optimization; implementation provided in GPflow.  
- Synthetic experiments show MOSM outperforms LMC/CONV/CSM and can model simultaneous derivative and delay structure not representable by linear combinations of univariate kernels.  
- Climate sensor experiment: MOSM is competitive but not consistently superior; one station shows MOSM advantage by KS test, other differences not significant.  
- Swiss Jura (metals) experiment: MOSM gives significant improvement for Cadmium; for Copper MOSM and CSM perform similarly and SM-LMC remains competitive.  
- Paper clarity: generally clear and well-organized, though some derivations are dense; more intuition on how phase vs delay shape time-domain covariances would help.  
- Originality: novel and natural extension of SM using Cramér’s theorem with complex spectral factors and explicit delays.  
- Significance: potentially high for multi-sensor/time-series settings where phase and delay matter, but real-data gains on current benchmarks are modest.  
- Strengths: principled, interpretable spectral construction; captures phase and delays; subsumes prior methods; closed-form kernels with standard GP training; strong synthetic validation.  
- Weaknesses/suggestions: evaluate on delay-centric real tasks, report full statistical significance (p-values) and multiple splits, provide ablation studies (no-delay, shared vs per-channel, rank/Q), and discuss computational complexity/scaling and sparse approximations.  
- Practical concerns: address identifiability and sensitivity between phase and delay and provide hyperparameter initialization guidance.  
- Pro acceptance: theoretically solid, novel, more expressive than prior MOGP SM variants, and demonstrates unique capabilities on synthetic data.  
- Con acceptance: limited empirical gains on standard benchmarks and incomplete statistical/ablation/scalability analysis.  
- Overall: sound, original, and well-motivated work that would benefit from more comprehensive experiments and analyses to demonstrate practical impact.  
- Questions for authors: provide p-values for all comparisons and multiple-comparison handling, characterize sensitivity/identifiability of phase vs delay and initialization effects, and report computational/parameter scaling with m and Q plus any sparse/inducing results.
