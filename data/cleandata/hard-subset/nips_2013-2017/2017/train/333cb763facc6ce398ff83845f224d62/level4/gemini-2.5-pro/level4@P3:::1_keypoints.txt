- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending single-output SM kernels to MOGPs using Cramér's theorem.  
- Builds a Hermitian positive-definite cross-spectral density matrix via products of complex-valued functions; inverse Fourier transform gives a valid MOGP kernel.  
- MOSM can model magnitude, frequency, phase relationships and explicit time/space delays between outputs — a key novel capability.  
- Validated on a synthetic dataset that highlights derivatives and delays; compared against existing MOGP methods on two real-world datasets.  
- Strength: Principled, elegant theoretical development that generalizes spectral kernel design to the multi-output setting.  
- Strength: Clearly positions MOSM relative to LMC, CONV and shows CSM as a constrained special case.  
- Strength: Joint modeling of phase shifts and delays in a single framework is a clear advance over prior work.  
- Strength: Exceptionally clear writing, organized derivation, and interpretable hyperparameters.  
- Strength: Potentially significant impact for domains with complex cross-channel dynamics (geostatistics, econometrics, sensor processing).  
- Weakness: Real-world experiments offer marginal improvements; MOSM performs similarly to CSM on the climate dataset.  
- Weakness: Analysis of the Jura results is superficial; lacks deeper investigation into causes (optimization, overfitting, data non-Gaussianity).  
- For acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound method; generalizes CSM and introduces delay modeling; clear exposition and strong synthetic proof-of-concept.  
- Against acceptance: Limited practical advantage shown on real datasets given added complexity; experimental analysis needs strengthening.  
- Conclusion: Strong and novel theoretical contribution with clear presentation, but real-world experimental validation could be more compelling.
