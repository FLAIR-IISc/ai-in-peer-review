

This paper introduces a multi-output Gaussian process covariance function constructed in the spectral domain. The authors extend the spectral mixture kernel framework from Wilson and Adams (2013) to the multi-output setting, addressing the key challenge of modeling cross-covariances, which do not need to be positive definite. By employing complex-valued cross-spectral densities and leveraging Cramér's theorem, the authors provide a principled approach to designing multi-output covariance functions that can model delays between channels in addition to phase differences, while maintaining full parametric interpretability.

The paper is well-structured with a concise introduction that effectively establishes the problem context and foundational GP concepts. The explanation of the spectral mixture kernel is clear and accessible, and the theoretical development of the proposed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel is rigorous and novel. The relationship to previous work, particularly the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel and Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC), is well-articulated, demonstrating clear advancement over existing approaches.

The experimental evaluation is appropriate for the paper's contributions, with validation on synthetic data demonstrating the model's ability to recover known covariance structures and comparisons on two real-world datasets (climate signals and heavy metal concentrations). While the Jura dataset results are somewhat difficult to intuitively interpret due to the high correlation between signals, the performance metrics (MAE) are presented clearly and the MOSM model achieves competitive results with statistical significance on the Cadmium data.

Strengths:
- Novel extension of spectral mixture approach to multi-output GPs
- Clear theoretical development using Cramér's theorem
- Full parametric interpretation of model parameters in spectral terms
- Demonstrated ability to model delays and phase differences not captured by prior methods
- Comprehensive experimental validation across synthetic and real-world data

Weaknesses:
- Limited intuitive explanation of experimental results, particularly for the Jura dataset
- Some technical aspects of the spectral domain formulation could benefit from additional clarification
- The practical significance of modeling delays could be better emphasized in the discussion

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the Gaussian process literature by providing a principled method for multi-output covariance design with clear parametric interpretation. The MOSM kernel advances the state of the art in multi-output Gaussian processes and offers a more expressive alternative to existing methods while maintaining theoretical soundness. The quality of the technical contribution, clarity of presentation, and appropriate experimental validation support acceptance.
