This paper introduces a new family of covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes by specifying cross-power spectral densities (cross-PSDs) and obtaining the corresponding cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transforms. The approach parallels [6] for single-output Gaussian processes, where the power spectral density is modeled as a mixture of Gaussians and then inverse-transformed to yield a flexible kernel with extrapolation capabilities. The proposed covariance is evaluated on a synthetic example and two real datasets: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura metal concentration dataset.

Strengths:
- Conceptual extension of [6] to the multi-output setting is natural and technically sound. Defining cross-PSDs and deriving cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transforms is a principled way to construct multi-output kernels.
- The connection to spectral mixture kernels suggests potential for capturing complex correlations and periodicities.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- A key motivation in [6] was to enable extrapolation through spectral mixture modeling. It would be valuable to demonstrate that the proposed multi-output covariance inherits similar extrapolation capabilities across outputs. As presented, this aspect is not evaluated.
- The experimental evaluation relies on common benchmarks (synthetic, weather, Swiss Jura), and the reported performance is broadly similar to existing multi-output covariance functions. The contribution would be more compelling with experiments on datasets where competing models are known to struggle (e.g., settings requiring long-range extrapolation, sharp spectral features, or complex inter-output phase relationships).
- Statistical significance is unclear. In Tables 1 and 2, the means and standard deviations suggest that many methods perform similarly within error bars. Please clarify whether you conducted statistical significance tests and report the outcomes.

Suggestions:
- Include experiments explicitly designed to test extrapolation across outputs (e.g., train on truncated time ranges or sparsely sampled frequencies and evaluate future horizons), comparing against strong multi-output baselines.
- Add tasks where spectral structure and cross-output dependencies are crucial and other models underperform, to highlight unique advantages of the proposed kernel.
- Report formal significance testing for Tables 1 and 2 and consider paired tests across splits to substantiate claims of improvement.
