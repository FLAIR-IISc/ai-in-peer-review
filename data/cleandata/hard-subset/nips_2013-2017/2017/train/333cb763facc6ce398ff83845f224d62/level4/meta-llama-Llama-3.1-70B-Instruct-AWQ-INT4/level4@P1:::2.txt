The paper formulates a new type of covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes. Instead of directly specifying the cross-covariance functions, the authors propose to specify the cross-power spectral densities and use the inverse Fourier transform to find the corresponding cross-covariances. This approach is an extension of the idea proposed by Wilson et al. (2013) for single-output Gaussian processes, where the power spectral density was represented through a mixture of Gaussians and then back-transformed to obtain a powerful kernel function with extrapolation abilities.

The authors apply the newly established covariance function to a synthetic data example and two real-world data examples: the weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset for metal concentrations. The experimental results show that the proposed method is competitive with other existing methods for multi-output Gaussian processes.

One of the motivations behind the spectral mixture kernel for single-output Gaussian processes was its ability to perform extrapolation. It would be interesting to explore whether the proposed multi-output spectral mixture kernel retains this ability. The experimental section only provides results on very common datasets, and the results obtained are similar to those obtained with other covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes. The impact of the paper could be increased by including experiments on datasets for which none of the other models are suitable.

The authors should also consider testing for the statistical significance of the results in Tables 1 and 2. It appears that, within the standard deviation, almost all models provide similar performance. This suggests that the differences between the models may not be statistically significant.

Weaknesses:

* The paper does not explore the extrapolation abilities of the proposed covariance function.
