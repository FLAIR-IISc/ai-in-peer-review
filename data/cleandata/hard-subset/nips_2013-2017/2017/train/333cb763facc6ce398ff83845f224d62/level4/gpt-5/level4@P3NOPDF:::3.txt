Summary
The paper proposes a new covariance structure for multi-output Gaussian processes that extends the spectral approach based on Bochner’s theorem to the multi-output setting. A notable strength is the interpretability of parameters, which capture meaningful cross-output properties such as delays and phase differences.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and challenging problem: designing expressive, interpretable kernels for multi-output GPs, for which relatively few methods exist.
- The method appears theoretically sound and is clearly derived.
- Empirical evidence on several datasets demonstrates effectiveness.
- The paper is well written overall; the method description and illustrative examples are appropriate and easy to follow.

Concerns and points for clarification
- Parameter learning: The paper provides limited details on training. Please clarify the optimization objective, initialization strategies, constraints/priors to ensure identifiability and positive definiteness, and any regularization used. Practical guidance would help readers reproduce and deploy the method.
- Data efficiency: It is unclear how robust the approach is with few training points. Given the potentially rich parameterization, a discussion of overfitting risks and mitigation (e.g., priors, tying parameters, or sparsity) would be valuable.
- Cross-scale correlations: Conceptually, channels with very different length-scales are often weakly correlated. Please explain how the model represents or limits strong correlations between a long length-scale channel and a short length-scale channel (e.g., via cross-spectral mixtures, delays, and phases), and whether this introduces identifiability or overfitting issues.
- Computational aspects: A brief complexity analysis with respect to the number of outputs, mixture components, and training points would be useful, along with implementation details (e.g., use of inducing points, structure exploitation, mini-batching) and typical runtimes.

Questions for the authors
- How does the method perform with few training points? Could you provide learning curves (performance vs. number of training points) and comment on stability across random initializations?
- Can a channel with a large length-scale be strongly correlated with a channel with a small length-scale in your model? If so, through which parameters does this arise, and under what conditions is it expected or discouraged?

Comments to improve the paper
- Section 4.1 (synthetic example): please specify the number of training points (and noise level, if applicable).
- Expand the description of parameter learning: optimization algorithm, initialization heuristics, regularization/constraints, and any sensitivity analysis.
- Include a short discussion comparing to other multi-output kernel constructions, emphasizing when your approach is preferable (e.g., interpretability, flexibility, or computational trade-offs).

Minor remarks
- Possible missing word(s) around lines 74 and 140; please proofread.
- Minor language fixes: “multioutputs GPs” → “multi-output GPs”; “informations” → “information.”
- A few small stylistic edits would further improve clarity and flow.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution that generalizes spectral kernel constructions to the multi-output setting with interpretable parameters and promising empirical results. With additional details on parameter learning, computational complexity, and behavior in low-data regimes, the paper would be even stronger. Subject to these clarifications, I anticipate this approach could become a standard tool for multi-output GPs.
