This paper introduces a new covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes. Rather than specifying cross-covariances directly in the input domain, the authors define cross-power spectral densities and obtain the corresponding cross-covariances via the inverse Fourier transform. The idea parallels [6] for single-output GPs, where a Gaussian mixture representation of the power spectral density is inverted to yield a flexible kernel with extrapolation capabilities. The proposed kernel is evaluated on a synthetic example and two real datasets: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura metal concentration dataset.

Extending [6] to the multi-output setting is a natural and elegant way to construct richer covariance functions across tasks. A key motivation in [6] was the ability to extrapolate beyond the observed range. It would be valuable to demonstrate that the multi-output version inherits this property. As presented, the experiments focus on standard benchmarks, and the reported performance appears broadly comparable to that of existing multi-output kernels. The contribution would be materially strengthened by including experiments on problems where competing models are known to underperform or fail, thereby highlighting unique advantages of the proposed kernel (e.g., tasks requiring long-range extrapolation across inputs or transfer across related outputs).
