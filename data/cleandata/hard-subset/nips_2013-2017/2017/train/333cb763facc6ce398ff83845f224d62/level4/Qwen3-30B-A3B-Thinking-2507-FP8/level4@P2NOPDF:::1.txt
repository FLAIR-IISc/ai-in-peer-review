

This paper introduces a novel multi-output Gaussian process covariance function defined in the spectral domain, extending the spectral mixture framework pioneered by Wilson and Adams (2013) to the multi-output setting. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while spectral-domain covariance design has proven powerful for single-output GPs, its extension to multi-output scenarios requires careful handling of cross-covariances, which need not be positive-definite. The proposed method elegantly resolves this challenge, yielding a spectral mixture kernel for multi-output GPs that maintains theoretical coherence while enabling flexible modeling of complex dependencies. This contribution is timely and well-motivated, as spectral-domain approaches have been underexplored relative to their potential for expressive kernel design.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a concise introduction that effectively contextualizes the problem within Gaussian process fundamentals. The explanation of the spectral mixture kernel is particularly clear, and the extensions to multi-output settings are presented with mathematical rigor. The relationship to prior work—especially the foundational spectral mixture kernel in [1]—is thoroughly discussed, and the novelty is compelling: the authors demonstrate how to construct valid multi-output spectral kernels without enforcing unnecessary positive-definiteness constraints on cross-covariances. This represents a meaningful advancement over existing multi-output GP methods, which often rely on restrictive assumptions or ad hoc constructions.

The experimental evaluation is adequate for the paper’s scope. The authors demonstrate performance on standard benchmarks, including the Jura dataset, where the model achieves competitive results. However, the analysis lacks deeper interpretability, particularly regarding the Jura dataset, where the physical or spatial intuition behind the model’s behavior remains opaque. While this limitation does not undermine the core contribution, additional insights into why the spectral mixture kernel excels in specific contexts would strengthen the empirical case. That said, for a methodological paper focused on kernel design, the experimental validation is sufficient and aligns with typical standards for the field.

The technical contributions are sound, with clear derivations and a well-structured presentation. The writing avoids unnecessary jargon, making the core ideas accessible to the broader NIPS audience. The authors successfully bridge theoretical insights (e.g., the spectral representation of cross-covariances) with practical implementation, which is crucial for adoption in applied settings. The paper’s novelty is unambiguous: it generalizes a single-output spectral technique to multi-output GPs while addressing a non-trivial mathematical challenge, offering a principled alternative to existing approaches like the linear model of coregionalization.

In summary, this work makes a valuable contribution to Gaussian process methodology by extending spectral-domain covariance design to multi-output problems. The technical depth, clarity of exposition, and novelty justify publication at NIPS. While the experimental section could benefit from more interpretive analysis, this does not detract from the paper’s primary strength: a rigorous and innovative kernel construction. The authors have clearly advanced the state of the art in GP kernel design, and their approach will likely inspire further work in spectral methods for multi-output learning. I recommend acceptance.
