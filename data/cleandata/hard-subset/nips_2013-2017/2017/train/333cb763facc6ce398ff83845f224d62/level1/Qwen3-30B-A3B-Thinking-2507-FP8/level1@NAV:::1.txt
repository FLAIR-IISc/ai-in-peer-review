

This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes, extending the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel [6] to the multi-output case using complex-valued cross-spectral densities and Cramér's theorem. The authors build upon the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5], which introduced phase shifts but could not model delays between channels. MOSM generalizes CSM by allowing channel-specific parameters for mean, covariance, delay, and phase, providing a more expressive model that can capture both phase differences and temporal delays. The paper demonstrates the method on synthetic data (learning derivatives and delayed signals) and two real-world datasets (climate signals and heavy metal concentrations), showing competitive performance against SM-LMC, CONV, and CSM.

Strengths include strong theoretical grounding using Cramér's theorem, which properly handles positive definiteness of the multivariate kernel. The clear parametric interpretation of all kernel parameters (frequencies, magnitudes, phases, delays) is a significant advantage over previous approaches like LMC that lack interpretability. The experimental validation is thorough, with the MOSM successfully modeling delayed signals where other methods fail. The paper is well-written and logically structured, with clear mathematical derivations.

However, the empirical improvements over CSM are modest in real-world applications. In the climate data experiment, MOSM shows similar performance to CSM without clear statistical significance. In the heavy metal dataset, MOSM outperforms other methods only for Cadmium (not Copper), and the gains are marginal. The paper could better justify the added complexity of the MOSM model by demonstrating cases where the delay modeling capability is essential rather than merely beneficial. While the theoretical extension is sound, the practical significance could be stronger.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution that extends the spectral mixture approach to MOGPs with meaningful new capabilities. The work is well-executed and makes a clear, incremental advance in the field. The minor improvements in real-world performance are offset by the theoretical novelty and interpretability benefits. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better emphasize the practical value of the delay modeling capability.
