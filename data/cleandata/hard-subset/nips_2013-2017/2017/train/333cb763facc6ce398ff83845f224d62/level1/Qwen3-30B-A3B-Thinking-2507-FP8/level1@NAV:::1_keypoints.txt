- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel extending single-output SM to multi-output GPs using complex-valued cross-spectral densities and Cramér’s theorem.
- Builds on Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) by introducing channel-specific mean, covariance, delay, and phase parameters to model both phase differences and temporal delays.
- The MOSM can capture delays between channels, a capability CSM lacked.
- Demonstrates performance on synthetic tasks (learning derivatives and delayed signals) and real-world climate and heavy-metal datasets.
- Compares MOSM against SM-LMC, CONV, and CSM, showing competitive results.
- Theoretical strength: solid grounding in Cramér’s theorem ensuring positive definiteness for multivariate kernels.
- Interpretability strength: clear parametric meaning for frequencies, magnitudes, phases, and delays compared to LMC.
- Experimental strength: thorough validation and successful modeling of delayed signals where other methods fail.
- Writing strength: well-written, logically structured paper with clear mathematical derivations.
- Empirical weakness: only modest improvements over CSM on real-world data.
- Climate experiment: MOSM performs similarly to CSM with no clear statistical significance.
- Heavy-metal experiment: MOSM outperforms others only for Cadmium, with marginal gains for Copper.
- Practical concern: added model complexity needs stronger justification by demonstrating cases where delay modeling is essential.
- Overall judgment: technically sound, incremental advance with theoretical novelty and interpretability; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to emphasize practical value of delay modeling.
