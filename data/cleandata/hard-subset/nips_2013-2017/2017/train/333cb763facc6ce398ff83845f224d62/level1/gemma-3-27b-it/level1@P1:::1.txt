This paper proposes a novel approach to constructing multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernels based on extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel concept. The core idea is to leverage Cramér’s theorem to design a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities, leading to a more expressive and interpretable MOGP kernel (MOSM) capable of modeling delays between channels in addition to phase differences. The authors provide theoretical justification, a synthetic validation, and comparisons to existing methods on two real-world datasets.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of previous work in MOGPs, particularly the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) [3], the Convolution Model (CONV) [4], the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5], and the SM kernel [6]. It correctly identifies the limitations of earlier approaches regarding interpretability and flexibility in modeling cross-covariances. The extension of the SM kernel idea using Cramér’s theorem is a logical progression and effectively addresses the limitations of CSM by offering a more general framework.  The paper correctly references key theoretical results like Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems [7, 8, 9].  Related work on spectral methods [17] is also mentioned, though perhaps briefly.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the MOSM kernel is mathematically well-grounded, based on established theorems (Cramér’s theorem). The connection between the spectral density and the covariance function via the inverse Fourier transform is clearly explained.
* **Expressiveness:** The proposed MOSM kernel offers greater flexibility than previous methods, specifically in its ability to model delays and phase differences between channels, addressing a significant limitation of existing approaches.
* **Interpretability:** The parametric form of the cross-spectral densities provides clear spectral interpretation of the parameters, which is a key advantage over black-box kernel methods.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides validation on both synthetic and real-world data. The synthetic experiment demonstrates the ability of the model to learn the expected covariance structure. The real-world experiments show competitive performance on climate and metal concentration datasets.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:**  The paper does not explicitly address the computational cost associated with the MOSM kernel, especially for large datasets. While GPflow is used, the scaling properties of the kernel with the number of outputs and data points are not discussed. This is a crucial consideration for practical applications.
* **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons are made to several baselines, the parameter tuning for those baselines isn't thoroughly described. A more extensive ablation study showing the impact of each parameter in the MOSM kernel would be beneficial.
* **Synthetic Data Limitations:** The synthetic experiment, while informative, feels relatively simple. More complex synthetic scenarios with varying levels of noise and correlation would further strengthen the validation.
* **Real-World Dataset Selection:** The choice of datasets (climate and metal concentration) is somewhat limited. Exploring other application domains would broaden the scope of the evaluation.  The conclusion acknowledges the limited significance of improvements on some datasets.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The relationship between MOSM and other recent advances in spectral GP methods, beyond those explicitly mentioned, could be elaborated on. For example, discussions on kernel learning and automatic relevance determination could strengthen the context.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP kernel design.
* The MOSM kernel offers increased expressiveness and interpretability compared to existing methods.
* The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the MOSM kernel is not addressed.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, with more extensive comparisons and ablation studies.
* The paper's contribution, while significant, is incremental and might not represent a major breakthrough in the field.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of MOGPs. The proposed MOSM kernel is a well-motivated and technically sound extension of existing methods. The empirical results are promising, and the theoretical justification is strong. However, the lack of discussion regarding computational complexity and the limited scope of the empirical evaluation raise some concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the computational complexity concerns, provide a more detailed description of the baseline parameter tuning, and consider expanding the empirical evaluation with more complex synthetic scenarios and/or additional real-world datasets.  A brief discussion of how the MOSM kernel might be adapted for large-scale applications (e.g., using sparse GP approximations) would also be valuable.
