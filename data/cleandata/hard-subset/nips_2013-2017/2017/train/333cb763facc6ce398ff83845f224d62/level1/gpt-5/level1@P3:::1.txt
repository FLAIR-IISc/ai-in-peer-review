Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernel, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, derived by specifying a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and invoking Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem). Each output i has its own per-output spectral parameters (weight wi, mean μi, covariance Σi), as well as a per-output delay θi and phase φi. Through a low-rank spectral factorization S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω), the cross-spectra Sij are constructed as mixtures of complex squared exponentials whose inverse Fourier transforms yield closed-form cross-covariances: exponentially windowed cosines with output-pair-specific frequency μij, bandwidth Σij, delay θij, and phase φij. This generalizes the spectral mixture (SM) kernel of Wilson & Adams (ICML 2013) to multiple outputs, and subsumes SM-LMC and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015) as special or more constrained cases. Unlike LMC/CONV, MOSM provides interpretable, output-pair-specific cross-covariances and can jointly model phase and delay.

Experiments validate MOSM on a synthetic tri-variate setting (original signal, derivative, and delayed signal) where the true cross-covariances are known; on a climate sensor imputation task; and on the Jura heavy metals dataset. MOSM often performs best or competitively in MAE, with particularly strong gains on the synthetic task and on Cadmium in Jura.

Quality
- Theory and correctness: The construction via Cramér’s theorem and a spectral factor S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) is a principled way to guarantee Hermitian PSD cross-spectra, hence a valid MOGP kernel. The use of complex SEs for R(ω) yields closed-form inverse transforms. However, there are a few notational and technical issues that should be clarified:
  - In Eq. (7) and the subsequent derivation, Sij should be Sij(ω)=R:i(ω)H R:j(ω). Later, the text writes Sij=Ri Rj (missing conjugation). Please ensure consistent use of Hermitian transpose to avoid ambiguity.
  - For real-valued processes, the standard conjugate symmetry is Sij(−ω)=Sij(ω)*. The paper enforces “symmetry with respect to ω” using (Sij(ω)+Sij(−ω))/2. This yields real cross-covariances in the presented parametrization, but the condition should be stated and justified more carefully (e.g., by showing that the chosen mirrored complex SE components imply the required conjugate symmetry and preserve PSD).
  - Identifiability: there are inherent symmetries between per-output (θi, φi, μi, Σi) that induce the same pairwise (θij, φij, μij, Σij). Some discussion of identifiability, parameter tying, and regularization would be useful.
  - Rank: The model is rank-Q in the spectral factorization (sum over q), which is expressive but still constrains the cross-spectral matrix rank at each ω. This is a reasonable tradeoff, but worth discussing explicitly.

- Empirical evaluation: The synthetic task is well motivated (derivatives and delays) and clearly highlights MOSM’s strengths beyond LMC/CONV. Real-data evaluations are reasonable and competitive. Nonetheless:
  - Evaluation metrics are limited to MAE; including (calibrated) log-likelihood or CRPS would assess uncertainty quality.
  - Initialization of SM/MOSM components is critical; details are missing. Please describe how μ, Σ, θ, φ were initialized and whether multiple restarts were used.
  - Model capacity and parameter counts differ across approaches. Reporting number of parameters per model and sensitivity to Q would help judge fairness.
  - Computational aspects (training time, scalability) are not reported. Given O(N^3) costs, a brief complexity analysis and any practical tricks (e.g., batching, inducing variables) would be valuable.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The spectral-domain construction is explained clearly, and the parameter interpretation (magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay) is a strength. A few suggestions:
- Fix the conjugation inconsistencies noted above and explicitly state the Hermitian and conjugate-symmetry conditions for real multi-output processes.
- In Eq. (9–11), make explicit whether φij=φi−φj appears with conjugation in the mirrored term, and show the short derivation to Eq. (10) to preempt confusion.
- Minor typos (e.g., “multiouput”, “wrt”) and notation (e.g., consistent use of superscripts (q)).

Originality
The work extends SM kernels to the multi-output setting with per-output parameters and, crucially, introduces a principled way to encode both delays and phase shifts via complex cross-spectra, while guaranteeing PSD through spectral factorization. CSM modeled phase but not explicit delays and often shared μ, Σ across outputs; MOSM strictly generalizes it. Using Cramér’s theorem this way for complex cross-spectra appears novel relative to LMC/CONV and prior spectral MOGP kernels.

Significance
MOSM offers an interpretable and flexible kernel family for MOGPs with closed-form covariances, enabling richer cross-channel structure (delays and phase). This is useful in sensor networks, spatiotemporal forecasting, and systems with known lags. Empirical gains on synthetic and one real dataset are convincing; on climate data, MOSM is competitive but not decisively better, which is unsurprising given high inter-sensor similarity. The approach is limited to stationarity and inherits SM’s sensitivity to initialization and potential overfitting, and scalability remains a challenge. Even so, this is a solid and useful contribution.

Pros
- Principled spectral construction guaranteeing PSD; clear parameter interpretability.
- Joint modeling of delays and phase differences; closed-form time-domain kernels.
- Generalization of SM-LMC and CSM; good synthetic and competitive real-data results.
- Clean connection to Cramér’s theorem; simple to implement in GP toolkits.

Cons
- Notational/technical ambiguities around conjugation and real-valuedness conditions.
- Limited evaluation metrics and discussion of initialization, parameter counts, and runtime.
- Assumes stationarity; no nonstationary extension or structured priors over delays/phases.
- Rank-Q factorization may be restrictive for some cross-spectral structures.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the Hermitian and conjugate symmetry conditions and ensure consistent use of R(ω)H in all equations; provide a short proof that the mirrored construction yields real-valued covariances while preserving PSD.
- Report initialization strategy, number of restarts, parameter counts, and training time; add predictive log-likelihood.
- Provide ablations varying Q and removing θ/φ to quantify their contributions.
- Discuss identifiability and potential priors/constraints on θ and φ.
- Consider or outline sparse/inducing-variable variants leveraging spectral structure.

Overall, this is a technically sound, interpretable, and generally well-presented kernel construction with meaningful improvements over prior spectral MOGP kernels. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarity and evaluation issues.
