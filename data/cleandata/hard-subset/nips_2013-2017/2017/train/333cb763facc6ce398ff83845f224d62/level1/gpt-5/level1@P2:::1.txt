Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to design cross-spectral densities directly in the Fourier domain using Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem), ensuring positive definiteness by factorizing the complex-valued cross-spectrum S(ω) as R(ω)H R(ω). Each per-output factor Ri(ω) is modeled as a complex Gaussian (square-exponential) with interpretable parameters (weight, mean frequency, spectral covariance, delay, and phase). Symmetrizing S(ω) yields real-valued cross-covariances whose inverse Fourier transform has a closed form: a SE envelope multiplied by a cosine with channel-pair-specific frequency, delay, and phase. The construction subsumes the SM-LMC and CSM kernels (Wilson & Adams 2013; Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015) and adds the ability to model delays jointly with phase shifts while retaining interpretability.

Experiments include: (i) a synthetic 3-output setting (original signal, derivative, and delayed version) showing MOSM uniquely extrapolates derivative and delay behavior; (ii) climate data imputation across four stations with similar performance to strong baselines; and (iii) Jura geostatistics where MOSM improves over alternatives on Cadmium, with competitive results on Copper.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The construction is principled. Enforcing positive definiteness via the spectral factorization S(ω)=RHR and symmetrization to obtain real kernels is sound. The resulting kernel expression and parameter interpretations follow cleanly. The derivations (pairwise parameters µij, Σij, θij, φij, weights) are consistent with Gaussian algebra.
- Empirical support: The synthetic task convincingly shows a case where linear latent-process models (LMC/CONV) struggle because the needed cross-covariances are not linear combinations of univariate kernels; MOSM succeeds. Real-data results are competitive, with statistically significant gains in some settings. However, datasets are modest, and ablations (e.g., effect of Q, rank) and runtime/scalability are not assessed.
- Reproducibility: Implementation uses GPflow/TensorFlow, but initialization strategies, constraints, and optimization details are sparse. No runtime/complexity reporting or code release is indicated.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written, with a clear progression from Cramér’s theorem to the MOSM kernel. The parameter interpretation (magnitude, mean frequency, bandwidth, delay, phase) is helpful and distinguishes auto- and cross-covariances.
- Some notational issues (e.g., factors of 1/2 in exponentials) could be double-checked; state explicitly the exact forms used for Ri(ω) and the symmetry operation. Clarify that summing Q components yields rank at most Q for S(ω) and discuss its implications.
- Add a brief treatment of identifiability: since cross-parameters depend on differences (θi−θj, φi−φj), global shifts are unidentifiable; suggest fixing a reference (e.g., θ1=0, φ1=0) or priors.

Originality:
- The move from real-valued cross-spectra (SM-LMC) and ad hoc complex components for phase (CSM) to a systematic complex cross-spectral factorization that naturally yields both phase and delay is novel. The per-output parameterization that induces pairwise cross-parameters is elegant and interpretable.
- The use of Cramér’s theorem for complex cross-spectra has precursors, but the specific MOSM parameterization, interpretation, and closed-form kernel are new and subsume prior spectral MOGP kernels.

Significance:
- Provides a flexible, interpretable family of MOGP kernels that can express asymmetric cross-covariances with both phase and delay—useful for multi-sensor signals, spatio-temporal processes, and cross-channel dependencies.
- Practical impact could be high if coupled with scalable inference. As presented, applicability is limited by cubic complexity and optimization challenges for many outputs and components.

Strengths:
- Principled construction guaranteeing positive definiteness.
- Clear spectral interpretation; explicit modeling of delay and phase across channels.
- Generalization that strictly subsumes SM-LMC and CSM.
- Closed-form time-domain kernel enabling standard GP inference.
- Convincing synthetic example demonstrating expressivity advantages.

Weaknesses:
- Scalability and computational complexity are not addressed (no inducing methods or structured approximations). Practical training for large m, N, Q is unclear.
- Optimization details are limited: initialization of frequencies/delays/phases, parameter constraints (e.g., phase wrap-around), and regularization are not discussed. Risk of overfitting and local minima in highly parameterized models is not analyzed.
- Identifiability: only relative delays/phases are identifiable; recommend explicit constraints. Potential redundancy between delay and phase for narrow-band components is not discussed.
- Expressivity vs. constraints: Cross-mean frequencies µij are coupled to per-output µi, µj; this may constrain cross-spectra to lie between channels’ spectra, which could be restrictive if cross-covariances exhibit frequencies absent in autospectra. An empirical ablation would help.
- Limited empirical diversity and no ablations on Q (rank), per-output vs. per-pair parameterization, or sensitivity to initialization.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a scalability section with either variational inducing features (e.g., inter-domain/Fourier features) or SKI/Kronecker/block-Toeplitz exploitation for stationary kernels to handle long time series and many outputs.
- Provide initialization heuristics (e.g., per-channel spectral peaks via periodograms) and constraints (fix a reference channel’s θ, φ; bound phases in [−π, π]; priors on delays).
- Include ablations: vary Q and report performance vs. runtime; assess rank sufficiency relative to m; test robustness to initialization.
- Report runtime and memory vs. baselines. Release code.
- Discuss identifiability and possible parameter degeneracies; consider reparameterizations tying φ and θ to µ for interpretability.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, principled, and interpretable extension of spectral GPs to the multi-output setting with capabilities (joint delay and phase) beyond prior art.
- Clear mathematical contribution with closed-form kernels and guaranteed PSD.
- Demonstrated benefits on a synthetic task that stresses cross-channel structure; competitive real-world performance.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited evaluation breadth and lack of scalability story may hinder impact.
- Missing ablations and optimization details reduce reproducibility and leave open questions about robustness.
- Gains over CSM/SM-LMC are modest on real data; stronger evidence on more diverse tasks would strengthen the case.

Overall recommendation:
A good scientific contribution with a principled kernel construction and clear interpretability that extends prior spectral MOGP methods. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying optimization/identifiability and discussing scalability, as the core idea is sound and broadly useful.
