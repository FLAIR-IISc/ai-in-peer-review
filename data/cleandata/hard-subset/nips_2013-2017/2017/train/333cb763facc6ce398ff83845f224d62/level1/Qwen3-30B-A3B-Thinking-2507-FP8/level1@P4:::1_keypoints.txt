- Proposes MOSM kernel extending Spectral Mixture to multi-output GPs, using complex-valued cross-spectral densities.  
- Builds on Cramér's Theorem to provide a principled, theoretically sound construction of multi-output covariance functions.  
- Key contribution: models both phase differences and explicit delays across channels, addressing a limitation of prior CSM approaches.  
- Derivation from complex spectral densities to real-valued covariances is mathematically rigorous with attention to positive definiteness.  
- Experiments include a synthetic recovery task and two real-world datasets, demonstrating the model’s capabilities and comparative performance.  
- Implementation details (GPflow/TensorFlow) are sufficient for reproducibility and practical use.  
- Paper clearly acknowledges limitations (e.g., need for sparse implementations) and discusses statistical significance appropriately.  
- Writing and organization are excellent; background and derivations are accessible and well-explained for readers familiar with GPs.  
- Figures effectively illustrate model behavior (e.g., effects of delay/phase parameters and synthetic signal recovery).  
- Originality: first explicit incorporation of delays in spectral mixture MOGPs, extending prior SM and CSM work.  
- Situates contribution well relative to LMC, CONV, CSM, and SM-LMC, showing a clear gap filled by MOSM.  
- Significance: useful for applications with temporal delays (e.g., sensor networks) and provides interpretable physical parameters.  
- Strengths summarized: strong theoretical foundation, clear derivation, comprehensive experiments, clarity, and unique delay modeling.  
- Weaknesses: lacks detailed computational complexity comparison with existing methods.  
- Weaknesses: limited analysis linking estimated parameters to true delays in the synthetic example.  
- Weaknesses: could more explicitly relate MOSM to other MOGP approaches like Matérn cross-covariances.  
- Pro arguments: novel, principled, interpretable, theoretically sound, and shows empirical advantages in some scenarios.  
- Con arguments: limited complexity analysis, marginal gains on some datasets, and possible smaller gap with CSM than claimed.  
- Overall assessment: significant theoretical contribution, appropriate validation, clear advantages in specific cases, recommended for acceptance.
