The paper proposes a novel approach to constructing multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernels by extending the spectral mixture (SM) concept to multiple outputs, leveraging Cramér’s Theorem. The core idea is to define complex-valued cross-spectral densities and then use the inverse Fourier transform to obtain the covariance function. This allows for modelling delays and phase differences between channels with a fully parametric interpretation. The paper is well-structured, clearly presenting the theoretical background and experimental validation.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the existing literature on MOGPs, particularly the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) [3, 4] and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5]. It identifies a limitation in CSM—the manual introduction of phase shifts—and aims to provide a more principled and flexible approach. The authors appropriately position their work within the broader context of Gaussian process kernel design, referencing Bochner’s and Cramér’s Theorems [7, 8, 9] as theoretical foundations. They connect to recent work on spectral mixture kernels [6] and their application to time series modelling [10, 11]. The comparison to related MOGP methods (CONV, SM-LMC, CSM) is relevant and helps to highlight the contribution of the proposed MOSM kernel.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The approach is firmly grounded in harmonic analysis and probability theory, using Cramér’s Theorem to guarantee positive definiteness. This is a significant strength.
* **Expressiveness:** The proposed MOSM kernel offers increased expressiveness compared to existing MOGP methods, enabling the modeling of delays and phase differences in addition to phase shifts, offering a potentially richer representation of inter-channel relationships.
* **Parametric Interpretation:**  The parameters of the kernel have clear spectral interpretations, which aids in understanding and potentially regularizing the model.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical derivations are presented in a clear and concise manner.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets provide evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The synthetic example effectively demonstrates the ability to recover delays and derivatives.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors acknowledge and appropriately position their work with respect to existing MOGP methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While the theoretical foundation is strong, the resulting kernel is relatively complex with numerous parameters. This may pose challenges for practical application, particularly with high-dimensional outputs. The need for careful parameter tuning and potential overfitting are concerns.
* **Limited Real-World Advantage:** While performing competitively on the real-world datasets, the advantage of MOSM over simpler methods (like CSM) is not always substantial, and often lacks statistical significance. The climate data experiment, in particular, shows little differentiation.  More compelling results on more complex real-world problems would strengthen the claims.
* **Computational Cost:** The increased complexity of the kernel likely translates to higher computational cost for training and prediction, although this is not explicitly discussed in the paper.  A discussion of computational scalability would be beneficial.
* **Rank-One Assumption:** The initial formulation of the spectral density (Sij) relies on a rank-one decomposition (Q=1). While the extension to arbitrary Q is mentioned, the impact of choosing larger Q on performance and computational cost isn’t thoroughly explored.
* **Lack of Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The sensitivity of the performance to variations in the hyperparameters is not thoroughly examined.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP kernel design.
* The proposed MOSM kernel offers increased expressiveness and a clear parametric interpretation.
* The experimental validation, while not overwhelmingly superior, demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach.
* The work contributes to the growing body of literature on spectral methods for Gaussian processes.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The increased complexity of the kernel may limit its practical applicability.
* The real-world results do not consistently demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.
* The computational cost of the proposed approach is not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of Gaussian process modeling. While the proposed MOSM kernel is relatively complex, its theoretical foundation, expressiveness, and clear parametric interpretation are valuable assets. The experimental results provide support for the effectiveness of the approach, though a more convincing demonstration of its superiority on challenging real-world problems would strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

* Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method and potential strategies for improving scalability.
* Explore the impact of different values of Q (rank of the spectral density matrix) on performance and computational cost.
* Conduct a more thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
* Consider applying the method to a more challenging real-world dataset where the benefits of the proposed kernel are more apparent.
* Explicitly state the limitations of the proposed approach.
