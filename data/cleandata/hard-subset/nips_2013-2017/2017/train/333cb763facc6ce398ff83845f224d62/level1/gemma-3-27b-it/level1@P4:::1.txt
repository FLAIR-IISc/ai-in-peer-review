The paper proposes a novel approach to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel concept to multiple outputs. The core idea is to define a family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and utilize Cramér's Theorem to construct a multivariate covariance function, termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. This kernel aims to offer more expressiveness than existing methods by modeling delays among channels alongside phase differences, while maintaining full parametric interpretability. The method is validated on synthetic data and compared to existing approaches on two real-world datasets.

The paper builds upon established work in Gaussian processes (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006), spectral mixture kernels (Wilson & Adams, 2013), and multi-output extensions (Alvarez et al., 2008; Genton & Kleiber, 2015). It directly addresses the limitations of earlier MOGP models like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, particularly their limited interpretability and inability to effectively model delays. The use of Cramér’s Theorem provides a sound theoretical foundation for the proposed kernel construction, drawing on work in time series analysis and signal processing (Yaglom, 1987; Kay, 1993). The comparison to CSM (Ulrich et al., 2015) is relevant, as the proposed method can be seen as a generalization.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The approach is well-grounded in Cramér's Theorem, providing a principled way to design multivariate covariance functions.
*   **Expressiveness:** The MOSM kernel introduces delay parameters, enhancing its ability to model complex relationships between outputs beyond phase differences.
*   **Interpretability:** The parameters of the kernel have clear spectral interpretations, facilitating understanding of the modeled dependencies.
*   **Synthetic Validation:** The synthetic example demonstrates the kernel’s ability to accurately recover known covariances, including derivatives and delays.
*   **Competitive Performance:** The experiments on real-world datasets show competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods, particularly for the Cadmium prediction task.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The introduction of numerous parameters (magnitude, delay, phase, spectral mixture components) might make model fitting and hyperparameter optimization challenging in high-dimensional settings.
*   **Computational Cost:** While not explicitly discussed, the increased complexity of the kernel is likely to result in a higher computational cost for training and prediction.
*   **Limited Statistical Significance:** The results on the climate data show no statistically significant improvement over CSM in many cases, and the authors acknowledge this limitation.
*   **Dependence on Spectral Assumptions:** The method relies on the assumption that the underlying processes are stationary, which may not hold in all real-world applications.
*   **Lack of Scalability Discussion:** The paper does not address scalability issues and does not discuss potential sparse approximations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP kernel design.
*   The MOSM kernel extends existing methods by incorporating delay parameters, increasing its modeling flexibility.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in specific application domains.
*   The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methodology and its theoretical foundations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The increased complexity of the MOSM kernel may limit its practical applicability in high-dimensional settings.
*   The lack of consistent statistical significance in the real-world experiments raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
*   The paper does not address scalability issues or discuss potential sparse approximations.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. While the increased complexity and limited statistical significance in some experiments are potential concerns, the theoretical soundness, expressiveness, and interpretability of the proposed MOSM kernel warrant acceptance. The authors clearly identify the strengths and limitations of their work. Addressing the scalability and computational cost concerns in future work would further enhance the impact of this research.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
