- Proposes MOSM: a new multi-output spectral mixture kernel for MOGPs designed directly in the spectral domain using Cramér's theorem.  
- Models cross-spectral density S(ω) as a sum of rank-one outer products of complex Gaussian vectors to ensure Hermitian positive-definiteness.  
- Inverse Fourier transform of S(ω) yields a valid covariance; hyperparameters map to magnitudes, frequencies, phase shifts, and inter-channel time delays.  
- Generalizes single-output SM and subsumes CSM as a constrained special case.  
- Validated on a synthetic task (function, derivative, delayed version) and two real-world datasets, showing MOSM can capture complex inter-channel relationships.  
- Theoretical development is sound, elegant, and well-motivated.  
- Hyperparameters are interpretable (especially delays/phase), aiding system identification in scientific applications.  
- Synthetic experiment is well-designed and convincingly demonstrates MOSM's superior expressiveness over LMC-like models.  
- Authors transparently report results and perform statistical tests.  
- MOSM has many hyperparameters (scales with outputs m, components Q, dimensions n), raising practical optimization challenges not addressed in the paper.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written with clear notation and logical structure; spectral-to-time interpretation is clearly explained.  
- Minor suggestions: explicitly show constraints reducing MOSM to CSM and briefly justify that sums of valid spectral density matrices remain valid.  
- Original and novel application of Cramér's theorem to multi-output spectral mixtures, enabling per-channel spectra, phase shifts, and time delays.  
- Significant potential impact for interpretability-focused domains (geophysics, econometrics, neuroscience); implementation in GPflow facilitates adoption.  
- On some real datasets, MOSM's performance gains over CSM are not always statistically significant, suggesting simpler models may suffice for some predictive tasks.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, high expressiveness/interpretability, generalization over prior methods, convincing synthetic validation, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical optimization concerns due to many hyperparameters and modest empirical gains in certain real-world cases.  
- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance.
