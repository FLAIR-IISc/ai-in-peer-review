This paper proposes a novel approach to constructing multi-output Gaussian Process (MOGP) kernels based on spectral mixture kernels and Cramér’s Theorem. The key idea is to design cross-spectral densities in the Fourier domain, allowing for flexible modeling of delays and phase differences between channels, offering a more interpretable alternative to existing methods like LMC and CSM. The authors provide theoretical justification, a synthetic validation, and comparisons on two real-world datasets.

The paper is well-motivated. MOGPs are important for a range of applications, and the limitations of existing approaches—particularly the lack of interpretability and difficulty in modeling channel relationships beyond simple linear combinations—are clearly articulated. The extension of the spectral mixture approach to the multivariate case via Cramér’s Theorem is a natural and principled idea. The theoretical grounding is solid, and the connection to Bochner’s Theorem for scalar GPs provides valuable context. The inclusion of delay and phase parameters is a significant improvement over existing methods, and the justification for doing so is convincing.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The theoretical framework is robust and well-explained, building upon established results. The proposed MOSM kernel is more expressive than existing methods, and the parametric interpretation of the kernel parameters (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay) is a major advantage. The synthetic experiment demonstrates the ability of the model to learn the desired relationships between channels. The comparisons on real-world datasets show competitive performance, and the ablation study (although limited to comparisons with existing methods) provides some insight into the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The writing is clear and concise, and the paper is well-organized. The use of TensorFlow and GPflow is commendable, promoting reproducibility.

However, there are also weaknesses. While the synthetic example showcases the model’s capabilities, it’s somewhat contrived and focused on very specific relationships (derivative and delay). More diverse synthetic scenarios would strengthen the validation. The experiments on real-world datasets don’t consistently demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing methods, and statistical significance is only claimed for one specific case.  The authors acknowledge this, but further analysis is needed to understand *why* the MOSM doesn’t consistently outperform the other models. The discussion section hints at potential avenues for future work (e.g., sparse implementation), but lacks a more detailed roadmap. The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to alternatives. The choice of Q=1 for the rank of the decomposition is somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from justification or exploration of different rank values. While the authors discuss the relationship to CSM, the limitations of CSM are not thoroughly explored - a deeper comparison highlighting exactly what MOSM *unlocks* that CSM cannot would be valuable. Furthermore, the connection to complex-valued GPs isn't fully fleshed out – simply stating the model results in a complex-valued GP doesn't fully explain how this is handled in practice or whether any specific considerations are required.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and principled approach to MOGP kernel design.
*   The theoretical framework is well-grounded and provides a clear justification for the proposed method.
*   The MOSM kernel is more expressive than existing methods and offers better interpretability.
*   The synthetic experiment demonstrates the ability to learn complex channel relationships.
*   The real-world experiments show competitive performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The real-world experiments don’t consistently demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing methods.
*   The synthetic example is somewhat contrived.
*   The computational complexity of the method is not discussed.
*   The choice of Q=1 is not adequately justified.
*   The connection to complex GPs requires further clarification.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of MOGPs. While the empirical results are not overwhelmingly conclusive, the theoretical framework, the novelty of the approach, and the potential for future work justify acceptance. I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions, focusing on: 1) Expanding the synthetic experiments to include more diverse scenarios. 2) Providing a more detailed analysis of the experimental results, including statistical significance testing and a discussion of the limitations of the MOSM model. 3) Discussing the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method. 4) Elucidating the implications of the complex-valued GP formulation. 5) Providing a stronger justification for the choice of Q=1 and exploring the effects of different rank values.
