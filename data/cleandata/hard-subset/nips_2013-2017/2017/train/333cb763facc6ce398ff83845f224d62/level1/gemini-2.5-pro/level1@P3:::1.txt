### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a new family of covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The authors take a principled approach, moving away from constructing kernels through linear combinations of latent processes (like LMC or convolution models) and instead designing them directly in the spectral domain. The key theoretical tool is Cramér's Theorem, the multivariate generalization of Bochner's Theorem.

The core idea is to define a parametric form for the matrix of cross-spectral densities, S(ω), ensuring it is Hermitian positive-definite by construction. This is achieved by modeling S(ω) as a sum of rank-one components, where each component is formed from an outer product of a vector of complex-valued Gaussian functions. Applying the inverse Fourier transform to this spectral density matrix yields a valid MOGP covariance function.

The resulting MOSM kernel is a mixture of components, where each component models the auto- and cross-covariances between channels. Crucially, the kernel's hyperparameters have a direct physical interpretation in terms of magnitudes, frequencies, phase shifts, and, most notably, time delays between the different output channels. This model generalizes the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel of Wilson & Adams (2013) and provides a more flexible and principled foundation than the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel of Ulrich et al. (2015), which this work shows to be a constrained special case. The authors validate their model on a synthetic task involving a function, its derivative, and a delayed version, and on two real-world datasets, demonstrating its ability to capture complex inter-channel relationships and achieve competitive or superior performance against existing MOGP models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical development is sound, elegant, and well-motivated. The use of Cramér's theorem to derive a flexible and interpretable kernel family is a significant contribution. The derivation from the proposed spectral density to the final covariance function is clear and correct. Claims are well-supported by the theory and the experiments. The synthetic experiment, in particular, is very well-designed; it creates a scenario with known derivative and delay relationships where models like LMC are expected to fail, providing a clean and compelling demonstration of MOSM's superior expressiveness. The authors are also commendably honest about their experimental results, performing statistical tests and noting when performance gains over baselines are not significant.
*   **Weaknesses:** The proposed MOSM kernel has a large number of hyperparameters (scaling with the number of outputs `m`, mixture components `Q`, and input dimensions `n`). This can make optimization challenging, potentially leading to issues with local minima or requiring careful initialization. The paper does not discuss these practical optimization challenges, which would be a valuable addition for practitioners seeking to apply the model.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

*   **Strengths:** The structure is logical, starting with a clear motivation and background before developing the proposed method step-by-step. The mathematical notation is consistent and the explanations are lucid. The connection between the parameterization in the spectral domain and the interpretable properties (delay, phase) in the time domain is made very clear. The experimental setup and results are presented transparently.
*   **Minor Suggestions:** While the relationship to CSM is clearly stated, explicitly showing the parameter constraints that reduce MOSM to CSM would further strengthen the connection for the reader. The transition from a rank-one model (Q=1) to the full mixture model is slightly abrupt; a brief sentence justifying that a sum of valid (positive-definite) spectral density matrices remains valid would complete the argument.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:** While the idea of using the Fourier domain to design kernels is established (e.g., the SM kernel), this paper is the first to provide a principled and general application of Cramér's theorem to construct an expressive and interpretable *multi-output* spectral mixture kernel. It moves beyond the constraints of LMC-based constructions used by prior work like CSM. The key novelty is the ability to jointly model distinct per-channel spectral properties, inter-channel phase shifts, and, crucially, inter-channel time delays (`θ_ij`). This latter capability, which arises naturally from the complex-valued spectral formulation, represents a significant advance over previous MOGP kernels.
*   **Weaknesses:** None to note. The work clearly builds upon, but is significantly different from, prior contributions.

**Significance:** The paper is highly significant and likely to have a strong impact.

*   **Strengths:** The MOSM kernel provides a powerful new tool for the MOGP toolkit. Its significance lies not just in predictive performance but in its interpretability. In many scientific and engineering applications (e.g., geophysics, econometrics, neuroscience), understanding the underlying system dynamics—such as lead/lag relationships, shared periodicities, and phase offsets between signals—is the primary goal. MOSM is uniquely suited for such discovery tasks. The compelling results on the synthetic derivative/delay problem showcase its potential for system identification. The provision of an implementation in the popular GPflow library will facilitate its adoption and encourage further research.
*   **Weaknesses:** The experiments on real-world data show that for some problems, the added flexibility of MOSM does not translate into statistically significant performance gains over the simpler CSM. While this does not diminish the theoretical contribution, it suggests that for some purely predictive tasks on highly correlated signals, simpler models may suffice.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**

1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a novel, principled, and elegant method for constructing MOGP kernels based on Cramér's theorem.
2.  **High Expressiveness and Interpretability:** The proposed MOSM kernel can model complex relationships including delays and phase shifts, with hyperparameters that have clear physical meaning.
3.  **Superiority over Existing Methods:** It generalizes prior spectral methods (SM, CSM) and overcomes key limitations of traditional LMC-based MOGPs.
4.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The synthetic experiment provides a clear and powerful demonstration of the model's unique capabilities. Performance on real-world tasks is strong.
5.  **Clarity and Quality of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

1.  **Practical Optimization Concerns:** The large number of hyperparameters may present optimization challenges in practice, a point that is not addressed in the paper.
2.  **Modest Empirical Gains on Some Datasets:** On the real-world datasets, the performance improvement over the state-of-the-art competitor (CSM) is not always statistically significant, although the authors are transparent about this.

### Overall recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on multi-output Gaussian processes. The theoretical development is strong, the resulting model is powerful and interpretable, and the experimental validation is convincing. The weaknesses are minor compared to the strengths of the contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
