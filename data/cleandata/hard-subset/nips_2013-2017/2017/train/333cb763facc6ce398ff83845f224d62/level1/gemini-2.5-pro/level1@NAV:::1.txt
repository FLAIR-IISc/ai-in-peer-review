This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel and expressive kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The work builds upon the well-established Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel for single-output GPs by Wilson & Adams (2013) and generalizes it to the multivariate case. The key theoretical contribution is a principled approach to designing the matrix-valued kernel in the frequency domain. By constructing a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and leveraging Cramér's Theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem), the authors ensure the resulting covariance matrix is positive definite. This construction allows the MOSM kernel to model not only magnitude and frequency but also explicit delays and phase shifts between output channels, making it more flexible than previous methods like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, which the paper shows to be a special case of MOSM.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a well-motivated and elegant theoretical extension of spectral mixture kernels to MOGPs. The use of Cramér’s Theorem to design kernels from a factor decomposition of the spectral density matrix is a strong and principled contribution. The ability to parametrically model inter-channel delays is a significant and novel capability that addresses a key limitation in existing spectral MOGP methods.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound, and the derivation is clear. The claims are well-supported by a carefully designed synthetic experiment that explicitly showcases the model's unique ability to learn derivative and delay relationships where latent process models like LMC would fail. This provides compelling evidence for the model's increased expressiveness.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides the necessary background on spectral methods and MOGPs, making the core contribution easy to follow for readers familiar with the area.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / areas for improvement):**
*   **Experimental Significance:** While the model performs competitively on the two real-world datasets, it does not consistently or significantly outperform the simpler CSM baseline. The authors are honest about this, but it tempers the practical impact demonstrated in the paper. The inclusion of a real-world problem where inter-channel delays are known to be a crucial feature would have made a more powerful case for the model's practical utility.
*   **Scalability and Optimization:** The paper does not discuss the challenges associated with optimizing the large number of hyperparameters that the MOSM model introduces, especially as the number of outputs and mixture components increases. A brief discussion of initialization strategies or optimization difficulties would make the paper more complete from a practical standpoint.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong theoretical contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in kernel design for MOGPs. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the novelty and significance of the proposed method.
