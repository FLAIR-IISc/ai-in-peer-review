- Introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel: construct MOGP covariance via parametric cross-spectral densities using Cramér's Theorem.  
- Ensures positive definiteness by factorizing S(ω)=R(ω)^H R(ω) and modeling R(ω) entries as sums of complex-valued squared-exponential functions.  
- Chooses this form because its inverse Fourier transform has a closed-form, yielding an interpretable, tractable covariance (Equation 11).  
- MOSM models channel-specific magnitudes, frequencies, lengthscales, pairwise phase shifts, and explicit time delays.  
- Generalizes single-output Spectral Mixture (Wilson & Adams 2013) to the multi-output case and is a principled alternative to LMC and convolution models.  
- Extends Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) by relaxing shared-parameter constraints and by explicitly modeling delays absent in CSM.  
- Validated on a synthetic dataset showing clear superiority in expressive tasks and evaluated on two real-world datasets where it is competitive or superior.  
- Technical quality: derivations are sound, the use of Cramér's Theorem is rigorous, and the expressiveness claims are well-supported.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, provides necessary background, and explains hyperparameter interpretations effectively.  
- Originality: novel parametric form for cross-spectral density enabling joint modeling of auto-covariances, phase shifts, and delays.  
- Significance: likely impactful for multivariate time-series/spatial tasks (neuroscience, finance, environmental science) that require modeling delays/phases.  
- Optimization concern: large number of hyperparameters (scales with m, n, Q) likely creates a highly multimodal NLL; practical optimization strategies are not discussed.  
- Model complexity vs. benefit: real-world gains over simpler CSM are modest and sometimes not significant, raising questions about practical necessity of MOSM’s full flexibility.  
- Suggestion: analyze learned parameters on real datasets to check if MOSM collapses to simpler structures (e.g., near-zero delays).  
- Scalability: inherits O(N^3) GP complexity; the paper lacks discussion of scalable approximations (e.g., variational Fourier features) despite spectral structure being exploitable.  
- Arguments for acceptance: principled/novel formulation, increased expressiveness, strong synthetic evidence, high significance, and excellent clarity.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient discussion of hyperparameter optimization and limited real-world performance improvements relative to model complexity.
