- Introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel: a parametric, expressive MOGP kernel designed in the spectral domain using Cramér's Theorem.  
- Proposes directly parametrizing the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) and ensuring Hermitian positive-definiteness via factorization S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω).  
- Models elements of R(ω) as mixtures of complex-valued squared-exponential functions and derives a closed-form MOSM kernel by inverse Fourier transform.  
- Generalizes Wilson & Adams' single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output setting and extends Ulrich et al.'s Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) approach.  
- MOSM naturally captures time delays, phase shifts, and channel-specific spectral properties with clear parametric interpretability.  
- Theoretical derivation is sound, elegant, and well-motivated, with correct use of spectral-domain principles and factor decomposition.  
- Synthetic experiments strongly support MOSM’s superiority, especially on tasks involving derivatives and delays that challenge LMC-style models.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized; derivations and figures (notably Figures 1 and 2) effectively convey intuition and results.  
- The approach is highly original, offering a principled framework for flexible, positive-definite cross-spectral density construction.  
- Significant contribution to MOGP literature, with practical relevance for domains involving latent delays and phase shifts.  
- Main weakness: MOSM introduces many hyperparameters (scales with Q*m), and the paper lacks discussion of optimization challenges, initialization, and local minima.  
- Secondary weakness: Real-world results show only modest and sometimes non-significant gains over CSM; benefits may be limited when outputs are highly correlated.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel principled framework, unique ability to model interpretable delays, high technical quality and clarity, and compelling synthetic demonstration.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical hyperparameter optimization issues are not addressed; real-world performance gains are inconsistent.  
- Conclusion: Strengths outweigh weaknesses; the paper is a high-quality, original, and significant advance warranting acceptance.
