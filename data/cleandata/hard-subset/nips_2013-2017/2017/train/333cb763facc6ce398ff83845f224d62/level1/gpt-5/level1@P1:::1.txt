Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Instead of constructing cross-covariances via linear combinations of latent processes (as in LMC/CONV), the authors design complex-valued cross-spectral densities and invoke Cramér’s theorem (multivariate Bochner) to guarantee positive definiteness via an explicit spectral factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω). Each column Ri(ω) is modeled as a complex Gaussian in frequency with amplitude wi, mean μi, diagonal covariance Σi, and a complex exponential encoding a per-output phase φi and delay θi. After symmetrization to ensure real-valued processes, the inverse Fourier transform yields closed-form cross-covariances:
kij(τ) = Σq α(q)ij exp(−0.5(τ + θ(q)ij)⊤ Σ(q)ij (τ + θ(q)ij)) cos((τ + θ(q)ij)⊤ μ(q)ij + φ(q)ij),
with per-pair parameters derived from per-output spectral parameters. As special cases, MOSM reduces to SM-LMC and to the Cross Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015) when parameters are constrained. Compared to CSM, MOSM additionally models inter-channel delays alongside phase and allows different auto-covariances per channel.

Experiments include: (i) a synthetic three-output signal (reference GP draw, its derivative, and a delayed version), showing MOSM uniquely recovers both derivative and delay relationships and extrapolates well; (ii) climate sensor imputation where MOSM performs comparably to CSM and better than SM-LMC/CONV in some cases; and (iii) the Jura heavy-metal dataset where MOSM improves over baselines for Cadmium and is competitive for Copper. Implementation uses GPflow/TensorFlow.

Quality
- Theory: The construction via Cramér’s theorem and spectral factorization is principled; symmetrization ensures real-valued outputs. The inverse Fourier transform is closed-form, yielding interpretable parameters (magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay). One notational issue: after introducing Sij(ω) = R:i(ω)H R:j(ω), the rank-1 case later writes Sij = Ri Rj without complex conjugation; it should be Sij = R̄i Rj to maintain Hermitian structure. Clarifying this would improve rigor.
- Correctness: Positive definiteness follows directly from S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω). The formulas for μij, Σij, and weights derived from products of complex Gaussians are standard and reduce correctly for i=j. The real-valued requirement is handled by spectral symmetrization.
- Empirical: The synthetic study is well-chosen to expose phase/delay advantages. Real-data evaluations are modest in size but include appropriate baselines (CONV, SM-LMC, CSM) and report MAE with some statistical testing. However, log predictive densities and training time/complexity are not reported, and significance tests are incomplete for comparisons involving CONV (due to borrowing results).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The background on Bochner/Cramér is succinct and helpful. Derivations are readable, and the spectral-to-time-domain parameter mapping is explicit and interpretable. Minor issues: the complex conjugation typo noted above; a few typographical errors; brief discussion on initialization and optimization would help reproducibility.

Originality
The work offers a novel and elegant extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting via complex-valued cross-spectral densities, enabling simultaneous modeling of phase and delay—beyond CSM’s phase-only treatment—and allowing channel-specific auto-spectra. The spectral factorization perspective for MOGPs is compelling and distinct from earlier LMC/CONV formulations. To my knowledge, prior uses of Cramér’s theorem for MOGPs did not cover complex cross-spectra with closed-form time-domain kernels and joint phase-delay interpretation.

Significance
The kernel is a useful addition to the MOGP toolbox: it is interpretable, flexible, and strictly generalizes SM-LMC/CSM. It should appeal to practitioners modeling multi-sensor signals with lags and phase shifts (e.g., climate, neuroscience, audio). While the experimental gains on real data are moderate, the synthetic results convincingly demonstrate capabilities that latent-process models struggle to capture. Lack of scalability currently limits impact for large datasets; nonetheless, the approach could integrate with inducing-variable/variational methods in the spectral domain.

Strengths
- Principled construction ensuring positive definiteness with a clear spectral interpretation.
- Ability to model both inter-channel phase and delay, with closed-form covariances.
- Generalizes prior SM-based MOGP kernels (SM-LMC, CSM) and allows channel-specific auto-covariances.
- Strong synthetic demonstration; competitive real-world performance.
- Implementation in a modern GP framework.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scalability: No sparse/variational implementation; complexity remains cubic in total observations. Suggest demonstrating compatibility with inducing-variable methods (e.g., Alvarez et al. 2010; Hensman et al. 2016; Tobar et al. 2015 inter-domain constructions).
- Parameterization/identifiability: Many parameters per output and component (μi, Σi, θi, φi, wi). Discuss initialization (e.g., per-channel SM fits, cross-correlation-derived delays), identifiability between θ and φ, and optimization stability.
- Empirics: Include log predictive density and computational costs; provide ablations (phase-only vs phase+delay; shared vs per-channel auto-spectra); report parameter counts to contextualize fairness vs baselines.
- Notation and minor errors: Fix the complex conjugation in Sij, clarify the symmetrization step, and clean typos.
- Scope: Stationary-only modeling; discuss extensions to nonstationary multi-output spectral kernels.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to Q and to initialization of θ and φ?
- Can θ capture long delays robustly without confusing with low-frequency phase offsets?
- Does increasing spectral rank Q in S(ω) meaningfully expand cross-output dependency structure beyond low-rank-in-frequency constraints?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, elegant, and general theoretical contribution with practical interpretability.
- Extends state of the art beyond CSM by modeling delays.
- Competitive results and a compelling synthetic showcase.
- Relevant to several NIPS topics (kernel methods, probabilistic modeling, time series).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scalability and empirical breadth; modest gains on real datasets.
- Minor theoretical/notation clarity issues.
- Limited discussion of optimization/initialization and identifiability.

Overall recommendation
I lean towards acceptance. The methodological contribution is solid and broadly useful, with clear connections to prior NIPS work on spectral kernels and MOGPs. Addressing the noted clarity and empirical evaluation points would further strengthen the paper.
