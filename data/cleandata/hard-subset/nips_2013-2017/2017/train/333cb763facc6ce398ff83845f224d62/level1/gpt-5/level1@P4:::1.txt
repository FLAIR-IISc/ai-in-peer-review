Summary
This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Building on the spectral construction of kernels via Bochner’s theorem for scalar GPs and its multivariate extension (Cramér’s theorem), the authors parameterize a family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities Sij(ω) that, when inverse-Fourier transformed, yield closed-form, stationary cross-covariances with interpretable parameters: frequency content (μij), lengthscales (Σij), magnitude (αij), phase (φij), and, crucially, input delays (θij) between channels. The construction enforces positive semidefiniteness in the spectral domain by factorizing the cross-spectral density matrix as S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω). A symmetrization step ensures real-valued processes. The resulting time-domain kernel is a sum over Q components of envelopes times cosines applied to shifted lags (τ + θ(q)ij), which generalizes Wilson and Adams’ SM kernel to the multi-output setting and subsumes SM-LMC and CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015) as special cases.

Experiments include: (i) a synthetic 3-output task (a function, its derivative, and a delayed version) to assess recovery of known auto- and cross-covariances; (ii) climate sensor imputation with simulated missing data; and (iii) spatial heavy metal prediction in the Jura dataset. MOSM performs best on the synthetic and Cadmium tasks and is competitive on the others, with some statistically significant gains over baselines.

Quality
- Theory: The core construction—designing S(ω) so that S(ω) ≽ 0 for all ω via S = RHR and then taking inverse Fourier transforms—is principled and standard under Cramér’s theorem. The use of complex-valued SE factors yields closed-form cross-covariances with delay and phase. The symmetrization S(ω) ← 0.5(S(ω) + S(−ω)) preserves positive semidefiniteness (sum/scalar multiple of PSD matrices) and ensures real-valued kernels.
- Notational correctness: There is a potential inconsistency. After introducing S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), the text states, for Q=1, Sij(ω) = Ri(ω)Rj(ω) (eq. 9), apparently dropping the conjugation on Ri. For Hermitian S and condition (6), entries should be Sij(ω) = overline{Ri(ω)} Rj(ω). The subsequent properties (e.g., Sij = overline{Sji}) and the derivation of θij, φij from channel-wise parameters depend on this. Please clarify and correct eq. (9) and the definitions of θij and φij accordingly.
- Real-valued processes: For real multivariate stationary processes, a standard requirement is Sij(−ω) = overline{Sij(ω)}. The proposed symmetrization and paired Gaussian terms produce real time-domain kernels, but it would help to explicitly verify that the constructed S satisfies the Hermitian symmetry in ω (not just at fixed ω) or to state clearly that the realness is ensured via the paired construction that yields the cos term in eq. (10).
- Positive definiteness in time domain: Since the kernel is obtained by inverse FT of an S(ω) that is PSD for all ω, the time-domain multivariate kernel is PSD. This point is clear and correct.
- Empirical evaluation: The synthetic task convincingly highlights MOSM’s unique capability to simultaneously model derivative and delay cross-covariances that are not linear combinations of univariate kernels. The real-data comparisons are fair (CONV, SM-LMC, CSM), and statistical tests are used where possible. However:
  - Only MAE is reported; test log-likelihood and uncertainty calibration would strengthen claims of probabilistic soundness.
  - No ablation isolating the impact of delay and phase (e.g., MOSM without θ, without φ) is provided.
  - Scalability and runtime are not reported; parameter count grows with m and Q.
  - Initialization and identifiability (trade-offs among μ, θ, φ) are not discussed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear progression from spectral theory to construction to experiments. The interpretability of parameters is explained well. Some issues:
- Fix the conjugation inconsistency noted above and carefully restate Hermitian properties of S.
- Minor typos and formatting (e.g., “integrable1” footnote, “multiouput”, “st Conference”).
- Explicitly restate the Hermitian symmetry condition Sij(−ω) = overline{Sij(ω)} for real processes and how the construction satisfies it.
- Provide a brief complexity and parameter-count analysis for MOSM vs. SM-LMC/CSM.

Originality
The work extends the spectral mixture approach to MOGPs using complex cross-spectral densities that allow both phase and explicit delays across outputs, with per-channel spectral parameters that induce pairwise cross-spectra via a low-rank factorization in the frequency domain. While related to CSM (phase) and SM-LMC, the introduction of delays and a principled Cramér-based construction with per-channel parameters is a meaningful and nontrivial generalization. To my knowledge, explicitly modeling delays through cross-spectral parameterization in MOGPs with closed-form kernels is novel.

Significance
The ability to jointly model phase and delays in cross-covariances is valuable for multi-sensor time series, geostatistics, and other domains where channel misalignment is common. The synthetic experiment is compelling; real-data gains are moderate but consistent in some settings. The approach is interpretable and may be adopted by practitioners working with MOGPs. Wider impact would be strengthened by scalability (sparse/inducing constructions), more diverse datasets with known cross-channel delays, and uncertainty-based metrics.

Pros
- Principled spectral-domain construction guaranteeing PSD via Cramér’s theorem.
- Interpretable parameters (frequency, lengthscale, magnitude, phase, delay).
- Strict generalization of SM-LMC and CSM; added expressivity with delays.
- Closed-form kernel allowing standard GP training.
- Convincing synthetic validation; competitive real-data results with some significant gains.

Cons
- Notational/technical ambiguity around complex conjugation and Hermitian symmetry.
- Limited evaluation metrics (no NLL/calibration), no runtime or scalability analysis.
- Lack of ablations isolating contributions of θ and φ.
- Potential identifiability issues and sensitivity to initialization not discussed.
- Improvements over strong baselines on real data are modest in places.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the conjugation in eq. (9) and explicitly verify Sij(−ω) = overline{Sij(ω)}.
- Report test log-likelihood and calibration metrics; include ablations (no-delay, no-phase).
- Provide parameter counts, runtime, and scalability comparisons; discuss initialization.
- Add an example with known cross-channel delays (e.g., shifted audio/EEG) to highlight interpretability by recovering θij.
- Briefly discuss extending to sparse/inducing approximations and multi-dimensional inputs n > 1 in practice.

Overall, this is a technically sound and interpretable extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output case with a clear theoretical foundation and practical appeal.
