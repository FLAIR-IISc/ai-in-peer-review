This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Building on Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s), the authors specify complex-valued cross-spectral densities via a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), then obtain closed-form, real-valued cross-covariances by symmetrizing the spectrum and inverse Fourier transforming. The resulting kernel generalizes spectral mixture (SM) kernels (Wilson & Adams, 2013) to the multivariate case and strictly subsumes SM-LMC and CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015): it provides per-pair parameters, allows phase shifts, and crucially models inter-channel delays. Experiments compare against LMC- and convolution-based MOGPs (Álvarez & Lawrence, NIPS 2008) on synthetic derivatives/delays, climate imputation, and Jura geostatistics, showing clear gains on the synthetic and cadmium tasks and competitive results elsewhere.

Quality: The construction is technically sound: positive definiteness is guaranteed by the spectral factorization, and real-valuedness by spectral symmetry. The inverse transforms yield closed forms with interpretable parameters (magnitude, frequency, phase, delay). Training is standard marginal likelihood optimization. However, the paper would benefit from discussion of identifiability (phase vs delay, per-channel means vs cross-pair means), initialization strategies, and the impact of restricting each spectral component to rank-one in S(ω). Scalability remains O(N^3) with full GPs; sparse/variational remedies are deferred to future work.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized, with precise statements of Bochner/Cramér and a careful derivation. Some notation could be streamlined (e.g., repeated super/subscripts), and a schematic linking per-channel parameters {µi, Σi, θi, φi} to induced cross-pair parameters would aid intuition.

Originality: The complex cross-spectral factorization with explicit delay parameters is a substantive and well-motivated generalization beyond SM-LMC/CSM and beyond real-valued multivariate Matérn families (Gneiting et al., 2010).

Significance: The ability to jointly model phase and delay with interpretable parameters is valuable for multi-sensor time series and spatial-temporal fusion. Empirical gains are compelling on synthetic and one real dataset, but broader benchmarks and ablations (e.g., removing delay/phase) would strengthen the case.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Principled, general construction with guaranteed PSD and clear interpretability.
- Strict generalization of prior spectral MOGP kernels (SM-LMC, CSM).
- Closed-form kernels enabling straightforward GP inference.
- Demonstrated benefits on derivative/delay recovery and cadmium prediction.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited empirical breadth and modest gains on some real tasks; only MAE reported.
- No scalable inference; feasibility for large N or many outputs untested.
- Identifiability and initialization not thoroughly addressed.
- Comparison to multivariate Matérn and other non-spectral MOGP baselines could be expanded.
