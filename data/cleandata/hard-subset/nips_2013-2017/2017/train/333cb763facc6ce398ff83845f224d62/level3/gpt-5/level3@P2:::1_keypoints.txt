- Proposes MOSM: a principled spectral-domain construction of multi-output GP kernels using Cramér’s theorem and factorization S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω).  
- Parameterizes each column Ri(ω) as complex-valued squared-exponential spectral components, enforcing Hermitian symmetry for real-valued processes.  
- Derives closed-form time-domain cross-covariances that include interpretable magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay parameters.  
- Auto-covariances reduce to standard spectral mixture (SM) kernels; MOSM subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases.  
- Provides a general recipe guaranteeing positive definiteness via low-rank frequency-domain factorization.  
- Theoretical contribution: clean, general construction of valid MOGP kernels with closed-form families including delay and phase.  
- Empirical contribution: synthetic experiment showing recovery of derivative and delayed channels where latent-process models struggle.  
- Empirical contribution: competitive results on two real datasets (climate sensor failure and Jura heavy metals), best on some tasks (e.g., Cadmium).  
- Strength: principled extension of SM to multi-output with full parametric interpretability and clear derivation.  
- Strength: closed-form kernel enables standard GP training and prediction and explains prior models as restricted cases.  
- Weakness: model is stationary; lacks discussion or demonstration of nonstationary extensions.  
- Weakness: potential overparameterization and identifiability issues with many per-output/per-component hyperparameters; needs discussion of initialization, regularization, and tying.  
- Weakness: unclear benefits of higher rank in S(ω); experiments mostly use Q=5 and rank-1 per component.  
- Weakness: real-data evaluation uses MAE only; should report NLL and calibration diagnostics to assess uncertainty quality.  
- Weakness: missing computational analysis — runtimes, scaling with m and N, conditioning, and sparse/variational extensions.  
- Weakness: real-data interpretability could be improved by visualizing learned cross-spectral parameters (delays/phases).  
- Quality: derivations appear sound and grounded in spectral theory; construction ensures PD and real-valuedness.  
- Clarity: paper is well organized and clearly written with careful connections to prior work; minor fixes suggested (parameter counts, optimization tips, typos).  
- Originality: novel in enabling complex cross-spectra with both phase and delay in a principled MOGP framework.  
- Significance: useful for practitioners needing interpretable cross-channel structure; framework is general though empirical gains are sometimes modest.  
- Key questions for authors: sensitivity to initialization, observed identifiability issues (delay vs phase, μ vs θ), empirical benefit of higher rank, training time and NLL comparisons, and visualization of learned delays/phases.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled, interpretable, theoretically grounded, and unifies prior spectral MOGP models.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited empirical breadth/diagnostics, no scalability experiments, limited discussion of identifiability and optimization, stationarity restriction not addressed empirically.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — valuable theoretical contribution that needs clarifications on initialization, identifiability, and computational aspects.
