This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a new covariance family for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) built by designing complex cross-spectral densities in the Fourier domain and invoking Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analog of Bochner’s theorem) to guarantee positive definiteness. The construction factors the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) as R(ω)H R(ω), with each channel endowed with its own Gaussian spectral factor. After symmetrization to ensure a real-valued process, the inverse Fourier transform yields closed-form cross-covariances that include: (i) frequency content and magnitudes, (ii) channel-specific lengthscales, and critically (iii) interpretable phase shifts and explicit delays between channels. This strictly generalizes prior spectral multi-output kernels based on LMC/CSM: SM-LMC is recovered by tying parameters across channels, and CSM is a restricted case without delays and with shared spectral parameters. Empirically, MOSM is validated on a synthetic task involving derivative and delayed signals, and on two real datasets (climate sensors and Jura heavy metals), showing competitive or improved accuracy with statistical tests where applicable.

Quality: The theoretical development is sound and well-justified. Positive definiteness follows directly from the spectral factorization and Cramér’s theorem; the resulting time-domain kernel is in closed form, enabling exact GP inference (modulo cubic scaling). The interpretability of parameters (µ, Σ, φ, θ) is clear, and the link between channel-wise spectral factors and cross-covariances is explicit. Experiments are sensible: the synthetic task is well chosen to reveal phase/delay benefits; real-world datasets reflect common multi-sensor/multivariate settings. However, the paper provides limited detail on optimization: initialization, constraints (e.g., ensuring identifiability of phase versus delay), learning dynamics, and failure modes are not discussed. Since spectral mixtures are known to be sensitive to initialization and local optima, more guidance and diagnostics would strengthen the work. Runtime and scaling comparisons are also missing.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The spectral construction is explained carefully, and the interpretability of parameters is communicated effectively via equations and figures. Minor edits would help: explicitly summarize training hyperparameters (e.g., priors, parameterizations, initialization from periodograms), and make the number of training points prominent in captions (especially for the synthetic example). Typos: a word appears to be missing around lines 74 and 140.

Originality: The kernel design is a nontrivial and novel extension of spectral mixture ideas to multi-output settings. In contrast to SM-LMC and CSM, MOSM allows channel-specific spectra and introduces interpretable delays in addition to phase, while preserving closed forms and positive definiteness via a principled spectral factorization. The relation to prior multivariate spectral constructions (e.g., Gneiting et al.’s Matérn cross-covariances and Ulrich et al.’s CSM) is appropriately discussed, and the strict generality over SM-LMC/CSM is clarified.

Significance: The ability to capture delays and phase differences with interpretable parameters is valuable for multi-sensor time series, spatial-temporal applications, and scientific modeling. The synthetic experiment highlights capabilities not available to latent-process-based models; real-data results are competitive, with statistically significant improvements in some settings. If optimization proves robust in practice, this kernel could become a standard tool for MOGPs, and the spectral construction opens doors to scalable inducing schemes.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Principled and general construction grounded in Cramér’s theorem, with closed-form kernels.
- Interpretable modeling of delay and phase across channels; strictly more expressive than SM-LMC/CSM.
- Strong synthetic validation where other models struggle; competitive real-data performance with significance testing.
- Clear relation to and unification of prior spectral MOGP approaches.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited discussion of training: initialization strategies, identifiability (phase vs delay), and optimization stability.
- No runtime/scaling analysis; lack of sparse/variational approximation in experiments.
- Real-world gains, while competitive, are modest in some cases; ablations on rank Q and parameter tying are limited.

Questions for the authors:
- How does MOSM behave with few training points per channel? Does the increased parameterization (per-channel spectra, phase, delay) risk overfitting, and do you recommend priors/regularization?
- For channels with very different lengthscales, Σij = 2 Σi(Σi+Σj)−1Σj implies cross-covariances narrower than both channels. Does this limit modeling when a long-scale channel correlates with a short-scale one? Any empirical guidance?
- How sensitive is training to initialization of µ, Σ, φ, θ? Do you use per-channel periodograms to initialize µ/Σ and cross-periodograms for θ/φ? Any annealing/constraint strategies to reduce local optima?
- What is the impact of increasing rank Q beyond 1 on performance and optimization? Are there settings where tying some parameters across channels improves robustness?
- Can you comment on computational cost versus SM-LMC/CSM and on prospects for sparse/variational MOSM (e.g., inter-domain Fourier features)?

Suggestions:
- Add an optimization section detailing parameterizations, constraints (e.g., reparameterize delays/phases to avoid redundancy when µ≈0), priors, and initialization heuristics.
- Provide ablations on Q and parameter tying, and a brief runtime/memory comparison.
- Consider a small-sample experiment to assess robustness and overfitting control.
- Make training set sizes explicit in figure captions; include standard deviations where possible.

Minor comments:
- Clarify the symmetry step for real-valued processes (Sij(ω) ← 0.5[Sij(ω)+Sij(−ω)]) and its effect on parameters.
- Fix minor typos; a word seems missing around lines 74 and 140.

Overall assessment: A technically solid and elegant generalization of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output settings, with clear interpretability and demonstrated benefits. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved discussion of optimization and practical training guidance.
