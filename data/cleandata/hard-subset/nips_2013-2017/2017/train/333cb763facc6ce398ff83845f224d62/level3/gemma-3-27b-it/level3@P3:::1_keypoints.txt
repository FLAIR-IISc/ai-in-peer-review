- Proposes MOSM: a novel multi-output spectral mixture kernel using complex-valued cross-spectral densities to model delays and phase differences.  
- Leverages Cramér’s Theorem (and Bochner’s) to construct a theoretically grounded multivariate covariance function.  
- Introduces delay and phase parameters directly in the spectral density for increased expressiveness and interpretability.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly structured, with a concise GP and spectral mixture background.  
- Derivation of MOSM is logical, well-explained, and builds on established theoretical foundations.  
- Strong originality: extends spectral mixture approach to multi-output setting in a novel way.  
- Theoretical soundness: principled justification via Cramér’s Theorem.  
- Parameters map cleanly to physical concepts (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay), aiding interpretability.  
- Synthetic experiments convincingly show recovery of known auto- and cross-covariances, including delays.  
- Real-world experiments are weak: climate data shows little differentiation across models likely due to high inter-signal correlation.  
- Jura dataset results are promising (MOSM outperforms on Cadmium) but hard to interpret without geological insight.  
- Experimental evaluation lacks statistical rigor (limited significance testing) and uses only MAE, offering insufficient insight into how MOSM works.  
- Would benefit from visualizations of predictions and learned covariance functions and deeper analysis of learned parameters.  
- Computational cost concern: spectral mixture approaches may be expensive with many mixture components and should be acknowledged/addressed.  
- Overall: valuable, theoretically solid contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen experimental validation and statistical analysis.
