Summary
This paper proposes a new family of covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), derived by specifying complex-valued cross-spectral densities and invoking Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem) to obtain valid cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform. The construction starts from a low-rank (rank-Q) factorization of the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), with each column Ri(ω) modeled as a complex squared-exponential in frequency with per-output parameters: magnitude, center frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay. Enforcing symmetry in ω yields real-valued GPs. The resulting Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel generalizes Wilson & Adams’ SM kernel to the multi-output setting and strictly contains the SM-LMC and CSM kernels: it recovers their behavior when per-output parameters are tied, but additionally supports channel-wise differences in spectral content and explicit delays in addition to phase shifts. Hence, MOSM offers a parametric and interpretable description of cross-channel relations (magnitude, frequency, phase, delay).

Related work is carefully positioned: LMC/CONV (Álvarez & Lawrence), SM (Wilson & Adams, ICML 2013), CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015), and multivariate Matérn constructions. Empirically, the paper evaluates MOSM on a trivariate synthetic example (learning derivatives and delays), a four-station climate dataset with simulated sensor failures, and the Swiss Jura heavy-metal dataset. MOSM matches or outperforms baselines, with the clearest gains on the synthetic task and the Cadmium task in Jura.

Quality
- Theory: The construction is principled. Positive semidefiniteness follows from the spectral factorization and the per-frequency PSD matrix being Hermitian PSD; the symmetrization to obtain real-valued processes is standard and maintains PSD. The inverse Fourier transform is tractable due to the Gaussian functional forms, leading to closed-form cross-covariances with interpretable parameters (αij, μij, Σij, φij, θij). This is technically sound.
- Empirics: The synthetic task convincingly demonstrates learning of cross-covariances not expressible as linear combinations of univariate kernels, including simultaneous derivative and delayed relationships. On real data, MOSM performs competitively. However:
  - The extrapolation ability that made SM compelling in the single-output case is not clearly demonstrated in multi-output settings beyond the synthetic example; the climate experiment primarily imputes missing segments among highly correlated channels and shows parity with CSM.
  - Statistical significance is only partially addressed (e.g., KS tests on selected cases). Given the overlapping error bars, a more systematic treatment (paired tests across runs and tasks) would strengthen claims.
  - Practical aspects (runtime, parameter counts as a function of m, Q, and dimensionality, optimization stability/initialization) are not discussed but matter for adoption.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The connection from Cramér’s theorem to the proposed kernel is clear, and the parameter interpretation is helpful.
- Suggestions:
  - Provide a brief derivation sketch from the rank-one spectral factors Ri(ω) to the closed-form time-domain kernel, including the role of the symmetrization step and how it affects αij.
  - Summarize parameter counts and constraints (e.g., how wij are determined from wi and guarantee PSD; whether phases and delays are identifiable up to symmetries).
  - Minor nits: fix typos (e.g., “multiouput”), ensure consistent notation for Σij and dimensionality of θij.

Originality
- The idea of constructing multi-output kernels via complex-valued cross-spectral densities with per-output parameters and explicit delay is a meaningful extension over CSM/SM-LMC. Allowing each output to have distinct μi, Σi, and introducing θi (delays) while ensuring PSD via spectral factorization appears novel and useful. The connection to prior NIPS work (e.g., CSM) is made and the strict generalization is clear.

Significance
- Conceptual significance is solid: MOSM broadens the design space for interpretable MOGP kernels and unifies several prior constructions. Practically, the empirical gains are modest on standard benchmarks; the strongest evidence is on the synthetic task and one real task (Cadmium). The paper would have higher impact if it showed clear wins in scenarios where modeling delay/phase is essential (e.g., multi-sensor forecasting with known propagation delays, multivariate EEG/MEG with traveling waves), and stronger multi-output extrapolation.

Pros
- Principled, general, and interpretable MOGP kernel derived from spectral factorization and Cramér’s theorem.
- Strict generalization of SM-LMC and CSM, adding explicit delays and per-output spectral parameters.
- Closed-form kernels enable straightforward GP inference and gradient-based learning.
- Convincing synthetic experiment demonstrating capabilities that latent-LMC models cannot capture.

Cons
- Limited demonstration of multi-output extrapolation advantages; real-data improvements are small on common benchmarks.
- Partial statistical analysis; unclear significance across all comparisons.
- Scalability and optimization details (parameter counts, initialization, runtime) are not explored.
- Potential overparameterization with m×Q per-output parameters could cause identifiability or optimization issues; ablations are missing.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you add a forecasting experiment that stresses long-range extrapolation with multiple outputs (e.g., where cross-channel delays/phase are critical)?
- Please report training time, memory, and parameter counts vs. m and Q, and compare to SM-LMC/CSM.
- How do you initialize μi, Σi, φi, θi? Do you use per-output periodograms/cross-periodograms? Any priors or constraints to aid identifiability?
- Could you provide an ablation tying subsets of parameters (e.g., tying μi or θi across outputs) to show where the gains come from?
- Consider a more comprehensive significance analysis (paired tests over multiple random seeds) and include confidence intervals on all metrics.
- Discuss potential sparse/inducing approximations tailored to the spectral construction (e.g., variational Fourier features) to improve scalability.

Overall assessment
A technically sound and elegant generalization of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting with interpretable parameters and the novel ability to jointly model phase and delay. The main limitation is empirical: on standard datasets MOSM is often on par with CSM, with few clear wins. Strengthening the empirical section with tasks that require delay/phase modeling and more rigorous significance analysis would considerably improve the paper’s impact. I lean positive on acceptance for the conceptual contribution, contingent on improved experiments and analysis.
