## Review of "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

This paper introduces a novel covariance structure for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs) based on extending the spectral (Bochner) approach. The key contribution lies in proposing a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and leveraging Cramér’s Theorem to construct interpretable covariance functions that model delays and phase differences between outputs. This is a valuable addition to the field, as defining effective and interpretable covariance structures for MOGPs remains a significant challenge with limited existing methodologies. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, effectively building intuition from single-output GPs and the spectral mixture kernel to the more complex multi-output setting. The illustrations and choices of parameters are relevant and help to understand the method's capabilities. The theoretical grounding via Cramér’s Theorem is solid, and the experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The synthetic example specifically highlights the ability to learn derivatives and delayed signals, showcasing a strength not easily captured by simpler models. 

However, a key weakness lies in the limited detail provided regarding parameter learning. While the authors mention maximizing the log-likelihood, there is a lack of discussion about the optimization challenges potentially faced in this process, particularly with the increased complexity of the kernel. This is a valid concern, as complex kernel functions can often lead to difficult optimization landscapes.  If parameter learning proves to be non-problematic in practice, this method indeed has the potential to become a standard approach for MOGPs.

I have two questions for the authors. First, how does the method perform when the number of training points is limited? The reported results utilize a reasonable number of samples, but the behaviour with scarce data is crucial for real-world applicability. Second, how does the model handle correlations between channels exhibiting drastically different length-scales? The proposed kernel appears to assume a certain degree of scale alignment, and understanding its behaviour when this assumption is violated would be beneficial. 

Finally, a minor comment: the paper mentions a missing word on lines 74 and 140 which should be corrected.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP covariance function design.
*   Addresses a challenging problem with limited existing solutions.
*   Clearly written and well-motivated.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on multiple datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of parameter learning challenges and potential optimization difficulties.
*   Lack of analysis regarding performance with limited data or varying length scales.



Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of MOGPs. Addressing the concerns regarding parameter learning and further investigating the method’s behaviour under different data conditions would greatly strengthen the contribution. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on a more thorough discussion of these points.
