This paper proposes a multi-output spectral mixture (MOSM) kernel that designs cross-covariances in the spectral domain using Cramér’s theorem, extending the single-output spectral mixture approach of Wilson & Adams (2013) to MOGPs. By parameterizing complex cross-spectral densities and enforcing Hermitian positive definiteness via a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), the authors derive closed-form, real-valued cross-covariances after symmetrization, with interpretable parameters (magnitude, frequency, phase, delay) per output pair. The construction strictly generalizes LMC/SM-LMC and CSM (Ulrich et al., 2015), enabling cross-covariances that are not themselves positive-definite, and adds explicit delay modeling beyond phase shifts. Experiments include a synthetic tri-variate example with derivatives and delays, climate imputation, and the Jura geostatistics dataset.

Quality: The theoretical development is technically sound and clearly grounded in Cramér’s theorem. The rank-one-per-component factorization gives a principled PSD parameterization and yields tractable kernels. Claims about expressivity (phase+delay, non-symmetric cross-covariances) are supported by the formulation and a well-chosen synthetic task where latent-process models underperform. Real-data results are competitive; however, experimental analysis provides limited intuition about what is learned (e.g., recovered delays/phases), and the Jura results are hard to interpret.

Clarity: The paper is well written, with an accessible introduction to GP and spectral fundamentals. The link to prior work (LMC, CONV, SM-LMC, CSM) is accurate and positions novelty clearly.

Originality: The move from real-valued spectral designs to complex, factorized cross-spectra with explicit phase and delay in a multi-output setting is a meaningful advance beyond CSM, which MOSM strictly generalizes.

Significance: The formulation offers a useful, interpretable tool for multi-sensor modeling where delays and phase shifts matter (e.g., climate, neuroscience, seismology). Broader impact will hinge on scalability and guidance for setting the rank and number of components.

Suggestions:
- Report learned phase/delay parameters with uncertainty, and include ablations on identifiability and robustness to initialization.
- Discuss computational complexity and provide a small-scale sparse variant or runtime/memory benchmarks.
- Explore higher PSD rank (Q > 1) and its effect on accuracy and interpretability.
- Provide more intuitive Jura analysis (e.g., spatial maps, spectra).

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled, novel extension with interpretable parameters
- Clear theory; closed-form kernels
- Strong synthetic validation; competitive real-data results

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited analysis of learned structures and scalability
- Rank/initialization/identifiability not thoroughly explored
- Some experiments (Jura) lack intuitive takeaways
