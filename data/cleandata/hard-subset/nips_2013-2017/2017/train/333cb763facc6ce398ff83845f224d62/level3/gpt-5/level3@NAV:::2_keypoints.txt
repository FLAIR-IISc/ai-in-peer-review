- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel using complex cross-PSDs and Cramér’s theorem to guarantee positive definiteness and derive closed-form cross-covariances.  
- Factorizes S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) to yield interpretable per-pair parameters (magnitude, frequency, phase, delay) and strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM.  
- Naturally extends Wilson & Adams’ single-output spectral mixture idea to the multi-output setting with an elegant construction.  
- Experiments on a synthetic tri-variate signal, a four-station weather dataset, and the Swiss Jura metal-concentration dataset.  
- Theoretical development is sound, with a clean sufficient condition for positive definiteness and closed-form, interpretable kernels.  
- Empirical results are competitive with SM-LMC/CSM/CONV but lack strong statistical support and significance testing.  
- Missing ablation studies (rank Q, number of components, initialization sensitivity) and no scalability analysis.  
- Presentation is overall clear but dense; Section 3 should be streamlined and some derivations moved to an appendix.  
- Needs clarification on symmetrization for real-valued processes and practical guidance on initialization and hyperparameter constraints.  
- Reproducibility would benefit from more training details and code release.  
- Originality is moderate but meaningful: nontrivial generalization via complex cross-PSD parameterization with explicit delays and per-pair parameters.  
- Potentially impactful for multi-channel systems with delays/phase shifts, but current gains on standard datasets are modest.  
- Suggests adding multi-output extrapolation demos on real data and tasks where LMC/CSM struggle (e.g., phase-shifted sensors, EEG/ECG, audio, power grids) to boost significance.  
- Pros: principled, interpretable construction; closed-form kernels; strong synthetic validation.  
- Cons: limited empirical advantage on benchmarks, unclear statistical significance, missing ablations/scalability/extrapolation demonstrations.  
- Overall: clever and promising kernel but borderline for acceptance pending stronger empirical evidence and statistical analysis.
