- Proposes MOSM kernel that extends spectral mixture to multi-output GPs using Cramér's theorem to model delays and phase differences between channels.  
- Provides a principled spectral-domain approach and derives a multi-output spectral mixture covariance with interpretable parameters.  
- Designs a complex-valued cross-spectral density matrix that yields valid cross-covariances and maintains positive definiteness.  
- Generalizes prior CSM work by modeling both phase shifts and time delays simultaneously.  
- Paper is clear and technically rigorous, with consistent notation and well-explained derivations.  
- Thorough background and connection to Wilson & Adams (2013) and Wilson et al. (2015) make the contribution accessible.  
- Synthetic experiments convincingly show MOSM can recover known cross-covariances for derivatives and delayed signals.  
- Real-world experiments (climate and Jura datasets) provide validation but show only modest improvements and limited differentiation in some cases.  
- Jura dataset results are hard to interpret due to high variable correlation and data complexity.  
- Lacks detailed analysis of which MOSM parameters drive improvements on real data.  
- Needs clearer discussion of when MOSM outperforms CSM, given similar performance on highly correlated data.  
- Comparison of computational complexity and scalability is missing and would strengthen the evaluation.  
- Overall technically sound, novel extension to multi-output setting, and offers increased interpretability.  
- Significance: meaningful theoretical advance with practical value for modeling cross-channel relationships, though real-world gains are modest.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Pros: principled extension, rigorous theory, novel delay+phase modeling, clear exposition, strong synthetic validation.  
- Cons: limited real-data insight, hard-to-interpret Jura results, insufficient computational efficiency comparison.
