This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel and expressive covariance function for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to generalize the single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel of Wilson and Adams (2013) to the multi-output setting. While previous work like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel by Ulrich et al. (2015) extended the SM kernel using the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) framework, this paper proposes a more fundamental approach. It designs a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and leverages Cramér’s Theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem) to derive the corresponding matrix-valued covariance function via the inverse Fourier transform. This principled construction results in a kernel that can model not only phase differences between output channels but also time delays, providing a richer and more interpretable parameterization of the cross-channel relationships. The model is validated on a synthetic task designed to highlight its expressiveness and compared against existing MOGP models on two real-world datasets: climate data and the Jura heavy metal concentration dataset.

### Strengths
1.  **Originality and Principled Approach:** The primary strength of this work is its elegant and principled derivation. Instead of heuristically modifying existing kernels, the authors start from first principles in the spectral domain. Using Cramér's Theorem to construct a valid MOGP kernel by designing a Hermitian positive-definite spectral density matrix is a powerful and clean methodology. The construction of this matrix via a factor decomposition (S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω)) is clever and guarantees the necessary properties while allowing for interpretable parameterization. This represents a significant conceptual advance over LMC-based spectral models.

2.  **Expressiveness and Interpretability:** The resulting MOSM kernel is highly expressive. It generalizes the CSM kernel by allowing channel-specific means and variances in the spectral domain (µi, Σi), which translates to more flexible auto-covariances. More importantly, it introduces an explicit delay parameter (θij) between channels, which is a feature not present in prior spectral MOGP kernels. The synthetic experiment, involving a function, its derivative, and a delayed version, provides a clear and convincing demonstration of the model's ability to capture these complex relationships where models based on linear combinations of latent processes fail.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The theoretical background on Bochner's and Cramér's theorems is presented clearly, providing the necessary foundation for the proposed method. The step-by-step construction of the cross-spectral density and the subsequent derivation of the MOSM kernel in Section 3.1 is easy to follow.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Experimental Impact on Real-World Data:** While the theory is compelling, the experimental results on the two real-world datasets are not as strong a demonstration of the model's superiority. On both the climate and Jura datasets, the MOSM performs comparably to the CSM kernel, and the performance gains are often not statistically significant, as noted by the authors. This raises the question of when the additional flexibility and complexity of MOSM (more hyperparameters) are truly beneficial in practice. The impact of the paper would be substantially greater if it included a real-world problem where modeling delays between channels is critical and where MOSM would demonstrably outperform other methods.

2.  **Missed Demonstration of Extrapolation Capabilities:** A key selling point of the original SM kernel was its remarkable ability to perform long-range extrapolation by discovering periodic patterns in the data. This paper, being a direct multi-output generalization, misses the opportunity to demonstrate analogous capabilities. The experiments focus on imputation (filling in missing data) rather than extrapolation (predicting into the future). A multi-output time-series extrapolation task would have been a more powerful showcase for a spectral kernel and would have better mirrored the strengths of its single-output predecessor.

3.  **Statistical Significance Reporting:** The reporting of statistical significance could be clearer. In Tables 1 and 2, the standard deviations are often large relative to the differences in mean performance, suggesting that many of the results are statistically indistinguishable. While the authors mention using a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test in the text, the tables themselves would benefit from standard conventions like bolding the best-performing model only when the improvement is statistically significant over the next-best competitor. This would provide a more transparent and honest assessment of the model's performance.

### Summary and Recommendation
This paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and elegant method for constructing expressive covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes. The use of Cramér's theorem to generalize the spectral mixture idea is a strong contribution to the field. The resulting MOSM kernel's ability to model inter-channel delays is a clear advantage over existing methods, as convincingly shown in the synthetic experiment.

However, the practical utility of this additional expressiveness is not fully established by the real-world experiments, where the proposed model offers only marginal gains over existing, slightly simpler models. The paper would be strengthened by either identifying a real-world application where modeling delays is key or by demonstrating the powerful multi-output extrapolation capabilities that one would expect from a spectral-based approach.

Despite the somewhat underwhelming experimental validation on real data, the novelty and elegance of the proposed theoretical framework are significant. The paper introduces a powerful new tool for the MOGP toolbox and opens up a promising direction for future research. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
