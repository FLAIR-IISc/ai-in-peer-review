**Summary**

This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a new and expressive parametric family of covariance functions for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The key contribution is a principled derivation based on Cramér's theorem, the multivariate generalization of Bochner's theorem. Instead of constructing the matrix-valued kernel directly in the time/space domain, the authors design a Hermitian positive-definite matrix of cross-spectral densities in the frequency domain and then obtain the kernel via the inverse Fourier transform. This construction guarantees the positive definiteness of the resulting MOGP kernel. The proposed MOSM kernel generalizes previous spectral approaches like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, and its parameters have a clear physical interpretation, corresponding to magnitudes, frequencies, delays, and phase shifts between the different output channels. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on a synthetic task involving derivatives and delays, and on two real-world datasets, showing competitive or superior performance against existing MOGP methods.

**Strengths**

*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The primary strength of this work is its solid theoretical underpinning. By starting from Cramér's theorem and designing a valid family of cross-spectral densities, the authors provide a principled and elegant way to construct MOGP kernels. This contrasts with more ad-hoc approaches and ensures the mathematical validity of the resulting covariance structure.
*   **Expressiveness and Interpretability:** The MOSM kernel is highly expressive. It can model not only phase differences (like the CSM kernel) but also time delays between channels, which is a novel and important feature for many time-series applications. Crucially, the hyperparameters are directly interpretable in the spectral domain (e.g., `θij` as delay, `φij` as phase), which is a significant advantage over less transparent models like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) or Convolutional GPs. The ability to learn these relationships from data is a major contribution.
*   **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the notoriously difficult problem of designing valid cross-covariance functions for MOGPs. The work provides a powerful new tool for practitioners and researchers, expanding the relatively small set of available flexible MOGP kernels.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the derivation is logical, and the connection to previous work is well-explained. The illustration in Figure 1 is particularly effective at demonstrating the flexibility afforded by the delay and phase parameters.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments effectively highlight the model's capabilities. The synthetic example (Section 4.1) is well-chosen to showcase the unique ability of MOSM to model complex relationships (derivatives and delays) that challenge other models. The model also demonstrates strong performance on real-world benchmarks.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Lack of Detail on Parameter Learning:** The most significant weakness is the lack of discussion regarding the optimization of the model's hyperparameters. The MOSM kernel introduces a substantial number of parameters (proportional to the number of outputs `m` and spectral components `Q`). The paper simply states that the negative log-likelihood is minimized with respect to these parameters. However, the optimization landscape for such a model is likely to be highly multi-modal and difficult to navigate. The authors should provide details on:
    *   Initialization strategy: How were the parameters initialized? This is crucial for non-convex optimization.
    *   Optimization challenges: Did the authors encounter issues with local minima? How sensitive is the final performance to initialization?
    *   Scalability: What is the computational complexity of training with respect to `m` and `Q`?
    Without these details, it is difficult to assess the practical usability of the method. If training is straightforward, this method is a clear winner; if it requires extensive tuning and multiple restarts, its practical appeal is diminished.
*   **Performance with Sparse Data:** The experiments are conducted with relatively large datasets (e.g., 1000 training points for the climate data). Given the large number of parameters, a key question is how the model behaves in low-data regimes. Is it prone to overfitting? Some discussion or an experiment on this aspect would strengthen the paper and clarify its domain of applicability.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the practical aspects of training the MOSM model, as mentioned in the "Weaknesses" section? Specifically, what initialization procedures were used, and how challenging was the optimization?
2.  How does the model handle correlations between channels with very different characteristics, for instance, a very smooth process (long length-scale) and a very noisy process (short length-scale)? The formulation for the combined covariance `Σij` seems to average the properties of `Σi` and `Σj`. Does this impose any limitations on the types of cross-correlations that can be modeled?
3.  In the synthetic experiment (Section 4.1), could you please state the total number of training points used? While the numbers for each component are given (N1=500, N2=400, N3=400), it would be clearer to state the total number of observations used to fit the joint MOGP model.

**Minor Remarks**

*   Line 74: A word appears to be missing. Perhaps "is given by the spectral mixture kernel"?

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on multi-output Gaussian processes. The proposed MOSM kernel is theoretically sound, highly expressive, and interpretable. While the paper would be improved by a more detailed discussion of the practicalities of model training, the core contribution is strong. If the optimization challenges are manageable, this method has the potential to become a standard and widely used tool for MOGP modeling. I recommend acceptance.
