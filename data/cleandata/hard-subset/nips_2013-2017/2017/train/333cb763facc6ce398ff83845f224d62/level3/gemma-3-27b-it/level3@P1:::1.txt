The paper proposes a novel approach to designing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by leveraging spectral methods. The authors extend the spectral mixture kernel concept, previously used for single-output GPs, to the multi-output setting, building upon Cramér’s Theorem—the multivariate extension of Bochner’s Theorem. This allows for the construction of kernels capable of modeling delays between channels, in addition to phase differences, offering greater expressiveness than existing methods like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and its variants. A key contribution is addressing the challenge of designing cross-covariances that don’t necessarily need to be positive definite, a common limitation in traditional MOGP formulations.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction to MOGPs and the limitations of current approaches. It provides a solid background on Gaussian processes and spectral mixture kernels, making it accessible to readers familiar with the general concepts. The extension to the multi-output case is logically presented, with a thorough derivation of the proposed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The mathematical formulation is clear and the connection to the underlying theory (Cramér’s Theorem) is well-explained. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature, citing relevant papers such as Wilson & Adams (2013) and Álvarez et al. (2008), and convincingly argue for the novelty of their approach.  

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical grounding and elegant extension of established techniques. The use of spectral methods offers a principled way to design covariance functions with explicit parametric interpretation, allowing for greater control over the modeled relationships between outputs. The ability to model delays, a feature not readily available in previous methods, significantly increases the flexibility of the proposed kernel.  The authors highlight the advantages over existing methods such as CSM (Ulrich et al. 2015), demonstrating that MOSM can handle more complex relationships between channels.

However, the experimental evaluation, while adequate, could be improved. The synthetic experiment effectively demonstrates the ability of the model to learn known relationships, including delays and derivatives. However, the real-world experiments, using climate data and heavy metal concentration data, lack a strong intuitive link between the model parameters and the underlying physical processes. While the results are competitive with other MOGP methods, it's difficult to discern *why* MOSM performs better (or worse) in specific scenarios. The Jura dataset, in particular, is described as difficult to interpret and the contribution of the proposed model there feels less compelling. Further analysis, perhaps visualizing the learned cross-covariances or relating them to domain-specific knowledge, would strengthen the empirical validation. Additionally, the ablation study could be improved.  The authors compare against several baselines but do not fully explore the impact of individual components within the MOSM kernel.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting with the ability to model delays is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is firmly grounded in mathematical theory (Cramér's Theorem) and provides a rigorous derivation of the proposed kernel.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the approach.
*   **Potential Significance:** The proposed kernel could be valuable in applications where modeling phase delays and complex inter-channel relationships is crucial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Intuition from Experiments:** The real-world experiments lack a strong connection to the underlying domain, making it difficult to assess the practical benefits of the proposed method.
*   **Ablation Study Could Be More Thorough:** A more detailed analysis of the impact of individual model components would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the clarity of the presentation and the potential significance of the proposed method outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
