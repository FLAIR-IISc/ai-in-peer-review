Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new family of multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) covariance functions, constructed by specifying complex-valued cross-power spectral densities (PSDs) and applying the inverse Fourier transform, invoking Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s). Concretely, the authors factor the cross-spectral matrix S(ω) as R(ω)H R(ω), choose complex Gaussian (SE) forms for the factors, enforce symmetry to obtain real-valued processes, and derive closed-form cross-covariances that include interpretable magnitudes, frequencies, phases, and delays. The resulting Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel extends the single-output spectral mixture (SM) idea of Wilson & Adams [6] to the multi-output setting in a principled way. It subsumes SM-LMC and CSM [5] as special cases while allowing output-specific spectral parameters and explicit inter-channel delays.

Experiments include: (i) a synthetic 3-output example (base signal, its derivative, and a delayed version) demonstrating recovery of known auto- and cross-covariances; (ii) a 4-station weather dataset with simulated sensor failures; and (iii) the Swiss Jura metal-concentration dataset. Results are competitive with SM-LMC, CSM, and CONV baselines, with clear gains on the synthetic task and mixed but broadly similar performance on real data.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction is well-justified. Using Cramér’s theorem to design PSDs and guarantee positive definiteness via a factorization S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) is clean and correct. The complex SE parameterization leads to closed-form cross-covariances with interpretable delay and phase. The symmetry trick to ensure real-valued processes is standard.
- Model flexibility and identifiability: Allowing per-output spectral parameters plus inter-output phase and delay is powerful. However, the paper could better discuss identifiability and parameter coupling (e.g., redundancies across weights, phases, and delays across components) and how initialization is handled in practice. The rank-Q extension is natural; empirical ablations varying Q would clarify when higher ranks help.
- Empirical support: The synthetic study convincingly shows MOSM can recover derivative and delay relationships and extrapolate both simultaneously, which latent-process MOGPs cannot capture. On climate and Jura datasets, improvements are modest and often comparable to CSM/SM-LMC; the paper notes one statistically significant win (Sotonmet) but for other cases differences are not significant. The reported standard deviations in Tables 1–2 suggest many results are within noise.
- Computation: Training uses exact GP inference; scalability is not addressed beyond discussion. Given the parameter-rich model, runtime and stability considerations (and sparse/inducing alternatives) merit more attention.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear derivation from spectral factors to time-domain kernels and helpful parameter interpretations (magnitude, frequency, phase, delay).
- Suggestions to improve clarity:
  - Summarize training details: initialization of spectral means/variances, delays, and phases; constraints; and regularization.
  - Explain the choice of statistical tests. For paired predictions, paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank may be more appropriate than KS.
  - Provide a brief complexity discussion and parameter count comparison vs baselines.

Originality
- The core idea—designing complex cross-PSDs via a factorization and using Cramér’s theorem—is a clever, principled extension of SM to multi-output, improving upon CSM by introducing interpretable, learnable inter-channel delays and allowing output-specific spectral parameters. It also clarifies how SM-LMC and CSM are nested special cases.
- Related work is adequately cited, including multivariate kernels via Cramér and real-valued cross-PSDs (e.g., [17]), highlighting the novelty of complex-valued cross-PSDs with both phase and delay.

Significance
- Conceptual significance is high: MOSM provides a general recipe to construct expressive multi-output kernels with interpretable cross-channel structure. This will likely be useful to practitioners needing phase/delay modeling (e.g., sensor networks, neuroscience, geostatistics).
- Empirical significance is currently modest on common benchmarks: performance is similar to strong existing kernels on weather and Jura, with clear benefits only on the synthetic demonstration. The impact would be strengthened by:
  - Demonstrating multi-output extrapolation akin to [6], e.g., long-horizon forecasting where SM kernels shine, and cases with clear inter-channel lags and asymmetries.
  - Including datasets where latent-process models perform poorly but delays/phases matter (multi-site climate with known advection delays, multi-channel physiological or neural recordings, motion capture with limb delays).
  - Ablations isolating the contribution of delays vs phases vs output-specific spectra, and of rank Q.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Principled, elegant extension of SM to MOGPs via cross-PSDs and Cramér’s theorem.
- Interpretable parameters (frequency, phase, delay, magnitude) at the pairwise level; subsumes SM-LMC and CSM.
- Closed-form cross-covariances; solid theoretical grounding and clear derivation.
- Strong synthetic demonstration of capabilities (derivative and delay).
- Competitive performance on real datasets.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited empirical advantage on standard benchmarks; many results appear statistically indistinguishable from CSM/SM-LMC.
- Missing a direct multi-output extrapolation study analogous to [6], where SM’s strengths are most evident.
- Insufficient discussion of identifiability, initialization, and optimization stability for the richer parameterization.
- No scalability solution; exact inference may limit applicability to larger multi-output settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add multi-output long-horizon forecasting tasks and real datasets with known inter-channel delays/phase shifts; report effect sizes and paired statistical tests.
- Provide ablations (with/without delays, phases, tied vs untied output spectra, varying rank Q) and analyze learned delays/phases against domain knowledge.
- Report training/runtime and parameter counts; explore sparse inter-domain inducing features (e.g., [22,23]) tailored to spectral structure.
- Include uncertainty calibration (e.g., coverage, CRPS) and robustness analyses.

Overall assessment
A technically sound and conceptually strong contribution that advances kernel design for MOGPs in a principled, interpretable way. The empirical section shows promise but does not yet convincingly demonstrate superiority on challenging, delay/phase-rich real tasks. I lean toward accept if space permits, especially given the elegance and potential impact of the construction; otherwise, a stronger empirical case would solidify the paper.
