

This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), extending the spectral mixture approach for single-output GPs to the multi-output setting. The authors build on Cramér's Theorem to construct a principled framework for designing matrix-valued covariance functions by directly modeling cross-spectral densities as complex-valued mixtures. This generalizes previous approaches like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5] by incorporating delays between channels alongside phase differences, providing richer parametric interpretation of cross-covariances. The work extends the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel [6] to multi-output settings, addressing a significant gap in MOGP literature where most existing methods (e.g., LMC [3], CONV [4]) lack clear interpretability of cross-channel relationships.

The paper's main strengths are its theoretical soundness, clear motivation, and effective validation. The authors demonstrate how their complex-valued cross-spectral densities, designed to satisfy Cramér's positive definiteness condition, yield interpretable parameters for delays, phase shifts, and spectral magnitudes. The synthetic experiment (Section 4.1) successfully recovers known covariances for a derivative and delayed signal without prior knowledge of their relationships, highlighting MOSM's ability to model non-linear dependencies that fail for latent-variable approaches. On real-world datasets (climate and metal concentration), MOSM achieves competitive results, with statistically significant improvements for Cadmium prediction (Table 3). The paper is clearly written with well-chosen illustrations (Fig. 1, 2) that effectively demonstrate the model's capabilities.

The work excels in originality and significance. It addresses a fundamental challenge in MOGP design—creating expressive yet positive-definite cross-covariances with interpretable parameters—by leveraging spectral domain design principles. This represents a meaningful advancement beyond CSM [5] (which lacks delay modeling) and SM-LMC [5], as shown in Section 3.3. The proposed framework could become a standard for MOGPs, especially for applications requiring physical interpretation of channel relationships (e.g., sensor networks or signal processing).

Regarding quality, the theoretical foundation is solid, with careful development from Bochner's to Cramér's Theorem. The experimental validation is comprehensive, though the paper could better address two limitations. First, the training procedure lacks detail: the paper mentions "hyperparameters are optimized" but doesn't discuss optimization challenges, convergence rates, or computational costs, which are crucial for practical adoption. Second, the synthetic experiment (4.1) omits the number of training points used for each channel (500, 400, 400), making it hard to assess sample efficiency. The authors should clarify how MOSM behaves with limited data—e.g., when N << m, which is common in multi-output settings.

A related concern is how MOSM handles channels with vastly different length-scales, a common scenario in real-world MOGPs. While the paper's flexible parameterization suggests it could accommodate this, the authors don't provide examples or analysis of such cases. This is particularly relevant given the Jura dataset's non-Gaussian Copper data (Section 4.3), where SM-LMC outperformed others despite its simplicity.

The paper is well-structured and clear, with only minor typos noted (lines 74, 140). The discussion (Section 5) appropriately identifies limitations and future work, including sparse implementations. However, the authors could strengthen the significance argument by more explicitly comparing MOSM's interpretability to alternative approaches—e.g., how the delay parameter enables causal inference in time-series data.

In conclusion, this work makes a strong theoretical and practical contribution to MOGP research. The MOSM kernel provides a principled, interpretable framework for modeling complex cross-channel relationships, with clear advantages over prior methods in certain scenarios. While the paper could improve by detailing optimization challenges and addressing sample efficiency, these are minor concerns for a high-quality technical contribution. The method's potential to become a standard for MOGP modeling, combined with its solid theoretical foundation and empirical validation, makes it a compelling candidate for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify training details and experimental setup.
