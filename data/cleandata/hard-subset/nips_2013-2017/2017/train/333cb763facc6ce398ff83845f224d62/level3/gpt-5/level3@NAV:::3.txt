Summary and relation to prior work: This paper introduces a new covariance construction for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by specifying a complex-valued cross-spectral density and invoking Cramér’s (multivariate Bochner) theorem to obtain a valid matrix-valued kernel. The resulting Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel strictly generalizes spectral-mixture-based approaches (Wilson & Adams, ICML’13) to the multi-output setting and subsumes LMC/SM-LMC and CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS’15) as special cases, while adding interpretable delay and phase parameters per output pair. This addresses a longstanding challenge in MOGPs—constructing expressive, interpretable cross-covariances while preserving positive definiteness (cf. Álvarez et al., NIPS’08; Genton & Kleiber, 2015).

Quality: The construction is theoretically sound: the PSD factorization guarantees Hermitian PSD matrices and thus valid covariance functions; closed-form inverse Fourier transforms yield practical kernels. Empirically, the method performs well on a synthetic tri-variate task (learning derivatives and delays) and two real datasets (climate sensors, Jura geostatistics). However, the paper provides limited detail on parameter learning. Given the non-convex objective and the enlarged parameter space (per-output means, variances, phases, delays, plus Q components), optimization and identifiability could be problematic. Please clarify initialization (e.g., periodogram-based, CSM warm-start), constraints/priors, regularization, and report training times and hyperparameter counts. Discuss ambiguity in the R(ω) factorization (scale/phase) and how you avoid degenerate solutions.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, illustrations are relevant, and the relation to CSM/SM-LMC is well explained. Minor edits: a word appears to be missing around lines 74 and 140. In the synthetic example (Sec. 4.1), please state prominently in the main text or figure caption the number of training points per channel.

Originality: High. The complex cross-spectrum with explicit phase and delay per pair, derived via Cramér’s theorem, is a principled and novel generalization beyond prior spectral MOGP kernels.

Significance: If training is robust, this could become a standard tool for MOGPs due to interpretability and flexibility. A sparse/variational extension (e.g., inter-domain spectral features; Hensman et al. 2016; Tobar et al. NIPS’15 workshop) would further increase impact.

Questions and suggestions:
- How does performance degrade with few training points? Please include a low-N study and/or informative priors on delays/phases.
- How does MOSM handle correlations between channels with very different length-scales? An ablation varying Σi across outputs would be informative.
- Report sensitivity to Q and provide guidance for model selection.
- Consider comparing against scalable variational MOGPs on larger datasets.

Pros:
- Principled, interpretable, and more expressive cross-covariances
- Solid theory; strong synthetic and competitive real-data results
- Clear writing and useful figures

Cons:
- Limited guidance on optimization/identifiability and scalability
- Missing low-data and disparate-length-scale analyses
- Minor presentation issues noted above
