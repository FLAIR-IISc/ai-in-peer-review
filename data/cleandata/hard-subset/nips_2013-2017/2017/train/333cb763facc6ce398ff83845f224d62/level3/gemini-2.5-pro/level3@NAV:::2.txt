This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a new family of covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The work builds upon Cramér's theorem, the multivariate generalization of Bochner's theorem, to establish a principled approach for kernel design in the spectral domain. Specifically, the authors define a parametric family of cross-power spectral densities and derive the corresponding cross-covariance functions via the inverse Fourier transform. This method is a direct and logical extension of the highly influential Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel from Wilson & Adams [6] to the multi-output setting, providing a flexible framework capable of modeling complex inter-channel dependencies like delays and phase shifts.

The primary strength of this work lies in its originality and theoretical elegance. The proposed method for constructing MOGP kernels from the spectral domain is a clever and promising approach. It provides a more expressive and interpretable framework than many existing methods, and the resulting ability to explicitly parameterize delays and phase differences between outputs is a significant and valuable contribution to the field of MOGP modeling.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its experimental evaluation, which does not sufficiently demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed method. The experiments are conducted on common benchmark datasets (a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura dataset), where the MOSM kernel achieves performance largely comparable to existing methods like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The reported standard deviations in Tables 1 and 2 are often large relative to the mean differences, which raises questions about the statistical significance of the claimed performance gains.

Furthermore, a key motivation for the original SM kernel was its remarkable ability to extrapolate. This paper misses a critical opportunity by not providing a compelling demonstration of analogous extrapolation capabilities in a multi-output context. The impact of the work could be substantially increased by including experiments on more challenging datasets where existing MOGP models are known to perform poorly, which would better highlight the practical advantages of MOSM's added flexibility.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically sound and novel method for MOGP kernel construction. While the contribution is promising, the empirical evidence is not yet strong enough to convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over the state of the art.
