- Proposes MOSM: a multi-output spectral mixture kernel built from complex cross-PSDs and Cramér’s theorem, yielding closed-form real cross-covariances with channel-specific magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay.
- Factorizes S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) with complex SE factors per channel to obtain analytic inverse Fourier transforms; autocovariances reduce to Wilson & Adams SM.
- Strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM; CSM appears as a special case with tied spectral parameters and no delays.
- Experiments: synthetic trivariate (reference, derivative, delayed), climate sensor imputation, Swiss Jura heavy-metal prediction; MOSM excels on synthetic task and is competitive on real benchmarks.
- Technical soundness: principled construction with positive-definiteness guaranteed by Cramér’s theorem and correct symmetrization for real processes.
- Interpretability: parameters are interpretable (magnitude, mean, bandwidth, phase, delay) and analytic inverses are provided.
- Claims vs. evidence: convincingly demonstrates joint phase and delay modeling on synthetic data; real-data gains over baselines are modest and not uniformly tested for significance.
- Completeness: lacks practical details on initialization, identifiability (delay vs. phase), and computational scaling with outputs and components Q.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and mathematically careful; Fourier mappings and parameter roles are clearly enumerated.
- Minor clarity suggestions: add complexity analysis, parameter counts vs. CSM/SM-LMC, and initialization guidance (periodogram/cross-periodogram).
- Originality: meaningful extension of SM to multi-output via complex cross-PSDs allowing channel-specific spectra, phase, and delays; unifies prior spectral multi-output kernels.
- Significance: promising for domains with inter-channel lags (neuroscience, climate, seismology), but limited immediate impact due to modest real-data gains.
- Strengths: principled theoretical guarantee, closed-form interpretable cross-covariances, and strict generalization of prior methods with strong synthetic validation.
- Weaknesses: limited empirical advantage on standard datasets, weak multi-output extrapolation demonstrations, partial reporting of statistical significance, and sparse practical guidance.
- Key suggested improvements: add a real extrapolation benchmark with cross-channel lags, uniform statistical tests/confidence intervals, ablations (delay vs. phase, tied vs. channel-specific), identifiability/initialization discussion, complexity analysis, and code release.
- Arguments for acceptance: conceptually clean, theoretically sound, unifies prior work, and fills capability gaps (phase+delay) shown on synthetic data.
- Arguments against acceptance: modest real-data improvements, missing compelling real-world extrapolation demonstration, and under-discussed practical/optimization issues.
- Overall verdict: solid, well-motivated contribution; leans toward acceptance if empirical evidence (especially extrapolation) and practical guidance are strengthened, otherwise borderline.
