

This paper extends spectral mixture kernels to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by proposing a complex-valued cross-spectral density framework built on Cramér's theorem, generalizing the single-output spectral mixture approach of Wilson et al. (2013) to model delays and phase shifts across channels. The authors construct the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel by designing Hermitian spectral densities \(S_{ij}(\omega)\) that decompose as \(R_i(\omega)R_j(\omega)\), ensuring positive definiteness via Theorem 2. This yields closed-form cross-covariances with interpretable parameters: delays (\(\theta_{ij}\)), phase shifts (\(\phi_{ij}\)), and spectral components (\(\mu_{ij}, \Sigma_{ij}\)), addressing limitations in prior methods like Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) and Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC), which lack delay modeling or enforce identical parameters across channels.

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical contribution is sound and well-motivated. The extension of spectral mixture principles to multi-output GPs via Cramér’s theorem is elegant, providing a principled path to design cross-covariances with clear physical interpretations. The synthetic experiment (Fig. 2) convincingly demonstrates MOSM’s ability to recover derivative and delayed signals—tasks where latent-process-based models (e.g., SM-LMC, CONV) fail due to their inability to model non-linear cross-covariances. The implementation using GPflow/TensorFlow is reproducible, and the method’s flexibility (e.g., rank \(Q\) generalization) is well-explained. The paper adequately references related work, particularly CSM (Ulrich et al., 2015) and LMC (Álvarez and Lawrence, 2008), positioning MOSM as a natural generalization.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary weakness is the lack of a compelling demonstration of *extrapolation capability*—a hallmark of Wilson et al.’s single-output spectral mixture kernel (2013). While the synthetic example shows imputation, it does not extend to out-of-distribution extrapolation (e.g., predicting beyond the training interval), which is critical for the method’s claimed advantages. In real-world experiments (climate and Jura datasets), MOSM’s performance (Tables 1–3) is competitive but not decisively superior to CSM or SM-LMC. The reported standard deviations (e.g., Table 2: MOSM vs. CSM MAE = 0.129 ± 0.003 vs. 0.130 ± 0.004) suggest no statistically significant gains (Kolmogorov-Smirnov test, \(\alpha=0.05\)), undermining the significance claim. The climate dataset’s high signal correlation likely masks MOSM’s potential, while the Jura dataset’s copper results show no clear MOSM advantage. The paper misses an opportunity to test on datasets where prior MOGP models fail (e.g., highly non-stationary or delayed signals), which would better showcase impact.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**  
*Pro:* Theoretical novelty in extending spectral mixture to multi-output via complex cross-spectral densities; clear parametric interpretation (delays, phase); strong synthetic validation of cross-covariance modeling; well-executed implementation.  
*Con:* Limited practical impact (performance similar to state-of-the-art on standard benchmarks); no extrapolation demonstration; insufficient statistical validation of results; real-world gains are marginal and not consistently significant.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper makes a technically valid contribution to MOGP kernel design, advancing the field by unifying delay modeling and phase shifts under a spectral framework. However, the significance is diminished by the lack of clear empirical advantages over CSM (which already handles phase shifts) and the absence of a compelling extrapolation case. While the method is sound and well-motivated, the results do not demonstrate a *substantial* improvement over existing approaches for typical MOGP tasks. The work is a solid incremental step but lacks the transformative impact expected for NIPS. The clarity is high, and quality is strong, but significance is moderate. Given the marginal performance gains and unfulfilled extrapolation promise, the paper is borderline. **Recommendation: Weak Accept.** It merits publication for its theoretical contribution but would benefit from additional experiments on challenging datasets or a stronger extrapolation demonstration to justify the significance claim.
