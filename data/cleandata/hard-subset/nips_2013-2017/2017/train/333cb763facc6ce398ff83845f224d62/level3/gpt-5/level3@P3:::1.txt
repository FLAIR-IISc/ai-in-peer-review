Summary
This paper proposes a principled spectral-domain construction for multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernels. Building on Wilson & Adams (2013) and Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem), the authors define a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities whose inverse Fourier transform yields a new multi-output spectral mixture (MOSM) kernel. The construction guarantees overall positive definiteness by ensuring that, for each frequency ω, the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) is Hermitian positive semidefinite via a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω). In the time domain, the resulting cross-covariances can have interpretable parameters—magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay—and need not be positive-definite or symmetric individually, as required for realistic inter-channel dependencies. The model strictly generalizes prior spectral-domain MOGP approaches: SM-LMC and CSM appear as special cases obtained by constraining per-output parameters. Experiments show clear advantages on a synthetic setting with derivatives and delays, and competitive performance on climate and Jura datasets.

Strengths
- Clear and principled extension of spectral mixture design to multi-output GPs: Using Cramér’s theorem to work in the spectral domain is elegant and technically sound.
- Expressive and interpretable cross-covariances: The complex exponential introduces phase and linear phase (delay) terms, enabling delayed, out-of-phase, and negative correlations—capabilities that prior LMC/CONV-style models lack or approximate indirectly.
- Positive definiteness handled correctly: The paper emphasizes the key distinction that cross-covariances need not be positive definite, while S(ω) must be PSD pointwise; the factorization-based construction directly enforces this.
- Closed-form time-domain kernel: The inverse Fourier pairs remain in closed form (Gaussian times cosine), preserving tractability and familiarity of SM kernels.
- Relation to prior work is carefully developed: The reduction to SM-LMC/CSM under parameter tying makes novelty and contribution precise.
- Writing and pedagogy: The introduction, review of GP/SM background, and derivations are clear; the spectral interpretation of parameters is well explained.

Weaknesses
- Limited experimental insight on real data: While results are adequate, gains over strong baselines (especially CSM) are modest in climate and partly in Jura, and the Jura task is difficult to interpret. The paper would benefit from case studies that visually and quantitatively interpret learned phases/delays and relate them to known domain phenomena.
- No ablations: There is no analysis isolating the contributions of delay vs. phase vs. per-output spectral parameters, or the effect of rank Q. An ablation would clarify when each modeling ingredient matters.
- Identifiability and invariances: Because θij = θi − θj and φij = φi − φj, absolute delays/phases are not identifiable; multiple parameterizations can yield the same cross-covariances. The paper does not discuss these symmetries, their impact on optimization, or initialization strategies.
- Scalability and parameter efficiency: The parameter count scales with m × Q × (3n + 2) (for diag Σ), which may be large; training remains O(N^3). There is limited discussion of runtime, initialization, or regularization (e.g., priors, constraints) to prevent overfitting.
- Rank limitation: Much of the exposition focuses on rank-one per component; it would help to analyze empirically whether higher rank is needed in practice and how it affects performance and stability.

Technical assessment (Quality)
- The construction is correct, with appropriate use of Cramér’s theorem and symmetry to ensure real-valued processes. The Hermitian PSD factorization is a clean way to guarantee positive definiteness.
- The derivation of cross-spectral parameters and their time-domain Fourier pairs appears sound and is carefully presented. The allowance for non-symmetric, non-PD cross-covariances is an important property for MOGPs.
- Experiments support the main claims: the synthetic task decisively demonstrates the advantages for delays and derivatives; real data results are competitive and include basic statistical testing.
- Missing: a discussion on optimization details (parameterization for positivity, initialization of μ, Σ, θ, φ, and w), sensitivity to Q, and computational/memory costs.

Clarity
- Very well written and organized. Background and related work are thorough and accurate. The spectral intuition is clear, and the special-case relationships to SM-LMC and CSM are explicit.
- Minor suggestion: clarify the precise symmetry property of K(τ) across outputs (e.g., K(τ) = K(−τ)T) and how the non-symmetry of cross-covariances reflects phase/delay.

Originality
- Extends spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting in a principled and more general way than prior MOGP spectral constructions. Modeling complex cross-spectra with explicit delay and phase, and deriving closed-form time-domain cross-covariances, is a meaningful advance. The specialization relationships to CSM and SM-LMC are convincing.

Significance
- Conceptually important: the approach will be of interest to researchers modeling multi-channel time series or spatial-temporal data where delays and phase shifts matter (e.g., sensor networks, neuroscience, geosciences).
- Practical impact is promising but under-demonstrated on real datasets; broader and more diagnostic experiments, and scalable inference, would increase significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (i) no delay, (ii) no phase, (iii) tied μ/Σ across outputs (CSM-like), (iv) vary rank Q. Report effects on both performance and learned interpretability.
- Report optimization details: initialization (e.g., spectral estimates), constraints/reparameterizations for Σ and w, and regularization.
- Provide runtime and parameter counts, plus a discussion of N and m scaling; consider including sparse/variational Fourier features or inter-domain inducing variables (as hinted in the discussion).
- Improve interpretability: visualize learned delay/phase on real data, and relate them to physical distances or known lags.
- Consider uncertainty metrics (calibration, negative log-likelihood) in addition to MAE.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, general spectral-domain construction for MOGPs with interpretable parameters.
- Technically sound and well explained; clear relationship to and generalization of prior work.
- Demonstrated ability to model delays and phases not handled by earlier kernels.

Cons against acceptance
- Modest experimental gains on real data; limited interpretability of learned structures there.
- Lack of ablations and scalability analysis; no discussion of identifiability and optimization challenges.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The method is novel, clean, and well presented; the theory is correct and the contribution is meaningful. Strengthen with ablations, interpretability analyses on real data, and a brief discussion of identifiability and scalability.

Reviewer confidence
High: familiar with GP kernels, multi-output models, and spectral methods.
