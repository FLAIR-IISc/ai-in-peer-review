This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel approach to constructing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to define cross-spectral densities in the Fourier domain and then leverage Cramér’s Theorem to obtain corresponding covariance functions. This extends the spectral mixture concept from single-output GPs [6] to the multi-output setting, offering a principled way to model delays and phase differences between channels with full parametric interpretability.

The motivation for this work is well-articulated. Existing MOGP methods, such as the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) [3] and Convolution Model (CONV) [4], often lack interpretability and can impose restrictive relationships between channels. More recent approaches like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5] introduce phase shifts but lack the flexibility offered by a more general spectral design. The authors convincingly argue that designing spectral densities directly provides a more expressive and interpretable framework.

The paper is technically sound and clearly presents the mathematical derivation of the MOSM kernel. Building on Cramér’s Theorem, the authors propose a family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and demonstrate how to construct a corresponding covariance function via the inverse Fourier transform. The inclusion of delay and phase parameters is a significant contribution, extending the capabilities of previous MOGP methods.  The connection to Bochner’s Theorem [7] and the explanation of the positive-definiteness guarantee through the spectral density construction are well explained.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, including a synthetic example and two real-world datasets: climate signals and heavy metal concentrations. The synthetic experiment effectively validates the ability of the MOSM kernel to capture known auto- and cross-covariances, including derivatives and delays.  The results on the real-world datasets are competitive with existing MOGP methods. However, as the authors acknowledge, the performance improvements are not always substantial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of directly designing cross-spectral densities and utilizing Cramér’s Theorem is a novel contribution to the field of MOGPs.
*   **Interpretability:** The MOSM kernel offers a clear parametric interpretation of the relationships between channels in terms of frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay.
*   **Expressiveness:** The proposed kernel is more expressive than previous methods, allowing for more flexible modeling of cross-covariance functions.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The work is grounded in solid mathematical theory, building upon Bochner’s and Cramér’s Theorems.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** Experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets provide a good evaluation of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Statistical Significance:** The experimental results lack a rigorous assessment of statistical significance. While standard deviations are reported, it is difficult to determine whether the observed differences in performance are statistically meaningful, particularly in Tables 1 and 2.
*   **Extrapolation Demonstration:** While the paper acknowledges the extrapolation ability of the spectral mixture approach in the single-output case [6], a direct demonstration of this capability for multi-output scenarios is missing. This would strengthen the argument for the advantages of the proposed method.
*   **Limited Impact on Standard Datasets:** The results on the climate and metal concentration datasets are competitive but not overwhelmingly superior to existing methods. Exploring datasets where current models struggle would better highlight the potential impact of the MOSM kernel.
*   **Computational Cost:** The authors do not discuss the computational complexity of the MOSM kernel, particularly in comparison to other MOGP methods.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to constructing MOGP covariance functions.
*   The proposed MOSM kernel offers increased expressiveness and interpretability compared to existing methods.
*   The experimental evaluation, though not conclusive, demonstrates the competitiveness of the MOSM kernel on standard datasets.
*   The work addresses an important problem in Gaussian process modeling and has the potential to advance the state of the art.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of statistical significance in the experimental results weakens the claim of superior performance.
*   The absence of a demonstration of extrapolation capabilities is a missed opportunity to highlight a key advantage of the spectral mixture approach.
*   The performance gains on the real-world datasets are not substantial enough to justify a strong recommendation for acceptance.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of Gaussian process modeling. While the experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing, the novelty, theoretical foundation, and interpretability of the proposed MOSM kernel are significant strengths.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Perform statistical significance testing on the experimental results.
2.  Include a demonstration of the extrapolation capabilities of the MOSM kernel, perhaps using the synthetic data or a modified real-world dataset.
3.  Discuss the computational complexity of the MOSM kernel in relation to other MOGP methods.
