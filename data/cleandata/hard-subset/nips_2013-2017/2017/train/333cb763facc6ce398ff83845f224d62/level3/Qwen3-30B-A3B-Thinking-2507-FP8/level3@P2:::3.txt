

# Review for "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

This paper proposes a novel multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernel called the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The authors extend the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel for single-output GPs to the multi-output case by constructing a complex-valued matrix of spectral densities that satisfies Cramér's Theorem (the multivariate version of Bochner's Theorem). The key innovation is the ability to model delays between channels in addition to phase differences, which previous methods like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel could not do. The MOSM kernel provides clear spectral interpretation of all parameters, including frequencies, magnitudes, phase shifts, and delays, making it more interpretable than prior approaches.

The paper builds on Wilson et al. (2013) who introduced the SM kernel for single-output GPs, and Ulrich et al. (2015) who proposed the CSM kernel for MOGPs. The authors correctly position their work as extending CSM to allow for delays between channels, which they demonstrate is a significant improvement. They also relate their work to the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and Convolution Model (CONV), showing that MOSM generalizes these approaches while providing better interpretability.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The theoretical foundation is strong, with a clear derivation of the MOSM kernel from Cramér's Theorem. The paper provides a sound mathematical justification for the kernel construction and demonstrates that it guarantees positive definiteness. The experimental results on synthetic data and two real-world datasets (climate signals and heavy metal concentrations) show the method is effective, with MOSM outperforming baselines on the heavy metal dataset. The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly regarding parameter learning details.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear mathematical notation and good use of figures to illustrate key concepts. The theoretical background is well-motivated, and the relationship between the proposed kernel and previous work is clearly articulated. The synthetic example (Section 4.1) effectively demonstrates the model's ability to learn delayed signals and derivatives without prior knowledge of these relationships.

*Originality:* The extension of spectral mixture to multi-output GPs with the ability to model delays is genuinely novel. Previous work like CSM could not model delays between channels, which is a significant limitation for applications where temporal or spatial relationships between outputs matter. The paper clearly differentiates MOSM from prior approaches and demonstrates its advantages.

*Significance:* The method addresses a fundamental challenge in multi-output GP modeling: designing interpretable cross-covariance functions. The clear spectral interpretation of parameters makes it valuable for practitioners who need to understand relationships between outputs. The paper shows that MOSM can become a standard tool for multi-output GP modeling, particularly for applications where delays or phase differences between channels are important.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* The paper lacks detail on parameter learning and optimization challenges. While the authors mention they use automatic differentiation via TensorFlow and GPflow, they don't discuss potential difficulties with optimizing the complex parameter space or the computational cost of training. This is a significant omission for a method that could become widely adopted.

*Clarity:* The synthetic example (Section 4.1) should explicitly state the number of training points for each channel. The paper notes a missing word in lines 74 and 140, which should be corrected before publication.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel extension of spectral mixture approach to multi-output GPs with interpretable parameters
- Theoretical soundness with clear derivation from Cramér's Theorem
- Demonstrated effectiveness on synthetic and real-world datasets
- Clear relationship to previous work with proper citations
- Well-written with strong mathematical presentation

*Con:*
- Insufficient detail on parameter learning and optimization challenges
- Missing training point counts in synthetic example
- The real-world dataset results (climate data) don't show statistically significant advantages over CSM

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. The MOSM kernel extends previous work by providing a more expressive model that can capture delays between channels while maintaining clear parameter interpretation. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the experimental results demonstrate practical utility.

The main concern is the lack of detail on parameter learning, which is crucial for practitioners considering implementing this method. However, this is a minor issue compared to the paper's strengths. The method has clear potential to become a standard for multi-output GP modeling, particularly for applications where understanding the relationship between channels is important.

The paper is well-written, clearly positioned within the literature, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance at NIPS 2017. The minor issues noted (training point counts, missing words) can be easily addressed in the camera-ready version.
