- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending spectral mixture kernels to MOGPs via complex-valued cross-power spectral densities.
- Builds on Cramér’s Theorem to obtain principled multivariate covariance functions through inverse Fourier transforms.
- MOSM models delays and phase differences across channels and allows channel-specific spectral parameters for greater expressiveness.
- Theoretical derivation is rigorous and connects clearly to prior work, notably extending the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel.
- The approach guarantees positive-definiteness via factor decomposition of the spectral density matrix.
- Implementation using GPflow is standard and appropriate.
- Synthetic experiment successfully recovers known cross-covariances (signal, derivative, delayed version), with MOSM uniquely extrapolating both derivative and delayed signals.
- Originality: clever and meaningful theoretical advancement over existing multi-output kernels.
- Clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and figures effectively illustrate concepts.
- Technical soundness: mathematically solid with appropriate theoretical guarantees.
- Missing demonstration of multi-output extrapolation analogous to Wilson & Adams (2013), a major omission given extrapolation is a key SM strength.
- Limited experimental impact: uses standard datasets (weather, Swiss Jura) where improvements are marginal and possibly not statistically significant.
- MOSM is very similar to CSM; empirical results don’t clearly justify added complexity.
- Significance is tempered by lack of clear experimental superiority and missing extrapolation validation.
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically sound extension, clear parametric interpretation, strong foundations, and convincing synthetic results.
- Arguments against acceptance: marginal empirical gains, no extrapolation demo, reliance on standard datasets, and unclear statistical significance.
- Recommendation: accept but strengthen impact with additional experiments demonstrating extrapolation and clear empirical advantages over existing methods.
