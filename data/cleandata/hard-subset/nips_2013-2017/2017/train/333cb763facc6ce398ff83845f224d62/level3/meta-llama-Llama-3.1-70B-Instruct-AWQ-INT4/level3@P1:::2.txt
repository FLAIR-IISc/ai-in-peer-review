This paper proposes a novel multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) covariance function, called the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, which is an extension of the spectral-mixture idea from [6] to multiple outputs. The MOSM kernel is designed by specifying cross-power spectral densities and using the inverse Fourier transform to obtain cross-covariances. This approach allows for the modeling of complex relationships between multiple outputs, including delays and phase differences.

The paper is well-motivated, and the proposed method is a clever and promising way to construct new multi-output covariance functions. The authors provide a thorough theoretical background, including the necessary definitions and theorems, and they clearly explain the construction of the MOSM kernel.

The experimental evaluation is conducted on a synthetic dataset, a weather dataset, and the Swiss Jura metal-concentration dataset. The results show that the MOSM kernel performs competitively with existing multi-output covariance functions, including the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC), the Convolution Model (CONV), and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. Firstly, the authors do not demonstrate the extrapolation ability of the MOSM kernel, which is an important feature of the spectral-mixture approach. Secondly, the experimental evaluation uses common datasets, and the results do not show a significant difference between the proposed model and existing models. Including datasets where other models perform poorly could increase the impact of the paper. Finally, the authors do not provide a clear indication of whether the results in Tables 1 and 2 are statistically significant, and the reported standard deviations suggest similar performance across models.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is a valuable contribution to the field of MOGPs. However, the authors could strengthen their paper by addressing the limitations mentioned above.
