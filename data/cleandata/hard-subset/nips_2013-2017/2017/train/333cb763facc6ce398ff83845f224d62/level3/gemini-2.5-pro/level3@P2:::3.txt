### Summary

This paper introduces a novel and principled approach for constructing expressive covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The proposed method, termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extends the spectral mixture (SM) kernel of Wilson & Adams (2013) to the multivariate setting. The core idea is to design a flexible parametric form for the matrix of cross-spectral densities in the frequency domain and then derive the corresponding matrix-valued covariance function via the inverse Fourier transform, justified by Cramér's Theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem). The resulting kernel is a sum of components, each of which can model not only magnitude and frequency relationships between output channels, but also physically interpretable phase differences and, crucially, time delays. This formulation generalizes previous MOGP methods based on spectral analysis, such as the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, by providing a more flexible parametric structure. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on a synthetic task designed to highlight its unique capabilities and on two real-world datasets, where it performs competitively with or surpasses existing state-of-the-art MOGP models.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical foundation, based on Cramér's Theorem and a rank-one decomposition of the spectral density matrix, is solid and well-explained. The derivation of the MOSM kernel from its spectral-domain definition is clear and principled.

The experimental validation is strong. The synthetic example, involving learning a function, its derivative, and a delayed version, is particularly insightful as it creates a scenario with non-trivial cross-covariances that LMC-based models cannot capture. The successful recovery of these relationships convincingly demonstrates the expressive power of the MOSM kernel. The real-world experiments on climate and geological data show that the model is robust and performs well on practical problems.

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the background section provides the necessary context on SM kernels and MOGPs. The illustrations, particularly Figure 1 which visualizes the impact of the delay and phase parameters, are helpful for building intuition.

### Originality and Significance

The problem of designing flexible, interpretable, and valid MOGP kernels is a long-standing and important one. This paper makes a significant and original contribution to this area. While the idea of using the spectral domain is not new (e.g., CSM), this work provides a more general and principled framework that naturally gives rise to interpretable parameters for delays and phase shifts, which previous methods either could not model or incorporated in a more ad-hoc manner. The proposed construction via a factor decomposition in the Fourier domain is elegant and guarantees the positive definiteness of the resulting kernel matrix.

The significance of this work is high. Given its expressiveness and interpretability, the MOSM kernel has the potential to become a standard tool for MOGP modeling, especially in domains like spatio-temporal analysis, econometrics, or neuroscience, where understanding delays and phase relationships between signals is crucial. If the practical challenges of training are manageable, researchers and practitioners are likely to adopt this method for a wide range of multi-output regression problems.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**
1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The paper presents a theoretically sound and elegant method for constructing MOGP kernels based on Cramér's Theorem, which is a significant generalization of prior spectral approaches.
2.  **High Expressiveness and Interpretability:** The MOSM kernel can model complex relationships between outputs, including time delays and phase shifts, which are crucial in many real-world applications. The model parameters have a clear physical interpretation in the frequency domain.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The synthetic experiment is carefully designed to showcase the unique advantages of the proposed model over existing methods. The results on real-world datasets demonstrate its practical utility and competitive performance.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a potentially complex topic accessible.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
1.  **Optimization Challenges:** The paper provides limited detail on the practical aspects of model training. The hyperparameter space of the MOSM kernel is very large, and the negative log-likelihood surface is likely to be highly multi-modal and difficult to optimize. A discussion of initialization strategies, optimization stability, and computational cost would greatly strengthen the paper. How sensitive are the final results to initialization? Were multiple restarts required to achieve the reported performance?
2.  **Performance in Low-Data Regimes:** Given the large number of parameters, the model could be prone to overfitting when training data is scarce. How does the model's performance degrade as the number of training points decreases?
3.  **Flexibility of Cross-Channel Correlations:** How does the model handle correlations between channels that have very different characteristic length scales? The coupling of parameters (e.g., for `Σij`) seems to suggest that the properties of the cross-covariance are tied to the auto-covariances. Is it possible for this structure to be overly restrictive in some cases?
