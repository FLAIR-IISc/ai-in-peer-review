

The paper proposes a novel Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), extending Wilson and Adams’ spectral mixture (SM) kernel [6] to the multi-output setting. Building on Cramér’s Theorem, the authors design complex-valued cross-spectral densities to model delays and phase shifts across channels—addressing a key limitation of prior work like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5], which cannot capture delays. The MOSM kernel generalizes CSM by allowing distinct parameters for auto-covariances and cross-covariances, while ensuring positive definiteness via a rank-one factorization of the spectral density matrix. The approach is theoretically grounded, with clear parametric interpretation of spectral components (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay), and validated on synthetic data and two real-world datasets (climate signals and heavy metal concentrations).

**Strengths**  
*Quality:* The theoretical derivation is sound and well-motivated. The extension via Cramér’s Theorem provides a principled framework for constructing positive-definite multivariate kernels, avoiding the pitfalls of linear models (e.g., LMC) that force shared spectral properties across channels. The closed-form covariance expression (Eq. 11) is elegant, and the synthetic experiment (Section 4.1) convincingly demonstrates MOSM’s ability to recover derivatives and delayed signals—tasks where latent-process-based models (CONV, SM-LMC) fail.  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written. Background on scalar GPs, spectral mixture kernels, and MOGPs is concise yet thorough, with clear definitions (e.g., Definitions 1–4) and intuitive explanations of the spectral domain design. The derivation of the MOSM kernel (Section 3.1) is logically structured and accessible.  
*Originality:* The work significantly extends Wilson’s SM kernel [6] and CSM [5] by introducing complex-valued cross-spectral densities to model delays—unachievable in prior methods. The relationship to CSM (Section 3.3) is explicitly clarified, confirming MOSM subsumes CSM as a special case (with identical channel parameters). This novelty is sufficient for publication, as no NIPS 2013–2017 paper addresses delays in cross-covariances via spectral methods.  
*Significance:* The parametric interpretation of delays/phase is theoretically valuable for applications like signal processing and sensor fusion, where temporal misalignment is common. The method advances kernel design for MOGPs, moving beyond linear combinations of latent GPs (e.g., LMC) toward more expressive, interpretable models.

**Weaknesses**  
*Significance:* The real-world experiments (Sections 4.2–4.3) show marginal gains. On climate data, MOSM outperforms CSM only for Sotonmet (statistically significant), but results for other sensors are indistinguishable (Table 2). For the Jura dataset, MOSM beats CSM in Cadmium prediction (MAE 0.127 vs. 0.148), but this difference is not statistically significant against CONV (results from [4] are not re-evaluated). Crucially, the authors do not demonstrate *why* delays matter in practice—e.g., no ablation on delay parameters or comparison to methods designed for time-lagged data. The Jura results are hard to interpret due to high correlation between metal concentrations, diminishing the perceived advantage of MOSM’s delay modeling.  
*Quality:* The experimental setup lacks depth. The climate data example (Section 4.2) shows all models capture similar signal behavior due to high correlation, yet the paper does not discuss *why* MOSM’s delay modeling doesn’t yield clearer gains here. The synthetic example (Section 4.1) is strong, but real-world validation remains underwhelming for a method claiming to model delays.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments**  
*Pro:*  
- Strong theoretical contribution extending spectral mixture kernels to MOGPs with a clear interpretation of delays/phase.  
- Well-structured, accessible writing with thorough background and precise mathematical derivations.  
- Synthetic experiments convincingly validate the model’s ability to recover complex relationships (e.g., derivatives, delays).  
- Novelty is significant and distinct from CSM/LMC (NIPS 2015/2008), aligning with NIPS’ focus on principled kernel design.  

*Con:*  
- Real-world results show minimal practical impact; gains on Jura climate data are not robust or statistically significant against baselines.  
- The paper does not provide evidence that delays are *necessary* for real-world performance (e.g., no ablation showing degradation without delay parameters).  
- Limited discussion of computational cost or scalability (e.g., compared to CONV [4] or sparse approximations [21]).  

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a technically sound, well-motivated contribution to MOGP kernel design. Its theoretical novelty and clarity are strong, but the experimental validation falls short of demonstrating substantial real-world significance. While the synthetic example is compelling, the real-world results do not convincingly show *why* MOSM’s delay modeling matters beyond the synthetic case. The work is suitable for NIPS if the authors address the experimental limitations in revision (e.g., by adding ablation studies on delay parameters, comparing against delay-specific baselines, or clarifying when delays are critical). As-is, the significance is moderate—this is a solid methodological advance but does not yet establish a clear practical advantage over existing MOGP approaches. I recommend *weak acceptance* pending minor revisions to strengthen the experimental analysis.
