

This paper extends the spectral mixture (SM) kernel framework [6] to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by designing a family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities, leveraging Cramér’s Theorem to construct a novel covariance function termed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM). The key innovation is modeling both delays (via phase shifts) and frequency-dependent phase differences across channels—addressing a limitation in prior work like Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) [5], which only incorporates phase shifts without delays. The MOSM kernel provides full parametric interpretability (e.g., frequency, magnitude, delay, phase) and ensures positive definiteness through a low-rank decomposition of the spectral density matrix. The authors validate MOSM on synthetic data (derivative/delayed signals), climate sensor networks, and the Jura heavy-metal dataset, comparing against SM-LMC, CONV, and CSM.

**Quality**  
The theoretical foundation is sound, with rigorous application of Cramér’s Theorem to guarantee positive definiteness. The derivation of the kernel (Eq. 11) and its spectral parameters (e.g., delay θ_ij, phase φ_ij) is mathematically precise. However, the paper misses a critical demonstration: unlike Wilson et al. [6] who showcased SM’s extrapolation power for single-output GPs, MOSM’s synthetic experiment (Section 4.1) only shows *interpolation* of derivatives/delays (Fig. 2), not extrapolation beyond the training interval. This omission weakens the claim that MOSM inherits SM’s extrapolation advantages. Experimental results are generally well-executed (TensorFlow/GPflow implementation), but statistical significance is underutilized: Tables 1–2 report standard deviations, yet no formal tests (e.g., t-tests) confirm whether MOSM’s slight MAE advantages (e.g., Jura Cadmium) are meaningful. The climate dataset’s high signal correlation (Section 4.2) likely masks MOSM’s true potential, as all models perform similarly.

**Clarity**  
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. Background on scalar SM kernels (Section 2.1) and MOGP fundamentals (Section 2.2) provide a strong foundation. The spectral density construction (Section 3.1) is logically developed, with helpful parameter interpretation (e.g., delay θ_ij). The transition from complex-valued to real-valued GPs via symmetry (Eq. 10) is elegantly explained. Minor suggestions: a brief intuitive sketch of how delays manifest in covariance plots (beyond Fig. 1) would aid readers, and the rank-Q extension (Section 3.1) could be more explicitly motivated.

**Originality**  
This is a strong contribution. While CSM [5] introduced phase shifts, MOSM is the first to model *delays* (θ_ij ≠ 0) as a natural consequence of complex-valued cross-spectral densities. The extension of SM’s spectral mixture to multi-output via Cramér’s Theorem is novel, and the paper properly situates itself against LMC, CONV, and CSM (Section 3.3). Related work is comprehensive, though it omits recent MOGP kernels like Matérn cross-covariances [17]—a minor gap given the focus on spectral methods.

**Significance**  
The significance is moderate. MOSM’s ability to jointly model delays and phase shifts is theoretically compelling and could benefit applications like sensor fusion (e.g., delayed climate measurements). However, the empirical impact is limited: MOSM outperforms baselines *only* on Jura Cadmium (Table 3, p<0.05) and shows marginal gains elsewhere. Crucially, it does not demonstrate clear superiority on *harder* datasets where prior MOGPs fail (e.g., non-stationary or highly delayed channels), as suggested in the key points. The climate dataset’s similarity (Section 4.2) and Jura’s Copper’s non-Gaussianity (Section 5) limit the narrative of MOSM as a transformative advance. The paper’s strength lies in its principled framework, not a breakthrough application.

**Pro/Con Summary**  
*Pro*:  
- Novel extension of SM to multi-output with interpretable delays/phase shifts.  
- Rigorous theoretical grounding via Cramér’s Theorem.  
- Clear parametric interpretation of all kernel parameters.  
- Competitive MAE on standard datasets (e.g., Jura Cadmium).  

*Con*:  
- Missing extrapolation demo (critical for SM’s appeal).  
- Marginal gains on real-world data; no "killer app" validation.  
- Statistical significance not rigorously established (Tables 1–2).  
- Climate data too correlated to reveal MOSM’s unique value.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a technically solid contribution to MOGP theory with a well-motivated extension of spectral mixture kernels. While the experimental impact is constrained by the datasets used, the theoretical novelty and interpretability are valuable for the field. To strengthen significance, the authors should: (1) add an extrapolation experiment (e.g., forecasting delayed signals beyond training), and (2) test MOSM on a dataset where prior MOGPs fail (e.g., non-stationary delays). As is, the paper is borderline for acceptance: the quality and originality are strong, but the significance is not demonstrably higher than existing methods. Given NIPS’ focus on impactful contributions, I recommend *weak acceptance* with a note that the authors should address extrapolation in revision.
