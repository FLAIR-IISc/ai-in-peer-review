This paper proposes a new multi-output GP kernel, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM), constructed by specifying a parametric family of complex cross-power spectral densities (PSDs) and invoking Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem) to guarantee positive definiteness. By factorizing the cross-PSD matrix as S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), and choosing channel-specific complex squared-exponential factors R_i(ω), the authors obtain closed-form, real-valued cross-covariances via inverse Fourier transform. Each cross-covariance is a spectral mixture with channel-pair-specific magnitude, frequency mean and bandwidth, plus phase and delay terms; autocovariances reduce to standard SM kernels [Wilson & Adams, ICML’13]. The construction strictly generalizes SM-LMC and the Cross Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [Ulrich et al., NIPS’15]: CSM’s shared spectral parameters and pure phase adjustments arise as a special case (no delays; tied means/bandwidths across channels). Experiments include a synthetic trivariate process (reference, derivative, delayed variant) and two standard benchmarks: climate sensor imputation and Swiss Jura heavy-metal concentration prediction. MOSM is competitive overall and shows clear benefits on the synthetic task.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction is principled. Cramér’s theorem ensures that if S(ω) is Hermitian PSD pointwise, the resulting matrix-valued covariance is valid. The rank-1 factorization and use of complex SE functions yield interpretable parameters and analytic inverse Fourier transforms. The symmetrization Sij(ω) ← 0.5(Sij(ω)+Sij(−ω)) properly enforces real-valued processes. The training procedure follows standard GP marginal likelihood optimization.
- Claims vs. evidence: The paper convincingly demonstrates parameter interpretability and the ability to jointly capture phase and delay, which CSM cannot express simultaneously. The synthetic derivative+delay task is well-chosen and highlights a genuine advantage over LMC/CONV/CSM. On climate and Jura, MOSM performs similarly to strong baselines; the authors provide a KS test for one dataset but significance is not uniformly reported across tables.
- Completeness: While the construction is clear, practical aspects could be better covered: initialization of spectral means/bandwidths/phase/delay, identifiability (e.g., delay–phase trade-offs at different frequencies), and computational scaling with number of outputs and components Q.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and mathematically careful; the Fourier-pair relationships and parameter mapping (magnitude, mean, bandwidth, phase, delay) are clearly enumerated.
- Minor suggestions: briefly summarize the computational complexity, parameter counts vs. CSM/SM-LMC, and provide guidance on initialization (e.g., periodogram or cross-periodogram peaks) and constraints for stable optimization.

Originality
- The idea to design multi-output kernels directly in the spectral domain with complex cross-PSDs and to allow channel-specific spectral parameters, phase, and explicit time delays is a meaningful extension of SM to the multi-output setting. It cleanly unifies and generalizes SM-LMC and CSM, offering a more expressive yet interpretable family.
- Related work is appropriately cited (LMC/CONV, CSM, multivariate Matérn, prior spectral GP work).

Significance
- The approach is promising and broadly useful for multi-sensor time series where phase and lag structure matter (e.g., neuroscience, climate networks, seismology, industrial sensing). However, empirical gains on real data are modest relative to CSM/SM-LMC, limiting immediate impact. Demonstrating clear wins on tasks with known inter-channel lags or out-of-phase dynamics would strengthen significance.

Strengths
- Principled construction via Cramér’s theorem with clear positive-definiteness guarantees.
- Closed-form cross-covariances with interpretable parameters (frequency, bandwidth, magnitude, phase, delay).
- Strict generalization of existing spectral multi-output kernels; subsumes CSM and SM-LMC as special cases.
- Strong synthetic validation showing capabilities (derivatives and delays) that LMC/CONV/CSM struggle with.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical advantage on standard real datasets; performance is often similar to CSM/SM-LMC.
- Extrapolation—a hallmark of SM—is not compellingly demonstrated in multi-output settings beyond the synthetic example. Real-world long-range extrapolation with cross-channel structure is missing.
- Statistical significance is reported only in part; Tables 1–2 read as roughly tied given standard deviations.
- Practical guidance is sparse (initialization, identifiability, parameter sensitivity, choice of rank Q).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a challenging multi-output extrapolation task where cross-channel phase/lag structure is crucial (e.g., sensor arrays with known delays, motion capture with joint-dependent lags, multi-lead ECG/EEG).
- Report statistical tests and confidence intervals uniformly across all tables; include effect sizes.
- Provide ablations isolating the roles of delay vs. phase, and of channel-specific vs. tied spectral parameters; study sensitivity to Q.
- Discuss identifiability and optimization: how are delays distinguished from phase across frequencies? How are parameters initialized in practice?
- Include a brief complexity analysis and a note on sparse/variational extensions (the discussion points to viable paths via inducing variables/variational Fourier features).
- Release code and initialization scripts to facilitate reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, theoretically sound extension that unifies and generalizes prior spectral multi-output kernels.
- Adds practically useful expressivity (simultaneous phase and delay) with interpretable parameters.
- Synthetic evidence shows clear capability gaps bridged by MOSM.

Arguments against acceptance
- Real-data advantages over strong baselines are limited; significance unclear in several tables.
- Missing a compelling, real-world extrapolation benchmark showcasing MOSM’s unique strengths.
- Practical aspects (initialization, identifiability, scaling) are under-discussed.

Overall: A solid and well-motivated contribution to multi-output GP kernel design. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can strengthen empirical evidence (especially extrapolation) and provide clearer guidance on practical use; otherwise this is borderline given the modest gains on standard datasets.
