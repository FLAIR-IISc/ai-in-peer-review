Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a new covariance construction for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) derived in the spectral domain via Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner’s theorem). The authors parameterize complex-valued cross-spectral densities through a low-rank factorization, ensuring positive definiteness by construction and yielding closed-form, real-valued cross-covariances after enforcing conjugate symmetry. The resulting kernel generalizes the single-output spectral mixture (SM) kernel and extends recent MOGP spectral constructions (SM-LMC and CSM) by introducing interpretable parameters per output pair: frequency content, amplitudes, phase shifts, and crucially, relative delays. Compared to LMC/CONV, which combine independent latent processes and tend to enforce symmetric, centered cross-covariances with limited interpretability, MOSM directly models cross-spectra and thus affords richer, physically interpretable relationships across channels.

Empirically, MOSM is validated on a synthetic setting (derivatives and delayed outputs), where it recovers known cross-covariances and extrapolates well, and on two real-world datasets (sensor temperatures; heavy metal concentrations), where it is competitive or better than SM-LMC, CSM, and CONV.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A principled, spectral-domain construction for MOGP kernels that subsumes SM-LMC and CSM, and adds delay parameters. The use of Cramér’s theorem with a positive-definite factorization gives a clean route to valid multi-output kernels.
- Interpretability: Parameters correspond to meaningful cross-channel properties (delays, phase differences, frequency means and variances, magnitudes), addressing a key limitation of LMC/CONV.
- Expressivity: The model admits asymmetric and sign-changing cross-covariances, and decouples auto-covariances across channels. The rank-Q extension allows richer cross-spectral structure.
- Theoretical soundness: The construction is mathematically consistent and clearly justified; the connection to Hermitian PSD matrices and inverse Fourier transforms is well explained.
- Empirical support: Strong synthetic results (simultaneously handling derivatives and delays), and solid performance on real data, with statistical testing where applicable.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written, the modeling choices are motivated, and illustrations are relevant.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Parameter learning details are under-specified. Optimizing highly multimodal spectral objectives is notoriously challenging. The paper omits crucial practicalities: initialization of frequencies/length-scales/delays/phases, parameter constraints or reparameterizations, priors/regularization, optimization schedule, and strategies to mitigate local optima or pathological solutions. Without these, reproducibility and robustness are uncertain.
- Identifiability and invariances. Since many cross-parameters appear as differences (e.g., θij = θi − θj, φij = φi − φj), there are global shift/phase invariances that can complicate optimization. Discussion of how these are handled (e.g., anchoring one channel, priors) would help.
- Scalability. Exact training is O(N^3) in the total number of observations across channels. While the discussion briefly mentions sparse extensions, no complexity analysis, runtime, or memory profile is provided. This is important for MOGP adoption.
- Limited ablation. It would be helpful to see sensitivity to the rank Q and to the number of spectral components, and comparisons between rank-1 versus higher-rank MOSM on datasets where multiple cross-spectral modes are expected.
- Some empirical results (climate) do not show clear gains over CSM; a deeper diagnosis of when delays and per-channel parameters matter in practice would strengthen the case.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The theory is sound and the kernel is correctly constructed; experiments are reasonable and include statistical tests. However, missing training details and practical guidance reduce the completeness for practitioners.
- Clarity: Clear and well-structured; figures support the narrative. Minor typos noted below.
- Originality: The approach is a substantive generalization of spectral MOGP kernels, extending prior SM-LMC/CSM with delay parameters and a PSD factorization framing.
- Significance: If training can be made robust, MOSM is likely to become a standard tool for interpretable multi-output GP modeling, particularly where delays and phase relationships are key.

Questions for the authors
- Behavior with few training points: How does MOSM perform in the small-data regime, especially for estimating delays and phases, which can be data hungry? Please include a learning-curve experiment varying the number of training points and the number of channels.
- Correlations across disparate length-scales: When channels have very different Σi, Σj, the induced cross-covariance uses Σij = 2Σi(Σi + Σj)−1Σj. How does this blending behave when one channel is very smooth and the other highly short-scale? Does MOSM avoid spurious high-frequency leakage or over-damping of cross-correlation? An experiment with markedly different length-scales would be instructive.
- Optimization details: How are µ, Σ, θ, φ initialized? Are phases learned freely in (−π, π] or unconstrained? Any constraints or priors to prevent degenerate solutions (e.g., extremely small Σ)? What optimizer, learning rates, early stopping, and restarts were used? Any use of periodograms for initial spectral estimates as in Wilson & Adams?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a section on training: initialization heuristics per channel (e.g., per-output periodograms), constraints/priors, and practical optimization tips; report sensitivity to initialization and the number of restarts.
- Provide complexity and runtime comparisons across models, plus memory usage.
- Include ablations: rank-Q effects, number of mixture components, and an experiment with few training points.
- Add an experiment illustrating channels with substantially different length-scales and/or widely separated frequency means.
- Clarify how cross-negative amplitudes are handled and whether any constraints are needed in practice to ensure numerical stability.
- Synthetic example: please state the exact number of training points per channel and the noise levels used (currently unclear in the manuscript).
- Minor typos: There appears to be a missing word around lines 74 and 140; please proofread.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Principled and general covariance construction for MOGPs in the spectral domain
- Interpretable parameters (delays, phase, frequency, amplitudes)
- Theoretically sound; subsumes prior spectral MOGP methods
- Effective on synthetic and real datasets; clear writing and figures

Cons:
- Insufficient detail on parameter learning and optimization
- Limited analysis of scalability and runtime
- Some empirical gains modest; ablations and small-data behavior not explored

Overall recommendation
A strong and timely contribution to multi-output GP modeling. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on adding practical training guidance and, if possible, a brief small-data and disparate-length-scale study. If optimization is not problematic as claimed, MOSM could become a standard kernel for multi-output GPs.
