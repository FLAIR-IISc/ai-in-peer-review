## Review of "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

This paper presents a novel extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) setting. The core idea is to leverage Cramér’s Theorem to construct a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities, enabling the design of more expressive and interpretable covariance functions for MOGPs. The authors effectively address the challenge of ensuring positive-definiteness while modeling potentially non-positive-definite cross-covariances, a common issue in MOGP design. 

The work builds logically upon prior research, particularly Wilson & Adams (2013) on spectral mixture kernels and earlier approaches to MOGP like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and Convolution models. The paper clearly articulates how the proposed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel extends the capabilities of existing methods, specifically by allowing for modeling of delays and phase differences between channels—features not easily captured by simpler approaches. The theoretical grounding is solid, with clear explanations of the underlying mathematical principles. 

The strengths of this paper are significant. The approach appears novel and technically sound, with a compelling argument for the benefits of spectral domain design in the multi-output context. The writing is excellent; the introduction is well-motivated, and the explanation of the spectral mixture kernel and its extensions is clear and concise. The relationship to prior work is well-explained, and the authors successfully position their contribution within the existing literature.

However, the experiments, while adequate for demonstrating the feasibility of the method, are somewhat limited in their interpretability. The Jura dataset, in particular, is mentioned as being difficult to interpret, and the synthetic example, though informative, doesn’t fully showcase the benefits of the proposed approach in a more complex, real-world scenario. While the synthetic results clearly demonstrate the ability to recover delays and phase shifts, the real-world experiments don’t provide as strong an intuitive grasp of *when* and *why* MOSM outperforms the baseline methods. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP covariance design.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in the field (positive-definite cross-covariances).
*   Extends existing work in a meaningful way.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited interpretability of experimental results.
*   Experiments could benefit from more complex datasets/scenarios to fully showcase the advantages of MOSM.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field. The clarity of the presentation and the novelty of the approach outweigh the limitations of the experiments. I recommend acceptance.
