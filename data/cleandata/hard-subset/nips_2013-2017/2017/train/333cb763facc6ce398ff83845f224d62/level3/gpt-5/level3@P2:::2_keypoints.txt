- Proposes Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernels via complex-valued cross-spectral densities and Cramér’s theorem, yielding interpretable cross-covariances with per-output magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay.  
- Constructs low-rank spectral factorization S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) with Gaussian frequency forms and enforces symmetry to obtain real-valued GPs.  
- MOSM generalizes Wilson & Adams’ SM and strictly contains SM-LMC and CSM, enabling channel-wise spectral differences and explicit delays in addition to phase shifts.  
- The theoretical construction is principled: PSD guaranteed by spectral factorization and symmetrization, inverse Fourier transforms yield closed-form kernels with interpretable parameters.  
- Synthetic experiments convincingly show MOSM can learn cross-covariances involving derivatives and delays that latent-LMC models cannot capture.  
- On real datasets (climate, Swiss Jura), MOSM performs competitively but shows clear gains mainly on the synthetic task and the Jura Cadmium task.  
- The paper does not clearly demonstrate the multi-output extrapolation advantages that made SM compelling in single-output cases.  
- Statistical significance is only partially addressed; overlapping error bars and limited tests weaken empirical claims.  
- Practical aspects (runtime, parameter counts vs. m and Q, optimization stability, initialization) are not discussed and matter for adoption.  
- Paper is generally well written; the connection from Cramér’s theorem to the kernel and parameter interpretations are clear.  
- Suggest adding a derivation sketch from rank-one spectral factors to time-domain kernel and clarifying symmetrization’s effect on αij.  
- Recommend summarizing parameter counts/constraints and commenting on identifiability of phases and delays.  
- Construction appears novel and useful by allowing distinct per-output μi, Σi and explicit θi while ensuring PSD.  
- Conceptually significant for expanding interpretable MOGP kernel design and unifying prior constructions.  
- Practical significance is limited by modest empirical gains on standard benchmarks; clearer wins in delay/phase-critical tasks would increase impact.  
- Pros: principled, general, interpretable, strict generalization of prior kernels, closed-form inference, and strong synthetic evidence.  
- Cons: limited multi-output extrapolation demonstration, incomplete statistical analysis, missing scalability/optimization details, potential overparameterization and identifiability issues.  
- Suggestions: add long-range multi-output forecasting experiments, report training time/memory/parameter counts, describe initialization and priors, include ablation studies tying parameters, perform paired significance tests, and discuss sparse/inducing approximations for scalability.  
- Overall: technically sound and elegant generalization with interpretable parameters and novel delay modeling; acceptance recommended if experiments and analysis are strengthened.
