## Review of Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes

This paper introduces a novel covariance structure for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) based on extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel approach through the application of Cramér’s Theorem. The authors propose a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities, providing a principled way to design multivariate covariance functions that model delays and phase differences between channels, offering improved interpretability compared to existing methods. The method is validated on synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating competitive performance.

The extension of Gaussian Processes to multiple outputs is a challenging problem, requiring careful consideration of the covariance matrix to balance expressiveness and positive definiteness. Early approaches, like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and Convolution models, often lack interpretability. More recent methods, such as the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, improve on this but introduce parameters with limited theoretical justification. This paper addresses these limitations by grounding the design of cross-covariances in a spectral framework, leveraging Bochner’s and Cramér’s Theorems to ensure positive definiteness and provide a clear connection between kernel parameters and spectral characteristics. This builds upon the successful application of spectral methods in single-output GP kernels [6] and provides a valuable contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is well-grounded in theoretical principles, utilizing Cramér’s Theorem to provide a robust framework for constructing positive-definite multivariate covariance functions. The connection to Bochner’s Theorem and spectral density estimation is clearly explained.
* **Interpretability:** The proposed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel offers a significant advantage in terms of parameter interpretability. The parameters directly relate to spectral properties like frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay, allowing for a more intuitive understanding of the relationships between output channels.
* **Generality & Flexibility:** The MOSM kernel generalizes previous approaches, including the CSM kernel [5], by allowing for more flexible modeling of cross-covariances, specifically the incorporation of delays and phase differences.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The synthetic example convincingly shows the ability to recover known cross-covariances, and the performance on climate and metal concentration data is competitive with existing state-of-the-art methods.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise yet informative. The illustrations, particularly in Section 4.1, effectively convey the key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Learning Detail:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the parameter learning process. While the authors mention using TensorFlow and GPflow, the optimization challenges, sensitivity to initialisation, and computational cost associated with learning the increased number of parameters are not adequately addressed. This is a potential concern, as the success of the method hinges on efficient and reliable parameter estimation.
* **Scalability/Few-Shot Learning:** The behavior of the method with limited training data is not explored. MOGPs, in general, can be data-hungry, and the increased complexity of the MOSM kernel might exacerbate this issue. Understanding its performance in low-data regimes is crucial for practical application.
* **Handling Varying Length-Scales:** The paper does not explicitly address how the model handles scenarios where different output channels exhibit vastly different length-scales. It would be beneficial to discuss this aspect and potentially provide insights into how the spectral mixture components can adapt to capture such differences.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP covariance function design.
* The proposed MOSM kernel offers significant advantages in terms of interpretability and flexibility compared to existing methods.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on diverse datasets.
* The work addresses a challenging problem in the field of Gaussian processes and has the potential to become a standard approach for multi-output modeling.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed discussion on parameter learning and potential optimization challenges is a concern.
* The scalability and performance with limited training data remain unexplored.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. The proposed MOSM kernel is theoretically well-motivated, interpretable, and empirically validated. While the lack of detail regarding parameter learning and scalability is a weakness, it does not outweigh the overall strengths of the work.  The question of parameter learning is crucial; if it is shown to be tractable (even with approximations), this method has a good chance of widespread adoption.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** The authors should address the following points in a revised version:

1.  Provide a more detailed description of the parameter learning process, including the optimization algorithm used, the computational cost, and any techniques employed to address potential optimization challenges.
2.  Include a discussion of the method’s behavior with limited training data, potentially through experiments with varying dataset sizes.
3.  Address how the model handles scenarios where different output channels exhibit vastly different length-scales.
4.  Clarify the number of training points used in the synthetic example (Section 4.1).
5. Correct the minor typos identified in lines 74 and 140.
