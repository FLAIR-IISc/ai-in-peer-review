## Review of "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

This paper proposes a novel approach to constructing multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) covariance functions by extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel concept from single-output GPs. The core idea is to define cross-spectral densities and leverage Cramér’s Theorem to obtain valid multivariate covariance functions. This allows for modeling delays and phase differences between outputs, offering increased expressiveness compared to existing methods.

The work builds upon the well-established framework of GPs and the SM kernel [6], addressing a key limitation of earlier MOGP approaches – the lack of interpretable parameters in cross-covariance functions. The authors clearly articulate the motivation and connect their work to existing literature, including the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The theoretical foundation based on Cramér’s Theorem is sound and provides a principled way to construct the proposed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. 

A significant strength of this paper is the clever extension of the spectral mixture idea to the multi-output setting. The ability to model delays and phase differences is a valuable addition to the toolkit of MOGP covariance functions. The experiments on a synthetic dataset effectively demonstrate the ability of the MOSM kernel to capture these relationships. The application to real-world datasets (weather and metal concentration) provides a practical evaluation, although the performance gains are modest.

However, a key weakness is the lack of a clear demonstration of the extrapolation ability analogous to that shown in [6] for single-output GPs. While the synthetic example showcases learning of relationships, it doesn't explicitly highlight extrapolation. Furthermore, the experimental results, while reasonable, don’t show a substantial improvement over existing methods. The reported standard deviations in Tables 1 and 2 suggest that the differences in performance between models are often not statistically significant, raising questions about the practical benefit of the proposed approach. The impact could be significantly increased by evaluating the MOSM kernel on datasets where existing models demonstrably struggle.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled extension of the spectral mixture kernel to multi-output GPs.
*   Clear theoretical foundation based on Cramér’s Theorem.
*   Potential for increased expressiveness through modeling of delays and phase differences.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of demonstration of extrapolation ability.
*   Modest performance gains on real-world datasets with questionable statistical significance.
*   Limited exploration of datasets where existing models perform poorly.



**Overall:** This paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of MOGPs. While the experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing, the theoretical novelty and potential for future development warrant consideration. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the extrapolation ability and providing a more thorough statistical analysis of the experimental results.
