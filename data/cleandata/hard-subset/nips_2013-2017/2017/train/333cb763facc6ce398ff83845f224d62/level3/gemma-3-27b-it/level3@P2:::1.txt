This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel approach to designing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) directly in the spectral domain. The authors leverage Cramér’s Theorem to construct a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities, enabling the modeling of delays and phase differences between channels – capabilities lacking in previous methods like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015]. The work builds upon the successful spectral mixture kernel for single-output GPs [Wilson & Adams, ICML 2013] and addresses the critical challenge of ensuring positive definiteness while maintaining expressive power in cross-covariance modeling.

The paper begins with a comprehensive introduction to MOGPs and the difficulties in designing appropriate covariance functions, particularly cross-covariances. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of classical approaches like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and the Convolution model (CONV) [Alvarez & Lawrence, NeurIPS 2008], which often lack interpretability and impose constraints on auto-covariances. They position their work as an extension of the CSM kernel, aiming for greater flexibility and a more principled approach based on spectral design. The theoretical foundation, utilizing Bochner’s and Cramér’s Theorems, is presented meticulously and convincingly. The derivation of the MOSM kernel, along with the explanation of its parameters in spectral terms, is clear and well-motivated.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the novelty of the proposed approach is significant. Extending the spectral mixture concept to multiple outputs with the ability to model delays and phase shifts is a valuable contribution to the field of Gaussian process modeling. Second, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough background on relevant concepts and seamlessly integrate theoretical explanations with practical considerations. The connection to existing work is explicitly stated, and the limitations of previous methods are effectively highlighted. Third, the theoretical justification based on Cramér’s Theorem is robust and ensures the validity of the proposed kernel. The parameter interpretation in the frequency domain adds to the practical usefulness of the model.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the applicability of the MOSM kernel, could be more insightful. The synthetic example effectively showcases the ability to recover known relationships but feels somewhat contrived. The real-world experiments on climate data and heavy metal concentrations, while using established datasets, don’t provide particularly compelling evidence of a substantial advantage over existing methods. The Jura dataset, in particular, is difficult to interpret, and the reported performance improvements are not always statistically significant. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed analysis of why MOSM performs well in specific scenarios (e.g., Cadmium concentration) but not in others (e.g., Copper concentration) would strengthen the paper. The lack of a clear ablation study, demonstrating the contribution of each component of the MOSM kernel (delay, phase shift, etc.), is also a minor drawback.  A deeper investigation into computational complexity and scalability, especially for high-dimensional output spaces, would also be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP covariance design.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method and its theoretical foundations.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in MOGP modeling: flexible and interpretable cross-covariance functions.
*   Builds upon established spectral mixture techniques, extending their applicability to multi-output scenarios.
*   Demonstrates the feasibility and potential of the proposed kernel through experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental results, while positive, are not overwhelmingly convincing and lack strong statistical significance in some cases.
*   Limited insights from the real-world experiments due to the inherent complexity of the datasets.
*   Absence of an ablation study to isolate the contribution of different kernel components.
*   Lack of discussion regarding computational complexity and scalability.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses in the experimental evaluation, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of Gaussian process modeling. The theoretical novelty, combined with the clarity of presentation and the potential for practical applications, makes it a strong candidate for acceptance to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance** with the suggestion that the authors consider expanding the experimental analysis to include a more comprehensive evaluation of the kernel’s performance and computational characteristics. Specifically, exploring different datasets and conducting an ablation study would further strengthen the paper’s conclusions.
