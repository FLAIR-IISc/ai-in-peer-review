- Introduces MOSM: a new covariance for MOGPs via complex cross-spectral density and Cramér’s theorem, generalizing SM to multi-output and subsuming LMC/SM-LMC/CSM while adding per-pair delay and phase.  
- Theoretical soundness: PSD factorization ensures Hermitian PSD matrices and valid kernels; closed-form inverse Fourier transforms give practical kernels.  
- Empirical performance: strong on a synthetic tri-variate task and two real datasets (climate sensors, Jura), but parameter-learning details are limited.  
- Optimization/identifiability concern: non-convex objective and large parameter space (per-output means, variances, phases, delays, Q) may cause problematic optimization and identifiability.  
- Requests for clarification: specify initialization (e.g., periodogram, CSM warm-start), constraints/priors, regularization, training times, and hyperparameter counts.  
- Ambiguity in R(ω) factorization: need explanation of scale/phase ambiguity and how degenerate solutions are avoided.  
- Clarity assessment: paper is clearly written with relevant illustrations and a good relation to CSM/SM-LMC.  
- Minor presentation issues: missing words around lines 74 and 140; state number of training points per channel in the synthetic example.  
- Originality: high — principled, novel use of complex cross-spectrum with explicit phase and delay per pair.  
- Significance: high potential to become a standard MOGP tool if training is robust; a sparse/variational extension would increase impact.  
- Suggested experiment: include a low-N study and/or informative priors on delays/phases to assess performance with few training points.  
- Suggested experiment: test handling of channels with very different length-scales (ablation varying Σi across outputs).  
- Model selection guidance: report sensitivity to Q and provide practical guidance for choosing Q.  
- Suggested comparison: evaluate against scalable variational MOGP methods on larger datasets.  
- Pros summary: principled, interpretable, expressive cross-covariances; solid theory; strong synthetic and competitive real-data results; clear writing and figures.  
- Cons summary: limited guidance on optimization/identifiability and scalability; missing low-data and disparate-length-scale analyses; minor presentation issues.
