- Paper extends spectral mixture kernels to multi-output GPs by designing complex-valued cross-spectral densities and deriving the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel via Cramér’s Theorem.  
- Key innovation: models both delays (via phase shifts with nonzero delay parameters) and frequency-dependent phase differences across channels, unlike prior CSM which only uses phase shifts.  
- MOSM offers full parametric interpretability (frequency, magnitude, delay, phase) and enforces positive definiteness through a low-rank spectral density decomposition.  
- Theoretical foundation is sound and derivations (e.g., Eq. 11, parameter interpretations) are mathematically precise.  
- Experimental evaluation on synthetic derivative/delayed signals, climate sensor networks, and the Jura heavy-metal dataset, comparing to SM-LMC, CONV, and CSM.  
- Major omission: no extrapolation experiments to demonstrate MOSM inherits SM’s extrapolation strengths—synthetic tests only show interpolation.  
- Statistical analysis is weak: Tables report std. devs but lack formal significance tests to confirm MAE improvements.  
- Climate dataset’s high inter-signal correlation likely masks MOSM’s advantages, leading to similar performance across models.  
- Empirical gains are limited—MOSM significantly outperforms baselines mainly on Jura Cadmium, with marginal or no gains elsewhere.  
- Clarity and organization are excellent; background and spectral construction are well explained and transitions to real-valued GPs are clear.  
- Minor clarity suggestions: add intuitive covariance plots illustrating delays and better motivate the rank-Q extension.  
- Originality is strong: first principled MOSM modeling of delays via complex cross-spectra and proper positioning against LMC, CONV, and CSM.  
- Significance is moderate: theoretically valuable but lacking compelling empirical demonstrations on challenging datasets or “killer” applications.  
- Recommendation: weak accept, with required revisions to add extrapolation experiments and tests on harder/non-stationary or highly-delayed datasets to bolster empirical impact.
