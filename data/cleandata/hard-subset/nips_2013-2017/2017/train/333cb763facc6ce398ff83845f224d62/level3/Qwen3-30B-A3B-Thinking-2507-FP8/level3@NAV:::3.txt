

This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), extending the spectral mixture approach to the multi-output setting through Cramér's theorem. The work builds upon previous MOGP methods like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, which lack the ability to model delays between channels while maintaining interpretable parameters. The MOSM kernel provides a principled framework for designing cross-covariance functions by constructing complex-valued cross-spectral densities, enabling clear parametric interpretation of delays, phase differences, and other spectral characteristics across channels.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a solid theoretical foundation and effective experimental validation. The authors show that MOSM successfully learns complex relationships in synthetic data (including derivative and delayed signals) and achieves competitive results on two real-world datasets (climate signals and heavy metal concentrations). The paper is clearly written with relevant illustrations and well-organized presentation.

Strengths include the novel theoretical contribution that generalizes spectral methods to MOGPs, the interpretable parameterization (delays, phase shifts), and the thorough experimental validation. The work addresses a significant challenge in MOGP literature where few methods provide both expressiveness and interpretability.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. It lacks sufficient detail on parameter learning and optimization challenges, which is concerning given the complexity of the model. The authors do not address how the method behaves with few training points (a critical practical consideration) or how it handles channels with substantially different length-scales. The synthetic example (Section 4.1) omits the number of training points, which is essential for reproducibility. Minor grammatical issues are present in lines 74 and 140.

The significance of this work is high as it provides a more expressive and interpretable framework for MOGPs that could become standard if parameter learning proves tractable. While the theoretical foundation is strong and the method shows promise, the lack of detail on optimization and the unanswered questions about performance under data scarcity represent important limitations that should be addressed in future work. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, though some practical concerns remain unresolved.
