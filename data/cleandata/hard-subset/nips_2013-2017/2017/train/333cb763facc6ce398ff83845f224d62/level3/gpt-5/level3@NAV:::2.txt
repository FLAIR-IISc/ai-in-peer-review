This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a principled construction of multi-output GP covariances obtained by specifying complex cross-power spectral densities (PSDs) and invoking Cramér’s theorem to guarantee positive definiteness, followed by inverse Fourier transforms to yield closed-form cross-covariances. The construction factorizes the cross-PSD matrix as S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), enabling interpretable per-pair parameters for magnitude, frequency, phase, and delay, and strictly generalizes SM-LMC and CSM. This extends the single-output spectral mixture idea of Wilson & Adams [6] to the multi-output setting in a natural and elegant way. Experiments include a synthetic tri-variate signal (reference, derivative, delayed copy), a four-station weather dataset, and the Swiss Jura metal-concentration dataset.

Quality: The theoretical development is sound and clearly grounded in Cramér’s theorem; the factorization-based PSD design is a clean sufficient condition for positive definiteness. The resulting kernel is in closed form and interpretable. Empirically, results on standard benchmarks are competitive with SM-LMC/CSM/CONV, and the synthetic study nicely illustrates the ability to recover delays and derivative relationships. However, the empirical support is limited: Tables 1–2 do not establish statistical significance (the reported standard deviations suggest overlapping performance), and ablations are missing (e.g., effect of rank Q, number of components, initialization sensitivity, and computational scaling).

Clarity: Overall well written, but dense. It would help to streamline Section 3, move some derivations to an appendix, clarify the symmetrization for real-valued processes, and provide guidance on initialization (e.g., cross-periodograms) and hyperparameter constraints. Reproducibility would benefit from more training details and code.

Originality: Moderate but meaningful. While related to CSM and SM-LMC, the complex cross-PSD parameterization with explicit delays and per-pair parameters is a nontrivial and useful generalization.

Significance: Potentially impactful for multi-channel systems with delays/phase shifts; currently, gains on common datasets are modest. Demonstrating multi-output extrapolation analogous to [6] on real data and adding tasks where LMC/CSM struggle (e.g., phase-shifted sensor networks, EEG/ECG, audio, power grids) would considerably strengthen impact.

Pros:
- Elegant, principled construction; interpretable parameters; clear generalization of prior work.
- Closed-form kernels; compelling synthetic validation.

Cons:
- Limited empirical advantage on standard datasets; unclear significance in Tables 1–2.
- Missing ablations, scalability analysis, and multi-output extrapolation demos on real data.

Overall: A clever and promising kernel; borderline for acceptance pending stronger empirical evidence and statistical analysis.
