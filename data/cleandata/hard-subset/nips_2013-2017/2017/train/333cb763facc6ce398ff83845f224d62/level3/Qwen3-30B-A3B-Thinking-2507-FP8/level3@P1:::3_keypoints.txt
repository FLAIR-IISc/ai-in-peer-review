- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output GPs, extending spectral mixture methods to multi-output settings.  
- Uses Cramér's Theorem to design matrix-valued covariance functions by modeling complex-valued cross-spectral densities.  
- Generalizes prior work (e.g., CSM) by incorporating inter-channel delays and phase differences for richer cross-covariance interpretation.  
- Extends the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to multi-output GPs, addressing interpretability gaps in existing MOGP methods (LMC, CONV).  
- Theoretical foundation is sound, with careful derivation from Bochner’s to Cramér’s Theorem.  
- MOSM yields interpretable parameters for delays, phase shifts, and spectral magnitudes that satisfy positive-definiteness.  
- Synthetic experiment (Sec. 4.1) successfully recovers known covariances (derivative and delayed signals) without prior relational knowledge.  
- Real-world experiments (climate and metal concentration) show competitive performance, with statistically significant improvement for Cadmium prediction.  
- Paper is clearly written with effective illustrations demonstrating model capabilities.  
- Originality and significance are high: addresses a fundamental MOGP challenge and could become a standard for interpretable cross-covariances.  
- Experimental validation is comprehensive but lacks detail on the training/optimization procedure (convergence, challenges, computational cost).  
- Synthetic experiment omitted number of training points in the text (500, 400, 400 were used), hindering assessment of sample efficiency.  
- The paper does not analyze behavior when channels have vastly different length-scales, an important real-world scenario.  
- Jura dataset result: SM-LMC outperformed others on non-Gaussian Copper data, suggesting limits in some cases.  
- Minor typos noted (lines 74, 140); discussion appropriately lists limitations and future work (e.g., sparse implementations).  
- Could strengthen claims by explicitly comparing MOSM’s interpretability to alternatives and discussing delay-based causal inference.  
- Overall recommendation: strong theoretical and practical contribution; accept with minor revisions to clarify training details and experimental setup.
