- Proposes MOSM: a principled, interpretable family of multi-output GP kernels built in the spectral domain using Cramér’s theorem.
- Factorizes cross-spectral density as S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) with each column Ri(ω) a complex Gaussian having channel-specific mean, covariance, magnitude, phase, and delay.
- Enforces spectral symmetry to ensure real-valued processes and obtains closed-form time-domain cross-covariances: Gaussian envelope times cosine with explicit phase shifts and delays.
- MOSM strictly generalizes prior spectral mixture models (SM, SM-LMC, CSM) and recovers them as special cases.
- Decouples per-channel auto-covariances and permits non-symmetric, delayed, and signed cross-covariances with clear spectral interpretation.
- Training via standard GP marginal likelihood over spectral parameters; prediction by standard MOGP conditioning.
- Synthetic experiment (reference, derivative, delayed signals) convincingly shows MOSM uniquely extrapolates derivative and delayed channels.
- Real-data experiments (climate sensors with failures, Jura heavy metals) show competitive or best results on some targets (e.g., Cadmium).
- Technical construction is sound: pointwise PSD guaranteed by factorization and symmetrization; inverse Fourier transforms are closed-form; rank-Q extension is straightforward.
- Empirical section is compact: reasonable baselines and statistical tests included but limited intuition about learned parameters and practical guidance.
- Computational complexity remains cubic (O(N^3)) with dense kernels; no scalable inference variant provided.
- Identifiability and sensitivity to initialization (common in SM kernels) are not deeply addressed; rank-1 vs higher-rank trade-offs not ablated.
- Paper is clearly written with strong parameter interpretation (frequency, bandwidth, magnitude, phase, delay) per output pair.
- Suggested clarity improvements: add computational complexity subsection, detail parameter constraints/initialization, visualize learned spectra/delays/phases, and discuss identifiability.
- The factorized complex-spectral approach is novel and more general than prior handcrafted phase approaches like CSM.
- Kernel is significant for modeling multivariate stationary processes, offering interpretable expressiveness useful in time series, sensor fusion, and spatiotemporal tasks.
- Pros: principled PSD-guaranteed construction, closed-form kernels, interpretable parameters, generalizes prior models, strong synthetic results.
- Cons: limited empirical scope and ablations, no scalable inference, potential identifiability/optimization issues, assumes stationarity, Jura results lack interpretability.
- Suggested improvements: visualize learned parameters, discuss initialization/priors and sensitivity, ablate rank Q and component counts, compare to multivariate Matérn, explore scalable variational/Fourier variants, and discuss delay vs phase identifiability.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — novel, principled, and useful kernel with strong theory and competitive results but needs expanded empirical analysis and scalability work.
