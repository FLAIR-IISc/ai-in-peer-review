- Introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending single-output SM to multi-output GPs via Cramér's theorem.  
- Key innovation: complex-valued cross-spectral densities that model delays and phase differences between channels.  
- Provides interpretable parameters (delays, phase differences) and theoretical positive-definiteness via spectral factorization.  
- Derives closed-form covariance functions enabling practical implementation.  
- Clearly positions method relative to LMC, CONV, and CSM, highlighting their limitations.  
- Strong theoretical foundation with sound motivation and connections to prior work.  
- Experimental validation: synthetic example recovers derivative and delayed signals without prior relationships.  
- Experimental validation: real-world results on climate and heavy metal data show competitive performance.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and well-illustrated (notably Figure 1).  
- Major weakness: insufficient detail on parameter learning and optimization (NLL given but no discussion of optimization challenges, local minima, or complexity).  
- Unaddressed questions: performance with limited training data and handling channels with very different length-scales.  
- Synthetic example lacks crucial details (number of training points) needed to assess performance under different data regimes.  
- Minor typographical errors noted (lines 74 and 140).  
- Assessment by criteria: Quality—technically sound but weakened by missing optimization details; Clarity—excellent; Originality—significant contribution; Significance—high if parameter learning is manageable.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions to address parameter learning, optimization strategies, convergence, and computational complexity.
