- Proposes MOSM: a new family of multi-output GP kernels built by designing complex-valued cross-PSDs and applying Cramér’s theorem.
- Factorizes cross-spectral matrix S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) and uses complex SE factors to obtain closed-form cross-covariances.
- Kernel yields interpretable pairwise parameters: magnitude, frequency, phase, and delay; symmetry ensures real-valued processes.
- MOSM principledly extends single-output spectral mixture (SM) to multi-output, subsuming SM-LMC and CSM as special cases.
- Experiments: synthetic 3-output (signal, derivative, delayed), 4-station weather with sensor failures, and Swiss Jura metal concentrations.
- Synthetic results convincingly recover auto- and cross-covariances and demonstrate simultaneous extrapolation of derivative and delay.
- Real-data results are competitive with SM-LMC, CSM, and CONV but improvements are modest and often statistically insignificant.
- Construction and theory are technically sound; using Cramér’s theorem and PSD factorization is correct and clean.
- Complex SE parameterization produces closed-form, interpretable kernels; symmetry trick is standard and appropriate.
- Model is flexible (output-specific spectra, phases, delays), but identifiability and parameter coupling issues are under-discussed.
- Initialization, optimization stability, and how redundancies across weights/phases/delays are handled need more clarification.
- Rank-Q extension is natural, but empirical ablations varying Q are missing and would clarify when higher rank helps.
- Training uses exact GP inference; scalability, runtime, and sparse/inducing alternatives are not addressed.
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful parameter interpretations and derivations.
- Suggested clarity improvements: detail training/initialization, constraints/regularization, choice of statistical tests, and complexity/parameter counts.
- Originality is strong: clever extension using complex cross-PSDs to enable learnable inter-channel delays and phases.
- Conceptual significance is high for applications needing phase/delay modeling (sensor networks, neuroscience, geostatistics).
- Empirical significance is currently modest; stronger demonstrations (long-horizon extrapolation, delay-rich real data) would help.
- Pros: principled formulation, interpretable parameters, closed-form cross-covariances, strong synthetic demo, competitive real performance.
- Cons: limited empirical advantage on benchmarks, missing multi-output extrapolation study, insufficient identifiability/initialization discussion, no scalability solution.
- Suggestions: add long-horizon/multi-output extrapolation tasks, datasets with known delays, ablations (delays vs phases vs spectra vs Q), report runtimes/parameter counts, and explore sparse/inducing methods.
- Overall: technically sound and conceptually strong with promising potential; reviewer leans toward accept if empirical evidence is strengthened.
