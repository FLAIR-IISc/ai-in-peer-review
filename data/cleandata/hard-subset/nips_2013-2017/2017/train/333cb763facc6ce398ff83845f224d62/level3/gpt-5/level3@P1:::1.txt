Summary
This paper proposes a principled and interpretable family of covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) constructed in the spectral domain. Building on Bochner’s theorem and its multivariate counterpart (Cramér’s theorem), the authors design complex-valued cross-spectral densities whose inverse Fourier transforms yield a Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The key idea is to factor the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) as R(ω)H R(ω), ensure Hermitian positive semidefiniteness at every ω, and then choose each column function Ri(ω) to be a complex Gaussian with channel-specific mean, covariance, magnitude, phase, and delay. After enforcing spectral symmetry to recover real-valued processes, the resulting time-domain cross-covariances have closed form: a Gaussian envelope times a cosine, with channel-pair–specific parameters, including explicit phase shifts and delays.

The MOSM kernel strictly generalizes prior spectral mixture approaches: it contains SM (Wilson & Adams, ICML’13) as the single-output special case; SM-LMC and CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS’15) arise when spectral means, covariances, and delays are tied across channels. Unlike LMC/CONV models that impose symmetric, centered cross-covariances and tend to share marginal behavior across outputs, MOSM decouples auto-covariances per channel and allows non-symmetric, delayed, and signed cross-covariances with clear spectral interpretation. Training proceeds by standard GP marginal likelihood optimization over spectral parameters plus noise terms; prediction follows standard MOGP conditioning.

Experiments include: (i) a synthetic 3-output scenario (reference, derivative, and delayed signals), where the ground-truth cross-covariances are known and MOSM is uniquely able to extrapolate both derivative and delayed channels; (ii) climate sensor data with simulated sensor failures, where MOSM performs competitively with SM-LMC/CSM/CONV; and (iii) the Jura geostatistical dataset for heavy metal concentrations, where MOSM provides the best results on Cadmium and competitive results on Copper. The authors also report KS tests indicating statistical significance in some climate cases.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction is well-justified. The factorization S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) guarantees positive semidefiniteness pointwise in ω, and the symmetrization step ensures real-valued processes. The inverse Fourier transforms are in closed form, and the rank-Q extension is straightforward. The connection to Cramér’s theorem is correct and crisply employed.
- Empirical support: The synthetic experiment convincingly demonstrates capabilities (simultaneous phase and delay) that LMC-style models lack. The real-data comparisons are reasonable baselines (CONV, SM-LMC, CSM) with matched numbers of components. Statistical tests are a welcome addition. That said, the empirical section is compact and provides limited intuition about learned parameters (e.g., recovered delays/phases on real data) and practical guidance (initialization, identifiability, and optimization behavior).
- Limitations not fully explored: Computational complexity remains O(N^3) with dense kernels; the paper discusses future sparse/variational Fourier directions but does not provide a scalable variant. Identifiability and sensitivity to initialization (well-known in SM kernels) are not deeply addressed. The rank-1 vs higher-rank choices in R(ω) and their trade-offs are not ablated.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, with a helpful recap of SM kernels and a precise presentation of the multi-output extension. The parameter interpretation (magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, delay, phase) for each output pair is a strength. The exposition of the factorization and symmetry constraints is clear.
- Suggestions: Add a short computational complexity subsection; include details on parameter constraints/priors and initialization strategy (e.g., periodogram-based initialization) that practitioners can follow; visualize learned spectra/delays/phases on the real datasets; briefly discuss identifiability (e.g., trade-offs between phase and delay).

Originality
- The extension from scalar SM to a multi-output kernel via complex cross-spectra and Cramér’s theorem is novel and elegant. While prior work (CSM) introduced phase via a handcrafted shift in the SM cross-spectrum, this paper provides a more general, factorized spectral construction that supports both phase and delay with channel-specific parameters and recovers previous models as special cases.
- Related work is appropriately covered (LMC/ICM/CONV, CSM, Matérn cross-covariances), and the distinction from CSM is made precise.

Significance
- The kernel offers an interpretable and more expressive family for multivariate stationary processes, likely to be useful in time series modeling, sensor fusion, and spatiotemporal tasks. The ability to capture both delays and phase shifts is practically important and uncommon in MOGP kernels.
- Empirical gains are clear in the synthetic setting and in one real dataset (Cadmium), with competitive results elsewhere. Broader demonstration and scalability would further increase impact.

Pros
- Principled spectral-domain construction with guaranteed PSD and closed-form time-domain kernels.
- Clear parameter interpretability (frequency, bandwidth, magnitude, phase, delay) per output pair.
- Strict generalization of SM/SM-LMC/CSM; recovers them as special cases.
- Strong synthetic results on delayed and derivative channels; competitive real-data performance.
- Well-written and well-situated within MOGP literature.

Cons
- Limited empirical scope and insight: few datasets, sparse ablations, and little analysis of learned cross-channel structure on real data.
- No scalable inference; cubic costs limit applicability to large datasets.
- Potential identifiability and optimization challenges not discussed; initialization strategy unclear.
- Assumes stationarity; nonstationary multi-output settings are not addressed.
- Jura results are hard to interpret; domain insight and visualization would help.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide qualitative visualizations of learned spectra, delays, and phases on the climate and Jura datasets.
- Discuss initialization, priors/constraints, and sensitivity; add an ablation on rank Q and number of components.
- Include comparisons to multivariate Matérn cross-covariances or other structured cross-kernels.
- Explore a scalable variant (e.g., variational Fourier features or inducing spectral features) and report timing/memory.
- Add a short discussion on identifiability of delay vs phase and guidelines for practitioners.

Recommendation
- Overall: Weak accept. The paper offers a principled and broadly useful kernel construction with clear novelty over prior spectral MOGP work, strong theory, and competitive empirical results. Expanding empirical analysis and addressing scalability would strengthen the contribution further.
