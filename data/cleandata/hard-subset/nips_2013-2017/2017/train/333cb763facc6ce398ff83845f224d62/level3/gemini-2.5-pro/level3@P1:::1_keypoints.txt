- Introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel: a multivariate extension of the SM kernel using a parametric cross-spectral density matrix.  
- Uses Cramér's theorem to ensure the inverse Fourier transform yields a valid, positive-definite matrix-valued covariance.  
- Derives MOSM via an elegant rank-one factorization of the cross-spectral density; derivation is clear and principled.  
- MOSM can model magnitude, phase differences, and explicit time delays between output channels, unlike prior kernels such as CSM.  
- Synthetic experiment convincingly shows MOSM jointly modeling a function, its derivative, and a delayed version—tasks where latent-process models struggle.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-supported by theory and experiments, and honestly assesses limitations.  
- Real-world results are competitive but do not consistently outperform CSM, especially on the climate dataset with highly similar signals.  
- Writing and organization are excellent; background on SM and Cramér's theorem and the derivation are easy to follow.  
- Figures (notably Figure 1) effectively illustrate the roles of delay and phase parameters.  
- The approach is highly original, using complex-valued spectral factorization to explicitly model delays via θ_ij.  
- MOSM offers a more general and interpretable spectral-domain framework compared to prior MOGP approaches.  
- The contribution is significant for GP modeling, enabling inference of delays, periodicities, and phase shifts relevant to many domains.  
- Main weakness: lack of a clear real-world "killer application" demonstrating decisive practical advantage over simpler models.  
- Experiments on Jura and climate datasets produce mixed or similar performance, leaving practical impact somewhat uncertain.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled, novel extension; expressive delay modeling; solid theoretical foundation; convincing synthetic demonstration.  
- Con for acceptance: empirical validation on real datasets does not yet conclusively show superiority over state-of-the-art.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with important theoretical contribution and clear write-up; recommended for acceptance.
