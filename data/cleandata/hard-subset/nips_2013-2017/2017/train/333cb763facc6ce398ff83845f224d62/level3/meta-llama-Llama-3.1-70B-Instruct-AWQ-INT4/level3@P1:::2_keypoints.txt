- Proposes MOSM kernel extending spectral mixture to multi-output by specifying cross-power spectral densities and using inverse Fourier transform to model delays and phase differences.
- Method is clever and well-motivated, with a thorough theoretical background and clear construction of the MOSM kernel.
- Experiments on synthetic, weather, and Swiss Jura datasets show MOSM performs competitively with LMC, CONV, and CSM kernels.
- Does not demonstrate extrapolation ability, a key feature of spectral-mixture approaches.
- Experimental evaluation uses common datasets and does not highlight scenarios where MOSM outperforms other models.
- Results in Tables 1 and 2 lack statistical-significance analysis; reported standard deviations suggest similar performance across models.
- Quality: 8/10 — technically sound and well-motivated but limited experimental validation and missing extrapolation demonstration.
- Clarity: 9/10 — well-written with thorough theory, though some mathematical derivations could be clearer.
- Originality: 8/10 — novel extension to multi-output, but the use of cross-power spectral densities is not entirely new.
- Significance: 7/10 — valuable contribution but impact could be increased with more challenging datasets and extrapolation results.
- Arguments for acceptance: proposes a novel promising method, provides solid theoretical development, and shows competitive empirical performance.
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks extrapolation demonstration, uses standard datasets that don’t show clear advantages, and omits statistical significance of results.
