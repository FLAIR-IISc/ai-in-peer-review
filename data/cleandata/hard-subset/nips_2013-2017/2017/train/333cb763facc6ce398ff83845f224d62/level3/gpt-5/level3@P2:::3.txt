Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The key idea is to design the cross-spectral density matrix S(ω) directly in the Fourier domain using Cramér’s theorem (multivariate Bochner), ensuring positive definiteness by a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω). Each column of R(ω) is modeled as a complex Gaussian (square-exponential) with channel-specific magnitude, frequency mean, spectral covariance, phase, and delay. After symmetrization to ensure real-valued processes, the inverse Fourier transform yields closed-form cross-covariances that generalize spectral mixture (SM) kernels to the multi-output case. MOSM includes interpretable parameters for inter-channel phase and delay, and allows different auto-spectral properties per channel.

The paper positions MOSM relative to:
- LMC/CONV models (Álvarez & Lawrence, NIPS 2008; Álvarez et al., AISTATS 2010), which build cross-covariances via linear mixing or convolution of latent GPs, but often tie auto-covariances across channels and offer limited spectral interpretability.
- SM-LMC and CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015), which use SM kernels and introduce complex cross-spectra to capture phase. The authors show MOSM subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases, while adding delays and allowing channel-specific spectral parameters.

Empirically, MOSM is validated on a trivariate synthetic example (reference, derivative, and delayed signal), climate station data with simulated sensor failures, and the Jura heavy metal dataset. Results show competitive or superior MAE, with particular strength on the synthetic task and Cadmium prediction.

Strengths
- Principled derivation: solid theoretical grounding via Cramér’s theorem and a constructive PSD factorization that guarantees positive definiteness.
- Interpretability: parameters correspond to frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay; cross-covariances can be non-symmetric and model negative correlations.
- Expressiveness: allows differing auto-spectral content across channels; generalizes prior SM-based MOGP kernels (SM-LMC, CSM).
- Clear writing and relevant illustrations; experiments include both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- Potential impact: if optimization is tractable, this could become a standard multi-output kernel family.

Weaknesses
- Optimization details are sparse. SM-style models can be sensitive to initialization and prone to local optima; the paper does not discuss initialization strategies, priors/constraints, or identifiability issues among per-channel parameters (µi, Σi, θi, φi).
- Scalability: complexity and memory growth with the total number of observations across all channels (and with m) are not discussed; guidance on sparse/inducing variants is deferred to future work.
- Evaluation breadth: only MAE is reported. Predictive log-likelihood and calibration would better assess probabilistic quality. An ablation isolating the benefits of delays/phases (e.g., MOSM vs MOSM with θ=0/φ=0 vs CSM) would clarify where MOSM adds value.
- Rank structure: the construction yields a sum of rank-1 cross-spectral components (Q terms). Discussion on how Q relates to the effective rank of inter-channel covariance and practical selection is limited.

Quality
Technically sound derivation with correct use of Cramér’s theorem, careful construction to ensure real-valued processes, and closed-form kernels. Claims are largely supported by experiments, though additional metrics and ablations would strengthen the case. Clarify optimization (parameterization, constraints, initialization from cross-periodograms, priors) and computational complexity.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable, with intuitive explanations and figures. Suggestions:
- Expand Section 3.2 with practical training details: parameterization for positivity of Σ, constraints on phases/delays, initialization heuristics (e.g., cross-spectral estimation), and early stopping/regularization.
- Clearly state at the start of Section 4.1 the number of training points per channel and total, for quick reproducibility.
- Report complexity with respect to number of channels, components Q, and total data size; note when Kronecker or inducing structure applies (aligned grids).
- Minor typos: a word appears to be missing around lines ~74 and ~140; please proofread.

Originality
The approach extends the SM kernel to the multi-output setting in a principled way via complex cross-spectral densities, enabling both phase and delay with channel-specific spectra. While related to CSM and LMC, the factorized complex PSD construction and parameter interpretation provide a novel and flexible generalization.

Significance
High potential: interpretable, general-purpose multi-output kernels are valuable in many applications (sensor networks, spatiotemporal modeling, multi-task regression). If training is robust, MOSM could become a go-to kernel, complementing or replacing LMC/CONV/CSM in practice. Demonstrated gains on synthetic and one real task; competitive elsewhere.

Pros for acceptance
- Theoretically principled, general, and interpretable kernel family for MOGPs.
- Subsumes prior SM-based MOGP kernels (SM-LMC, CSM), adds delays and richer cross-structure.
- Solid empirical performance and clear exposition.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited guidance on optimization and scalability; potential practical hurdles with few data or many parameters.
- Evaluation could be more comprehensive (log-likelihood, calibration, ablations).
- Discussion of rank/Q selection and identifiability is brief.

Questions for the authors
- How does MOSM behave in the low-data regime per channel? Do you recommend priors on µ, Σ, θ, φ, or regularization to avoid overfitting?
- How does the model handle channels with markedly different length-scales? Any observed optimization pathologies or parameter coupling due to the Σij formula?
- What initialization strategy do you use in practice (e.g., per-channel periodogram/CSD peaks, shared seeds across channels)? Please describe in detail.
- How do you select Q? Any BIC/AIC or validation guidance?
- What is the training complexity and memory footprint as a function of m, Q, and total N across channels? Any speed-ups when inputs are aligned?
- Could you report NLL and calibration (e.g., PICP, CRPS) to assess uncertainty?

Suggestions
- Add an ablation: MOSM vs CSM vs MOSM without delays/phases; and sensitivity to Q.
- Include a low-data experiment and one with heterogeneous channel length-scales.
- Provide code and initialization recipes for reproducibility.
- Consider simple priors and reparameterizations (e.g., log-cholesky for Σ, bounded phases) to stabilize training; discuss identifiability (e.g., φ and θ trade-offs).

Overall recommendation
Leaning toward accept. The paper offers a principled and interpretable advance in MOGP kernel design with promising empirical results. Strengthening the optimization and scalability discussion and adding ablations/metrics would further solidify the contribution.
