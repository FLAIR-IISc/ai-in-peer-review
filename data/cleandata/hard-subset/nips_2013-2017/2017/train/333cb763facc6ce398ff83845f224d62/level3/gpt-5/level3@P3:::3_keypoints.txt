- Proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for MOGPs, derived in the spectral domain via Cramér’s theorem with a low-rank factorization of cross-spectral densities.
- MOSM yields closed-form, real-valued cross-covariances with interpretable per-pair parameters: frequency, amplitude, phase, and relative delay.
- Generalizes single-output SM and extends SM-LMC/CSM by directly modeling cross-spectra and allowing delays and asymmetric/sign-changing cross-covariances.
- Theoretical construction is sound, leveraging Hermitian PSD matrices and inverse Fourier transforms to guarantee positive definiteness.
- Expressive model: decouples auto-covariances, supports asymmetric/sign-changing cross-covariances, and can be extended with rank-Q for richer cross-spectral structure.
- Empirical validation: recovers known cross-covariances on synthetic data (derivatives and delays) and is competitive or better than SM-LMC, CSM, and CONV on two real datasets.
- Paper is generally clear, well-written, and well-illustrated.
- Strength: principled spectral-domain route to valid multi-output kernels that subsumes prior spectral MOGP methods.
- Strength: increased interpretability addressing limitations of LMC/CONV by exposing delays and phase relationships.
- Weakness: parameter learning details are under-specified—initialization, constraints, priors, regularization, optimization schedules, and mitigation of local optima are missing.
- Weakness: identifiability and global shift/phase invariances (parameters as differences) are not discussed or resolved.
- Weakness: scalability concerns—O(N^3) training cost stated but no complexity analysis, runtimes, or memory profiles provided.
- Weakness: limited ablation studies on rank Q and number of spectral components; sensitivity analyses absent.
- Empirical concern: some datasets (climate) show no clear gains over CSM; need diagnostics for when delays and per-channel parameters matter.
- Questions for authors: performance in small-data regimes and learning curves for delays/phases across varying training sizes and channel counts.
- Questions for authors: behavior when channels have disparate length-scales (possible high-frequency leakage or over-damping) and request an experiment demonstrating this.
- Questions for authors: full optimization details—initialization of µ, Σ, θ, φ; parameter constraints; optimizer, learning rates, restarts; use of periodograms.
- Suggestions: add a training section with initialization heuristics, constraints/priors, optimization tips, and sensitivity to restarts; provide complexity/runtime/memory comparisons; include ablations (rank-Q, mixture components, few-data experiments); test disparate length-scale scenarios; clarify handling of negative cross-amplitudes; state synthetic experiment details and fix minor typos.
- Pros for acceptance: principled, general construction; interpretable parameters; theoretical soundness; effective on synthetic and real data; clear presentation.
- Cons against acceptance: insufficient training/optimization details, limited scalability analysis, modest empirical gains in places, and missing ablations.
- Overall recommendation: lean toward acceptance contingent on adding practical training guidance and, if possible, small-data and disparate-length-scale experiments to validate robustness.
