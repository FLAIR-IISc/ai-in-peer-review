This paper introduces a novel approach to defining covariance structures for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs), extending the spectral (Bochner) method with a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities. The core idea leverages Cramér’s Theorem to construct kernels that model delays and phase differences between outputs, offering increased expressiveness and interpretability compared to existing methods like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. 

The work is well-motivated, addressing a known challenge in MOGP development – the difficulty of constructing informative and interpretable cross-covariance functions. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of previous approaches and position their method as a principled extension of the spectral mixture approach for scalar GPs. The paper builds logically, starting with background on GPs and spectral methods, then presenting the theoretical framework based on Cramér’s Theorem, and finally demonstrating the method's performance on synthetic and real-world datasets. 

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The foundation of the method in Cramér's Theorem provides a strong theoretical justification for the proposed kernel construction.
* **Interpretability:**  The parameters of the MOSM kernel (delay, phase, magnitudes, etc.) have clear spectral interpretations, aligning with the advantages of single-output GPs.
* **Expressiveness:** The ability to model delays and phase differences, which are not easily captured by previous methods, enhances the model’s flexibility.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the mathematical concepts and the motivation behind the design choices. The illustrations (e.g., Fig. 1) are relevant and help to understand the behavior of the kernel.
* **Comprehensive Comparison:** The experimental evaluation includes comparisons to several relevant MOGP methods (CONV, SM-LMC, CSM) on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Learning Details:** The paper lacks detailed discussion of the parameter learning process (optimization). It’s unclear how challenging the optimization is, particularly with the increased number of parameters introduced by the MOSM kernel.  This is a crucial point, as difficulties in optimization could limit the practical applicability of the method.
* **Scalability:** While the paper mentions implementation in TensorFlow and GPflow, it doesn't address the computational complexity of the method, especially for large datasets.
* **Limited Analysis of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments primarily focus on comparing performance across methods using default or tuned hyperparameters. More in-depth analysis of the sensitivity of the MOSM kernel to hyperparameter choices would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP covariance function design.
* The method addresses a significant challenge in the field and offers increased expressiveness and interpretability.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on a variety of datasets.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detail regarding the optimization process and potential training difficulties is a concern.
* The computational scalability of the method is not discussed.




**Questions for the Authors:**

* **Scalability with Few Data Points:** How does the method behave with few training points?  The synthetic example uses a relatively large number of samples. Does the method require a substantial amount of data to effectively estimate the increased number of parameters?
* **Handling Different Length-Scales:**  How does the model handle correlations between channels with very different length-scales? Does the spectral mixture approach effectively capture these scenarios, or are there limitations?
* **Optimization Challenges:**  What optimization algorithms were used, and were any specific techniques (e.g., regularization, initialization strategies) employed to address potential optimization difficulties? Could you provide more detail on the computational cost of training the MOSM kernel compared to the other methods?

**Minor Comments:**

* The synthetic example (Section 4.1) should state the exact number of training points used for each signal (reference, derivative, delayed).
* A word appears to be missing in lines 74 and 140 (“recall that” and “accordingly”).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with the potential to become a standard approach for multioutput GPs *if* the parameter learning is manageable. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the experimental results are promising. Addressing the concerns regarding optimization and scalability would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors providing more details on the optimization process and discussing potential limitations related to computational cost.
