This paper introduces a new covariance structure for multioutput Gaussian processes (MOGPs) that generalizes the spectral (Bochner) approach. The proposed method, called the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, is a principled approach to design multivariate covariance functions. The MOSM kernel is able to model delays among channels in addition to phase differences and is thus more expressive than previous methods, while also providing full parametric interpretation of the relationship across channels.

The MOSM kernel is based on a family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities, which are designed to provide physical parametric interpretation and closed-form covariance functions after applying the inverse Fourier transform. The kernel is constructed using Cramér's Theorem, which is the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem. The resulting kernel has spectral-mixture positive-definite kernels as autocovariances, while the cross-covariances are spectral mixture functions with different parameters for different output pairs.

The paper provides a clear and well-organized presentation of the proposed method, including a detailed derivation of the MOSM kernel and its properties. The authors also provide an experimental evaluation of the proposed method on synthetic and real-world datasets, including climate signals and metal concentrations. The results show that the MOSM kernel is competitive with existing MOGP models in terms of the mean absolute error.

The paper is well-written, and the illustrations and choices are relevant. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the related work and the contributions of the paper. The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results.

One of the strengths of the paper is that the model parameters are interpretable, which is a significant advantage over previous methods. The MOSM kernel provides a clear interpretation of the relationship across channels, including delays, phase differences, and magnitudes. This is a significant contribution to the field of MOGPs, as it provides a more expressive and interpretable model.

Another strength of the paper is that it provides a principled approach to design multivariate covariance functions. The use of Cramér's Theorem provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method, and the resulting kernel has desirable properties such as positive definiteness and stationarity.

One of the weaknesses of the paper is that it lacks detail on parameter learning. The authors mention that the model parameters are learned by maximizing the log-probability of the data, but they do not provide further details on the optimization procedure or the computational complexity of the method. This is a potential concern, as the optimization of the model parameters can be challenging, especially for large datasets.

Another weakness of the paper is that it does not provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of the method. The authors mention that the method can be implemented using automatic differentiation, but they do not provide further details on the computational cost of the method.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of MOGPs by providing a more expressive and interpretable model. The MOSM kernel is a principled approach to design multivariate covariance functions, and it has desirable properties such as positive definiteness and stationarity. The paper is well-written, and the experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.

However, the paper could be improved by providing more details on parameter learning and computational complexity. Additionally, the authors could provide more analysis on the behavior of the method with few training points and on the handling of correlations between channels with very different length-scales.
