

This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the single-output spectral mixture approach [6] to multi-output Gaussian processes through a principled construction of cross-spectral densities. Building on Cramér's theorem as the multivariate extension of Bochner's theorem, the authors design a family of complex-valued spectral densities that yield closed-form covariance functions with clear parametric interpretation for delays, phase shifts, and cross-correlations across channels.

The theoretical contribution is sound and well-motivated, providing a meaningful generalization of the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5] by explicitly modeling delays between channels. The synthetic experiment successfully demonstrates the model's ability to learn derivative and delayed relationships that latent-process-based models cannot capture, highlighting a key advantage over existing approaches.

However, the experimental evaluation has significant limitations. The real-world experiments on weather and Swiss Jura datasets show competitive but not clearly superior performance compared to existing methods (CSM, SM-LMC, CONV), with results in Tables 1 and 2 showing minimal differences that appear statistically insignificant given the reported standard deviations. Crucially, the paper fails to demonstrate the extrapolation ability for multi-output data that was a key strength of the single-output spectral mixture approach [6], which is particularly important for practical applications.

The significance of the work would be substantially enhanced by including datasets where previous methods perform poorly, rather than using the same common datasets. The paper lacks discussion of when MOSM would be particularly advantageous over existing techniques, and the claim of "more expressive" kernels is not convincingly supported by the empirical results.

While the theoretical extension is clever and technically sound (addressing Quality and Originality well), the experimental evaluation does not sufficiently demonstrate the claimed advantages, particularly regarding extrapolation and performance on challenging datasets (limiting Significance). The paper would benefit from additional statistical testing and experiments on datasets where existing methods fail to show the full potential of the proposed approach.
