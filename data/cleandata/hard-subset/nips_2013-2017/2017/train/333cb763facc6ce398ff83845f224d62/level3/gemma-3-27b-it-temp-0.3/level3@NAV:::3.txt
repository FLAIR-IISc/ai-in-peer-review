## Review of "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

This paper introduces a novel approach to defining covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs) by extending the spectral mixture kernel concept. The core idea is to leverage Cramér’s Theorem to construct a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities, enabling the modeling of delays and phase differences between outputs – features lacking in many existing methods. This is a significant contribution, as defining effective and interpretable cross-covariances remains a key challenge in the field of MOGPs. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of previous approaches like LMC and CSM, and positioning the proposed method as a generalization that addresses these shortcomings. The theoretical foundation is solid, building upon established results like Bochner’s and Cramér’s Theorems. The experimental validation, including a synthetic example and comparisons on two real-world datasets (climate signals and metal concentrations), demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed MOSM kernel. The writing is clear and the illustrations are relevant, aiding in understanding the concepts. 

A key strength lies in the interpretability of the model parameters – delays, phase differences, and spectral characteristics – which aligns with the benefits of single-output GPs. If the parameter learning process proves robust, this method has the potential to become a standard approach for MOGPs. However, the paper lacks detailed discussion of the parameter learning aspect. While successful application is demonstrated, the potential for optimization difficulties with the increased parameter space is a concern. A more in-depth analysis of the optimization landscape and computational cost would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP covariance function design.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in the field – defining interpretable cross-covariances.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Clear writing and relevant illustrations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited detail on parameter learning and potential optimization challenges.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   How does the method perform with limited training data? Does the increased parameter space exacerbate overfitting in low-data regimes?
*   How does the model handle correlations between channels exhibiting significantly different length-scales? Does the spectral mixture approach adequately capture these relationships?

**Comments:**

*   The synthetic example (Section 4.1) should explicitly state the number of training points used.
*   There appear to be missing words in lines 74 and 140.



Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to significantly impact the field of MOGPs. Addressing the concerns regarding parameter learning would further solidify its contribution and increase its likelihood of becoming a widely adopted method.
