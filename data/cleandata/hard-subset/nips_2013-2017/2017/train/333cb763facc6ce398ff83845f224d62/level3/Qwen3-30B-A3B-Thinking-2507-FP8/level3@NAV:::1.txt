

This paper proposes a multi-output spectral mixture (MOSM) kernel that extends Wilson and Adams' (2013) spectral mixture approach to the multi-output Gaussian process setting. The authors build upon Cramér's theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner's theorem) to construct a principled framework for designing matrix-valued covariance functions through complex-valued cross-spectral densities. The key innovation lies in modeling cross-spectral densities as complex-valued square-exponential functions, which enables the kernel to capture delays and phase differences across channels while maintaining positive definiteness through the rank-one decomposition of the spectral density matrix.

The paper excels in clarity and theoretical foundation. The introduction and background sections provide excellent context for Gaussian processes and spectral mixture kernels, and the derivation of the MOSM kernel is well-explained with clear mathematical justification. The relationship to prior work (particularly the Cross-Spectral Mixture kernel) is thoroughly addressed, and the novelty of incorporating both delays and phase shifts is convincingly established.

Regarding quality, the theoretical contributions are sound and the derivation is technically correct. However, the experimental validation presents a notable weakness: while the synthetic example demonstrates the model's ability to capture derivative and delayed relationships, the real-world experiments on climate data and the Jura dataset provide limited interpretation. The Jura results, in particular, show marginal improvements over CSM without clear explanation of why the proposed method outperforms existing approaches in specific scenarios.

The significance of this work is moderate. The ability to model delays and phase differences represents a meaningful extension to multi-output GP modeling, with potential applications in signal processing and time series analysis. However, the practical benefits over existing methods are not strongly demonstrated in the real-world experiments, and the statistical significance testing for the Jura dataset is insufficient.

Strengths: Strong theoretical foundation, clear exposition, well-motivated novelty. Weaknesses: Limited practical interpretation of results, especially on the Jura dataset, and insufficient statistical validation.

Overall, this paper makes a technically sound contribution to multi-output Gaussian processes with clear theoretical advances. The work is well-written and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. While the experimental validation could be strengthened, the theoretical contribution is significant enough to warrant publication at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve the interpretation of the Jura dataset results.
