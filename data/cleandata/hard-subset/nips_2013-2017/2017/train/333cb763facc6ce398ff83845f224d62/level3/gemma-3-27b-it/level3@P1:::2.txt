This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel approach to constructing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to define cross-spectral densities and leverage Cramér’s Theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner’s Theorem) to derive corresponding covariance functions via the inverse Fourier transform. This extends the spectral mixture concept introduced in Wilson & Adams (2013) and further developed in Tobar et al. (2015) to the multi-output setting, allowing for the modeling of delays and phase differences between channels – a feature not readily available in previous methods like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) [Rasmussen & Williams, 2006] or the Cross-Spectral Mixture kernel (CSM) [Ulrich et al., 2015].

The authors motivate their work by highlighting the challenges in designing MOGP kernels, specifically the need to balance expressiveness with positive-definiteness. They argue that operating in the spectral domain simplifies this process, as ensuring positive-definiteness of the spectral density is generally easier than for the covariance function directly. The proposed MOSM kernel offers a parametric interpretation of the relationships between channels, relating parameters directly to spectral properties like frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay. The paper provides a thorough background on GPs, spectral kernels, and existing MOGP approaches, clearly positioning the contribution within the field.

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical grounding and the potential for creating flexible and interpretable MOGP kernels. The extension of the spectral mixture approach to multiple outputs is a clever idea, and the use of Cramér’s Theorem provides a principled framework for constructing positive-definite covariance functions. The synthetic experiment effectively demonstrates the ability of the model to identify known auto- and cross-covariances, including delays, showcasing its expressive power.  The inclusion of real-world datasets (climate signals and metal concentrations) offers some validation of the method’s practical applicability.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the introduction mentions extrapolation as a benefit of spectral mixture kernels, this isn't explicitly demonstrated in the multi-output setting with the synthetic data or in the results.  A stronger demonstration of the benefits analogous to those shown in Wilson & Adams (2013) would significantly strengthen the argument for the approach.  Furthermore, the experimental evaluation, while utilizing common datasets, doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing methods. The performance appears comparable to CSM, and the statistical significance of the reported results is unclear. The reported standard deviations in Tables 1 and 2 suggest that the performance differences across models are often within the margin of error.  The authors acknowledge this, but a more rigorous statistical analysis would be beneficial. Additionally, the performance on the Jura dataset (metal concentrations) is better than other methods, but the choice of dataset might be somewhat convenient as it benefits the particular expressiveness of the MOSM kernel.  Including datasets where existing methods perform well, and MOSM struggles, would provide a more balanced assessment.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of spectral mixture kernels to multi-output GPs.
*   Principled theoretical foundation based on Cramér’s Theorem.
*   Clear parametric interpretation of the covariance function.
*   Demonstrated ability to model delays and phase differences.
*   Sound experimental validation on standard datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of conclusive evidence of superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   Insufficient statistical analysis to support performance claims.
*   Missing demonstration of extrapolation capabilities in the multi-output setting.
*   Potential bias in dataset selection.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of MOGPs. While the experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing, the theoretical framework and the potential for creating flexible and interpretable kernels are promising.  I recommend **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding statistical significance and including a more thorough demonstration of the method's extrapolation capabilities. A more critical discussion of the limitations and potential drawbacks of the approach would also be beneficial.
