This paper introduces a new family of kernels for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), called the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The authors build upon the spectral approach for single-output GPs (Wilson & Adams, 2013) by leveraging Cramér's Theorem, the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem. This allows for a principled design of the matrix-valued spectral density, from which the matrix-valued covariance function is derived via the inverse Fourier transform. The key idea is to model the cross-spectral densities as a mixture of complex-valued Gaussian functions. This principled construction allows the MOSM kernel to model not only phase differences (as in the related Cross-Spectral Mixture kernel of Ulrich et al., 2015) but also explicit time delays between output channels, all while maintaining a clear, interpretable parameterization and guaranteeing the positive definiteness of the resulting kernel matrix. The proposed method is validated on a synthetic task designed to highlight its unique capabilities and compared against existing MOGP methods on two real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposed MOSM kernel is a novel and principled extension of the highly successful Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output setting. This addresses a long-standing challenge in the MOGP literature: designing expressive, interpretable, and valid cross-covariance functions. While other methods exist (LMC, Convolution Models, CSM), the MOSM provides a more general and flexible framework derived directly from first principles in the spectral domain. The ability to model time delays between channels is a significant and unique contribution that is not explicitly handled by previous spectral MOGP methods. If the optimization challenges are manageable, this method has the potential to become a standard and widely used tool for MOGP modeling.

2.  **Quality and Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is technically sound. The use of Cramér's Theorem as the theoretical foundation is appropriate and elegant. The construction of the Hermitian positive-definite spectral density matrix S(ω) through a rank-Q factor decomposition (S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω)) is a clever and effective way to ensure the validity of the resulting kernel. The experimental evaluation is solid. The synthetic experiment in Section 4.1 is particularly compelling, as it clearly demonstrates the model's ability to recover known derivative and delay relationships that other methods fail to capture. The real-world experiments show that the model is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art baselines.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The theoretical development, starting from Bochner's Theorem for the single-output case and extending to Cramér's Theorem for the multi-output case, is clear and logically sound. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind their method and the interpretation of the resulting parameters. The illustrations, particularly Figure 1 which visualizes the effect of phase and delay parameters, are very effective at building intuition.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Optimization Challenges:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of discussion regarding the optimization of the hyperparameters. The MOSM kernel introduces a significant number of parameters (for Q components and m channels, the number of parameters scales with Q*m). The spectral mixture kernel for single-output GPs is already known to have a challenging, multi-modal likelihood surface. It is highly likely that these challenges are exacerbated in the multi-output setting. The paper simply states that the negative log-likelihood is minimized with respect to the parameters, without discussing initialization strategies, the risk of local optima, or the computational cost. This is a crucial practical detail that is currently missing.

2.  **Performance in Low-Data Regimes:** Related to the optimization challenge, it is unclear how the model performs in low-data regimes. With a large number of parameters to learn from the data, the model may be prone to overfitting when training data is scarce. A discussion or an experiment addressing the model's robustness to the amount of training data would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Questions and Suggestions for Authors:**

1.  Could the authors elaborate on the practical aspects of training the MOSM model? How were the hyperparameters initialized? Were multiple restarts used? How does the training time compare to the baseline models?

2.  How does the model handle correlations between channels with very different length-scales or frequencies? The parameterization seems to couple the parameters for each channel pair (i,j) within a single component q (e.g., via Σij and µij). Does this pose a limitation if channel i is very smooth (long length-scale) while channel j is very noisy (short length-scale)?

3.  The synthetic experiment (Section 4.1) is very effective. However, the exact number of training points for each of the three signals (N1, N2, N3) is not stated. Please clarify this in the text.

4.  Minor remarks:
    *   Line 74: A word appears to be missing. "as is [10, 11]" might be better as "as is shown in [10, 11]".
    *   Line 140: Similarly, "given the spectral mixture kernel" would be more grammatical as "given by the spectral mixture kernel".

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a significant and elegant contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. The proposed MOSM kernel is well-founded in theory, highly expressive, and demonstrates strong empirical performance. The main weakness is the lack of detail on the practicalities of model training, but this does not undermine the core contribution. I recommend acceptance.
