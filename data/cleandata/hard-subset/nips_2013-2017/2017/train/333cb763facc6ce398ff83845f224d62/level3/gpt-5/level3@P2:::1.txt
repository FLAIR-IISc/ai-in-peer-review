Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a principled, spectral-domain construction of covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Building on Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem), the authors design complex-valued cross-spectral densities via a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), with each column Ri(ω) parameterized as a complex-valued squared exponential in frequency. Enforcing Hermitian symmetry yields real-valued processes after inverse Fourier transform. The resulting Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel has closed-form time-domain covariances: each cross-covariance is a spectral mixture with interpretable magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, and, importantly, delay parameters. The auto-covariances reduce to standard spectral mixture (SM) kernels (Wilson & Adams, ICML 2013).

The paper relates MOSM to prior multi-output constructions: the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and convolutional models (Álvarez & Lawrence; Álvarez et al., NIPS/AISTATS), and to spectral approaches for cross-spectra (Ulrich et al., NIPS 2015, CSM). MOSM subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases and allows distinct per-output spectral parameters and explicit inter-output delays, which are not available in CSM. The paper also connects to multivariate covariance literature (e.g., Genton & Kleiber; Gneiting et al.) and notes related spectral constructions with real-valued cross-spectra.

Theoretical contribution
- A clean, general recipe to construct valid MOGP kernels by specifying complex cross-spectra and guaranteeing positive definiteness through a low-rank factorization in the frequency domain.
- A closed-form family of cross-covariances that includes delay and phase, with interpretable parameters and a clear specialization to known kernels.

Empirical contribution
- Synthetic example demonstrating simultaneous recovery of derivative and delayed channels where latent-process models struggle.
- Two real datasets (climate sensor failure; Jura heavy metals) comparing MOSM to CONV, SM-LMC, and CSM. MOSM is competitive overall and clearly best on some tasks (e.g., Cadmium, and one climate sensor case).

Strengths
- Well-motivated, principled extension of SM kernels to the multi-output setting with full parametric interpretability (frequency, bandwidth, magnitude, phase, delay).
- Clear derivation and guarantees via Cramér’s theorem and frequency-domain factorization.
- Subsumes SM-LMC/CSM and explains when those models arise as restricted cases.
- Closed-form time-domain kernel enables standard GP training and prediction.
- Synthetic experiment convincingly shows advantages of modeling both delay and phase.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Stationarity: MOSM (as presented) is stationary; many multi-output applications exhibit nonstationary cross-dependencies. A discussion or pointer to nonstationary extensions would help.
- Overparameterization and identifiability: With per-output, per-component parameters (μi, Σi, θi, φi, wi), the number of hyperparameters can be large. Please discuss identifiability (e.g., trade-offs between φ and θ, or near-degenerate μi across outputs), initialization strategies, and regularization or tying strategies. An ablation on the effect of allowing delays versus only phases would clarify practical gains.
- Rank and number of components: The paper mostly uses Q=5 and rank-1 per component. What is the benefit of higher rank Q for S(ω)? A brief empirical comparison (rank vs performance) would be valuable.
- Metrics and uncertainty: Real-data evaluation uses MAE only. Adding negative log-likelihood and calibration diagnostics would better test probabilistic quality and whether MOSM’s extra flexibility improves uncertainty quantification.
- Computational aspects: Please report runtimes, scaling with m and N, and conditioning issues in optimization. Since the model shares the O(N^3) GP cost, a short discussion or a small-scale demonstration of sparse/variational versions (e.g., inducing features in the spectral domain) would strengthen the paper.
- Empirical intuition: While the synthetic example is instructive, the real-data section would benefit from showing learned cross-spectral parameters (e.g., estimated delays and phases) to provide intuition and interpretability. The Jura results are difficult to interpret without such insights.

Quality
- The derivations appear sound and well-grounded in known spectral theory. The construction ensures positive definiteness, and the real-valuedness is properly handled by spectral symmetrization.
- Empirical validation is adequate but could be expanded in breadth and diagnostics (see above).

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and clearly written. The connections to prior work are carefully explained.
- Minor suggestions: quantify parameter counts and discuss identifiability; collect practical optimization tips (initializing μi, Σi from periodograms, constraining φ/θ ranges); fix small typos.

Originality
- The approach is novel in enabling complex cross-spectra with both phase and delay in a principled MOGP construction, generalizing SM and CSM and offering a unifying spectral-domain perspective.

Significance
- Likely to be useful for practitioners needing interpretable cross-channel structure (delays/phases) and for researchers building more flexible MOGP kernels. While empirical gains over strong baselines are sometimes modest, the framework’s generality and interpretability are valuable.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to initialization of μi, Σi, θi, φi? Do you use spectral heuristics?
- Do you observe identifiability issues between delay and phase, or between μ and θ, in practice?
- What is the empirical benefit of higher rank Q (beyond rank-1 per component)?
- Can you report training time and NLL across methods?
- Could you visualize learned delays/phases on the climate or Jura datasets?

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, general, and interpretable kernel family for MOGPs.
- Clear theoretical grounding; subsumes prior spectral MOGP models.
- Demonstrated benefits on synthetic and competitive results on real datasets.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited empirical breadth and diagnostics; modest improvements on some real tasks.
- No scalability experiments; limited discussion of identifiability and optimization.
- Stationarity restriction not addressed empirically.

Overall recommendation
- Weak accept. The paper provides a clean, theoretically grounded extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output case with interpretable phase and delay, and it unifies/supersedes prior spectral MOGP kernels. With minor clarifications on initialization, identifiability, and computational aspects, this will be a useful contribution.
