This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel and flexible covariance function for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The core idea is to extend the design of kernels in the spectral domain, a concept popularized for single-output GPs by the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel (Wilson & Adams, 2013), to the multi-output setting. The authors leverage Cramér’s Theorem, the multivariate generalization of Bochner’s Theorem, to propose a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities. This principled approach ensures the resulting matrix-valued covariance function is positive definite while allowing for highly expressive cross-covariances that can model delays and phase shifts between output channels—a key challenge in MOGP modeling.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The proposed method is a well-motivated and significant contribution to the MOGP literature. By building the covariance from a factor decomposition of the cross-spectral density matrix, the authors provide an elegant solution to the difficult problem of designing valid and interpretable cross-covariance functions. The ability to explicitly model delays between channels, in addition to phase differences, is a clear and powerful extension over previous spectral approaches like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel.
*   **Quality and Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically sound. The introduction and background provide a clear motivation and the necessary theoretical fundamentals. The derivation of the MOSM kernel is presented logically and is easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to prior art (e.g., LMC, CSM), making the novelty of their contribution unambiguous.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Experimental Intuition:** While the experiments are adequate to validate the method, they could provide deeper intuition. The synthetic experiment effectively demonstrates the model's unique ability to capture derivative and delay relationships. However, the real-world experiments on the climate and Jura datasets are less compelling. The performance gains are sometimes marginal or not statistically significant, and the Jura dataset, in particular, is difficult to interpret. The paper would be strengthened by either including an analysis of the learned spectral parameters (delays, frequencies) on the real-world data to offer insights, or by applying the model to a problem where inter-channel delays are known to be a critical feature.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, theoretically-grounded, and important extension to spectral mixture kernels for the multi-output setting. The clarity of the exposition and the significance of the methodological contribution are major strengths. The minor weakness in the experimental section does not detract from the overall value of the work. I recommend acceptance.
