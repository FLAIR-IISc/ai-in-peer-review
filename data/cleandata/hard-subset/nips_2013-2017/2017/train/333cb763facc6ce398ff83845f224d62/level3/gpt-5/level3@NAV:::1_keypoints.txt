- Proposes a multi-output spectral mixture (MOSM) kernel designing cross-covariances in the spectral domain via Cramér’s theorem.
- Parameterizes complex cross-spectral densities and enforces Hermitian PSD via factorization S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω), yielding closed-form real cross-covariances after symmetrization.
- Produces interpretable parameters per output pair (magnitude, frequency, phase, delay).
- Strictly generalizes LMC/SM-LMC and CSM and allows cross-covariances that are not themselves positive-definite while adding explicit delay modeling beyond phase shifts.
- Experiments include a synthetic tri-variate task with derivatives and delays, climate imputation, and the Jura geostatistics dataset.
- Theoretical development is sound and grounded in Cramér’s theorem; rank-one-per-component factorization gives a principled PSD parameterization and tractable kernels.
- Expressivity claims (phase+delay, non-symmetric cross-covariances) are supported by formulation and the synthetic task where latent-process models underperform.
- Real-data results are competitive but provide limited intuition about learned parameters (e.g., recovered delays/phases), making Jura results hard to interpret.
- Paper is well written with an accessible introduction and accurate positioning relative to prior work (LMC, CONV, SM-LMC, CSM).
- Originality: meaningful advance by moving to complex, factorized cross-spectra with explicit phase and delay in multi-output setting.
- Significance: useful, interpretable tool for domains where delays/phase shifts matter (climate, neuroscience, seismology), but broader impact depends on scalability and rank/component guidance.
- Suggest reporting learned phase/delay with uncertainty and adding ablations on identifiability and robustness to initialization.
- Suggest discussing computational complexity, providing runtime/memory benchmarks or a small-scale sparse variant.
- Suggest exploring higher PSD rank (Q>1) to study effects on accuracy and interpretability.
- Suggest providing more intuitive Jura analysis (e.g., spatial maps, spectra).
- Pros: principled novel extension, interpretable parameters, clear theory, closed-form kernels, strong synthetic validation and competitive real-data results.
- Cons: limited analysis of learned structures and scalability; rank/initialization/identifiability not thoroughly explored; some experiments (Jura) lack intuitive takeaways.
