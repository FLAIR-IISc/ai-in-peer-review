This paper proposes a novel multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) covariance function based on extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel concept from Wilson & Adams (2013) to multiple outputs via Cramér’s Theorem. The core idea is to define cross-spectral densities and then use the inverse Fourier transform to obtain cross-covariances, offering a parametric and interpretable approach to model dependencies between outputs, including delays and phase differences. This builds upon previous work like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, aiming for greater flexibility and expressiveness.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the challenge of designing interpretable and positive-definite cross-covariance functions in MOGPs. The extension of the SM kernel concept is a natural progression, and the use of Cramér’s Theorem provides a solid theoretical foundation. The authors clearly articulate the advantages of their approach, particularly the ability to model delays and phase shifts not readily captured by existing methods. The experimental validation on a synthetic dataset, a weather dataset, and the Swiss Jura metal-concentration dataset provides a reasonable assessment of the method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed MOSM kernel represents a novel and promising way to construct multi-output covariance functions, extending a successful single-output technique to the multi-output setting.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The use of Cramér’s Theorem provides a strong theoretical justification for the proposed method, ensuring positive definiteness.
* **Interpretability:** The kernel parameters have clear spectral interpretations (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay), enhancing understanding of the modeled relationships.
* **Expressiveness:** The proposed kernel’s ability to model delays and phase differences expands the range of dependencies that can be captured compared to previous methods like CSM.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical concepts and the proposed methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Extrapolation Ability:** A key strength of the original SM kernel (Wilson & Adams, 2013) was its extrapolation ability. The paper acknowledges this but lacks a corresponding demonstration for the multi-output extension. Showing that MOSM can extrapolate effectively would significantly strengthen the contribution.
* **Statistical Significance:** The experimental evaluation, while thorough, suffers from a lack of clear statistical significance testing. The reported standard deviations in Tables 1 and 2 suggest that the performance differences between models are often small, raising questions about whether the observed improvements are statistically meaningful.  A more rigorous statistical analysis is needed to substantiate the claims of superiority.
* **Dataset Limitations:** The experimental results are somewhat limited by the choice of datasets. While common and well-established, the climate dataset exhibits high correlation between signals, potentially masking the benefits of the more expressive MOSM kernel. Including datasets where existing models perform poorly would provide a more compelling demonstration of the method’s advantages.
* **Complexity vs. Performance:** The added complexity of the MOSM kernel (more parameters) does not consistently translate to superior performance across all datasets. The performance gains, when observed, are often marginal.  A clearer understanding of the trade-off between model complexity and performance is needed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP covariance function design.
* The proposed MOSM kernel offers improved expressiveness compared to existing methods.
* The experimental evaluation, despite limitations, demonstrates competitive performance.
* The work has the potential to advance the field of MOGPs and enable more accurate modeling of complex multi-output systems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of demonstration of extrapolation ability weakens the connection to the original SM kernel.
* The lack of statistical significance in the experimental results casts doubt on the observed performance improvements.
* The datasets used may not be sufficiently challenging to fully highlight the advantages of the proposed method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of Gaussian process modeling. The proposed MOSM kernel is a clever and promising extension of the spectral mixture concept. While the paper has some weaknesses, particularly regarding statistical significance and the lack of extrapolation demonstration, the novelty, theoretical foundation, and potential impact of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the statistical significance issue and a discussion on the challenges and potential approaches to demonstrate the extrapolation capabilities of MOSM. A brief analysis of the computational cost compared to simpler models would also be valuable.
