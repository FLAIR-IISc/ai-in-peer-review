This paper proposes a novel approach to multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) covariance function design, extending spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting. The key idea is to define a family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and leverage Cramér’s Theorem to construct a multivariate covariance function (MOSM) that can model delays and phase differences between channels, providing a more expressive and interpretable framework than existing methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the limitations of existing MOGP approaches, such as the lack of interpretability in latent variable models and the limitations of the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The background section provides a concise and accessible review of relevant GP concepts and the spectral mixture kernel, setting the stage for the proposed method. The derivation of the MOSM kernel is logically presented and well-explained, building upon established theoretical foundations like Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems.

The main strength of this work lies in its originality. Extending the spectral mixture approach to the multi-output setting in this way, particularly with the inclusion of delay and phase parameters directly within the spectral density formulation, appears novel and addresses a significant challenge in MOGP modeling. The theoretical justification based on Cramér’s Theorem provides a strong foundation for the proposed method. The paper appropriately positions its contribution relative to prior work, clearly articulating how MOSM generalizes the CSM kernel and offers greater flexibility.  The paper also does a good job of explaining the connection between the parameters of the spectral density and their physical interpretation in terms of frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay.

However, the experimental evaluation is a notable weakness. While the synthetic example effectively demonstrates the ability of MOSM to recover known auto- and cross-covariances, including delayed signals, the real-world experiments are less convincing. The climate data experiment shows limited differentiation between the models, with all models performing reasonably well likely due to the high correlation between the climate signals. The Jura dataset results are more promising, with MOSM outperforming other methods on the Cadmium estimation task, but the interpretation of this dataset is inherently complex, and it’s difficult to draw strong conclusions without a deeper understanding of the underlying geological processes.  The limited statistical significance testing further weakens the claims. The MAE metric alone does not provide sufficient insight into *how* MOSM achieves its performance; visualizations of the predicted outputs and learned covariance functions would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed MOSM kernel is a novel extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting, addressing a gap in existing MOGP methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is grounded in solid theoretical foundations (Cramér’s Theorem) and provides a principled approach to covariance function design.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and explains complex concepts in an accessible manner.
*   **Potential Significance:** A flexible and interpretable MOGP framework has the potential to be useful in a variety of applications where modeling relationships between multiple outputs is crucial.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Experimental Insight:** The real-world experiments lack sufficient depth and statistical rigor to definitively demonstrate the superiority of MOSM.
*   **Dataset Complexity:** The Jura dataset is difficult to interpret, making it challenging to assess the true impact of the proposed method.
*   **Computational Cost:** While not explicitly discussed, spectral mixture kernels can be computationally expensive, especially with a large number of mixture components. This should be acknowledged and potentially addressed in future work.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations in the experimental evaluation, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of Gaussian process modeling. The proposed MOSM kernel offers a novel and theoretically sound approach to multi-output covariance function design, with the potential to improve the performance and interpretability of MOGP models. While the experiments do not provide overwhelming evidence of superiority, the theoretical contributions and clear presentation justify acceptance, with the recommendation that the authors strengthen the experimental validation in future work, perhaps with more targeted datasets or detailed analysis of the learned parameters.

**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions - strengthen experimental validation and statistical analysis).
