- Proposes MOSM: a novel Multi-Output Spectral Mixture kernel using complex-valued cross-spectral densities to model delays and phase shifts across channels.  
- Builds on Cramér’s Theorem to ensure positive-definite multivariate kernels via rank-one factorization of the spectral density matrix.  
- Provides a clear parametric interpretation of spectral components (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay).  
- Generalizes and subsumes the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel by allowing distinct auto- and cross-covariance parameters.  
- Derivation yields a closed-form covariance expression (Eq. 11) that is elegant and theoretically grounded.  
- Synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate MOSM’s ability to recover derivatives and delayed signals where latent-process models fail.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written with concise, thorough background and accessible derivations.  
- Novelty is significant: addresses delays in cross-covariances via spectral methods not covered in prior NIPS work.  
- Real-world experiments show only marginal gains; statistically significant improvements are limited to a few cases (e.g., Sotonmet, Cadmium).  
- Fails to demonstrate why delays matter in practice—no ablation on delay parameters or comparisons to delay-specific baselines.  
- Jura results are hard to interpret due to high correlation between metal concentrations, weakening the claimed advantage.  
- Experimental setup lacks depth and discussion on why MOSM’s delay modeling does not yield clearer real-world benefits.  
- Paper omits discussion of computational cost and scalability compared to alternatives (e.g., CONV, sparse approximations).  
- Recommendation: technically sound and novel but under-validated empirically; suggest weak acceptance pending stronger experiments (ablation, comparisons, clarify practical scenarios where delays are critical).
