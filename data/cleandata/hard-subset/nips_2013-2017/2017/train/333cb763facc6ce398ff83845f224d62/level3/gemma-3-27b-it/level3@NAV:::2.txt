## Review of "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

This paper proposes a novel approach to constructing multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) covariance functions by extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel concept from single-output GPs. The core idea is to define cross-spectral densities and then leverage Cramér’s theorem to derive the corresponding multivariate covariance function, enabling the modeling of delays and phase differences between outputs. This is a clever extension of previous work, building upon the successful SM kernel framework and addressing limitations in existing MOGP approaches like the linear model of coregionalization (LMC) and its variants. The authors clearly situate their work within the broader context of MOGP research, referencing relevant literature and highlighting the trade-offs between expressiveness and interpretability in covariance function design. 

The strength of the paper lies in its theoretical foundation and the potential for building more expressive and interpretable MOGP models. The use of Cramér’s theorem provides a principled way to construct valid covariance functions, and the ability to model delays and phase shifts could be particularly valuable in applications where temporal relationships between outputs are important. The experimental evaluation, encompassing a synthetic dataset, a climate dataset, and a geological dataset, demonstrates the method’s applicability to different types of data. 

However, a key weakness is the lack of a compelling demonstration of the extrapolation capabilities analogous to those shown in the original SM kernel paper [6]. While the synthetic example showcases learning of derivatives and delays, a more direct comparison demonstrating superior extrapolation performance against other MOGP methods would strengthen the contribution. Furthermore, the experimental results, while promising, largely show comparable performance to existing methods. The claim of outperforming on the Jura dataset for Cadmium prediction is noted, but the lack of statistical significance in other cases (and the acknowledgement of this in the paper) weakens the overall impact. The authors correctly identify this issue and suggest future work on sparse implementations, which could improve scalability and performance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled extension of the SM kernel to multi-output GPs.
*   Theoretical soundness based on Cramér’s theorem.
*   Potential for modeling complex relationships between outputs (delays, phase shifts).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of a clear demonstration of extrapolation ability.
*   Experimental results show similar performance to existing methods, with limited statistical significance.
*   Impact could be increased with datasets where current MOGP models struggle.



Overall, this is a solid contribution with a well-defined theoretical basis and potential for future development. While the experimental results aren’t groundbreaking, the proposed method represents a promising new direction for MOGP covariance function design. I recommend **acceptance** contingent on the authors addressing the limitations regarding extrapolation demonstration and more robust statistical analysis of the experimental results, or at least a thorough discussion of why statistically significant improvements are difficult to achieve with the chosen datasets.
