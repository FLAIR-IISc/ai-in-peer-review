This paper proposes a novel and principled method for constructing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The core idea is to generalize the spectral approach for single-output GPs to the multi-output setting. While single-output spectral mixture (SM) kernels [6] are derived by defining a power spectral density (PSD) as a Gaussian mixture and applying Bochner's theorem, this work defines a matrix of cross-spectral densities and applies Cramér's theorem, the multivariate extension of Bochner's theorem. The authors propose a specific parametric form for the cross-spectral density matrix by constructing it from complex-valued spectral factors, which guarantees the necessary positive-definiteness. The resulting MOSM kernel is highly expressive, capable of modeling not only frequency and lengthscale relationships but also interpretable delays and phase differences between output channels. This provides a more flexible alternative to previous MOGP methods such as the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel [5].

The main strength of this paper is its originality and theoretical elegance. The extension of the spectral mixture idea from a single output to multiple outputs via Cramér's theorem is a clever and natural generalization. It provides a principled and constructive way to build a rich class of MOGP covariance functions directly in the spectral domain, sidestepping the often-difficult task of ensuring the positive-definiteness of a matrix-valued kernel in the time/space domain. The resulting MOSM kernel, with its explicit parameters for delays (θij) and phase shifts (φij), offers enhanced interpretability compared to many existing MOGP models. The synthetic experiment is a key strength, as it is well-designed to showcase the unique capabilities of the proposed model. By successfully learning the relationships between a function, its derivative, and a delayed version of itself, the authors convincingly demonstrate the superior expressiveness of MOSM in a setting where simpler models based on linear combinations of latent processes would likely fail. This provides strong support for the paper's central claims.

While the conceptual contribution is strong, the experimental evaluation on real-world data is less convincing and represents the main weakness of the work. The paper evaluates MOSM on two common benchmark datasets: a weather dataset and the Swiss Jura metal concentration dataset. In these experiments, the proposed model performs competitively with existing methods like CSM and SM-LMC, but it does not demonstrate a clear and consistent advantage. For instance, in Tables 2 and 3, the reported mean absolute errors are often very close across the top-performing models, and the standard deviations suggest that the performance differences may not be statistically significant. The authors commendably perform statistical tests, but the conclusion is often that MOSM's performance is similar to that of CSM. The impact of the paper could be significantly increased by demonstrating the model's utility on datasets where its unique features are indispensable. The true power of MOSM likely lies in applications with complex, time-lagged relationships between signals (e.g., in econometrics, neuroscience, or multi-sensor physical systems), where existing models might perform poorly. Demonstrating a clear win on such a problem would make a much stronger case for the practical significance of the proposed kernel.

Furthermore, a key appeal of the original SM kernel [6] was its remarkable ability for long-range extrapolation of periodic data. The current paper is missing a compelling multi-output analogue of this demonstration. While the synthetic experiment involves imputation, it does not showcase the kind of periodic extrapolation that made the original paper so influential. A multi-output experiment—for example, predicting several correlated, periodic time series far into the future after training on an initial segment—would be a powerful addition. This would directly test whether MOSM can successfully learn and extrapolate the shared periodic structure, including any phase and delay relationships, which is a natural and important use-case for this class of models.

In summary, this paper presents a well-motivated, original, and theoretically sound approach for constructing expressive multi-output covariance functions. The proposed MOSM kernel is a valuable contribution to the MOGP literature. The primary weaknesses lie in the experimental section, which, while technically sound, fails to compellingly demonstrate a practical advantage over existing methods on standard benchmarks and misses an opportunity to showcase the model's extrapolation capabilities. Despite these limitations, the novelty and elegance of the underlying idea make this a solid contribution to the field. Researchers working on MOGPs are likely to find this work interesting and build upon these ideas.
