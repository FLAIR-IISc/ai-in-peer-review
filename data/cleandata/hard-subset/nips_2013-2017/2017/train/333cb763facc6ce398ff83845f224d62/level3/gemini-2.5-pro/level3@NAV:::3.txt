This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel and expressive covariance structure for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The method generalizes the well-known spectral mixture approach to the multivariate case by directly modeling a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities. By leveraging Cramér’s Theorem, the multivariate extension of Bochner's Theorem, the authors derive a valid matrix-valued covariance function from these densities. This provides a more principled and flexible framework than existing MOGP methods like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) or the more recent Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel.

The paper has numerous strengths. **(Quality)** The proposed method is theoretically sound, and its effectiveness is demonstrated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, where it performs competitively with or outperforms state-of-the-art MOGP models. **(Clarity)** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with clear motivation and relevant illustrations that effectively showcase the model's capabilities. **(Originality & Significance)** Designing expressive covariance functions for MOGPs is a significant challenge, and this work provides an elegant and novel solution. A key contribution is the interpretability of the model's parameters, which directly correspond to meaningful properties such as delays, phase differences, and channel-specific frequencies. This high degree of interpretability is a major advantage over many existing methods. If the associated optimization challenges can be overcome, MOSM has the potential to become a standard and widely used kernel for MOGP applications.

The main weakness of the paper is the lack of detail regarding parameter learning. The MOSM kernel introduces a large number of hyperparameters, and the optimization landscape is likely to be challenging. A discussion of initialization, potential training difficulties, or sensitivity to optimization choices would significantly strengthen the work. This raises a few questions:
1. How does the model behave in the low-data regime? Its flexibility might make it prone to overfitting with few training points.
2. How does the model handle correlations between channels with very different length-scales?

Finally, a few minor suggestions:
- For clarity, the synthetic example (Section 4.1) should explicitly state the total number of training points used across all processes.
- There appear to be minor typos, such as missing words, in a couple of places (e.g., lines 74, 140).

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the MOGP literature that addresses a difficult problem in a principled way.
