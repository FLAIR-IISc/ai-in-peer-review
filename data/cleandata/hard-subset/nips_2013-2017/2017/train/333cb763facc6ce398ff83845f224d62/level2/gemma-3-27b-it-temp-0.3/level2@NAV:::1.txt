## Review of "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of designing expressive and interpretable covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The authors critique existing approaches like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) for lacking interpretability and propose a novel kernel, the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture kernel (MOSM), built upon Cramér’s Theorem. The core idea is to define a family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and leverage the inverse Fourier transform to construct a covariance function that can model delays and phase differences between channels, offering greater flexibility than previous methods.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing MOGP approaches and positioning MOSM as a principled extension of the Spectral Mixture kernel. The mathematical development, building from Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems, is sound and the derivation of the MOSM kernel is presented logically. A key strength is the explicit parametric interpretation of the kernel’s components in spectral terms – frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay – which enhances its usability. The experimental validation on synthetic data effectively demonstrates the ability of MOSM to capture known relationships, and the comparison against existing methods on real-world datasets (climate signals and metal concentrations) provides a reasonable assessment of its performance.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of computational complexity. While GPflow is used for implementation, the scaling of MOSM with the number of outputs and data points isn’t explicitly addressed. Furthermore, the results on the real-world datasets are somewhat mixed. While MOSM performs well on the Jura dataset, the improvements are not consistently significant across all scenarios, and the climate data results show limited advantage over simpler models. The discussion of these results could be more nuanced.  Finally, while the connection to CSM is mentioned, a more detailed comparison highlighting the specific advantages of MOSM beyond the ability to model delays would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled approach to MOGP kernel design based on spectral methods.
*   Clear parametric interpretation of the kernel components.
*   Demonstrated ability to model delays and phase differences.
*   Competitive performance on real-world datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational complexity.
*   Mixed results on real-world datasets with lack of consistent statistical significance.
*   Could benefit from a more detailed comparison with related work (CSM).

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of MOGPs. While some aspects could be strengthened, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical soundness, and the promising experimental results suggest that this work is worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. The authors should consider expanding the discussion of computational cost and providing a more in-depth analysis of the experimental results.
