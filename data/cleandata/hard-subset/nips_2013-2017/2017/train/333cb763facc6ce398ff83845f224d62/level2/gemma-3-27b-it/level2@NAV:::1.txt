## Review of "Spectral Mixture Kernels for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes"

This paper proposes a novel approach to designing covariance functions for multi-output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs) based on extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel concept. The authors leverage Cramér’s Theorem to construct a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities, aiming for more expressive and interpretable MOGP kernels that can model delays between channels beyond simple phase shifts. The core idea – building kernels directly from spectral densities – is a sound and potentially powerful one, offering a principled way to ensure positive-definiteness.

The paper is well-structured, clearly outlining the limitations of existing MOGP approaches like LMC and CSM, and positioning their work as a natural extension. The theoretical foundation, built upon Bochner’s and Cramér’s Theorems, is presented concisely and accurately. The proposed Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel offers advantages in terms of parameter interpretability, allowing for explicit control over frequency, magnitude, phase, and delays in cross-covariance.

The experimental evaluation is a strength, demonstrating the method’s performance on synthetic data, clearly showing its ability to capture known relationships (derivatives and delays).  The comparison against existing methods on real-world datasets (climate and metal concentrations) is also valuable, although the performance gains are somewhat mixed, with the MOSM often competitive but not overwhelmingly superior. The statistical significance testing on the climate data adds credibility to the findings. 

However, the paper could be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion of the computational complexity of the MOSM kernel, particularly in high-dimensional output spaces. While the authors mention a potential future direction involving sparse implementations, a preliminary analysis would be beneficial. Additionally, the connection to existing work on complex-valued GPs could be more thoroughly explored.  The paper also lacks discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity and practical considerations for choosing the number of spectral components (Q).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to MOGP kernel design.
*   Clear theoretical motivation and derivation.
*   Demonstrated ability to model complex relationships (delays, phase shifts).
*   Solid experimental evaluation with comparisons to existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity not explicitly addressed.
*   Limited discussion on hyperparameter tuning and practical considerations.
*   Performance gains on real-world datasets are not always substantial.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of MOGPs. While some areas could benefit from further elaboration, the core idea is innovative and well-executed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the computational complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity aspects. The authors should also briefly discuss limitations and potential future work on scaling the method to higher dimensions.
