Summary
This paper proposes a new family of kernels for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. Building on Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate extension of Bochner’s theorem), the authors design a parametric, complex-valued cross-spectral density S(ω) that guarantees positive definiteness by factorizing S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω). Each column function Ri(ω) is a complex Gaussian whose parameters admit direct spectral interpretation: frequency (µi), lengthscales (Σi), magnitude (wi), delay (θi), and phase (φi). The induced time-domain cross-covariances kij(τ) are obtained in closed form via inverse Fourier transform and are sums over Q components: exponential envelopes times cosines with pair-specific amplitudes, frequencies, phases, and delays. This extends prior work on spectral kernels for single-output GPs [Wilson & Adams, ICML’13] and multi-output coregionalization/spectral approaches (LMC/SM-LMC and CSM; e.g., Ulrich et al., NIPS’15) by allowing both phase differences and channel delays, with pair-dependent parameters and clear interpretability.

The paper validates the approach on: (i) synthetic 3-output data where one channel is a derivative and another is a delayed version of a latent process; (ii) climate sensor imputation; (iii) the Jura heavy metal dataset. MOSM matches or outperforms baselines (CONV, SM-LMC, CSM), with clear gains on the synthetic task and the Cadmium prediction task.

Quality
- Theory: The construction via Cramér’s theorem and the PSD factorization is principled and, if implemented with S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), ensures positive semidefiniteness for each ω. The real-valuedness of the GP is enforced by symmetrizing the spectrum. The closed-form time-domain expressions are useful and interpretable.
- Technical concerns:
  - The exposition around Sij(ω) occasionally elides conjugation; Sij should be conj(Ri) Rj to ensure Hermitian S(ω). The paper mostly aligns with this (given the resulting phase/delay differences), but it would help to state Sij(ω) = conj(Ri(ω))Rj(ω) explicitly and keep 1/2 factors consistent across equations.
  - Identifiability: Only relative phase and delay are identifiable (φij, θij). Absolute φi and θi are not. It would be good to fix a reference channel (e.g., φ1=0, θ1=0) or discuss constraints/priors to avoid redundant degrees of freedom.
  - Optimization/Initialization: The parametrization introduces many parameters per component and per channel (wi, µi, Σi, θi, φi). The paper would benefit from details on initialization strategies (e.g., spectral density estimates per channel, shared µ initialization, tying/regularizing Σi), and whether any constraints are applied to mitigate local optima or overfitting.
  - Scalability: Training appears to use dense GP inference (O(N^3)). The climate experiment trains on a 1000-subsample, suggesting scalability limits. A discussion of computational cost, runtime, and memory, plus a sparse/inducing implementation (as suggested in the discussion) would strengthen the contribution.
- Experiments: The synthetic example is compelling and clearly illustrates the advantage of modeling both phase and delay. Real-data improvements are modest in the climate task (as expected due to high inter-sensor similarity) and clearer for Cadmium in Jura. Reporting standard deviations and conducting KS tests is appreciated; however, please add details on Q selection, number of random restarts, initialization, and sensitivity to Q and to the presence/absence of delay/phase parameters (ablation).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear motivation for designing in the spectral domain and a lucid interpretation of parameters. Minor issues to improve clarity:
- Ensure notational consistency: explicitly state Sij(ω) = conj(Ri)Rj and check the consistent use of 1/2 factors in Gaussian exponents across equations (8–10).
- Summarize the mapping from per-channel parameters (µi, Σi, θi, φi) to pairwise parameters (µij, Σij, θij, φij, αij) in a compact, error-checked form.
- Provide a short algorithmic description (training loop, parameter constraints) and complexity discussion.

Originality
The work meaningfully extends SM/CSM kernels to a more expressive multi-output setting by introducing per-channel spectral parameters and explicit delay modeling within a PSD factorization guaranteed by Cramér’s theorem. This unifies and generalizes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases. While spectral constructions for MOGPs exist, the specific factorized, complex cross-spectral design with explicit, interpretable delay and phase—and a closed-form time-domain kernel—is a notable contribution.

Significance
The approach brings increased expressiveness and interpretability to MOGPs, which is valuable for tasks with physical delay/phase relationships (sensor networks, multi-channel signals). The empirical gains are strongest where such structure matters (synthetic, Cadmium). With a scalable implementation (e.g., inducing spectral features), MOSM could see broader adoption. As presented, the significance is moderate to strong, with clear potential impact.

Strengths
- Principled, general construction via multivariate spectral theory.
- Interpretable parameters (magnitude, frequency, phase, delay) per output pair.
- Closed-form time-domain kernels and inclusion of prior methods as special cases.
- Strong synthetic demonstration; competitive real-data results.
- Thoughtful discussion of future sparse extensions.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarify conjugation and Gaussian exponent factors to avoid confusion.
- Discuss identifiability; fix a reference channel or add constraints/priors.
- Provide initialization details, number of restarts, and ablations (e.g., removing delay or phase).
- Report computational cost and scaling; consider adding a sparse/inducing baseline or preliminary results.
- Explore larger m or longer time series to test stability and scalability.
- Consider regularization or tying of parameters (e.g., sharing µ across channels in some components) to reduce variance on small datasets.

Questions for authors
- How are µi, Σi initialized across channels/components? Do you tie any parameters during early optimization?
- Have you tried fixing a reference channel (φ1=0, θ1=0) to address identifiability? Does it improve stability?
- How sensitive are results to Q? Could you report validation curves versus Q?
- What are the runtime and memory footprints for each experiment?
- Can MOSM handle improper/complex outputs if desired (without symmetrization), and would that offer advantages for certain signals?

Overall evaluation
A solid and well-motivated extension of spectral mixture kernels to the multi-output setting, with clear theoretical grounding, interpretability, and promising results. With clarifications and additional experimental details (initialization, scaling, ablations), this would be a strong contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
