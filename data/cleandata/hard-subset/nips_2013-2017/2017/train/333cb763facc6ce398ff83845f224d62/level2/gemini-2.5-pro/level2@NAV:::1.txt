This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a novel and expressive covariance function for Multi-Output Gaussian Processes (MOGPs). The work builds upon the foundational Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel by leveraging Cramér’s Theorem—the multivariate extension of Bochner’s Theorem—to construct a flexible family of cross-covariance functions from first principles. By defining a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities, the authors derive a kernel that can model not only phase differences, as in the related Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, but also explicit time delays between channels, all while maintaining interpretable parameters.

The primary strength of this paper is its significant and well-founded contribution to MOGP modeling. The derivation of the MOSM kernel is theoretically elegant, providing a principled approach to designing expressive multi-output kernels. The resulting model is highly flexible, and its unique ability to jointly model delays and phase shifts represents a notable advance over prior art. This superior expressiveness is convincingly demonstrated in the synthetic experiment, where MOSM successfully recovers the complex cross-covariance structures between a function, its derivative, and its delayed version—a challenging task where competitor models based on linear combinations of latent processes falter. The clear parametric interpretation is another major advantage for practitioners.

The main weakness is that the empirical evaluation on real-world datasets does not fully showcase the model's advanced capabilities. While MOSM performs competitively, the performance gains over the CSM model are often not statistically significant. This may indicate that the chosen datasets do not contain the complex inter-channel relationships that MOSM is specifically designed to capture. To strengthen the claims, the authors could apply their model to datasets where inter-channel delays are known to be a critical feature. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of limitations, such as the potential challenges of optimizing the large number of hyperparameters, which can be prone to local optima.

Despite this, the paper presents a clear, novel, and significant contribution. The theoretical development is sound, and the proposed kernel addresses an important gap in MOGP modeling. This work is a valuable addition to the literature and will likely inspire further research.
