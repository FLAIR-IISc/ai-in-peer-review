

This paper proposes a Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel from single-output to multi-output GPs. The key innovation is the introduction of a complex-valued cross-spectral density that enables modeling of both phase differences and delays between channels, which previous methods like Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) could not handle. The authors build upon Cramér's Theorem to construct a valid multivariate covariance function, ensuring positive definiteness through a factor decomposition of the spectral density matrix.

The paper is well-structured and technically sound, with clear theoretical foundations and proper connections to prior work. The synthetic experiment effectively demonstrates the model's ability to learn derivatives and delayed signals without prior knowledge of relationships. On real-world datasets (climate and heavy metal concentration), MOSM shows competitive performance, with statistically significant improvements over other methods for Cadmium prediction.

Strengths include: 1) A theoretically grounded approach to constructing MOGP kernels using spectral densities, providing clear parametric interpretation; 2) The ability to model delays between channels, which is a novel feature compared to existing methods; and 3) The synthetic experiment that effectively validates the claimed capabilities.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. First, the novelty claim requires refinement: while MOSM extends CSM by adding delay parameters, it's not clear how significantly this generalizes existing work. The delay parameter θij appears to be a natural extension of the phase shift parameter, and the paper doesn't sufficiently articulate why this generalization is significant or how it differs from previous work on modeling time lags in GPs.

Second, the climate dataset results are underwhelming. The paper acknowledges high signal correlation, but doesn't provide analysis of when MOSM would outperform CSM in less correlated settings—the primary use case for the delay parameter. The paper should include a more thorough analysis of when the delay parameter provides practical advantages.

Third, computational complexity is not discussed. For large-scale applications, the computational cost of MOSM (involving matrix operations for each channel pair) could be prohibitive compared to simpler approaches like LMC, which is increasingly important in modern machine learning applications.

Fourth, statistical significance testing is limited. While they use Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests for climate data, they don't perform similar tests for the heavy metal dataset, making it difficult to assess the true significance of reported improvements.

Finally, the paper doesn't compare with more recent MOGP methods presented at NIPS 2015-2017. For example, Hensman et al.'s "Variational Fourier Features" (NIPS 2016) could provide relevant context on scalability, which the authors should address.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a modest contribution to the MOGP literature. The extension of the spectral mixture approach to model delays is theoretically interesting, but the practical significance is limited by the experimental results and lack of comparison with more recent methods. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points above, particularly regarding the novelty claim, analysis of when the delay parameter provides advantages, and discussion of computational considerations.
