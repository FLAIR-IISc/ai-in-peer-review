This paper proposes a novel approach to constructing multi-output Gaussian process (MOGP) kernels, extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel concept to the multivariate case. The key idea is to define cross-spectral densities in the Fourier domain, leveraging Cramér’s Theorem to guarantee positive definiteness. The resulting “Multi-Output Spectral Mixture” (MOSM) kernel allows for modeling delays and phase differences between channels, providing a more expressive and interpretable alternative to existing MOGP methods.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. It effectively builds upon prior work in scalar GPs, spectral mixture kernels, and MOGPs. The authors correctly identify the limitations of earlier MOGP approaches like LMC and CSM, specifically their lack of interpretability and inability to model delays. The use of Cramér’s Theorem is appropriate and well-justified, providing a rigorous theoretical foundation for the proposed kernel construction. The explanation of the MOSM kernel's parameters in terms of spectral characteristics (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay) is a significant strength. The synthetic example is well-designed to demonstrate the ability of the model to learn derivatives and delayed signals, highlighting the benefits of the proposed approach.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the mathematical derivations are generally clear, they are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations at certain points, particularly regarding the complex-valued spectral densities and their connection to delays and phase shifts. The experimental evaluation, while thorough, doesn’t always convincingly demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing methods. On the climate data, the performance is comparable to other methods, and statistical significance is only confirmed for one sensor. The comparison to CSM is rather dismissive – while MOSM *can* represent CSM as a special case, the potential benefits of the more general formulation deserve more emphasis in the results discussion. The statement that the copper dataset's non-Gaussianity hinders performance of SM-LMC isn't sufficiently substantiated. It would be helpful to include visualizations of the learned kernels or cross-spectral densities to provide more insight into the model’s behavior. 

The writing quality is generally good, but some phrases are slightly awkward. More careful editing could improve clarity. The references are comprehensive and relevant. The inclusion of TensorFlow and GPflow implementation details is also beneficial for reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The MOSM kernel represents a significant advancement in MOGP kernel design, offering increased expressiveness and interpretability.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The use of Cramér’s Theorem provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for the proposed method.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper effectively explains the motivation and underlying principles of the MOSM kernel.
*   **Demonstrated Capability:** The synthetic example successfully demonstrates the ability to learn derivatives and delayed signals.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Performance Gains:** The performance improvements on the real-world datasets are not consistently significant.
*   **Dense Mathematical Formulation:** Some parts of the mathematical derivations could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
*   **Dismissive Comparison to CSM:** The benefits of the more general MOSM formulation could be better highlighted.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of MOGPs. While the experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing, the theoretical novelty and potential for improved modeling of complex relationships between outputs justify acceptance. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides a solid foundation for future research.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity concerns and to provide a more nuanced discussion of the experimental results, including visualizations of learned kernels and a more thorough comparison to CSM.
