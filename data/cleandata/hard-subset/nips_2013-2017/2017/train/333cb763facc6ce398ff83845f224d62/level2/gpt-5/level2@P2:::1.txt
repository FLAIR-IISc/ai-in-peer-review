Summary
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Building on Cramér’s theorem (the multivariate analogue of Bochner’s theorem), the authors design a parametric family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities using rank-Q factorizations S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), with each channel’s column Ri modeled as a complex squared-exponential (SE) in the frequency domain. Enforcing real-valued processes via spectral symmetrization yields, in closed form, a multi-output covariance with interpretable per-pair parameters: magnitude, frequency, bandwidth, phase, and delay. MOSM strictly generalizes prior SM-LMC and CSM kernels: compared to CSM, it introduces channel-specific spectral parameters and explicit inter-channel delays in addition to phase shifts. Experiments on synthetic data (derivatives and delays), climate sensors, and the Jura geostatistics benchmark show that MOSM can learn cross-relationships with delays and phases and is competitive or superior to SM-LMC, CONV, and CSM baselines.

Relation to prior work
This work connects to (i) single-output spectral mixture kernels (Wilson & Adams, ICML’13), (ii) multi-output formulations via LMC/CONV (Álvarez & Lawrence, NIPS’08), and (iii) CSM (Ulrich et al., NIPS’15). The use of Cramér’s theorem to construct valid multivariate kernels in the spectral domain is appropriate and aligns with other real-valued spectral constructions (e.g., Matérn cross-covariances). The paper clearly positions MOSM as a strict generalization of SM-LMC and CSM.

Quality
- Theory: The construction is principled. The positive semidefiniteness of S(ω) follows from the factorization S = RH R, and real-valuedness is ensured by spectral symmetrization S(ω) ← 0.5(S(ω) + S(−ω)). The inverse Fourier transform yields a closed-form kernel with delay and phase parameters. Two minor technical issues should be corrected for rigor and clarity:
  1) In Sec. 3.1, after introducing S = RH R, the text states Sij(ω) = Ri(ω)Rj(ω). This should read Sij(ω) = conj(Ri(ω)) Rj(ω) to match the Hermitian factorization and the later expressions for phase/delay.
  2) The text claims “both negatively- and positively-correlated signals (αij ∈ R)”; however αij as defined is nonnegative. Negative cross-covariances arise from the cosine factor, not from αij changing sign. Please rephrase.
- Identifiability: In 1D, φij and θij can interact through the term cos(μij τ + φij + μij θij) while the SE envelope centers at −θij. This helps identifiability, but in regimes with broad bandwidths Σij−1 or small |μij|, φ/θ trade-offs may persist. A brief discussion or practical constraints (e.g., priors tying θ across components) would strengthen the presentation.
- Parameterization and complexity: The per-channel, per-component parameters {wi, μi, Σi, θi, φi} lead to O(m Q (3n+2)) parameters. This expressivity is a strength, but may overfit in small-data or many-output settings, and optimization may be sensitive to initialization. Practical guidance (initialization via periodograms for μ, bandwidth from local spectral spreads, weak priors tying Σ across channels) would be helpful.
- Empirics: The synthetic task convincingly demonstrates MOSM’s unique ability to capture derivatives and delays. On climate and Jura, MOSM is competitive; it significantly outperforms on Cadmium but not on Copper, and is comparable to CSM on climate. The evaluation would benefit from:
  - Reporting runtime and optimization stability across methods.
  - Sensitivity to Q, and to initialization strategies.
  - Uncertainty calibration (e.g., NLL, predictive intervals) in addition to MAE.
  - Clarifying whether baseline hyperparameters were tuned comparably (e.g., Q shared across models may not be optimal for all).

Clarity
Overall well-written and mathematically clear, with intuitive interpretation of parameters. Suggested improvements:
- Fix the conjugation issue and the αij sign statement.
- Notation: Q is used as rank and then as number of mixture components; be explicit early that increasing rank is equivalent to adding components and eventually present the general Q case (which you do), but avoid ambiguity.
- Minor typos (e.g., “multiouput”, “assure”, “wrt”).
- Provide a concise summary of parameter counts and computational complexity.

Originality
Introducing explicit inter-channel delays in addition to phase within a general spectral mixture framework for MOGPs, derived via Cramér’s theorem and a complex SE factorization, is a meaningful and novel extension beyond CSM/SM-LMC.

Significance
The method offers a flexible, interpretable family of MOGP kernels that can encode realistic cross-channel structure (delays and phase). The synthetic results clearly showcase capabilities not available to latent-process LMC/CONV. Real-world improvements are modest but competitive; with better initialization and sparsification, MOSM could be impactful for multi-sensor time series, geostatistics, and spatiotemporal modeling.

Pros
- Principled construction with closed-form kernels via Cramér’s theorem.
- Interpretable per-pair parameters (frequency, bandwidth, phase, delay).
- Strict generalization of SM-LMC and CSM.
- Strong synthetic demonstration of capabilities (derivatives and delays).
- Competitive performance on real data; statistically significant gains in one task.

Cons
- Minor theoretical/notation inaccuracies (conjugation; αij statement).
- Potential over-parameterization; no discussion of regularization or sharing strategies.
- Limited analysis of optimization sensitivity and runtime.
- Real-world gains are modest; significance tests are limited.
- No scalable/sparse version demonstrated, which limits applicability to larger datasets.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct the conjugation and αij statements; add a short appendix confirming that symmetrization preserves positive semidefiniteness of S(ω).
- Discuss identifiability and provide practical parameter-tying or priors (e.g., shared Σ across channels, or structured θ priors).
- Include initialization heuristics (periodogram for μ, bandwidth from spectral peaks, φ/θ from cross-spectral phase) and a sensitivity study to Q and initialization.
- Report training time and numerical stability; consider comparing against additional structured multi-task kernels (e.g., separable kernels with coregionalization).
- Explore or at least prototype a sparse variational variant leveraging inter-domain Fourier features to demonstrate scalability.

Overall recommendation
A solid, principled, and interpretable contribution to MOGP kernels, with clear theoretical grounding and meaningful empirical validation. With minor corrections and expanded practical guidance, it merits acceptance.
