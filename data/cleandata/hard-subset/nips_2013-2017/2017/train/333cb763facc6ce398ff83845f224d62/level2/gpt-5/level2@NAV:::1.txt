This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, a principled construction of matrix-valued stationary covariance functions for multi-output GPs using complex cross-spectral densities and Cramér’s theorem. The core claims are: (1) MOSM generalizes SM and CSM kernels by enabling interpretable, parametric modeling of per-pair phase and delay while guaranteeing positive definiteness via a factorization of the cross-spectral density; (2) MOSM provides more expressive cross-covariances than LMC/CONV-based models; and (3) MOSM improves or matches state of the art on synthetic and two real datasets. The work builds on prior NIPS contributions on convolved/lattice multi-output GPs and spectral mixture kernels (Álvarez & Lawrence 2008; Ulrich et al. 2015), extending them with a full complex spectral parameterization and clear mapping between spectral parameters and cross-covariance features.

Quality: The theoretical construction is sound and well-motivated. The positive-definiteness argument via S(ω)=R(ω)H R(ω) is standard and appropriate; the resulting inverse transforms are derived in closed form. Experiments are reasonable: a synthetic task with known cross-covariances (derivative and delay) convincingly supports claim (1), showing MOSM uniquely recovers both relationships. Real-data imputation on climate and Jura yields competitive MAE, with modest gains and limited statistical validation (KS tests on subsets; more appropriate paired tests could be used).

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with correct use of spectral GP terminology and related work. Notational overload around Q (rank vs number of components) could be clarified, and training details (initialization, optimization settings, constraints) should be expanded for reproducibility. Public code availability would help.

Originality: The complex cross-spectral design with explicit pairwise phase and delay and a clean Cramér-based derivation is novel relative to SM-LMC/CSM, which are recovered as special cases. The parameter interpretability is a strength.

Significance: The ability to model delays and phase across channels in a principled, scalable kernel family is practically useful for multi-sensor time series. Empirical gains on real data are modest; broader benchmarks and ablations would strengthen impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled, general spectral construction with clear interpretability; subsumes prior SM-LMC/CSM.
- Strong synthetic evidence demonstrating unique capabilities (delay + phase).
- Clean theory-to-practice pipeline with closed-form kernels.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited empirical breadth and modest gains on real data; sparse/large-scale training not addressed.
- Statistical testing and reporting could be stronger; reproducibility details are light.
- Potential overparameterization/identifiability not analyzed; guidance on initialization lacking.

Suggestions:
- Add ablations isolating delay vs phase, rank >1 vs 1, and number of components.
- Use paired tests (e.g., Wilcoxon/paired t) across test points and include confidence intervals.
- Discuss complexity, identifiability, and practical initialization; consider a sparse MOSM baseline.
- Evaluate on more challenging multi-output tasks (e.g., EEG/MEG, motion capture, audio, spatiotemporal climate). Overall: Weak accept.
