This paper introduces the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, extending the spectral mixture concept to multi-output Gaussian processes. The core idea is to define a family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and leverage Cramér’s Theorem to construct multivariate covariance functions, enabling the modeling of delays and phase differences between channels. The work builds upon previous approaches like the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernels, aiming for greater expressiveness and parametric interpretability. The method is validated on synthetic data and benchmarked against existing approaches on two real-world datasets.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation and technical details of the proposed method. The connection to Bochner’s and Cramér’s theorems is effectively used to provide a theoretical foundation. The extension of the spectral mixture kernel to the multi-output setting is a logical and potentially valuable contribution.  The inclusion of delay and phase components within the kernel formulation is a notable enhancement. The experiments, including both synthetic and real-world data analysis, provide initial evidence of the method’s effectiveness. Related work is appropriately cited and contextualized. 

A strength of the work is the careful mathematical derivation and justification of the proposed kernel. The clear explanation of the parameters in the spectral domain enhances interpretability.  The validation on synthetic data demonstrates the ability to recover known characteristics, and the comparison on real-world datasets shows competitive performance. The authors also acknowledge limitations and potential future work, such as a sparse implementation. 

However, the paper could be improved in several areas. While the comparison to CSM is mentioned, the specific advantages of MOSM *beyond* the ability to model delays and phase shifts are not thoroughly articulated.  The experimental results, particularly on the climate data, do not show a clear and consistent advantage over existing methods. The statistical significance testing is limited to one case (Sotonmet), leaving the reader uncertain about the robustness of the findings. More detailed discussion of the parameter sensitivity and the computational cost of the proposed method would be beneficial.  The notation could be more consistent, for instance, clearly defining and using subscripts to differentiate between parameters associated with different channels (e.g., Σ<sub>ij</sub> vs. Σ<sub>i</sub>). The discussion of the complex-valued Gaussian processes could be expanded, as it is not a standard technique and may require more explanation for readers unfamiliar with the concept.  The choice of Q=1 for the rank of the PSD matrix is justified, but an exploration of higher ranks and their impact on performance and interpretability would be valuable.  Finally, the connection to existing methods like the Generalized Cross-Covariance (GCC) formulation in signal processing could be explored.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of the spectral mixture kernel to the multi-output setting.
*   Clear theoretical foundation based on Cramér's Theorem.
*   Ability to model delays and phase differences between channels.
*   Competitive performance on real-world datasets.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited demonstration of clear advantages over existing methods, particularly on the climate data.
*   Insufficient statistical significance testing.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity and computational cost.
*   Potential for improved clarity in notation and explanation of complex-valued GPs.



Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution to the field of multi-output Gaussian processes. While the experimental results could be more compelling, the theoretical novelty and potential for improved modeling capabilities justify acceptance, contingent on addressing the identified weaknesses through revisions. Essential revisions include performing more rigorous statistical significance testing, providing a more detailed discussion of the method’s limitations and computational cost, and clarifying the advantages over CSM beyond the delay and phase components. Optional revisions include exploring higher ranks for the PSD matrix and expanding on the complex-valued GP aspect.
