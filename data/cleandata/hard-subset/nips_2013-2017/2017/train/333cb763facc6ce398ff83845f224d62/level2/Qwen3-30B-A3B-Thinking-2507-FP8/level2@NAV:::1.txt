

This paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output setting using Cramér's Theorem. The main claim is that MOSM provides a principled approach to model cross-covariances with interpretable parameters for delays, phase shifts, and spectral content across channels, outperforming previous methods like Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) and Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) in specific scenarios.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding, clearly deriving MOSM from Cramér's Theorem and establishing its positive definiteness. The experimental validation is comprehensive: synthetic data shows MOSM successfully learns derivative and delayed relationships (where previous methods fail), while real-world experiments on climate data and heavy metal concentrations demonstrate competitive performance. The MOSM achieves statistically significant improvements over CSM for Cadmium prediction and shows consistent results across multiple datasets.

The paper's key strengths include its clear theoretical framework, excellent parameter interpretability in spectral terms, and well-designed experiments that validate the claimed advantages. The authors properly contextualize their work within the MOGP literature, acknowledging CSM as a special case of MOSM and demonstrating how MOSM generalizes previous approaches.

However, the paper could strengthen its contribution by more thoroughly addressing when MOSM should be preferred over simpler alternatives like CSM. The climate data results show MOSM's performance is comparable to CSM without clear statistical significance, raising questions about practical advantages. Additionally, the paper could better explain the trade-offs between model complexity and performance gains.

The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in MOGP modeling. While the claimed novelty in modeling delays is well-supported, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations and scenarios where MOSM provides clear advantages over existing methods. The authors have presented a valuable extension to MOGP kernels with strong theoretical foundations and promising experimental results that advance the state of the art in multi-output Gaussian processes. I recommend acceptance.
