This paper proposes a novel approach to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) based on extending the spectral mixture (SM) kernel concept to multiple outputs using Cramér’s theorem. The key idea is to design a family of complex-valued cross-spectral densities and then construct the covariance function via the inverse Fourier transform. This allows for modeling delays and phase differences between channels with full parametric interpretability. The paper provides a theoretical foundation, validation on synthetic data, and comparison with existing methods on two real-world datasets.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established framework of Gaussian processes and multi-output extensions. Early MOGP approaches, such as the Linear Model of Coregionalization (LMC) and Convolution model, are acknowledged as lacking interpretability. More recent work, like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel, improves interpretability by introducing phase shifts, but the authors argue this addition is somewhat *ad hoc*. The paper correctly positions its contribution as a more principled approach to spectral design, drawing parallels to the single-output SM kernel and leveraging Cramér’s theorem to ensure positive definiteness.  The related work section is adequate, covering key contributions in the field. The connection to work using spectral methods, like [6], is clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation based on Cramér’s theorem provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed kernel.  This addresses the positive definiteness concerns inherent in designing cross-covariances.
* **Interpretability:** The parametric form of the kernel, directly linked to spectral parameters like frequency, magnitude, phase, and delay, offers increased interpretability compared to earlier methods. This is a valuable contribution.
* **Expressiveness:** The ability to model delays *and* phase differences simultaneously offers increased flexibility and potentially better performance in applications where these effects are significant.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical derivations are presented clearly. The connection between the spectral density and the covariance function is well explained.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world data provide evidence of the method’s effectiveness, and the comparison to existing MOGP methods is a strong point.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While interpretability is a strength, the increased number of parameters in the MOSM kernel (compared to simpler methods) could make it more challenging to train and potentially prone to overfitting, especially with limited data. This is not thoroughly discussed.
* **Computational Cost:** The inverse Fourier transform involved in constructing the kernel might introduce computational overhead. The computational complexity is not addressed explicitly.
* **Limited Statistical Significance:** While the paper presents results, the statistical significance of improvements, especially on the real-world datasets, is somewhat weak. The KS test only shows significance for one sensor in the climate data and it is not clear why. More robust statistical testing would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Synthetic Data Setup:** The synthetic experiment focuses on a very specific scenario (derivative and delayed signal). While useful for validation, it doesn’t necessarily represent the breadth of challenges faced in real-world MOGP applications.
* **Connection to CSM:** The authors state that CSM is a special case of MOSM. While technically true, the conditions for this reduction are quite restrictive (identical parameters across channels). This feels somewhat downplayed.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to MOGP kernel design.
* The method offers improved expressiveness and interpretability over existing methods.
* The empirical results, while not overwhelmingly conclusive, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the underlying concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The increased complexity and potential computational cost are not adequately addressed.
* The statistical significance of the improvements on real-world datasets is limited.
* The synthetic experiment is somewhat restrictive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a solid contribution to the field of MOGPs. The theoretical grounding and increased expressiveness of the proposed kernel are valuable. While there are some weaknesses regarding complexity, computational cost, and statistical significance, these are not fatal.  The paper is of sufficient quality for acceptance, but I would recommend the authors address the reviewer concerns regarding computational complexity, statistical testing, and discussion of potential overfitting issues.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed kernel.
2.  Perform more thorough statistical analysis of the real-world results (e.g., paired t-tests, confidence intervals).
3.  Expand the discussion on the trade-off between model complexity and potential overfitting.
4.  Briefly address the limitations of the synthetic experiment and suggest directions for future work.
