- Proposal: Novel MOGP kernel extending the spectral mixture idea to multiple outputs via Cramér’s theorem, using complex cross-spectra and inverse Fourier transform to model delays and phase differences with parametric interpretability.  
- Validation: Theoretical foundation presented, tested on synthetic data and compared to existing methods on two real-world datasets.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on GP/MOGP literature (LMC, convolution) and improves on CSM by offering a more principled spectral-design approach; related work coverage is adequate.  
- Theoretical strength: Derivation via Cramér’s theorem ensures positive definiteness and provides strong theoretical soundness.  
- Interpretability strength: Kernel parameters map directly to spectral quantities (frequency, magnitude, phase, delay), improving interpretability over prior methods.  
- Expressiveness strength: Can model delays and phase differences simultaneously, increasing modeling flexibility.  
- Presentation strength: Clear writing and mathematical exposition, with a well-explained link between spectral density and covariance.  
- Empirical strength: Experiments on synthetic and real data demonstrate effectiveness and compare favorably to existing MOGP methods.  
- Complexity weakness: MOSM introduces many parameters, raising training difficulty and overfitting risk that isn’t thoroughly discussed.  
- Computational cost weakness: Potential overhead from inverse Fourier transform and overall computational complexity are not explicitly addressed.  
- Statistical-significance weakness: Real-world improvements show limited statistical significance (KS test only significant for one sensor); more robust testing is needed.  
- Synthetic experiment weakness: Synthetic setup is narrowly focused (derivative and delayed signal) and may not reflect broader MOGP challenges.  
- CSM connection caveat: Claim that CSM is a special case is technically true but requires restrictive parameter conditions and is somewhat downplayed.  
- For acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound, more interpretable/expressive, empirically promising, and well-written.  
- Against acceptance: Unaddressed complexity/computational concerns, limited statistical evidence, and restrictive synthetic validation.  
- Overall: Solid contribution deserving acceptance with minor revisions to address noted concerns.  
- Required revisions: Discuss computational complexity, perform more thorough statistical analyses (e.g., paired tests, CIs), expand on model complexity vs overfitting trade-offs, and acknowledge synthetic experiment limitations with future work directions.
