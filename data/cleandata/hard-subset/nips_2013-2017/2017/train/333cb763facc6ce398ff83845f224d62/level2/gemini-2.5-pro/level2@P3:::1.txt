### Summary

This paper introduces a novel, flexible kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs), termed the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel. The approach extends the work on single-output Spectral Mixture (SM) kernels by designing a parametric family of cross-spectral densities in the Fourier domain. By leveraging Cramér's theorem (the multivariate generalization of Bochner's theorem), the work provides a principled way to construct a valid matrix-valued covariance function. The proposed cross-spectral densities are constructed via a factor decomposition of the spectral density matrix, which ensures the resulting multivariate kernel is positive definite. A key contribution is that the resulting MOSM kernel can explicitly model not only magnitude and frequency relationships but also phase differences and time delays between different output channels, offering greater expressiveness than previous methods like the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a synthetic task designed to highlight its unique capabilities and on two real-world datasets, where it is shown to be competitive with or superior to existing MOGP models.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development is well-founded, building logically from Cramér's theorem to the final form of the MOSM kernel. The use of a rank-Q factor decomposition to construct a Hermitian positive-definite spectral density matrix is an elegant and correct way to guarantee the validity of the resulting MOGP kernel.

The claims are well-supported by the provided experiments. The synthetic experiment, which involves learning the relationships between a function, its derivative, and a delayed version, is particularly effective. It clearly illustrates a scenario where the unique properties of the MOSM kernel (specifically, its ability to model delays and non-symmetric cross-covariances) are essential for good performance, and where simpler models like SM-LMC and CONV fail. The experiments on real-world climate and geological data demonstrate that the model performs competitively against state-of-the-art MOGP methods.

To further strengthen the paper, the analysis of the real-world experiments could be expanded. While the MAE results are presented, it would be highly informative to include an analysis of the learned hyperparameters for these datasets. For instance, do the learned delay (`θ_ij`) and phase (`φ_ij`) parameters on the climate or Jura data take on non-trivial values? Showing that the model leverages its additional flexibility to discover meaningful inter-channel relationships in real data would provide stronger evidence for its practical significance beyond the synthetic case.

### Clarity

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The background section provides the necessary prerequisites on SM kernels and MOGPs for the reader to understand the main contribution. The derivation of the MOSM kernel in Section 3 is presented step-by-step and is easy to follow. The figures, particularly Figure 1, are helpful in building intuition for the kernel's parameters.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to provide a brief discussion on the complexity of the model, both in terms of computation and the number of hyperparameters. The MOSM kernel introduces a significant number of parameters compared to some alternatives. A comment on the potential challenges for optimization and any strategies employed (e.g., initialization) would be beneficial for practitioners seeking to apply this model.

### Originality

The work is highly original. While the idea of designing kernels in the spectral domain is not new, its application to the multi-output case via a direct and flexible parameterization of the cross-spectral density matrix using Cramér's theorem is a novel and significant contribution. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from prior work. It correctly identifies the CSM kernel as a special case of MOSM where delays are not modeled and other parameters are shared across channels. The principled construction of a kernel that can jointly model delays and phase shifts is a key innovation that pushes the state-of-the-art in expressive covariance functions for MOGPs.

### Significance

The proposed MOSM kernel is a significant contribution to the literature on Gaussian processes. It provides a powerful and highly expressive tool for modeling vector-valued data that is likely to be useful for researchers and practitioners in many fields where MOGPs are applied, such as geostatistics, econometrics, and multi-sensor signal processing. The interpretability of the kernel's parameters (frequency, delay, phase) is a major advantage. By providing a general framework that subsumes previous spectral MOGP kernels, this work advances our understanding of how to construct rich covariance structures. The future work suggested by the paper, namely developing a sparse implementation, points to a clear and important path for making this expressive model scalable to larger datasets, which would further increase its impact.

### Arguments for acceptance:

*   **Principled and Novel Theory:** The paper provides a well-founded and elegant method for constructing expressive MOGP kernels based on Cramér's theorem.
*   **Increased Expressiveness:** The resulting MOSM kernel can model delays and phase shifts between outputs, a key capability missing in prior spectral MOGP models. This is clearly demonstrated in a compelling synthetic experiment.
*   **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making the technical contribution easy to understand and appreciate.
*   **Strong Potential Impact:** The proposed kernel is a powerful addition to the MOGP toolbox and is likely to be widely adopted and built upon.

### Arguments against acceptance:

*   **Mixed Real-World Results:** While competitive, the performance gains on the two real-world datasets are not uniformly dramatic, which might suggest that the added model complexity is not always necessary. A more detailed analysis of the learned parameters on these datasets would help clarify when the model's full expressiveness is beneficial.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its strong theoretical contribution and novelty, far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a complete and significant piece of research.
