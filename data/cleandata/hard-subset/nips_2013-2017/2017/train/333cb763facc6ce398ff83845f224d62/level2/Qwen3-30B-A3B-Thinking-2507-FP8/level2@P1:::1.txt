

This paper proposes a novel approach to multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs) by extending the Spectral Mixture (SM) kernel to the multi-output case through the design of complex-valued cross-spectral densities. The authors introduce the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel, which leverages Cramér's Theorem to construct valid multivariate covariance functions that can model delays between channels in addition to phase differences. The theoretical foundation is solid, building on Bochner's Theorem (extended to multivariate processes via Cramér's Theorem) to provide a principled way to design cross-covariances with clear parametric interpretation.

The paper's main strengths include a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a key limitation in previous MOGP approaches (specifically, the inability to model delays between channels). The MOSM kernel extends the Cross-Spectral Mixture (CSM) kernel by incorporating delay parameters through complex-valued spectral densities, which is validated through both synthetic experiments and real-world applications. The synthetic example demonstrating the ability to learn derivatives and delayed signals is particularly compelling, as it shows MOSM's superiority over latent process-based methods (like SM-LMC and CONV) in scenarios where cross-covariances cannot be expressed as linear combinations of univariate kernels. The experimental validation on climate data and heavy metal concentration datasets demonstrates competitive performance, with MOSM outperforming other methods on the Cadmium prediction task (statistically significant at α=0.05).

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a thorough background section that carefully contextualizes the work within the literature on MOGPs. The authors properly cite relevant prior work, including the SM kernel (Wilson et al., 2013), CSM (Ulrich et al., 2015), and related approaches. The mathematical presentation is precise, and the connection between the spectral domain design and the resulting covariance functions is clearly explained.

However, there are a few areas for improvement. First, while the paper demonstrates MOSM's ability to model delays and phase shifts, it would be beneficial to include a more detailed discussion of how the additional parameters (θij and φij) specifically contribute to the model's performance in the real-world examples. The current presentation focuses on the model's ability to capture these features but doesn't explicitly link parameter values to performance improvements. Second, the comparison with the CSM kernel is somewhat limited; while the paper correctly identifies CSM as a special case of MOSM, a more nuanced comparison of how the additional flexibility of MOSM translates to performance gains across different datasets would strengthen the argument. Third, the computational complexity of MOSM is not discussed, which is important for practical applications. While the authors note they implemented the model using GPflow, a brief discussion of scalability considerations would be valuable.

The significance of this work is clear: it provides a more expressive kernel for MOGPs that can capture richer relationships between outputs, which is valuable for applications like fault detection, data imputation, and denoising. The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial, and the empirical results support the claim that MOSM can model relationships that previous methods cannot. The ability to jointly model delays and phase differences is particularly novel and useful for real-world time-series applications.

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the high correlation in the climate data leading to similar performance between MOSM and CSM) and provide appropriate context for their results.

In summary, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Gaussian processes by extending the spectral mixture approach to multi-output settings with the ability to model delays between channels. The theoretical foundation is strong, the experiments are well-designed, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over previous methods in specific scenarios. The work is original, significant, and well-executed, making it a strong candidate for NIPS 2017. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points above, particularly regarding the detailed interpretation of the delay parameters and a more thorough comparison with CSM.
