Summary
The paper proposes the Multi-Output Spectral Mixture (MOSM) kernel for multi-output Gaussian processes (MOGPs). Building on Cramér’s theorem, it constructs complex-valued, Hermitian positive-definite cross-spectral density matrices via a factorization S(ω) = R(ω)H R(ω), with each column Ri(ω) modeled as a complex Gaussian in frequency. After enforcing conjugate symmetry to ensure real-valued processes, the inverse Fourier transform yields closed-form, stationary cross-covariances that (i) have spectral-mixture structure, (ii) admit phase shifts and, crucially, (iii) include explicit inter-channel delays. The model subsumes SM-LMC and CSM as special cases by allowing channel-specific frequency means, bandwidths, phases, and delays. Experiments include: a synthetic task involving a latent function, its derivative, and a delayed version; climate sensor imputation; and heavy metal concentration prediction. MOSM matches or improves upon SM-LMC, CSM, and convolutional MOGPs, with clear gains in the synthetic and Cadmium tasks.

Relation to prior work
- Spectral mixture kernels for single-output GPs: [6]. The paper adopts the “design S(ω), invert to k(τ)” paradigm.
- Multi-output kernels via LMC/ICM and process convolutions: [3, 4, 12, 13].
- Cross-spectral mixture for MOGPs with phase shifts: [5]. MOSM extends this by allowing delays and per-channel spectral parameters, and derives the construction from Cramér’s theorem.
- Multivariate Matérn: [17] offers parametric cross-covariances but without explicit delay/phase interpretability.
- Sparse and inter-domain methods that could enable scalability: [21–23].

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and principled construction: Uses Cramér’s theorem and a PSD factorization to guarantee positive definiteness while enabling expressive cross-covariances.
- Interpretability: Parameters map to magnitudes, frequencies, bandwidths, phases, and delays per pair of outputs.
- Expressiveness beyond CSM/SM-LMC: Simultaneously models phase and delay, and allows channel-specific spectral characteristics.
- Closed-form kernels: Efficient evaluation and gradients; straightforward integration into standard GP training.
- Empirical validation: Synthetic example elegantly demonstrates recovery of derivative and delayed cross-covariances; competitive performance on real data.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Scalability and complexity: Training remains O(N^3), and the number of hyperparameters scales as O(mQn) with per-output parameters; guidance on selecting Q and regularization is limited.
- Initialization and identifiability: Learning phases and delays alongside frequencies can be multi-modal; no description of initialization heuristics (e.g., periodogram-based) or priors/constraints to mitigate confounding between φ and θ.
- Rank structure: Each component is rank-1 in output space; overall cross-spectral rank ≤ Q. Discussion of when this is sufficient for complex inter-output dependencies is brief.
- Evaluation metrics: Focus on MAE; no assessment of uncertainty quality (e.g., predictive log-likelihood, calibration), which is important for GP-based models.
- Statistical testing: Using KS tests on MAE may not be ideal for paired comparisons; paired tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank) would better reflect per-split differences.
- Clarity point: The text states “allows for negatively- and positively-correlated signals (αij ∈ R)”, but αij is nonnegative; the cross-covariance changes sign via the cosine term. A minor phrasing fix would avoid confusion.

Quality
The construction appears technically sound: factorization ensures PSD in the spectral domain; symmetry enforces real-valued processes; inverse Fourier transforms yield closed-form kernels with delay and phase. Empirical studies are carefully implemented and include comparisons to relevant baselines. More detail on optimization (initialization, priors, constraints) would strengthen reproducibility and robustness claims.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with clear derivations and parameter interpretations. Adding a concise complexity/parameter-count summary, an explicit discussion of rank, and a short subsection on initialization and practical training tips would improve clarity. Correcting the minor wording around αij would help.

Originality
The work provides a principled multivariate spectral-mixture construction that simultaneously enables phase and delay, extends CSM/SM-LMC, and frames the design via Cramér’s theorem with complex PSDs. This combination and the resulting closed-form cross-covariances represent a meaningful incremental advance.

Significance
The approach offers a flexible, interpretable MOGP kernel likely to be useful in multi-sensor time series and spatial-temporal modeling. While real-data gains are modest in some settings, the synthetic and Cadmium results demonstrate scenarios where added expressiveness matters. A scalable variant could increase practical impact.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Provide practical guidance: parameter count vs. m, Q, n; complexity per iteration; recommendations for choosing Q.
- Document initialization strategies for μ, Σ, φ, θ (e.g., channel-wise spectral peaks, cross-spectral phase slopes for delays), and any priors/constraints to reduce confounding.
- Report predictive log-likelihood and calibration (e.g., coverage) alongside MAE.

Nice-to-have:
- Ablation: compare MOSM to variants with φ=0 and θ=0, and with shared μ/Σ across channels, to quantify gains from phase/delay and per-channel spectra.
- Extend baselines to include multivariate Matérn [17] and ICM to contextualize performance across classes of multi-output kernels.
- Inspect learned delays/phases in climate data for physical plausibility and interpretability.
- Discuss the implications of rank ≤ Q in the spectral domain and provide guidance on when Q needs to be large.

Pros
- Principled, interpretable derivation from Cramér’s theorem.
- More expressive than CSM/SM-LMC by modeling both phase and delay with per-channel spectra.
- Closed-form kernels with clear parameter semantics.
- Strong synthetic results; competitive real-data performance.

Cons
- Scalability and training details under-specified; potential identifiability challenges.
- Limited uncertainty evaluation and statistical testing choices.
- Rank-1-per-component restriction and stationarity may limit some applications.
