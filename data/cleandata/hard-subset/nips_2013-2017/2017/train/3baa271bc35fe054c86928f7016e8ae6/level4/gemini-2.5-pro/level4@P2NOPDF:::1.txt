This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The objective is to select a summary set T from a ground set E, processed in a single pass, that is resilient to adversarial deletions. Specifically, the guarantee is that after an adversary removes any `m` elements from T, it is still possible to extract a `k`-element subset S from the remaining elements of T that achieves a high value for a given monotone submodular function `f`.

Submodular maximization is a fundamental problem with well-established applications in machine learning, and its streaming variants are an active area of research. This work introduces a robustness requirement motivated by scenarios where an initial solution is subject to subsequent modifications. For example, in a recommender system, a user might be presented with a set of items but may reject a subset of them. The proposed model aims to ensure that the remaining options still contain a high-quality selection.

The primary contribution is a single-pass streaming algorithm that achieves a constant-factor approximation for this robust maximization problem. The algorithm returns a summary set T of size O((m log k + k) log^2 k).

**Comments:**

The technical contributions of the paper appear to be sound and significant. The proposed algorithm is novel, and its analysis is non-trivial and technically sophisticated. To the best of my assessment, the results are correct.

However, my primary concern is the paper's relevance and potential impact on the NIPS community. While the problem is theoretically interesting, the motivation for this specific formulation feels underdeveloped. The presented applications are somewhat generic, and the link to pressing real-world machine learning problems is not convincingly established. This weakness in motivation raises questions about the generality and utility of the proposed model. A stronger justification for why this particular adversarial deletion model is important, and how it relates to or improves upon other notions of robustness, would be necessary to strengthen the paper's contribution.

In summary, the paper offers a technically impressive solution to a difficult algorithmic problem. However, its overall impact is limited by a weakly motivated problem statement that may be too narrow to capture the interest of a broad machine learning audience.
