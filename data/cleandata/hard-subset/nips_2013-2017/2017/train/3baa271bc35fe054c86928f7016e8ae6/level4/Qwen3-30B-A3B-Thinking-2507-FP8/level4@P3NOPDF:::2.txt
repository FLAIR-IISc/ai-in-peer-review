

This paper introduces a two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint \(k\). The first stage employs a novel streaming algorithm, STAR-T, which leverages a partitioning structure and a decreasing thresholding rule to handle element losses. The second stage applies a simple greedy method. The authors establish a constant-factor approximation guarantee for STAR-T as \(k \to \infty\) (Theorem 4.1) and prove via Lemmas 4.2–4.3 that the combined STAR-T-Greedy framework effectively manages element loss. Experiments demonstrate competitive performance on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation tasks.  

The work is significant as the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, addressing a gap with clear potential for applications in large-scale streaming settings. A notable practical advantage is the minimal hyperparameter sensitivity: while STAR-T involves parameters \(\tau\) and \(w\), Theorem 4.5 provides a principled method for setting \(\tau\), and the experiments show that \(w=1\) suffices across all tested scenarios, eliminating the need for hyperparameter tuning. This convenience enhances the method’s usability.  

However, two critical issues require clarification. First, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient expressiveness. The STAR-T-Greedy algorithm does not consistently outperform the Sieve-Streaming (Sieve-St) baseline across datasets or metrics (e.g., Table 2 shows Sieve-St achieves higher influence spread in 3/5 datasets). The paper attributes this to "robustness," but without statistical significance testing or analysis of *why* STAR-T-Greedy underperforms in specific cases, the claim of robustness remains unsubstantiated. A more thorough comparison—including runtime analysis, sensitivity to loss rates, and additional baselines (e.g., Robust-Greedy) would strengthen the empirical contribution.  

Second, the objective function for influence maximization is inconsistent with the standard model in Kempe et al. (SIGKDD 2003). The paper defines \(f(Z) = |N(Z) \cup Z|\) (line 219), which includes the seed set \(Z\) itself. In contrast, Kempe’s model uses \(f(Z) = |\bigcup_{v \in Z} N(v)|\) (the spread of influence from \(Z\) to neighbors, excluding \(Z\)). This discrepancy is problematic because:  
1. The paper claims to address Kempe’s influence maximization problem but uses a different objective.  
2. The definition \(f(Z) = |N(Z) \cup Z|\) corresponds to the *closed neighborhood* model, which is less common and may not align with the problem’s intended interpretation.  
The authors must clarify which model they adopt, justify their choice, and demonstrate that STAR-T-Greedy’s performance is meaningful *under their chosen objective*. Without this, the experimental results for influence maximization lack contextual validity.  

The theoretical contributions are sound but require minor precision. Theorem 4.1 states a constant approximation "when \(k\) is up to infinity," which is misleading; the guarantee holds for arbitrary \(k\) (as implied by the proof), not just as \(k \to \infty\). The phrasing should be corrected to "for any \(k\)" or "for arbitrary \(k\)." Additionally, while Lemmas 4.2–4.3 establish element loss handling, the connection to the streaming setting (e.g., how losses occur during the stream) could be more explicitly motivated.  

Overall, the paper presents a novel and practically useful algorithm for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the hyperparameter simplicity is a strong point. However, the experimental section must be substantially revised to address the inconsistent baseline comparisons and the objective function discrepancy. The current results do not convincingly support the claims of robustness or superiority over existing methods.  

**Recommendation**: Minor Revision. The authors should:  
1. Re-evaluate the experimental comparisons with Sieve-St (and other baselines), including statistical tests and analysis of performance variance across datasets.  
2. Explicitly define and justify the influence maximization objective (closed vs. open neighborhood), and re-run experiments under the standard Kempe model to validate claims.  
3. Correct the phrasing in Theorem 4.1 and clarify the element loss mechanism in the streaming context.  

With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field. As presented, the experimental weaknesses undermine the practical significance of the theoretical advances.
