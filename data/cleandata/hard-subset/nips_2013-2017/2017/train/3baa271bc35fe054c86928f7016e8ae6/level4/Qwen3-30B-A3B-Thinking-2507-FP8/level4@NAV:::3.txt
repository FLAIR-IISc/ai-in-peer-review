

This paper addresses the challenging problem of submodular maximization in a streaming and robust setting, where the goal is to select k high-value elements from a small retained set after processing a stream, while remaining resilient to the removal of m elements post-stream. Streaming submodular maximization has been extensively investigated due to large-scale applications requiring limited data passes, and robustness has recently gained traction for scenarios involving potential element removals. This work constitutes the first to jointly address both streaming and robustness aspects.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains O((m log k + k) log² k) elements after the stream and achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of 0.149 while being robust to m element removals. STAR-T integrates partitioning and thresholding techniques using buckets, adapting bucket-filling strategies from prior robust submodular work. Experimental results demonstrate strong performance in influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, even against streaming algorithms that anticipate removals in advance.

While the joint problem setting is complex and the constant-factor approximation represents a significant theoretical contribution, the paper has several weaknesses. The absence of non-trivial lower bounds to complement the upper bound is notable. The analysis description is inadequate; despite claiming "innovative analysis," the proof sketch fails to clearly articulate the novel elements or core steps. Furthermore, the influence maximization experiments lack convincing power: the simplified objective (based solely on seed node neighborhood size) does not align with standard influence maximization formulations where function values depend on the entire network structure for any seed set, leaving unclear how such objectives would operate within the streaming framework.
