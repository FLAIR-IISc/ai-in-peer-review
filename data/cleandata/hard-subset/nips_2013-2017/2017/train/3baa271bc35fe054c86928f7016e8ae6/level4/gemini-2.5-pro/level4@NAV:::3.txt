This work addresses the problem of submodular maximization within a combined streaming and robust framework. In this setting, the goal is to identify a high-value set of `k` elements from a small memory buffer populated during a data stream, under the condition that up to `m` items might be removed after the stream concludes. Streaming submodular maximization is a well-established field, driven by large-scale applications where limited passes over the data are practical. Similarly, robustness has recently become an area of interest, motivated by situations where chosen elements may become unavailable, requiring solutions that can withstand such removals. This paper is the first to jointly consider the challenges of streaming and robust submodular maximization.

The authors introduce the STAR-T algorithm, which retains `O((m log k + k )log^2 k)` elements and provides a constant (0.149) approximation guarantee, while also being robust to the deletion of `m` items. The method leverages a combination of partitioning and thresholding techniques common in submodular optimization, utilizing a bucket-based approach. While similar bucket-based ideas have appeared in previous work on robust submodular optimization, this algorithm employs a distinct method for filling the buckets. The paper's experiments demonstrate that the algorithm performs effectively in influence maximization and movie recommendation applications, even when compared against streaming algorithms that have oracle access to the identities of the removed elements.

Given the dual challenges of the streaming and robust setting, the problem is formidable, and obtaining a constant factor approximation is a strong theoretical contribution. However, the paper has several aspects that could be enhanced. The theoretical results would be stronger if complemented by non-trivial lower bounds to situate the algorithm's upper bound. The presentation of the analysis could also be improved; the authors describe their analysis as "innovative" but do not explain what makes it so, and the proof sketch does not make the main ideas and logical steps easy to follow. Finally, the influence maximization application could be more persuasive. The experiments utilize a highly simplified objective based solely on the neighborhood size of the seed nodes. It is not apparent how traditional influence maximization objectives, where the function value for any seed set is dependent on the entire network, would be feasible in a streaming context.
