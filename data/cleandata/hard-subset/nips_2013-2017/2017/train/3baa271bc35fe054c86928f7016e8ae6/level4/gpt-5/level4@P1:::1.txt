Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies a robust variant of streaming submodular maximization. The goal is to construct, in a single pass over a stream, a set T such that after deleting any set of m elements from T, one can still select a size-k subset S ⊆ T \ E with high objective value for a monotone submodular function f. This robust setting is motivated by scenarios where items must be removed post hoc (e.g., a user rejects certain recommended movies). The paper gives a constant-factor approximation while storing a summary of size O((m log k + k) log^2 k). There are known streaming algorithms for the non-robust problem; this work addresses robustness to deletions.

Quality:
To the best of my understanding, the results are correct. The solution is non-trivial, and both the algorithm and its analysis are technically challenging. The paper claims a constant-factor approximation with the stated memory bound, achieved via a structured streaming summary and a post-hoc greedy selection after deletions.

Clarity:
The core problem and high-level approach are understandable: build a streaming summary T that remains useful after removing any m elements, and then run a greedy selection over the remainder. However, the applications and motivation presented are not very compelling, which weakens the narrative for why this robust variant is essential in practice.

Originality:
The problem formulation is interesting and the approach appears novel relative to standard streaming submodular maximization. The method and analysis to achieve robustness to arbitrary deletions in a one-pass streaming setting are non-trivial.

Significance:
While the technical contribution is solid, I have concerns about relevance to the conference audience. The applications and their motivation seem weak, and the problem scope feels narrow. This reduces the perceived impact for a broad machine learning venue, despite the correctness and technical depth of the work.

Pros:
- Addresses a meaningful robustness question in streaming submodular maximization.
- Provides a constant-factor approximation with a concrete memory bound O((m log k + k) log^2 k).
- Algorithm and analysis are technically sophisticated.
- Results appear correct.

Cons:
- Applications and motivating scenarios are not convincingly developed.
- The problem seems not general enough to appeal broadly to the conference community.
- Relevance and impact for NIPS are unclear given the current motivation.

Overall assessment:
The paper delivers a correct and technically challenging solution with a clear approximation and memory guarantee for robust streaming submodular maximization. However, the practical motivation and breadth of impact are limited, and I am not convinced of its fit for the conference as currently framed.
