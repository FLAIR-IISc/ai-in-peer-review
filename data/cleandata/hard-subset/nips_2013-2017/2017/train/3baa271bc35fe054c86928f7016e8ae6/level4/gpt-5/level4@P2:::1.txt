Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in a single-pass streaming setting, with the added requirement that up to m elements may be deleted after the stream ends. The authors propose a two-stage approach. In the streaming stage, STAR-T maintains a compact summary S by partitioning memory into exponentially sized buckets and using exponentially decreasing marginal-gain thresholds to decide placement. In the query stage, after any set E of at most m elements is removed, running GREEDY on S\E returns a k-element solution. The paper proves a constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149 (approaching this constant as k grows), with memory O((k + m log k) log k) if the optimal value is known (and with a standard parallel-threshold trick à la SIEVE-STREAMING, memory grows by a log_{1+ε} factor to eliminate dependence on OPT).

This work connects to (i) classical GREEDY for submodular maximization (Nemhauser et al.), (ii) streaming algorithms such as SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al.), (iii) robust non-streaming methods (e.g., Orlin–Schulz–Udwani; PRO by Bogunovic et al. with partitioned structures), and (iv) concurrent deletion-robust streaming work (Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17), which achieves a 1/2−ε guarantee with O(mk log k/ε) memory. The contribution here is a streaming algorithm with robustness to arbitrary deletions, leveraging a PRO-like partitioned structure but placing elements via thresholds rather than greedy filling, yielding logarithmic dependence on k in memory (outside the parallelization factor).

Quality:
- The algorithmic idea and analysis appear technically sound. The case analysis (either many full buckets or few) is plausible, and the use of GREEDY’s approximation and submodularity to bridge between bounds is standard yet nontrivial here.
- The proof sketches are coherent, but a few points would benefit from clarification to ensure rigor:
  - The “on average” argument in Lemma 4.2 (existence of a full bucket minimally affected by E) should be stated formally (e.g., via a counting or averaging argument).
  - Line 5 of Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}, whereas the earlier description states τ/2^i. Please reconcile these for consistency.
  - There is an inconsistency in the memory bound: the abstract/conclusion mention O((k + m log k) log^2 k), while Theorem 4.1 states O((k + m log k) log k). Please unify and explain which bound holds under which assumptions (e.g., with/without OPT guessing).
  - Provide explicit time complexity per item for STAR-T (number of threshold checks) and query-time complexity for STAR-T-GREEDY.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The two-stage setting and partitioned-threshold structure are explained intuitively. However, the dependence on the unknown OPT and the parallelized guessing scheme could be described more concretely (choice of ε, number of instances, and practical overhead). Notation is mostly consistent; minor issues as noted above.

Originality:
The combination of a PRO-style partitioned layout with streaming threshold placement for deletion robustness is new. Compared to concurrent deletion-robust streaming work, this method trades a smaller approximation ratio for substantially improved memory scaling in k. This is a meaningful algorithmic trade-off.

Significance:
The problem is relevant to data summarization with “right-to-be-forgotten,” failures, or preference shifts. The theoretical result is a first constant-factor guarantee with polylog memory in k and linear in m up to logs. Empirically, the method matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (even when the latter knows deletions), on dominating set and personalized recommendation. That said, the practical impact is limited by a modest constant (≈0.149) and experiments that, while indicative, are relatively narrow and do not compare against the concurrent deletion-robust streaming baseline.

Pros for acceptance:
- Introduces a novel, principled streaming structure for robust submodular maximization with a clear analysis.
- Memory-efficient summary with one pass; deletion-agnostic and reusable for multiple queries.
- Solid connection to and extension of well-known streaming and robust submodular techniques.
- Empirical results show competitive performance despite a weaker theoretical constant.

Cons for acceptance:
- Approximation constant is low compared to known 1/2−ε guarantees (albeit with better memory scaling); discussion of this trade-off could be deeper.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: few datasets, no comparison to the deletion-robust streaming algorithm of Mirzasoleiman et al. (ICML’17), and limited stress-testing across m/k regimes.
- Some inconsistencies in stated memory bounds and thresholds; clarity issues around OPT-guessing overhead.
- Application motivation feels somewhat narrow; the paper could better articulate broader NIPS-relevant use cases.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Reconcile and clearly state the memory bound(s), and unify the threshold rule across text and pseudocode.
- Include a head-to-head comparison with the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method, or at least a careful discussion of when STAR-T’s memory advantages outweigh its smaller constant in practice.
- Add sensitivity analyses: varying m relative to k, varying w, adversarial vs. random deletions, and query-time algorithm choice (GREEDY vs. SIEVE on S\E).
- Report runtime and throughput, and the practical overhead of maintaining multiple threshold guesses.
- Expand experimental scope (e.g., larger or more diverse datasets) and provide ablations illustrating the effect of the partitioned thresholds.

Overall assessment:
Technically solid and novel, with an appealing memory–approximation trade-off for robust streaming submodular maximization. While the empirical motivation and breadth could be stronger, the work is relevant to scalable learning and summarization. I lean toward a borderline accept if the clarity and evaluation issues can be addressed; otherwise borderline.
