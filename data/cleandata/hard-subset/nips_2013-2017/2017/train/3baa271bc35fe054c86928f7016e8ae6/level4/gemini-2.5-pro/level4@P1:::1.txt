This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a streaming setting, with an additional requirement for robustness against adversarial removals. The authors propose a two-stage model where, first, a single-pass streaming algorithm (STAR-T) constructs a robust summary `S` of the data. In the second stage, after any `m` elements are removed from this summary, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remainder of `S` to find a `k`-sized solution. The STAR-T algorithm is based on a novel partitioned data structure where elements are added to buckets based on an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this method provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee, while the summary `S` requires `O((m log k + k) log^2 k)` space. This work extends ideas from streaming submodular maximization (e.g., Sieve-Streaming) to a robust setting, which has previously been studied primarily in the offline context.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Quality and Originality:** The paper's primary strength is its technical contribution. The proposed STAR-T algorithm and its corresponding analysis are non-trivial and technically challenging. The method of combining a partitioned data structure with an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule is a novel and clever way to ensure robustness within the constraints of a single-pass streaming model. To the best of my knowledge, the results presented are sound and constitute the first constant-factor approximation guarantee for this specific robust streaming submodular maximization problem.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the proof sketch provides good intuition for the main theoretical result.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Significance and Motivation:** My main concern is the significance of the problem as formulated. While technically interesting, the practical motivation for this specific two-stage model feels somewhat weak. The paper presents applications such as movie recommendation, where a user might be uninterested in some of the initial suggestions. However, the paper does not convincingly argue why a pre-computed robust summary is the most natural or necessary solution. In many scenarios, a simpler approach might be to just re-run a fast, non-robust streaming algorithm on the original dataset while excluding the removed items. The paper would be significantly stronger if it could more clearly delineate the real-world scenarios where this pre-computation is critical and such alternatives are infeasible. As presented, the problem formulation feels somewhat niche, which may limit its broader impact.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper solves a technically difficult theoretical problem with a novel algorithm and a rigorous analysis. It provides the first constant-factor approximation for robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, which is a clear and valuable contribution to the submodular optimization literature.

*   **Con-acceptance:** The practical motivation for the specific problem setting is not well established. The applications provided do not make a compelling case for the necessity of this complex framework over simpler, more direct approaches, raising questions about the overall significance and potential impact of the work for the NIPS community.
