This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a challenging setting that combines two constraints: the data arrives in a stream, and the solution must be robust to the removal of up to `m` elements. The authors propose a two-stage approach. In the first stage, a single-pass streaming algorithm called STAR-T constructs a summary `S` of the data stream. This algorithm uses a novel structure of partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds. In the second stage, after an arbitrary set `E` of `m` elements is removed, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining elements of the summary (`S \ E`) to produce the final `k`-element solution `Z`.

This work builds upon two distinct lines of research. It extends streaming submodular maximization algorithms, most notably SIEVE-STREAMING [8], by incorporating robustness. It also adapts techniques from the non-streaming robust submodular maximization literature [10, 11], particularly the use of a partitioned structure, to the streaming context. The problem is well-motivated by applications like data summarization and recommender systems, where initial solutions may need to be revised due to failures or changing user preferences without reprocessing the entire dataset.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Technical Depth:** The paper tackles a novel and important combination of challenges: robustness, submodularity, and streaming computation. The proposed STAR-T algorithm, with its partitioned structure and exponentially decreasing thresholds, is a non-trivial and clever contribution. The theoretical analysis that combines these elements to yield a constant-factor approximation guarantee is technically sophisticated and appears sound.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The main theoretical result (Theorem 4.1) provides a constant-factor approximation for the robust streaming problem. Furthermore, the memory complexity of the summary `S` is O((k + m log k) log k), which is notable for its logarithmic, rather than linear, dependence on `k`. This compares favorably to concurrent work [18] which has a linear dependence on `k`.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do a good job of explaining the intuition behind their algorithm's design and providing a high-level sketch of the proof, which is helpful for the reader. The problem statement and the two-stage procedure are clearly defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Significance of Empirical Results:** While the experiments validate the algorithm's performance, the results are not as compelling as the theoretical contributions. The primary baseline is SIEVE-STREAMING, which is given the significant advantage of knowing the removed set `E` in advance. The proposed STAR-T-GREEDY and STAR-T-SIEVE methods perform comparably to this powerful baseline, and in some cases are slightly outperformed. While matching this baseline is an achievement, one might hope for a more decisive empirical demonstration of the benefits of the proposed robust framework, especially given its increased complexity. It would strengthen the paper to discuss why simply matching the baseline is a significant result in this context.

2.  **Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The motivation for robustness is strong. However, there is a subtle disconnect between the general motivation and the specific adversarial model evaluated in the experiments. In the experiments (e.g., Figures 2a-d), the removed set `E` is chosen as a subset of the algorithm's summary `S`. This represents a worst-case scenario for the proposed algorithm. While this is a valid stress test, the motivating examples (e.g., a user deciding they don't like a genre of movie) correspond to removals from the entire ground set `V`, where `E` may have little or no intersection with `S`. It would be beneficial for the authors to include experiments or discussion on this more general case of removals from `V \ S`, as the algorithm's resilience in that scenario is less clear from the current results.

**Summary and Justification:**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The problem of robust streaming submodular maximization is both novel and relevant. The algorithm and its analysis are technically impressive. The main weaknesses lie in the experimental evaluation, where the empirical gains over a strong baseline are modest, and the connection between the experimental setup and the motivating applications could be made clearer.

Despite these weaknesses, the novelty of the problem formulation and the strength of the theoretical results make this a solid contribution to the NIPS community. The paper addresses a difficult problem and provides a complete piece of work with sound theoretical analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and challenging problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms, submodular optimization, and robustness.
*   Presents a technically deep solution with a constant-factor approximation guarantee.
*   The algorithm's memory complexity is favorable compared to concurrent work.
*   The paper is well-written and clear.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical results show performance that is comparable to, but not decisively better than, a strong baseline, which may limit the perceived practical impact.
*   The experimental validation focuses on a specific adversarial model (removals from the summary `S`) which may not fully capture all the scenarios described in the motivation.

I recommend accepting this paper. The theoretical contributions are significant enough to outweigh the limitations of the experimental section.
