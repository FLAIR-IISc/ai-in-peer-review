

This paper presents the first joint study of streaming and robust submodular maximization. In this setting, the goal is to select $k$ elements of high value from a small set retained during a single pass over the stream, where up to $m$ elements may be removed after the stream concludes. Streaming submodular maximization has been widely studied due to its applicability in large-scale settings requiring limited data passes. Robustness has recently gained attention for scenarios where element removals may occur, and the solution must remain effective despite such removals. The authors introduce the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains $O((m \log k + k) \log^2 k)$ elements after processing the stream and achieves a constant-factor $(0.149)$ approximation guarantee while being robust to the removal of up to $m$ elements. STAR-T combines partitioning and thresholding techniques using buckets; while bucket-based approaches were previously used in robust submodular optimization, STAR-T fills buckets via a novel strategy. Experiments demonstrate strong performance in influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, even against streaming algorithms that know the removals in advance.

The problem setting—integrating streaming and robustness—is challenging, and achieving a constant-factor approximation constitutes a significant theoretical advance. However, the paper would benefit from several improvements. First, the absence of non-trivial lower bounds to complement the upper bound weakens the theoretical contribution. Second, the analysis lacks clarity: while the authors describe an "innovative analysis," they do not specify its novel aspects, making it difficult to follow the core ideas and proof structure from the sketch. Third, the influence maximization experiments require strengthening. The objective used—based solely on the neighborhood size of seed nodes—is highly simplified and does not reflect traditional influence maximization, where function values depend on the entire network structure for arbitrary seed sets. It remains unclear how such objectives would be compatible with the streaming framework, leaving the practical relevance of the application less convincing.
