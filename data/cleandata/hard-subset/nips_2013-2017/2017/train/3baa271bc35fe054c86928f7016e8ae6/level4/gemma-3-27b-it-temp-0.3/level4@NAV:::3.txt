This paper addresses the challenging problem of maximizing a submodular function in a streaming environment while ensuring robustness to element removals. Existing work has focused on streaming submodular maximization, motivated by large-scale applications with limited data access, and on robust submodular maximization, addressing scenarios where elements may be lost. This work is the first to jointly tackle both aspects, a significant contribution to the field.

The authors present the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains a set of O((m log k + k)log^2 k) elements and provides a constant-factor (0.149) approximation guarantee, even when m elements are subsequently removed. The algorithm leverages partitioning and thresholding techniques, employing buckets to manage the selection process. While bucket-based approaches have appeared in prior robust submodular optimization work, this paper introduces a novel bucket-filling strategy. The effectiveness of STAR-T is demonstrated through experiments in influence maximization and movie recommendation, showing competitive performance even against streaming algorithms with prior knowledge of removals.

Achieving a constant-factor approximation for this combined streaming and robust setting is a noteworthy accomplishment. However, the paper could benefit from further development in several areas. The inclusion of lower bounds would provide valuable context for the achieved upper bound. The analysis, while technically sound, lacks clarity; the authors claim an “innovative analysis” without sufficiently highlighting its key contributions, making it difficult to follow the core ideas and proof steps. Finally, the influence maximization application could be strengthened. The experimental evaluation relies on a simplified objective function (neighborhood size), and it remains unclear how more standard influence maximization objectives – which consider network-wide effects – could be adapted to a streaming context.
