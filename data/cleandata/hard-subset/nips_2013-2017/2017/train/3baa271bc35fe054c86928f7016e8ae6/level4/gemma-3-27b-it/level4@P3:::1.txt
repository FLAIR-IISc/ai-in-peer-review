This paper addresses the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, a challenging extension to the classical submodular maximization problem. The authors consider a scenario where elements arrive in a stream, and after a summary set is constructed, a subset of *m* elements may be removed. The goal is to find a summary set that allows for a near-optimal solution even after such removals. This is formalized as a two-stage procedure: a streaming stage to construct a robust summary, and a query stage to find a good solution after elements have been removed. The authors propose an algorithm, STAR-T, based on a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding, and prove a constant-factor approximation guarantee.

The problem setting is well-motivated by applications such as data summarization, influence maximization, and personalized recommendation systems, where robustness to failures or changing preferences is crucial. The introduction effectively outlines these applications and highlights the limitations of existing approaches when dealing with streaming data and the need for robustness. The authors clearly articulate the two-stage nature of the problem and the challenges it presents.

The technical contributions of the paper are significant. The design of the STAR-T algorithm, with its partitioning structure and decreasing thresholding rule, appears novel and thoughtfully crafted. The approximation guarantee, while constant, is a valuable result in this complex setting. The paper builds upon prior work in streaming submodular maximization (e.g., [8]) and robust submodular maximization ([9, 10, 11]), appropriately citing and differentiating their approach. The extension to handle an unknown optimal value using multiple parallel instances, as presented in Theorem 4.5, further strengthens the work.

However, the presentation could be improved for clarity. While the technical details are present, some sections (particularly the proofs in the supplementary material) may be difficult to follow without a strong background in submodular optimization. The notation, while standard within the field, could benefit from more explicit definitions early on. The experimental results, while demonstrating the effectiveness of the algorithm, could be more comprehensive.  A more detailed comparison to other relevant baselines, beyond SIEVE-STREAMING, would be helpful.  The discussion of why *w*=1 works well in practice, despite the theoretical bound requiring a larger value, is also worth expanding.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The problem setting and proposed algorithm are novel and contribute to the growing body of work on robust and streaming submodular optimization.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee provides a solid foundation for the algorithm's performance.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and its relevance to real-world applications.
*   **Sound Technical Approach:** The partitioning structure and thresholding rule appear well-motivated and contribute to the algorithm’s effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** Some sections, particularly the proofs, are difficult to follow without a strong background in the field.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate good performance, they could be more comprehensive with a broader comparison to baselines and a more in-depth analysis of the results.
*   **Application Weakness:** While the application areas are mentioned, they don't entirely justify the complexity and constraints of the problem. The scenarios feel somewhat contrived.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging and potentially important problem with a novel algorithmic approach.
*   The theoretical guarantees provide a strong justification for the algorithm's performance.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The clarity of the presentation could be improved.
*   The relevance to NIPS, while present, is not overwhelmingly strong. The specific constraints of the problem might limit its broader applicability.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more thorough.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of submodular optimization. While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty, theoretical guarantees, and experimental validation make it a worthwhile contribution. I would lean towards **Accept**, but with the caveat that the authors address the clarity concerns and consider expanding the experimental evaluation. A minor revision addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper.
