This paper proposes a two-stage procedure for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint `k`. The first stage introduces a novel robust streaming algorithm, STAR-T, which is based on a partitioning structure and a decreasing thresholding rule. The second stage then applies a simple greedy method to the output of the first stage. The authors prove that STAR-T achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee in the limit as `k` approaches infinity (Theorem 4.1). Furthermore, Lemmas 4.2 and 4.3 establish that the full two-stage procedure is robust to the loss of elements from the solution set. The proposed algorithm is evaluated on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation tasks, where it demonstrates strong empirical performance.

This work is noteworthy as it presents, to my knowledge, the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization. The development of algorithms for this streaming setting is a valuable contribution, as it has the potential for a wide range of applications. This paper is an enlightening first step in this direction. A practical strength of the proposed STAR-T algorithm is its handling of hyperparameters. The authors provide a method to approximate the threshold parameter `τ` in Theorem 4.5, and they demonstrate empirically that a simple fixed value of `w=1` performs well. This makes the algorithm convenient to apply without requiring extensive hyperparameter tuning.

Despite these strengths, my main concern lies with the experimental evaluation, which I found to be not entirely convincing. Specifically, the proposed STAR-T-Greedy algorithm does not consistently outperform the Sieve-St baseline across all settings presented.

Additionally, I found the choice of objective function for the influence maximization experiment to be unusual. The paper defines it as `f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z|` (line 219), which is a simple neighborhood-coverage function. This deviates from the standard influence spread models (e.g., Independent Cascade, Linear Threshold) presented in the cited work by Kempe et al. (2003). As a result, it is unclear how the proposed algorithm would perform on the more standard and complex task of maximizing influence spread in a social network. The performance on this simplified objective may not be representative of its performance on the problem as it is typically defined.
