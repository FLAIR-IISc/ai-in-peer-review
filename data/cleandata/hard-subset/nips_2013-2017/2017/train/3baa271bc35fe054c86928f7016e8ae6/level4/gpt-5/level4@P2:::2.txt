Summary
The paper tackles robust monotone submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint k in a streaming setting where, after a single pass, up to m items may be removed before the final solution is selected. The authors propose a two-stage approach: (i) a streaming algorithm, STAR-T, that constructs a compact “robust summary” using a partitioned structure of buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds; and (ii) a query-time step, STAR-T-GREEDY, that runs GREEDY on the remaining summary after any adversarial removal of up to m elements. The analysis shows that, for appropriate parameters, STAR-T stores O((k + m log k) log k) elements when τ approximates f(OPT), and achieves a constant-factor approximation after removal; with a parallel-threshold scheme (as in SIEVE-STREAMING), the space becomes O((k + m log k) log2 k) and the factor approaches ≈0.149 as k grows. The algorithm conceptually extends the thresholding ideas of SIEVE-STREAMING [8] and the non-uniform partitioning ideas from offline robust methods such as PRO [11] to the streaming robust setting. The paper also discusses concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [18], which attains a 1/2 − ε approximation but with memory scaling linearly in k (O(mk log k/ε)), whereas STAR-T’s dependence on k is only logarithmic in the robust component. Experiments on (i) dominating set (coverage) in social graphs and (ii) personalized movie recommendation demonstrate performance comparable to or better than baselines, including a streaming method that is allowed to know the removals in advance.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Robustness to deletions is important in streaming summarization and recommendation scenarios where re-scanning the entire data is infeasible.
- Algorithmic novelty: The partitioned, exponentially decreasing thresholding within a single-pass robust summary is a neat synthesis of ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING and non-uniform partitioning (e.g., PRO), adapted to the deletion-robust streaming setting.
- Theoretical guarantees: Single-pass, small-space summary with a constant-factor guarantee after arbitrary removal of up to m items; careful case analysis (full vs non-full partitions) underpins the proofs. The unknown-OPT issue is handled via parallel instances.
- Practicality: The method does not require tuning many hyperparameters; in experiments, w=1 works well, simplifying deployment and, empirically, robustness extends beyond the proved regime.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modest approximation constant: The worst-case factor (~0.149) is significantly below 1/2 from concurrent work [18], though STAR-T uses much less memory in k. A clearer discussion of the practical impact of this gap would help.
- Experimental scope: The “influence maximization” experiment actually evaluates dominating set coverage, f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z|, rather than diffusion-based spread (IC/LT) as in Kempe et al. (2003). Results for the actual IC/LT models would strengthen claims related to influence maximization or the text should consistently refer to the task as dominating set/coverage.
- Baseline positioning: STAR-T-GREEDY is not consistently superior to SIEVE-STREAMING (even when the latter knows the removals), though it is often comparable. A richer empirical analysis (varying m, k, w) and reporting run-time/memory would make the practical advantages clearer.
- Parameter realism: Experiments routinely use w=1 and sometimes remove more than k elements, which exceeds the theoretical regime for the stated guarantees. This is valuable empirically, but a sensitivity study aligning with theory (e.g., scaling w with m and k) is needed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Proof sketches are plausible and the decomposition into cases (many full buckets vs many non-full buckets) is well motivated. Handling unknown OPT with parallel thresholds mirrors established practice in streaming submodular maximization and appears correct.
- Empirical rigor: The two tasks are reasonable, but additional baselines (e.g., the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18], and smaller-scale comparisons to offline robust algorithms like PRO [11]) would better contextualize performance. Include function-evaluation counts and memory usage.

Clarity
- Writing and organization are generally clear. It would help to:
  - Emphasize early that the graph experiment is dominating set coverage, not diffusion-based spread; avoid calling it “influence maximization” unless IC/LT results are provided.
  - Provide guidance on setting w in practice relative to m and k, and report the actual memory footprint.
  - Summarize where the log factors in the space bound come from (streaming structure vs parallel thresholds), reconciling O((k + m log k) log k) with the log2 k bound mentioned in abstract/conclusion.

Originality
- The partitioned thresholding in a deletion-robust streaming setup is a novel combination of prior ideas, with a new analysis yielding memory-logarithmic dependence on k in the robust component. The work is concurrent with [18] but explores a different part of the approximation–memory trade-off.

Significance
- The problem is important and the approach is practical, especially when deletions are common and reprocessing full streams is infeasible. Despite a conservative worst-case constant, the method’s small memory footprint and competitive empirical results suggest potential for adoption.

Pros
- Single-pass, low-memory robust summary; query-time reuse for multiple deletions.
- Clear algorithmic structure; simple second stage (GREEDY).
- Solid theoretical guarantees, including handling unknown OPT.
- Empirical competitiveness even when the non-robust baseline is advantaged.

Cons
- Approximation constant is low compared to concurrent work.
- Experiments do not cover standard influence diffusion models; some claims could be more precise.
- Limited sensitivity analysis for m, w, and τ-grid; no time/memory reporting.
- No empirical comparison to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify task naming and evaluate on IC/LT diffusion if claiming influence maximization; otherwise, consistently refer to dominating set/coverage.
- Add experiments varying m and w, including settings that satisfy the theoretical w ≥ ⌈4 log k · m/k⌉, and report performance–memory trade-offs.
- Report memory usage, number of function evaluations, and wall-clock runtime for all methods.
- If feasible, compare to the concurrent streaming robust method [18] (or at least provide a synthetic comparison illustrating the approximation–memory trade-off).
- Provide guidance for practitioners on choosing τ-grid and w, including heuristics that worked well.

Overall, the paper offers a well-motivated and thoughtfully analyzed approach to deletion-robust submodular maximization in streaming. With clearer experimental positioning and broader evaluations, it could have strong impact.
