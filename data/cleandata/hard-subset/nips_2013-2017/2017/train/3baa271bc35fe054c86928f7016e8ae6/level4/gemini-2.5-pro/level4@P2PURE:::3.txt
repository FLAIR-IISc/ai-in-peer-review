This paper addresses the problem of submodular maximization under both streaming and robustness constraints. The objective is to select `k` high-value elements from a data stream such that the solution remains effective even if an adversary removes up to `m` elements after the stream. This setting is well-motivated: streaming algorithms are essential for large-scale applications requiring few data passes, while robustness is crucial in scenarios where elements may become unavailable. This work is the first to jointly consider these two challenges.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which processes the stream in a single pass and retains O((m log k + k )log^2 k) elements. The algorithm achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of 0.149, robust to the removal of `m` elements. Technically, STAR-T combines partitioning and thresholding techniques using a novel bucket-filling strategy that extends ideas from prior work on robust submodular optimization. Experiments on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks demonstrate strong empirical performance, with the proposed algorithm outperforming even non-robust streaming baselines that have oracle access to the set of removed elements.

The paper tackles a challenging and novel problem by combining streaming and robustness constraints, and achieving a constant-factor approximation in this setting is a significant theoretical contribution. However, several aspects of the paper could be improved. First, the theoretical results would be stronger if complemented by non-trivial lower bounds to better contextualize the upper bound. Second, the presentation of the theoretical analysis lacks clarity. The authors describe their analysis as "innovative" but do not explain what makes it novel, and the provided proof sketch is difficult to parse, making it challenging to grasp the core ideas of the argument. Finally, the experimental validation on the influence maximization task is not fully convincing. The experiments use a highly simplified objective function—the neighborhood size of the seed nodes—rather than a standard influence spread metric. It remains unclear how traditional influence maximization objectives, which typically require global network information, could be adapted to the streaming model, and the paper would benefit from a discussion of this limitation.
