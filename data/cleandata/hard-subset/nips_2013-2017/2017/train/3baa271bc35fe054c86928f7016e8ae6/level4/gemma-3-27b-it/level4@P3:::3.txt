This paper addresses the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. This is a valuable contribution, as it combines two important aspects – efficient processing of large datasets (streaming) and resilience to data loss or inaccuracies (robustness) – that are crucial for many real-world applications. The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains a compact summary of the stream and guarantees a constant-factor approximation even after removing *m* elements from the summary. This work builds upon existing research in both streaming and robust submodular maximization, offering a novel approach to address the combined problem.

The core idea of STAR-T, utilizing a partitioned thresholding scheme with exponentially decreasing thresholds, is well-motivated and appears promising. The theoretical guarantee of a 0.149-approximation (as *k* grows large) is a significant achievement. The experimental results on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation demonstrate that STAR-T performs competitively with, and sometimes outperforms, existing streaming algorithms, even those benefiting from knowledge of the removed elements. The ability to re-use the robust summary for different removal scenarios (as shown in the movie recommendation experiment) is a practical strength.

However, the paper could benefit from several improvements. While the authors mention the "innovative analysis," the proof sketch in Section 4 lacks sufficient detail to fully appreciate the novelty and key insights. Expanding on the core ideas behind the analysis would significantly improve clarity. Specifically, explaining *why* the partitioning and thresholding scheme leads to robustness would be helpful.  A discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm, beyond the space complexity, would also be valuable. 

The lack of lower bounds is a notable omission. Establishing some theoretical limits on the achievable approximation ratio in this setting would provide a stronger context for the obtained results.  Regarding the experiments, the dominating set application could be strengthened by comparing against more sophisticated influence maximization objectives beyond simply maximizing the size of the neighborhood. The chosen objective, while simplifying the evaluation, may not fully capture the complexities of real-world influence maximization tasks.  Finally, the parameter sensitivity of the algorithm is not fully explored; a brief discussion of how the choice of parameters like *w* affects performance would be useful. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the first work to address the combined problem of robust and streaming submodular maximization.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a strong theoretical result.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate competitive performance against existing algorithms in relevant applications.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The ability to handle data removal and re-use the robust summary are valuable features.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Analysis Clarity:** The proof sketch lacks detail and doesn’t fully convey the innovative aspects of the analysis.
*   **Missing Lower Bounds:**  The absence of lower bounds weakens the theoretical contribution.
*   **Simplified Experimental Setup:** The influence maximization application uses a simplified objective function.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The problem is important, the proposed algorithm is well-motivated, and the results are promising. While improvements to the clarity of the analysis and the inclusion of lower bounds would strengthen the paper, the current version is of sufficient quality for acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, with the suggestion that the authors address the clarity of the analysis in a revised version.
