- Problem: Tackles robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting where up to m elements can be removed after one-pass processing.  
- Algorithm: Proposes STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm with summary size O((m log k + k) log² k) and a constant-factor (0.149) approximation against any m-element removal.  
- Novelty: First work to simultaneously address streaming constraints and robustness to removals for submodular maximization.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on SIEVE-STREAMING and PRO but extends them by combining streaming and robustness requirements.  
- Theoretical design: Uses partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds and provides a technically involved analysis.  
- Theoretical guarantee: Establishes a constant-factor approximation despite dual streaming and robustness constraints—an impressive theoretical result.  
- Experiments: Evaluated on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, showing STAR-T matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when SIEVE-STREAMING knows removed elements.  
- Strengths: Novel algorithmic design, sound theoretical results, and compelling empirical validation across diverse applications.  
- Clarity: Writing is generally clear and the two-stage (streaming/query) problem formulation is well motivated.  
- Missing analysis: Paper lacks non-trivial lower bounds to complement its upper bound guarantees.  
- Proof clarity: The claimed “innovative analysis” is not clearly distinguished from prior work and the proof sketch needs more detailed explanation of key insights.  
- Application realism: Influence maximization experiment uses a simplified objective f(Z)=|N(Z)∪Z| that doesn’t capture full network-dependent influence functions.  
- Improvements suggested: Provide lower bounds, clarify novelty in analysis, expand the proof sketch, and test on more realistic influence objectives.  
- Significance: High practical importance and theoretically sound solution with demonstrated empirical effectiveness.  
- Acceptance pros/cons: Pros—novel setting, strong theoretical guarantee, and solid experiments; Cons—missing lower bounds, insufficiently explained analysis novelty, and simplified influence maximization objective.
