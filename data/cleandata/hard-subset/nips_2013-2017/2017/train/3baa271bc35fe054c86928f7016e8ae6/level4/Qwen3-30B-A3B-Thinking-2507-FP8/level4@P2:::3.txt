

This paper addresses the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where elements arrive sequentially and up to m elements may be removed from the final summary. This represents a significant combination of two important problem settings that have been studied separately: streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., [9, 10, 11]). Previous robust submodular work typically assumes offline processing, while streaming approaches generally ignore robustness requirements. The authors present STAR-T, a novel streaming algorithm that maintains a summary of size O((m log k + k) log^2 k) and achieves a constant-factor (0.149) approximation guarantee even after removal of any m elements.

The key innovation lies in their partitioned thresholding approach, which arranges the summary into partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds. This differs from previous robust approaches like PRO [11], which constructs buckets greedily one after another, and from streaming methods like SIEVE-STREAMING [8], which uses a fixed threshold. The authors prove that after any m elements are removed from the summary, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) running on the remaining elements achieves the stated approximation guarantee. This is a technically impressive result, as it simultaneously addresses the challenges of streaming and robustness with a single-pass algorithm.

The paper makes strong contributions to the literature, particularly in addressing a practically important problem that has been largely overlooked in prior work. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, though the description of the "innovative analysis" could be more explicit about what distinguishes it from previous approaches. The authors claim their analysis combines "properties of the described robust structure and decreasing greedy thresholding rule," but a clearer explanation of the novel analytical techniques would strengthen the paper.

In terms of significance, the problem addressed has clear practical applications in large-scale machine learning systems where data arrives rapidly and robustness against element removal is required. The experimental validation demonstrates promising results in two applications: dominating set (using ego-Twitter and Amazon product co-purchasing networks) and personalized movie recommendation (using MovieLens 1M). The paper shows that STAR-T-GREEDY outperforms or matches SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows in advance which elements will be removed, which is a strong practical validation.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved. First, the influence maximization experiments use a simplified objective function (f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|) that doesn't capture the complexity of traditional influence maximization problems where the objective depends on the entire network structure. It would be valuable to see how their approach handles more standard influence maximization objectives in a streaming setting, as this is a major application area for submodular optimization.

Second, the paper lacks non-trivial lower bounds to complement their upper bound. While they achieve a 0.149 approximation, it would be useful to know how close this is to the theoretical limit for this problem.

Third, the analysis section could be improved in clarity. The proof sketch is somewhat opaque, and it's difficult to extract the main ideas of the analysis. A more detailed explanation of why their thresholding scheme enables robustness would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

Strengths:
- Novel combination of streaming and robustness in submodular maximization
- Strong theoretical results with constant-factor approximation guarantee
- Comprehensive experimental validation showing competitive performance
- Practical relevance to large-scale machine learning applications
- Clear problem statement and well-structured paper

Weaknesses:
- Simplified objective in influence maximization experiments
- Lack of non-trivial lower bounds
- Insufficiently detailed explanation of the analytical innovations
- Limited connection to recent NIPS work on related topics

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a practically important problem with a technically sound solution. The theoretical results are strong, and the empirical validation is promising. While the paper could be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly the influence maximization objective and the analytical clarity, it represents a significant advance in robust streaming submodular optimization.

Pro:
- Novel problem setting that combines two important research directions
- Strong theoretical guarantees with a constant-factor approximation
- Practical validation in multiple real-world applications
- Clear presentation of the algorithm and its properties

Con:
- Simplified objective function in influence maximization experiments
- Limited discussion of the analytical innovations
- Absence of lower bounds to complement the upper bound
- Limited connection to recent NIPS work on related topics

The paper should be accepted as it makes a significant contribution to a relevant problem area with strong theoretical results and practical validation. The weaknesses noted are important but do not undermine the core contribution. The authors' approach provides a valuable solution to a challenging problem with clear practical implications, and their results advance the state of the art in submodular optimization.
