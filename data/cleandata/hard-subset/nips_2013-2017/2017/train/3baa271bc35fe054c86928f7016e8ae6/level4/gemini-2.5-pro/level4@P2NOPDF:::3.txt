This paper addresses the problem of submodular maximization in a combined streaming and robust setting. The goal is to select a high-value set of `k` elements from a data stream, using limited memory, such that the solution's quality is resilient to the adversarial removal of up to `m` elements post-stream. While streaming submodular maximization is a well-studied problem motivated by large-scale applications, and robust optimization has recently gained attention for its practical relevance, this paper is the first to jointly consider these two challenging constraints.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of 0.149 while maintaining `O((m log k + k) log^2 k)` elements in memory. The algorithm integrates partitioning and thresholding techniques, employing a bucket-based approach reminiscent of prior work in non-streaming robust optimization. However, the authors introduce a novel method for populating these buckets tailored to the streaming context. Experimentally, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of STAR-T on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks. Notably, the algorithm performs competitively even against non-robust streaming algorithms that are given oracle knowledge of which elements will be removed.

The primary strength of this work lies in tackling a novel and difficult problem. Combining the constraints of single-pass streaming and adversarial robustness is challenging, and obtaining a constant-factor approximation is a significant theoretical contribution. However, the paper could be strengthened in several key areas.

First, the theoretical analysis would be more complete with the inclusion of non-trivial lower bounds. Such bounds would help contextualize the upper bound achieved by STAR-T and provide insight into the fundamental difficulty of the problem.

Second, the presentation of the theoretical analysis could be substantially improved. The authors refer to their proof technique as an "innovative analysis" but do not elaborate on what makes it novel compared to previous work. The provided proof sketch is dense and difficult to follow, making it hard to extract the main technical ideas and high-level steps of the argument. A clearer exposition of the core analytical contributions would significantly increase the paper's impact.

Finally, the experimental validation on the influence maximization task is not entirely convincing. The study employs a simplified proxy objective: the size of the immediate neighborhood of the seed nodes. It is unclear how standard influence maximization objectives, which depend on the global network structure and are typically evaluated via simulations, could be computed in a streaming setting. The paper would be more compelling if it either addressed the challenge of adapting traditional influence functions to the streaming model or focused its experiments on applications where the submodular objective is more naturally suited to single-pass computation.
