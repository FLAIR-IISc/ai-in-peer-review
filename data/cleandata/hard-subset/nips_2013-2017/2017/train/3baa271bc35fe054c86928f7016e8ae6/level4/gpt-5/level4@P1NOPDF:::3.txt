This paper studies submodular maximization in a setting that is both streaming and robust. The goal is to select k high-value elements while storing only a small subset during a single pass, and to remain robust to the removal of up to m items after the stream. Streaming submodular maximization has been widely studied due to large-scale applications where only a few passes are feasible, and robustness has recently received attention to handle deletions. To my knowledge, this is the first work to jointly consider streaming and robustness.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which retains O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements after the stream and achieves a constant 0.149-approximation guarantee, even after the adversarial removal of m elements. The approach combines partitioning and thresholding techniques with bucketed data structures. While buckets have appeared before in robust submodular optimization, STAR-T fills them in a different way. Empirically, the algorithm performs well on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, and even competes favorably with streaming baselines that are given the deletion set in advance.

With both streaming and robustness requirements, the problem is challenging, and obtaining a constant-factor approximation is a strong contribution.

That said, several aspects could be improved:

- The paper would benefit from nontrivial lower bounds to complement the upper bound, clarifying the inherent trade-offs between robustness, memory, and approximation in the streaming model.

- The presentation of the analysis could be clearer. The authors refer to an “innovative analysis” but do not articulate what is innovative about it, and the proof sketch makes it difficult to extract the main ideas and the structure of the argument. A higher-level roadmap (key lemmas, how partitioning/thresholding interact with robustness, where the constant 0.149 arises, and tightness of the log factors in space) would help substantially.

- The influence maximization application is not entirely convincing. In the experiments, the objective is simplified to the size of the neighborhood of the seed nodes. It is unclear how standard influence maximization objectives—where the value depends on the entire network for any seed set—can be accommodated in a streaming setting. A discussion of whether and how the method could handle more realistic objectives (e.g., ones defined under diffusion models) would strengthen the empirical section.

Overall, the combination of streaming and robustness with a constant-factor approximation is a notable result, and the algorithmic ideas are interesting. Clearer exposition of the analysis and a more compelling influence maximization evaluation (or justification of the simplified surrogate) would improve the paper. Additional theoretical context via lower bounds would further solidify the contribution.
