

This paper addresses a two-stage robust streaming submodular maximization problem: first, constructing a compact summary set S (size O((k + m log k) log k)) via a single pass over data; second, after any m elements are removed from S, producing a k-sized solution Z from S \ E with a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The authors propose STAR-T (Streaming Robust submodular algorithm with Partitioned Thresholding) and its query-stage counterpart STAR-T-GREEDY. The key innovation lies in their partitioned thresholding structure, where buckets across exponentially increasing partitions use decreasing thresholds (τ/2^i), enabling robustness against arbitrary m-element removals without prior knowledge of E.

The work builds meaningfully on prior NIPS contributions. It extends the streaming framework of SIEVE-STREAMING [8] to the robust setting, while drawing inspiration from Bogunovic et al.'s non-streaming robust partitioning [11]. Unlike [11] which requires centralized computation, STAR-T operates in one pass with sublinear space, matching the practical utility of streaming methods like [8] while adding robustness. The theoretical novelty is substantial: the authors prove a 0.149(1 - 1/log k)-approximation guarantee (approaching 0.149 as k→∞), which is non-trivial given the dual challenges of streaming constraints and adversarial removals. The analysis carefully balances two cases (partitions with many full buckets vs. sparse partitions), leveraging submodularity and greedy properties to derive tight bounds. The memory bound O((k + m log k) log k) also offers a practical advantage over concurrent work [18] (which uses O(mk log k/ε) memory with linear k-dependence).

Strengths are clear: the algorithm is technically sound, the analysis is rigorous, and the experimental validation on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks demonstrates strong empirical performance. Notably, STAR-T-GREEDY matches or exceeds SIEVE-STREAMING—even when the latter knows E in advance—showing robustness without compromising on quality. The two-stage design also enables interactive use cases (e.g., regenerating recommendations after user preferences change), a valuable practical feature.

However, the paper's relevance to NIPS 2017 warrants careful consideration. While the technical contribution is strong, the motivation for robustness could be strengthened to better align with NIPS' focus on machine learning applications. The applications described (movie recommendations, dominating sets) are valid but lack specific connection to contemporary NIPS-relevant challenges like large-scale interactive learning or network robustness. For instance, the movie recommendation scenario could better articulate how *real-world* user feedback loops (e.g., implicit ratings) necessitate robustness beyond simple "already-seen" removals. The authors cite related work on robust influence maximization [14], but the paper itself does not explicitly position robustness as a critical need in modern streaming ML pipelines (e.g., for adaptive recommendation systems or sensor networks where element removals are inherent).

The primary weakness is thus the application motivation rather than technical merit. The problem is well-defined and the solution is novel, but the paper would benefit from deeper discussion of *why* robustness is increasingly essential in NIPS-scale streaming scenarios—e.g., citing how recent NIPS work on adaptive submodular optimization [16,17] highlights the need for such resilience. Without this, the contribution risks appearing as a technical extension rather than a forward-looking advancement for the field.

**Pro Acceptance:**  
- Strong theoretical contribution with novel partitioning/thresholding structure  
- Tight constant-factor approximation guarantee for a non-trivial problem  
- Practical memory efficiency (logarithmic in k vs. linear [18])  
- Empirical validation showing superiority over state-of-the-art streaming baselines  

**Con Acceptance:**  
- Application motivation could be more tightly linked to NIPS-relevant ML challenges  
- Limited discussion of how robustness addresses emerging needs in large-scale streaming ML  

The paper makes a solid technical contribution to submodular optimization, but its impact on NIPS would be amplified by stronger contextualization within the conference's focus on scalable machine learning. As written, the work is technically excellent and relevant to NIPS' algorithms track, but the application narrative could better justify its significance for the broader ML community. Given the high quality of the theory and experiments, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the motivation section.
