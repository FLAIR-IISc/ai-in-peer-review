This paper tackles the problem of submodular maximization in a streaming and robust context, where the objective is to identify k high-value elements from a limited set retained during the stream, considering that m items may be removed post-stream. The study of streaming submodular maximization has garnered significant attention due to its applications in large-scale settings where only a few data passes are feasible. Robustness has also been a focus area, driven by scenarios where elements may be removed, and the goal is to obtain solutions resilient to such removals. Notably, this paper is the first to address the combined problem of streaming and robust submodular maximization.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which retains O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements after the stream and achieves a constant (0.149) approximation guarantee while ensuring robustness to the removal of m elements. This algorithm leverages partitioning and thresholding techniques for submodular optimization using buckets, building upon previous work on robust submodular optimization. Experimental results demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness in influence maximization and movie recommendation applications, even when compared to streaming algorithms with prior knowledge of removed elements.

The problem setting, incorporating both streaming and robustness aspects, poses significant challenges, and achieving a constant factor approximation is a notable accomplishment. However, several aspects of the paper warrant improvement. Non-trivial lower bounds would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the problem, complementing the upper bound. The analysis could be clarified, as the authors mention an "innovative analysis" without elaborating on its innovative aspects, making it difficult to discern the main ideas and steps. Furthermore, the influence maximization application could be more convincing, particularly considering traditional influence maximization objectives that depend on the entire network for any set of seed nodes, and how these might be adapted to a streaming setting.
