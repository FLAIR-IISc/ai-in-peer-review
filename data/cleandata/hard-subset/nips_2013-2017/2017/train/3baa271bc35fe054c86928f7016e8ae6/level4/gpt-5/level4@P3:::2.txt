Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in the streaming setting. The goal is to build, in one pass, a compact summary S that remains useful even after an arbitrary set E of up to m elements is removed; at query time, a solution of size at most k is selected from S \ E via a simple greedy procedure.

The proposed method, STAR-T, organizes the retained elements into exponentially sized partitions and buckets, and applies exponentially decreasing thresholds to decide where (and whether) to store each arriving element. The query-time algorithm, STAR-T-GREEDY, simply runs greedy on S \ E. The main theoretical result (Theorem 4.1) shows that, with appropriate parameters, STAR-T stores at most O((k + m log k) log k) elements when given a threshold τ approximating OPT, and that STAR-T-GREEDY returns a set Z with value at least c · f(OPT(k, V \ E)), where c → 0.149 as k → ∞. When OPT is unknown, a standard parallel-guessing strategy à la SIEVE-STREAMING [8] yields an extra log factor (Theorem 4.5), leading to O((k + m log k) log^2 k) memory and a (1 + ε)-loss in the approximation constant.

This work draws on and extends two lines of research: (i) robust submodular maximization in centralized settings (e.g., Krause et al. [9], Orlin et al. [10], and PRO [11]), where robustness is achieved via partitioning structures; and (ii) submodular streaming algorithms (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]), where thresholding is used to control memory and approximation. Concurrent work [18] also addresses robust streaming under cardinality constraints, achieving a 1/2 − ε approximation but with memory O(mk log k/ε), linear in k, whereas STAR-T’s memory dependence on k is logarithmic (through log k factors).

Strengths
- Novel algorithmic design: the combination of exponentially growing buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds is elegant and well-motivated by the analysis.
- Provable robustness in streaming: a one-pass algorithm with a constant-factor guarantee after arbitrary deletion of m elements from the summary is nontrivial and useful.
- Memory–approximation tradeoffs: compared to concurrent work, STAR-T offers substantially milder memory growth in k, which is crucial for large-scale streams.
- Practical evaluation: experiments on graph coverage (dominating set) and personalized movie recommendation demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines, including a streaming baseline that knows the deletions E in advance.

Weaknesses and questions
- Experimental scope and claims: STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING (often slightly better, sometimes slightly worse), but not consistently better. The paper would benefit from a more systematic exploration of regimes (varying m, k, and memory) where STAR-T’s robustness provides a clear advantage, and from reporting summary sizes and runtime.
- Influence maximization terminology: the “dominating set” objective f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z| is a coverage objective and not the diffusion-based spread models of Kempe et al. (IC/LT). The paper should clarify this distinction and avoid conflating dominating set with influence diffusion; if possible, include results under IC/LT or cite prior work using dominating set as a proxy.
- Parameterization and clarity: STAR-T’s threshold is described in the text as τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Please reconcile and standardize the presentation. Theoretical robustness depends on w ≥ ⌈4 m log k / k⌉, yet experiments use w = 1 while testing removals beyond the theoretical regime (e.g., removing 2k elements from S). It would help to (i) report the resulting w bound for the experimental m and k, (ii) study sensitivity to w, and (iii) include results within the guaranteed regime.
- Practicality of oracle access: robust streaming requires evaluating marginal gains f(e | B) on the fly. For some objectives, this is efficient; for others (e.g., influence under IC/LT), it is costly. Please discuss evaluation complexity and any approximations used.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound and carefully reasoned. The two-case analysis (many full buckets vs. many non-full buckets) and the balancing arguments in Lemmas 4.2–4.4 lead cleanly to the final constant c ≈ 0.149. Assumptions and memory bounds are explicit. The practical section is solid but could be stronger with additional baselines (e.g., centralized PRO [11] when feasible), ablations (effect of w and τ guesses), and runtime/memory reporting.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. A few points need clarification: the exact threshold rule (τ/2^i vs. τ/min{2^i, k}), consistent asymptotic statements for the summary size (O((k + m log k) log k) with known τ vs. O((k + m log k) log^2 k) with guessing), and more explicit guidance on setting w in practice. Improving the discussion around the relationship to influence maximization would also reduce potential confusion.

Originality
The partitioned thresholding structure for robust streaming is, to my knowledge, new, and the analysis yields a constant factor with logarithmic memory dependence on k (up to log factors). The work is distinct from SIEVE-STREAMING and from prior robust, non-streaming algorithms (e.g., PRO), and it complements concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [18] with different tradeoffs.

Significance
Robust streaming summaries are important in modern settings where data are too large to store, deletions are common (right to be forgotten, failures, user constraints), and reprocessing the full stream is infeasible. The proposed approach is likely to be useful for practitioners working with coverage- and facility-location–type objectives, and as a building block for more complex robust pipelines.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and novel contribution: first robust streaming method with constant-factor guarantee and mild memory growth in k.
- Solid theoretical guarantees and analysis.
- Practical algorithm that is easy to deploy (one pass, simple query-time greedy).
- Empirical results show competitive performance to strong baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental evaluation could be broader and more aligned with the theoretical regime (report w, m, summary size, runtimes; sensitivity studies).
- Potential confusion around “influence maximization” versus dominating set; lack of diffusion-model results.
- Minor inconsistencies in the description of thresholds and memory bounds.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the exact threshold schedule and ensure consistency across text and pseudocode.
- Report the size of S and runtime/marginal evaluation costs in experiments; include sensitivity to w and τ-guessing grid.
- Either avoid calling the graph task “influence maximization” or add experiments under standard diffusion models (or discuss why they are impractical in streaming and how to adapt).
- Include ablations where m varies and stays within/outside the theoretical guarantees to illustrate robustness tradeoffs.

Overall, this is a well-executed and timely contribution to robust submodular streaming. With strengthened experiments and clarified presentation, it would make a valuable addition to NIPS.
