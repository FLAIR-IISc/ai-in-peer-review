This paper proposes a two-stage procedure for robust submodular maximization with a cardinality constraint k. A robust streaming algorithm, STAR-T, is designed in the first stage, leveraging a novel partitioning structure and an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. The second stage employs a simple greedy method. The authors prove that STAR-T achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee as k approaches infinity (Theorem 4.1). Lemmas 4.2-4.4 provide theoretical justification for the robustness of STAR-T when combined with the greedy method in the face of element loss.

The work is significant as it represents a first attempt at a streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, an area with potential for broad applicability. The two-stage approach is well-motivated, addressing scenarios where data summarization needs to be resilient to element removal. The authors address the challenge of needing to estimate the unknown parameter τ (related to the optimal solution) in Theorem 4.5, providing a parallel variant to approximate it. The observation that the algorithm performs well with w=1 simplifies its practical use.

A key concern lies in the limited expressiveness of the experimental results. While STAR-T-GREEDY demonstrates comparable or slightly better performance than SIEVE-STREAMING in some scenarios, it doesn’t consistently outperform it. Further investigation into the conditions where STAR-T excels would be valuable. 

Additionally, there’s a discrepancy in the formulation of the objective function for the dominating set problem (line 219) compared to the standard formulation presented in the cited work by Kempe et al. (2003). The authors use f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|, while Kempe et al. focus on maximizing the expected spread of influence. It is unclear how well STAR-T performs in the context of this more standard influence spread model. Addressing this discrepancy and clarifying the relevance to existing work on influence maximization would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting.
*   Theoretical guarantees with clear approximation bounds.
*   Well-motivated problem setting with potential applications.
*   Practical considerations addressed (e.g., estimation of τ, choice of w).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental results are not consistently superior to existing methods.
*   Discrepancy in the objective function used for the dominating set problem compared to standard literature.
*   The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small and diminishes as k grows.
