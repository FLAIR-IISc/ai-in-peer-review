The paper investigates the problem of maintaining a robust set of elements – a set *T* selected from a ground set *E* in a streaming manner – that guarantees a near-optimal solution even after the removal of *m* elements. Specifically, the objective is to identify *T* such that, following the deletion of any *m* elements, a subset *S* of *k* elements maximizing a submodular function *f* can still be efficiently found.

The motivation stems from the broad applicability of submodular function maximization and the existence of streaming algorithms addressing it. The authors highlight the practical value of a *robust* solution, citing scenarios like recommendation systems where initial selections may require adjustment due to user feedback. 

The paper presents a constant-factor approximation algorithm for this problem, achieving a solution set *T* of size *(m log k + k) log<sup>2</sup>k*. 

My assessment is that the technical content is sound; the results appear correct and the algorithm and its analysis demonstrate considerable technical depth. However, my primary concern relates to the paper's fit within the scope of this conference. While the problem itself is interesting, the presented applications feel somewhat tenuous and the problem formulation lacks broad generalizability.
