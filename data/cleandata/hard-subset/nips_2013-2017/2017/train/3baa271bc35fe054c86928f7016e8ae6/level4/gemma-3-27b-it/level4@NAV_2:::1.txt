This paper investigates robust streaming submodular maximization, a problem motivated by data summarization scenarios where the selected summary must remain effective even after a subset of its elements are removed. Specifically, the authors aim to find a set T of elements in a streaming fashion, guaranteeing a constant-factor approximation to the optimal solution achievable on the remaining set after removing any m elements from T, with respect to a monotone submodular function f. This builds upon existing work in submodular maximization [7] and streaming algorithms [8, 12, 13], addressing a crucial robustness requirement often overlooked in practical applications like influence maximization [3] and recommender systems [6].

The core contribution is the STAR-T algorithm, a two-stage procedure utilizing a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. This algorithm efficiently maintains a robust summary of size O((k + m log k) log^2 k) and, in the query stage, employs a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) to extract a near-optimal solution from the remaining elements after a removal of size m. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating a constant-factor approximation guarantee, along with empirical validation on the dominating set problem [19, 20] and personalized movie recommendation using the MovieLens 1M dataset [21].

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm and its analysis appear technically sound. The partitioning structure and decreasing thresholding rule are novel and cleverly designed to address the robustness constraint. The theoretical bounds are clearly stated and justified.
*   **Non-trivial Contribution:** Achieving a constant-factor approximation for this problem is a significant result, particularly given the streaming and robustness constraints. The analysis appears complex and demonstrates a deep understanding of submodular function properties.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions of the problem and algorithm. The notation is consistent, and the figures are informative.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results provide evidence that the algorithm performs well in practice, matching or outperforming existing approaches like SIEVE-STREAMING [8], even when SIEVE-STREAMING has prior knowledge of the removed elements.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Application Motivation:** While the authors mention potential applications, the motivating examples (influence maximization, recommender systems) feel somewhat superficial. The benefits of robustness are stated but not strongly demonstrated with compelling use cases or comparisons to alternative robust solutions.
*   **Problem Specificity:** The problem formulation, while interesting, is relatively specific. The dependence on the removal parameter 'm' and the constant-factor approximation guarantee might not translate well to all submodular maximization scenarios.
*   **Space Complexity:** Although O((k + m log k) log^2 k) is polynomial, the logarithmic factors could be significant for very large k and m, potentially limiting scalability. A discussion of these limitations would be beneficial.
*   **Comparison to Related Work:** While the authors reference related work [9, 10, 11, 18], a more thorough comparison to these approaches, specifically highlighting the trade-offs between memory usage, approximation guarantees, and runtime, would strengthen the contribution. The relationship to [18] should be elaborated, given the similar goals and the differing memory complexities.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in submodular maximization.
*   The proposed algorithm and analysis are technically sound and represent a significant contribution to the field.
*   The empirical results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical performance.
*   The work extends existing streaming submodular maximization algorithms to a robust setting.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The application motivation is weak and lacks compelling examples.
*   The problem formulation is relatively specific, potentially limiting the broader impact of the work.
*   The space complexity might be a concern for very large-scale applications.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid technical paper with a novel and well-supported contribution. While the application motivation could be stronger and the problem formulation is somewhat specific, the technical merits of the work are significant. I believe this paper is borderline acceptable for NIPS, leaning towards acceptance, provided the authors can address the concerns regarding application relevance and discuss the limitations of the space complexity. I recommend the authors carefully consider these points in a revised version.
