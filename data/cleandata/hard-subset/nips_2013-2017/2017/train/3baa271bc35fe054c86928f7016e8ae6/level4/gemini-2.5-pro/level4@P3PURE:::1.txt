This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The objective is to design a single-pass algorithm that selects a set of elements, T, from a ground set E. This set T must be robust in the sense that even after an adversary deletes any m elements, the remainder of T is guaranteed to contain a k-element subset S that provides a constant-factor approximation for a given monotone submodular function f. The authors motivate this problem with applications where an initial solution may be revised, such as a recommendation system where a user might reject a subset of the recommended items.

The main contribution is a streaming algorithm that achieves a constant-factor approximation for this problem, returning a robust set T of size O((m log k + k) log² k).

The paper's technical results appear to be sound. The proposed solution is non-trivial, and both the algorithm and its analysis are technically challenging. However, my primary concern is the paper's relevance and potential impact on the NIPS community. While the problem is theoretically interesting, the motivation for this specific formulation is not compelling. The application scenarios feel somewhat contrived, and the paper does not convincingly argue that this particular model of robustness addresses a pressing or widespread need. Due to the narrowness of the problem definition and the weakness of its practical motivation, the paper's overall significance for this conference is questionable.
