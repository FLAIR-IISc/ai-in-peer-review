Summary
The paper studies robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint k in a streaming setting where up to m items can be removed after the stream. It proposes a two-stage approach. In the streaming stage, the STAR-T algorithm builds a compact summary using a partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds; elements are placed into the first non-full bucket whose threshold they meet. In the query stage, after any set E of size at most m is removed, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on S \ E to produce a size-k solution.

Theoretically, the paper proves a constant-factor approximation guarantee. Theorem 4.1 shows that the approximation ratio approaches a constant (c → 0.149) as k → ∞. Lemmas 4.2–4.3 establish that STAR-T combined with the simple greedy method remains effective despite the removal of elements. Experiments on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation indicate that the proposed method performs well in practice.

Strengths
- Originality and potential impact: To my knowledge, this is the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization. The streaming setting is important and may have wide-ranging applications, making the contribution timely and enlightening.
- Algorithmic design: The partitioned thresholding with exponentially decreasing thresholds is simple and intuitive, yet it enables a provable constant-factor approximation when combined with the second-stage greedy.
- Practical usability: STAR-T has two hyperparameters, τ and w. Theorem 4.5 explains how to approximate τ via parallel instances, and the experiments show that w = 1 works well. This makes STAR-T convenient to deploy without extensive hyperparameter tuning.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation: The experimental section is not very expressive. In particular, STAR-T-GREEDY does not always outperform SIEVE-STREAMING, which reduces the empirical support for the claimed advantages.
- Influence maximization objective: The paper uses f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z| (line 219) for influence maximization, which differs from the probabilistic diffusion objective in Kempe, Kleinberg, and Tardos (KDD 2003). It is therefore unclear how STAR-T would perform under that standard spread model.

Comments on review criteria
- Quality: The theoretical results are clearly stated and technically sound to the extent presented in the main text: Theorem 4.1 provides a constant-factor guarantee (with the ratio approaching 0.149 as k grows), and Lemmas 4.2–4.3 support robustness to deletions. However, the empirical evidence is mixed, as STAR-T-GREEDY does not consistently beat SIEVE-STREAMING.
- Clarity: The high-level presentation of the two-stage framework and the partitioned thresholding scheme is clear. The discussion of hyperparameters is helpful: τ is addressed by Theorem 4.5, and w appears to be easy to set in practice. The choice of influence maximization objective should be clarified with respect to the standard model in the literature.
- Originality: The paper’s streaming treatment of robustness appears novel. The partitioned thresholding structure and the analysis tailored to robustness are interesting contributions.
- Significance: Robust data summarization in streaming is important. The theoretical guarantees are nontrivial, and the approach could be adopted in applications where deletions occur. The experimental section, however, could be stronger to better demonstrate practical gains over strong baselines.

Pros for acceptance
- First streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization with a constant-factor guarantee (c → 0.149).
- Conceptually simple two-stage design with a practical summary that can answer multiple deletion queries.
- Hyperparameter handling is straightforward (τ approximated as in Theorem 4.5; w = 1 works in experiments), making the method convenient to use.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical results are not consistently superior: STAR-T-GREEDY does not always outperform SIEVE-STREAMING.
- The influence maximization objective differs from the standard spread model (Kempe et al., 2003), leaving the method’s performance on that model unclear.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and timely contribution introducing a streaming approach to robust submodular maximization with provable guarantees and an appealingly simple design. While the experimental evaluation could be more compelling and the choice of influence objective should be clarified relative to standard models, the theoretical and algorithmic contributions are solid and potentially impactful.
