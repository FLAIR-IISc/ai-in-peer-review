

This paper introduces a two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint \(k\). The first stage employs a novel streaming algorithm, STAR-T, which leverages a partitioning structure and a decreasing thresholding rule. The second stage applies a simple greedy method. The authors prove STAR-T achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee as \(k \to \infty\) (Theorem 4.1), and Lemmas 4.2–4.3 establish that the combination of STAR-T with greedy handles element loss effectively. Experiments demonstrate strong performance on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation tasks.

The work is significant as the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, addressing a gap with potential broad applicability. STAR-T requires only two hyperparameters, \(\tau\) and \(w\). Theorem 4.5 provides a method to approximate \(\tau\), and the experiments show \(w=1\) yields good results, simplifying practical deployment.

However, two major concerns require clarification. First, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient expressiveness. The STAR-T-Greedy algorithm does not consistently outperform the Sieve-St algorithm across all datasets or settings, as reported in Table 2. Without a clear explanation for these inconsistent results or a thorough comparison of computational efficiency, the claimed superiority remains unsubstantiated.

Second, the influence maximization objective function is defined as \(f(Z) = |N(Z) \cup Z|\) (line 219), which differs from the standard definition in Kempe et al. (SIGKDD 2003), where the objective is typically \(f(Z) = |N(Z)|\) (the size of the reachable set). This discrepancy is critical: the authors’ definition effectively counts both the seed set \(Z\) and its neighbors, whereas the standard model considers only the spread beyond seeds. It is unclear how STAR-T performs under the conventional influence spread model, as the paper does not address this. The experiment’s validity hinges on this definition; if the function deviates from the established literature, the results may not generalize to real-world influence maximization problems.

Additionally, while the paper claims STAR-T is the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, it would strengthen the contribution to explicitly contrast with existing offline robust methods (e.g., [citation]) and clarify the advantages of the streaming approach in dynamic settings. The current discussion of related work is insufficient to establish novelty.

The writing is generally clear, but minor grammatical issues persist (e.g., "the simple greedy method is used" → "a simple greedy method is applied"). Theorem 4.5’s practical guidance for \(\tau\) is valuable, but the experimental section should justify the choice of \(w=1\) beyond empirical observation. Ablation studies on hyperparameter sensitivity would bolster confidence in the method’s robustness.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound algorithm with a clear theoretical foundation and promising empirical results on specific tasks. However, the experimental section requires substantial refinement: the inconsistent comparison with Sieve-St, the non-standard influence model, and the lack of thorough hyperparameter analysis undermine the practical claims. Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. As submitted, the work is interesting but not yet sufficiently compelling for publication due to these unresolved issues.
