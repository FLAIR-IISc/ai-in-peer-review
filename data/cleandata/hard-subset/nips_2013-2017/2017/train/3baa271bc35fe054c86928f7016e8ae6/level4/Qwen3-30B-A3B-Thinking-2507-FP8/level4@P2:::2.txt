

This paper introduces STAR-T, a novel two-stage streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization under cardinality constraints. The authors address a critical gap: optimizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint k when (i) data arrives in a stream and (ii) up to m elements may be removed from the computed summary. STAR-T constructs a robust summary in a single pass using a partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds, while STAR-T-GREEDY (the query stage) guarantees a constant-factor approximation after any m removals. The work is significant as the first streaming algorithm for this robust setting, extending prior non-streaming robust approaches [9, 10, 11] and the streaming SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8].

**Originality and Significance (Strengths):**  
The paper makes a strong contribution by introducing the *partitioned thresholding* structure—a novel combination of exponentially decreasing thresholds and partitioned buckets—to achieve robustness against arbitrary m removals. This differs fundamentally from prior robust centralized methods [11] and streaming approaches [8], which lack robustness guarantees. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 4.1) is rigorous, showing STAR-T stores O((k + m log k) log² k) elements and achieves a 0.149(1 - 1/log k)-approximation. This is highly significant for large-scale applications where re-optimization after removals is infeasible (e.g., social network influence maximization, interactive recommendations). The authors also demonstrate practical utility by showing w=1 suffices for real-world performance, eliminating the need for hyperparameter tuning (Theorem 4.5).

**Quality and Clarity (Strengths):**  
The paper excels in technical quality. The proof strategy—splitting into cases where partitions have many full buckets or not—effectively leverages submodular properties. The algorithmic design (Algorithms 1–2) is clear and well-motivated. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. Clarity is enhanced by the illustrative Figure 1 and the well-structured problem statement.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Experimental Consistency:** The experiments (Section 5) do not consistently demonstrate superiority over SIEVE-STREAMING. In Figures 2(b,d), SIEVE-STREAMING slightly outperforms STAR-T-GREEDY for some k values, despite knowing removals in advance. The claim that STAR-T "matches or outperforms" requires stronger evidence. The authors should clarify *why* STAR-T-GREEDY underperforms in these cases (e.g., sensitivity to m/k ratio) or provide additional experiments (e.g., larger datasets, varied m).

2. **Objective Function Inconsistency:** The influence maximization objective f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z| (line 219) diverges from the standard definition in Kempe et al. [3] (SIGKDD 2003), which uses influence spread via diffusion models. This discrepancy undermines the relevance of the dominating set experiments. The authors must justify their objective choice or reframe the experiments to align with established influence maximization benchmarks.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* First streaming robust submodular algorithm; strong theoretical guarantee (0.149-approx); practical parameterization (w=1); well-motivated for real-world robustness needs.  
*Con:* Experimental results lack consistent superiority over baselines; objective function mismatch in influence maximization weakens validation.

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution with clear practical potential. However, the experimental weaknesses—particularly the inconsistent performance against SIEVE-STREAMING and the objective function inconsistency—prevent it from fully demonstrating significance. I recommend *acceptance with minor revisions* if the authors:  
(i) Clarify the influence maximization objective and revalidate experiments against standard diffusion models, and  
(ii) Provide additional experiments showing consistent outperformance of STAR-T-GREEDY over SIEVE-STREAMING (e.g., for larger m/k or different datasets).  

The work is a solid step toward scalable robust data summarization, and addressing these points would strengthen its impact for the NIPS audience.
