

This paper introduces a two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint \(k\). The first stage employs a streaming algorithm, STAR-T, which leverages a partitioning structure and a decreasing threshold rule. The second stage applies a simple greedy method. The authors establish a constant-factor approximation guarantee for STAR-T as \(k\) grows large (Theorem 4.1), and Lemmas 4.2–4.3 demonstrate that combining STAR-T with the greedy method effectively handles element loss.

The experimental evaluation shows strong performance on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation tasks. This work is noteworthy as the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, offering potential for broad applicability in streaming settings. The STAR-T algorithm has two hyperparameters, \(\tau\) and \(w\). Theorem 4.5 provides guidance on approximating \(\tau\), and the experiments indicate \(w=1\) suffices, making the method straightforward to deploy without extensive hyperparameter tuning.

However, the experimental section presents significant concerns. The STAR-T-Greedy algorithm does not consistently outperform the Sieve-St algorithm across all tested scenarios, undermining the claim of superior empirical performance. Furthermore, the objective function for influence maximization is defined as \(f(Z) = |N(Z) \cup Z|\) (line 219), which differs from the standard cascade model used in the seminal Kempe et al. (SIGKDD 2003) reference. This discrepancy raises questions about the validity of the experimental results, as the performance of STAR-T on this non-standard model does not necessarily reflect its behavior under the established influence spread framework. It remains unclear how the proposed method would perform on the conventional objective used in the field.
