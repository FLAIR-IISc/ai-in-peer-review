This paper proposes a two-stage procedure for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint k. The first stage introduces a robust streaming algorithm, STAR-T, built on a partitioning structure with a decreasing-threshold rule. The second stage applies a simple greedy selection. The authors prove that STAR-T achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee for arbitrarily large k (Theorem 4.1). Lemmas 4.2–4.3 show that combining STAR-T with a simple greedy method remains effective under element deletions. Experiments demonstrate performance on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation.

The work is interesting and, to my knowledge, presents the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization. The streaming setting may have broad applicability, and the framework is insightful. STAR-T involves two hyperparameters, τ and w. Theorem 4.5 explains how to approximate τ, and the experiments indicate that w=1 works well in practice, making the method convenient to use without extensive hyperparameter tuning.

My main concern is that the experimental evaluation is not very compelling. The STAR-T-Greedy algorithm does not consistently outperform the Sieve-St baseline, which makes it harder to assess the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

Additionally, the influence maximization objective is defined as f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z| (line 219), which differs from the standard diffusion-based objectives in Kempe, Kleinberg, and Tardos (KDD 2003). It is unclear how STAR-T performs under those standard spread models. Clarifying this discrepancy and evaluating STAR-T on the commonly used influence propagation models would strengthen the empirical case.
