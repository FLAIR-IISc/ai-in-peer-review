Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies monotone submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in a challenging two-stage setting: (i) a single-pass streaming stage that must retain a small summary, and (ii) a robust query stage in which up to m elements may be removed before constructing a size‑k solution using only the retained summary. The authors propose STAR-T, which organizes the summary into partitions of exponentially growing bucket sizes, paired with exponentially decreasing thresholds, and a query procedure (STAR-T-GREEDY) that runs GREEDY on the surviving elements. They prove a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149 in the limit) after any adversarial removal of m elements, while storing O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements.

The approach extends streaming thresholding ideas (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) to a robust setting by coupling partitioned storage with decreasing thresholds, and relates to non-streaming robust methods that use bucketed structures. Experiments on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation indicate that STAR-T matches or outperforms strong streaming baselines, even when the baselines are given the advantage of knowing the deleted subset in advance.

Strengths:
- Problem importance and novelty: Combining robustness with single-pass streaming for submodular maximization is timely and nontrivial. To my knowledge, this is the first constant-factor result for the robust streaming setting with near-linear memory in k plus O(m log^2 k) overhead.
- Algorithmic design: The partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds are natural and efficient. The query-time reuse of a single robust summary across multiple deletions is compelling in interactive scenarios.
- Theory: A one-pass algorithm with a constant-factor approximation (0.149 asymptotically) against any m deletions and bounded memory is a strong theoretical contribution.
- Empirics: The method performs competitively on two data summarization tasks, even compared to streaming methods that are granted oracle knowledge of the removed set.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Analysis exposition: The paper alludes to an “innovative analysis,” but the proof sketch does not clearly highlight what is technically new beyond prior robust bucket structures. It would help to: (a) isolate the key lemmas that rely on the exponentially decreasing thresholds; (b) explain the balancing argument that yields the 0.149 constant; and (c) clarify the two-case analysis (many full buckets vs. mostly non-full) in a self-contained high-level outline before diving into details.
- Lower bounds: Complementary lower bounds (e.g., on achievable approximation vs. memory with robustness) would strengthen the theoretical narrative and help contextualize the 0.149 constant and the O((m log k + k) log^2 k) space.
- Influence maximization setting: The “dominating set” surrogate objective is a substantial simplification. While understandable for scalability, it is unclear how standard diffusion-based influence objectives (e.g., IC/LT) fit into this streaming framework. A discussion of feasibility, estimators, or known streaming approximations for these models would improve the experimental credibility, even if full-scale experiments are out of scope.
- Practical considerations: The algorithm requires a threshold τ tied to OPT; the paper sketches a standard multi-guess approach. It would help to clearly state the resulting space/time overhead and show a sensitivity analysis (e.g., ε vs. memory/quality trade-offs) in the experiments.

Quality:
The technical claims appear sound and the structure is well motivated. The memory bound and approximation guarantee are carefully argued, but the presentation of the analysis could be clearer, particularly regarding what aspects are novel and how the thresholds interact with robustness to control the effect of deletions.

Clarity:
The algorithmic description is accessible and the empirical section is easy to follow. The analysis section would benefit from a more explicit high-level roadmap and a concise statement of the main proof ideas before the case splits and balancing arguments.

Originality:
The paper is original in jointly addressing streaming and robustness with a one-pass algorithm and constant-factor guarantees. While both partitioned buckets and thresholding have precedents, their combination for robustness in streaming, together with the associated analysis, is novel.

Significance:
The results are practically relevant for robust data summarization at scale. The ability to precompute a single robust summary and answer multiple queries after different deletions is particularly useful. The theoretical guarantee and space bound should be of interest to the community.

Pros:
- First constant-factor algorithm for robust streaming submodular maximization with one pass.
- Clean algorithmic design (partitioned buckets with decreasing thresholds) and reusable robust summary.
- Solid theoretical bounds and competitive empirical performance against strong baselines.

Cons:
- Limited discussion of traditional diffusion-based influence models and their compatibility with the streaming framework used in experiments.
- Lack of lower bounds and limited insight into the tightness of the 0.149 constant.
- Analysis section could better highlight the core innovative ideas and guiding intuition.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a clear, stand-alone high-level proof outline emphasizing what is new in the analysis and why the exponential threshold scheme is crucial.
- Provide a brief discussion (or citation) on handling diffusion-based influence functions in streaming/robust settings.
- Include a short sensitivity study on τ-guessing (ε) and memory parameter w, and discuss empirical robustness to m and k.
- If possible, provide even a simple impossibility or trade-off result to contextualize the obtained constant and space complexity.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution with solid theory and promising empirical evidence; improving the clarity of the analysis and the experimental discussion would further strengthen the paper.
