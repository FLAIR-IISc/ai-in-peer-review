

This paper addresses the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where elements arrive sequentially, and m elements may be removed from the summary after processing. The authors propose STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm that constructs a summary of size O((m log k + k) log² k) with a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) against any m-element removal. This work builds on previous research in streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]) and robust submodular optimization (e.g., PRO [11]), but makes the novel contribution of simultaneously addressing both streaming and robustness requirements, which has not been previously considered.

The paper demonstrates significant theoretical contributions with a well-structured algorithm using partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds. The theoretical analysis, while somewhat dense, establishes a constant-factor approximation guarantee that is particularly impressive given the dual constraints of streaming and robustness. The experimental evaluation in two practical applications (dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation) shows that STAR-T matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter has prior knowledge of the removed elements, which is a strong empirical validation of the approach.

Strengths include the novel algorithmic design that combines partitioning and thresholding techniques in a way that simultaneously handles streaming constraints and robustness requirements. The theoretical results are technically sound, and the experimental validation across two diverse applications provides compelling evidence of practical utility. The paper also clearly articulates the two-stage nature of the problem (streaming and query stages), which is well-motivated by real-world applications.

However, there are several areas for improvement. First, the paper lacks non-trivial lower bounds to complement the upper bound guarantees, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. Second, while the authors claim an "innovative analysis," the paper does not sufficiently clarify what makes the analysis novel or how it differs from previous approaches in robust submodular optimization. The proof sketch would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the key insights that enable the constant-factor guarantee. Third, the influence maximization application uses a simplified objective function (f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|) that does not fully capture traditional influence maximization problems where the objective depends on the entire network structure. It would be more convincing to demonstrate how the approach handles more complex influence functions in a streaming setting.

The writing is generally clear, though the theoretical section could be improved with more explicit explanations of the innovative aspects of the analysis. The significance of the work is high, as it addresses a practically important problem with a theoretically sound solution that has demonstrated empirical effectiveness. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the limitations of the current approach and potential extensions.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel problem setting combining streaming and robustness, strong theoretical results with constant-factor guarantee, compelling experimental validation in two applications, well-motivated two-stage approach.

Arguments against acceptance: Lack of lower bounds, insufficient explanation of the innovative aspects of the analysis, simplified influence maximization objective that doesn't fully represent traditional problems.
