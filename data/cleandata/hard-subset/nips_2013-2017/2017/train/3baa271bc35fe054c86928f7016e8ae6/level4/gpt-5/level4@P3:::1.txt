Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies robust streaming maximization of a monotone submodular function under a cardinality constraint. In the streaming stage, the algorithm sees elements once, retains a compact summary S, and must be robust to any subsequent removal of up to m elements. In the query stage, after an arbitrary set E of size at most m is removed, the goal is to extract a size-k subset Z ⊆ S \ E with large value.

The authors propose STAR-T, a one-pass algorithm that organizes the retained summary into partitions of exponentially increasing bucket sizes with exponentially decreasing marginal-gain thresholds. Each arriving element is placed into the first non-full bucket whose threshold it meets. After removals, a simple greedy procedure (STAR-T-GREEDY) run on S \ E returns the final k-element set.

The main theoretical result shows that, with appropriate thresholding, STAR-T stores at most O((m log k + k) log k) elements when the value f(OPT(k, V \ E)) is known (and O((m log k + k) log2 k) with standard parallel guesses when it is not), and STAR-T-GREEDY achieves a constant-factor approximation: asymptotically 0.149 · f(OPT(k, V \ E)). The analysis hinges on the structure induced by the partitions and thresholds, with case distinctions depending on whether many buckets are full.

This work builds on streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING by Badanidiyuru et al., KDD’14), robust non-streaming algorithms (e.g., Orlin et al. 2016; PRO by Bogunovic et al., ICML’17), and is concurrent with Mirzasoleiman et al. (ICML’17), who give a 1/2 − ε approximation in the robust setting but with memory scaling O(m k log k/ε). The key novelty here is a robust streaming design whose memory dependence on k is only logarithmic for the robustness component (O(m log2 k) vs. O(mk log k/ε)).

Quality
- Theory: The algorithm and analysis appear technically sound. The memory bound and 0.149 constant-factor approximation are clearly stated, with a proof sketch that explains the core case analysis. The paper carefully treats the unknown-OPT issue via parallel thresholds, as is standard in streaming.
- Experiments: Two applications (dominating set on Twitter/Amazon graphs and personalized movie recommendation on MovieLens) demonstrate that STAR-T-GREEDY matches or slightly outperforms streaming/non-streaming baselines that are given the advantage of knowing the deletions in advance. Results include random and adversarial deletions and an interactive scenario (genre filtering).
- Reproducibility: Algorithms are simple to implement given an oracle for f. Some practical choices (e.g., setting τ and w, number of parallel instances) could be documented more explicitly.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The partitioned-threshold structure and the two-stage protocol are clearly described, and pseudocode is provided. A few clarifications would help:
- Threshold formula: The text says the threshold for partition i is τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Please reconcile this to avoid confusion.
- Memory bounds: The abstract cites O((m log k + k) log2 k), while Theorem 4.1 yields O((m log k + k) log k) when τ is known and Theorem 4.5 adds a log factor. Summarize these cases together in the main text.
- Practical guidance: Explain how τ and w are chosen in experiments, the number of parallel instances used, and the empirical sensitivity to these parameters.

Originality
The partitioned thresholding scheme adapted to the streaming, robust setting is novel relative to both SIEVE-STREAMING and prior robust non-streaming approaches such as PRO. The analysis that balances guarantees across partitions to obtain a constant approximation is also new. The problem formulation—robust streaming maximization—has seen concurrent independent work, and this paper presents a different memory–approximation tradeoff.

Significance
The robust streaming model is relevant to large-scale summarization where elements may be removed post hoc (failures, preferences, “right to be forgotten”). The paper advances the state of the art by providing a one-pass robust algorithm with compact memory and a constant-factor guarantee. The approximation constant is modest, but the memory scaling is attractive compared to prior robust approaches. The experiments support practical utility, though broader, higher-scale evaluations would further strengthen the case.

Strengths
- One-pass algorithm with a simple, implementable structure and clear robustness guarantee.
- Memory that scales as O(m log2 k) (plus O(k log2 k)), substantially improving the dependence on k over concurrent work.
- Constant-factor approximation with a clean, case-based analysis.
- Empirical comparisons that are arguably conservative against the proposed method (baselines know deletions in advance), yet STAR-T remains competitive.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Approximation factor (≈0.149) is relatively low; discuss whether tighter constants or dependence on w are possible.
- Dependence on an approximation to f(OPT) via parallel instances increases memory by another log factor; quantify the practical overhead and how τ was chosen in experiments.
- Robustness parameter w: Theory requires w ≥ 4 log k · m/k; experiments use w = 1 even when removals exceed k, which goes beyond the formal guarantee. Include results varying w and characterize the tradeoff empirically.
- Scope of applications and scale: Experiments focus on coverage-style objectives; consider adding settings where f is expensive to evaluate (e.g., influence maximization via simulations) to demonstrate oracle efficiency, or larger-scale streams with runtime/oracle-call counts.
- Minor presentation issues: fix typos and ensure consistency in notation and thresholds.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and timely robust streaming variant with a principled, novel algorithm.
- Strong memory–approximation tradeoff and one-pass design.
- Solid theory complemented by practical evidence and interactive use cases.

Arguments against acceptance
- Modest approximation constant and limited empirical scale.
- Practical guidance on choosing τ, w, and the number of parallel instances is light.
- Application motivation could be strengthened with settings where deletions are central and m is large relative to k.

Overall assessment
A technically solid and well-motivated contribution to robust streaming submodular maximization with a novel algorithmic design and meaningful theoretical and empirical results. While the approximation constant and empirical scope leave room for improvement, the paper is within the scope of NIPS and would be of interest to the community working on large-scale submodular optimization.

Recommendation: Borderline accept
