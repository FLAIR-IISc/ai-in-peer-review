- Problem: Robust streaming maximization of a monotone submodular function under a cardinality constraint, remaining near-optimal after removal of up to m elements.
- Applications: Relevant to large-scale ML tasks like data summarization, influence maximization, facility location, and exemplar-based clustering.
- Motivation note: Robustness is important for sets subject to failures, removals, or user preferences, but the paper could more strongly justify relevance to the conference.
- Method: Proposes a two-stage approach—STAR-T (one-pass streaming construction robust to m removals) and STAR-T-GREEDY (query-stage selection of ≤k elements).
- Technique: Uses a partitioned thresholding strategy to construct a robust candidate set in streaming fashion.
- Theoretical result: Provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the robust submodular maximization problem.
- Experiments: Empirical evaluation on two data summarization tasks shows matching or better performance than existing greedy and streaming baselines.
- Strength: Tackles a challenging problem and offers a novel, technically sound algorithm with thorough analysis.
- Strength: Analysis and presentation of theoretical guarantees are rigorous and well-presented.
- Strength: Experimental results convincingly demonstrate practical effectiveness.
- Weakness: Problem statement and motivation could be stronger and more clearly tied to the conference audience.
- Weakness: Paper lacks sufficient discussion of limitations and directions for future work.
- Weakness: Some notation and terminology are unclear or could be simplified; more diagrams/illustrations would aid understanding.
- Originality: Novel algorithm and analysis for robust streaming submodular maximization using partitioned thresholding.
- Significance: Addresses an important problem with practical applications and delivers provable guarantees and empirical validation.
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, technically solid contribution with constant-factor guarantees and convincing experiments.
- Rejection arguments: Insufficiently strong motivation/relevance, limited discussion of limitations/future work, and some clarity issues in notation and presentation.
