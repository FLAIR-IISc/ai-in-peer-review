This paper introduces a two-stage procedure for the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a streaming and robust setting. The problem is formulated as follows: first, a single-pass streaming algorithm processes a dataset to create a compact and robust summary `S`. Second, after an arbitrary set of `m` elements is removed from this summary, a query algorithm must efficiently find a high-quality solution of size at most `k` from the remaining elements, without accessing the original dataset.

The proposed solution consists of the `STAR-T` algorithm for the streaming stage and the `STAR-T-GREEDY` algorithm for the query stage. The `STAR-T` algorithm is the main contribution, employing a novel structure of partitions and buckets combined with an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. Elements from the stream are placed into buckets based on their marginal gain relative to these thresholds. The resulting summary `S` is then used by `STAR-T-GREEDY`, which is simply the standard greedy algorithm run on the summary after `m` elements have been removed.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that their method achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The key ideas in the proof involve analyzing two cases: one where a sufficient number of buckets in the summary are full, and another where they are not. This work builds upon and extends concepts from previous literature in both streaming submodular maximization (e.g., the thresholding idea from SIEVE-STREAMING by Badanidiyuru et al., 2014) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., the partitioned structure is reminiscent of the non-streaming algorithm by Bogunovic et al., 2017). The paper also compares its memory complexity favorably against a concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017), noting its logarithmic dependence on `k`.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee (Theorem 4.1), and the proof sketch clearly outlines the main arguments, which appear correct. The authors are also careful to address the practical issue of not knowing the optimal value `f(OPT)` beforehand, providing a standard parallelization approach to approximate it (Theorem 4.5). However, a point of concern lies in the experimental setup for the dominating set problem. The objective function is defined as `f(Z) = |N(Z) U Z|`, which is the size of the set and its neighborhood. While this is a valid and well-studied submodular function, framing this experiment as "influence maximization" could be misleading, as this objective is distinct from the stochastic spread models (like Independent Cascade or Linear Threshold) commonly associated with that term since the work of Kempe et al. (2003). It would be beneficial for the authors to clarify this distinction or to evaluate their algorithm on a standard influence spread model.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement is well-motivated with compelling examples. The `STAR-T` algorithm is explained effectively, and the partitioned structure is helpfully illustrated in Figure 1. The theoretical results are stated precisely, and the proof sketches provide good intuition for the main analysis.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. It addresses a novel and practical problem at the intersection of three important areas: submodular maximization, streaming algorithms, and robust optimization. The proposed `STAR-T` algorithm, which combines a partitioned data structure with an exponentially decreasing thresholding scheme, is a novel contribution tailored specifically for this challenging setting. To my knowledge, this paper and its concurrent counterpart are the first to provide provable guarantees for robust submodular maximization in the streaming model.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is of high significance. As datasets grow and machine learning systems are deployed in dynamic and sometimes adversarial environments, the need for algorithms that are both scalable (streaming) and resilient (robust) is paramount. This work provides a foundational contribution in this direction. The proposed two-stage framework is particularly relevant for applications where a large summary can be pre-computed, and fast, robust queries are needed later. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by future research in this area.

One of the paper's primary strengths is its empirical performance. The key comparison is against SIEVE-STREAMING, a strong baseline that is given the significant advantage of knowing the removed set `E` in advance. In this difficult setting, STAR-T-GREEDY’s ability to match, and sometimes slightly outperform, this "clairvoyant" baseline is a powerful demonstration of its effectiveness. The draft's presentation could be improved by emphasizing this point more strongly; achieving comparable performance without knowing the removals is a major success, not a neutral outcome.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Important Problem:** The paper formalizes and provides the first solution (along with a concurrent work) for robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, a problem with clear practical relevance.
2.  **Novel Algorithm and Strong Theory:** The proposed `STAR-T` algorithm is an elegant and novel combination of ideas. The theoretical analysis is solid, providing a constant-factor approximation guarantee.
3.  **Favorable Memory Complexity:** The algorithm's memory requirement has a logarithmic dependence on `k`, which is an advantage over concurrently developed methods.
4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that the algorithm performs exceptionally well in practice, often matching the performance of a powerful baseline that is given access to future information (i.e., the set of removed items).

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Clarity of Experimental Framing:** The experiment on the dominating set problem is presented as "influence maximization," which could cause confusion due to the use of a non-standard objective function for that task. This should be clarified.
2.  **Understated Empirical Success:** The paper could frame its experimental results more effectively. Matching a clairvoyant baseline is a significant achievement for a robust algorithm, and this strength could be highlighted more clearly.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It makes a significant and original contribution to an important and emerging area of machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
