This paper addresses the problem of submodular maximization under the joint constraints of a streaming model and a robust objective. In this setting, the goal is to select a high-value set of `k` elements from a data stream, such that the set's value remains high even after an adversary removes up to `m` chosen elements. While streaming submodular maximization and robust submodular maximization have been studied independently—motivated by large-scale data and the need for resilient solutions, respectively—this work is the first to tackle the combined problem.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which in a single pass requires O((m log k + k) log² k) space and provides a constant-factor (0.149) approximation guarantee. The algorithm is robust to the adversarial removal of any `m` elements from the solution. The approach builds on established partitioning and thresholding techniques that utilize buckets, but introduces a novel method for populating these buckets within the streaming context. The authors experimentally validate their algorithm on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, demonstrating strong empirical performance. Notably, STAR-T remains competitive even when compared to non-robust streaming algorithms that have oracle access to the set of elements that will be removed.

The combined streaming and robust setting is highly challenging, and achieving a constant-factor approximation is a significant theoretical contribution. Nevertheless, the paper could be improved in several areas.

First, the theoretical analysis would be strengthened by the inclusion of non-trivial lower bounds to contextualize the tightness of the authors' upper bound guarantee.

Second, the presentation of the theoretical analysis could be improved. The authors describe their analysis as "innovative" but do not elaborate on what aspects are novel. Furthermore, the provided proof sketch is dense, making it difficult to discern the core ideas and key steps of their argument. A more detailed and intuitive explanation of the proof strategy would greatly benefit the paper.

Finally, the influence maximization application is not entirely convincing. The experiments employ a simplified objective function based only on the size of the immediate neighborhood of the seed nodes. It remains unclear how the proposed streaming model could accommodate standard influence maximization objectives (e.g., based on propagation models like IC or LT), which require global network information to evaluate the function for an arbitrary set of seeds. This simplification raises questions about the algorithm's applicability to more realistic influence maximization scenarios.
