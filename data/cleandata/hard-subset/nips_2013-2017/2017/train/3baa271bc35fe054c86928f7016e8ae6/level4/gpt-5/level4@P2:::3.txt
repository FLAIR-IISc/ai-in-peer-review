Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles monotone submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in a challenging two-stage setting: elements are seen in a single streaming pass, a small summary is stored, and after the stream up to m items may be removed adversarially; at query time, one must return a size-k set using only the retained summary. The authors propose STAR-T, which organizes the stored elements into exponentially sized bucket partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds, and a second-stage procedure (STAR-T-GREEDY) that runs GREEDY on the surviving elements S \ E. Assuming an estimate of OPT, the algorithm stores O((k + m log k) log^2 k) elements, and after any removal of m elements achieves a constant-factor approximation (asymptotically 0.149). When OPT is unknown, they run multiple instances with geometrically spaced thresholds, incurring an additional logarithmic factor.

This work bridges two active lines: streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING achieving ~0.5 in the non-robust case) and robust submodular maximization in the offline setting (e.g., bucketed/partitioned constructions like PRO). The authors also discuss concurrent work on deletion-robust streaming that attains a 1/2 − ε guarantee but with O(mk log k/ε) memory; in contrast, STAR-T’s dependence on k in the “robust” term is only logarithmic. Experiments on graph dominating-set proxies and a personalized recommendation objective show that STAR-T’s query-stage performance matches or exceeds baselines, including those that are given the removed set in advance.

Quality:
- Theory: The algorithm is one-pass and yields a constant-factor approximation after adversarial deletion, with memory scaling near-linearly in k and only logarithmically in k for the m-dependent term. The proof strategy hinges on (i) ensuring many full buckets at some partition or (ii) exploiting properties of the last underfull bucket, plus standard GREEDY bounds. The presentation states a 0.149 constant (as k grows) and provides a path to handle unknown OPT via parallel thresholds. While the key lemmas are plausible and aligned with known techniques, the sketch does not fully surface the core proof invariants and how the partitioned thresholds interact with deletions.
- Empirics: The evaluations are sensible for summarization tasks, and the comparisons are favorable even when baselines know E. The choice to test both GREEDY and SIEVE-STREAMING at query time is helpful. Runtime/memory measurements and sensitivity to w/thresholding would strengthen the case.

Clarity:
The high-level description of STAR-T and its structure is clear. The analysis section, however, repeatedly refers to “innovative analysis” without crisply isolating the novel technical ingredients. The role of each lemma, the balancing of bounds, and the necessity of the threshold schedule could be explained more intuitively. It would also help to clarify the space bounds: some places state O((k + m log k) log k) versus O((k + m log k) log^2 k), and to explicitly separate the cost with known-OPT versus parallel-threshold variants.

Originality:
The combination of a streaming summary that is robust to deletions, realized via partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds and a tailored analysis, appears novel. The memory-approximation trade-off versus concurrent deletion-robust streaming is particularly interesting, as the robust memory term is only logarithmic in k.

Significance:
A deletion-robust one-pass summary is valuable for large-scale settings where recomputation is infeasible. Although the constant is modest, the guarantee is the first of its kind under these constraints, and the empirical results suggest good practical performance. This could influence subsequent work on robust streaming summaries and deletion-resilient models.

Strengths:
- First principled treatment of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting with a provable constant factor.
- Careful algorithmic design (partitioned buckets, decreasing thresholds) yielding compact summaries.
- Competitive empirical results on two applications, including interactive scenarios and query-time flexibility (GREEDY or streaming on S \ E).
- Favorable memory–approximation trade-offs compared to concurrent deletion-robust streaming methods.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Analysis exposition: Provide a more intuitive walkthrough of the two main cases, the role of full vs. underfull buckets, and how thresholds protect against deletions. A pictorial invariant or potential function would help.
- Constant and practicality: The 0.149 factor is conservative; discuss typical empirical constants and how parameter choices (w, threshold grid) affect quality and memory in practice.
- OPT dependence: Explain the parallel-threshold construction more concretely, its additional space factor, and guidance on choosing ε. Include ablations showing robustness to mis-specified τ.
- Space bounds: Reconcile O((k + m log k) log k) vs. log^2 factors and clearly separate the base algorithm (known τ) from the parallel variant.
- Experimental realism: The “influence” objective uses neighborhood coverage; consider adding at least a small-scale study under standard diffusion models (IC/LT) or justify the proxy more rigorously. Report wall-clock, oracle-call counts, and memory usage. Explore larger deletions (|E| > m) systematically and sensitivity to w.
- Related work: Expand the comparison to concurrent deletion-robust streaming in more detail (assumptions, oracle model, memory, approximation), and clarify advantages/limitations.

Pro-accept:
- Novel and well-motivated problem setting; first constant-factor robust streaming summary for submodular maximization.
- Solid algorithmic idea with a clean one-pass design and clear query-stage interface.
- Good empirical performance and practical appeal.

Con-accept:
- The analytical novelty is not sufficiently distilled in the exposition; constants are small and space bounds have minor inconsistencies across sections.
- Experimental influence-maximization proxy may understate difficulty; limited systems-level evaluation.

Overall recommendation:
Borderline accept. The problem is important, the approach is original, and the theoretical guarantee under stringent constraints is valuable. With improved clarity of the analysis and stronger empirical justification (especially for influence), this would be a clear accept.
