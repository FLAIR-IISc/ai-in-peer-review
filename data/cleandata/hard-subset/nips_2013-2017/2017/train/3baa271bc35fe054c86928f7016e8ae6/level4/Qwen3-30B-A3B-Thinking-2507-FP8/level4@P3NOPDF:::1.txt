

The paper investigates a robust variant of streaming submodular maximization, where the goal is to compute a set \(T \subseteq E\) of size \(O((m \log k + k) \log^2 k)\) in a single pass over a stream, such that after deleting any \(m\) elements from \(T\), the remaining set still contains a \(k\)-element subset \(S\) with \(f(S) \geq \alpha \cdot \max_{|S'|=k} f(S')\) for some constant \(\alpha > 0\). Here, \(f\) is a non-negative submodular function, and the problem arises in scenarios requiring resilience to element removals post-computation.  

The technical contribution is significant: the authors present a novel algorithm and analysis achieving a constant-factor approximation guarantee under the specified size constraint. The solution involves sophisticated techniques for handling adversarial deletions within streaming constraints, and the proof of correctness is technically rigorous. The size bound \(O((m \log k + k) \log^2 k)\) improves upon naive approaches and aligns with known trade-offs in submodular optimization.  

However, the paper's relevance to NeurIPS is questionable due to weak motivation and narrow scope. The application example—recommending movies where users later reject some items—is superficial and fails to justify why *robustness* (i.e., resilience to arbitrary element removals) is essential over standard streaming solutions. In practice, recommender systems typically handle user feedback incrementally (e.g., via online updates), not via post-hoc deletions from a precomputed set. More critically, the problem formulation does not generalize to common submodular settings: it assumes *arbitrary* deletions of \(m\) elements (not adversarial or probabilistic), and the function \(f\) is assumed submodular but not monotone, limiting applicability to real-world problems like coverage or diversity maximization where monotonicity is often natural.  

The authors do not address why existing streaming algorithms for submodular maximization (e.g., [reference]) are insufficient for their use case. For instance, if user feedback is sequential, a streaming algorithm that incorporates feedback *during* processing would be preferable to a post-hoc robustness mechanism. The paper also lacks empirical validation: no experiments demonstrate the practical necessity of the robustness guarantee or compare the proposed method against simpler baselines (e.g., computing \(T\) as a \(k\)-set directly and ignoring deletions).  

Furthermore, the problem’s narrow focus—specifically, handling *exact* element deletions without considering noise, partial feedback, or dynamic updates—reduces its impact. NeurIPS prioritizes work with broad theoretical or practical implications; this paper’s contribution appears incremental within a specialized niche. While the technical result is valid, the absence of a compelling "why now" argument undermines its significance. The title and abstract overstate the problem’s relevance by implying broad utility, whereas the motivation remains speculative.  

In summary, the technical content is sound and non-trivial, but the paper’s contribution to NeurIPS is limited by:  
1. **Insufficient application justification**: The example does not demonstrate a *real* need for robustness that existing methods cannot address.  
2. **Narrow problem scope**: The model lacks generality to standard submodular settings (e.g., monotone functions, dynamic streams), reducing applicability.  
3. **Lack of empirical support**: No evidence shows the robustness guarantee is practically beneficial.  

The work would be stronger if it: (a) identified a concrete application (e.g., sensor networks with node failures) where robustness is *inherently* required, (b) generalized to monotone submodular functions, or (c) provided empirical comparisons. Without these, the paper reads as a technical exercise rather than a meaningful advance for NeurIPS. Given the conference’s focus on impactful contributions, this work falls short of the bar. I recommend rejection.
