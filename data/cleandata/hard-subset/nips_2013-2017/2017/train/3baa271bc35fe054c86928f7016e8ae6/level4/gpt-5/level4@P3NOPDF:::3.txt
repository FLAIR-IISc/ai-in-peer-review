Summary
The paper studies submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming model with robustness to the worst-case removal of up to m elements after the stream. The authors propose STAR-T, which stores O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements and guarantees a constant-factor (0.149) approximation to the optimal value that can be achieved by k elements after any adversarial removal of m items. The algorithm uses a partitioning-and-thresholding scheme with buckets. Experiments on influence maximization (with a simplified coverage objective) and movie recommendation indicate good empirical performance, including against a streaming baseline that is given the set of removed items in advance.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and practically relevant setting that combines two challenging constraints—streaming and robustness—where prior work typically handles only one at a time.
- Provides a constant-factor approximation with explicit space bounds that are near-linear in k and m up to polylogarithmic factors.
- The algorithmic design is simple enough to be implementable, yet nontrivial; the bucketed thresholds are well-motivated and connect to recent advances in robust submodular optimization.
- Empirical results suggest the method is competitive in practice, even under strong testing conditions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Lack of lower bounds: There is no nontrivial space-approximation or approximation-factor lower bound specific to the robust streaming setting. This limits our ability to judge how tight the algorithm’s memory and approximation guarantees are. It would be valuable to at least discuss known barriers or provide reductions that imply hardness or space lower bounds.
- Analysis clarity: The paper claims an “innovative analysis” but does not clearly articulate what is fundamentally new. The proof sketch is difficult to follow; the roles of the partitions, thresholds, and bucket-filling policy—and how they interact to ensure robustness—are not distilled into a concise set of ideas before diving into technical details.
- Approximation constant and dependencies: The 0.149 constant is respectable, but the path to this number (and whether it can be improved with tuning) is opaque. Likewise, it is not clear whether the log^2 k factor in storage is necessary or an artifact of the analysis.
- Assumptions and parameter knowledge: It is unclear whether the algorithm requires prior knowledge of m and k (and the value range for thresholds) and how sensitive performance is to mis-specification. The paper should clarify the model (adversarial vs. random order) and whether the algorithm is order-robust.
- Per-item processing time and practical memory: While asymptotic space is given, the practical constants, update time per element, and cost of post-processing after deletions are not reported. These are critical in streaming settings.
- Experimental realism for influence maximization: The influence objective is simplified to one-hop neighborhood coverage, which does not capture dependencies in standard diffusion models. It is unclear how STAR-T would handle objectives where evaluating f(S) requires global graph computations. The comparison to a baseline that knows the removed items in advance is interesting but may not reflect realistic constraints or may advantage certain methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the “innovative analysis.” Provide a high-level roadmap that: (i) explains the intuition behind partitioning and threshold choices; (ii) highlights what fails with naive bucket filling and how your variant fixes it; and (iii) lists the key lemmas, their roles, and the logical progression to the final guarantee. A small illustrative example showing bucket behavior before and after deletions would help.
- Discuss lower bounds or barriers. Even partial results—e.g., connections to known streaming lower bounds for coverage, or hardness of robust selection under adversarial deletions—would contextualize the achieved space and approximation factors. If tight lower bounds are out of reach, state this explicitly and position your result relative to related impossibility results.
- Parameterization and adaptivity. State clearly what parameters must be known a priori (m, k, value range, curvature if used), how thresholds are set, and whether the algorithm can be made parameter-robust (e.g., doubling/guessing schemes). Analyze or at least empirically evaluate sensitivity to mis-specified m and k.
- Complexity details. Report per-item update time, memory footprints (with constants), and post-deletion reconstruction cost. Provide throughput and memory usage on the experimental datasets to demonstrate practicality.
- Experimental suite. To strengthen the empirical case:
  - Include more standard submodular objectives beyond one-hop coverage, such as facility location, weighted coverage, or determinantal diversity, where function evaluation is feasible in streaming. For influence, consider approximate objectives (e.g., sketch-based proxies) or at least multi-hop heuristics to narrow the realism gap.
  - Compare against both non-robust streaming baselines (to quantify the cost of robustness) and offline robust algorithms (to gauge the streaming gap).
  - Vary k and m over a wide range; plot robustness curves (value vs. number of deletions) and sensitivity to stream order (random vs. adversarial permutations).
  - Provide ablations on bucket sizes, the threshold schedule, and the number of partitions to show their effect on quality and memory.
  - Ensure fairness of the “knows deletions” baseline by clarifying what information each method uses during streaming and how this affects feasibility.
- Presentation and notation. Define all notation locally; avoid burying critical constants. Summarize the space bound and approximation guarantee in the main text with clear dependencies on m, k, and any probability parameters. Include a compact table of theoretical guarantees for STAR-T and baselines.

Minor comments
- Clarify whether deletions are worst-case adversarial after the stream or can occur during streaming, and whether STAR-T handles online deletions.
- State assumptions on monotonicity, normalization, and curvature (if any).
- Provide code or pseudocode with all implementation choices needed to reproduce results.

Overall evaluation
The paper tackles a compelling and nontrivial problem and provides the first constant-factor algorithm for robust submodular maximization in the streaming model, with solid theoretical guarantees and encouraging empirical results. However, the clarity of the analysis, the lack of lower bounds or discussion of optimality, and the limited realism of the influence maximization experiments weaken the impact. I lean toward a weak accept if the authors can significantly improve the exposition of the analysis, clarify assumptions and complexity, and broaden/strengthen the experiments; otherwise, this is borderline.
