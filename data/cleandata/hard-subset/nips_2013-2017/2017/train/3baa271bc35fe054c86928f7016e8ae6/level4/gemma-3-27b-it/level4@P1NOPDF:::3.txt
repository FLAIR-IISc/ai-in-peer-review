This paper addresses the problem of submodular maximization in a streaming and robust setting. The goal is to identify a set of *k* high-value elements from a stream of data, while ensuring the solution remains effective even after a subset of *m* elements are removed. Streaming submodular maximization has received considerable attention due to its applicability to large-scale problems where only a limited number of passes over the data are feasible. Robustness has recently emerged as another important consideration, motivated by scenarios where elements may be lost and the solution needs to be resilient to such removals. This work is the first to jointly tackle the challenges of streaming and robust submodular maximization.

The authors introduce the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains O((m log k + k)log<sup>2</sup> k) elements after processing the stream and provides a constant-factor (0.149) approximation guarantee while being robust to the removal of *m* elements. The algorithm combines partitioning and thresholding techniques for submodular optimization, leveraging a bucket-based approach. While similar bucket-based ideas have appeared in previous work on robust submodular optimization, this paper employs a novel bucket-filling strategy. The algorithm’s performance is empirically demonstrated on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, showing competitive results even against streaming algorithms with prior knowledge of element removals.

The combined streaming and robustness requirements create a challenging problem setting, and achieving a constant-factor approximation is a significant accomplishment. However, several aspects of the paper could be strengthened. A companion lower bound, even a non-trivial one, would help contextualize the upper bound. The analysis could also benefit from improved clarity; the authors mention an “innovative analysis” but don’t elaborate on its novelty, making it difficult to follow the core ideas and key steps of the proof sketch. Finally, the application to influence maximization could be more compelling. The experiments utilize a simplified objective function—namely, the size of the neighborhood of the seed nodes—which doesn’t fully capture the complexity of traditional influence maximization objectives where the function's value depends on the entire network for a given set of seed nodes. It’s unclear how these more traditional objectives could be adapted to a streaming environment.
