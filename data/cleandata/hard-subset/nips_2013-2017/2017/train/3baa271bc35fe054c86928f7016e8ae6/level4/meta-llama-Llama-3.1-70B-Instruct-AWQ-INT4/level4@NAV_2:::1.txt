The paper studies the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint in a streaming fashion with an additional robustness requirement. The goal is to find a set of elements that can withstand the removal of up to m elements and still produce a good solution. This problem has applications in data summarization, influence maximization, and personalized recommendation systems.

The authors propose a two-stage procedure, consisting of a streaming algorithm STAR-T that constructs a robust summary, and a query algorithm STAR-T-GREEDY that produces a solution from the summary. The paper shows that STAR-T achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the robust submodular maximization problem, while using a memory of size O((k + m log k) log k).

The paper's results are technically sound, and the algorithm and its analysis are non-trivial. However, I have some concerns regarding the relevance of this paper to the conference. While the problem is interesting, the applications and their motivation could be stronger. The paper could benefit from more discussion on the practical implications of the robustness requirement and how it arises in real-world scenarios.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their algorithm and its analysis. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide sufficient background information on submodular functions and streaming algorithms.

The originality of the paper lies in its novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule, which allows for a constant-factor approximation guarantee in the robust setting. The paper also provides a thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance and shows that it matches or outperforms existing greedy and streaming methods in several data summarization tasks.

The significance of the paper is that it provides a solution to a challenging problem in submodular optimization, which has applications in various fields. The paper's results could be of interest to researchers working on data summarization, influence maximization, and personalized recommendation systems.
