This paper introduces a two-stage procedure for the problem of robust submodular maximization subject to a cardinality constraint `k`. The proposed method first employs a novel streaming algorithm, STAR-T, which utilizes a partitioning structure and a decreasing thresholding rule to select a candidate set of elements. In the second stage, a simple greedy algorithm is applied to this candidate set to produce the final solution. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for their approach, establishing a constant approximation guarantee for the STAR-T algorithm as `k` approaches infinity (Theorem 4.1). Furthermore, Lemmas 4.2 and 4.3 demonstrate that the combined STAR-T-Greedy procedure is robust to the loss of elements from the selected set. The effectiveness of the algorithm is evaluated on two applications: influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation.

**Strengths:**

A significant contribution of this work is its novelty. To my knowledge, this is the first paper to address robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. This is an important and challenging problem, given the ubiquity of streaming data, and this work could inspire further research in this direction.

From a practical standpoint, the STAR-T algorithm is well-designed. While it has two hyperparameters, `τ` and `w`, the authors provide a method to approximate `τ` (Theorem 4.5) and demonstrate empirically that a simple choice of `w=1` performs well. This convenience significantly enhances the usability of the algorithm, as it reduces the need for extensive hyperparameter tuning.

**Weaknesses:**

My primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation, which could be strengthened to make a more compelling case for the proposed algorithm's superiority. The results indicate that the proposed STAR-T-Greedy algorithm does not consistently outperform the Sieve-St baseline across all settings. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss the conditions under which their algorithm is expected to excel and provide more analysis on these comparative results.

Furthermore, there is a significant point of confusion regarding the setup of the influence maximization experiment. The objective function used, `f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|` (line 219), measures the size of the seed set plus its immediate neighbors. This is a simplification and differs from the standard stochastic influence spread models (e.g., Independent Cascade or Linear Threshold) established in the seminal work by Kempe, Kleinberg, and Tardos (2003) that the paper cites. This choice of objective function makes it difficult to assess how the algorithm would perform on the more common and complex problem of maximizing influence spread. I would encourage the authors to either clarify the rationale for using this simplified objective or, ideally, evaluate their method using a standard influence spread model to demonstrate its performance on the problem as it is typically understood in the literature.
