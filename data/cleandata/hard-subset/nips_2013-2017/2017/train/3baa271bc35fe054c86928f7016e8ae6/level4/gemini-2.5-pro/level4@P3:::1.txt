### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a streaming setting, with the additional requirement of robustness against adversarial removals. The problem is framed in a two-stage process. In the first stage, a single pass is made over a data stream to create a compact and robust summary, `S`. In the second stage, after an arbitrary set `E` of up to `m` elements is removed from the summary `S`, a near-optimal `k`-sized solution `Z` must be constructed from the remaining elements `S \ E`.

The authors propose a novel two-stage procedure:
1.  **STAR-T (Streaming Robust Thresholding):** A one-pass streaming algorithm that builds the robust summary `S`. Its core idea is a partitioning structure where elements are placed into buckets based on their marginal gain against exponentially decreasing thresholds. This structure is designed to guarantee that the summary `S` contains high-quality elements distributed in a way that is resilient to removals.
2.  **STAR-T-GREEDY:** A simple greedy algorithm run on the remaining summary `S \ E` during the query stage to produce the final solution `Z`.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee (approximately 0.149). The algorithm produces a summary of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`, which is notably efficient in its dependence on `k`.

This work builds upon the established literature of both streaming submodular maximization and robust submodular maximization. It extends the thresholding ideas from algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING [8] to the robust setting and adapts a partitioning structure similar to that used in the centralized robust algorithm PRO [11], but employs a novel threshold-based filling mechanism suitable for streaming. The paper also contrasts its results with the concurrent work of Mirzasoleiman et al. [18], highlighting a key advantage in memory complexity (logarithmic vs. linear dependence on `k`).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and challenging problem at the intersection of three important areas: submodular optimization, streaming algorithms, and robustness. Providing a single-pass, low-memory algorithm with a constant-factor approximation guarantee for this problem is a significant contribution. The improved memory complexity over concurrent work (`O(log k)` vs. `O(k)`) makes this approach particularly relevant for scenarios where the desired solution size `k` is large.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed STAR-T algorithm is elegant, and its analysis is technically sound and non-trivial. The proof strategy, which involves balancing bounds derived from two distinct cases (partitions being mostly full vs. mostly not full), is clever and demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem structure. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis provided.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm's structure is clearly explained with an illustrative figure, and the problem statement is precise. The relationship to prior work is clearly articulated, which helps place the contribution in the proper context.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Motivation of the Adversarial Model:** My main concern lies with the specific formulation of the robust problem. The model assumes that the `m` adversarial removals occur from the algorithm's summary `S` (`E ⊆ S`). This implies an adversary who not only knows the data stream but also the exact internal state and output of the streaming algorithm. While theoretically interesting, the practical motivation for this specific model feels somewhat weak. In many applications (like the movie recommendation example), removals are based on external factors (e.g., a movie has already been seen) that are independent of whether the item was selected for the summary. It would strengthen the paper to include a more detailed discussion on the practical scenarios where this specific `E ⊆ S` adversarial model is most appropriate, and how it compares to the arguably more natural model where removals `E` can come from the entire ground set `V`.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method performs well, matching or outperforming a strong non-robust baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING) that has the advantage of knowing the removed set in advance. However, the evaluation could be more comprehensive. The experiments do not compare against other robust methods, such as the concurrent work [18] or a potential streaming adaptation of centralized methods like [11]. While implementing other methods may be difficult, their absence makes it harder to gauge the practical performance of STAR-T relative to the state of the art in robust optimization.

3.  **The Constant Factor:** While achieving any constant factor is a strong theoretical result, the proven approximation ratio of 0.149 is quite modest. Although theoretical bounds are often loose, this may limit the practical appeal for applications requiring solutions very close to optimal. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss whether this constant is an artifact of the analysis or inherent to the problem.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and important problem: robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting.
*   The proposed algorithm and its analysis are technically strong, non-trivial, and represent a solid research contribution.
*   The algorithm achieves a significantly better memory complexity (logarithmic dependence on `k`) compared to concurrent work, which is a key advantage for scalability.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly positions its contributions within the existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical motivation for the specific adversarial model (removals from the algorithm's summary `S`) could be stronger and its implications better discussed.
*   The experimental comparison is somewhat limited, as it primarily measures performance against a non-robust baseline rather than other robust algorithms.
*   The proven constant in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable and technically sound contribution to the literature on submodular optimization. The development of a single-pass, low-memory streaming algorithm for the robust submodular maximization problem is a significant achievement. Despite some concerns about the practical motivation of the specific adversarial model and the scope of the experiments, the strengths of the paper—particularly its technical novelty and superior memory complexity—outweigh these weaknesses. The work is likely to be of interest to researchers in large-scale machine learning and optimization and could inspire further research in this area. I recommend acceptance.
