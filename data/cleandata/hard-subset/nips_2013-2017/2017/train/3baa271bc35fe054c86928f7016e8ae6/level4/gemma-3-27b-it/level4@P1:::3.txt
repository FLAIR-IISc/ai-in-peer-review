This paper studies the problem of submodular maximization in a streaming and robust setting. In this setting, the goal is to find a set of *k* elements with high value from a stream of data, while ensuring the solution remains effective even after a subset of *m* elements are removed. Streaming submodular maximization has been widely studied due to its relevance to large-scale applications where repeated access to the entire dataset is impractical. Robustness has also recently gained attention, motivated by scenarios where elements may be lost or become invalid, and the goal is to maintain solution quality despite such removals. This work is the first to jointly address the challenges of streaming and robust submodular maximization.

The authors develop the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains a set of size O((m log *k* + *k*) log² *k*) after processing the stream and achieves a constant (0.149) approximation guarantee while being robust to the removal of *m* elements. The algorithm combines partitioning and thresholding techniques for submodular optimization, utilizing buckets to store elements. While similar bucket-based approaches have been used in prior work on robust submodular optimization, this paper introduces a novel method for filling these buckets. The algorithm’s performance is demonstrated experimentally in influence maximization and movie recommendation applications, outperforming or matching existing streaming algorithms even when those algorithms have prior knowledge of the elements to be removed.

The problem setting, combining streaming and robustness, is challenging, and achieving a constant factor approximation is a significant result. However, several aspects of the paper could benefit from further development. Providing non-trivial lower bounds would strengthen the theoretical contribution. The presentation of the analysis could also be improved; while the authors highlight its novelty, the core ideas and key steps are not clearly articulated in the proof sketch. Finally, the influence maximization application could be more compelling. The experiments employ a simplified objective function based solely on the size of the neighborhood of seed nodes. It is unclear how this objective relates to traditional influence maximization models where the function’s value depends on the entire network for a given set of seed nodes, and how such models could be adapted to a streaming setting. 

Arguments for acceptance:

*   Addresses a novel and important problem – combining streaming and robust submodular maximization.
*   Provides a strong theoretical guarantee – a constant factor approximation.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results on relevant applications.
*   The algorithm's design, employing a novel partitioning and thresholding scheme, is a valuable contribution.

Arguments against acceptance:

*   Lack of lower bounds.
*   The analysis section could be clearer and more detailed.
*   The influence maximization application is somewhat limited by the simplified objective function.
