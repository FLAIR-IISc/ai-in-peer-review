This paper studies the problem of submodular maximization in a streaming and robust setting, where the goal is to identify the k most valuable elements from a small subset retained during the stream, with the added challenge that up to m items may be removed after the stream. The problem of streaming submodular maximization has been extensively studied due to its relevance to large-scale applications where only a limited number of passes over the data is feasible. Robustness has also gained attention in recent years, driven by scenarios in which certain elements may be removed, and the objective is to obtain solutions that are resilient to such removals. Notably, this paper is the first to address the joint problem of streaming and robust submodular maximization.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements after the stream and achieves a constant (0.149) approximation guarantee while being robust to the removal of m elements. This algorithm leverages a combination of partitioning and thresholding techniques for submodular optimization, utilizing a bucket-based approach. Although similar ideas employing buckets have been used in prior work for robust submodular optimization, the authors' approach differs in how the buckets are populated. Experimental results demonstrate that this algorithm performs well in influence maximization and movie recommendation applications, even when compared to streaming algorithms with prior knowledge of which elements will be removed.

The problem setting, incorporating both streaming and robustness aspects, is particularly challenging, making the achievement of a constant factor approximation a significant result. Nevertheless, there are several aspects of the paper that could be improved. The addition of non-trivial lower bounds to complement the upper bound would have strengthened the paper. Furthermore, the writing of the analysis could be enhanced; the authors mention an "innovative analysis" but fail to elaborate on its innovative aspects, making it difficult to discern the primary ideas and steps of the analysis from the proof sketch. Finally, the influence maximization application could be more convincing. In the experiments, the authors employ a highly simplified objective for influence maximization, solely considering the size of the neighborhood of the seed nodes. It is unclear how traditional influence maximization objectives, where the function value depends on the entire network for any set of seed nodes, could be accommodated within a streaming setting.
