This paper presents a two-stage approach to robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint k. In the first stage, the authors introduce a streaming algorithm, STAR-T, which uses a partitioned data structure with buckets and an exponentially decreasing thresholding schedule to build a compact, deletion-robust summary in a single pass. In the second stage, a simple GREEDY procedure is applied to the retained summary after up to m elements are removed. Theorem 4.1 establishes a constant-factor approximation guarantee that approaches a fixed constant as k grows large. Lemmas 4.2–4.3 show how the partitioning and thresholds, combined with GREEDY in the query stage, mitigate the effect of removed items. Empirically, the method performs well on tasks framed as influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation.

This work is compelling as, to my knowledge, it is the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization. The streaming formulation is broadly applicable, and the paper is insightful in both algorithm design and analysis. STAR-T has two hyperparameters, τ and w. The authors explain how to approximate τ via parallel instances (Theorem 4.5), and they observe that w=1 works effectively in experiments, which makes the method practical without extensive hyperparameter tuning.

My main concern is that the experimental evaluation is not particularly revealing: STAR-T-GREEDY does not consistently outperform SIEVE-STREAMING, and the margins are sometimes small.

Additionally, the influence maximization objective used, f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z| (line 219), differs from the diffusion-based spread models in Kempe et al. (SIGKDD’03). It is therefore unclear how STAR-T would behave under standard influence propagation models, and some clarification or additional experiments would help bridge this gap.
