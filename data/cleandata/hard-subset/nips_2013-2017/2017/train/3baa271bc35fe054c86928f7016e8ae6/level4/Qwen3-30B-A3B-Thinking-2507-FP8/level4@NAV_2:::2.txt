

This paper introduces a two-stage framework for robust submodular maximization under cardinality constraints, addressing the challenge of maintaining solution quality after arbitrary removal of up to m elements from the summary. The authors propose STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that constructs a partitioned summary using exponentially decreasing thresholding, followed by STAR-T-GREEDY for query processing. The theoretical contribution (Theorem 4.1) establishes a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for the robust problem, with a summary size of O((k + m log k) log² k). This work builds on NIPS 2016/2017 contributions like [11] (PRO) and [8] (SIEVE-STREAMING), extending streaming submodular optimization to robust settings while achieving better memory efficiency than concurrent work [18] (O(m log² k) vs. O(mk log k/ε)).

Strengths:  
1. **Originality**: First streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization (distinct from [9,11,18] which lack streaming guarantees). The partitioned thresholding structure and exponential decay rule represent novel algorithmic design.  
2. **Quality**: Theoretical analysis (Lemmas 4.2–4.4) rigorously proves constant-factor approximation for any m, with tight bounds on summary size. The extension to unknown τ (Theorem 4.5) via parallel instances is well-executed.  
3. **Significance**: Solves a practically relevant problem (e.g., network robustness, interactive recommendations) with applications in influence maximization and movie recommendation. The "one summary for multiple removals" capability (Section 5.2) demonstrates strong practical utility.  
4. **Clarity**: Algorithms 1–2 are well-defined; the partition structure (Figure 1) and theoretical claims are clearly articulated. The experimental setup (Section 5) adequately supports the claims.  

Weaknesses:  
1. **Experimental expressiveness**: While STAR-T-GREEDY matches SIEVE-STREAMING in Figures 2(a,c), it underperforms in Figures 2(b,d) (greedy removal of 2k elements). The paper should clarify *why* SIEVE-STREAMING slightly outperforms in these cases, especially since it knows removals in advance.  
2. **Objective function discrepancy**: The influence maximization objective f(Z)=|N(Z)∪Z| (line 219) differs from the standard spread model in Kempe et al. (SIGKDD 2003), which uses f(Z)=|∪_{v∈Z} N(v)|. This inconsistency requires justification, as it affects the interpretation of "influence" in the experiments.  
3. **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: Though w=1 works in practice (Section 5), the theoretical bound requires w≥⌈4m log k / k⌉. The paper should discuss when this approximation is tight versus when w=1 suffices.  

Pro/Con Acceptance:  
✅ **Pro**: First streaming solution for robust submodular maximization with strong theoretical guarantees; practical implementation with minimal hyperparameters; significant applications in large-scale data summarization.  
❌ **Con**: Experimental comparisons lack statistical rigor in key cases (e.g., Figure 2b/d); objective function definition conflicts with established literature, weakening the influence maximization validation.  

The paper makes a valuable contribution to robust streaming optimization. With minor revisions to address the experimental clarity and objective function consistency, it would be a strong fit for NIPS. The theoretical advances and practical framework outweigh the current experimental limitations.
