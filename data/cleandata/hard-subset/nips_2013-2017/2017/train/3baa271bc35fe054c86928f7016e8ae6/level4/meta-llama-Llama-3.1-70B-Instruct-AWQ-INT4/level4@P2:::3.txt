This paper studies the problem of submodular maximization in a streaming and robust setting. The authors consider a scenario where the goal is to find k elements of high value among a small number of elements retained during the stream, where m items might be removed after the stream. This problem setting is particularly relevant to large-scale machine learning applications where only a small number of passes over the data is reasonable, and robustness is essential to account for potential removals of elements.

The paper relates to previous work on submodular maximization, including the greedy algorithm [7] and the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8]. The authors also mention related work on robust submodular maximization, such as [9] and [10]. However, this paper is the first to consider jointly the problem of streaming and robust submodular maximization.

The authors develop the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements after the stream and achieves a constant (0.149) approximation guarantee while being robust to the removal of m elements. The algorithm combines partitioning and thresholding techniques for submodular optimization using buckets, similar to previous work on robust submodular optimization. However, the buckets are filled in a different fashion, which allows for a more efficient streaming algorithm.

The theoretical analysis of the algorithm is sound, and the authors provide a proof sketch of the main result. However, the writing of the analysis could be improved, as it is not entirely clear what is innovative about the analysis and how it differs from previous work. Additionally, it would have been nice to see some non-trivial lower bounds to complement the upper bound.

The experimental evaluation of the algorithm is convincing, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in influence maximization and movie recommendation applications. However, the influence maximization application could be more convincing, as the authors use a very simplified objective that only consists of the size of the neighborhood of the seed nodes. It is not clear how traditional influence maximization objectives, where the value of the function depends on the entire network for any set of seed nodes, could fit in a streaming setting.

Overall, the paper presents a strong result, and the STAR-T algorithm is a valuable contribution to the field of submodular maximization. However, there are some aspects of the paper that could be improved, such as the writing of the analysis and the experimental evaluation.
