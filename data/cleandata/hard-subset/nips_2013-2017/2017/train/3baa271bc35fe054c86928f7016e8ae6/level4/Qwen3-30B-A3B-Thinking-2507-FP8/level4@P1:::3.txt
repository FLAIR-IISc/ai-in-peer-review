

This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, where the goal is to maintain a small summary of elements during a single pass over data that remains robust to the removal of up to m elements after the stream. The authors propose STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that constructs a summary of size O((m log k + k) log² k) and guarantees a constant-factor approximation (0.149) after any m elements are removed. The work is significant as it combines two active research directions—streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]) and robust submodular optimization (e.g., [9, 11])—which have previously been studied separately.

The main contribution is a novel algorithmic approach using partitioned thresholding, where the summary is organized into partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds. This differs from prior robust submodular work [11] that fills buckets greedily, and from streaming methods [8] that use uniform thresholds. The authors provide a careful theoretical analysis showing that a simple greedy algorithm on the remaining elements achieves a constant-factor guarantee. Their experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance in two applications: dominating set (on Twitter and Amazon networks) and personalized movie recommendation (using MovieLens 1M), even against SIEVE-STREAMING which knows the removed elements in advance.

The paper has several notable strengths. First, the problem formulation is well-motivated by real-world scenarios where robustness to element removal is critical (e.g., network failures, user preferences). The theoretical contribution is strong, with a clear analysis showing the constant-factor guarantee under reasonable memory constraints. The experimental results are convincing, particularly in demonstrating that a single robust summary can handle multiple different removals (Section 5.2), which is a practical advantage over re-optimizing after each removal. The writing is generally clear, and the connection to prior work is well-established.

However, the paper could be strengthened in several ways. First, while the authors claim an "innovative analysis," they do not clearly articulate what makes their analysis novel compared to prior robust submodular work [9, 10, 11]. The proof sketch (Section 4) is somewhat opaque, making it difficult to grasp the key insights. A more detailed explanation of how the partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholds enable the robustness guarantee would significantly improve the paper. For instance, the relationship between the partitioning strategy and the ability to handle arbitrary removals of up to m elements is not sufficiently highlighted.

Second, the influence maximization experiments could be more convincing. The authors use a simplified objective f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z| (neighborhood size), whereas traditional influence maximization typically involves more complex diffusion models (e.g., independent cascade). It's unclear how their approach would extend to these more realistic objectives in a streaming setting. A brief discussion of potential extensions or limitations would strengthen the applicability of their method.

Third, the paper lacks non-trivial lower bounds to complement their upper bound. While obtaining tight bounds for this combined problem might be challenging, the absence of any lower bound discussion makes it difficult to assess the optimality of their approximation ratio.

The paper is well-organized and technically sound, with clear algorithm descriptions and solid experimental validation. The writing is mostly clear, though some parts of the theoretical analysis could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The experimental setup is appropriate for their claims, and the results are consistent with the theoretical guarantees.

In terms of the four NIPS criteria:
- **Quality**: The theoretical results are sound, with careful analysis of the approximation guarantee. The experiments validate the claims effectively. The paper is complete and honest about its contributions.
- **Clarity**: The problem statement and algorithm descriptions are clear, but the analysis section would benefit from more intuitive explanations of the novel aspects.
- **Originality**: The joint consideration of streaming and robustness is novel. The partitioned thresholding approach represents a meaningful advancement over prior work in both domains.
- **Significance**: The problem is important for large-scale applications where both streaming constraints and robustness are required. The method provides a practical solution that outperforms alternatives even when they have prior knowledge of removals.

Pros:
- First work to combine streaming and robust submodular maximization
- Theoretical guarantee of 0.149 approximation with reasonable memory usage
- Practical implementation showing robustness to multiple removal scenarios
- Clear experimental validation in two meaningful applications

Cons:
- Insufficient explanation of what makes the analysis "innovative"
- Influence maximization objective is overly simplified
- No lower bounds to complement the upper bound
- Some aspects of the theoretical analysis are hard to follow

Overall, this is a strong contribution that makes a meaningful step toward solving a practically important problem. The paper's main weakness lies in the lack of clarity around the analytical innovations, which would be addressed by a more detailed explanation of the key insights. With these improvements, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, it is a solid theoretical and empirical advance that deserves consideration for acceptance.
