- Problem setup: two-stage streaming + robust submodular maximization — build compact summary S in one pass, then after up to m removals, find a size-k solution from S without re-accessing data.  
- Algorithms: STAR-T for streaming summary construction; STAR-T-GREEDY is standard greedy run on the summary after removals.  
- STAR-T novelty: partitioned buckets combined with an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule; elements placed in buckets by marginal gain.  
- Theoretical guarantee: constant-factor approximation (Theorem 4.1) proven via two-case analysis (sufficient full buckets vs. not).  
- Proof ideas: analysis relies on bucket fullness structure and thresholding; builds on SIEVE-STREAMING and partitioning ideas from prior work.  
- Practical issue addressed: unknown f(OPT) handled by a standard parallelization scheme (Theorem 4.5).  
- Memory complexity: favorable — logarithmic dependence on k, compares well to concurrent methods.  
- Empirical setup concern: experiment uses f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z| (dominating set) which is submodular but is not the standard stochastic influence spread; calling it “influence maximization” is misleading.  
- Suggested fix: clarify the distinction or evaluate on standard spread models (Independent Cascade / Linear Threshold).  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, clearly organized; algorithm and partition structure are well illustrated (Figure 1); proofs give good intuition.  
- Originality: highly original — addresses a novel intersection of submodular maximization, streaming, and robustness; among the first to give provable guarantees in this setting.  
- Significance: high — relevant for scalable, adversarial, or dynamic deployment scenarios where compact robust summaries and fast queries are needed.  
- Empirical performance: strong — STAR-T-GREEDY often matches or slightly outperforms a clairvoyant SIEVE-STREAMING baseline that knows the removals in advance.  
- Presentation suggestion: emphasize the significance of matching a clairvoyant baseline, as this is a major empirical success.  
- Arguments for acceptance: formalizes a novel problem, proposes an elegant algorithm with solid theory, has good memory bounds, and strong empirical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: experimental framing ambiguity and underemphasis of empirical strengths.  
- Overall recommendation: strengths substantially outweigh minor weaknesses — recommend acceptance.
