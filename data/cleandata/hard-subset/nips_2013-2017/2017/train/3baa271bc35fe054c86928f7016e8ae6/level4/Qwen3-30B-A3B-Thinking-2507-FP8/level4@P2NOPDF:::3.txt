

This paper addresses the challenging problem of submodular maximization in a streaming setting with robustness guarantees. The authors consider a scenario where a stream of elements must be processed with limited memory, followed by the removal of up to m elements, requiring the solution to remain effective despite these removals. While streaming submodular maximization is well-studied for large-scale data, and robust submodular maximization has gained recent attention for handling element removals, this work presents the first unified approach for both aspects. The problem is significant due to real-world applications where data arrives sequentially and some elements may become unavailable post-processing.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains O((m log k + k) log² k) elements during the stream and achieves a 0.149-approximation guarantee while tolerating the removal of any m elements. STAR-T integrates partitioning and thresholding techniques using buckets, adapting ideas from prior robust submodular optimization but with a novel bucket-filling strategy. Experiments demonstrate competitive performance in influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, even against streaming algorithms that know the removed elements in advance.

The theoretical contribution is strong, as achieving a constant-factor approximation for this combined setting is non-trivial. However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the absence of non-trivial lower bounds for the problem is notable. While the upper bound is promising, the lack of matching lower bounds (e.g., showing that 0.149 cannot be improved) weakens the theoretical narrative. The authors should either provide such bounds or clarify why they are infeasible.

Second, the analysis lacks sufficient clarity. The paper claims an "innovative analysis" but fails to articulate what makes it innovative or to distill the core insights from the proof sketch. Key steps and high-level intuition are buried in technical details, making it difficult for readers to grasp the algorithm’s guarantees. A more structured exposition—highlighting the novel technical lemmas or how the bucket strategy avoids prior limitations—would significantly strengthen the contribution.

Third, the experimental validation for influence maximization is insufficient. The authors use a simplified objective that only considers the size of the neighborhood around seed nodes, ignoring the standard influence maximization problem where the objective depends on the entire network structure (e.g., expected influence spread via diffusion models). This simplification is problematic because: (1) it does not reflect real-world influence maximization, which requires global network knowledge; and (2) it is unclear how the proposed streaming approach could handle the full objective, given that standard influence functions are not streamable. The experiments should either use a more realistic objective or explicitly justify why the simplified version is meaningful in the streaming context.

Additional minor concerns include: (1) The paper does not clarify whether the m removals are adversarial or stochastic, though the robustness guarantee suggests adversarial removals; (2) The space complexity notation could be more precise (e.g., explicitly state the log factors); (3) The connection between the theoretical guarantees and the experimental results is not sufficiently explained—how do the 0.149 approximation and space bounds manifest in practice?

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem and delivers a technically sound algorithm with a solid theoretical guarantee. The experimental results are promising but require stronger justification for the influence maximization application. Addressing the gaps in lower bounds, analysis exposition, and experimental relevance would elevate this work to a compelling contribution. As it stands, the technical merits are clear, but the presentation and validation need refinement to fully convince the community. The authors should prioritize clarifying the analysis and strengthening the experimental setup to align with standard influence maximization practices.
