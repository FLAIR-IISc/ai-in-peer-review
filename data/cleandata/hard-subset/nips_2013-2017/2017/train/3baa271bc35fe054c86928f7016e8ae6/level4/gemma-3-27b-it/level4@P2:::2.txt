This paper introduces a two-stage procedure, STAR-T, for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint. The first stage employs a novel streaming algorithm that partitions the incoming data and utilizes a decreasing thresholding rule to construct a robust summary. The second stage leverages a simple greedy algorithm applied to this summary. The authors prove a constant-factor approximation guarantee for STAR-T as the cardinality constraint *k* approaches infinity (Theorem 4.1), and provide supporting lemmas (4.2-4.3) demonstrating the algorithm's resilience to element removal. 

The work is significant as it presents the first streaming algorithm specifically designed for robust submodular maximization, a problem with increasing relevance in applications demanding resilience to data loss or changing conditions. The theoretical analysis appears thorough, and the provision of a method for approximating the threshold parameter *τ* (Theorem 4.5) and demonstrating good performance with *w*=1 simplifies practical application.

The paper effectively positions its contribution within the existing literature on submodular optimization, streaming algorithms, and robust optimization. It correctly identifies the limitations of traditional greedy methods in large-scale and dynamic settings, and highlights the need for robust solutions where data may be lost or become unreliable. The connections drawn to related work, including references to [8, 9, 10, 11], demonstrate a good understanding of the field.

However, the experimental evaluation, while demonstrating competitive performance, could be strengthened. The results do not consistently show STAR-T-Greedy outperforming SIEVE-STREAMING, and a more detailed analysis of the scenarios where each algorithm excels would be valuable.  A clearer explanation of *why* STAR-T performs well in these specific applications is warranted.

Furthermore, there is a discrepancy in the definition of the objective function for the dominating set problem (line 219) compared to the cited work by Kempe et al. [3]. The presented objective,  f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|, differs from the standard influence maximization objective. The paper should clarify whether this is a deliberate modification and, if so, justify the change and discuss its implications for the applicability of STAR-T to standard influence maximization problems. Is the performance observed representative of the typical influence maximization task?

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** First streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** Proven constant-factor approximation guarantee.
*   **Practicality:**  Simplified parameter tuning (w=1 works well).
*   **Relevance:** Addresses an important problem with growing applications.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Experimental Superiority:** STAR-T-Greedy does not consistently outperform SIEVE-STREAMING.
*   **Objective Function Discrepancy:** Inconsistency in the dominating set objective function compared to standard literature.
*   **Experimental Detail:** Lack of deeper analysis of experimental results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The theoretical results are strong and provide a solid foundation for the proposed algorithm.
*   The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance, even if not consistently superior to existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental results are not compelling enough to definitively demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.
*   The discrepancy in the objective function definition raises concerns about the applicability of the algorithm to standard problem settings.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns raised regarding the experimental evaluation and the objective function definition. A more detailed analysis of the experimental results, along with a clarification of the objective function, would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
