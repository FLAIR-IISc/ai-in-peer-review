This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, a challenging combination of constraints with significant practical relevance. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm employing a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule for the streaming phase, followed by a simple greedy algorithm in the query stage. They provide a theoretical guarantee of constant-factor approximation as *k* approaches infinity (Theorem 4.1) and demonstrate empirical performance on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks. 

The work is a valuable contribution as it represents, to the best of my knowledge, the first streaming algorithm designed specifically for robust submodular maximization. The potential applications of such an algorithm are broad, particularly in scenarios where data arrives continuously and robustness to element removal is critical. The practical consideration of approximating the threshold parameter *τ* (Section 4.1) and demonstrating effective performance even with *w*=1 simplifies the algorithm’s usability.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of streaming, robustness, and submodular maximization is a novel problem setting, and the proposed approach is a significant first step toward addressing it.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee, even with the stated limitations, provides a strong foundation for the algorithm’s performance. The analysis appears rigorous and well-motivated.
*   **Practical Considerations:** Addressing the need to estimate *τ* and showing good performance with *w*=1 enhances the algorithm’s practicality.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experimental results are encouraging, the comparisons to SIEVE-STREAMING are not consistently favorable. The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of the conditions under which STAR-T outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, and vice-versa. The justification for choosing the specific removal strategies (random vs. greedy) should also be elaborated upon.
*   **Objective Function Discrepancy:** There’s a discrepancy between the objective function used for the dominating set problem in the experiments (f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z|) and the standard influence maximization objective as cited in the related work (Kempe et al., 2003). The authors should clarify this difference and explain how STAR-T’s performance translates to the more conventional influence maximization model. Is this a valid proxy, or does it change the problem substantially?
*   **Limited Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The effect of varying *w* (beyond demonstrating it works well at *w*=1) isn't explored. A more comprehensive sensitivity analysis could reveal trade-offs and provide guidance for practitioners.
*   **Space Complexity:** The space complexity of O((k + m log k) log k) is stated, but a more intuitive understanding of how this scales with large *m* and *k* would be helpful. A concrete example with realistic values would aid comprehension.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a novel and important problem with a sound theoretical foundation.
*   The proposed algorithm is a significant contribution to the field and opens avenues for future research.
*   The experimental results, while not conclusive, demonstrate promising performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and convincing.
*   The discrepancy in the objective function for the dominating set problem raises concerns about the applicability of the results to standard influence maximization.
*   The lack of detailed parameter sensitivity analysis limits the practical guidance provided.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened and some clarifications are needed regarding the objective function, the novelty of the problem setting, the theoretical guarantees, and the practical considerations addressed make this a worthwhile paper for NIPS. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed comparison of STAR-T and SIEVE-STREAMING, analyzing the conditions under which each algorithm performs best.
2.  Clarify the objective function used for the dominating set problem and explain its relevance to the standard influence maximization model.
3.  Consider conducting a sensitivity analysis of the *w* parameter.
4.  Provide a concrete example illustrating the space complexity for realistic values of *k* and *m*.
