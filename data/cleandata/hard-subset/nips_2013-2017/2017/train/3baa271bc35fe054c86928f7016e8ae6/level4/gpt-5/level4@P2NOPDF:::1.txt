Problem studied: The paper addresses robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. Given a ground set E arriving as a stream and a nonnegative submodular function f, the goal is to maintain a set T such that, after the adversarial deletion of any m elements from T, one can still extract a subset S of size k that (approximately) maximizes f(S). In other words, the algorithm must produce a compact summary from which a high-quality k-element solution remains available even after up to m deletions.

Applications: Maximizing submodular functions under a cardinality constraint has many applications, and there is a substantial literature on streaming algorithms for this task. A robust variant is useful when selected items may later become unavailable or must be removed, for example in recommendation settings where certain items are later deemed unsuitable (e.g., a user indicates disinterest in some recommended movies). The paper positions its contribution as enabling such post hoc deletions without having to rescan the stream or rerun the algorithm from scratch.

Results: The paper presents a streaming algorithm that achieves a constant-factor approximation for the robust objective. It returns a set T of size (m log k + k) log^2 k, from which a size-k subset with guaranteed approximation quality can be extracted even after any m elements are deleted. To the best of my understanding, the analysis establishes this guarantee under standard submodularity assumptions, and the bounds scale reasonably with m and k.

Comments: The results appear correct, and the algorithm along with its analysis is nontrivial and technically involved. The construction of the summary set and the handling of deletions are thoughtfully designed, and the theoretical guarantees are clearly stated. However, my main concern is the fit for the conference. The paper’s motivation and application scenarios are only lightly developed, and the problem formulation feels somewhat narrow relative to the broader machine learning audience. Strengthening the empirical motivation, clarifying practical use cases, or demonstrating integration with real-world pipelines would improve the paper’s relevance. As it stands, while the theoretical contribution is solid, its practical impact and appeal to the conference community are not fully convincing.
