This paper studies the problem of submodular maximization in a streaming and robust setting, where the goal is to select k elements of high value from a small subset of elements retained during the stream, with the possibility of m items being removed after the stream. The problem of streaming submodular maximization has been extensively studied due to its applications in large-scale settings, where only a small number of passes over the data is feasible. Robustness has also gained attention in recent years, motivated by scenarios where some elements may be removed, and the objective is to obtain solutions that are resilient to such removals. This paper is the first to consider the joint problem of streaming and robust submodular maximization.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains O((m log k + k )log^2 k) elements after the stream and achieves a constant (0.149) approximation guarantee while being robust to the removal of m elements. The algorithm combines partitioning and thresholding techniques for submodular optimization using buckets. Although similar ideas employing buckets were used in previous work on robust submodular optimization, the buckets are filled in a distinct manner in this paper. Experimental results demonstrate that the algorithm performs well in influence maximization and movie recommendation applications, even when compared to streaming algorithms that have prior knowledge of which elements will be removed.

Considering both the streaming and robustness aspects, the problem setting is challenging, and obtaining a constant factor approximation is a significant achievement. However, there are several aspects of the paper that could be improved. Providing non-trivial lower bounds to complement the upper bound would have strengthened the paper. The writing of the analysis could also be improved, as the authors mention an "innovative analysis" without elaborating on its innovative aspects, making it difficult to extract the main ideas and steps of the analysis from the proof sketch. Furthermore, the influence maximization application could be more convincing. The experiments use a simplified objective for influence maximization, which only considers the size of the neighborhood of the seed nodes. It is unclear how traditional influence maximization objectives, where the value of the function depends on the entire network for any set of seed nodes, could be adapted to a streaming setting.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and challenging problem, and the proposed algorithm achieves a notable result. With some improvements to the analysis and experimental evaluation, the paper could be even more impactful.
