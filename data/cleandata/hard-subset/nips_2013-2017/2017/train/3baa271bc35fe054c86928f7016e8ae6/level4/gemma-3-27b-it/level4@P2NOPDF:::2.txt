This paper introduces a two-stage approach to robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint *k*. The first stage employs a novel streaming algorithm, STAR-T, built upon a partitioning structure and a decreasing thresholding strategy. The second stage utilizes a simple greedy method to refine the solution. A key theoretical result (Theorem 4.1) demonstrates that STAR-T achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee even as *k* approaches infinity. Supporting lemmas (Lemmas 4.2 and 4.3) establish the algorithm's resilience to element loss when combined with the greedy step.

The authors validate their approach through experiments on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation, reporting promising results. This work is significant as it presents the first streaming algorithm specifically designed for robust submodular maximization—a potentially impactful contribution, given the broad applicability of streaming settings. The insights provided by this work are highly valuable. 

STAR-T incorporates two hyperparameters, τ and *w*. The paper offers guidance on approximating τ (Theorem 4.5) and demonstrates through experimentation that setting *w* = 1 yields satisfactory performance. This simplifies the practical application of STAR-T by reducing the need for extensive hyperparameter tuning.

However, the experimental evaluation raises concerns. While the proposed STAR-T-Greedy algorithm performs well in certain instances, it does not consistently outperform the Sieve-St algorithm. This lack of consistent superiority weakens the empirical support for the method. 

Furthermore, the definition of the objective function for Influence Maximization (f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z| on line 219) deviates from the standard formulation presented in the cited reference (Kempe, Kleinberg, and Tardos, SIGKDD 2003). This discrepancy is unclear and casts doubt on the direct applicability and interpretation of STAR-T’s performance within the well-established spread model commonly used in influence maximization. A clarification of how STAR-T’s objective function relates to, or differs from, the traditional model is crucial for understanding its effectiveness in this context.
