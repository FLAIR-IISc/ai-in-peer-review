The paper "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach" studies the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint k, with the additional twist of elements arriving in a streaming fashion and m items being removed after the stream is finished. The authors propose a two-stage procedure, consisting of a streaming algorithm STAR-T and a query algorithm STAR-T-GREEDY, which achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee.

The problem of maximizing submodular functions has multiple applications, including data summarization, influence maximization, and facility location. The authors provide a clear motivation for studying the robust version of this problem, where the solution needs to be resilient to the removal of up to m elements. They demonstrate the relevance of this problem in various applications, such as image and document summarization, personalized movie recommendation, and dominating set problem.

The paper's results are correct to the best of my understanding. The solution is non-trivial, and the algorithm and its analysis are technically challenging. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including a novel partitioning structure and an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. They also demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on two different data summarization tasks.

However, I have some concerns regarding the relevance of this paper to the NIPS conference. While the problem is interesting, the applications and their motivation could be stronger. The paper could benefit from more discussion on the implications of the robust submodular maximization problem in real-world applications and its connection to other areas of machine learning.
