This paper tackles the challenging problem of submodular maximization in a streaming and robust setting. This setting considers selecting a set of *k* elements with high submodular value from a stream of data, while simultaneously guaranteeing performance even after *m* elements are removed post-stream. Streaming submodular maximization is well-motivated by large-scale applications where data access is limited to a small number of passes, and recent work has highlighted the importance of robustness to element removal. This paper represents a significant contribution as the first to jointly address both streaming and robustness constraints.

The authors introduce the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains O((m log k + k)log<sup>2</sup> k) elements after processing the stream and achieves a constant-factor (0.149) approximation guarantee, robust to the removal of *m* elements. The algorithm leverages a combination of partitioning and thresholding techniques, employing buckets to manage the candidate elements. While bucket-based approaches have appeared in prior work on robust submodular optimization, this paper innovatively modifies the bucket filling strategy to suit the streaming context.  Experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, even exceeding the performance of streaming algorithms with prior knowledge of element removals.

The problem setting is undeniably challenging, and achieving a constant-factor approximation is a substantial accomplishment. However, several aspects of the paper could benefit from further development. The absence of lower bounds weakens the understanding of the achievable approximation ratios. Establishing even non-trivial lower bounds would strengthen the theoretical contribution. 

Furthermore, the presentation of the analysis could be improved. The authors claim an “innovative analysis,” but this claim is not adequately substantiated. The proof sketch is dense, and the core ideas and critical steps are not clearly articulated, making it difficult to follow the reasoning. A more detailed explanation and a clearer highlighting of the novel analytical techniques would significantly improve readability and impact.

Finally, the application to influence maximization feels somewhat limited. The experiments employ a simplified objective function that focuses solely on the size of the neighborhood of selected seed nodes. This simplification does not fully capture the complexities of traditional influence maximization objectives, where the value is dependent on the entire network’s response to a seed set.  It is unclear how these more conventional, network-dependent influence maximization objectives could be effectively adapted to a streaming setting, and the paper does not adequately address this point.  A more thorough discussion of the limitations of this simplified objective and potential directions for incorporating more realistic network effects would be valuable.

Despite these criticisms, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The combination of streaming and robustness constraints is a natural and important direction, and the STAR-T algorithm provides a solid initial solution. Addressing the concerns regarding lower bounds, analytical clarity, and the influence maximization application would further elevate the impact of this work.
