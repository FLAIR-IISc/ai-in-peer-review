### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a challenging setting that is simultaneously streaming and robust. The problem is framed in a two-stage process: first, a single pass is made over a data stream to create a compact and robust summary `S`. Second, after an adversary removes an arbitrary set of `m` elements from `S`, a final solution of size `k` is extracted from the remaining summary elements. This formulation is well-motivated by large-scale applications where data cannot be stored entirely and where the selected summary might be subject to failures or adversarial attacks.

The authors propose a novel two-stage procedure. The first stage employs the STAR-T algorithm, a single-pass streaming method that constructs the summary `S` using a clever partitioning structure. This structure consists of partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds and buckets of exponentially increasing size. The second stage uses a standard greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) on the remaining summary `S \ E` to find the final solution. The main theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee of approximately 0.149 for this procedure, while maintaining a summary of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`.

The paper situates its contributions effectively within the literature, drawing inspiration from the thresholding ideas of SIEVE-STREAMING for the streaming aspect and the partitioning/bucketing structures of recent non-streaming robust methods. It correctly identifies its novelty in being one of the first works to jointly tackle the streaming and robust settings, acknowledging concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017). The effectiveness of the proposed method is demonstrated empirically on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, where it performs comparably to or better than strong baselines that are given the advantage of knowing the removed elements in advance.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming model is both novel and highly significant. It addresses a practical and difficult challenge at the intersection of two important areas in machine learning. The proposed two-stage framework is a natural and well-motivated formulation for this problem. A positive result in this area is a valuable contribution to the field.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a strong theoretical result, proving a constant-factor approximation guarantee in this challenging combined setting. The STAR-T algorithm is an elegant and non-trivial combination of partitioning and thresholding techniques. The analysis, while dense, appears to be technically sound.

*   **Experimental Quality:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed to highlight the algorithm's main strength: robustness. Comparing STAR-T-GREEDY against SIEVE-STREAMING, where the latter is given oracle access to the set of removed elements, is a particularly strong and convincing evaluation. The results demonstrate that the proposed method is not only theoretically sound but also practically effective, often matching or outperforming this powerful baseline. The interactive movie recommendation scenario provides a compelling use case.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement)**

*   **Clarity of the Analysis:** While the algorithmic idea is presented clearly, the proof sketch for the main theorem (Theorem 4.1) is difficult to follow. The authors mention an "innovative analysis" but do not provide sufficient intuition in the main text to help the reader understand the core ideas. The interplay between the two cases (many full buckets vs. few full buckets) and how they are balanced to derive the final constant is not clearly explained. A more intuitive walkthrough of the proof strategy would significantly strengthen the paper's clarity and impact.

*   **Theoretical Completeness:** The paper establishes a constant-factor upper bound on the approximation ratio. However, it provides no discussion on corresponding lower bounds. While proving tight lower bounds can be very difficult, some discussion on what might be achievable or why the current constant is non-trivial in a broader context would help situate the theoretical contribution more completely.

*   **Scope of the Influence Maximization Experiment:** The dominating set objective used in the experiments is a simplified proxy for influence maximization. Standard influence models (e.g., Independent Cascade, Linear Threshold) require knowledge of the full graph structure to evaluate the objective function, which makes them difficult to adapt to a streaming setting where edges or nodes arrive sequentially. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and justify why the neighborhood-based objective is a reasonable choice for their evaluation, or discuss the challenges of applying their method to more traditional influence maximization objectives.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a strong contribution by tackling a novel, important, and challenging problem. The proposed algorithm is elegant, the theoretical guarantee is significant, and the experimental results are convincing. The primary weaknesses relate to the clarity of the theoretical analysis and minor limitations in the experimental scope, both of which are addressable. The strengths of the paper, particularly the novelty of the problem and the quality of the main result, significantly outweigh these weaknesses. This work is a solid step forward for large-scale submodular optimization and is likely to inspire follow-up research. I recommend acceptance.
