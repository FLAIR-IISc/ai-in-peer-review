This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure. The first stage introduces STAR-T, a novel streaming algorithm that utilizes a partitioning structure and a decreasing threshold rule to select a candidate set of elements. The second stage applies a simple greedy algorithm to this candidate set. The authors provide theoretical guarantees, proving that STAR-T has a constant approximation factor (Theorem 4.1) and that the full two-stage method is robust to the loss of elements from the candidate set (Lemmas 4.2-4.3). The proposed method is evaluated empirically on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Impact:** The primary contribution of this work is the development of what appears to be the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization. This is a significant and timely contribution, as streaming data models are increasingly relevant for large-scale applications. The proposed approach is theoretically sound and could inspire further research in this area.

2.  **Practicality of the Algorithm:** A notable strength is the practicality of the STAR-T algorithm. While it has two hyperparameters, τ and w, the authors provide a method for approximating τ (Theorem 4.5) and demonstrate empirically that a simple setting of w=1 performs well. This convenience significantly lowers the barrier to adoption and makes the algorithm more appealing for practical use without extensive hyperparameter tuning.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

My main concerns lie with the empirical evaluation and the formulation of one of the key experiments.

1.  **Ambiguous Experimental Performance:** The experimental results show that the proposed STAR-T-Greedy algorithm does not consistently outperform the Sieve-St baseline. While the proposed method is novel and designed for a different setting (robust streaming), its superiority is not clearly established in the presented experiments. A more thorough comparison or an analysis of the scenarios where STAR-T-Greedy excels would strengthen the paper's claims.

2.  **Unconventional Objective for Influence Maximization:** A significant point of confusion arises from the influence maximization experiment. The objective function is defined as f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z| (line 219), which represents the total number of nodes in the seed set and their immediate neighbors. This differs substantially from the standard stochastic influence spread models, such as Independent Cascade or Linear Threshold, that were introduced in the cited work by Kempe et al. (2003) and are commonly used in the literature. Could the authors clarify the motivation for choosing this simpler, non-stochastic objective? It is not clear how the performance on this function translates to the more common and complex influence spread problem, which is arguably the primary motivation for this application. This choice of objective function may limit the generalizability of the conclusions drawn from this experiment.

In summary, the paper introduces a novel and theoretically grounded algorithm for an important problem. However, its practical effectiveness is not fully convincing due to mixed experimental results and a questionable choice of objective function in a key application. Addressing these experimental concerns would significantly improve the paper.
