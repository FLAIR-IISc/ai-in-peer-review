This paper proposes a two-stage procedure for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint `k`. The problem is set in a streaming context where, after a single pass over the data, an adversary can remove up to `m` elements from the algorithm's selected summary.

In the first stage, the proposed streaming algorithm, STAR-T, processes the data stream to create a robust summary. The algorithm's design is based on a novel partitioning structure and an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. In the second stage, after an arbitrary set of `m` elements is removed from this summary, a simple greedy algorithm is run on the remaining elements to produce the final solution. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The experimental results on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation tasks demonstrate the algorithm's practical performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming context. To my knowledge, this is one of the first works to formally tackle this combined setting, making it a novel and timely contribution. The problem is well-motivated, and a solution would have a wide range of applications in large-scale machine learning.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** A key strength of this work is its theoretical contribution. The authors provide a formal proof (Theorem 4.1) that their two-stage procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The analysis, which relies on partitioning the solution space and using decreasing thresholds, is non-trivial and technically sound.

3.  **Practicality:** On a practical note, the authors thoughtfully address the challenge of setting the algorithm's hyperparameters, `τ` and `w`. Theorem 4.5 provides a method for approximating the threshold `τ` when the optimal value is unknown. Furthermore, the experiments show that a simple setting of `w=1` works well in practice. This attention to implementation details enhances the algorithm's usability and makes it more accessible for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Experimental Results:** My primary concern regards the experimental evaluation. While the proposed STAR-T-GREEDY algorithm performs well, it does not consistently outperform the SIEVE-STREAMING baseline, even when the baseline is given the significant advantage of knowing the removed elements in advance. For instance, in Figures 2(b,d), SIEVE-STREAMING shows slightly better performance for some values of `k`. While matching the performance of such a powerful baseline is commendable, the results are not as compelling as they could be and slightly temper the paper's claims of superior practical performance.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Setup:** There is a lack of clarity regarding the objective function used in the dominating set experiment (Section 5.1). The paper frames this task as an instance of influence maximization and cites the seminal work of Kempe et al. (2003). However, the objective function used, `f(Z) = |N(Z) U Z|`, corresponds to the standard dominating set problem. This is different from the influence spread models (e.g., Independent Cascade or Linear Threshold) that are standard in the influence maximization literature. It is therefore unclear how STAR-T would perform on these more common influence models. It would strengthen the paper if the authors could clarify the reason for this choice or discuss the expected performance of their algorithm on traditional influence spread models.
