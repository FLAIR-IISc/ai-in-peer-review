## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

This paper investigates a robust version of the classic submodular maximization problem in a streaming setting. The authors aim to identify a set *T* of elements from a ground set *E* such that, even after the removal of any *m* elements from *T*, a subset *S* of *T* with *k* elements can still be found to approximately maximize a given submodular function *f*.

The motivation stems from the desire for solutions that remain effective despite potential disruptions or changes in the input. The authors highlight a scenario where an initially selected set (e.g., recommended movies for a user) may require adjustment due to user disinterest, necessitating a robust solution that can quickly adapt to element removals. While the potential applications of submodular maximization are well-established, and streaming algorithms for the standard problem are known, the justification for focusing on this *robust* variant feels somewhat limited. The presented example is plausible, but the paper lacks a compelling discussion of other significant application areas where this robustness is critical. 

The core technical contribution of the paper lies in providing a constant-factor approximation algorithm for this robust submodular maximization problem. The algorithm returns a set *T* of size *O(m log k + k) log² k*. The analysis appears technically sound, and the algorithm itself represents a non-trivial extension to existing techniques for submodular maximization. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the underlying theory and employ sophisticated analytical tools in their proof. 

However, my primary concern relates to the overall relevance of this work to NIPS. While technically correct and well-executed, the problem definition feels specialized and lacks broad applicability. The problem isn’t presented as a generalization of other well-known problems, nor does it immediately suggest clear connections to other active research areas within the conference scope. The assumption that *m* elements might be removed *after* a solution is found feels less fundamental than, for example, dealing with noisy or adversarial inputs *during* the streaming process. 

Specifically, the size of the solution *T* is dependent on *m*, the number of potential removals. This dependence suggests that the algorithm’s utility diminishes as *m* grows larger, which seems counterintuitive for a ‘robust’ solution. A discussion on the impact of *m* on the practical usefulness of the algorithm, and potential scenarios where this scaling is acceptable, would strengthen the presentation.

**Strengths:**

*   Technically sound and correct.
*   Non-trivial algorithm and challenging analysis.
*   Addresses a natural extension to the submodular maximization problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited motivation and weak application scenarios.
*   Questionable relevance to the core NIPS audience.
*   Dependence on *m* in the solution size impacts practical utility and requires further justification.
*   Problem lacks generality; it doesn’t clearly connect to broader research themes.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically competent solution to a well-defined, albeit specialized, problem. However, the limited motivation, relatively narrow applicability, and the dependence on a parameter representing *post*-solution removals raise concerns regarding its fit within the NIPS conference. While the technical quality is high, the novelty and impact appear insufficient to warrant a strong recommendation for acceptance at this time. I would lean towards **reject** with the suggestion that the authors consider expanding the scope of the work, exploring broader applications, or clarifying the specific scenarios where this robust approach offers a significant advantage over existing methods.
