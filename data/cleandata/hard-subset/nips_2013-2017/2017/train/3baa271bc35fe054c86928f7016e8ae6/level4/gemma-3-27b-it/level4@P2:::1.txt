This paper addresses the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, aiming to identify a representative subset of elements from a data stream while ensuring resilience to the removal of a fixed number of elements. Specifically, the authors seek a set *T* such that, after removing any *m* elements, a greedy algorithm can still find a near-optimal solution of size *k* with respect to a monotone submodular function *f*. This is motivated by scenarios where extracted summaries need to remain useful even after some components are lost – for example, in personalized recommendations where user preferences change.

The authors present an algorithm, STAR-T, with a theoretical guarantee of a constant-factor approximation. The algorithm maintains a set of size O((m log *k* + *k*) log<sup>2</sup> *k*) and leverages a novel partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds to achieve robustness. 

This work builds upon a strong foundation of previous research in submodular maximization, streaming algorithms (e.g., [8]), and robust optimization [9, 10, 11]. It effectively extends the ideas of SIEVE-STREAMING [8] to the robust setting, introducing a new thresholding scheme and accompanying analysis. The paper also relates to recent work on robust influence maximization and interactive submodular optimization [3, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18].

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The algorithm and its analysis appear technically sound and non-trivial. The proofs, while not detailed within the review length constraints, are referenced to supplementary material.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of streaming, robustness, and submodular maximization is a novel contribution. The partitioned thresholding approach is a creative solution to the challenge of maintaining a robust summary.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The constant-factor approximation guarantee provides a strong theoretical foundation for the algorithm's performance.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation demonstrate the practical effectiveness of STAR-T, showing competitive performance against existing methods, even when those methods have access to information (the removed elements) that STAR-T does not.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Application Motivation:** While the problem is well-defined, the motivating applications feel somewhat limited in their impact. The examples (dominating set, movie recommendation) are illustrative but could be strengthened with more compelling real-world scenarios. The connection to the “right to be forgotten” [18] could be further explored.
*   **Generality:** The problem is somewhat specific. It focuses on a fixed *m* removals. While reasonable, it doesn't address scenarios where the number of potential removals is unbounded or adaptive.
*   **Space Complexity:** While theoretically sound, the O((m log *k* + *k*) log<sup>2</sup> *k*) space complexity could be a limitation for extremely large datasets or very large values of *m* and *k*.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel solution to a challenging problem.
*   The theoretical guarantees are strong and supported by experimental results.
*   The work advances the state-of-the-art in robust submodular optimization and has the potential to be valuable to researchers working in this area.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The application motivation is not fully convincing, and the problem’s generality is limited. 
*   The paper’s relevance to NIPS is marginal; it’s a strong technical result, but its impact within the broader machine learning community may be limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid technical paper with a well-defined problem, a novel solution, and strong theoretical guarantees. However, the limited application motivation and specificity of the problem raise concerns about its overall significance for NIPS. I would lean towards **weak accept**, contingent on the authors strengthening the discussion of potential applications and acknowledging the limitations of the problem’s generality. A more compelling demonstration of broader impact would significantly increase my confidence in a positive recommendation.
