

This paper presents a novel two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization under cardinality constraints in streaming settings. The authors propose STAR-T (Streaming Robust submodular algorithm with Partitioned Thresholding) for the streaming phase, which constructs a robust summary of size O((k + m log k) log k) using a partitioned thresholding structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds. The query stage then employs a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) on the remaining elements after any m elements are removed from the summary. The theoretical contribution establishes a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) as k → ∞, which represents a significant advancement over prior non-streaming robust approaches like PRO [11] and the streaming SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8].

The work makes a clear contribution to the literature by addressing a practically important gap: the intersection of streaming submodular optimization and robustness requirements. Previous work on robust submodular maximization (e.g., [9, 10, 11]) focused on centralized settings, while streaming approaches (e.g., [8, 12, 13]) ignored robustness. The authors effectively bridge this gap, demonstrating that their partitioned thresholding approach can handle arbitrary removals of up to m elements while maintaining a constant-factor guarantee. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with careful handling of the exponential thresholding rule and partition structure, though the constant factor (0.149) could potentially be improved.

The paper's clarity is generally good, with precise algorithm descriptions and clear theoretical results. The partitioning structure (Figure 1) effectively visualizes the bucket system, though the thresholding mechanism could be explained more intuitively. The experimental section demonstrates the approach on two meaningful applications: dominating set problems (using Twitter and Amazon networks) and personalized movie recommendations (using MovieLens data). The results show that STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows the removed elements in advance) while requiring no prior knowledge of removals.

The paper's strengths are substantial. It is the first to address robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, which has significant practical implications for large-scale applications where data is too large to store and robustness against element removal is critical. The theoretical guarantee is non-trivial, and the practical validation on real-world datasets demonstrates clear relevance. The implementation details are well-handled, with the authors showing how to approximate the unknown parameter τ (via parallel instances) and demonstrating that w=1 works well in practice.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in its expressiveness. While STAR-T-GREEDY matches SIEVE-STREAMING in most cases, it does not consistently outperform it (as claimed in the abstract), and the paper fails to provide a comprehensive comparison across different values of m, k, and removal patterns. The comparison with SIEVE-STREAMING should be more rigorous, including statistical significance testing across multiple datasets and parameter settings.

Another concern is the objective function used in the influence maximization application. The paper uses f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z| (line 219), which differs from the standard influence spread function in Kempe et al. [8] (which is based on expected number of influenced nodes). This discrepancy makes it difficult to assess how the algorithm performs on the standard influence maximization problem. The authors should clarify this choice and provide a comparison with the standard influence spread metric.

The paper also lacks sufficient discussion of the practical memory requirements. While the theoretical bound is O((k + m log k) log k), the numerical results (Section 5) suggest that w=1 works well in practice, which contradicts the theoretical requirement of w ≥ ⌈4dlog kem/k⌉. This discrepancy needs explanation.

Pro and Con Acceptance:
- Pro: First work addressing robust submodular maximization in streaming settings; novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding; theoretically sound with constant-factor guarantee; practical applications validated on real datasets.
- Con: Experimental evaluation lacks depth and expressiveness; objective function in influence maximization differs from standard definition; theoretical memory requirement contradicts practical findings; constant factor (0.149) could be improved.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization, addressing an important gap between streaming algorithms and robustness requirements. The theoretical framework is sound, and the practical applications demonstrate clear relevance. The main weaknesses are in the experimental evaluation and the presentation of the influence maximization case, which would benefit from more thorough analysis and comparison. With minor revisions to strengthen the experimental section and clarify the influence maximization objective, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel solution that advances the state of the art in submodular optimization. The practical significance is clear, and the theoretical foundation is solid, though the experimental validation could be more comprehensive. The authors should address the objective function discrepancy and provide a more detailed comparison of the algorithm's performance across different scenarios.
