## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission: Robust Submodular Maximization via Streaming and Thresholding

This paper presents a novel two-stage algorithm, STAR-T combined with a greedy post-processing step, for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint *k*. The core contribution lies in the design of STAR-T, a streaming algorithm leveraging a partitioning structure and a decreasing thresholding rule to efficiently handle data streams. The theoretical analysis demonstrates a constant-factor approximation guarantee for STAR-T when *k* approaches infinity (Theorem 4.1), and Lemmas 4.2 and 4.3 establish its resilience to element loss when paired with the greedy refinement step. The effectiveness of the proposed approach is demonstrated through experiments on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation tasks.

The work is potentially significant as it appears to be the first streaming algorithm specifically designed for *robust* submodular maximization. This is a valuable contribution, as the streaming setting is increasingly relevant in many real-world applications, and this work offers a promising direction for future research. The simplicity of parameter tuning is also a practical strength. The authors provide guidance for approximating the threshold parameter τ (Theorem 4.5) and empirically demonstrate that setting *w*=1 yields good performance, making the algorithm easier to deploy without extensive hyperparameter optimization. 

However, while the theoretical foundations seem sound and the idea is interesting, the experimental evaluation presents some concerns. The results do not consistently demonstrate a clear advantage of the STAR-T-Greedy algorithm over the Sieve-St algorithm. A more comprehensive comparison, including statistical significance tests, would be necessary to definitively establish the benefits of the proposed approach. Furthermore, the limited scale and scope of the experiments make it difficult to assess the algorithm’s performance under a broader range of conditions.

A more critical issue concerns the objective function used for the influence maximization experiments. The paper defines f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z| (line 219), which deviates from the standard spread model defined in the cited work by Kempe, Kleinberg, and Tardos (2003). This discrepancy is not adequately justified and raises questions about the validity of the influence maximization results. It is unclear how the algorithm performs in the context of the widely-adopted, and theoretically-studied, independent cascade or linear threshold models. A clarification regarding this difference, and perhaps results using a more standard objective function, would significantly strengthen the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel approach to robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting.
*   Constant-factor approximation guarantee for the STAR-T algorithm (when k is large).
*   Relatively simple parameter tuning.
*   Potential for broad applicability in dynamic environments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited and inconclusive experimental results.
*   Objective function mismatch in influence maximization experiments compared to standard literature.
*   Lack of statistical significance testing in the experimental evaluation.
*   The impact of the two hyperparameters (τ and w) could be further investigated. Although w=1 is sufficient in the experiment, it is not clear why.



**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Expand the experimental evaluation with more datasets, larger problem instances, and a more thorough comparison to existing algorithms (including Sieve-St and other relevant baselines).
*   Include statistical significance tests to support performance claims.
*   Justify the use of the non-standard objective function for influence maximization, or provide results using the standard spread model.
*   Investigate the sensitivity of the algorithm to the hyperparameters τ and *w*.
*   Discuss the computational complexity of STAR-T and its scalability with respect to the input stream size and the cardinality constraint *k*.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper introduces an interesting and potentially valuable algorithm for robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. However, the current experimental results are not compelling enough to fully demonstrate its advantages, and the discrepancy in the influence maximization objective function raises concerns. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly improve the paper and strengthen its contribution. I currently rate this paper as **weak reject**. Further revision addressing the concerns raised could potentially warrant reconsideration.
