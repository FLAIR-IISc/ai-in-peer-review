Summary:
The paper proposes a two-stage method for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint k. In the streaming stage, the STAR-T algorithm maintains a partitioned structure with a decreasing-threshold selection rule; in the post-processing stage, a simple greedy algorithm is applied. Theoretical results show that STAR-T achieves a constant-factor approximation independent of k (Thm. 4.1), and Lemmas 4.2–4.3 establish robustness to the loss of elements when combined with the final greedy step. Experiments on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation illustrate the practical performance of the approach. The method has two hyperparameters, τ and w; Thm. 4.5 provides a way to approximate τ, and the authors report that w=1 works well empirically, making the method easy to deploy.

Strengths:
- Novelty: To my knowledge, this is the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, which is an important and timely contribution with potential for broad applicability.
- Theory: The analysis is solid and delivers a constant-factor approximation guarantee that does not deteriorate with k. The robustness guarantees against element loss are clearly articulated.
- Practicality: The algorithm has few hyperparameters, and the guidance on τ together with the empirical choice w=1 reduces tuning burden.
- Scope: Demonstrations on two representative tasks (influence maximization and recommendation) suggest the approach can generalize across application areas.

Concerns:
- Experimental strength: The empirical evaluation is not fully convincing. In particular, STAR-T+Greedy does not consistently outperform Sieve-Streaming, which weakens the case for its practical superiority in the standard (non-robust) regime. Given the theoretical focus on robustness, it would be valuable to more clearly demonstrate advantages specifically under deletions/attacks.
- Influence maximization objective: The paper’s objective for influence maximization is f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z| (as stated around line 219), which corresponds to a coverage-style surrogate rather than the expected spread under the Independent Cascade or Linear Threshold models used in Kempe et al. (2003). This mismatch makes it difficult to interpret how the proposed method would perform on the canonical diffusion models. Without experiments under IC/LT (or a strong justification that coverage is a faithful proxy), the empirical relevance for influence maximization is unclear.
- Comparative baselines: Beyond Sieve-Streaming, it would be helpful to include stronger or more diverse baselines (e.g., robust or deletion-aware methods, where applicable) and to compare across both robust and non-robust settings to highlight the trade-offs introduced by STAR-T.
- Reporting: The paper would benefit from more detailed reporting of runtime, memory usage, and throughput in the streaming stage, since practicality is central to the streaming setting.

Questions for the authors:
- Robustness setting: What is the exact deletion model (e.g., adversarial vs. random, and the maximum number m of deletions)? How do the guarantees scale with m in practice, and are there experiments varying m?
- Thresholding scheme: How sensitive is performance to τ beyond the approximation suggested by Thm. 4.5? Is there an adaptive or data-driven way to estimate τ on the fly in the stream?
- Role of w: While w=1 seems to work well, can you provide an ablation over different w values and discuss the trade-off between robustness, utility, and memory?
- Influence objective: Please clarify the rationale for using f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|. If it is intended as a computationally efficient surrogate, can you provide correlations with IC/LT spread on smaller graphs, or include at least some experiments under standard diffusion simulations?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Expand the robustness-focused experiments: Systematically vary the number and strategy of deletions and demonstrate where STAR-T+Greedy outperforms non-robust baselines, including Sieve-Streaming, under these conditions.
- Include IC/LT experiments: Evaluate on standard diffusion models (IC and/or LT) or provide strong empirical evidence that the coverage proxy closely tracks expected spread.
- Add ablations and sensitivity analyses for τ and w, and report time/memory overheads to validate the streaming practicality.
- Clarify presentation: Replace “k up to infinity” with “approximation factor independent of k,” ensure theorem/lemma numbering is consistent, and give more intuition on the partitioning and threshold schedule.

Overall assessment:
This is a theoretically strong and novel contribution introducing a streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization with clear approximation guarantees and practical design. However, the empirical section does not yet fully substantiate the claimed advantages, especially for influence maximization under standard diffusion models and in robustness-centric scenarios. With stronger experiments—particularly under deletions and canonical IM objectives—and clearer reporting of efficiency and sensitivity, the paper would make a compelling case. As it stands, I find the work promising and impactful on the theory side, but I encourage the authors to bolster the empirical validation to match the strength of the analysis.
