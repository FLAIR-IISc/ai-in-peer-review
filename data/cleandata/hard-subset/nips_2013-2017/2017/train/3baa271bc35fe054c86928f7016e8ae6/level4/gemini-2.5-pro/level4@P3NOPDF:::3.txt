This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The goal is to select a high-value set of `k` elements from a data stream, while maintaining robustness against the adversarial removal of up to `m` elements from the solution. Streaming submodular maximization is a well-studied area, motivated by large-scale applications where only a few passes over the data are feasible. Robustness has also gained recent attention in submodular optimization, driven by scenarios where selected elements may become unavailable. This paper is the first to jointly consider these two critical constraints.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which processes the stream in a single pass while maintaining a memory footprint of `O((m log k + k) log^2 k)` elements. The key theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee of 0.149 for the robust objective. The algorithm builds upon established partitioning and thresholding techniques, adapting the use of "buckets" from prior work on robust submodular optimization to the streaming context. The paper's empirical evaluation demonstrates strong performance on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, where STAR-T is competitive even against streaming baselines that have oracle access to which elements will be removed.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a novel and challenging problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization. Combining these two practical constraints is well-motivated and addresses a clear gap in the literature.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Result:** The primary strength of this work is the development of an algorithm that provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee. Achieving any constant factor in this highly constrained setting is a significant theoretical achievement.

3.  **Promising Empirical Performance:** The experiments show that the proposed algorithm is not only theoretically sound but also practically effective. The comparison against an oracle-based streaming algorithm is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed robust approach.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Lower Bounds:** While the paper provides a strong upper bound on the approximation ratio, the theoretical contribution could be further strengthened by including a discussion of lower bounds. This would help contextualize the achieved constant (0.149) and clarify how close it is to the best possible guarantee in this model. Even a simple, non-trivial lower bound would add significant value.

2.  **Clarity of the Analysis:** The presentation of the theoretical analysis could be significantly improved. The authors mention an "innovative analysis" but do not elaborate on what makes it novel compared to prior work. The provided proof sketch is dense and difficult to parse, obscuring the key technical insights and the main flow of the argument. A more intuitive, high-level overview of the proof strategy—highlighting the main steps and novel ideas—would greatly enhance the paper's readability and impact.

3.  **Convincingness of the Influence Maximization Experiment:** The experimental validation for the influence maximization application could be more compelling. The paper employs a simplified objective function based solely on the size of the immediate neighborhood of the seed nodes. It is unclear whether this formulation is representative of standard influence maximization problems (e.g., using IC or LT models), where the function value depends on complex, network-wide simulations that are not naturally streaming-friendly. The authors should either clarify how traditional influence objectives could be handled in their framework or, alternatively, better motivate the choice of the simplified objective and discuss its relevance to real-world applications.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces and provides the first constant-factor approximation for an important and challenging problem. The weaknesses are primarily in the presentation and the scope of the experimental validation, which could be addressed in a revised version.
