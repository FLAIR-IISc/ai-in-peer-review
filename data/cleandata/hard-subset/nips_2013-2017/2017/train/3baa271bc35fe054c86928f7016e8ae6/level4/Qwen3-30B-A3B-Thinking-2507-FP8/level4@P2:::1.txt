

This paper addresses the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, where the goal is to compute a small summary set S that remains useful even after any m elements are removed from S. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm: STAR-T for constructing a robust summary in a single pass over the data, and STAR-T-GREEDY for finding a good solution from the remaining elements after removal. The main theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) with a summary size of O((k + m log k) log² k).

The paper builds on previous work in submodular maximization, particularly the streaming algorithm SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014), which achieves a 0.5-approximation with O(k log k) memory. It also relates to robust submodular maximization work (Krause et al., 2008; Orlin et al., 2016; Bogunovic et al., 2017), though the authors extend these results to the streaming setting with a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule.

Strengths: The theoretical analysis is thorough, with a detailed proof of the constant-factor approximation guarantee. The algorithm design is technically sophisticated, combining a partitioned structure with an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. The experimental results in two application domains (dominating set and movie recommendation) demonstrate that STAR-T-GREEDY matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows the removed elements in advance), even when the latter has this advantage. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise algorithm descriptions and helpful illustrations.

Weaknesses: The applications section is underdeveloped. The motivating examples (e.g., movie recommendations where users may have watched some recommended movies) don't strongly justify why robustness to element removal is more critical than other challenges in streaming submodular maximization. The problem appears somewhat specialized for a general conference like NIPS, as the robustness requirement may not be broadly applicable across the field. While the theoretical results are strong, the practical significance is not sufficiently emphasized.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The approximation guarantee is constant (0.149), which is competitive with prior work. The experimental evaluation is well-designed, with appropriate baselines and multiple metrics. However, the treatment of the threshold parameter τ (which depends on the unknown OPT value) is somewhat superficial in the main text, though the supplementary material provides a solution for approximating τ.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The algorithm descriptions are precise, and the figures effectively illustrate the partitioning structure. The supplementary material provides comprehensive proofs for the theoretical results. The paper would benefit from slightly more detailed discussion of how to practically implement the threshold parameter τ.

Originality: The paper makes a novel contribution to the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust submodular maximization. The partitioned thresholding approach is distinct from prior work, particularly in how it handles the robustness requirement within the streaming setting. While there is related work on robust submodular maximization, the streaming aspect and the specific algorithmic design are new.

Significance: The problem addressed is relevant to specific applications where data summaries must remain useful after some elements are removed, but the significance for the broader machine learning community is limited. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this robustness requirement is more important than other challenges in streaming submodular optimization (e.g., handling larger datasets, higher cardinality constraints, or different function types). The applications section fails to strongly motivate the problem's relevance to NIPS attendees.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The theoretical results are strong and novel, the algorithm is technically sophisticated, and the experimental results demonstrate practical value. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on streaming submodular maximization.

Con arguments for rejection: The significance is limited to a narrow application domain, the motivation for the robustness requirement is weak, and the problem may be too specialized for NIPS' general audience.

Overall, the paper presents a technically strong contribution to the specific problem of robust streaming submodular maximization. However, the significance for NIPS is questionable due to the limited scope of the problem and weak application motivation. While the theoretical results are impressive, the paper doesn't sufficiently convince the broader NIPS community that this specific problem is of general interest. The authors should strengthen the significance argument by better connecting the robustness requirement to broader challenges in machine learning, or by demonstrating broader applicability beyond the two presented use cases.
