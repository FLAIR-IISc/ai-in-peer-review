This paper studies the problem of submodular maximization in a streaming and robust setting, where the goal is to identify k elements of high value from a small subset of elements retained during the stream, with the added complexity that up to m items may be removed after the stream. The problem of streaming submodular maximization has been extensively studied due to its large-scale applications, where only a small number of passes over the data is feasible. Robustness has also garnered attention recently, motivated by scenarios where some elements may be removed, and the objective is to obtain solutions that are robust to such removals. This paper is the first to jointly consider the problem of streaming and robust submodular maximization.

The authors develop the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements after the stream and achieves a constant (0.149) approximation guarantee while being robust to the removal of m elements. This algorithm combines partitioning and thresholding techniques for submodular optimization, utilizing buckets. Similar ideas employing buckets were used in previous work for robust submodular optimization, but here the buckets are filled in a distinct manner. The authors demonstrate experimentally that this algorithm performs well in influence maximization and movie recommendation applications, even in comparison to streaming algorithms that have prior knowledge of which elements are removed.

Considering both the streaming and robustness aspects, the problem setting is challenging, and obtaining a constant factor approximation is a strong result. However, there are several aspects of the paper that could be improved. The inclusion of non-trivial lower bounds to complement the upper bound would have strengthened the paper. The writing of the analysis could also be improved, as the authors mention an "innovative analysis" without elaborating on its innovative aspects, making it difficult to extract the main ideas and steps of the analysis from the proof sketch. Furthermore, the influence maximization application could be more convincing. The experiments employ a simplified objective for influence maximization, focusing solely on the size of the neighborhood of the seed nodes. It is unclear how traditional influence maximization objectives, where the value of the function depends on the entire network for any set of seed nodes, could be adapted to a streaming setting.
