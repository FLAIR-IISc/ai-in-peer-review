This paper introduces a two-stage method for robust submodular maximization subject to a cardinality constraint k. The first stage presents STAR-T, a novel robust streaming algorithm that utilizes a partitioning structure and a decreasing thresholding rule. This is followed by a second stage that employs the simple greedy method. The authors provide theoretical guarantees, proving that STAR-T achieves a constant approximation ratio as k approaches infinity (Theorem 4.1) and that the combined approach effectively handles the loss of elements (Lemmas 4.2-4.3). Empirical evaluations on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation tasks demonstrate the proposed algorithm's effectiveness.

A key contribution of this work is its novelty; it introduces what appears to be the first streaming algorithm designed for robust submodular maximization. This approach is particularly insightful, as the streaming setting holds potential for a wide range of applications. Regarding the algorithm's practicality, STAR-T has two hyperparameters, τ and w. The authors provide a method for approximating τ (Theorem 4.5) and demonstrate empirically that a fixed value of w=1 performs well. This significantly enhances the algorithm's usability, as it reduces the need for extensive hyperparameter tuning.

My primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation, which could be more compelling. Specifically, the proposed STAR-T-Greedy algorithm does not consistently outperform the Sieve-St baseline across all experiments. Furthermore, there is a potential issue with the influence maximization experiment. The objective function defined in the paper (f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z|, line 219) appears to deviate from the standard influence spread model presented in the cited work by Kempe, Kleinberg, and Tardos (2003). This discrepancy makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's performance on the conventional formulation of the problem.
