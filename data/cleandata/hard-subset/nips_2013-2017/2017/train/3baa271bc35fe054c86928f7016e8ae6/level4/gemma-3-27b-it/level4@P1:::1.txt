The paper studies a robust streaming submodular maximization problem. The goal is to find a set T from a ground set V in a streaming fashion such that, even after removing any m elements from T, a subset S of size k can be found to approximately maximize a submodular function f. 

Applications of submodular maximization are numerous, and streaming algorithms are well-established for the standard problem. This work motivates a robust version by considering scenarios where elements of the selected set may become invalid or undesirable after the initial selection (e.g., a user losing interest in recommended movies).

The paper presents a constant-factor approximation algorithm, STAR-T, for this problem, returning a set T of size O((m log k + k) log^2 k). The technical contributions involve a novel partitioning structure and an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule, along with a correspondingly complex analysis.

To the best of my understanding, the paper’s results are technically sound and the solution is non-trivial. The algorithm and its analysis demonstrate a significant technical effort. However, my primary concern relates to the paper’s relevance to NIPS. While the problem is interesting, the provided application scenarios and their motivation feel somewhat weak. Furthermore, the problem itself, while a valuable generalization, may not be broadly applicable enough to warrant a presentation at this conference. 

Arguments for acceptance:
* **Technical soundness:** The algorithm and analysis appear rigorous and correct.
* **Novelty:** The proposed approach and approximation guarantees represent a novel contribution to the field of submodular optimization.
* **Problem formulation:** The robust streaming setting addresses a practical concern in many real-world applications.

Arguments against acceptance:
* **Application strength:** The motivating examples lack impact and don’t fully justify the complexity of the solution.
* **Generality:** The problem, while interesting, may be too specific to find broad applicability within the NIPS community.
* **Conference fit:** The work feels somewhat specialized and may be better suited to a more focused conference on submodular optimization or data summarization.

I recommend checking recent NIPS proceedings to confirm that this work represents a significant departure from prior contributions. While the technical quality is high, I am not convinced that the problem and its solution are sufficiently broad and impactful for NIPS 2017.
