

This paper addresses the challenging problem of submodular maximization under combined streaming and robustness constraints. In this setting, the goal is to select k high-value elements from a small retained set after processing a stream, while ensuring robustness against the removal of up to m elements. Streaming submodular maximization has been widely studied due to large-scale applications requiring limited data passes, and robustness has recently gained traction for scenarios where element removals may occur. The authors present the first framework to jointly tackle both aspects.

The proposed STAR-T algorithm maintains O((m log k + k) log² k) elements post-stream, achieves a constant-factor (0.149) approximation guarantee, and remains robust to m element removals. STAR-T integrates partitioning and thresholding via bucket-based techniques, adapting prior robust optimization approaches with a novel bucket-filling strategy. Experiments demonstrate strong performance in influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, even outperforming streaming baselines that know the removed elements in advance.

The joint consideration of streaming and robustness is highly non-trivial, and the constant-factor guarantee constitutes a significant theoretical advance. However, the paper has several shortcomings that weaken its contribution. First, the absence of non-trivial lower bounds leaves the optimality of the approximation factor unclear. Second, the analysis lacks sufficient detail: while the authors claim an "innovative analysis," they fail to clarify what distinguishes it from prior work or outline the core ideas in the proof sketch. As a result, it is challenging to discern the key technical insights. Finally, the influence maximization evaluation requires strengthening. The experiments employ an overly simplified objective that only considers the size of seed nodes' neighborhoods, neglecting standard influence maximization formulations where function values depend on the full network structure. It remains unclear how such network-dependent objectives could be accommodated within a streaming framework.

The technical contribution is compelling, but the paper would benefit from addressing these gaps. Specifically, providing lower bounds, elaborating on the analytical innovations, and using more realistic influence maximization objectives would substantially enhance the work's impact and credibility. The experimental claims, particularly regarding influence maximization, currently lack convincing support.
