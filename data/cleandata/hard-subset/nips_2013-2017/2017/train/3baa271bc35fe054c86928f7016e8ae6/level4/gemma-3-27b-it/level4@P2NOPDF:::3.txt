This paper addresses the challenging problem of maximizing a submodular function in a streaming environment while also providing robustness to potential element removals. Prior work has extensively studied streaming submodular maximization, motivated by large-scale applications with limited data access, and robust submodular maximization, driven by scenarios where elements may become unavailable. This work is the first to jointly consider these two aspects, presenting a significant contribution to the field.

The authors introduce the STAR-T algorithm, which demonstrably balances efficiency and performance. The algorithm maintains a set of O((m log k + k)log² k) elements after processing the stream and achieves a constant-factor (0.149) approximation guarantee even when up to *m* elements are removed. STAR-T leverages a combination of partitioning and thresholding techniques, utilizing buckets to manage candidate elements. While bucket-based approaches have appeared in prior work on robust submodular optimization, this paper innovatively defines a novel bucket-filling strategy.  Experimental validation on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks demonstrates competitive performance, even when compared to streaming algorithms with foreknowledge of element removals.

Obtaining a constant-factor approximation for this combined streaming and robust setting is a noteworthy achievement. However, the paper would benefit from several improvements. The inclusion of non-trivial lower bounds would provide valuable context and strengthen the theoretical foundation of the results.  Currently, the lack of such bounds leaves the tightness of the approximation guarantee unclear. 

Furthermore, the presentation of the analysis could be improved. The authors claim an "innovative analysis," but this assertion is not sufficiently elaborated upon. The proof sketch is dense and makes it difficult to discern the key ideas and steps involved. A more detailed explanation, or a clearer highlighting of the novel aspects of the analysis, would enhance understanding. 

Finally, the application to influence maximization feels somewhat limited. The experiments utilize a simplified objective function – solely the size of the neighborhood of selected seed nodes. This simplification may not fully capture the complexity of typical influence maximization problems, where the value of a seed set often depends on network-wide effects.  It is unclear how standard influence maximization objectives, which evaluate the function’s value across the entire network for a given seed set, could be effectively adapted to a streaming setting and remain computationally tractable. A discussion of these limitations and potential avenues for addressing them would strengthen the application presented.
