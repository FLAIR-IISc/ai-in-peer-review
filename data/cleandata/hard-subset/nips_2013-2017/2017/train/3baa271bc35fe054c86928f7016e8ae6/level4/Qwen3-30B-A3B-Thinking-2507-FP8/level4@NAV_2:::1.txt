

The paper addresses the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, where the goal is to find a small representative subset of elements in a streaming fashion that remains robust to the removal of any m elements. This is formalized as a two-stage problem: (1) a streaming algorithm constructs a robust summary S, and (2) after any m elements are removed from S, a query algorithm produces a solution of size k from the remaining elements. The authors propose STAR-T, a streaming algorithm with a novel partitioned thresholding structure, and STAR-T-GREEDY, a simple greedy algorithm for the query stage. They prove that STAR-T constructs a summary of size O((m log k + k) log^2 k) and that STAR-T-GREEDY achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee after any m elements are removed.

The work builds upon previous NIPS contributions on streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING by Badanidiyuru et al., NIPS 2014) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., Bogunovic et al., ICML 2017), extending these ideas to the robust streaming setting. The paper's partitioned thresholding approach and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule represent a significant technical contribution that distinguishes it from previous work.

Quality: The theoretical results are technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a detailed analysis showing that STAR-T-GREEDY achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149(1-1/log k)) after any m elements are removed. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that the method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING in two data summarization tasks (dominating set and personalized movie recommendation), even when SIEVE-STREAMING knows the removed elements in advance.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithms are presented with pseudocode and detailed explanations, and the theoretical results are supported by comprehensive proofs in the supplementary material. The experimental section is well-structured and provides clear comparisons with relevant baselines.

Originality: The paper makes a novel contribution by introducing a partitioned thresholding structure for robust streaming submodular maximization. This is distinct from previous work on robust submodular maximization (e.g., [9, 10, 11]) and streaming submodular maximization (e.g., [8, 12, 13]), as it addresses both streaming and robustness simultaneously in a single framework. The paper effectively distinguishes its approach from related work.

Significance: While the problem has practical relevance for large-scale data summarization where elements may need to be removed (e.g., personalized recommendations), the applications are somewhat limited to two specific domains. The theoretical results provide a valuable framework for robust data summarization in streaming settings, but the paper could better establish the broader significance of the problem for the machine learning community.

Strengths: The technical contribution is strong, with a novel algorithm design and non-trivial analysis. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates practical utility. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from previous work.

Weaknesses: The motivation for the robust streaming setting could be strengthened by discussing more compelling applications or providing a clearer argument for why this specific problem is important beyond the two examples provided. The problem formulation is somewhat narrow compared to other submodular maximization settings, and the paper could better address how this work advances the field beyond incremental improvements.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a significant technical contribution to the area of streaming submodular maximization, with a novel algorithm and strong theoretical guarantees. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate practical utility.

Arguments against acceptance: The applications are somewhat limited in scope, and the problem, while interesting, may not represent a sufficiently broad or significant contribution for NIPS compared to other recent work in the field. The paper would benefit from a stronger argument for the broader significance of the robust streaming framework.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the area of streaming submodular maximization, though its significance could be better established with broader applications and a clearer argument for why this specific robust streaming framework is important for the wider machine learning community.
