This paper addresses the problem of submodular maximization in a streaming and robust setting, where the objective is to select k high-value elements from a small subset retained during the stream, with the possibility of m items being removed after the stream. The study of streaming submodular maximization has been extensive due to its applications in large-scale settings where only a limited number of passes over the data is feasible. Robustness has also gained attention recently, driven by scenarios where elements may be removed, and the goal is to obtain solutions that are resilient to such removals. This work is the first to jointly consider the problem of streaming and robust submodular maximization.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which maintains O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements after the stream and achieves a constant (0.149) approximation guarantee while being robust to the removal of m elements. This algorithm combines partitioning and thresholding techniques for submodular optimization using buckets, similar to previous work on robust submodular optimization, but with a distinct approach to filling the buckets. Experimental results demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness in influence maximization and movie recommendation applications, even compared to streaming algorithms with prior knowledge of removed elements.

The problem setting, incorporating both streaming and robustness aspects, is challenging, and achieving a constant factor approximation is a significant result. However, several aspects of the paper could be improved. Non-trivial lower bounds would complement the upper bound and strengthen the results. The analysis writing could be enhanced; the authors mention an "innovative analysis" but fail to elaborate on its innovative aspects, making it difficult to discern the main ideas and steps of the analysis from the proof sketch. Furthermore, the influence maximization application could be more convincing. The experiments employ a simplified objective for influence maximization, focusing solely on the size of the neighborhood of seed nodes. It is unclear how traditional influence maximization objectives, which depend on the entire network for any set of seed nodes, could be adapted to a streaming setting.

The authors' experimental evaluation demonstrates the STAR-T algorithm's performance in real-world applications, but additional insights into its behavior and comparisons with other robust streaming algorithms would be beneficial. Providing more detailed explanations of the analysis and its innovative components would also improve the paper's clarity and impact.
