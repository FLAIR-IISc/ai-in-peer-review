This paper addresses the problem of monotone submodular function maximization subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a challenging setting that combines two constraints: the data arrives in a single-pass stream, and the solution must be robust to the adversarial removal of `m` elements. The authors propose a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a novel streaming algorithm, STAR-T, processes the data stream to create a compact summary. This algorithm is distinguished by its use of a partitioned structure with exponentially sized buckets and an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. In the second stage, after an adversary removes up to `m` elements from this summary, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining elements to produce the final solution. The paper provides a theoretical analysis showing that this procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee, with the summary size being O((k + m log k) log k). This work builds upon and extends ideas from both streaming submodular maximization, such as the thresholding technique in SIEVE-STREAMING [8], and robust submodular maximization, adopting a partitioning structure similar to the centralized algorithm in [11]. It also contrasts with concurrent work [18] by achieving a memory complexity that is logarithmic in `k`, rather than linear.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Technical Quality:** The paper tackles a novel and practical combination of streaming processing and robustness requirements for submodular maximization. The proposed STAR-T algorithm is a non-trivial synthesis of partitioned data structures and a multi-level thresholding scheme, which is a clever adaptation of existing streaming techniques to the robust setting. The theoretical analysis appears sound and provides the first constant-factor approximation guarantee for this problem with a memory requirement that scales favorably with the solution size `k`.
*   **Significance:** The problem formulation is highly relevant for large-scale machine learning applications where data cannot be stored indefinitely and deployed solutions must be resilient to unforeseen changes, such as system failures or shifts in user preferences. The ability to generate a robust summary in a single pass and then quickly adapt to removals without accessing the original dataset is a significant practical advantage. The improved memory complexity over concurrent work makes the approach more scalable.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The algorithms are described precisely, and the inclusion of a proof sketch for the main theorem effectively conveys the core ideas of the analysis. The experimental setup is well-explained and the results are presented in an understandable manner.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Motivation of Applications:** While the paper provides two interesting applications in dominating sets and personalized movie recommendation, the motivation for the specific robust streaming model could be strengthened. For example, it would be beneficial to elaborate on why, in these scenarios, it is strictly necessary to work with a pre-computed summary and why re-streaming or querying a subset of the original data is not a viable alternative. A more detailed narrative connecting the theoretical model to concrete operational constraints in real-world systems would enhance the paper's perceived impact.
*   **Experimental Comparison:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates that the proposed method performs comparably to or better than a strong baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING that knows the removed set in advance). This is a solid result. However, the evaluation could be made more comprehensive. A comparison with the concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. [18], even if just in terms of memory usage versus performance on a single dataset, would provide valuable context. Additionally, comparing against a baseline where the non-streaming robust algorithm PRO [11] is run on a randomly sampled subset of the stream could offer further insights into the trade-offs of the proposed approach.
*   **Approximation Constant:** The derived constant in the approximation guarantee (≈0.149) is modest. While proving any constant factor for this challenging problem is a notable achievement, it would be valuable for the authors to include a brief discussion on whether this constant is a byproduct of the proof technique or reflects a fundamental limitation of the algorithm's design. This could also help motivate future work in this area.
