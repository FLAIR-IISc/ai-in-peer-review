

This paper addresses the challenging problem of submodular maximization under both streaming constraints and robustness to element removals. The authors propose the first algorithm, STAR-T, for this joint setting, where the goal is to select a set of size $k$ from a stream such that the solution remains robust against the removal of up to $m$ elements after the stream. The algorithm maintains $O((m \log k + k) \log^2 k)$ elements during the stream and achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of 0.149. STAR-T innovatively adapts bucket-based partitioning and thresholding techniques from prior robust submodular work, but with a distinct bucket-filling strategy tailored for streaming. Experiments demonstrate strong performance in influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, even when adversaries know the removed elements in advance.

The problem formulation is well-motivated, combining two active research areas (streaming and robust submodular optimization) with clear practical relevance. The theoretical contribution is significant: achieving a constant approximation for this combined setting represents a notable advance. The algorithm design is carefully constructed, and the experimental results support the practical viability of the approach.

However, the paper would benefit from several improvements. First, the absence of non-trivial lower bounds is a notable gap. While the upper bound is strong, a matching or near-matching lower bound would better contextualize the approximation ratio and highlight the algorithm’s optimality. The current lack of such bounds leaves open whether the 0.149 factor could be improved or if it is inherently limited.

Second, the technical analysis requires greater clarity. The paper claims an "innovative analysis" but fails to articulate what makes it innovative or to distill the key insights from the proof sketch. The current exposition makes it difficult to verify the correctness of the approximation guarantee or to grasp the core ideas driving the analysis. A more structured presentation of the proof strategy, highlighting novel technical lemmas or the interplay between streaming and robustness, would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution.

Third, the influence maximization experiments require stronger justification. The authors use a simplified objective—only the size of the neighborhood around seed nodes—which does not align with standard influence maximization formulations where the objective depends on the entire network structure (e.g., expected spread via diffusion models). This choice obscures whether the algorithm can handle the full problem. A more convincing evaluation would either: (a) demonstrate how the standard objective fits within the streaming model (e.g., via sketching techniques), or (b) provide experiments with a realistic objective that captures global network dynamics. Without this, the applicability of the algorithm to real-world influence maximization remains unclear.

Minor points: The algorithm name "STAR-T" appears inconsistent with the paper’s notation; it should be standardized. The experimental setup for movie recommendation could better clarify how the streaming and robustness aspects are integrated into the application. The writing occasionally uses passive voice ("it is shown experimentally") and vague phrasing ("performs well"), which could be tightened for impact.

In summary, this paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing the first algorithm for robust streaming submodular maximization with a constant approximation guarantee. The theoretical result is strong, and the experiments provide initial evidence of practical utility. However, the paper’s impact would be significantly enhanced by addressing the gaps in the lower bounds, clarifying the analysis, and strengthening the experimental justification for influence maximization. With these revisions, it would be a compelling addition to the literature.
