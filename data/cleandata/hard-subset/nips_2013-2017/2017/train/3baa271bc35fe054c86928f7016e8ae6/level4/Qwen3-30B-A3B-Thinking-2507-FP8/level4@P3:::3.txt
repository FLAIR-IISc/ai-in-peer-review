

This paper addresses the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where the algorithm must process elements in a single pass, retain a compact summary, and guarantee robustness against the removal of up to $m$ elements from the summary. The authors propose STAR-T, a novel two-stage algorithm combining partitioned thresholding with exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds. The key contribution is a theoretical guarantee of a constant-factor $(0.149)$ approximation after any $m$-element removal, with a summary size of $O((k + m \log k) \log^2 k)$. This work extends SIEVE-STREAMING [8] (NIPS 2014) to the robust setting while improving over concurrent work [18] by reducing memory dependence on $k$ from linear to logarithmic. The paper also demonstrates practical efficacy in influence maximization (using a simplified objective) and personalized movie recommendation, where STAR-T-GREEDY matches or exceeds SIEVE-STREAMING—even when the latter knows the removed elements in advance.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Theoretical Novelty and Soundness:** The paper provides a clean, self-contained proof of the approximation guarantee, leveraging a partitioned bucket structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds. This generalizes prior robust approaches [11] (ICML 2017) by avoiding sequential greedy bucket filling and enabling robustness for *any* $m$ (not just $m = o(\sqrt{k})$). The analysis is technically rigorous, though the proof sketch could better highlight *why* the thresholding rule is innovative—specifically, how the exponential decay enables balancing the two cases (full vs. sparse partitions) without prior knowledge of $f(\text{OPT}(k, V \setminus E))$.  
2. **Practical Validation:** Experiments convincingly show STAR-T’s robustness in two real-world applications. In influence maximization (Section 5.1), STAR-T-GREEDY matches SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows $E$) despite $E$ being sampled *from the summary itself*—a strong test of robustness. The movie recommendation case (Section 5.2) demonstrates adaptability to user-specific removals (e.g., genre filtering, already-watched movies), where STAR-T-GREEDY nearly matches the oracle GREEDY.  
3. **Clarity and Organization:** The problem statement, algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1–2), and theoretical results are well-structured. The supplementary material (referenced for detailed proofs) enhances reproducibility. The memory bound $O((k + m \log k) \log^2 k)$ is a clear improvement over [18]’s $O(mk \log k / \epsilon)$.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Lack of Lower Bounds:** The paper establishes an upper bound of $0.149$, but no matching lower bound is provided. For a strong theoretical contribution, it would be valuable to state whether this constant is tight or if better guarantees are possible (e.g., via reduction to known results [10, 11]). This omission limits the perceived completeness of the theoretical contribution.  
2. **Analysis Clarity:** The claim of an "innovative analysis" (Section 4) is underdeveloped. The proof sketch (Theorem 4.1) does not explicitly contrast the novelty with prior work [11], making it hard to grasp the core insight. A brief sentence on *how* the partitioning and thresholding interact to avoid the $O(m^2 \log k)$ bucket sizes in [11] would strengthen the argument.  
3. **Influence Maximization Simplification:** The objective $f(Z) = |N(Z) \cup Z|$ (Section 5.1) is a significant simplification of standard influence maximization (e.g., [3], SIGKDD 2003), where influence spreads probabilistically over the entire network. While the paper acknowledges this, it does not discuss *how* to extend STAR-T to more complex influence models (e.g., using Monte Carlo approximations [8]) in a streaming setting. This weakens the applicability argument for a key application domain.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pros:*  
- First unified solution for streaming + robust submodular maximization with strong theoretical guarantees.  
- Practical validation shows robustness against *any* $m$-element removal, outperforming baselines even when they know $E$ in advance.  
- Memory bound is theoretically superior to concurrent work [18].  
*Cons:*  
- Missing lower bounds for the approximation ratio.  
- Analysis lacks explicit comparison to prior robust methods [11].  
- Influence maximization experiment uses an overly simplistic objective.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This paper makes a significant contribution to the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization, addressing a natural and increasingly relevant problem in large-scale data summarization. The theoretical result is solid, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the experiments are convincing for the chosen applications. The weaknesses are substantive but not fatal: the absence of lower bounds is a notable gap, and the influence maximization simplification could be better contextualized. However, the core algorithmic advance is novel and impactful for NIPS readers. The authors could address these points in a revision (e.g., adding a lower bound discussion or expanding on influence model extensions).  

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions). The work is technically strong, addresses a meaningful problem, and advances the state of the art in streaming submodular optimization. The minor weaknesses do not undermine the paper’s core contribution.
