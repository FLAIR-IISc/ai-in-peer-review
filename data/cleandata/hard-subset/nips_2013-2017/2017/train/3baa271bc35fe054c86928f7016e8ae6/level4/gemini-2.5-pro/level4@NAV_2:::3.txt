This paper studies the problem of monotone submodular maximization subject to a cardinality constraint, extended to a setting that is simultaneously streaming and robust. In this two-stage model, an algorithm makes a single pass over the data to create a small, robust summary. Subsequently, after an adversary removes up to `m` items from this summary, a final solution of size `k` must be extracted from the remaining elements. This work is the first to jointly address the challenges of streaming data access and robustness, building upon prior work in streaming submodular maximization and robust optimization but tackling a new, combined problem domain.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which employs a novel partitioning structure with exponentially-sized buckets and an associated exponentially-decreasing thresholding rule. This algorithm constructs the robust summary using `O((m log k + k) log^2 k)` memory in one pass. For the query stage, the paper proposes running a simple greedy algorithm on the summary after elements have been removed. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this procedure achieves a constant-factor (0.149) approximation guarantee. The authors empirically validate their method on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, demonstrating strong performance that is competitive with, and at times superior to, streaming baselines that have the advantage of knowing in advance which items will be removed.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its novel formulation of the robust streaming submodular maximization problem and the development of the first algorithm with a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The problem itself is well-motivated and highly relevant to large-scale applications where data summaries must be resilient to failures, updates, or even adversarial attacks. The proposed algorithm is an elegant, non-trivial combination of partitioning and thresholding ideas. Furthermore, the experimental results are compelling, showing the algorithm performs on par with a strong baseline that is given a significant informational advantage, which underscores the practical viability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**
While the theoretical contribution is strong, several aspects of the paper could be improved.
1.  **Clarity of the Analysis:** The presentation of the theoretical analysis could be significantly enhanced. The proof sketch in the main paper is difficult to follow, and the authors’ claim of an "innovative analysis" is not sufficiently elaborated upon. It would be beneficial for the authors to better articulate the key technical novelties and intuitions behind their proof.
2.  **Theoretical Context:** The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of lower bounds for this problem. While the constant-factor upper bound is a solid result, providing a lower bound would help contextualize the tightness of the analysis and the inherent difficulty of the problem.
3.  **Experimental Setup:** The experimental validation on the dominating set problem could be more convincing. The objective function used is a simplification of influence maximization. It is not immediately clear how the streaming approach would handle more standard influence models (like IC or LT), where evaluating the objective function often requires simulations or access to the entire graph structure, which seems to conflict with the streaming model's constraints.

In summary, this paper presents a solid contribution by tackling a new, challenging, and important problem. The development of a constant-factor approximation algorithm is a strong result, supported by promising experiments. The primary weaknesses relate to the clarity of the theoretical exposition and a potentially oversimplified experimental task. However, the significance of the core contribution is high.
