Summary:
The paper studies robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. Given a ground set E arriving as a stream and a nonnegative submodular function f, the algorithm maintains a summary T such that, after the stream ends, if any m elements are deleted from T, one can still extract a size-k subset S ⊆ T \ D whose value f(S) is within a constant factor of the optimal size-k solution on the (undeleted) ground set. The proposed method achieves a constant-factor approximation while outputting a summary of size O((m log k + k) log^2 k).

Contributions and strengths:
- Addresses a timely and technically interesting variant that combines two important themes: streaming constraints and robustness to adversarial deletions.
- Provides a constant-factor approximation with polylogarithmic memory dependence on k and linear dependence on m, which is appealing for streaming.
- The algorithmic ideas and analysis appear nontrivial and technically solid. To the best of my understanding, the results are correct.
- The work offers a principled approach to building a robust coreset T from which a near-optimal k-set can be recovered even after deletions.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Motivation and applications are underdeveloped. The paper briefly mentions recommendation scenarios (e.g., users vetoing items), but does not convincingly argue that deletions of up to m items are a central practical requirement nor that the specific robustness model aligns with real deployments. Additional concrete domains (e.g., sensor placement under failures, data summarization with post hoc content filtering, active learning with label constraints) would strengthen the case.
- Empirical evaluation is missing or minimal. Even synthetic experiments would help illuminate trade-offs among approximation quality, summary size, runtime, and robustness level m, and compare against strong streaming baselines without robustness.
- Clarity on assumptions and the precise problem model should be improved. It is not entirely clear whether f is assumed monotone, whether the algorithm knows k and m in advance, whether deletions are fully adversarial and occur only after the stream, and what oracle access to f is assumed (value queries only?). These details matter both for theory and practice.
- The practicality of the space bound and constants is unclear. While O((m log k + k) log^2 k) is asymptotically attractive, the hidden constants and the per-element update time could make the method heavy in practice, especially for large m.
- Positioning relative to prior work needs strengthening. There is a sizable literature on streaming submodular maximization and on robust submodular selection (without streaming). The paper should more explicitly compare guarantees, assumptions, and space/approximation trade-offs with these lines of work, and clarify whether similar robustness had been explored in streaming before.

Clarity and presentation:
- Overall well written, but several definitions could be centralized and stated precisely early on (streaming model, deletions, oracle access, monotonicity, normalization).
- Clearly state the main theorem with all quantifiers and parameter dependencies, and highlight the approximation factor achieved.
- Provide pseudocode and a high-level roadmap that explains the key invariants and why the summary T is robust to m deletions.
- Include an intuitive example illustrating how the structure of T enables recovery after deletions.

Questions for the authors:
- Is f assumed to be monotone and normalized? Does the result extend to non-monotone submodular functions or other constraints (e.g., matroids)?
- Does the algorithm require prior knowledge of k and m? If m is unknown or misspecified, what degrades?
- Are deletions assumed to be adversarial and revealed only after the stream finishes? Can the approach handle deletions interleaved with the stream?
- What are the hidden constants in the approximation and space bounds? What is the per-item update time?
- Are there lower bounds showing that the dependence on m and log factors is necessary (up to logs)?

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- Strengthen the motivation with concrete, realistic use cases and a discussion of why post hoc deletions are common and important.
- Add empirical results comparing with strong streaming baselines (non-robust algorithms and simple robust heuristics) on synthetic and, if possible, real datasets. Report approximation quality, memory footprint, runtime, and sensitivity to m and k.
- Expand the related work section to clearly delineate what is new: robustness to deletions in the streaming model, the specific space bound, and the constant-factor guarantee.
- Discuss potential extensions (e.g., matroid constraints, sliding-window streams, unknown m) and limitations.
- Tighten notation and fix minor grammatical issues (e.g., “its analysis,” “m log k”).

Overall assessment:
This is a technically solid paper with a novel and well-founded contribution at the intersection of streaming and robust submodular maximization. The main theoretical result is interesting. However, the current motivation and empirical support are not strong, and the application narrative feels thin for the conference audience. With improved positioning, clearer modeling assumptions, and some empirical validation, the paper would make a more compelling case.
