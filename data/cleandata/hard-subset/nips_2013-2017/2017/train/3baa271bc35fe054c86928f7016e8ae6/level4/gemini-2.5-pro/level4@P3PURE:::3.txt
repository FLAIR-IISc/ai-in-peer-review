This paper addresses the problem of submodular maximization under both streaming and robust constraints. The objective is to select a set of `k` elements that maximizes a submodular function, chosen from a small summary of a data stream, while being robust to the adversarial removal of up to `m` elements from the solution. While streaming submodular maximization is a well-studied area motivated by large-scale applications requiring a limited number of data passes, robustness has gained attention more recently. This is crucial in scenarios where elements of a solution may be compromised or removed, and the goal is to maintain high performance despite these removals. This work is the first to jointly consider these two challenges.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which requires a memory footprint of `O((m log k + k) log^2 k)` elements. For this space complexity, it achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of 0.149 while ensuring robustness against the removal of `m` elements. The algorithm leverages a combination of partitioning and thresholding techniques, employing a 'bucket'-based approach. While similar bucket-based ideas have appeared in prior work on robust submodular optimization, the authors introduce a novel method for populating these buckets within the streaming context. The paper's experiments demonstrate that STAR-T performs well on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks. Notably, it shows strong performance even when compared to non-robust streaming algorithms that have oracle access to which elements will be removed.

The problem setting is novel and challenging, and achieving a constant-factor approximation in this combined streaming and robust model is a significant theoretical contribution. However, several aspects of the paper could be strengthened.

First, the theoretical results would be more complete with the inclusion of non-trivial lower bounds to complement the algorithm's upper bound on the approximation ratio.

Second, the clarity of the analysis could be improved. The authors describe their analysis as "innovative" but do not elaborate on what makes it so. The provided proof sketch is difficult to follow, making it hard for the reader to distill the main technical ideas and key steps of the proof.

Finally, the experimental validation on the influence maximization task could be more convincing. The authors employ a simplified objective function based only on the neighborhood size of the seed nodes. It remains unclear how the proposed streaming approach would handle standard, more complex influence maximization objectives where the function value for any given seed set depends on the entire network graph, a property that seems at odds with the streaming model.
