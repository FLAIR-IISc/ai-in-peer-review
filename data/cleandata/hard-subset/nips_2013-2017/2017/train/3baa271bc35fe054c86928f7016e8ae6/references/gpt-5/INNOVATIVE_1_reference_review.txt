Summary
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, introducing a two-stage framework. In the streaming stage, STAR-T builds a compact, robust summary S via a novel partitioned structure: each partition consists of exponentially sized buckets, and elements are admitted using exponentially decreasing thresholds tied to the partition index. In the query stage, after any set E of up to m elements is removed from the universe, a greedy routine run only on S\E returns a size-k solution. The analysis shows a constant-factor approximation (≈0.149 as k→∞) and a one-pass memory bound of roughly O(k log k + m log^2 k) elements. A parallel, multi-τ variant removes the need for a priori knowledge of f(OPT). Experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation indicate performance comparable to SIEVE-STREAMING—even giving SIEVE-STREAMING the advantage of knowing E in advance—and demonstrate an interactive use case.

Strengths
- Novelty: The partitioned-thresholding design is an elegant, streaming analogue of robust non-uniform partitioning ideas (e.g., PRO), combined with sieve thresholds. The decreasing thresholds across partitions are a neat conceptual advance for robustness in streams.
- Theory: Single-pass, deletion-robust summary with a constant-factor guarantee against OPT(V\E) using only S\E. The memory dependence on m is nearly optimal-looking, and crucially the dependence on k is logarithmic, improving over concurrent work that is linear in k.
- Practicality: Simple to implement (just thresholds and disjoint buckets), and the query stage is just GREEDY over S\E. The parallel-τ trick follows established sieve methods and makes the approach practical without oracle access to OPT.
- Empirics: On two applications, the approach matches or slightly outperforms strong baselines, including one that knows E in advance, and shows an interactive “one summary, many E” capability.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Inconsistencies in space bounds and thresholds:
  - Different parts of the paper state O((k + m log k) log^2 k) vs O((k + m log k) log k) for |S|. Theorem 4.1 and the counting derivation suggest |S| ≤ (log k + O(1))wk, which with w = Θ(m log k/k) yields O(k log k + m log^2 k). Please unify the statement across abstract, contributions, theorem, and conclusion.
  - Threshold rule: text says τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i,k}. The latter seems right (to cap the last partitions) but the narrative should match the algorithm.
- Approximation factor: The constant 0.149 is modest compared to 1/2−ε in concurrent work, though your k-dependence in memory is better. Some discussion of the trade-off frontier (approximation vs memory) would strengthen the contribution.
- Unknown-OPT instantiation: The multi-τ construction is standard, but implementation details are not described in the experiments. Did the experiments use an oracle τ, or parallel instances? What range and granularity for τ were used?
- Experimental evaluation:
  - Form of removal: In dominating set, E is sampled as a subset of S (which disadvantages your method) and also removed greedily in another setting. While this is informative, the robustness guarantee is for E ⊆ V; consider also random/adversarial E drawn from V to align with the theory.
  - Parameter regimes: The experiments often set w=1 and remove up to k or 2k elements; this exceeds the theoretical bound for w=1 (m ≲ k/4 log k). While it’s great that performance remains competitive, report the actual |S| and memory vs k,m to show empirical scaling and to validate the implied w–m relationship.
  - Efficiency: No runtime or throughput results are reported. Since streaming is a core motivation, basic timings and update rates (and how they compare to SIEVE-STREAMING) would be valuable.
- Presentation:
  - Several proofs are deferred; that’s fine, but the main text proof sketches could be tightened, especially the argument that some full bucket has f(B\E) ≈ τ after at most m deletions (the “on average |B∩E| is small” step deserves a precise statement in the main text).
  - Minor notation nits: avoid mixing τ/2^i and τ/min{2^i,k}; consistently denote partitions and indices.

Quality
The technical core is sound and thoughtfully analyzed. The main claims are supported by nontrivial lemmas covering complementary cases. Some mismatches and missing implementation details (τ selection, exact space usage) should be fixed, and adding efficiency measurements would strengthen the empirical case.

Originality
High. The partitioned thresholding strategy for robust streaming is a fresh and compelling synthesis that goes beyond straightforward adaptations of SIEVE-STREAMING or centralized robust constructions. The memory-approximation trade-off compared to concurrent work is a notable contribution.

Significance
Significant. Robust streaming summarization is important in practice (deletions, “right to be forgotten”, personalization). The approach gives a reusable summary with query-time adaptability, and the logarithmic dependence on k in memory is attractive for large k. I expect others to build on the structure and analysis.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel algorithmic idea with principled analysis.
- Strong motivation and a practically useful two-stage paradigm.
- Memory scaling advantage versus prior/concurrent robust streaming work.
- Competitive results on realistic tasks; interactive use case.

Cons for acceptance
- Inconsistencies in statements (space bounds, thresholds) and modest approximation constant.
- Limited experimental breadth and lack of runtime/memory reporting.
- Incomplete clarity on τ instantiation in practice.

Suggestions for improvement
- Unify all space and threshold statements; correct Algorithm 1/text to be consistent.
- Explicitly report |S| and timings versus k,m,w; include experiments with E ⊆ V.
- Clarify how τ is handled in experiments; specify the parallel τ grid and its overhead.
- Briefly position the memory–approximation trade-off against concurrent 1/2−ε results.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a genuinely new and promising direction with solid theory and a simple, implementable method. Despite some presentation and experimental gaps, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance.
