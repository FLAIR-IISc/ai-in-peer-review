

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper proposes a two-stage streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, where the goal is to create a summary set that remains effective even after the removal of up to m elements. The authors introduce a partitioned thresholding approach (STAR-T) for the streaming stage, followed by a greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) for the query stage.

The paper builds on existing work in submodular maximization and streaming algorithms, particularly extending SIEVE-STREAMING to the robust setting. The theoretical analysis claims a 0.149 approximation guarantee for the robust problem, which is notably lower than the standard 0.63 guarantee for non-robust submodular maximization. The memory complexity is O((k + m log k) log²k), which is higher than the O(k log k) complexity of SIEVE-STREAMING.

**Strengths:**
1. The problem formulation is well-motivated, addressing a real challenge in large-scale data summarization where robustness against element removal is needed.
2. The algorithm design is clearly presented with well-structured pseudocode.
3. The experimental validation demonstrates that the proposed method performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING, even when SIEVE-STREAMING has the advantage of knowing in advance which elements will be removed.
4. The paper acknowledges related work, including concurrent work [18], though it doesn't sufficiently address how their approach differs.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The theoretical guarantee of 0.149 is quite weak compared to standard submodular maximization algorithms. This is a significant drawback that the authors don't adequately address. For context, the standard greedy algorithm for non-robust submodular maximization achieves 0.63, and the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm achieves 0.5 - ε.

2. The experimental comparison is unfair: SIEVE-STREAMING is allowed to know in advance which elements will be removed, while STAR-T must work with a precomputed summary. In real-world applications, you would not know which elements will be removed in advance, making this comparison misleading.

3. The memory complexity (O((k + m log k) log²k)) is higher than SIEVE-STREAMING's O(k log k), which is a significant practical drawback for large-scale applications. The paper doesn't convincingly justify this higher memory cost given the modest improvement in robustness.

4. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from the concurrent work [18], which also addresses robust streaming submodular maximization. The authors claim their memory complexity is better (O(m log²k) vs. O(mk log k/ε)), but they don't provide a direct comparison of the constant factors or practical performance.

5. The paper claims to "outperform" SIEVE-STREAMING in some cases, but the experiments don't demonstrate a clear advantage over standard methods when the robustness requirement is considered. The improvement is marginal at best.

**Quality Assessment:**
The theoretical analysis appears technically sound, though the low approximation guarantee (0.149) is concerning. The experimental methodology is reasonable but contains an unfair comparison point. The paper is a complete piece of work, but it doesn't sufficiently address the weaknesses of the proposed approach.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The algorithm descriptions are precise, and the theoretical results are presented with appropriate detail.

**Originality:**
The partitioned thresholding approach is novel, but the paper doesn't sufficiently establish how this differs from prior work in the robust submodular maximization space. The novelty is incremental rather than transformative.

**Significance:**
The problem is significant, but the solution doesn't advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The 0.149 approximation guarantee is substantially worse than standard results, and the memory complexity is higher than existing streaming algorithms. The practical advantages of the proposed approach aren't compellingly demonstrated.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem and presents a technically sound algorithm, the theoretical guarantee is too weak (0.149) compared to standard results (0.63), and the experimental comparison is misleading. The memory complexity is also higher than the state-of-the-art streaming algorithm. The contribution is incremental and doesn't sufficiently advance the field of robust submodular maximization.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing robust submodular maximization methods, a stronger theoretical guarantee, and a more convincing justification for the higher memory complexity. Without these improvements, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the novelty and practical value of the proposed approach.
