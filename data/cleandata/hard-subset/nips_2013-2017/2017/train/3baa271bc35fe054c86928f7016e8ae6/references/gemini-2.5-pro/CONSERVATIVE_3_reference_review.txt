**Summary**
This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage framework: first, a single-pass streaming algorithm builds a summary set `S` that is robust to the removal of up to `m` elements. Second, after an arbitrary set `E` of `m` elements is removed from the universe, a standard greedy algorithm can be run on the remaining summary `S \ E` to find a `k`-sized solution. The proposed streaming algorithm, STAR-T, is based on a novel partitioned structure where elements are added to buckets based on exponentially decreasing thresholds. This approach extends the thresholding idea from the established SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8] and adapts a partitioning structure reminiscent of non-streaming robust methods like PRO [11]. The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the solution obtained in the second stage, using a summary set of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The authors also provide experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, comparing their method to a non-robust streaming baseline.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis provides the first constant-factor approximation guarantee for this specific robust streaming problem with a memory footprint that is polylogarithmic in `k`. The proof sketch appears plausible, breaking the analysis down into different cases based on the state of the buckets in the summary. This is a complete piece of work with a non-trivial theoretical contribution.

However, there are weaknesses in the connection between theory and practice, and in the experimental validation.
1.  **Discrepancy in Parameter Settings:** The theory requires the memory parameter `w` to be at least `⌈4 dlog k em / k⌉` to achieve the approximation guarantee. In the experiments, however, the authors use `w=1`, stating that it "works well in practice". This is a significant disconnect. While it is common for theoretical bounds to be loose, this gap raises questions about whether the theoretical analysis truly captures the mechanism behind the algorithm's empirical success, or if the good performance is due to other factors not covered by the theory.
2.  **Handling of `τ`:** The algorithm relies on a threshold parameter `τ` that is a function of the unknown optimal value `f(OPT(k, V \ E))`. The proposed solution is to run multiple instances in parallel, which is a standard technique but increases space complexity by a `log` factor. The experimental section does not specify how `τ` was set. Was this parallel-instance method used? Or was `τ` tuned as a hyperparameter? This lack of detail hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the practical performance of the proposed method as described.
3.  **Experimental Rigor:** The choice of baseline is a major concern. The authors compare their robust algorithm against SIEVE-STREAMING [8], a non-robust algorithm, by giving it an oracle (i.e., knowledge of the removed set `E` in advance). While this is a creative way to construct a strong baseline, it is not a direct comparison against an established method for the *same problem*. The concurrent work of Mirzasoleiman et al. [18] provides a direct competitor for robust streaming submodular maximization. A comparison against their method, or another baseline actually designed for robustness, would be necessary for a thorough validation. Without it, the claim of "matching or outperforming" is less impactful.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The core idea of partitioned thresholding is explained clearly, and Figure 1 provides a helpful illustration of the summary structure. The problem statement and the two-stage procedure are well-defined. The motivation for the problem is strong and supported by relevant examples.

**Originality:** The proposed algorithm, STAR-T, is novel. It effectively combines two established lines of work: the greedy thresholding from streaming submodular maximization [8] and the partitioned/bucketed structure from non-streaming robust submodular maximization [11]. The analysis required to prove an approximation guarantee for this new combination appears to be a significant and original contribution. The paper is clear about its relationship to prior work, including the concurrent work [18], and correctly highlights its primary advantage over that work (logarithmic vs. linear dependence of memory on `k`).

**Significance:** The problem of robust data summarization in a streaming context is of high practical importance. As datasets grow and real-time processing becomes more common, the need for single-pass algorithms that can handle uncertainty or adversarial removals is pressing. The paper's contribution of a theoretically-grounded algorithm with low memory requirements is therefore significant. The logarithmic dependence of memory on `k` is a notable achievement that could make this approach scalable to problems with large `k`.

However, the significance of the practical results is tempered by the aforementioned weaknesses in the experimental evaluation. The approximation constant of 0.149 is also quite low, which may limit its appeal in practice, though obtaining any constant guarantee is a valuable theoretical first step.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-motivated and important problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization.
*   Presents a novel and elegant algorithm (STAR-T) that combines ideas from established methods in a non-trivial way.
*   Provides the first constant-factor approximation guarantee for this problem with a memory complexity that is polylogarithmic in the solution size `k`, a significant theoretical improvement over concurrent work.
*   The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The empirical validation is insufficient. The primary baseline is not a true competitor for the same task, and a comparison against the most relevant related work [18] is missing.
*   There is a significant gap between the parameter settings required by the theory (`w`) and those used in the experiments, casting doubt on the practical relevance of the theoretical analysis.
*   Key experimental details, such as the setting of the crucial parameter `τ`, are omitted, which undermines the reproducibility and credibility of the results.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a strong theoretical contribution to an important problem. The algorithmic idea is clever and the analysis appears sound. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes rigorous validation against established methods, I find the experimental section to be unconvincing. The lack of comparison to a proper robust streaming baseline and the disconnect between the theoretical requirements and the practical implementation weaken the paper's claims. While the theoretical novelty is noteworthy, the work in its current form does not provide sufficient evidence to be endorsed as a practically superior method. Therefore, I lean towards rejection, though I would encourage the authors to resubmit with a more thorough and rigorous empirical evaluation.
