## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where the goal is to maintain a representative subset of data that remains useful even after a certain number of elements are removed. The authors propose a two-stage procedure, STAR-T, which involves a streaming algorithm to build a robust summary, followed by a greedy algorithm applied to the summary in response to removal queries.  The core contribution lies in a partitioned thresholding approach during the streaming phase, designed to ensure robustness and achieve a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The paper also presents experimental results validating the approach on both a synthetic dominating set problem and a personalized movie recommendation task, demonstrating competitive performance against existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Setting:** The problem of robust streaming submodular maximization is well-motivated and relevant to several practical applications, especially in scenarios with large datasets and potential data failures or evolving user preferences.
* **Technical Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach appears to be a novel and insightful way to structure the summary set for robustness. The combination of exponentially decreasing thresholds with the partitioning scheme is a key technical contribution.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The paper provides a theoretical analysis guaranteeing a constant-factor approximation, which is crucial for establishing the quality of the solution.  The analysis appears thorough and addresses the complexities of the streaming and query stages.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling. Demonstrating performance comparable to (and in some cases exceeding) SIEVE-STREAMING, *even when SIEVE-STREAMING is given foreknowledge of removals*, is a strong point. The experiments cover multiple relevant applications.
* **Interactive aspect:** The paper highlights the utility of precomputing a single robust summary for multiple removal scenarios.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible. The algorithm descriptions are clear, and the notation is reasonably consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Constants:** While the theoretical guarantee is a constant factor approximation, the constant itself (0.149) is relatively small and depends on `dlog ke`, potentially limiting the practical performance in certain cases.  The impact of this constant on real-world applications is not thoroughly discussed.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the memory parameter `w` seems critical. While the authors provide a theoretical lower bound, the experiments suggest that `w=1` performs well, raising questions about the tightness of the theoretical bound. A more detailed sensitivity analysis of `w` would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** Although the comparison to SIEVE-STREAMING is favorable, a more comprehensive comparison against other relevant streaming submodular maximization algorithms beyond SIEVE-STREAMING would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Space Complexity:**  The space complexity of O((k + m log k) log k) is reasonable, but the `log k` factor could become significant for very large values of `k`.  Exploring techniques to mitigate this, even at the cost of slightly weaker guarantees, could be valuable.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theory relies on the assumption of a normalized monotone submodular function.  Discussing the implications of relaxing these assumptions, or demonstrating robustness to slight violations, would improve the generalizability of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to robust streaming submodular maximization. The problem setting is important, the technical contributions are significant, and the empirical results are encouraging. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is somewhat small and the parameter sensitivity requires further investigation, these are not major flaws.  

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and challenging problem.
* Introduces a novel and well-motivated algorithmic approach.
* Provides a solid theoretical analysis with approximation guarantees.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance.
* Contributions align well with the scope of NIPS.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
* Parameter sensitivity could limit practical applicability.
* Comparison to a limited set of baselines.



**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept**.  I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is suitable for presentation at NIPS. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the work has the potential to impact research in areas such as data summarization, influence maximization, and recommender systems.  I would suggest that the authors address the reviewer comments regarding the constant factor, parameter sensitivity, and baseline comparison in a revised version of the manuscript.
