

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper introduces a novel two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors propose a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary of size O((k + m log k) log² k) in one pass, followed by a simple greedy query stage (STAR-T-GREEDY) that handles any removal of up to m elements from the summary. The key novelty lies in their partitioned thresholding approach, where elements are assigned to buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds, creating a structure that enables robustness without requiring reoptimization after removals.

The paper makes a strong contribution by extending submodular streaming techniques to the robust setting, which is particularly valuable for applications like social network influence maximization and personalized recommendations where element removals are common. The authors demonstrate how their approach outperforms or matches SIEVE-STREAMING (a state-of-the-art streaming submodular maximization algorithm) even when SIEVE-STREAMING knows in advance which elements will be removed.

The theoretical results are solid, with Theorem 4.1 establishing a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for the robust streaming problem. While the approximation factor is modest compared to standard submodular maximization (1-1/e ≈ 0.632), this is expected due to the additional robustness requirement. The authors provide a clear proof sketch and address the challenge of unknown f(OPT(k, V\E)) through a clever parallelization approach (Theorem 4.5).

The experimental validation is compelling, with two distinct applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) showing competitive performance against strong baselines. The movie recommendation experiments are particularly convincing, demonstrating that the same robust summary can handle multiple different removal scenarios, which is a practical advantage not present in standard streaming approaches.

What makes this paper stand out is its bold approach to a challenging problem. While previous work on robust submodular maximization focused on centralized settings ([9], [10], [11]), this paper successfully adapts these concepts to the streaming setting with a novel algorithmic structure. The partitioned thresholding approach represents a creative solution to the memory-approximation trade-off in robust settings, and the theoretical analysis elegantly combines properties of the robust structure with the decreasing thresholding rule.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization. The authors carefully position their work relative to related literature, including the concurrent work [18], and clearly articulate their theoretical advantages (logarithmic in k vs. linear for [18]).

The main weakness is that the approximation factor (0.149) is relatively low compared to standard submodular maximization, but this is a necessary trade-off for the robustness requirement. The paper acknowledges this limitation and provides a clear theoretical explanation for it.

This work represents a significant step forward in the field of streaming submodular optimization, addressing a practically important problem with a novel algorithmic approach. The two-stage framework provides a practical solution to a real-world challenge (handling element removals without reprocessing the entire dataset), and the theoretical guarantees are sound for the problem setting.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be a strong contribution to the field. The partitioned thresholding approach is genuinely novel, the problem is well-motivated, and the results are compelling. The paper doesn't just incrementally improve upon existing streaming submodular maximization techniques—it fundamentally addresses a new dimension of the problem (robustness) with a creative solution.

I believe this paper would be of significant interest to the NIPS community, particularly to researchers working on streaming algorithms, submodular optimization, and practical applications of these techniques. The work opens up new avenues for research in robust streaming optimization and has clear practical implications for large-scale machine learning applications.

**Recommendation**: Accept. This paper presents a novel, bold approach to a challenging problem with strong theoretical guarantees and compelling experimental results. The contribution is significant and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature.
