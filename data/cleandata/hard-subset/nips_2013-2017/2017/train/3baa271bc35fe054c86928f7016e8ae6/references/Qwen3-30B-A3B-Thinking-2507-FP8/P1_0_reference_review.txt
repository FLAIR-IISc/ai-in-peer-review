

# Peer Review: "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where the goal is to create a small representative summary that remains effective even after the removal of up to m elements. The authors propose a two-stage procedure: (1) a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary S of size O((m log k + k) log² k) in a single pass, and (2) a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that, given any removed set E of size ≤ m, returns a solution of size ≤ k with a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The key innovation is a partitioned thresholding approach where the summary is structured into partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing thresholds.

The work builds upon SIEVE-STREAMING [8] for non-robust submodular maximization and extends it to handle robustness requirements. It relates to prior robust submodular maximization work [9, 10, 11] but provides a streaming algorithm with better memory complexity (O((m log k + k) log² k) vs. O(mk log k) in [18]).

## Strengths

1. **Novel Algorithmic Approach**: The partitioned thresholding structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a clever and well-motivated extension of SIEVE-STREAMING to the robust setting. This approach elegantly balances the need for robustness with space efficiency.

2. **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis showing a constant-factor approximation guarantee (c ≈ 0.149). The proof is carefully structured, handling multiple cases through well-crafted lemmas (4.2-4.4), which demonstrates strong technical depth.

3. **Practical Validation**: The experimental section convincingly demonstrates that the proposed method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed) in two practical applications: dominating set and movie recommendation. This is particularly impressive given that SIEVE-STREAMING has the advantage of knowing the removals in advance.

4. **Clear Differentiation from Related Work**: The paper effectively distinguishes its contributions from [18] (which has linear dependence on k in memory requirements) and other robust submodular maximization approaches.

5. **Practical Implementation Insight**: The authors demonstrate that their method is more resilient in practice than the theoretical bounds would suggest, as evidenced by successful performance even when |E| >> k.

## Weaknesses

1. **Approximation Factor**: The approximation factor (0.149) is significantly lower than the (0.5-ε) achieved by SIEVE-STREAMING for the non-robust case. While the paper acknowledges this trade-off for robustness, it would be helpful to have a more detailed discussion of this trade-off and whether it's acceptable for the applications they target.

2. **Practical Memory Complexity**: The memory complexity O((m log k + k) log² k) is slightly higher than SIEVE-STREAMING's O(k log k), though better than [18]'s O(mk log k). The paper could better contextualize this trade-off for practitioners.

3. **Handling Unknown τ**: While the paper addresses the issue of not knowing f(OPT(k, V\E)) through a parallel variant, this increases memory by log^(1+ε) k. The paper could provide more intuition about when this parallelization is necessary in practice.

4. **Limited Comparison with Other Robust Methods**: The experimental comparison is primarily against SIEVE-STREAMING, but there are other robust submodular maximization approaches (like those in [11]) that could provide additional context for the performance claims.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The experimental validation is thorough and relevant. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the approximation factor trade-off).

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise, and the theoretical results are logically presented. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality**: The partitioned thresholding approach for robust streaming submodular maximization represents a genuine novelty. While the paper builds on SIEVE-STREAMING, the extension to robustness with the specific thresholding scheme is original and non-trivial.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is highly relevant for real-world applications where data may be subject to removals (e.g., network failures, user preferences). The two-stage approach with a precomputed robust summary that enables multiple queries is particularly valuable for practical implementation.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of streaming submodular maximization by addressing the important practical challenge of robustness. The theoretical results are solid, the experimental validation is convincing, and the approach has clear practical value.

The main concern is the approximation factor being substantially lower than non-robust methods, but this is a necessary trade-off for robustness. The authors appropriately acknowledge this trade-off and demonstrate that their approach performs well in practice despite this theoretical limitation.

The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and makes a significant contribution to the field. It is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses a meaningful problem with practical implications.

## Final Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a novel and well-justified approach to robust streaming submodular maximization with strong theoretical guarantees and compelling empirical results. The contribution is significant, and the paper is well-written with appropriate context and differentiation from related work. The minor weaknesses identified do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.
