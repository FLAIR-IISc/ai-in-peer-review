Summary
This paper tackles robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting under a cardinality constraint. The authors propose a two-stage approach: (i) STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm that maintains a “partitioned” summary S composed of exponentially increasing buckets with exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds; and (ii) a query stage that runs GREEDY on S\E to produce a k-set after the adversary removes up to m elements. The main theorem claims a constant-factor approximation (c ≈ 0.149 as k→∞) with space O((k + m log k) log k) under knowledge of a threshold τ depending on OPT, and a multi-guess variant (à la Sieve-Streaming) that removes the need to know OPT at the cost of an extra log factor. Empirically, on dominating set and movie recommendation, the method is competitive with Sieve-Streaming (sometimes slightly better), even when Sieve-Streaming is given the deleted set in advance.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and increasingly relevant problem (robust streaming submodular maximization), with clear applications.
- Algorithmic structure is simple and implementable: partitioned buckets with thresholds; query-time GREEDY on S\E.
- Theoretical guarantee is constant factor and memory dependence on m is polylogarithmic in k rather than linear in k (contrast with some prior work); single-pass streaming.
- Empirical validation on two tasks, including an interactive recommendation scenario that matches the motivation.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty and positioning: The algorithmic ideas are very close to a combination of PRO (non-uniform partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes) and Sieve-Streaming (thresholding). The specific “partitioned thresholding” scheme is incremental; it is not fully clear how fundamentally new it is versus combining existing templates, nor how essential the exponentially decaying thresholds are beyond tuning constants.
- Weak constant and missing baselines: The proved factor (≈0.149) is substantially below the 1/2−ε bound of the concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [18]. There is no empirical comparison to [18] or to strong centralized robust baselines (e.g., PRO) in an offline setting to understand practical trade-offs. Given the conservative evaluation stance, this is a significant omission.
- Clarity/inconsistencies:
  - Space bound is inconsistent: the abstract/conclusion state O((k + m log k) log^2 k), whereas Theorem 4.1 and Eq. (3) give O((k + m log k) log k). Which is correct? The variant with parallel guesses (Thm 4.5) adds another log factor; overall bounds should be stated consistently and prominently.
  - Threshold rule: earlier text says partition i uses threshold τ/2^i; Algorithm 1 line 5 uses τ/min{2^i,k}, which differs when k<2^i. Please reconcile.
  - τ in Theorem 4.1 looks malformed: τ = 1 + (1−e−1)(1−e−1/3)(1−1/dlog k) f(OPT(…)). Is there a missing division? The intended scaling must be clarified.
- Assumption on τ and practical overhead: STAR-T depends on an α-approximation to OPT; removing this assumption requires maintaining multiple parallel instances, increasing space by a log factor. There is no runtime/space-overhead analysis or sensitivity to ε. How is τ estimated in practice? Experiments do not discuss this.
- Robustness parameter vs practice: The theory requires w ≥ ceil(4 m log k / k). For m ≈ k, w is Ω(log k), making S fairly large. Experiments use w=1 and remove up to 2k elements, well beyond the theoretical regime, yet claim good performance. While encouraging, this makes the empirical section less supportive of the stated guarantees.
- Experimental methodology and coverage:
  - In dominating set, E is sampled from S (the summary) rather than from V, which biases the experiment against the proposed method (authors note this), but also makes it hard to compare fairly with realistic use where deletions come from V. Please report both settings.
  - No wall-clock, memory usage, or query-time cost comparisons are given. Query-time GREEDY on S\E can be O(k|S|), which may be nontrivial for large S.
  - No sensitivity to m, w, τ/ε; limited datasets; no adversarial removals beyond a simple greedy heuristic; no ablations to justify partitioning and threshold schedule.
- Proof sketch gaps: Some arguments (e.g., existence of a bucket with small intersection with E) rely on averaging/pigeonhole effects across w buckets. This needs to be precisely stated (adversarial E, worst-case bounds) and tied cleanly to the choice of w. As written, several steps are deferred to the supplement and the sketch is hard to verify.

Quality
- The high-level analysis direction is plausible. However, the constant is weak relative to concurrent work, and several key statements are unclear or inconsistent (τ, thresholds, space). The experiments are not comprehensive and omit strong baselines and runtime/memory reporting.

Clarity
- Writing is generally readable, but the noted inconsistencies and typos hinder reproducibility. Algorithm 1’s threshold rule and Theorem 4.1’s τ definition and space bounds must be fixed. Provide explicit pseudocode and complexity for the multi-guess variant.

Originality
- The combination of non-uniform partitioning (as in PRO) with streaming thresholding (as in Sieve-Streaming) is incremental. The particular “partitioned thresholding” and its analysis are contributions, but the conceptual novelty is modest.

Significance
- The problem is significant. A one-pass, deletion-robust streaming method with near-constant-memory-per-m is useful. However, given the weak factor, missing comparisons to the 1/2−ε concurrent method, and limited empirical validation, the current impact is uncertain.

Questions for authors
1. Clarify the space bound (log k vs log^2 k) and the exact τ definition. Reconcile threshold definitions (τ/2^i vs τ/min{2^i,k}).
2. Provide runtime and memory breakdowns (including the multi-guess variant) and query-time costs.
3. Compare empirically to [18] (deletion-robust streaming) and, if possible, to offline robust baselines.
4. Report sensitivity to m, w, and ε, and results when E⊆V is chosen adversarially/randomly, not only from S.
5. Can the constant be improved? Any adaptivity that avoids maintaining many guesses?

Pros
- Addresses an important robust streaming setting
- Simple, implementable algorithm with a constant-factor guarantee
- Memory scaling in m does not depend linearly on k
- Some positive empirical results on two tasks

Cons
- Incremental novelty; weak constant vs concurrent work
- Inconsistent statements and unclear threshold/τ definitions
- Limited experiments; missing strong baselines; no runtime/memory reporting
- Theory–practice mismatch on w and m; reliance on unknown OPT

Recommendation: Weak reject. With clarified analysis, stronger/more comprehensive empirical comparisons (especially to [18]), and cleaned-up presentation, this could become a solid contribution.
