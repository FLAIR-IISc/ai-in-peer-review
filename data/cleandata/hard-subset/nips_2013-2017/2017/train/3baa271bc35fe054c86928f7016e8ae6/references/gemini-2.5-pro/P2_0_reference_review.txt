### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a novel two-stage framework: (1) a streaming stage where a single pass is made over the data to create a robust summary `S`, and (2) a query stage where, after an adversary removes up to `m` elements `E` from `S`, a near-optimal `k`-sized solution is found from the remaining elements `S \ E` without accessing the original data stream.

The core contribution is the STAR-T algorithm for the streaming stage. It uses a novel structure of partitions and buckets, where partitions have exponentially decreasing quality thresholds and buckets within them have exponentially increasing capacity. This allows the algorithm to capture both high-value individual elements and diverse sets of elements. For the query stage, the paper proposes simply running the standard GREEDY algorithm on the remaining summary.

The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation guarantee (approaching 0.149) for this two-stage procedure, while requiring the summary `S` to have a size of `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The work builds upon ideas from streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) and centralized robust submodular maximization, but its combination into a robust *streaming* algorithm with this specific partitioned structure and analysis is new. The authors validate their approach on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, showing it performs comparably or better than an oracle version of SIEVE-STREAMING that knows the removed elements in advance.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis provides a formal, constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is the gold standard for this class of problems. The proof sketch, while dense, follows a logical progression of handling different cases (partitions being mostly full vs. mostly not full) and balancing the resulting bounds. The memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)` is a significant result, particularly its logarithmic dependence on `k`, which compares favorably to the linear dependence in concurrent work [18]. The experimental evaluation is strong and well-designed. Comparing against SIEVE-STREAMING with foreknowledge of the removed set `E` is a powerful baseline that effectively demonstrates the utility of the pre-computed robust summary.

*   **Weaknesses:** The derived approximation constant of 0.149 is modest. While achieving any constant factor is a significant theoretical hurdle, the practical implication of this specific value is not discussed. It is unclear if this is a loose bound from the analysis or reflects the algorithm's actual worst-case performance. Additionally, there is a notable gap between the theoretical requirement for the memory parameter `w` (`w >= ⌈4 dlog k e m / k⌉`) and the empirical finding that `w=1` "works well in practice". A deeper discussion or an ablation study on the effect of `w` would strengthen the paper by bridging this theory-practice gap.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but could be improved.

*   **Strengths:** The problem statement is exceptionally clear, and the motivation for the two-stage robust streaming setting is compelling and well-illustrated with examples. The high-level description of the STAR-T algorithm and its partitioned structure (nicely illustrated in Figure 1, which is unfortunately not included in the provided text but is referenced) is intuitive. The experimental section is clear and the results are easy to interpret.

*   **Weaknesses:** The main text's presentation of the theoretical analysis is very dense. While space is limited, more intuition behind the case analysis (Lemmas 4.2 vs. 4.3 & 4.4) would greatly aid the reader. For instance, a sentence explaining *why* the analysis splits based on whether a partition has more than half its buckets full would be helpful. The jump from the individual lemmas to the final balanced bound in Equation (5) is also quite abrupt. A minor point: the threshold in Algorithm 1, line 5, is `τ/min{2^i, k}`. While consistent with the bucket size constraint, it might be slightly more elegant to use `τ/2^i` as described in the text, since `min{2^i, k}` only differs from `2^i` for the very last partitions where `2^i > k`.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions.

*   **Strengths:** The two-stage problem formulation for *streaming* robust submodular maximization is novel and highly relevant. While robust submodular maximization and streaming submodular maximization have been studied separately, this work is among the first to successfully combine them. The STAR-T algorithm's design, with its unique partitioned structure and exponentially decreasing thresholds, is a novel and non-trivial extension of existing thresholding techniques. This structure is key to providing robustness. The analysis technique that balances multiple lower bounds on the solution quality appears novel in this context.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be influential.

*   **Strengths:** The paper addresses a practical and increasingly important problem at the intersection of large-scale data processing, robustness, and machine learning. The ability to build a small, robust summary in one pass that can then be queried repeatedly for different removal scenarios (as demonstrated in the movie recommendation experiment) is a powerful capability. Researchers working on data summarization, streaming algorithms, and robust optimization are likely to build upon these ideas. The favorable memory complexity makes the algorithm practical for settings where `k` is large but `m` is moderate, a common scenario.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Important Problem:** The paper introduces and provides a strong solution for the robust streaming submodular maximization problem, which has clear practical applications.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Results:** It provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee with a favorable (logarithmic in `k`) memory complexity, a key metric for streaming algorithms.
3.  **Novel Algorithmic Idea:** The STAR-T algorithm's partitioned thresholding structure is an elegant and effective new technique.
4.  **Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is well-executed against strong baselines, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Modest Approximation Constant:** The theoretical guarantee of 0.149, while a constant, is low, and the paper does not discuss its tightness.
2.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The discrepancy between the required value of the memory parameter `w` in theory and the value used in practice could be better explained.
3.  **Clarity of Analysis:** The proof sketch in the main paper is difficult to follow without significant effort, and could benefit from more intuition.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to the field of submodular optimization. It tackles a novel and important problem with a technically sound and original approach. The strengths—a well-motivated problem, a novel algorithm, a constant-factor guarantee with good memory complexity, and strong empirical validation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the clarity of the theoretical exposition and the tightness of the derived bounds. The paper is a solid contribution and should be accepted at NIPS.
