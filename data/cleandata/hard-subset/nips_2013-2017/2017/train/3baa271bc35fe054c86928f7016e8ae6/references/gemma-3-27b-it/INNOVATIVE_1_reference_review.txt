This paper tackles the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The core idea – using a partitioned thresholding approach – is quite clever and represents a genuinely novel contribution. The authors convincingly argue the motivation for robustness in data summarization and successfully extend existing streaming submodular maximization techniques to address this challenge. While the theoretical analysis appears intricate and the experimental validation, while comprehensive, doesn't definitively *outperform* existing methods in all cases, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these minor shortcomings. I believe this paper pushes the field forward and warrants acceptance.

**Summary:** The paper introduces STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm for robust streaming submodular maximization. The streaming stage builds a partitioned summary with decreasing thresholds, and the query stage uses a greedy algorithm on this summary to handle element removals. The authors provide theoretical guarantees and empirical validation on several datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach to robust streaming submodular maximization is a significant contribution. It’s not a simple extension of existing techniques but a well-thought-out modification.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a theoretical approximation ratio, which is crucial for establishing the quality of the solution. The analysis, while complex, appears sound.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is relevant to numerous real-world applications (summarization, recommendation, influence maximization), and the streaming setting is essential for handling large-scale data.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors perform extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing their algorithm against relevant baselines. The inclusion of both dominating set and recommendation applications is a plus.
*   **Interactive Nature:** The ability to efficiently generate sub-summaries after removals is a compelling feature, opening doors to interactive applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Constant:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149) is relatively small, though this is a common issue in submodular optimization. The dependence on  `1/dlog ke` further diminishes the practical guarantee as `k` grows.
*   **Experimental Results:** While comprehensive, the experimental results mostly *match* the performance of SIEVE-STREAMING, rather than consistently surpassing it.  The argument that SIEVE-STREAMING benefits from knowing the removals *a priori* is valid, but a more substantial performance gain would strengthen the paper.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the memory parameter *w* seems somewhat ad-hoc. More analysis on the impact of *w* on performance and theoretical guarantees would be helpful.
*   **Clarity of Analysis:**  While the authors claim to provide a detailed proof, some parts of the analysis appear dense and might be difficult for readers unfamiliar with the field to fully grasp. More intuitive explanations or intermediate steps could improve readability.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   The theoretical guarantees, while not perfect, establish a foundation for understanding the algorithm's performance.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical viability of the approach.
*   The potential impact of this work on applications like data summarization and recommendation systems is significant.
*   The authors have clearly articulated the problem, their solution, and its limitations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The approximation guarantee has a small constant factor.
*   The experimental results do not decisively outperform existing methods.
*   The analysis could be more accessible to a broader audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses.  The novel approach, theoretical analysis, and practical relevance make it a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. While the performance gains in the experiments are modest, the ability to achieve comparable performance to SIEVE-STREAMING *without* prior knowledge of removals is a notable achievement. I recommend acceptance, with a minor request for the authors to consider clarifying the analysis and potentially exploring more extensive parameter tuning in the experiments.
