Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in the streaming setting. It proposes a two-stage procedure: (i) a one-pass streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a compact, deletion-robust summary S using a partitioned bucket structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds; (ii) a query-time algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that, after an arbitrary removal of up to m elements, runs GREEDY on S\E to output a size-k solution. Theoretical guarantees include a constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149 (as k grows) and space O((k + m log k) log k) when OPT is known, or O((k + m log k) log^2 k) with a standard parallel-guessing scheme when OPT is unknown. The key novelty is the partitioned thresholding scheme (threshold τ/2^i across partitions and exponentially growing bucket sizes), which blends ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING (thresholding in the non-robust setting) with structural insights used in robust non-streaming methods such as PRO.

Relative to prior work: SIEVE-STREAMING achieves 0.5−ε for non-robust streaming. Robust non-streaming (e.g., PRO) uses partitions/buckets but needs multiple passes over data. Concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [18] achieves 1/2−ε but with space O(mk log k/ε), linear in k; STAR-T trades a smaller approximation constant for much better dependence on k (logarithmic), which can be critical at large k. Empirical studies on dominating-set and recommendation tasks show performance comparable to SIEVE-STREAMING that is allowed to know deletions in advance.

Quality:
- Theory: The main theorem and lemmas are plausible and technically careful. The analysis considers two complementary cases (many full buckets vs. none) and balances bounds to obtain the constant factor, while rigorously controlling memory via w ≥ ⌈4 m log k / k⌉. The extension to unknown OPT via parallel guessing is standard and sound. One minor concern is consistency around thresholds: the text describes thresholds τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i,k}. This should be clarified (likely equivalent for i with 2^i ≤ k).
- Empirics: The experiments are relevant and indicate that the method is competitive with strong baselines. However, they are somewhat limited: (i) no direct comparison to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming algorithm [18]; (ii) limited exploration of the robustness parameter m and the memory parameter w (w is set to 1 in practice, and removals > k are considered, but systematic sweeps of m/k and sensitivity analyses would strengthen claims); (iii) no explicit reporting of memory and runtime at stream and query time; (iv) the parallel-guessing variant (unknown OPT) appears not to be evaluated.
- Reproducibility: Datasets and objectives are standard; more details (or code) would help reproduce the exact configurations and deletion protocols.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized, with intuitive explanations of the structure and analysis. The proof sketch is helpful; full proofs are deferred to the supplement.
- Minor inconsistencies to fix: thresholds (τ/2^i vs τ/min{2^i,k}); occasionally O((k+m log k) log k) vs O((k+m log k) log^2 k)—the latter appears to be for the unknown-OPT parallel variant as formalized in Theorem 4.5, so spell out this distinction early to avoid confusion.
- Some typos and formatting glitches (e.g., “st Conference …”, “peformance”), but nothing that impedes comprehension.

Originality:
- The partitioned, exponentially decreasing thresholding and the robust streaming analysis appear novel. The work clearly differentiates itself from SIEVE-STREAMING and PRO, and positions its trade-offs cleanly against concurrent streaming-robust results (better space dependence on k, worse constant).

Significance:
- Robust summarization with streaming constraints is important in large-scale ML systems where re-accessing data is infeasible and deletions can be adversarial. The memory improvement (logarithmic in k) is practically meaningful. While the theoretical constant is modest, the empirical results suggest the approach is competitive and the ideas are likely to influence subsequent work.

Pros:
- Conceptually clean algorithm with a principled partitioned thresholding design.
- Single pass; strong space bounds with attractive dependence on k.
- Constant-factor approximation under adversarial deletions; practical performance competitive with informed baselines.
- Clear relation to and extension of established streaming and robust submodular methods.

Cons:
- Approximation constant (≈0.149) is low compared to 1/2−ε in concurrent work; the practical trade-off vs. memory should be more thoroughly quantified.
- Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive (systematic m, w sensitivity; runtime/memory reporting; comparison to [18]).
- Minor clarity inconsistencies around thresholds and big-O factors.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the threshold expression in Algorithm 1 and its equivalence to τ/2^i; unify the big-O space claims (separate known-OPT vs unknown-OPT cases upfront).
- Include empirical memory and runtime, and a sensitivity study over m/k and w.
- If possible, add a comparison (theoretical or empirical) to the concurrent 1/2−ε method to better highlight the space–approximation trade-off.
- Provide details on the parallel-guessing implementation used in experiments (or explain why a single τ sufficed).

Overall recommendation:
Lean accept. The paper is technically solid, addresses a timely and practically important problem with a novel algorithmic structure, and offers an attractive memory–accuracy trade-off for robust streaming submodular maximization. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying minor inconsistencies would further improve the paper.
