

# Peer Review: "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary
This paper introduces a two-stage algorithm for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. The authors propose a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that builds a robust summary set S of size O((k + m log k) log k) in a single pass, followed by a query stage where a greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) runs on S \ E to produce a solution after any removal of up to m elements. The key innovation is a partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing thresholds. The authors prove a constant-factor approximation guarantee (c = 0.149(1-1/log k)).

## Strengths
1. **Problem Relevance**: The problem of robust submodular maximization is highly relevant to real-world applications where data may be corrupted or removed (e.g., social network influence maximization, recommendation systems).

2. **Technical Soundness**: The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with detailed proofs in the supplementary material. The partitioned thresholding approach is novel and well-justified through careful analysis of bucket properties and marginal gains.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks demonstrate practical utility, showing that their method performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows removals in advance) despite not having this information.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Low Approximation Guarantee**: The theoretical approximation factor (0.149) is significantly lower than standard submodular maximization algorithms (e.g., 1-1/e ≈ 0.632 for greedy). This limits the practical appeal of the method, especially when compared to non-robust streaming algorithms that achieve 0.5-ε approximations with similar space complexity.

2. **Practicality of Threshold Parameter**: The algorithm requires knowing f(OPT(k, V\E)), which is not feasible in practice. While the authors propose a workaround using multiple parallel instances, this increases space complexity by a factor of log(1+ε) k, making the practical space complexity O((k + m log k) log k log(1+ε) k), which is higher than the theoretical bound.

3. **Inadequate Comparison with Concurrent Work**: The paper mentions Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017) as concurrent work but fails to provide a thorough comparison. Their algorithm achieves a 1/2 - ε approximation with O(mk log k/ε) memory, while the authors claim O((k + m log k) log k) memory. The paper should clarify the trade-offs between these approaches, especially since the memory complexity of Mirzasoleiman's method depends linearly on k (while the authors' depends logarithmically).

4. **Experimental Setup Limitations**: The experiments compare against SIEVE-STREAMING knowing removals in advance, but the authors don't test against a more realistic baseline where SIEVE-STREAMING must also handle removals without prior knowledge. This makes it difficult to assess the true practical advantage of their approach.

## Technical Concerns

The proof of Theorem 4.1 relies on balancing two cases (full buckets vs. not-full buckets), but the constant 0.149 appears somewhat arbitrary and derived from the specific balancing point. The paper doesn't explore whether this constant can be improved with different threshold parameters or partitioning schemes.

The analysis assumes normalized monotone submodular functions, but the paper doesn't address how the algorithm would perform with non-normalized functions, which might be common in practice.

## Significance Assessment

While the problem is significant, the low approximation factor and the impractical threshold requirement significantly limit the paper's impact. The two-stage approach is conceptually appealing for applications where precomputation is feasible but reoptimization after removals is not, but the practical utility is questionable given the weak theoretical guarantees.

The paper builds well on existing work in streaming submodular maximization (SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular maximization, but doesn't clearly distinguish how their approach advances the state of the art beyond the concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al.

## Recommendations

1. **Clarify the Trade-offs**: Provide a more detailed comparison with Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017), including explicit trade-offs between approximation factor, memory complexity, and practical applicability.

2. **Improve Practicality**: Explore whether the threshold parameter τ can be estimated more effectively without requiring multiple parallel instances, or develop a method that avoids this parameter altogether.

3. **Strengthen Experiments**: Include experiments comparing against a more realistic baseline where SIEVE-STREAMING must handle removals without knowing them in advance.

4. **Theoretical Improvement**: Investigate whether the approximation factor can be improved beyond 0.149, or at least provide intuition for why this constant appears in the analysis.

## Conclusion

This paper presents a theoretically sound approach to robust streaming submodular maximization with a novel partitioned thresholding structure. While the problem is important and the technical analysis is solid, the low approximation factor (0.149) and impractical threshold requirement significantly limit its potential impact. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these concerns and providing a clearer comparison with the concurrent work in the field.

I recommend rejection, as the paper does not sufficiently advance the state of the art compared to existing methods. The practical limitations outweigh the theoretical novelty, especially given the availability of a concurrent approach with better approximation guarantees. However, with the suggested improvements, this could be a strong contribution to the field.
