Summary
The paper studies robust submodular maximization in the streaming model, where one must construct a compact summary in a single pass such that, after the adversarial removal of up to m elements, a good size-k solution can be extracted from the summary without revisiting the stream. The authors propose STAR-T, which organizes stored elements into exponentially sized partitions and buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds; at query time, GREEDY is run on S\E. The main theorem gives a constant-factor approximation (c ≈ 0.149·(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉)) using space O(k log k + m log^2 k) per τ-guess (or with a log1+ε k factor when τ is unknown). Experiments on dominating set and MovieLens suggest performance matches or sometimes outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter is allowed to know the removals in advance.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practically relevant variant (robustness to deletions) in the challenging streaming setting.
- One-pass algorithm with a simple structure (partitioned buckets with thresholds) and an intuitive query stage.
- Nontrivial analysis yielding a provable constant-factor approximation with space that scales only logarithmically in k (vs. linear in k in concurrent [18]).
- The summary can serve multiple deletion queries without reprocessing the stream, aligning with interactive use cases.
- Empirical results show competitive performance with a strong streaming baseline.

Weaknesses
- The approximation constant (≈0.149) is rather weak compared to 1/2 − ε (concurrent [18]); the paper argues better memory, but the tradeoff is not quantified empirically.
- Heavy reliance on a threshold τ that depends on f(OPT). The practical multi-instance mechanism and its overhead are not evaluated; it can add a log1+ε k factor in space and engineering complexity.
- Clarity/consistency issues:
  • Space bound is stated inconsistently: O((k + m log k) log k) in the theorem derivation vs. O((k + m log k) log^2 k) in the abstract/conclusion.
  • Algorithm description says thresholds τ/2^i; Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}—this discrepancy should be reconciled and its effect on analysis clarified.
  • The formula defining τ in Theorem 4.1 is hard to parse (typesetting/parentheses).
- Experimental evaluation is limited and omits critical baselines:
  • No comparison to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18] (even if concurrent, the authors cite it and should contextualize empirically).
  • No comparison to centralized robust baselines (e.g., PRO [11]) to show the gap to offline robust methods.
  • No sensitivity studies for m, w, τ-guessing (number of instances), or approximation-vs-space tradeoffs; runtime/memory usage are not reported.
- The experiments use w = 1 while theory requires w ≥ ⌈4⌈log k⌉ m/k⌉. Several settings remove ≥ k or even 2k elements; thus the theoretical guarantees do not apply to the tested regimes. This undermines the empirical support for robustness claims.
- Empirical protocol details are missing (how τ is selected in practice; number of τ-instances; stopping/implementation details), reducing reproducibility.

Quality
The technical core is interesting and appears sound at a high level, but the constant is weak and there are a few presentation/consistency issues that need fixing. The empirical section does not adequately validate the theoretical contributions: the parameter regimes do not match the guarantees, the key overheads of the practical variant are not measured, and state-of-the-art robust streaming baselines are missing.

Clarity
Generally readable, but there are several notational and typographical glitches (threshold definition, τ formula, space bounds) that obscure the main claims. The practical algorithmic details for unknown OPT are under-specified. Figures are suggestive but lack runtime/space reporting.

Originality
Combines partitioned bucket structures (as in PRO) with streaming thresholding; the analysis for robustness with logarithmic dependence on k in memory is a novel twist. The idea is incremental relative to prior partitioning and sieve-thresholding work, but the setting (robust streaming) is important and underexplored.

Significance
If the memory–approximation tradeoff and practical implementation were more thoroughly validated, this could be a useful tool. As presented, the low constant, lack of thorough comparisons, and gaps between theory and experiments limit immediate impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important robust streaming problem with a simple, practical architecture.
- Theoretical guarantee in a challenging setting; sublinear memory with only log dependence on k.
- Empirical results indicate competitiveness to a strong streaming baseline on two tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Weak approximation constant and missing empirical evaluation of the memory–accuracy tradeoff.
- Incomplete and somewhat inconsistent presentation (thresholds, τ, space bounds).
- Experiments do not align with theoretical parameter conditions and lack critical baselines and resource reporting.
- Practical variant (unknown OPT) not evaluated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Reconcile and clearly state all space bounds; fix threshold definitions and τ expression.
- Provide a detailed practical algorithm for unknown OPT, including number of parallel instances and its effect on memory/runtime; report these in experiments.
- Compare against [18] (even if concurrent) on standard benchmarks, and include centralized robust baselines to contextualize the achievable gap.
- Include sensitivity analyses over m, w, and k; report memory and wall-clock time.
- Use parameter regimes that satisfy the theoretical conditions, or explicitly separate “beyond-guarantee” explorations.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is important and the approach promising, but the current paper lacks rigor in empirical validation (particularly vis-à-vis state-of-the-art robust streaming baselines and resource tradeoffs) and has clarity inconsistencies that should be addressed. With stronger experiments and cleaner presentation, this could become a solid contribution.

Confidence
Moderate.
