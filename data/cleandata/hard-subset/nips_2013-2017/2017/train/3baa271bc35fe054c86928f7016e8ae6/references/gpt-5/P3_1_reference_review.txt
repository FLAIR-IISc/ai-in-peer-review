Summary
The paper studies deletion-robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in the streaming model. It proposes STAR-T, a one-pass algorithm that builds a summary S arranged into exponentially sized partitions with w·⌈k/2^i⌉ buckets per partition i, and assigns each arriving element to the first non-full bucket whose marginal gain exceeds a partition-specific threshold that decreases exponentially. In the query stage, after any adversarial removal E with |E| ≤ m, STAR-T-GREEDY runs GREEDY on S\E to return a size-k set. The main theorem shows a constant-factor guarantee: f(Z) ≥ c·f(OPT(k, V\E)) with c → 0.149 as k → ∞, using space O((k + m log k) log k) for a single τ guess, and O((k + m log k) log^2 k) with the standard parallel-threshold trick to remove dependence on f(OPT). This trades a smaller approximation factor for substantially reduced memory compared to the concurrent robust streaming approach [18], which achieves ~1/2 but uses O(m k log k/ε) space. Experiments on dominating set (Twitter and Amazon graphs) and MovieLens-based recommendation show that the two-stage procedure often matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows E in advance, and competes closely with offline GREEDY in smaller settings.

Relation to prior work
- Non-streaming robust submodular maximization: Orlin–Schulz–Udwani [10] and PRO [11] achieve constant approximations with O(m^2 log k) and a partitioned structure, respectively.
- Streaming submodular maximization: SIEVE-STREAMING [8] yields ~1/2 with O(k log k) memory in the non-robust case via thresholding.
- Concurrent robust streaming [18] attains ~1/2−ε but uses O(m k log k/ε) memory.
The proposed STAR-T marries the non-uniform partition structure of PRO with streaming thresholding ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING, yielding robustness to m deletions with memory scaling O(m polylog k + k polylog k).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean partitioned, thresholded streaming design that is robust to post hoc adversarial deletions; the analysis is tailored to the partition structure.
- Strong space-approximation trade-off: Memory O(m log^2 k + k log^2 k) (with τ guessing) is substantially better than O(m k log k) in [18], especially when k is large.
- Simple query stage (GREEDY on S\E) with a constant-factor guarantee; reusability for multiple deletions without rescanning the stream.
- Empirical evaluation on two tasks with nontrivial objectives; shows that small constants can work well (e.g., w=1), and robustness appears to extend beyond proved limits in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Approximation factor: 0.149 is quite conservative relative to 0.5−ε in [18] and 0.387 in centralized robust algorithms. The paper leans on space advantages; a discussion quantifying regimes where memory is the bottleneck would be helpful.
- Time/Oracle complexity: Per-element processing potentially checks O(∑i w·k/2^i)=O(wk) buckets, which could be expensive for large k. The paper does not analyze or report per-element oracle calls or wall-clock times, nor discuss lazy/approximate marginal gains to reduce cost.
- Parameter τ and practical implementation: STAR-T requires τ that depends on f(OPT(k, V\E)) although E is unknown during streaming. Section 4.1 proposes the usual parallel thresholds, multiplying space by O(log k), but details of the range construction and how to obtain lower/upper bounds independent of E are scant, and experiments do not report the overhead of running multiple instances. Clarify whether experiments used parallel instances, and if not, how τ was set.
- Inconsistencies/clarity:
  - Thresholding rule is described as τ/2^i in the text but Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Please unify and justify the exact form; the k-cap makes sense for last partitions but should be consistent in analysis.
  - Space bounds fluctuate: Theorem 4.1 gives O((k + m log k) log k), whereas intro/conclusion state O((k + m log k) log^2 k). I believe the extra log comes only when using parallel τ; please state both clearly and tie them to the corresponding settings.
  - τ expression in Theorem 4.1 appears garbled; provide a clean, interpretable formula or inequality range for τ, and explain how it is balanced between Lemmas 4.2–4.4.
- Robustness parameter w: Theory suggests w ≥ ⌈4 m log k/k⌉, yet experiments fix w=1 even when m≥k. While this is an encouraging empirical finding, more systematic study of the sensitivity to w and m/k would strengthen claims.
- Baselines: For completeness, an empirical comparison to the concurrent robust streaming method [18] would be ideal (or, at least, a thorough discussion of expected empirical trade-offs), though the authors note concurrency.

Quality
The theoretical development is generally sound: the two-case analysis (many full buckets vs. mostly non-full) and the way E is spread across buckets via the pigeonhole principle are convincing. However, some proof details are deferred and some constants and thresholds need clearer exposition in the main text. Experimental evidence is positive but does not report speed/oracle costs and does not vary w or the τ-guessing mechanism.

Clarity
The paper is well structured, with clear motivation and good positioning. Address the noted inconsistencies (threshold formula, space bounds, τ expression). Adding a complexity section quantifying per-element oracle calls would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The partitioned thresholded streaming design is a novel and natural synthesis of PRO and SIEVE-STREAMING, with a new analysis to obtain deletion robustness with polylog(k) dependence in space.

Significance
Addresses an important and practical problem (robust summarization with limited memory). Theoretical guarantees fill a gap by providing strong memory efficiency with a constant factor. Potentially impactful for large-scale summarization systems that must support “right to be forgotten” queries without rescanning data.

Questions for authors
- Precisely specify the threshold rule (τ/2^i vs τ/min{2^i,k}) used in both algorithm and proofs.
- How is τ chosen in experiments? Did you run parallel instances, and what was the additional memory/time? What bounds did you use independent of E?
- What is the per-element number of oracle calls in practice (mean/max), and how does it scale with k and w? Any use of lazy evaluations?
- Can you report sensitivity to w and m/k, including memory–accuracy trade-offs?
- Any preliminary comparison to [18], or synthetic experiments highlighting the memory–approximation trade-off?

Pros
- Novel, principled algorithm with strong memory efficiency.
- Solid theory; practically useful two-stage design.
- Empirical results on real datasets; reusability across queries.

Cons
- Low approximation constant; unclear runtime/oracle complexity.
- Some presentation inconsistencies; τ choice in practice under-specified.
- Limited sensitivity analysis; no comparison to [18].

Overall assessment
A solid and timely contribution with a compelling space–quality trade-off for robust streaming submodular maximization. With clarifications and additional runtime/sensitivity analysis, I lean toward acceptance.
