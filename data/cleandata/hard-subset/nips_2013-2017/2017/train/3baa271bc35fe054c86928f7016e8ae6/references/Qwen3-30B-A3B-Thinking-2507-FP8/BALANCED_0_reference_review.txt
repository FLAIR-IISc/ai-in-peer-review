

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, where the goal is to create a small representative summary set S that remains useful even after up to m elements are removed. The authors propose a two-stage procedure: a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary in one pass, followed by a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that efficiently computes a solution of size k after any m elements are removed from S.

The paper builds on several important lines of work: the classic greedy algorithm for submodular maximization (Nemhauser et al., 1978), streaming submodular maximization (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014), and robust submodular maximization (Krause et al., 2008; Bogunovic et al., 2017). The key innovation is the partitioned thresholding approach that combines exponentially decreasing thresholds with a bucket-based structure, enabling robustness to any m removals while maintaining a small summary size.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Algorithmic Approach**: The partitioned thresholding structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a significant contribution that effectively addresses the robustness requirement in the streaming setting. This is a clear advancement over previous robust submodular maximization methods that were not designed for streaming.

2. **Strong Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper provides a rigorous analysis showing that the algorithm achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee (c ≈ 0.149) for the robust problem. The proof technique combining properties of the robust structure with the decreasing thresholding rule is novel and well-executed.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The problem is well-motivated with compelling real-world applications in dominating set problems and personalized recommendations. The experimental results demonstrate that the algorithm performs competitively against SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed), even outperforming it in some cases.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with clear pseudocode for both algorithms, thorough experimental validation, and good connection between theory and practice.

## Weaknesses

1. **Relatively Low Approximation Factor**: The approximation factor (0.149) is significantly lower than what can be achieved in the non-robust streaming setting (0.5-ε). While the paper acknowledges this as a trade-off for robustness, it would be stronger to provide a lower bound showing this factor is tight or to discuss why this trade-off is necessary.

2. **Memory Complexity**: The memory requirement O((k + m log k) log² k) is higher than some previous streaming algorithms (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING uses O(k log k)). While the paper notes this is a trade-off for robustness, it would be helpful to see a more detailed comparison of memory requirements across different algorithms.

3. **Practical Implementation of τ**: While Section 4.1 addresses how to approximate f(OPT(k, V \ E)), the approach increases the space complexity by a factor of log₁₊ᵋ k. The paper could better explain the practical trade-offs between this increased space and the improved robustness.

4. **Limited Baseline Comparison**: The experiments primarily compare against SIEVE-STREAMING (which has prior knowledge of removals), but don't compare against other robust submodular maximization algorithms that might be applicable in the streaming setting.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Provide a Lower Bound**: Establish a lower bound showing that the approximation factor cannot be improved, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

2. **Expand Experimental Comparison**: Include comparisons against other robust submodular maximization algorithms (e.g., PRO from Bogunovic et al., 2017) to better contextualize the performance.

3. **Clarify Practical Trade-offs**: In the experiments, include a plot showing the trade-off between memory usage and approximation quality for different values of w (the memory parameter).

4. **Improve Discussion of Limitations**: More clearly articulate the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the relatively low approximation factor compared to non-robust methods.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in streaming submodular optimization. The two-stage robust framework is well-motivated, the algorithm design is clever, and the experimental results are promising. However, the relatively low approximation factor (0.149) compared to non-robust streaming algorithms (which achieve ~0.5) and the higher memory complexity are notable limitations that should be addressed.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, but the current approximation guarantee is somewhat weak for practical applications where high-quality summaries are needed. With the suggested improvements, particularly the addition of a lower bound and more comprehensive experimental comparisons, this paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS.

**Decision: Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the authors would need to address the weaknesses noted in the review before final acceptance)
