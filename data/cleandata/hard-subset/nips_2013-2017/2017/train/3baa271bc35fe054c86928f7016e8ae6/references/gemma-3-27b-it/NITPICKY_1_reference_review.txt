This paper presents a two-stage streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, addressing a relevant and increasingly important problem. The core idea of partitioned thresholding appears novel and the authors provide theoretical guarantees. However, the presentation suffers from several issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to concerns about the clarity of the analysis and the experimental setup. While the contributions are potentially valuable, the number of identified flaws raises serious concerns about the paper’s readiness for NIPS.

**Summary:**

The paper tackles the problem of maintaining a robust submodular summary in a streaming setting, aiming to handle removals of up to *m* elements. It proposes a partitioning-based approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds, theoretically guaranteeing a constant-factor approximation. The work builds upon existing streaming and robust submodular maximization techniques, attempting to bridge the gap between them.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Robustness is crucial in many real-world applications of submodular maximization, and a streaming solution is essential for large datasets.
*   **Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach appears to be a novel contribution, combining ideas from existing work in a creative way.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a theoretical approximation bound, which, while not particularly strong (c = 0.149 as k approaches infinity), demonstrates a degree of rigor.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both the dominating set problem and movie recommendation provide some evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Notation:** The presentation is often dense and difficult to follow. Several notational inconsistencies exist (e.g., the meaning of *w* changes slightly between theory and implementation). The variable τ is introduced as depending on the unknown OPT value and then the algorithm is presented as if it were known, requiring careful reading to understand the practical implications.
*   **Theoretical Analysis – Lack of Detail:** The proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, which is acceptable, but the main text lacks sufficient intuition to understand the critical steps. For example, the jump between Eq. (4) and the final bound feels abrupt. There's insufficient justification for why the presented combination of lemmas achieves the stated bound.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of parameters, particularly *w*. The discussion on the practical value of *w*=1 feels somewhat hand-wavy and lacks sufficient justification. The explanation of how τ is approximated is also lacking.
*   **Experimental Setup – Incomplete Details:** The experimental section lacks crucial details. For example, in the movie recommendation setting, the values of α and the dataset split aren’t consistently reported. Comparing against GREEDY requires careful justification, as GREEDY isn't a streaming algorithm. The claim that STAR-T-GREEDY “almost matches” GREEDY is vague and requires quantifiable support. Why is SIEVE-STREAMING considered a fair comparison, given its different characteristics?
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** The claim that the algorithm "matches (and in some cases outperforms) the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm" requires stronger statistical validation. Visual inspection of the graphs doesn't provide sufficient evidence.
*    **Redundancy:** Some content, like the discussion of related work, feels repetitive.

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

*   **Theorem 4.1:** The dependence of the size bound O((k + m log k) log k) on *w* is not fully clarified in relation to the parameter setting w ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem / k ⌉.
*   **Algorithm 1:** Step 6 ("break") seems unnecessary. The inner loop breaks after assigning an element to a bucket, but this doesn't affect the correctness.
*   **Section 5:** The ablation study comparing different components of the algorithm (e.g., STAR-T-GREEDY vs. STAR-T-SIEVE) would be beneficial.
*    **Space Complexity:** The claimed space complexity seems high, particularly given the performance observed in experiments with *w*=1.
*   **Definition of Robustness:** The definition of robustness used is relatively weak, requiring only a solution within a constant factor of the optimal solution after removals. A stronger robustness guarantee would be more desirable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   Theoretical analysis, although somewhat lacking in detail, provides some guarantees.
*   Empirical results suggest the algorithm is practical.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity and numerous minor technical flaws.
*   Insufficient detail in the theoretical analysis and experimental setup.
*   The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is small.
*   Parameter sensitivity and lack of practical guidance for parameter selection.
*   The abundance of minor issues suggests a lack of careful attention to detail.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a novel approach, the numerous identified weaknesses, particularly concerning clarity, detail, and experimental rigor, significantly detract from its overall quality. I believe the paper is not ready for NIPS at this time. **Recommendation: Reject.**

The authors should address the clarity issues, provide more detailed explanations of the theoretical analysis, strengthen the experimental validation, and carefully address the specific concerns raised in this review. A major revision with a significantly improved presentation and more rigorous evaluation would be necessary for reconsideration.
