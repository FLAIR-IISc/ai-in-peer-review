This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure, STAR-T, that first constructs a robust summary of the data in a single pass and then utilizes a greedy algorithm on this summary to find a solution even after a certain number of elements are removed. The problem is well-motivated, and the proposed approach is a valuable contribution.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively establishes the problem context and highlights the challenges of large-scale data summarization with robustness requirements. The problem statement is precise, and the related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches, including relevant streaming and robust submodular maximization algorithms (SIEVE-STREAMING, PRO, etc.). The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in practice. The inclusion of experiments in both a graph-based application (dominating set) and a recommendation scenario strengthens the validation.

A significant strength of this work is the combination of theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The theoretical bounds provided in Theorem 4.1, while potentially loose as noted by the authors (especially as *k* increases), establish a constant-factor approximation for the robust streaming problem. The experimental results demonstrate that STAR-T performs competitively with existing state-of-the-art algorithms, even in scenarios where those algorithms have access to information (e.g., the removed elements) that STAR-T does not. The interactive personalized movie recommendation experiment highlights a practical benefit of the approach – the ability to quickly adapt to changing user preferences without recomputing the entire summary. The handling of the unknown OPT value via parallel runs (Theorem 4.5) is a smart addition.

However, there are also some weaknesses. The parameter *w*, while theoretically justified, is somewhat opaque in its connection to practical performance. The experiments suggest that *w* = 1 works well, which seems surprisingly low given the theoretical bounds. A more detailed exploration of the impact of *w* on performance, and perhaps a more intuitive explanation of its role, would be beneficial. Additionally, the space complexity of O((k + m log k) log k) might be a limitation for very large values of *k* and *m*.  Although the authors mention this is logarithmic, a more detailed discussion on the practicality of this space requirement for very large datasets would be valuable. The analysis relies on several lemmas, and while the sketch is provided, a more detailed presentation of the proofs in the main body or a well-structured appendix would improve readability.  Finally, the comparison against SIEVE-STREAMING could be strengthened by providing more detailed runtime comparisons. While the paper demonstrates comparable objective values, knowing the computational cost difference would provide a more complete picture.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a well-motivated and important problem in large-scale machine learning.
*   Proposes a novel and theoretically sound algorithm (STAR-T).
*   Provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee for robust streaming submodular maximization.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance against existing algorithms in practical applications.
*   Presents a clear and well-organized exposition of the problem, approach, and results.
*   Includes insightful experiments that validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical bounds might be loose and the constant factor relatively small.
*   The role and impact of the parameter *w* are not fully explored.
*   The space complexity could be a limitation for extremely large datasets.
*   Detailed proofs are relegated to the supplementary material.
*   Runtime comparison with baselines could be more thorough.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The problem is important, the proposed approach is well-motivated and theoretically justified, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness in practice. The novelty of combining robustness with streaming algorithms is significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding the parameter *w*, space complexity discussion, and potentially including more detailed proofs or clearer references to the supplementary material. I would also suggest adding runtime comparisons to the experimental results.
