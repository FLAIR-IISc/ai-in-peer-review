Summary
The paper studies robust submodular maximization in a streaming two-stage setting: (i) a single-pass streaming stage constructs a compact summary S robust to the removal of up to m elements; (ii) at query time, after any set E with |E| ≤ m is removed, a solution of size ≤ k is extracted using only S \ E. The proposed algorithm, STAR-T, arranges S into partitions of exponentially growing bucket sizes and assigns exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds across partitions. Each streamed element is inserted into the first non-full bucket where its marginal gain exceeds the partition’s threshold. At query time, STAR-T-GREEDY runs standard GREEDY on S \ E. The main guarantee is a constant-factor approximation f(Z) ≥ c·f(OPT(k, V \ E)) with c → 0.149 as k grows, using space O(k log k + m log^2 k) (or O((k + m log k) log^2 k) with parallel thresholding to estimate f(OPT)). Experiments on dominating set (Twitter and Amazon graphs) and interactive movie recommendation show STAR-T-GREEDY matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING that is allowed to know E in advance, and that a single summary can support multiple different removals.

Relation to prior work
The algorithm extends SIEVE-STREAMING’s thresholding to a robust setting and leverages a non-uniform, exponentially partitioned structure akin to PRO (Bogunovic et al., ICML’17), but with online thresholded placement instead of offline greedy filling. It improves memory dependence on k over concurrent deletion-robust streaming work (Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17) which achieves ~1/2−ε approximation but uses O(mk log k/ε) memory. It also connects to classical greedy guarantees for monotone submodular maximization (Nemhauser et al.) and robust non-streaming formulations (Orlin–Schulz–Udwani).

Quality
- Theory: The analysis is careful and nontrivial, combining two cases (many full buckets vs. many non-full buckets) and balancing bounds to obtain the final constant. The space bound derivation is clear. The need for τ ≈ f(OPT) is addressed via a standard parallel-thresholding scheme at an extra log factor in space, preserving a near-constant approximation. Assumptions (monotone, normalized submodular; cardinality constraint) are standard.
- Approximations: The constant 0.149 is modest compared to 0.5 in non-robust streaming or the 1/2−ε of the concurrent robust method, but the memory dependence on k is substantially better (logarithmic rather than linear), making the trade-off meaningful.
- Algorithmic cost: Per-element processing requires scanning up to O(log k) partitions and, across buckets, O(wk) in the worst case; with w ≈ 4 m log k / k, this is O(m log k) per item. This is not discussed explicitly and may be large for big m. In practice, the paper uses w=1, reducing per-item cost to O(k), but this weakens the worst-case robustness guarantees. A runtime analysis and empirical time/memory measurements would strengthen the paper.
- Experiments: The experimental methodology is generally fair and informative. The baselines include SIEVE-STREAMING with advance knowledge of E (a strong baseline), and GREEDY in the smaller recommendation task. Results show STAR-T(-GREEDY) competitive to strong baselines, even under adversarial deletions. However, experiments could benefit from:
  - Reporting wall-clock time and memory footprint vs. k and m.
  - Sensitivity to w and to the τ-parallelization overhead in practice.
  - More systematic sweeps over m (including mis-specified m at build time vs. actual deletions at query time).
Overall, the theoretical claims are well supported; the empirical section is solid but could be deeper on efficiency metrics.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized, with helpful intuition (partitioned buckets, exponentially decreasing thresholds) and pseudocode. A few minor clarity issues:
- Threshold notation: early text says threshold τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Clarify and unify the description.
- Space bounds: the abstract/conclusion mention O((k + m log k) log^2 k), while Theorem 4.1 gives O((k + m log k) log k) and Theorem 4.5 adds another logarithmic factor for parallel τ. Make these statements consistent and explicitly distinguish the known-OPT and parallel variants.
- Runtime per element is not analyzed; adding this would improve reproducibility.
Otherwise, the related work is adequately referenced and the proofs are outlined with details deferred to the supplement.

Originality
The partitioned-thresholding design for a streaming, deletion-robust summary is novel. Although the partition structure is reminiscent of PRO, the streaming placement via thresholds and the analysis that balances two structural regimes are new. The trade-off achieved—constant-factor approximation with O(k log k + m log^2 k) space and one pass—appears to be a meaningful advance over prior streaming robust methods.

Significance
Robustness to deletions is increasingly important (e.g., right-to-be-forgotten, failures/preferences), and streaming constraints are ubiquitous. The paper provides the first (to my knowledge) one-pass streaming algorithm with constant-factor approximation under deletions and sublinear space with only logarithmic dependence on k. The ideas (partitioned storage with decreasing thresholds, two-regime analysis) are likely to influence follow-up work, and the practical utility of reusable summaries across multiple queries/removals is compelling.

Pros
- Conceptually simple, one-pass algorithm with a clean structure and constant-factor guarantee under deletions.
- Strong memory bound with only log k dependence (vs. linear) and linear dependence on m up to log factors.
- Clear theoretical analysis; addresses unknown f(OPT) via standard parallelization.
- Experiments demonstrate practical competitiveness, even against advantaged baselines; interactive recommendation use case is appealing.

Cons
- Approximation factor (≈0.149) is small; no empirical comparison to the concurrent 1/2−ε robust streaming method.
- No explicit runtime analysis; per-element cost may be high for large m; empirical efficiency metrics are missing.
- Minor inconsistencies (threshold formula, space bounds between sections).
- Robustness depends on w tied to m; practical guidance for choosing w and performance under mis-specified m could be elaborated.

Suggestions
- Add per-element time complexity analysis and report runtime/memory in experiments.
- Unify threshold/space statements and clarify the exact constants/log factors for both τ-known and τ-parallel variants.
- Include sensitivity to w and m (including over-/under-provisioning m at build time).
- If feasible, compare empirically to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming baseline.

Overall assessment
The paper is technically sound, original, and addresses a significant problem with a practical and theoretically grounded solution. Despite a modest approximation factor and some missing efficiency analyses, the memory-approximation trade-off and empirical performance make a strong case. I lean toward acceptance.
