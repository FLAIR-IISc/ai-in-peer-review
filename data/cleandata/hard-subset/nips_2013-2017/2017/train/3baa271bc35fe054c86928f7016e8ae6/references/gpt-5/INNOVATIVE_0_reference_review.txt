Summary
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting with a two-stage procedure. In stage one, the proposed STAR-T algorithm processes a stream in one pass and builds a small robust summary S using a novel partitioned thresholding scheme: partitions contain exponentially sized buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds; each arriving element is inserted into the first non-full bucket for which its marginal gain exceeds the corresponding threshold. In stage two, given an arbitrary removal E of up to m elements, the algorithm runs GREEDY on S\E to return a size-k solution. Theoretically, with memory |S| = O(k log k + m log2 k) (and an additional log factor to eliminate the need for knowing f(OPT)), the method achieves a constant-factor approximation, c ≈ 0.149 (as k → ∞), to OPT(k, V\E). Compared to concurrent robust streaming work achieving 1/2 − ε but using O(mk log k/ε) memory, the key selling point here is a much milder (logarithmic) dependence on k in the memory for robustness. Empirically, on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, the method matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING—even when SIEVE-STREAMING is given the deletions in advance—and supports multiple interactive queries from the same robust summary.

Strengths
- Novelty/significance: The partitioned thresholding design for robust streaming is conceptually new and elegant. Achieving a constant-factor robust guarantee with single-pass streaming and memory scaling as O(k log k + m log2 k) is a meaningful advance, especially contrasted with prior O(mk) memory.
- Clear algorithmic idea: Two-stage design decouples summary construction from query-time recomputation, enabling interactive reuse across many deletions.
- Solid theory: The analysis combines structure (partitions/buckets) and decreasing thresholds to handle adversarial deletions; the balance-of-bounds argument is clean. The parallel-instance trick removes the need to know f(OPT), at the cost of a standard extra log factor.
- Practical promise: Despite a modest worst-case constant, experiments show the approach competitive or better than strong baselines, even under deletions unfavorable to STAR-T.

Weaknesses
- Approximation factor: The constant 0.149 is small compared to 1/2 − ε in concurrent work. The paper justifies this via markedly better memory, but a discussion of the practical trade-off frontier (approximation vs memory) would help.
- Per-element processing cost: Each incoming element may be tested against O(w k) total capacity across O(log k) partitions/buckets. Even with early stopping, a discussion and measurements of oracle evaluations/throughput are needed; otherwise streaming feasibility at scale is uncertain.
- Empirical scope: Only two applications/functions are evaluated, with limited dataset diversity. There is no wall-clock or memory/runtime breakdown, no sensitivity to w, and no comparison to the concurrent [18] (even though the theoretical comparison is emphasized).
- Clarity/consistency: There are minor inconsistencies:
  - Threshold is described as τ/2^i in the text but Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Please reconcile.
  - Memory bounds alternate between O((k + m log k) log k) and O((k + m log k) log2 k) across sections; align statements and constants.
  - The choice and practical tuning of τ (and the parallel-instance scheme) deserve more explicit guidance for practitioners.
- Assumptions/practicalities: The oracle cost of f(e | B) can be high in some applications; lazy evaluation or sketching could be discussed. Query-time cost GREEDY on S\E is O(k|S|); empirical evidence that this is acceptable at scale would be valuable.

Questions for the authors
- Can you quantify the number of marginal evaluations per element in practice and propose/measure lazy or batched variants?
- How sensitive are results to w? Is w = 1 generally sufficient, and up to what m/k ratios?
- Can your analysis be refined to improve the constant c, e.g., via curvature or alternative thresholds?
- Could you add an empirical comparison against [18], or at least a synthetic study highlighting the memory–approximation trade-off?
- Please clarify the threshold formula and unify the memory bounds throughout the paper.
- Will you release code?

Relation to prior work and originality
- The paper clearly builds on non-streaming PRO [11] and SIEVE-STREAMING [8], but contributes a distinct, streaming-robust summary structure and a new analysis. The memory scaling with m and k is notably different from concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [18]. Related work coverage is adequate, and the positioning is fair.

Quality and clarity
- The theoretical development appears sound, with proof sketches that match the intuitions of submodular analysis. The writing is generally clear, though the noted inconsistencies should be fixed. Experiments support the main claims but could be strengthened with runtime/memory data and broader domains.

Significance
- The approach opens a useful direction for robust streaming summarization with reusable summaries under multiple deletions/queries and improved memory scaling. I expect others to build on these ideas.

Recommendation
- Accept (7/10). The paper introduces a fresh and practically relevant algorithmic idea with nontrivial theory and promising empirical behavior. Despite a small constant and some missing empirical details, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance.

Confidence
- 3.5/5 (familiar with submodular streaming/robustness, did not verify all proofs end-to-end).
