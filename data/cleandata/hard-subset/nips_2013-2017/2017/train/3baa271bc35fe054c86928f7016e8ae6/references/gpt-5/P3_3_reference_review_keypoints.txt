- Summary: Proposes STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm with partitioned exponentially-sized buckets and decreasing thresholds, then runs GREEDY on S \ E after up to m adversarial deletions to return a k-size solution with approximation ≈0.149 and space O(k log k + m log^2 k) (noting a reported inconsistency).

- Relation to prior work: Extends SIEVE-STREAMING-style thresholding to deletion-robust streaming and adapts exponential partitioning ideas from offline robust methods; trades a lower approximation constant for much lower memory dependence on k versus concurrent work.

- Main theoretical claim: Constant-factor approximation (c ≈ 0.149 as k→∞) with one pass, oracle access, adversarial deletions, and memory scaling nearly linear in k plus m log^2 k.

- Novelty/technical idea: Partitioned thresholding with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and a pigeonhole-style replicated buckets argument to control deletions is clean and insightful.

- Practicality: Simple query phase using GREEDY on S \ E; empirical results show competitiveness with a clairvoyant baseline and robustness beyond theoretical m.

- Space bound inconsistency: Paper states two different space bounds (one with log^2 k factor versus one with log k); this needs reconciliation as it affects the headline.

- τ dependence and guessing: Threshold τ depends on OPT(k, V \ E); the parallel-τ remedy is plausible but the paper omits clear details on τ range/initialization, selection among instances at query time, and added query cost.

- Approximation constant concern: 0.149 is conservative compared to non-robust and offline robust results; discussion on possible improvements or lower bounds is missing.

- Per-element time complexity: Element processing may cost O(wk) ≈ O(m log k) checks, which can be large when m is a substantial fraction of k; runtime discussion and throughput numbers are lacking.

- Notation and clarity issues: Inconsistent threshold formula (τ/2^i vs τ/min{2^i,k}), garbled τ in Theorem 4.1, misleading “on average” phrasing in Lemma 4.2, and minor typographical/reference errors.

- Experimental gaps: No empirical comparison to the closest concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18]; practice uses w=1 despite theory requiring larger w, and sensitivity analyses for w and m are missing.

- Implementation details missing: Paper omits how τ is handled in practice (parallel instances or scheduling) and the query-time procedure for selecting S among parallel runs.

- Question 1: Which space bound is correct—O(k log k + m log^2 k) or O((k + m log k) log^2 k)?

- Question 2: How are τ guesses initialized without knowing E, what OPT bounds are used, how is the best instance selected at query time, and what is the query-time overhead?

- Question 3: What is the per-element processing time in the implementation, and how does it scale with m and k empirically?

- Question 4: Can the analysis be tightened to improve the 0.149 constant, and are there empirical approximation ratios reported?

- Question 5: Can the approach be extended to other constraints like matroids or knapsack?

- Pros: Clear, novel algorithmic idea with solid analysis for adversarial deletions; strong memory–approximation trade-off versus concurrent work; simple query mechanism and promising experiments.

- Cons: Space bound inconsistency, clarity/notation issues, low theoretical constant, lack of runtime analysis, and missing empirical comparison to the most relevant baseline.

- Recommendation: Borderline accept — original and meaningful advance with promising empirical evidence, but needs clarification and additional evaluation to strengthen the paper.
