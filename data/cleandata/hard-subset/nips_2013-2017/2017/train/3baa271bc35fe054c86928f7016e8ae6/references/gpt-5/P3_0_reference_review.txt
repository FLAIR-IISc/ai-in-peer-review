Summary and context
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization in the single-pass streaming setting. The goal is to compute a compact summary S that, after arbitrary deletion of up to m elements, still supports constructing a near-optimal size-k solution using only S. The proposed two-stage procedure consists of: (i) STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that organizes elements into partitions of exponentially growing bucket sizes (up to min{2^i, k}) with exponentially decreasing thresholds; and (ii) a query-time recovery stage that runs GREEDY on S\E for any adversarial deletion set E, |E| ≤ m. The main guarantee: with space O((k + m log k) log k) for known τ (and O((k + m log k) log k log_{1+ε} k) when τ is estimated via parallel guesses), the query-time set achieves a constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉) to OPT(k, V\E). This trades off a lower approximation constant for much smaller memory than the concurrent work [18], which achieves ~1/2 − ε with O(m k log k/ε) space. Experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation show performance close to SIEVE-STREAMING that is given the deletions in advance.

Quality
– Theory: The analysis is novel and technically sound in structure. The partitioned, thresholded streaming construction and its case analysis (many full buckets vs. none) are clear high-level ideas. Lemmas 4.2–4.4 combine GREEDY’s standard guarantees with submodularity and occupancy arguments over buckets to control the damage from deletions. The memory bound scales as O((k + m log k) log k) for known τ and increases by an extra log factor when τ is unknown, as in SIEVE-STREAMING.

However, there are several issues that need clarification/correction:
1) Inconsistencies in space bounds across the paper: the abstract/intro/conclusion state O((k + m log k) log^2 k); Theorem 4.1 gives O((k + m log k) log k), and Theorem 4.5 adds another log factor. Please reconcile these and state the precise bounds per setting (known τ vs. guessed τ).
2) τ specification in Theorem 4.1 appears malformed in the typeset equation; as written, it’s unclear whether τ equals a constant times OPT or its reciprocal. This needs to be clearly and correctly stated.
3) Small-k regime: the constant c = 0.149(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉) becomes vacuous for k ≤ 2. State the range of k for which the bound is nontrivial and whether any alternative bound holds for small k.
4) Time/oracle complexity: STAR-T potentially checks up to O(w Σ_i k/2^i) = O(w k log k) buckets per element in the worst case (each requiring a marginal gain evaluation). Please provide per-element and total oracle query complexity, along with practical speed-ups in implementation.
5) The appearance of 1 − e^{-1/3} in Lemma 4.3 deserves more intuition: it’s nonstandard relative to the canonical 1 − 1/e, so an explanation of the “1/3” factor (e.g., effective budget share or structural limitation) would help readers follow the proof idea.

– Experiments: The datasets and tasks are relevant. The design of comparing to SIEVE-STREAMING that knows E in advance is a strong baseline (conservative in favor of the baseline), and the results indicate practical competitiveness.

That said:
1) Missing baselines: There is no empirical comparison to deletion-robust streaming [18] or to centralized robust methods (e.g., PRO [11]) on manageable instances. Including at least [18] on smaller graphs would strengthen the empirical story.
2) Resource reporting: Provide memory footprint (|S|) and wall-clock throughput; give sensitivity to w and m, and ablations for w=1 vs. larger values.
3) Fairness of evaluation: In the graph experiments, E is sampled from S, which is adversarial for STAR-T but not realistic. Also report results where E is chosen from V (the actual model) and report uncertainty (e.g., standard errors).
4) For the “unknown τ” variant, clarify whether experiments used a single τ or the multi-instance scheme; if the latter, report the extra memory factor and its impact.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, but several notation and consistency issues should be fixed:
– Threshold definition: The text says threshold τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. These agree for i < log k and equal τ/k at the last partition; please unify the presentation and explain this choice explicitly.
– Notation: Be consistent with log bases and ceiling notation (⌈log k⌉ vs. dlog ke). Avoid overloading “e” (Euler’s constant vs. element variable).
– Spell out the exact dependence of w on m, k in one place and carry it into the final space bounds.
– Provide a succinct theorem table summarizing approximation vs. memory vs. passes for STAR-T (known/unknown τ), SIEVE-STREAMING, PRO [11], and [18].

Originality
The work advances robust submodular maximization in the streaming model by introducing a partitioned, thresholded summary with exponentially varying capacities and thresholds, and by providing the first constant-factor guarantee with memory logarithmic in k and linear in m up to log factors. The approach is related to PRO’s partitioning, but the streaming thresholding rule and analysis are new. The trade-off against [18] (much less memory, lower constant) is valuable and clearly articulated.

Significance
This is a meaningful contribution: robust summarization under deletions is increasingly important (right to be forgotten, failures, personalization constraints), and streaming constraints are common. The memory-approximation trade-off compared to [18] gives practitioners an additional point on the Pareto frontier. The query-time design that reuses S for multiple E is attractive.

Pros
– Single-pass robust streaming algorithm with compact memory.
– Clean two-stage design; reusable summaries for multiple deletion queries.
– Theoretical constant-factor guarantee for arbitrary E with O((k + m log k) log k) space (known τ).
– Empirical evidence of competitiveness against strong baselines.

Cons
– Low approximation constant (≈0.149), with degeneracy for small k.
– Several clarity issues (τ formula, space bounds, threshold presentation).
– Missing runtime/oracle complexity and sensitivity analyses.
– No empirical comparison to [18]; limited reporting of resource usage.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying/correcting the noted inconsistencies, adding resource/sensitivity reporting, and, if possible, including a comparison to [18] on a smaller instance. The paper offers a new and useful point in the robust streaming landscape with a sound methodology and practical relevance.
