- Tackles robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting with deletions.  
- Proposes STAR-T: a two-stage partitioned thresholding algorithm assigning stream elements to buckets by exponentially decreasing thresholds.  
- Query stage applies a greedy algorithm on the maintained summary to produce a robust solution.  
- Provides theoretical approximation guarantee (constant ≈ 0.149) and memory bounds scaling with log k.  
- Empirical validation on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks shows competitive or better performance versus SIEVE-STREAMING.  
- Positions work relative to prior robust and streaming submodular literature and builds on SIEVE-STREAMING ideas.  
- Addresses relevant applications like influence maximization and personalized recommendation.  
- Strength: tackles an important practical robustness problem for streaming submodular selection.  
- Strength: novel partitioned thresholding scheme balancing robustness and efficiency.  
- Strength: provable theoretical guarantees and defined memory bounds.  
- Strength: interactive two-stage design supports multiple removal scenarios.  
- Strength: clear and well-organized presentation.  
- Weakness: small approximation constant (0.149) may limit practical performance and needs sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness: memory parameter w heavily impacts performance; practical tuning guidance is lacking.  
- Weakness: experimental scope limited—more datasets, baselines, and realistic removal (from full dataset) needed.  
- Weakness: analysis assumes threshold τ known a priori; running parallel instances adds overhead.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of practical implications when k is very large despite log k scaling.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, promising experiments, clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: small constant, tuning and scaling challenges, and insufficient experimental breadth.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions focusing on approximation discussion, parameter tuning guidance, expanded experiments, and scalability discussion.
