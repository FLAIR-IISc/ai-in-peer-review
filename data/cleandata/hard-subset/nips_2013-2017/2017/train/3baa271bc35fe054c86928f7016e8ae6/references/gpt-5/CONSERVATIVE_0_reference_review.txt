Summary
This paper studies robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting with cardinality constraint k and up to m deletions. The authors propose STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that organizes selected elements into partitions of exponentially increasing bucket sizes and uses exponentially decreasing thresholds to decide placement. In the query stage, after a set E of size ≤ m is removed, they run GREEDY on S\E to return a size-k set. The theory claims: with w ≥ ⌈4 m log k / k⌉, STAR-T stores O((k + m log k) log k) elements (with an extra log(1+ε) factor when OPT is unknown) and achieves a constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149 (as k → ∞) against OPT(k, V\E). The approach combines “partitioned buckets” (cf. PRO in the centralized robust setting) with streaming thresholding (cf. SIEVE-STREAMING). Experiments on dominating set (Twitter/Amazon graphs) and MovieLens show performance comparable to GREEDY/SIEVE-STREAMING baselines, even when the baselines are allowed to know the deletions in advance.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and practically motivated problem: robust streaming summarization where recomputing post-deletion is infeasible.
- Algorithmic ideas are reasonable extensions of established techniques: non-uniform partitioning (from centralized robust work) plus streaming thresholding.
- Theoretical guarantees: one-pass, bounded memory, and a constant-factor approximation under deletions, with memory dependence O(m log k) (up to log factors) rather than O(mk) as in concurrent work.
- Query-stage decoupling is attractive for interactive scenarios; empirical results show that a single summary can serve multiple deletion patterns reasonably well.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness:
- Several inconsistencies in statements and algorithm specification:
  - Memory: Introduction and Conclusion claim O((k + m log k) log^2 k), Theorem 4.1 states O((k + m log k) log k). Which is correct, and where does the extra log factor arise (and how does it interact with the parallel τ-instances in Section 4.1)?
  - Thresholds: text says partition i uses τ/2^i, Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. The distinction matters for both analysis and implementation; please reconcile.
  - The proof sketch for Lemma 4.2 informally appeals to “on average” arguments about |Bi,j ∩ E|, but deletions are worst-case/adversarial. The formal bound should be a pigeonhole-style counting argument; please present the exact bound and its dependence on w and the number of full buckets.
- The constant 0.149 is quite weak, and the guarantee depends on a fairly delicate balancing of two cases. While constants are not everything, the gap to 1/2−ε (albeit with worse memory in [18]) is large.
- The algorithm requires an α-approximation to OPT for τ; the parallel-instance scheme introduces an extra log(1+ε) factor in memory, but the paper does not quantify the total space concretely nor the effect on empirical performance.
- Experimental evaluation is limited relative to the ambition:
  - No comparison to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming algorithm [18], even on modest scales where its higher memory is feasible. Since [18] achieves a 1/2−ε approximation, an empirical comparison would be informative even if memory is larger.
  - No sensitivity analyses: performance vs m (including larger m), vs w (the memory knob), and vs ε (number of τ-instances). In all experiments w=1, yet theory suggests w should scale with m; a careful study of this tradeoff is missing.
  - Limited reporting on summary size, memory usage, and running time. For a streaming paper, throughput and memory footprints are key.
  - Dominating-set experiments remove elements only from S (the authors note this hurts their method), which corresponds to the adversarial choice but does not explore broader deletion patterns; more comprehensive stress tests are warranted.
- Some steps rely on GREEDY in the query stage; in massive settings this may not be acceptable. The STAR-T-SIEVE variant is mentioned but not analyzed theoretically.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable, but the aforementioned inconsistencies (memory bounds, thresholds) and notation choices reduce clarity.
- Important details (e.g., precise value of τ in Theorem 4.1 and its derivation, the exact space bound when OPT is unknown) should be centralized and consistently stated.
- Provide explicit pseudocode for the parallel τ-instances and how they are synchronized in a single pass.

Originality:
- The combination of partitioned buckets (as in PRO) with streaming thresholding is a natural extension rather than a radical novelty. The novelty lies mainly in the streaming analysis for robustness with improved memory dependence on k. This is fine, but the relation to [11] (PRO) and [8] (SIEVE-STREAMING) should be positioned more crisply.

Significance:
- Potentially useful if memory is constrained and deletions are common. However, the relatively weak constant, reliance on unknown OPT, and limited empirical validation against stronger baselines reduce confidence in immediate impact. The lack of comprehensive resource reporting further complicates practical adoption.

Questions for the authors
1. Please reconcile the memory bounds: is it O((k + m log k) log k) or O((k + m log k) log^2 k) in the known-OPT setting? What is the exact total space with the parallel τ-instances?
2. Clarify the threshold rule: τ/2^i vs τ/min{2^i, k}. Which one is analyzed? If the latter, how do proofs change?
3. Can you provide an explicit, adversarial (not average) bound for Lemma 4.2 and remove informal language?
4. How does performance degrade as m increases and w is kept fixed? Conversely, for fixed m, how does increasing w affect quality and memory?
5. Can you compare against [18] on smaller instances to substantiate the claimed space–quality tradeoff?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a principled streaming design.
- Provides a constant-factor robustness guarantee with improved (logarithmic in k) memory dependence vs concurrent work.
- The interactive use-case is appealing, and preliminary experiments are encouraging.

Cons for acceptance
- Inconsistencies and clarity issues in algorithm description and bounds.
- Weak constant and reliance on unknown OPT; limited discussion of practical parameterization.
- Empirical evaluation lacks key baselines, resource metrics, and sensitivity analyses; evidence is insufficient to endorse a new algorithm over established methods.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The theoretical direction is reasonable, but the current presentation has technical inconsistencies and the experiments do not thoroughly validate the claimed advantages over strong baselines. Strengthening clarity, adding direct comparisons to [18], reporting resource usage, and providing sensitivity studies would substantially improve the case.
