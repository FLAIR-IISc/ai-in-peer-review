**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The goal is to select a small, robust summary `S` from a data stream in a single pass, such that even after an adversary removes up to `m` elements from `S`, a near-optimal solution of size `k` can still be recovered by running a simple greedy algorithm on the remainder of `S`. The authors propose a two-stage procedure. The first stage, an algorithm named STAR-T, builds the summary `S` using a novel structure of partitions and buckets with exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds. The second stage, STAR-T-GREEDY, applies a standard greedy algorithm to `S \ E` for any removal set `E` of size at most `m`. The main theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee for this procedure, using a summary of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The approach is evaluated empirically on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, where it performs comparably to or better than a non-robust streaming baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING) that is given advance knowledge of the removed elements.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The problem formulation is well-motivated and highly relevant. Combining the challenges of large-scale data (streaming), robustness to deletions, and submodular optimization addresses a practical and important gap in the literature. The two-stage framework, where a summary is built once and queried multiple times, is particularly compelling for interactive applications.
2.  **Novel Algorithm:** The STAR-T algorithm introduces a novel and interesting structure that combines partitioned summaries with an exponentially decreasing thresholding scheme. This appears to be a non-trivial extension of ideas from prior work like SIEVE-STREAMING to the robust setting.
3.  **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a formal, constant-factor approximation guarantee (Theorem 4.1), which is a strong theoretical result for this challenging problem. The authors also correctly address the practical issue of not knowing the optimal value a priori by proposing a standard parallelization technique (Theorem 4.5).
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are conducted on real-world datasets and showcase the practical utility of the proposed method. The comparison against a strong baseline that knows the deletions in advance is a compelling way to demonstrate the effectiveness of the robust summary. The interactive movie recommendation scenario is an excellent example of the problem's relevance.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novel ideas, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in clarity, technical precision, and experimental methodology, which collectively undermine the contribution.

1.  **Clarity and Notation:** The paper is difficult to parse due to non-standard and undefined notation.
    *   Expressions like `dlog ke` and `wdk/2ie` are used extensively without definition. One might infer they mean `ceil(log k)` and `w * ceil(k/2^i)`, respectively, but this forces the reader to guess and significantly hinders readability. Standard mathematical notation must be used.
    *   In Algorithm 1, line 5, the threshold is given as `τ/min{2i, k}`. However, the text repeatedly describes it as `τ/2i`, an exponentially decreasing threshold. These are not equivalent, as for `2i >= k`, the threshold in the algorithm becomes constant at `τ/k`. This inconsistency between the description, the algorithm, and potentially the analysis is a critical point of confusion.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Precision:**
    *   The memory complexity bound stated in Theorem 4.1 as `O((k + m log k) log k)` appears inconsistent with the derivation. The proof sketch in Eq. (3) leads to a size of `(log k + 5)wk`. Substituting the required setting `w = O(m log k / k)` yields a size of `O(m log^2 k)`. This does not match the theorem's statement. This discrepancy raises concerns about the correctness of the stated complexity, a key contribution of the paper.
    *   The proof sketch for Theorem 4.1 is extremely high-level. Key steps, such as the derivation of Eq. (5) by "balancing out" two inequalities, are presented without any justification or intuition, making the proof difficult to verify.
    *   The final approximation constant `c ≈ 0.149` is quite small. While any constant factor is a valuable theoretical result, the practicality of such a loose bound should be discussed.

3.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:**
    *   A major weakness is the disconnect regarding the parameter `w`. The theory requires `w >= ceil(4 * dlog ke * m / k)`, meaning `w` must scale with the robustness parameter `m`. However, all experiments are run with `w=1`. The paper mentions this works well in practice but offers no explanation for this significant discrepancy. Does this imply the theoretical analysis is overly loose, or does the method only work in practice under conditions not captured by the theory? This is not addressed, leaving the reader to question the relationship between the theoretical claims and the empirical results.
    *   Similarly, the theory requires a parallel guessing scheme for the unknown value `τ`, which increases memory by a logarithmic factor. The experimental section does not state whether this was used. If a single `τ` was used, it is critical to explain how it was chosen, as this is a key parameter. This omission hinders reproducibility.

4.  **Experimental Design:**
    *   In the dominating set experiments, the removed elements `E` are sampled from the algorithm's own summary `S`, rather than the ground set `V`. The authors claim this is a more adversarial setting for their algorithm, but it is also a deviation from the problem definition (`E ⊆ V`). This choice needs stronger justification. Results for the standard setting should also be included for a fair comparison.
    *   The experimental results lack rigor. For instance, figures are presented without error bars or any measure of variance, despite results being averaged over 100 trials.

5.  **Minor Issues:**
    *   The footnote on the first page contains a formatting error: "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems".
    *   The formatting of references is inconsistent (e.g., [1] contains trailing commas ", ,").

**Decision**

While the paper tackles an important problem with a novel approach, the execution is marred by significant issues. The lack of clarity from non-standard notation, the inconsistency in the algorithm's description, the potential error in the memory complexity analysis, and the unexplained chasm between the theoretical requirements and the experimental setup are all serious concerns. A paper for NIPS must meet a high standard of technical precision and clarity, and this work, in its current form, does not. The accumulation of these flaws, both major and minor, prevents a confident assessment of the work's correctness and overall contribution. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors have a promising direction, but the paper requires a major revision to address these fundamental issues before it can be considered for publication.
