## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm, STAR-T, designed for scenarios where data arrives sequentially and the chosen summary set must remain effective even after some elements are removed. The core idea involves partitioning the summary set into buckets with exponentially increasing sizes and associated decreasing greedy thresholds. The theoretical analysis provides guarantees on the approximation ratio and memory usage, and experimental results demonstrate competitive performance against existing streaming algorithms, even when those algorithms have prior knowledge of element removals.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon a strong foundation of prior research in submodular maximization, streaming algorithms, and robustness. It explicitly acknowledges and differentiates itself from work on non-streaming robust submodular maximization ([9, 10, 11]), standard streaming submodular maximization (e.g., [8, 5, 12, 13]), and related work in robust influence maximization and interactive submodular optimization.  The authors correctly position their contribution as an extension of SIEVE-STREAMING [8] to the robust setting, and highlight the novelty of their partitioned thresholding approach and analysis.  They also appropriately note the concurrent independent work of [18], contrasting their logarithmic memory dependence on *k* with the linear dependence of that work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Important Problem:** Robustness is a critical consideration for real-world applications of submodular maximization, and the streaming setting dramatically increases the challenge. This paper addresses a highly relevant and practical problem.
*   **Novel Approach:** The partitioned thresholding scheme is a clever way to balance exploration (allowing for diverse elements) and exploitation (focusing on high-marginal-gain elements) in the streaming context, while ensuring robustness.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a clear and rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing approximation bounds and memory complexity.  The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a significant result.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is well-designed, demonstrating competitive performance on two different application domains (dominating set and movie recommendation) and comparing against relevant baselines.  The fact that the algorithm performs well even when *w*=1 in practice is a strong indication of its efficiency.
*   **Interactive Component:** The demonstration of reusing the precomputed summary for different removal scenarios highlights an important practical advantage – the ability to quickly adapt to changing conditions.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm descriptions are clear and the theoretical arguments are presented logically.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Constant Factor in Approximation:** The constant factor (0.149) in the approximation guarantee is relatively small. While theoretically significant, it might be a limiting factor in practice. The authors acknowledge this, and its dependency on `dlog ke` is a potential concern for very large `k`.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The algorithm relies on several parameters (k, m, τ, w). The paper provides guidance on setting *w*, but the choice of τ seems to require knowledge of f(OPT(k, V \ E)), which is not available a priori. While the authors address this in section 4.1, the additional overhead of parallel instances to approximate this value might be significant.
*   **Complexity of Proofs:** The detailed proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, which is standard, but some of the core arguments (e.g., the balancing of Lemma 4.3 and 4.4) could benefit from a more concise and intuitive explanation within the main text.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are well-executed, they are limited to two datasets.  Exploring performance on a wider range of data distributions and graph structures would strengthen the empirical validation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The problem addressed is important, the proposed approach is novel and well-motivated, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are convincing. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small and the parameter tuning process could be simplified, these are minor weaknesses. The potential for practical impact is high, and the paper is well-deserving of acceptance.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm.
*   Provides strong theoretical guarantees.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance in experiments.
*   Offers a practical interactive component.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Small constant factor in the approximation guarantee.
*   Parameter tuning might be challenging.
*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.



**Recommendation:**

**Accept.** I believe this paper meets the criteria for acceptance at NIPS. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the work represents a valuable addition to the literature on submodular optimization and streaming algorithms.
