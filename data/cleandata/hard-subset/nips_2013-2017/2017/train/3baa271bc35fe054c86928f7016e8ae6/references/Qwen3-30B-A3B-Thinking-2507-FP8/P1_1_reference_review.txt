

# Peer Review: "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary

This paper addresses a practically important problem in large-scale data summarization: robust submodular maximization under streaming constraints. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm (STAR-T for streaming phase, STAR-T-GREEDY for query phase) that constructs a robust summary set S (of size O((k + m log k) log² k)) during a single pass over the data, such that after any m elements are removed from S, a simple greedy algorithm on the remaining elements achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee (c ≈ 0.149). The work builds upon the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8] but introduces a novel partitioned thresholding structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds to achieve robustness.

The paper relates well to previous work, particularly the robust submodular maximization literature [9,10,11] and streaming submodular maximization [8,12,13]. It extends these by providing a streaming solution for the robust setting with a theoretically sound analysis. The authors also acknowledge and compare with concurrent work [18] that achieves a 1/2 - ε approximation but with higher memory complexity (O(mk log k/ε) vs. our O((k + m log k) log² k)).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization with a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The partitioned thresholding approach is novel and elegantly combines properties of robustness with streaming constraints.

2. **Practical Validation**: The experimental results are compelling, showing that their approach matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed) in two real-world applications: dominating set problems and personalized movie recommendations. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the practical utility of their robust summary approach.

3. **Clear Structure**: The paper is well-organized with a clear separation of the streaming and query phases. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with multiple lemmas addressing different cases in the proof of the main theorem.

4. **Addressing a Gap**: The work fills an important gap between robust submodular maximization (which typically assumes offline processing) and streaming submodular maximization (which typically ignores robustness requirements).

## Weaknesses

1. **Approximation Factor**: The approximation factor (c ≈ 0.149) is relatively low compared to standard submodular maximization (1-1/e ≈ 0.632). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it's worth noting that this factor is significantly lower than what would be expected for standard streaming submodular maximization, which typically achieves around 0.5.

2. **Practical Implementation Complexity**: The need to approximate f(OPT(k, V\E)) (as shown in Theorem 4.5) requires maintaining multiple parallel instances of the algorithm, increasing the space complexity by a factor of log₁₊ᵋ k. This could be a significant practical limitation for large datasets.

3. **Comparison with [18]**: While the paper acknowledges concurrent work [18], the comparison is limited. A more detailed comparison of the memory complexity and approximation guarantees would strengthen the paper's position in the literature.

4. **Theoretical Tightness**: The paper doesn't explore whether the O((k + m log k) log² k) memory bound is tight or whether it can be improved. A discussion of lower bounds would provide more context for the theoretical contribution.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. While the approximation factor is relatively low, this is a common trade-off when addressing robustness in submodular maximization, and the paper provides a theoretically sound solution to a practically important problem.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature between robust submodular optimization (typically offline) and streaming submodular optimization (typically non-robust). The two-stage approach elegantly separates the robust summary construction from the query phase, enabling efficient responses to arbitrary element removals without reprocessing the entire dataset.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with a well-motivated problem, novel algorithmic approach, thorough theoretical analysis, and compelling experimental validation. The weaknesses identified are important but do not undermine the paper's overall contribution.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Solves an important practical problem (robustness in streaming settings)
- Novel algorithmic approach with a clean theoretical analysis
- Strong experimental results demonstrating practical utility
- Clear relation to and extension of prior work

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Approximation factor is relatively low (0.149)
- Requires multiple parallel algorithm instances for practical implementation
- Memory complexity might be high for very large datasets

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a problem of significant importance to large-scale machine learning applications. It advances the state of the art in streaming submodular maximization by incorporating robustness guarantees, which is a significant contribution to the field.
