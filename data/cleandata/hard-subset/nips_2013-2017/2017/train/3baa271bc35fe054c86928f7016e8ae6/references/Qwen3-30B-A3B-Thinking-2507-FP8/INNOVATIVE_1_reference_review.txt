

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper presents a novel two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: how to create a data summary that remains useful even after arbitrary removal of up to m elements, without needing to reprocess the entire dataset. Their key innovation is a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a partitioned summary with exponentially decreasing thresholds, followed by a simple greedy query stage that achieves constant-factor approximation guarantees.

The paper's main contribution is the partitioned thresholding approach, which arranges elements into exponentially sized buckets across partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds. This structure enables robustness against any m removals while maintaining space efficiency. The theoretical analysis is particularly impressive, combining properties of the robust structure with the decreasing thresholding rule to prove a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The authors show their solution matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (a state-of-the-art non-robust streaming algorithm) even when SIEVE-STREAMING knows in advance which elements will be removed.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While robust submodular maximization has been studied (e.g., [9, 10, 11]), the streaming robust version has remained largely unexplored. Previous robust approaches (like PRO [11]) are centralized and do not scale to streaming settings. The authors' partitioned thresholding structure represents a fundamentally different approach from both robust centralized algorithms and standard streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING [8]. The two-stage framework (streaming summary + query stage) is particularly elegant and practical for real-world applications where data arrives continuously and robustness is required.

The paper's significance is strong. The problem of robust data summarization arises in numerous applications including influence maximization, recommender systems, and facility location. The authors demonstrate this through compelling experiments on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks. Their method achieves performance comparable to SIEVE-STREAMING (which has perfect knowledge of removals), showing the practical viability of their approach. The experimental results are well-designed and convincingly show the robustness of their method even when removing more than k elements from the summary.

While the approximation guarantee (0.149) is modest compared to standard submodular maximization (1-1/e ≈ 0.63), the paper justifies this trade-off through the robustness requirement. The theoretical analysis is complex but sound, and the authors provide a practical solution for estimating τ without knowing OPT(k, V\E) in advance.

I note that the memory usage (O((k + m log k) log²k)) is higher than SIEVE-STREAMING's O(k log k), but the authors correctly argue this is necessary for robustness. The paper's strength lies in its bold approach to a difficult problem that others have avoided, rather than achieving the absolute best approximation ratio.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The connection to related work is thorough, and the authors appropriately acknowledge the independent concurrent work [18], while highlighting their key theoretical and practical contributions. The experimental section is strong, with multiple scenarios demonstrating the robustness of their approach.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over methodological perfection, I find this paper highly compelling. The partitioned thresholding approach represents a genuinely new direction in submodular optimization, and the two-stage robust framework is both elegant and practically valuable. While the approximation ratio is not state-of-the-art, it is justified by the additional robustness requirement, and the authors provide a clear theoretical explanation for this trade-off.

The paper's main weakness is the modest approximation ratio, but this is an inherent limitation of the robustness requirement rather than a flaw in the approach. The authors' solution is the first to address this specific problem in the streaming setting with a constant-factor guarantee, making it a significant contribution.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of submodular optimization and streaming algorithms. The novelty of the approach, combined with strong theoretical results and practical validation, makes it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

Strengths:
- Novel two-stage robust framework for streaming submodular maximization
- Partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a bold and elegant solution
- First algorithm to provide constant-factor approximation for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Strong experimental validation showing practical performance comparable to non-robust baselines
- Clear connection to important real-world applications

Weaknesses:
- Approximation guarantee (0.149) is modest compared to standard submodular maximization
- Theoretical analysis is complex, which may limit accessibility

Recommendation: ACCEPT. This paper introduces a genuinely new concept that addresses a meaningful problem in large-scale machine learning. The approach is bold, the theoretical contribution is significant, and the practical results validate the approach. The minor weaknesses are outweighed by the novelty and significance of the contribution.
