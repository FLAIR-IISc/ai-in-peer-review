This paper tackles the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, aiming to find a representative subset of data resilient to element removals, without requiring full dataset access. The core idea is a two-stage approach: a streaming algorithm builds a robust summary with a partitioned thresholding scheme, and a query stage uses a greedy algorithm on the summary to respond to removals. The paper establishes theoretical bounds guaranteeing a constant-factor approximation and demonstrates empirical performance on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks.

The work builds upon existing literature in submodular maximization (Nemhauser et al., 1978; Krause et al., 2010) and streaming algorithms (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014). It extends recent advances in robust submodular maximization (Krause et al., 2008; Orlin et al., 2016; Bogunovic et al., 2017) to a streaming setting. The authors appropriately position their contribution relative to SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014) and PRO (Bogunovic et al., 2017), highlighting improvements in memory requirements. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the two-stage setting.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of partitioned thresholding with a streaming approach to *robust* submodular maximization appears novel.  The specific design of the partition structure and the decreasing threshold scheme represent a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the proposed algorithm. The theorem and accompanying proof sketch are well-presented. The extension to address the unknown `f(OPT(k, V \ E))` via parallel instances is a practical and valuable addition.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The algorithms (1 and 2) are clearly described, and the problem statement is well-defined.  Figures (though not referenced in the text) visually illustrate the partitioning scheme.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets (dominating set and movie recommendation) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison against relevant baselines (SIEVE-STREAMING, RANDOM, STAR-T-SIEVE) is comprehensive.
* **Practical Considerations:** Addressing the impracticality of knowing `f(OPT(k, V \ E))` upfront by proposing a parallel algorithm shows attention to real-world applicability.  Demonstrating robustness even with more removals than theoretically guaranteed suggests practical resilience.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Figure References:** The figures (1 and 2) are not referenced in the main text, diminishing their impact. Describing them briefly would greatly enhance understanding.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper provides a theoretical bound on `w`, the sensitivity of performance to this parameter (and others like `tau`) isn't thoroughly explored in the experiments. A more detailed ablation study would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** While memory complexity is discussed, the computational complexity of both the streaming and query stages isn't explicitly stated. This would be valuable for understanding scalability.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While comparisons are present, the choice of baselines could be broadened. For example, comparing against online submodular maximization algorithms could provide further insights.
* **Minor Notation Issues:** A few instances of inconsistent notation (e.g., using "V \E" instead of "V \ E" consistently) could be clarified.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for a challenging problem – robust streaming submodular maximization.
* The theoretical guarantees are strong and the empirical results are compelling.
* The work addresses a practical need for robust data summarization in large-scale settings.
* The inclusion of a practical extension to handle unknown optimal values demonstrates thoughtfulness.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of explicit computational complexity analysis is a minor drawback.
* The limited exploration of parameter sensitivity and baseline comparisons doesn't fully showcase the robustness of the method.
* The unreferenced figures hinder accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of submodular optimization. The proposed algorithm is novel, well-motivated, and supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. While there are minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the work clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
