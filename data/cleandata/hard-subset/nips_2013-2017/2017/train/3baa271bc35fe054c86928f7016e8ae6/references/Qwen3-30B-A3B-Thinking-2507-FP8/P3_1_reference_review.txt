

# Peer Review: "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary
This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm (STAR-T and STAR-T-GREEDY) that constructs a robust summary set S in a single pass over the data, and then efficiently produces a solution after any m elements are removed from S. The key innovation is a partitioned thresholding approach where elements are placed in buckets organized into partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds. The paper demonstrates that their algorithm achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149(1-1/log k)) while using O((k + m log k) log k) memory, which is better than the O(mk log k) memory of concurrent work [18].

## Strengths

1. **Novel Algorithmic Design**: The partitioned thresholding approach is a significant contribution to the field. The idea of organizing elements into partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds is elegant and well-motivated by the robustness requirement. This structure enables them to handle the robustness constraint while maintaining sublinear memory.

2. **Strong Theoretical Guarantees**: The paper provides careful theoretical analysis with a clear proof structure. The authors establish a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for their robust streaming algorithm, which is non-trivial given the additional robustness constraint. The proof technique cleverly combines properties of the partitioned structure with greedy thresholding.

3. **Well-Designed Experiments**: The experiments on two distinct applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) convincingly demonstrate the practical value of their approach. The key finding that their method performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed) is particularly compelling.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The pseudocode for the algorithms is clear and the problem statement is well-defined.

## Weaknesses

1. **Approximation Factor**: The approximation factor of 0.149 is significantly lower than the 0.63 achieved by standard greedy algorithms for non-robust submodular maximization. While the paper acknowledges this is a trade-off for robustness, it could better explore whether this gap is inherent to the robust setting or if there's room for improvement. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of this trade-off.

2. **Memory Complexity Discrepancy**: The paper claims O((k + m log k) log² k) memory in the title, but Theorem 4.1 shows O((k + m log k) log k) memory. The discrepancy between the title and the proof should be clarified.

3. **Practical vs. Theoretical w**: The theoretical analysis requires w ≥ ⌈4dlog kem/k⌉, but the experiments use w=1 with good results. The authors don't adequately explain this disconnect between theory and practice, which weakens the theoretical contribution.

4. **Comparison to Robust Streaming Baselines**: While they compare against SIEVE-STREAMING (which doesn't handle robustness), they don't compare against a robust version of SIEVE-STREAMING. This would provide a more direct comparison for the robust streaming setting.

## Originality and Significance

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on streaming submodular optimization. While there is related work on robust submodular maximization (e.g., [11]) and streaming submodular maximization (e.g., [8]), the combination of robustness with streaming is novel. The partitioned thresholding approach is a significant methodological innovation that enables their results. The theoretical guarantees are non-trivial for the robust streaming setting, and the practical performance demonstrates real-world relevance.

The problem addressed is important for applications where data might be subject to removals (e.g., network failures, user preferences changing), making this work relevant to practitioners. The two-stage approach is particularly valuable as it allows for precomputation of a robust summary that can be used for multiple query scenarios.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization. The theoretical results are sound, the experimental validation is compelling, and the problem addressed is both important and novel. The main weakness is the relatively low approximation factor, but this is a reasonable trade-off for the robustness constraint.

The paper should be accepted to NIPS. I recommend minor revisions to clarify the memory complexity discrepancy in the title and to better explain the practical use of w=1 despite the theoretical requirement.

## Final Assessment

The paper addresses an important problem with a novel algorithmic approach that provides both theoretical guarantees and practical performance. While the approximation factor is lower than standard non-robust methods, the trade-off is justified by the robustness requirement. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical value of the approach. This paper represents a significant advance in robust streaming submodular optimization and would be valuable to the NIPS community.
