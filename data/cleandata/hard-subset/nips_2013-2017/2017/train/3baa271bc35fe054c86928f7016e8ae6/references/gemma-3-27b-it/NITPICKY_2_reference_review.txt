This paper presents a two-stage streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, addressing the scenario where a representative subset needs to remain effective even after the removal of a certain number of elements. While the problem statement is well-motivated and the proposed approach builds upon existing streaming and robust submodular maximization techniques, the review reveals a substantial number of minor flaws and a lack of complete clarity in certain aspects, leading to a recommendation of rejection.

**Summary:** The authors propose STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that constructs a partitioned summary with decreasing greedy thresholds, and STAR-T-GREEDY, a query algorithm that operates on this summary. The theoretical analysis provides an approximation guarantee, and experiments demonstrate competitive performance against SIEVE-STREAMING.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Robustness is a crucial consideration in many real-world applications of submodular maximization, and the streaming setting is essential for large-scale datasets.
*   **Clear Structure:** The partitioned thresholding approach is conceptually sound and provides a framework for balancing robustness and efficiency.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee, while somewhat weak (c=0.149 as k approaches infinity), establishes a theoretical foundation for the algorithm.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation problems provide some evidence of practical effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notation and Clarity:** The paper suffers from inconsistent notation and a lack of clarity in several places. For instance, ‘w’ is introduced as a memory parameter but its role in the asymptotic analysis and practical implementation is not immediately obvious. The precise relationship between ‘w’ and the actual memory usage isn’t clear, especially considering the mention of O((k + m log k) log k) memory complexity.  The explanation of τ is initially confusing, as it's defined in terms of an unknown value f(OPT(k, V \ E)).
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems highly sensitive to the choice of parameters, especially ‘w’ and τ. The claim that w=1 works well in practice is not fully substantiated and feels ad-hoc. The connection between τ and the desired approximation ratio isn't clearly articulated beyond the initial definition.
*   **Approximation Ratio:** The constant factor of 0.149 is extremely small, rendering the approximation guarantee largely theoretical. While the authors acknowledge this diminishes as k approaches infinity, it significantly limits the practical usefulness of the algorithm. The impact of the '1/dlog ke' term further reduces this already weak bound.
*   **Proof Details:** The proofs are relegated to supplementary material, hindering assessment of their correctness and rigor. While referencing supplementary material is acceptable, key insights of the proof should be explained in the main paper. The proof sketch is too high-level to gain confidence in the correctness of the theoretical claims.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. The parameter choices for SIEVE-STREAMING and RANDOM are not fully described. The impact of different removal strategies (random vs. greedy) on the performance of each algorithm should be discussed in more detail. The claim of "matching or outperforming" SIEVE-STREAMING is not consistently supported by the figures. Figures 2(a,c) and 2(b,d) are nearly identical, presenting the same data which appears to be a mistake.
*   **Related Work:** The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive. While the authors mention relevant papers, a more detailed comparison of their approach to existing methods (e.g., [18]) in terms of both theoretical guarantees and practical performance would be beneficial.
*   **Minor Issues:** There are several minor formatting and grammatical errors throughout the paper.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in submodular maximization.
*   Presents a novel algorithm with a theoretical guarantee.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance in limited experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Extremely weak approximation guarantee.
*   Lack of clarity in notation and explanation.
*   Parameter sensitivity and lack of practical guidance for parameter tuning.
*   Insufficient detail in the proof and experimental setup.
*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the relevance of the problem and the novelty of the approach, the numerous weaknesses significantly outweigh the strengths. The extremely weak approximation guarantee, combined with the lack of clarity and practical guidance, make this paper unsuitable for NIPS. The experimental results, while promising, are not compelling enough to justify acceptance given the other shortcomings.  The authors need to significantly improve the clarity of the presentation, strengthen the theoretical results (or provide a more compelling justification for the weak guarantee), and conduct more rigorous experiments before resubmitting.



**Recommendation:** Reject.
