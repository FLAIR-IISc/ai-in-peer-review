

# Review: Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where the goal is to construct a small summary set S that remains useful even after the removal of up to m elements. The authors propose a two-stage procedure: (1) a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs S in one pass over the data, and (2) a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that, given any removal of m elements from S, returns a set Z of size at most k with a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The key technical contribution is a partitioned thresholding approach where the summary is arranged into partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds.

The work builds upon previous research on robust submodular maximization [9, 10, 11] and streaming submodular maximization [8, 12, 13]. While [11] provides a centralized robust algorithm with similar structure, the authors extend it to the streaming setting with a novel analysis that allows robustness to any number of removals m as long as they use O(m log² k) memory. The paper also relates to concurrent work [18], which provides a 1/2-ε approximation but requires O(mk log k/ε) memory.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper presents a novel algorithmic approach for robust streaming submodular maximization. The partitioned thresholding structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is elegant and well-motivated by the problem requirements.

2. **Theoretical Guarantees**: The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis proving a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for their algorithm. The proof is well-structured, with clear lemmas and careful handling of different cases. The extension to handle the unknown parameter τ is particularly well-executed.

3. **Practical Validation**: The experiments on two real-world applications (dominating set and personalized movie recommendation) demonstrate that the algorithm performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING (which has the advantage of knowing removals in advance), showing the practical relevance of their approach.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, with clear algorithm descriptions and a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to experiments. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses
1. **Approximation Ratio**: The approximation ratio of 0.149 is significantly lower than the standard greedy algorithm's 1-1/e ≈ 0.632 for non-robust submodular maximization. This might limit the practical applicability of the method, especially since the paper doesn't clearly explain why such a low ratio is acceptable for the robust setting.

2. **Space Complexity**: The space complexity O((k + m log k) log² k) is higher than the non-robust SIEVE-STREAMING's O(k log k). While the authors note that their dependence on k is logarithmic rather than linear, this still represents a substantial increase in space requirements that should be more thoroughly discussed in the context of practical applications.

3. **Experimental Comparison**: The experiments compare against SIEVE-STREAMING that knows removals in advance, but don't include a comparison with the non-robust SIEVE-STREAMING on the same data (before any removals). This would better illustrate the trade-off between robustness and performance. Additionally, the paper doesn't discuss how the parameter w should be chosen in practice (beyond noting that w=1 works well).

4. **Theoretical Bound Tightness**: The authors show that their approach outperforms the theoretical bound in practice (e.g., "our method is more resilient in practice than what the proved bound alone would guarantee"). It would be valuable to provide a more detailed discussion of why the theoretical bound is loose compared to empirical results.

## Recommendations for Improvement
1. **Clarify the Practical Trade-off**: The paper should explicitly discuss the trade-off between robustness and approximation ratio. A table comparing the approximation ratio, space complexity, and practical performance for different methods would be valuable.

2. **Expand Experimental Section**: Include comparisons with the non-robust SIEVE-STREAMING on the original data (before removals) to better illustrate the cost of robustness. Also, provide more details on how to choose the parameter w in practice.

3. **Refine Theoretical Analysis**: The authors could explore whether the approximation ratio can be improved by modifying the thresholding scheme or by using a different partitioning strategy. This would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

4. **Discuss Computational Complexity**: While the query stage uses a simple greedy algorithm, the paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the streaming algorithm or the query stage in detail. Adding this would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's practical feasibility.

## Overall Assessment
This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the area of streaming submodular optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the experimental validation demonstrates practical relevance. The main limitation is the relatively low approximation ratio (0.149), which may be a concern for practitioners. However, the authors effectively demonstrate that their approach achieves competitive performance in practice despite this theoretical limitation.

The paper's strength lies in its elegant solution to a challenging problem with important applications in large-scale machine learning. While the approximation ratio is lower than in non-robust settings, the robustness guarantee is valuable in many real-world scenarios where data may be removed or corrupted.

I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses noted above in the camera-ready version, particularly regarding the approximation ratio discussion and experimental comparisons. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and aligns well with NIPS's focus on high-quality theoretical and practical contributions to machine learning.
