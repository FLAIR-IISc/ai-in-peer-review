- Paper proposes STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm with exponentially sized partitions and thresholding, plus STAR-T-GREEDY for query-time robust selection after up to m deletions.  
- Main theoretical guarantee: constant-factor approximation f(Z) ≥ c·f(OPT) with c → 0.149 as k → ∞, using space O((k + m log k) log k) for a single τ and O((k + m log k) log^2 k) with parallel τ guessing.  
- Space–approximation trade-off: substantially lower memory than concurrent robust streaming [18] (O(m k log k/ε)) at the cost of a smaller approximation factor.  
- Empirically, STAR-T often matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (even when SIEVE knows E) and is close to offline GREEDY on smaller instances.  
- Novelty: clean synthesis of PRO’s partitioned structure with SIEVE-STREAMING thresholding to achieve deletion robustness in streaming.  
- Strength: simple two-stage design (stream summary + GREEDY) that supports multiple deletions without rescanning the stream.  
- Strength: strong memory efficiency scaling as O(m polylog k + k polylog k), useful when k is large.  
- Strength: experiments on real datasets show practical effectiveness and that small constants (e.g., w=1) can work well.  
- Weakness: low approximation constant (≈0.149) compared to ~0.5−ε in [18] and 0.387 in centralized robust methods.  
- Weakness: per-element processing may require checking O(wk) buckets; per-element oracle/time complexity is not analyzed or reported.  
- Weakness: STAR-T needs τ depending on f(OPT) during streaming; parallel thresholding is proposed but range construction and practical overhead are under-specified.  
- Clarity issue: inconsistency in threshold rule (τ/2^i vs τ/min{2^i,k}); needs unification and justification.  
- Clarity issue: fluctuating space bounds between theorem and intro/conclusion; clarify when extra log factor arises (parallel τ).  
- Clarity issue: τ expression in Theorem 4.1 is garbled and needs a clean, interpretable formula tied to the lemmas.  
- Concern: theoretical requirement w ≥ ⌈4 m log k/k⌉ contrasts with experiments that use w=1 even for m≥k; sensitivity to w and m/k is not systematically studied.  
- Baselines: no empirical comparison to concurrent robust streaming [18]; a discussion or experiments on memory–approximation trade-offs vs [18] is missing.  
- Quality: theoretical development is generally sound, with a convincing two-case analysis, but some proof details and constants are deferred and need clearer exposition.  
- Experimental limitations: lack of reported wall-clock times, oracle call counts, and details on whether parallel τ instances were used.  
- Clarity suggestion: add a complexity section quantifying per-element oracle calls to aid reproducibility.  
- Originality: the partitioned thresholded streaming design is a novel and natural contribution combining prior ideas.  
- Significance: addresses practical needs for deletion-robust summarization with strong memory guarantees and potential impact for large-scale systems.  
- Questions for authors: specify the exact threshold rule used and reconcile with proofs.  
- Questions for authors: explain how τ was chosen in experiments, whether parallel instances were run, and the added memory/time overhead.  
- Questions for authors: report per-element oracle call stats (mean/max), scaling with k and w, and whether lazy evaluation was used.  
- Questions for authors: provide sensitivity analysis to w and m/k and memory–accuracy trade-offs.  
- Questions for authors: provide any preliminary comparison or discussion relative to [18], or synthetic experiments showing memory–approximation trade-offs.  
- Pros: principled, memory-efficient algorithm with solid theory and useful two-stage design.  
- Cons: conservative approximation factor, unclear runtime/oracle complexity, and presentation/experimental gaps.  
- Overall assessment: solid and timely contribution with compelling space–quality trade-offs; acceptance recommended pending clarifications and additional runtime/sensitivity analyses.
