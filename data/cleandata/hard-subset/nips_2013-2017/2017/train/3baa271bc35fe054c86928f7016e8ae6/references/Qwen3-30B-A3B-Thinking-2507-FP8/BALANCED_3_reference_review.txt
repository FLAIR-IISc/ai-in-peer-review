

# Review: Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach

This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, proposing a two-stage approach that constructs a robust summary in the streaming phase and then efficiently handles arbitrary removals of up to m elements in the query phase. The authors introduce a novel partitioned thresholding approach (STAR-T) that organizes the summary into partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds.

**Quality:** The paper presents a theoretically sound algorithm with a rigorous analysis. The authors prove that STAR-T stores O((k + m log k) log² k) elements while achieving a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149(1-1/log k)) for the robust submodular maximization problem. The proof strategy is well-structured, with careful handling of two cases (partitions with many full buckets vs. partitions with mostly empty buckets). The experimental results on two real-world applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) demonstrate that the proposed method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which has the advantage of knowing which elements will be removed), providing strong empirical validation. However, the theoretical approximation guarantee (0.149) is significantly lower than the 0.5-ε guarantee of SIEVE-STREAMING for the non-robust problem, which may limit practical appeal.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, contributions, and algorithm descriptions are precise. The partitioned thresholding approach is well-explained with a helpful illustration (Figure 1). The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented effectively with appropriate baselines. The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the exponentially decreasing thresholds are necessary for robustness, and the proof sketch would be more accessible with a simplified version of the key lemmas.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on robust submodular maximization and streaming algorithms. It builds on prior work [9, 10, 11] on robust submodular maximization and [8, 12, 13] on streaming submodular maximization, but introduces a novel partitioned thresholding approach that allows handling robustness against any number of removals m with space complexity that depends logarithmically on k (O((k + m log k) log² k)), while [18] uses O(mk log k/ε) memory. The authors properly contextualize their work against related literature, including the concurrent work [18].

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to large-scale machine learning applications where data is too large to reprocess after removals. The two-stage approach (precomputation followed by query) is particularly practical for real-world applications like personalized recommendations where user preferences change dynamically. The experimental results on two distinct applications demonstrate the practical utility of the approach, showing that it can match or outperform baselines that have the advantage of knowing which elements will be removed.

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated problem addressing a practical challenge in large-scale data summarization
- Novel partitioned thresholding approach with rigorous theoretical analysis
- Comprehensive experimental validation on two distinct real-world applications
- Clear distinction from related work, including concurrent work [18]
- Demonstrates the practical utility of a single robust summary for multiple query scenarios

**Weaknesses:**
- The approximation guarantee (0.149) is significantly lower than non-robust streaming algorithms (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING's 0.5-ε), which may limit practical adoption
- The theoretical analysis is complex and may be difficult to follow without the supplementary material
- The paper doesn't fully explain why their approach achieves better robustness despite the lower approximation guarantee
- The experimental section could provide more detail on parameter sensitivity (e.g., how w affects performance)
- The comparison with [18] is mentioned but not deeply explored

**Recommendation:**
I recommend this paper for acceptance with minor revisions. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates practical value. The theoretical contribution is novel and significant for the robust submodular maximization problem in streaming settings. While the approximation guarantee is lower than non-robust algorithms, this is expected given the additional robustness requirement.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Provide a more intuitive explanation of why the exponentially decreasing thresholds are necessary for robustness.
2. Include a comparison of the approximation guarantees and space complexities across all relevant methods in a table for easier reference.
3. Add a discussion of the practical tradeoffs between the approximation guarantee and robustness in real-world applications.
4. Expand the experimental section to include sensitivity analysis of key parameters (like w) and more detailed results for different values of m.
5. Clarify the relationship with the concurrent work [18] in the main text, not just in the references.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization, with clear theoretical and practical significance. The authors have made a meaningful advance in addressing the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting.
