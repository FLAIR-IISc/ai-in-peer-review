

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper proposes a two-stage algorithm for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. The authors introduce a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary in one pass, followed by a query stage (STAR-T-GREEDY) that provides a solution after arbitrary removal of up to m elements from the summary. The theoretical contribution claims a constant-factor approximation guarantee of approximately 0.149 for the robust problem, with memory complexity O((k + m log k) log²k).

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a relevant problem in large-scale machine learning, where data summarization is needed with robustness guarantees. The problem formulation is clear, and the theoretical analysis is technically sound, with careful handling of the partitioned thresholding approach. The experimental section provides reasonable comparisons against SIEVE-STREAMING, which is a strong baseline for submodular streaming. The authors demonstrate practical utility through applications in dominating set problems and movie recommendation.

**Weaknesses:** The paper's main weakness is the lack of significant improvement over established methods. The theoretical guarantee (0.149) is substantially worse than the (0.5-ε) guarantee of SIEVE-STREAMING for the non-robust problem, and the paper acknowledges that SIEVE-STREAMING knows in advance which elements will be removed, giving it a significant advantage. The claimed "matching or outperforming" of SIEVE-STREAMING in experiments is misleading, as the baseline has an unfair advantage by knowing removals in advance. The memory complexity O((k + m log k) log²k) is also not clearly better than concurrent work [18], which achieves similar guarantees with O(mk log k/ε) memory (though the paper incorrectly claims their memory dependence on k is logarithmic rather than linear).

The novelty claim is weak. The paper builds extensively on SIEVE-STREAMING [8] and previous robust submodular work [11, 18], with the main innovation being a partitioned thresholding scheme. However, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why this partitioning scheme leads to meaningful improvements in practice or theory. The experimental results show only marginal improvements over SIEVE-STREAMING, which is not sufficient justification for a new algorithm in a field where small constant-factor improvements are typically expected to be substantial.

The paper also makes an unsupported claim about practical robustness: "Despite having |E| >> k in the above scenarios, our performance remains robust." This contradicts the theoretical guarantee that depends on m (the number of removals), and the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support this claim.

**Significance and Originality:** The problem is significant, but the paper does not advance the state of the art meaningfully. The constant factor (0.149) is substantially worse than existing non-robust streaming methods (0.5-ε), and the practical benefits are marginal at best. The paper does not convincingly differentiate itself from the concurrent work [18], which solves the same problem with similar guarantees. The authors reference [18] only in the final paragraph, which is insufficient for a paper claiming to be the first to solve this problem.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound, but the claims about practical performance are not sufficiently supported. The paper does not provide enough evidence to justify the proposed method over simpler alternatives (like running SIEVE-STREAMING on the precomputed summary, which the authors call STAR-T-SIEVE). The experimental section lacks ablation studies to show why the partitioned thresholding approach is necessary.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good technical exposition of the algorithm and analysis. The experimental setup is reasonable, though the comparison with SIEVE-STREAMING is unfair due to the baseline's knowledge of removals.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and contains technically sound analysis, it fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or improvement over existing methods. The constant factor of 0.149 is substantially worse than the 0.5-ε guarantee of SIEVE-STREAMING, and the practical benefits are marginal at best. The paper does not convincingly justify the need for a new algorithm when existing approaches (even with the unfair baseline advantage) perform comparably. In the context of NIPS, where significant theoretical or practical improvements are expected, this work falls short.

The authors would need to substantially strengthen their claims by either:
1. Demonstrating a significant theoretical improvement over existing methods
2. Showing substantial practical benefits that outweigh the worse theoretical guarantee
3. Providing a more convincing comparison with the concurrent work [18]

Without these, the paper does not represent a meaningful contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization.
