## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm, STAR-T, which first constructs a robust summary of the data using a partitioned thresholding scheme, and then uses a greedy algorithm on this summary to provide a solution after an arbitrary removal of elements. They provide theoretical guarantees showing a constant-factor approximation, and demonstrate through experiments that their approach performs competitively with existing methods, even when those methods have prior knowledge of the removals.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Robustness is a critical concern in many real-world applications of submodular maximization, and the streaming setting adds further practical relevance.
* **Novel Approach:** The partitioned thresholding scheme with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a clever idea for balancing space usage, robustness, and approximation quality.  The analysis appears sophisticated and successfully handles the challenges of this approach.
* **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:**  The paper provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is a strong result for this challenging problem.  The analysis seems rigorous, although detailed verification requires careful scrutiny of the supplementary material.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on both the dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the algorithm, particularly its ability to compete with algorithms that *know* the removals in advance.
* **Interactive Setting:** The discussion of interactive applications (e.g., user preference changes in movie recommendation) highlights the practical utility of the two-stage approach.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Constant:** While a constant-factor approximation is guaranteed, the actual constant (0.149) is relatively small, especially as k grows large. The dependency on `1/dlog k` in the constant might limit practical performance for very large `k`.
* **Space Complexity:** The space complexity of O((k + m log k) log k) is reasonably good, but the parameter `w` and its impact on the constant factors are not entirely clear.  The practical performance with w=1 is encouraging, but the theoretical guarantee depends on w being sufficiently large.
* **Comparison to Baselines:** While the comparison to SIEVE-STREAMING is valuable, it would be stronger to include comparisons to other relevant algorithms for robust submodular maximization, particularly those that *don't* require advance knowledge of removals, even if they are not streaming.  The RANDOM baseline is useful, but less informative than comparing to other algorithms designed for robustness.
* **Assumptions in Experiments:** The experimental setup has some assumptions (e.g., removing elements greedily) which, while reasonable, might not be representative of all real-world scenarios.
* **Lack of Scalability Experiments:** It would be beneficial to see experiments on even larger datasets to better understand the scalability of the approach, as the theoretical complexity could become a bottleneck for extremely large `k` and `m`.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically well-supported approach. The combination of theoretical guarantees and empirical validation is convincing. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small, and there are some areas for improvement in the experimental evaluation, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and practically relevant problem.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated algorithmic approach.
* Provides strong theoretical guarantees.
* Demonstrates competitive performance through empirical evaluation.
* Clear and well-written.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
* The space complexity, while reasonable, could be a limitation for very large-scale problems.
* The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with comparisons to a broader range of baselines and scalability tests.

**Rating:**

**Strong Accept (8/10)**

I believe this paper is a valuable contribution to NIPS and deserves to be accepted. The authors have made a significant advance in the area of robust submodular maximization, and the work is likely to be of interest to a broad audience of researchers and practitioners.  I recommend the authors consider addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above in a final revision.  Specifically, discussing the practical implications of the constant factor and exploring scalability on even larger datasets would be beneficial.
