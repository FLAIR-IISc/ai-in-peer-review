

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

The paper proposes a two-stage algorithm for robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The first stage (STAR-T) creates a robust summary set S using a one-pass streaming algorithm that partitions elements into buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds. The second stage (STAR-T-GREEDY) uses a simple greedy algorithm on S \ E (after any removal of up to m elements) to produce a solution of size at most k. The authors prove that their approach achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149(1-1/log k)) while using O((k + m log k) log^2 k) space.

This work builds upon SIEVE-STREAMING [8] for non-robust submodular maximization and extends it to the robust setting. The authors relate their work to [11] (PRO) which proposed a centralized robust algorithm, and to [18] which independently developed a concurrent streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization. The authors note that [18] provides a 1/2 - ε approximation but uses O(mk log k/ε) memory, whereas their approach has logarithmic dependence on k.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with careful proofs of the approximation guarantees. The authors address the robustness requirement by considering two cases: when at least half of a partition's buckets are full, and when no such partition exists. The proof strategy combines properties of submodular functions with greedy approximation guarantees. The paper also provides a practical extension to handle unknown f(OPT(k, V \ E)) by maintaining multiple parallel instances. The experimental validation on two applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) demonstrates competitive performance against SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows removals in advance) and random selection.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, algorithms, and theoretical results. The figures effectively illustrate the bucket structure. The writing is precise and accessible to the NIPS audience. The supplementary material provides additional details for the proofs, enhancing reproducibility.

**Originality:** The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds represents a novel contribution to the robust streaming submodular maximization problem. While [18] independently developed a similar algorithm, the authors' approach with its specific thresholding scheme and analysis appears distinct. The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from prior work, particularly [11] and [18]. The two-stage framework for robustness is a meaningful extension of streaming submodular maximization.

**Significance:** The problem of robust submodular maximization in streaming settings is significant for large-scale applications where data is too large to store. The authors demonstrate practical utility through experiments on real-world problems. However, the approximation guarantee (0.149) is substantially weaker than the standard (1-e^-1) ≈ 0.632 guarantee for non-robust submodular maximization, which may limit practical adoption. The memory requirement (O((k + m log k) log^2 k)) is higher than some prior work, though the dependence on k is logarithmic rather than linear.

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear approximation guarantees
- Practical implementation and validation on two real-world applications
- Clear exposition of the partitioned thresholding approach
- Careful comparison with relevant prior work
- Demonstrated robustness against multiple types of element removal

**Weaknesses:**
- The approximation guarantee (0.149) is significantly weaker than standard results for non-robust submodular maximization
- Memory complexity (O((k + m log k) log^2 k)) is higher than [18]'s O(mk log k/ε) (though with different k dependence)
- The practical advantage over non-robust algorithms when robustness is not required is not demonstrated
- The paper could better clarify the relationship and differences with [18] (which was published concurrently)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel algorithmic approach for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Solid theoretical foundation with complete proof of approximation guarantee
- Practical validation showing competitive performance against strong baselines
- Addresses an important problem in large-scale data summarization
- Clear and well-structured presentation

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The approximation guarantee is substantially weaker than standard results
- Memory complexity is higher than some prior work (though with different dependence on k)
- Limited demonstration of practical advantage in scenarios where robustness is not required
- The paper could better differentiate from concurrent work [18]

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. While the approximation guarantee is weaker than standard non-robust results, the two-stage framework and partitioned thresholding approach are novel and well-justified. The experimental results demonstrate practical viability. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem. The main limitation is the weaker approximation guarantee compared to non-robust approaches, but this is a necessary trade-off for the robustness requirement.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization with a novel approach to robustness. The theoretical and empirical results are sound, and the work is well-positioned within the context of recent NIPS research.
