This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure – STAR-T – which first constructs a robust summary of the data in a single pass, and then uses a greedy algorithm on this summary to find a solution after removing elements. The core idea is a partitioned thresholding approach, which organizes the summary into buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds. The theoretical analysis provides approximation guarantees, and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance against existing methods.

The paper is well-motivated, tackling a practically relevant problem with strong connections to data summarization, influence maximization, and recommendation systems. The introduction clearly articulates the challenges of robustness and streaming, and positions the contribution effectively. The problem statement is precise, and the related work section is comprehensive, referencing relevant papers in robust submodular maximization and streaming algorithms. Notably, the authors carefully compare their work to both SIEVE-STREAMING and more recent approaches like PRO, acknowledging their limitations and highlighting the advantages of their method.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness and the novelty of its approach. The partitioning structure with decreasing thresholds is a clever idea, and the analysis appears rigorous (although a complete verification requires careful study of the supplementary material). The theoretical bounds, while complex, provide a guarantee of constant-factor approximation under certain conditions. The experiments are well-designed, covering two different applications and comparing against strong baselines. The ability to reuse the precomputed summary for multiple removal scenarios is a particularly attractive feature.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149) is quite small, which raises concerns about the practical usefulness of the algorithm. While the authors demonstrate competitive performance in their experiments, the gains over SIEVE-STREAMING are often marginal.  The dependence on parameters like `w` and the difficulty in setting the threshold `τ` (requiring an estimate of the optimal value) could make the algorithm challenging to tune in practice.  The notation is dense and can be difficult to follow, particularly in the theoretical sections.  The description of the experimental setup, while present, could benefit from more detail (e.g., specific parameter settings, statistical significance tests). Additionally, the discussion of the parallel variant in Theorem 4.5 feels somewhat abrupt and could be expanded. The claim that their method is more resilient than the theoretical bounds suggest warrants further investigation and justification. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to a relevant problem.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with approximation guarantees.
*   Competitive performance against strong baselines in practical experiments.
*   Addresses an important gap in the literature on robust submodular maximization.
*   Potential for real-world applications in data summarization and recommendation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is very small.
*   Performance gains over existing methods are often marginal.
*   Parameter tuning and threshold estimation could be challenging.
*   Dense notation and complex theoretical sections might limit accessibility.
*   Limited exploration of the practical implications of the resilience exceeding theoretical bounds.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The technical novelty and rigorous analysis are commendable. However, the small constant factor in the approximation guarantee and the marginal performance gains raise some concerns.  I believe the paper is currently borderline. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should focus on improving the constant factor in the approximation guarantee, providing a more detailed analysis of the experimental results (including statistical significance), and addressing the concerns about parameter tuning and practical applicability. A more accessible presentation of the theoretical sections would also be beneficial. If the authors can address these points, the paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to NIPS.
