### NIPS Review

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure to address this challenge. In the first stage, a novel single-pass streaming algorithm, STAR-T, processes the data to create a robust summary set `S`. This algorithm is based on a partitioned structure where elements are assigned to buckets based on their marginal gain against exponentially decreasing thresholds. In the second stage, after an arbitrary removal of up to `m` elements from the summary `S`, a simple greedy algorithm is run on the remaining elements to produce the final solution. The main theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee for this procedure, with a summary set of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The authors validate their approach on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, comparing it favorably to the established SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm, even when the latter is given advance knowledge of the elements to be removed.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem. Combining the challenges of large-scale data (streaming), data summarization (submodular maximization), and resilience to failures or adversarial attacks (robustness) is highly relevant to many practical machine learning applications.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a complete theoretical analysis, culminating in a constant-factor approximation guarantee (Theorem 4.1). While the constant itself is modest, proving any constant-factor guarantee in this challenging robust streaming setting is a non-trivial achievement. The memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)` is also a strong result, particularly its logarithmic dependence on `k`, which compares favorably to concurrent work.

3.  **Algorithmic Design:** The STAR-T algorithm is a clever extension of the thresholding idea from SIEVE-STREAMING [8] to the robust setting. The partitioned structure with exponentially sized buckets and decreasing thresholds is an interesting and principled way to build a summary that retains both high-value elements and a degree of diversity needed for robustness.

4.  **Experimental Setup:** The decision to compare against SIEVE-STREAMING with an oracle (i.e., knowing which elements will be removed) is a strong choice. It provides a challenging baseline and effectively demonstrates the value of the pre-computed robust summary. The results, showing that the proposed method is competitive, lend credibility to the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Low Approximation Constant:** The primary theoretical weakness is the value of the approximation constant, which is approximately 0.149. While being a constant is important theoretically, such a low value raises questions about the practical tightness of the analysis and the quality of the solution guaranteed. Established non-robust methods offer much stronger guarantees (e.g., 0.5 for SIEVE-STREAMING, 1-1/e for offline greedy), and this discrepancy is significant.

2.  **Limited Novelty of Core Concepts:** The proposed method builds heavily on existing ideas. The streaming thresholding approach is adapted from SIEVE-STREAMING [8], and the partitioned structure for robustness is noted to be similar to that in the centralized algorithm of Bogunovic et al. [11]. While the combination is novel and tailored to the robust streaming problem, the work can be viewed as an incremental, albeit skillful, synthesis of established techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach.

3.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice in Experiments:** There is a notable disconnect between the theoretical requirements and the experimental implementation. The theory requires the memory parameter `w` to scale with `m` and `log k` (`w ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem / k ⌉`) to prove the robustness guarantee. However, the experiments are conducted with `w=1`, which the authors note works well. This suggests either that the theoretical analysis is loose or that the algorithm's practical performance relies on factors not fully captured by the worst-case analysis. This weakens the validation of the theoretical model.

4.  **Scope of Experimental Validation:** The experiments are conducted on moderately sized datasets (e.g., Twitter and Amazon graphs with ~1.7M edges). While standard, these datasets may not fully represent the massive-scale scenarios where streaming algorithms are indispensable. Furthermore, the baselines are limited. A comparison against the concurrent work of Mirzasoleiman et al. [18] would have been valuable, though its absence is understandable. The comparison is primarily against a non-robust streaming method, which, while cleverly adapted, is not a direct competitor in robust optimization.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and structured. The problem is clearly defined, and the proposed two-stage algorithm is explained logically. The illustration in Figure 1 is helpful. However, there is a minor ambiguity between the text and the pseudocode. The text describes the threshold for partition `i` as `τ/2i`, whereas Algorithm 1 (line 5) uses `τ/min{2i, k}`. While these are identical for `i` where `2i < k`, the distinction could be clarified.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid and technically sound contribution to an important and challenging problem. The authors develop a novel algorithm by combining and extending established ideas in a clever way, and they support it with both a formal approximation guarantee and convincing experimental results against a strong baseline.

However, the work's significance is tempered by the very low theoretical approximation constant and the fact that its core ideas are extensions of prior work. The gap between the theory's requirements for memory (`w`) and the experimental setup also raises questions about the tightness of the analysis.

Despite these criticisms, the paper successfully demonstrates a practical and effective method for robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming model, which is a valuable contribution. It advances the state of the art in this specific subfield and is likely to be built upon by future research. The work meets the standards for NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical problem at the intersection of streaming, submodularity, and robustness.
*   Provides the first (or among the first) single-pass streaming algorithms for this problem with a constant-factor approximation guarantee and a strong memory bound.
*   The algorithm is novel in its specific construction, combining ideas from different sub-literatures.
*   Experimental results are positive and the comparison against an oracle-enhanced baseline is compelling.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The theoretical approximation constant is very low (0.149), limiting the strength of the guarantee.
*   The contribution can be seen as incremental, combining ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING [8] and robust maximization [11].
*   The experimental validation does not fully align with the theoretical assumptions (e.g., the value of `w`), suggesting a potential weakness in the analysis.
