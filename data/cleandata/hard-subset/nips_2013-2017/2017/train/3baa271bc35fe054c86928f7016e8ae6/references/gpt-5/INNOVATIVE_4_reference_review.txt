Summary
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting with a two-stage framework. Stage 1 (streaming): STAR-T maintains a succinct robust summary S using a novel partitioned structure of buckets whose sizes grow exponentially across partitions, coupled with exponentially decreasing thresholds. Each arriving element is placed into the first non-full bucket whose marginal gain passes the partition’s threshold; otherwise it’s discarded. Stage 2 (query): after any adversarial removal E of up to m elements, the algorithm runs GREEDY only on S \ E to extract a k-set.

The main theorem guarantees, under a single pass and sublinear memory, a constant-factor approximation to OPT(k, V \ E) after removal of any |E| ≤ m. The stated approximation is 0.149 · (1 − 1/⌈log k⌉), and the space is O((k + m log k) log k) when OPT is known up to a constant factor (with a standard parallel-guessing scheme adding another O(log k) factor). Memory scales only logarithmically with k and linearly with m (up to log factors). Empirically, on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation, the method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING—even when SIEVE-STREAMING is told E in advance—and the precomputed summary can be reused for multiple removals.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on greedy-thresholding ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING for non-robust streaming, and on partition/bucket structures reminiscent of PRO in centralized robust submodular maximization, but combines them in a genuinely new streaming algorithm and analysis for robustness. It also contrasts with concurrent work on deletion-robust streaming (1/2 − ε guarantee) that uses O(mk log k/ε) memory; here the memory dependence on k is only logarithmic, at the cost of a smaller constant.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual contribution: The partitioned thresholding structure for robust streaming is elegant and, to my knowledge, new. The analysis leverages the structure in a nontrivial way to get robustness against arbitrary removals with only logarithmic dependence on k.
- Theoretical guarantees: One pass, sublinear space in n, logarithmic in k, linear in m (up to logs), and a constant-factor approximation for arbitrary removals. The query-time solution uses standard GREEDY on the summary—simple and practical.
- Practical relevance: Robust streaming summaries are important for applications where re-accessing the full data is infeasible and multiple “right-to-be-forgotten” style removals happen.
- Empirical validation: On two meaningful tasks, the method matches or slightly improves on strong streaming baselines even when the baseline is advantaged by knowing the deletion set. The reuse of a single summary across multiple queries/removals is compelling.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Approximation constant: The proven constant 0.149 is modest. While acceptable for a streaming-robust setting, a discussion contrasting this with the 1/2 − ε constant of the concurrent memory-heavier approach would help clarify the trade-off space.
- Dependence on OPT estimate: The algorithm requires τ ≈ OPT; the parallel guessing scheme adds a log factor in memory. The paper sketches this but could give a more concrete description of practical parameterization and the empirical overhead of multiple instances.
- Minor inconsistencies/clarity: 
  • The text alternates between thresholds τ/2^i and τ/min{2^i, k}; please unify and explain the rationale. 
  • The space bound is stated as O((k + m log k) log k) in Thm 4.1, but the abstract and conclusion mention O((k + m log k) log^2 k). Presumably the extra log comes from parallel guessing (Theorem 4.5); please reconcile explicitly.
- Empirical scope and ablations: Experiments use w = 1 even when theory suggests w should scale with m; although results are good, a sensitivity study vs. w and m would strengthen the practical story. Reporting per-element processing time and query-time would help practitioners assess deployment cost. Additional datasets or tasks (e.g., influence maximization variants) would broaden evidence.
- Comparisons: While comparing to SIEVE-STREAMING is natural, it would be useful (if feasible) to discuss or empirically compare against the concurrent deletion-robust streaming approach [18], at least in terms of theoretical trade-offs and memory/accuracy regimes.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the structure and lemmas are plausible, and the proof sketch is coherent. The constant and space trade-offs appear carefully reasoned. The few noted inconsistencies are likely presentation issues. Empirical methodology is reasonable and shows the intended robustness and reusability of the summary, though more extensive ablations would improve completeness.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized, with helpful algorithms and proof sketches. Cleaning up the threshold definition, space complexity statements, and clearly separating the single-τ vs. multi-τ variants would improve readability. More intuition behind key lemmas (especially how the bucket fullness and deletions interplay) would help non-experts.

Originality
High. The partitioned, exponentially sized buckets combined with exponentially decreasing thresholds in a streaming robust setting, and the corresponding analysis, are fresh and likely to influence follow-up work.

Significance
High. Robust streaming submodular maximization is important in practice; the proposed memory-approximation trade-off is attractive, and the two-stage design with reusable summaries is impactful.

Pros
- Novel partitioned thresholding design for robust streaming.
- One-pass, sublinear memory with only log dependence on k.
- Constant-factor robustness to arbitrary deletions, with simple query-time GREEDY.
- Solid empirical evidence and practical usefulness for multiple queries.

Cons
- Small approximation constant vs. some concurrent alternatives.
- Reliance on OPT guessing; added log factor and limited guidance on practical tuning.
- Minor presentation inconsistencies; limited ablation/comparisons.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper proposes a genuinely new and useful approach to an important problem, with solid theory and promising practice. The novelty and direction outweigh the modest constant and presentation nits. I encourage the authors to clarify the threshold and space bounds, add guidance on τ/w in practice, and, if space permits, include a brief sensitivity analysis. Confidence: high.
