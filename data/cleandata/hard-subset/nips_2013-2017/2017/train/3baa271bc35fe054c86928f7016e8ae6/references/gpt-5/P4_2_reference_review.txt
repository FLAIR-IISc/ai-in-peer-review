Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting with a cardinality constraint. The goal is to maintain a small summary S in one pass such that, after the adversarial removal of up to m elements, one can extract a size-k subset from S\E with a constant-factor approximation to OPT(k, V\E), without revisiting the stream.

The proposed algorithm STAR-T constructs S via a partitioned, exponentially thresholded structure: partitions i = 0..⌈log k⌉ contain w·⌈k/2^i⌉ buckets of capacity min{2^i, k}. Each partition has a threshold decreasing roughly as τ/2^i (or τ/min{2^i, k} in the pseudocode), and elements are inserted into the first non-full bucket whose marginal exceeds the partition’s threshold. At query time, STAR-T-GREEDY runs GREEDY on S\E to select k items.

Theoretically, with w ≈ Θ((m log k)/k), the paper proves a constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉) using a single pass and summary size |S| = O((k + m log k) log k) when τ ≈ f(OPT(k, V\E)). When τ is unknown, a parallel instantiation trick akin to SIEVE-STREAMING [Badanidiyuru et al., KDD’14] yields a factor (1+ε) loss and an extra O(log k) in space. The analysis leverages two regimes: (i) a partition with many full buckets implies one bucket remains valuable post-deletion; (ii) otherwise, balancing bounds based on the last partition’s non-full bucket yields the guarantee.

This work connects to: (i) robust (non-streaming) submodular maximization (Krause et al. JMLR 2008; Orlin et al. IPCO 2016; PRO by Bogunovic et al., ICML 2017) which uses partitioned structures but not streaming; and (ii) streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) which is non-robust. It also discusses a concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML 2017], which achieves 1/2−ε approximation but uses O(mk log k/ε) memory versus this work’s O((k + m log k) polylog k).

Strengths
- Novel algorithmic design combining partitioned buckets and exponentially decreasing thresholds to handle robustness in a single pass.
- Memory-approximation tradeoff: substantially improved space dependence on m and k (O((k + m log k) log^2 k) without τ, versus O(mk log k/ε) in concurrent work) at the cost of a smaller constant.
- Clean two-stage workflow: one-time summary supports multiple future deletions E, practical for interactive settings.
- Theoretical guarantees are self-contained, with clear case analysis and constants derived from standard GREEDY bounds.
- Experiments on social networks and movie recommendation show performance comparable to non-robust baselines that know E in advance, even for m > k in practice.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Approximation constant is relatively small (≈0.149), which may be pessimistic in practice; a discussion of potential tightening would help.
- Minor inconsistencies:
  - Threshold rule is described as τ/2^i in text but τ/min{2^i, k} in Algorithm 1; please unify and justify the final choice.
  - Space complexity alternates between O((k + m log k) log k) (Theorem 4.1) and O((k + m log k) log^2 k) (intro/conclusion). Clarify which holds in the known-τ vs unknown-τ settings and state final bounds prominently.
  - The expression for τ in Theorem 4.1 appears typographically garbled; please restate precisely.
- τ unknown case: The paper asserts existence of a good instance among parallel runs. Clarify the query-time selection (e.g., run GREEDY on each S and pick the best? cost implications?) and whether any heuristic can prune instances.
- Computational cost per stream element may be O(w k log k) marginal evaluations in the worst case; report time/throughput, and discuss any constant-factor engineering (e.g., early stopping, lazy evaluation).
- Empirical evaluation:
  - No comparisons to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18]; even if concurrent, a qualitative runtime/space/approximation discussion or post-hoc experiment would be informative.
  - Limited analysis of robustness vs m and sensitivity to w; include ablations showing space-quality tradeoffs and actual memory usage versus baselines.
  - Report running times and summary sizes; include sensitivity to k.

Quality
- Generally sound and well-grounded in established submodular analysis (GREEDY bounds, submodularity properties). The bucketing argument for deletions via averaging and the γ term is plausible. Proof sketches are coherent; full proofs would be needed to verify constants. Minor notation/typo issues should be fixed.

Clarity
- The high-level ideas and algorithms are clearly described. Some notation inconsistencies (threshold rule, τ expression, space bound) should be cleaned up. Including a brief pseudocode for the multi-τ variant and the query-time selection would improve readability.

Originality
- The combination of partitioned bucketing with exponentially decreasing thresholds for robust streaming appears novel relative to PRO and SIEVE-STREAMING. The specific memory-approximation tradeoff is a new contribution.

Significance
- Addresses a relevant and increasingly important setting (robustness + streaming). The improved space dependence is valuable for large-scale applications. While the approximation constant is modest, the practical results and the ability to handle multiple future deletions from a single summary are useful.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled streaming approach to a timely robust optimization problem.
- Strong memory bound with single-pass processing and constant-factor guarantee.
- Practical two-stage workflow with supportive experiments.

Cons for acceptance
- Small approximation constant and lack of thorough runtime/space experiments.
- Presentation issues (threshold rule, space complexity, τ specification).
- No empirical comparison to the concurrent 1/2−ε streaming robust method.

Questions for authors
1) Please clarify and unify the threshold rule and the final space bounds in each setting (known τ vs unknown τ).
2) How do you select among parallel instances at query time, and what is the computational overhead?
3) Can you report per-element processing time, total runtime, and memory footprint versus baselines, and ablate w and m?
4) Is there a pathway to larger constants (e.g., refined balancing of Lemmas 4.3/4.4, or tighter γ dependence)?

Overall: This is a solid contribution with an interesting algorithmic design and useful memory guarantees for robust streaming submodular maximization. With improved clarity and more comprehensive empirical evaluation, it would be a strong addition.
