

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where the goal is to create a summary set that remains useful even after some elements are removed. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm: a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary set S in one pass, followed by a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that efficiently returns a solution after any removal of up to m elements from S. The paper makes a novel contribution through its partitioned thresholding approach, where elements are assigned to buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds across partitions.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on recent work in submodular streaming (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., PRO, robust influence maximization). It extends SIEVE-STREAMING to the robust setting with a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. The authors acknowledge concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017), noting their algorithm uses O(mk log k/ε) memory compared to the paper's O((k + m log k) log² k) memory, which is a significant improvement in the dependence on k.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured. The paper provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149 as k→∞) for the robust setting, which is impressive given the added complexity of handling removals. The authors carefully address the challenges of the robust setting and provide detailed proofs for their claims. The experimental section demonstrates competitive performance against SIEVE-STREAMING (which has the advantage of knowing removals in advance) in two practical applications: dominating set and movie recommendation.

**Originality:** The partitioned thresholding approach is novel, particularly the exponentially decreasing thresholding rule across partitions. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, including the recent PRO algorithm (Bogunovic et al., 2017) and the concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. The combination of the partition structure with the decreasing thresholds represents a significant methodological advance.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is important for real-world applications where data is too large to store and where robustness against element removal is critical. The two-stage approach is practical for scenarios where removals happen after the summary is created, which is common in many applications. The experimental validation on realistic datasets demonstrates the practical relevance of the approach.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** The theoretical guarantee of 0.149 is significantly weaker than the 0.5-ε guarantee of SIEVE-STREAMING for the non-robust problem. While the paper acknowledges the trade-off between robustness and approximation ratio, it doesn't fully explain why the ratio is so much lower (the non-robust case achieves ~0.63, while the robust case achieves ~0.15). This is a substantial gap that needs more careful discussion.

**Clarity:** The algorithm description is clear, but the theoretical analysis in Section 4 could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the exponentially decreasing thresholds work. The proof sketch in Theorem 4.1 is somewhat dense, and the connection between the partition structure and the approximation guarantee could be made more explicit.

**Significance:** The experimental comparison is limited by the fact that SIEVE-STREAMING is allowed to know removals in advance, which is unrealistic in the query stage. The paper doesn't demonstrate how much the performance would degrade if SIEVE-STREAMING didn't have this advantage. A more realistic comparison would be valuable.

## Specific Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Theoretical Guarantee Explanation:** The paper should more thoroughly discuss why the robustness requirement leads to such a lower approximation ratio. A comparison with the theoretical lower bounds for robust submodular maximization would help contextualize the result.

2. **Realistic Experimental Comparison:** The experiments should include a comparison where the baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING) does not know removals in advance, to better demonstrate the practical advantage of the proposed approach.

3. **Parameter Selection Guidance:** The paper uses w=1 in experiments but states w ≥ ⌈4dlog kem/k⌉ in theory. The authors should provide more guidance on selecting w in practice, including how it affects performance.

4. **Memory Analysis:** The paper claims O((k + m log k) log² k) memory, but the analysis in Equation (3) suggests O((k + m log k) log k) memory. The authors should clarify this discrepancy.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with a clear theoretical contribution to the field of streaming submodular maximization. The partitioned thresholding approach is novel and well-analyzed, and the experimental results demonstrate practical utility. However, the significantly lower approximation ratio compared to non-robust methods is a notable weakness that needs better justification.

**Recommendation:** Accept

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The theoretical guarantees, while lower than non-robust approaches, are justified by the added robustness requirement. The practical experiments show the method is competitive with baselines that have an unfair advantage. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in large-scale machine learning applications.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Clarify the relationship between the approximation ratio and the robustness requirement
2. Provide more realistic experimental comparisons
3. Resolve the memory complexity discrepancy
4. Offer more practical guidance for parameter selection

The paper would benefit from addressing these points in a revision, but as is, it represents a valuable contribution to the field that merits acceptance at NIPS.
