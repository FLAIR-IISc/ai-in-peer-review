This paper tackles the important problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, a challenging combination of several desirable properties for large-scale data analysis. The authors present a two-stage procedure, STAR-T, with a partitioned thresholding approach, providing theoretical guarantees and demonstrating empirical performance on several tasks.

The core idea of partitioning the data stream into buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds is clever and well-motivated. This allows the algorithm to capture a robust summary while maintaining a reasonable memory footprint. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, building upon previous work in submodular maximization and streaming algorithms. The experimental evaluation is also well-conducted, comparing STAR-T against relevant baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showcasing its effectiveness in different scenarios. The inclusion of scenarios such as removing movies by genre and already-seen movies demonstrates the practicality of the interactive nature of the proposed approach.

However, several weaknesses require attention. First, while the authors acknowledge the dependency on the unknown value f(OPT(k, V \ E)), the method for approximating it (described in Theorem 4.5) adds significant complexity and increases the space requirement by a factor of log(1+k). This factor could become substantial for large k, diminishing the practical benefits of the streaming approach.  The impact of this approximation on overall performance needs more detailed exploration.

Second, the constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149, as k approaches infinity) is relatively small. While theoretically sound, it raises questions about the practical utility of the algorithm when strong approximation guarantees are desired. A discussion of the tightness of this bound, or potential avenues for improvement, would be valuable.

Third, the experimental section, while comprehensive, lacks a detailed analysis of the parameter sensitivity of STAR-T.  Specifically, how does the performance change with varying values of 'w' (the memory parameter) and 'τ' (the thresholding parameter)? A more systematic parameter study would enhance the understanding of the algorithm's behavior.  Furthermore, reporting confidence intervals for the experimental results would strengthen the conclusions drawn.

Fourth, the presentation of related work could be improved.  While relevant papers are cited, a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs between different robust submodular maximization algorithms (e.g., PRO [11]) and their suitability for different application scenarios would provide valuable context. Specifically, comparing the memory usage and approximation guarantees more directly with PRO would be insightful.

Finally, while the authors mention the theoretical memory complexity of O((k + m log k) log k), the practical memory requirements are not explicitly discussed in relation to the dataset sizes used in the experiments.  Providing a rough estimate of the memory footprint for the specific datasets would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach is a novel contribution to the field of robust streaming submodular maximization.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis and establishes a constant-factor approximation guarantee.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm on several relevant tasks.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  The problem addressed is highly relevant to numerous applications in machine learning and data summarization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Small Constant Factor:** The approximation guarantee has a relatively small constant factor (0.149).
*   **Approximation Overhead:** The method for approximating f(OPT(k, V \ E)) introduces additional complexity and memory overhead.
*   **Limited Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The experimental section lacks a thorough parameter study.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of robust streaming submodular maximization. The proposed algorithm, STAR-T, is novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. However, the relatively small constant factor in the approximation guarantee and the overhead associated with approximating the optimal value warrant careful consideration.  

**Recommendation:**

Reject with encouragement to revise.  The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly focusing on improving the approximation guarantee (if possible), providing a more detailed parameter sensitivity analysis, and discussing the practical implications of the memory requirements.  Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact. I would be happy to review a revised version.
