## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm that first constructs a robust summary of the data in a single pass and then utilizes a greedy algorithm on this summary to find a solution after arbitrary removals.  The core contribution lies in a novel partitioning scheme with exponentially decreasing thresholds, allowing for a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The paper provides both theoretical analysis and empirical validation on real-world datasets, demonstrating competitive performance against existing streaming algorithms, even when those algorithms have prior knowledge of removals.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the limitations of greedy algorithms for large datasets and the need for streaming approaches (like SIEVE-STREAMING). The connection to prior work on robust submodular maximization (e.g., [9, 10, 11]) and interactive submodular optimization is clearly outlined. The authors effectively distinguish their contribution by focusing on a streaming, robust setting while achieving logarithmic memory dependence on *k*, contrasting with linear dependence in concurrent work ([18] cited in the paper).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a clever way to balance robustness and efficiency in a streaming context. This is the paper’s primary contribution.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a strong result, particularly considering the complexity of the problem and the streaming constraint. The analysis appears rigorous, although detailed proofs are relegated to the supplementary material.
* **Practical Relevance:** The two-stage setup is highly practical.  Precomputing a robust summary is useful in scenarios with frequent data updates or limited access to the full dataset.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation provide compelling evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness.  The comparison against SIEVE-STREAMING and other baselines is well-executed. The demonstrated ability to handle more removals than theoretically guaranteed is encouraging.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem statement and proposed solution are clearly explained.  The figures are helpful in illustrating the algorithm's structure.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of STAR-T depends on the choice of parameters like *w* and *τ*. The paper offers some guidance on setting *w*, but a more thorough analysis of parameter sensitivity, and potentially adaptive parameter selection strategies, would be beneficial.  The approximation of *τ* (discussed in section 4.1 and the supplementary material) feels slightly hand-wavy and relies on further assumptions.
* **Logarithmic Dependence on *k*:** While a logarithmic memory dependence on *k* is an advantage compared to other approaches, the constant factor hidden within O((k + m log k) log k) could be significant.  A more precise analysis of the space complexity, including a discussion of practical memory requirements for different values of *k* and *m*, would be valuable.
* **Assumptions in Experiments:** The experimental setup involves comparing against algorithms with varying levels of knowledge (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING knowing removals beforehand). While this is a fair comparison, it's important to explicitly acknowledge the impact of this difference in setup on the results.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The experiments are conducted on moderately sized datasets.  A discussion of the potential scalability challenges for truly massive datasets (e.g., billions of elements) would strengthen the paper. Could the partitioning scheme become a bottleneck?
* **Minor Clarity Issues:**  The notation can be dense at times. More frequent explanations of key symbols would improve readability. For example, the meaning of  α1 and α2 in Theorem 4.1 isn’t immediately apparent without tracing back through the proof sketch.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem (robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting).
* The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
* The work has the potential to impact a variety of applications, including data summarization, influence maximization, and recommender systems.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and contributions.



**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The sensitivity to parameter choices and the potentially large constant factor in the space complexity warrant further investigation.
*  The limited scalability discussion leaves open questions about the algorithm's performance on truly massive datasets.
*  While the theoretical analysis is strong, a more intuitive explanation of the core ideas (partitioning and thresholding) would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The proposed algorithm is novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While some aspects of the work could be further refined (parameter sensitivity, scalability), the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity, space complexity analysis, and a more detailed discussion of scalability.  The authors should also consider adding a short paragraph summarizing the main ideas in non-technical language to make the work more accessible to a broader audience.



**Confidence:** 8/10
