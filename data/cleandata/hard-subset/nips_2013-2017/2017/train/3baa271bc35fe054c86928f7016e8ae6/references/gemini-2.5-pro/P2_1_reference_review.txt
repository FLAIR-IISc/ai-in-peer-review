### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage framework where: 1) a single-pass streaming algorithm (STAR-T) constructs a robust summary `S` of the data, and 2) a query-stage algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) uses this summary to find a near-optimal solution `Z` of size `k` *after* an arbitrary set `E` of up to `m` elements has been removed from the summary. This setup is highly practical, as it allows for recovering from element removals (due to failures, updates, or adversarial actions) without needing to re-process the entire original dataset.

The core technical contribution is the STAR-T algorithm, which uses a novel partitioned thresholding scheme. The summary `S` is structured into `log k` partitions, each containing multiple buckets. Partitions are associated with exponentially decreasing thresholds, while the maximum size of buckets within them increases exponentially. This structure is designed to capture both high-value elements and a diverse set of alternatives for robustness. The authors provide a theoretical analysis proving that their method yields a constant-factor approximation guarantee while using `O((k + m log k) log k)` memory, which is notably sublinear in the size of the original dataset and has a favorable logarithmic dependence on `k`. The theoretical claims are supported by strong experimental results on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, where the proposed method performs comparably or even better than a strong baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING) that has the unfair advantage of knowing the removed elements in advance.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality.

**Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by a rigorous analysis. The main result, Theorem 4.1, provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for this challenging robust streaming problem. The proof sketch is logical and relies on a careful case analysis based on whether partitions become "full." The strategy of balancing two complementary lower bounds on the solution quality is a standard and powerful technique in approximation algorithms, and it is applied effectively here. The handling of the unknown optimal value `f(OPT)` by running parallel instances of the algorithm is a well-established technique (borrowed from prior work like SIEVE-STREAMING) and is correctly applied to make the algorithm fully practical.

**Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is convincing. The choice of baselines is strong; comparing against SIEVE-STREAMING, which is given oracle access to the removed set `E`, sets a very high bar. The fact that STAR-T is competitive in this setting strongly validates its effectiveness. The inclusion of both random and adversarial (greedy) removal strategies for the dominating set problem demonstrates the method's robustness under different failure models. The movie recommendation experiment provides a compelling, practical use-case for the interactive nature of the problem, showing how a single pre-computed summary can handle diverse, ad-hoc queries (e.g., filtering by genre).

**Completeness:** This is a complete piece of work, presenting a novel algorithm, a full theoretical analysis, and a thorough empirical validation. The authors are honest about the limitations (e.g., the small theoretical constant) but show that the practical performance is much better.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Organization:** The structure is logical, moving from a clear problem statement to the algorithm description, theoretical guarantees, and finally experimental validation. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem with concrete examples and situating the work within the existing literature.

**Writing and Presentation:** The exposition is clear and precise. The description of the STAR-T algorithm, including its partitioned structure (Figure 1), is intuitive and easy to grasp. The theoretical claims are stated clearly and upfront. The experimental setup and results are presented lucidly, with informative plots that effectively convey the key takeaways. A reader with a background in submodular optimization and streaming algorithms would have no trouble understanding the contributions and reproducing the results.

### Originality

The paper makes several original and significant contributions.

**Problem Formulation:** While robust submodular maximization and streaming submodular maximization have been studied separately, this work is one of the first to tackle their intersection in this practical two-stage setting. The specific robustness guarantee—resilience against removals from the *summary* itself, with recovery possible without accessing the original data stream—is novel and highly relevant.

**Algorithmic Contribution:** The STAR-T algorithm's design is the main novelty. It elegantly extends the concept of greedy thresholding from SIEVE-STREAMING to a more complex, structured summary. The partitioned architecture with coupled exponential bucket sizes and thresholds is a clever way to ensure that the summary contains not just a few high-value elements (captured by the initial high-threshold partitions) but also a diverse pool of backup elements (captured by the later, lower-threshold partitions) to provide robustness.

**Relation to Prior Work:** The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to existing literature, including centralized robust algorithms ([10], [11]), non-robust streaming algorithms ([8]), and concurrent work ([18]). The differentiation from the concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. [18] based on memory complexity (logarithmic vs. linear dependence on `k`) is a key and important distinction that highlights a significant advantage of their approach.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a lasting impact.

**Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is of great practical importance. In many large-scale machine learning systems that rely on data summarization (recommender systems, news feeds, network monitoring), the ability to efficiently update or repair a summary in the face of changing conditions or component failures is critical. This paper provides a principled and provably effective solution for this scenario.

**Advancement of the State-of-the-Art:** The paper provides the first constant-factor approximation algorithm for robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting with a memory footprint that scales only logarithmically with `k`. This is a significant theoretical advancement. The strong empirical performance further suggests that the proposed algorithm is not just of theoretical interest but is a practical tool.

**Future Impact:** The ideas presented here, particularly the partitioned thresholding technique, could inspire further research. This algorithmic design principle may be adaptable to other robust optimization problems in streaming or distributed settings. Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale data summarization are likely to use and build upon these ideas.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is an excellent paper that tackles a timely and important problem with a novel algorithm, strong theoretical guarantees, and compelling experimental results. It is well-written, clearly positions its contributions, and represents a significant advance in the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
