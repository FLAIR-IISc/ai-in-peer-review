### Paper Summary
This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The goal is to design a single-pass algorithm that uses sublinear memory to construct a small summary set `S` from a large dataset `V`. This summary set must be robust, meaning that after an arbitrary removal of up to `m` elements from `S`, one can still find a `k`-sized solution that provides a good approximation to the optimal `k`-sized solution from the original dataset (excluding the removed elements).

The authors propose a two-stage procedure. In the first (streaming) stage, their algorithm, STAR-T, processes the data stream once. It maintains a structured summary `S` composed of `log k` partitions. Each partition `i` has an associated threshold `τ/2^i` and contains multiple buckets. An incoming element is added to the first available bucket `B` in the structure if its marginal gain `f(e|B)` exceeds the bucket's partition-specific threshold. This design cleverly combines the thresholding idea from the streaming algorithm SIEVE-STREAMING [8] with a partitioned structure inspired by the non-streaming robust algorithm PRO [11]. In the second (query) stage, after a set `E` of at most `m` elements is removed, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining elements `S \ E` to find the final `k`-sized solution.

The main theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee (approaching 0.149) for this procedure. The algorithm constructs a summary of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`, which is notably logarithmic in `k`. The work is related to and improves upon concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. [18], which provides a better constant but with a memory requirement linear in `k`. The paper also demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed method on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The authors provide a complete theoretical analysis with a proof sketch in the main body and full proofs in the appendix. The analysis for the approximation guarantee is non-trivial, carefully balancing two cases: one where a partition contains many full buckets, and another where all partitions have many non-full buckets. The claims are well-supported by this analysis. The experimental evaluation is also well-designed. The comparison against SIEVE-STREAMING with an oracle (i.e., knowing the removed set `E` in advance) is a strong baseline, and the proposed method performs comparably or better, validating its practical utility.

A minor weakness is that the theoretical approximation constant of 0.149 is somewhat low. While achieving any constant factor in this challenging setting is a significant result, a discussion on the potential tightness of the analysis would be beneficial. It is unclear if this constant is an artifact of the proof technique or reflects a fundamental limitation. Additionally, there is a gap between the theoretical requirement for the memory parameter `w` (`w >= O(m log k / k)`) and its practical setting (`w=1`). The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of why the algorithm performs well empirically with a much smaller `w`, as this could offer deeper insights into its behavior.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem statement is precise, and the proposed two-stage framework is easy to understand. The STAR-T algorithm is presented with clear pseudocode, and the partitioned bucket structure is effectively illustrated in Figure 1. The proof sketch for the main theorem provides good intuition for the key steps in the analysis. The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the contributions and potentially reproduce the results.

**Originality:**
The paper addresses a novel and important problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization. While building on established concepts like greedy thresholding from SIEVE-STREAMING [8] and partitioned structures for robustness from PRO [11], the synthesis of these ideas into a new algorithm for the robust streaming setting is original. The exponentially decreasing threshold scheme across partitions is a key novelty that enables the theoretical guarantees. The analysis technique is also innovative. The authors properly situate their work with respect to the literature, including a fair comparison to the concurrent work of [18], highlighting their key advantage in memory complexity.

**Significance:**
The problem of building robust summaries from massive data streams is of high practical importance. Applications abound in areas like influence maximization, recommender systems, and facility location, where summaries must remain effective even if some of their components become unavailable or are adversarially removed. The proposed two-stage approach, where an expensive one-pass computation yields a robust summary that can be queried quickly and repeatedly, is a practical and valuable paradigm. The algorithm's low memory footprint (logarithmic in `k`) makes it particularly attractive for large-scale applications. Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale data summarization are likely to build upon these ideas.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper is one of the first to formally define and provide a solution for robust submodular maximization in the challenging streaming model.
2.  **Elegant Algorithmic Design:** STAR-T is a novel algorithm that effectively combines ideas from streaming and robust optimization in a non-trivial manner.
3.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee, backed by a rigorous proof. The memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)` is a significant strength, comparing favorably to concurrent work.
4.  **Practical Relevance and Strong Empirical Results:** The problem is highly relevant, and the experiments demonstrate that the proposed method is competitive with, and sometimes superior to, a strong baseline that has access to future information.

**Arguments for Rejection (Cons):**
1.  **Low Approximation Constant:** The theoretical approximation guarantee of 0.149 is modest. The paper lacks a discussion on whether this bound is tight.
2.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The value of the memory parameter `w` required by the theory is much larger than the value (`w=1`) shown to be effective in practice, and this discrepancy is not discussed.
3.  **Limited Experimental Baselines:** While the comparison to an oracle-aided SIEVE-STREAMING is strong, a comparison against the concurrent work [18] or the non-streaming PRO [11] (on smaller data) would have provided a more complete empirical picture. (This is a minor point, as comparing to concurrent work can be difficult).
