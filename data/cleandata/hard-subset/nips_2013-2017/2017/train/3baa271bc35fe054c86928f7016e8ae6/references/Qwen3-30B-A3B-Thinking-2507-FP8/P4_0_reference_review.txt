

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work
This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in streaming settings, where the goal is to build a summary set S that remains useful even after up to m elements are removed. The authors propose a two-stage approach: a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary S in one pass, followed by a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that computes a solution of size k on S\E for any removed set E with |E|≤m. The paper builds upon SIEVE-STREAMING [8] for non-robust streaming submodular maximization and extends the robust submodular maximization work of [11] to the streaming setting. The authors demonstrate their approach on two applications: dominating set and personalized movie recommendation.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound solution with a comprehensive theoretical analysis. The authors prove that their algorithm achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of 0.149(1-1/log k) for the robust submodular maximization problem. The proof is well-structured, handling different cases based on the partition structure of the summary set S. The theoretical results are supported by a clear analysis of how the exponentially decreasing thresholding rule interacts with the partitioned structure.

The experimental section validates the theoretical claims, demonstrating that the proposed method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed) on two practical applications. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate baselines including random selection and variants of their own algorithm.

## Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem statement is precise, and the algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are presented with sufficient detail. The theoretical results are clearly stated and the proof sketch provides a good overview of the main ideas. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to allow for reproducibility. The figures effectively illustrate the experimental results.

## Originality
The paper makes a novel contribution to the robust submodular maximization problem by introducing a partitioned thresholding approach for the streaming setting. While previous work on robust submodular maximization [9, 10, 11] has focused on centralized algorithms, this paper extends the problem to the streaming setting with a one-pass algorithm. The partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds represents a novel algorithmic approach that enables the robustness guarantee. The paper also acknowledges related concurrent work [18], noting that their memory complexity (O((k + m log k) log²k)) has a more favorable dependence on k than the O(mk log k/ε) of [18].

## Significance
The problem addressed is significant for large-scale machine learning applications where data is too large to store in memory, and robustness to element removal is required. The two-stage framework provides a practical solution that separates the expensive summary construction from the efficient query phase. While the approximation guarantee (0.149) is lower than the standard greedy approximation (1-1/e ≈ 0.632) for non-robust problems, the paper demonstrates through experiments that the approach remains effective in practice. The practical applications to dominating set and movie recommendation demonstrate the relevance of the problem.

## Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with relevant applications
- Novel two-stage framework for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with careful case handling
- Strong experimental results showing competitive performance
- Practical implementation details that facilitate reproducibility

## Weaknesses
- The approximation guarantee (0.149) is substantially lower than the standard 0.632 for non-robust submodular maximization, making it unclear when the robustness trade-off is justified
- The memory complexity (O((k + m log k) log²k)) is higher than non-robust streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING (O(k log k))
- The paper doesn't sufficiently explore when the robustness is needed versus simply re-running a non-robust algorithm on the reduced dataset
- The comparison to SIEVE-STREAMING is favorable, but it would be more informative to compare with robust non-streaming algorithms

## Recommendation
The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization by addressing the robustness requirement in a streaming setting. The theoretical results are sound, and the experimental validation demonstrates practical relevance. While the approximation guarantee is lower than standard non-robust approaches, the paper convincingly demonstrates that the approach remains effective in practice for the applications studied.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel two-stage framework for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Strong theoretical foundation with comprehensive analysis
- Practical experimental validation on relevant applications
- Addresses a meaningful problem in large-scale machine learning

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The approximation guarantee (0.149) is substantially lower than standard approaches
- Memory complexity is higher than non-robust streaming algorithms
- The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate when robustness is worth the trade-off

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in robust streaming submodular optimization. While the approximation guarantee is lower than standard non-robust approaches, the paper provides a meaningful solution to a practical problem that has not been adequately addressed in previous work. The theoretical and experimental results are well-supported, and the paper is clearly written. I recommend acceptance.
