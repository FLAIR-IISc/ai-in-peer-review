This paper addresses the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, a crucial task for large-scale data summarization requiring resilience to element removals. The authors propose a two-stage procedure, STAR-T, consisting of a streaming algorithm to construct a robust summary and a greedy algorithm to extract a representative subset after removals. The core idea is a partitioned thresholding approach, organizing the summary into partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying the limitations of existing approaches when dealing with both large datasets and the need for robustness. It builds upon prior work in submodular optimization, streaming algorithms (SIEVE-STREAMING), and robust set cover problems. Specifically, it acknowledges and contrasts its approach with the work of Orlin et al. and Bogunovic et al., highlighting the advantages of their method, particularly in terms of memory usage (logarithmic dependence on k vs. linear dependence). The connection to related areas like influence maximization and interactive machine learning is also appropriately highlighted.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of partitioned thresholding with a streaming algorithm for *robust* submodular maximization is novel. The exponentially decreasing thresholds and the analysis combining them are significant contributions.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the proposed algorithm, backed by rigorous theoretical analysis.  The bound on the size of the retained set, O((k + m log k) log k), is also valuable.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The algorithms are presented clearly, and the theoretical sections are relatively easy to follow, given the complexity of the topic.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation on both the dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks is strong. The results demonstrate that STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably to or even outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, even when SIEVE-STREAMING has prior knowledge of removals. The demonstration of the utility of a single robust summary across multiple query scenarios (different removal sets) is a compelling practical benefit.
*   **Addressing Limitations:**  The authors acknowledge and address potential limitations, such as the need for approximating the optimal function value in the initial phase and providing a parallel variant to tackle this issue.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The performance of the algorithm is dependent on the choice of parameters like *w* and *τ*. The paper provides guidance on setting *w*, but the sensitivity of the performance to these parameters and the practical impact of poor parameter choices could be discussed further.
*   **Constant Factors:** While the theoretical guarantees establish a constant-factor approximation, the constant itself (0.149) is relatively small.  The authors could explore potential avenues for improving this constant.
*   **Assumptions:** The analysis relies on the assumption of a normalized, monotone submodular function. Discussing the implications of violating these assumptions or exploring extensions to handle non-monotone functions would be valuable.
*   **Space Complexity in Practice:** While the theoretical space complexity is favorable, the constant factors hidden within the O notation could be significant in practice. The experiments show w=1 works well, but this isn't justified theoretically beyond the asymptotic bound.


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable guarantees.
*   The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed algorithm.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical contributions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
*   The sensitivity of the algorithm to parameter choices and the impact of violating assumptions are not fully explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The proposed algorithm, STAR-T, offers a practical and theoretically sound solution for robust streaming submodular maximization. The clarity of the presentation, combined with the compelling experimental results, makes this paper a worthy addition to NIPS. Despite the minor weaknesses mentioned above, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh its shortcomings.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
