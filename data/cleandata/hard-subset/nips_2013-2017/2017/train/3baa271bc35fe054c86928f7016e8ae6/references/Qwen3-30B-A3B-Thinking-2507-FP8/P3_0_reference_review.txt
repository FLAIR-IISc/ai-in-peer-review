

# Peer Review: Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a critical gap in streaming submodular maximization: the need for robust solutions that maintain performance after removal of up to m elements from the summary. While SIEVE-STREAMING [8] provides an efficient streaming algorithm for standard submodular maximization (achieving 0.5-ε approximation with O(k log k) memory), it lacks robustness guarantees. The authors extend this line of work by proposing a two-stage procedure (STAR-T for streaming, STAR-T-GREEDY for query) that creates a summary robust against m element removals. Their key innovation is a partitioned thresholding approach where the summary is structured into exponentially increasing buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds. This differs from prior robust approaches like PRO [11] that use fixed-size buckets, and addresses limitations in [18] which requires O(mk log k) memory.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Algorithmic Structure**: The partitioned thresholding approach is elegantly designed, with buckets of exponentially increasing size and exponentially decreasing thresholds. This structure efficiently balances the trade-off between memory usage and robustness.

2. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis with careful proofs for the approximation guarantee (0.149(1-1/log k)) and memory bound (O((k + m log k) log²k)). The proof strategy cleverly handles two cases: when a partition has many full buckets versus when all partitions have mostly empty buckets.

3. **Practical Validation**: The experimental results on two distinct applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) demonstrate that the method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even though SIEVE-STREAMING has the advantage of knowing which elements will be removed. This is particularly impressive given the theoretical approximation factor is lower than SIEVE-STREAMING's 0.5-ε.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The paper addresses the challenge of unknown f(OPT(k, V\E)) through a parallel variant with only a logarithmic space overhead, making the approach practical.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Limitation**: The approximation factor of 0.149 is significantly lower than the 0.5-ε achieved by SIEVE-STREAMING for the non-robust case. While the paper acknowledges this trade-off, it doesn't sufficiently explore whether the robustness guarantee justifies this reduction, especially since the robustness requirement is non-standard.

2. **Memory Complexity**: The O((k + m log k) log²k) memory bound has an extra log²k factor compared to SIEVE-STREAMING's O(k log k). The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this is necessary or how it compares to the O(mk log k) bound in [18].

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The choice of w (which determines the number of buckets per partition) is theoretically bounded as w ≥ ⌈4dlog kem/k⌉, but the paper notes that w=1 works well in practice. This disconnect between theory and practice needs better explanation.

4. **Experimental Comparison**: While the paper compares against SIEVE-STREAMING, it would be more compelling to compare against a robust baseline that doesn't know which elements will be removed (e.g., a standard streaming algorithm run on the entire dataset after removals). The current comparison may overstate the advantage of their method.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the growing literature on streaming submodular maximization. The paper makes a clear and meaningful advance by addressing the robustness requirement, which is crucial for many practical applications. The algorithmic design is novel, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental validation is convincing.

However, the theoretical approximation factor (0.149) is substantially lower than the standard streaming submodular maximization result (0.5-ε), which raises questions about the practical value of the robustness guarantee. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify this trade-off, especially since the robustness requirement is not standard in most applications.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a valuable contribution to the field. While the theoretical approximation factor is a notable limitation, the practical performance in experiments is promising. The authors successfully address the challenge of robustness in streaming settings with an elegant algorithmic structure.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization, addressing a practically important problem that has received little attention. The theoretical and empirical results are sound, and the paper is well-written. The main areas for improvement would be:

1. More thorough discussion of the trade-off between the approximation factor and robustness
2. Better explanation of the parameter w choice in practice
3. Additional comparison against a robust baseline that doesn't know the removals in advance

The paper advances the state of the art in robust streaming submodular maximization and provides a valuable algorithmic framework with clear practical applications.
