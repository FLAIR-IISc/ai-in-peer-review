Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting: given a monotone submodular function and cardinality k, construct in one pass a compact summary S so that, after an arbitrary removal of up to m elements (e.g., failures or adversarial deletions), one can, using only S, output a size-k set with value within a constant factor of the post-removal optimum on V \ E. The proposed two-stage method consists of STAR-T (streaming construction) and STAR-T-GREEDY (query-time greedy run on S \ E). The key algorithmic idea is a partitioned structure of buckets with capacities doubling across partitions and exponentially decreasing marginal thresholds across partitions; arriving elements are inserted into the first non-full bucket whose threshold they meet. The memory is O(k log k + m log^2 k), and the query-time greedy achieves a constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149(1 − 1/log k). When OPT is unknown, running multiple parallel instances over a geometric grid of thresholds yields a (1+ε)-approximation loss with an extra log factor in space.

This work builds on and combines: (i) classical submodular streaming via thresholding (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING), (ii) non-streaming robust submodular maximization with partitioned structures (e.g., PRO), and (iii) concurrent deletion-robust streaming approaches. Compared to the concurrent work [18] (1/2−ε approximation with O(mk log k/ε) memory), the present paper attains much better dependence on k (logarithmic rather than linear), at the cost of a weaker constant.

Strengths:
- Problem importance and scope: Robust data summarization under streaming constraints is timely and broadly relevant (influence maximization, recommendation, facility location).
- Novelty in design and analysis: The partitioned, exponentially decreasing thresholding scheme is a clean extension of sieve-style methods to the robust setting; the analysis carefully exploits bucket fullness and cross-partition structure.
- Memory-approximation tradeoff: Achieves O(k log k + m log^2 k) space with a constant factor guarantee independent of n and stream length; the dependence on k is a notable improvement over prior deletion-robust streaming methods.
- Two-stage utility: A single summary supports many different deletion queries without re-accessing the stream, which is useful in interactive settings.
- Empirical validation: On dominating set and movie recommendation, performance matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter is given E in advance, and the same summary serves multiple queries.

Weaknesses:
- Approximation factor: The constant 0.149 is relatively low compared to 0.5−ε in the concurrent work. The practical gap may be small, but the theoretical constant is modest.
- Clarity/inconsistencies: There are noticeable inconsistencies/typos:
  • The threshold per partition is described as τ/2^i in text, yet Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i,k}; please reconcile and state the intended rule clearly.
  • The memory bound alternates between O((k + m log k) log^2 k) and O((k + m log k) log k) across sections; Theorem 4.1 and Eq. (3) imply O(k log k + m log^2 k). Make the bound consistent throughout, including the abstract/conclusion.
  • The expression for τ in Theorem 4.1 appears malformed (“τ = 1 / + (1−e−1)(1−e−1/3)(1−1/dlog ke) f(OPT(…))”); please fix constants and parentheses and explain how τ is chosen.
- Time complexity and implementation details: STAR-T may scan many buckets per element (worst-case O(wk) checks, i.e., Θ(m log k) with the chosen w). The paper does not analyze or empirically report per-element processing time or function evaluation counts, which are critical for streaming.
- Empirical scope: Experiments do not include the concurrent deletion-robust method [18], nor ablations of w versus robustness m and space. The results use w=1 even when m can exceed k; more systematic plots of performance versus |E| and versus w (quality–memory tradeoff) would strengthen the empirical case. Scalability to larger streams/functions (beyond two datasets) is not demonstrated.
- Assumptions and robustness: While the theory allows E ⊆ V, much of the intuition assumes deletions from S; a short discussion on how guarantees degrade when |E| > m (empirically explored) would be helpful.

Quality:
- Technical arguments appear plausible and are outlined with clear case splits (many full buckets vs. mostly non-full). However, given the centrality of the balancing steps and averaging arguments, the full proofs (supplement) should be checked carefully, and the main text should correct typos and be explicit about constants, thresholding, and the choice of w.

Clarity:
- Generally well-organized, but the aforementioned inconsistencies and notation issues hinder readability. Clarify the algorithmic thresholds, unify the space bound, and correct the τ expression. Briefly discuss the per-element processing complexity and how to implement bucket selection efficiently.

Originality:
- The partitioned thresholding scheme for robust streaming and its analysis are original combinations of known building blocks. The improved space dependence on k relative to concurrent work is a notable contribution.

Significance:
- The ability to precompute a compact, deletion-robust summary with one pass and answer many queries is practically useful. Despite the modest constant, the asymptotic space improvement over methods linear in k is significant, and the empirical results are encouraging.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix inconsistencies (threshold rule, memory bounds, τ definition) and add a brief complexity analysis (per-element checks, function evaluations). Consider simple data structures or round-robin/randomized assignment to avoid scanning many buckets per element.
- Expand experiments: vary w and m systematically; report oracle-call counts and throughput; include a comparison to [18] when feasible; provide larger-scale datasets or synthetic stress tests.
- Discuss how performance degrades as |E| increases beyond m; if possible, provide a simple extension or heuristic guarantee.
- Release code and scripts to aid reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a clean, theoretically grounded method.
- Strong space guarantees with only logarithmic dependence on k.
- Practical two-stage utility with compelling empirical behavior.

Cons for acceptance:
- Low approximation constant compared to concurrent work.
- Clarity issues and typos in core statements.
- Missing runtime analysis and broader experimental comparisons/ablations.

Recommendation: Accept, contingent on fixing clarity issues and strengthening the empirical/complexity discussion. Confidence: High.
