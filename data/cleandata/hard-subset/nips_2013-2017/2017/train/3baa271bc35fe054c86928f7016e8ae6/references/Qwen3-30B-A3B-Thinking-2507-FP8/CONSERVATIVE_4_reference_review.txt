

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper proposes a two-stage algorithm for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings, where the goal is to create a summary set that remains effective even after the removal of up to m elements. The authors introduce STAR-T for the streaming stage (creating a robust summary) and STAR-T-GREEDY for the query stage (producing a solution after removals). The theoretical analysis claims a constant-factor approximation guarantee of approximately 0.149, with a summary size of O((k + m log k) log k).

## Strengths

The problem addressed is well-motivated, as robustness against element removal is increasingly important in practical applications like recommendation systems and network analysis. The paper builds on established submodular optimization techniques and properly references prior work on streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular maximization. The experimental validation across two applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) provides some practical evidence of the approach's viability.

## Weaknesses

The primary concern with this paper is the extremely low approximation guarantee of 0.149, which is significantly worse than standard greedy algorithms (1-1/e ≈ 0.632) and even SIEVE-STREAMING (0.5-ε). The authors acknowledge this is a drawback but do not convincingly justify why such a low approximation factor is acceptable in practice. The theoretical analysis relies on a parameter τ that depends on the unknown value f(OPT(k, V \ E)), which they address with a parallel variant that increases memory requirements by a factor of log1+ k. This makes the memory complexity O((k + m log k) log k log1+ k), which is substantially higher than SIEVE-STREAMING's O(k log k).

The experimental results are underwhelming. The authors claim their method "matches (and in some cases outperforms)" SIEVE-STREAMING, but Figures 2(a-d) show only marginal differences that are not statistically significant. The "outperformance" appears to be within a few percentage points at best, and the paper fails to demonstrate why these marginal gains justify the substantially lower approximation guarantee and higher memory requirements.

The theoretical analysis has several questionable aspects. The proof of Theorem 4.1 (particularly Lemma 4.2) assumes a specific structure where "at least half of the buckets are full" in some partition, but the paper does not provide evidence that this condition is likely to be satisfied in practice. The approximation guarantee depends on the parameter w ≥ ⌈4dlog kem/k⌉, which for moderate values of m and k can lead to very large memory requirements (e.g., w = 4m for k=1000 would increase the memory by a factor of 4m).

## Originality and Significance

The partitioned thresholding approach is novel in the context of robust submodular maximization, but the significance is limited by the low approximation guarantee. The paper claims to extend SIEVE-STREAMING to the robust setting, but the extension appears to come at a significant cost in approximation quality. The authors cite related work on robust submodular maximization, but the connection to the most recent results (e.g., [18]) is not sufficiently developed.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and the algorithm descriptions are clear. However, the theoretical analysis would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the approximation factor is so low and how this compares to the standard greedy algorithm's performance in the same robust setting.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the problem is important and the paper demonstrates some technical effort, the extremely low approximation guarantee (0.149) is a fundamental limitation that undermines the practical significance of the approach. The authors do not provide sufficient evidence to justify why such a low approximation factor is acceptable in practice, especially when compared to existing methods that achieve much better approximation guarantees with comparable or better memory requirements.

The paper's contribution would be significantly stronger if it could demonstrate a meaningful advantage over existing methods (e.g., a higher approximation factor, lower memory requirements, or better empirical performance in challenging scenarios), but the current results do not support such a claim. The marginal experimental gains reported do not justify the substantially lower theoretical guarantee.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with the state-of-the-art, particularly addressing why a 0.149 approximation factor is preferable to existing methods that achieve 0.5-ε. Without this, the contribution appears to be a marginal improvement with significant theoretical drawbacks.
