## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm, STAR-T, designed to efficiently maintain a representative subset of data that remains useful even after a certain number of elements are removed. The core idea is a partitioned thresholding scheme, where the data stream is processed and elements are assigned to buckets based on their marginal gain relative to exponentially decreasing thresholds. A greedy algorithm then operates on the resulting set in the query stage to provide a robust solution.  The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the approximation ratio and bounds on the memory usage, and demonstrates through experiments on real-world datasets (dominating set and movie recommendation) that the proposed approach performs competitively with, and sometimes outperforms, existing streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the context of submodular maximization, streaming algorithms, and robust optimization. They acknowledge related work in non-streaming robust submodular maximization ([9, 10, 11]) and streaming submodular algorithms ([5, 8, 12, 13]).  The paper builds upon the ideas of SIEVE-STREAMING [8] by extending greedy thresholding to the robust setting and introducing a novel partitioning scheme. The connection to influence maximization and personalized recommendation scenarios is clearly articulated. They appropriately compare to and contrast their approach with related methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important and practical problem:** Robustness is crucial for real-world applications of submodular maximization, especially in dynamic environments.
*   **Novel Algorithm Design:** The partitioned thresholding scheme with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a clever approach for balancing robustness and efficiency.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a theoretical approximation guarantee, though the constant factor is relatively small.  The memory usage bounds are also well-defined.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on two diverse applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the algorithm. The comparison to SIEVE-STREAMING is particularly insightful.
*   **Interactive Nature:** The two-stage procedure allows for interactive usage, providing different sub-summaries for multiple removal scenarios.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear problem statement and explanation of the proposed solution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Small Approximation Constant:** The constant factor (0.149) in the approximation guarantee is relatively small. While theoretically sound, this might limit the practical applicability of the algorithm in some scenarios.  It would be helpful to discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to this constant.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The parameter 'w' (memory parameter) significantly impacts performance, and setting it appropriately requires careful consideration. The paper suggests a theoretical lower bound, but practical guidelines for tuning this parameter would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the experiments demonstrate good performance, the scope could be expanded to include a wider range of datasets and robustness parameters. Comparing to other baseline algorithms (beyond SIEVE-STREAMING and RANDOM) would also strengthen the analysis.  The choice of removing elements *from the summary* in the experiments seems artificial; removing elements from the full dataset, and then selecting a robust summary would be more representative of a real application.
*   **Theoretical Assumptions:** The analysis relies on the assumption that the threshold parameter τ is known *a priori*. The paper addresses this with Theorem 4.5, but the additional overhead of maintaining multiple parallel instances might negate some of the performance benefits.
*   **Discussion of practical implications of log k scaling**: While the memory usage scaling with log k is good, the paper does not discuss the practical limitations given very large k.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and theoretically grounded solution.
*   The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the algorithm.
*   The work contributes to the growing body of research on robust and scalable submodular maximization.
*   The clarity of presentation makes the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The small approximation constant might limit the practical value of the algorithm.
*   The parameter tuning and scaling of the algorithm with log k could require significant effort in practice.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of submodular maximization. While the approximation constant is somewhat limiting, the novelty of the approach, the strong theoretical guarantees, and the promising experimental results outweigh this concern. The paper is well-written and clearly presents a significant advancement in the area of robust streaming algorithms.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017, contingent on the authors addressing the following points:

1.  **Discussion of Approximation Constant:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the implications of the small approximation constant (0.149) and its sensitivity to different parameters.
2.  **Parameter Tuning Guidelines:** Offer practical guidelines for choosing the parameter 'w' based on the specific application and available resources.
3.  **Expanded Experimental Evaluation:** Consider expanding the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of datasets, robustness parameters, and baseline algorithms.  Re-evaluate the experiments by removing elements from the full dataset and then selecting a summary, rather than from the summary itself.
4.  **Discussion of Scalability**: Expand the discussion on the practical implications of the log k scaling of the algorithm.
