This paper tackles the important problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, a crucial component for large-scale data summarization in dynamic environments. The core idea of partitioning the data stream and employing decreasing thresholds is novel and potentially impactful. The authors demonstrate a constant-factor approximation guarantee, a significant achievement given the inherent challenges of the problem. This work directly addresses a gap in existing literature, extending streaming submodular maximization to a robust setting and offering a solution that doesn't require re-optimization over the entire dataset after removals.

The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The problem statement is clearly articulated, and the motivation is well-established with relevant applications. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the connection to related work, particularly SIEVE-STREAMING, is clearly drawn. The experimental evaluation provides encouraging results, showing competitive performance against strong baselines. The ability to reuse the precomputed summary for multiple removal scenarios is a particularly valuable practical advantage.

However, the theoretical guarantees, while impressive, come with caveats. The constant factor in the approximation guarantee degrades as the dataset size increases (approaching 0.149 as k goes to infinity). This practical limitation isn't thoroughly discussed. The assumption of knowing `τ` upfront, even with the extension in Section 4.1, feels somewhat artificial. While the parallel approach addresses this, it increases space complexity and might not be feasible in all streaming scenarios.

A minor weakness is the experimental setup. The choice of parameters (especially `w`) and the justification for these choices are not fully explored. A more extensive sensitivity analysis would strengthen the empirical evaluation.  While the comparisons to SIEVE-STREAMING are valuable, comparing against more recent advancements in streaming submodular maximization, even if not explicitly robust, would further position this work. The discussion of practical resilience exceeding theoretical bounds is intriguing, but lacks detailed analysis. Why does w=1 work well despite the theoretical requirement for a larger value?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach is a genuinely new contribution to the field.
*   **Significance:** Robust streaming submodular maximization is a fundamentally important problem with broad applicability.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:**  The constant-factor approximation guarantee, despite its limitations, is a significant theoretical result.
*   **Practical Value:**  The ability to efficiently handle removals without re-optimization is highly valuable in dynamic real-world applications.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Degrading Constant Factor:** The approximation guarantee degrades with dataset size, limiting practical applicability.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The impact of parameter choices (like `w`) requires further investigation.
*   **Theoretical vs. Practical Discrepancy:** The observed practical resilience exceeding theoretical bounds isn’t fully explained.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of streaming submodular maximization. While the theoretical guarantees have limitations, the core idea is strong, and the experimental results are promising. The practical benefits of the proposed approach are substantial. I lean towards acceptance, recognizing the value of bold ideas and acknowledging that minor flaws are acceptable when introducing a fundamentally new approach. The potential impact of this work outweighs its current limitations.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding the parameter sensitivity and discussion of the approximation guarantee’s degradation.
