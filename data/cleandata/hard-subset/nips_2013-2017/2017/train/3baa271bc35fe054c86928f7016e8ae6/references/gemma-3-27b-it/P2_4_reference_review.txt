## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm, STAR-T, which first constructs a robust summary of the data in a single pass, and then utilizes a greedy algorithm on this summary to provide solutions even after elements are removed. The core idea is a partitioned thresholding scheme that organizes the summary into buckets with decreasing thresholds, enabling robustness against removals. The authors provide theoretical guarantees, demonstrating a constant-factor approximation, and validate their approach through experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, showing competitive performance against existing streaming algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Robustness is a critical consideration in many real-world applications of submodular maximization, and handling it in a streaming setting is particularly valuable for large datasets.
* **Novel approach:** The partitioned thresholding scheme is a clever idea that balances memory usage and robustness.  The exponential decrease in thresholds is well-motivated and contributes to the theoretical guarantees.
* **Strong theoretical guarantees:** The paper provides a clear and rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing a constant-factor approximation for the proposed algorithm.
* **Empirical validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the algorithm, showcasing competitive performance against strong baselines in relevant applications.  The ability to maintain performance even with removals exceeding the theoretical limit (k) is encouraging.
* **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly presents the problem, proposed solution, theoretical analysis, and experimental results.  The inclusion of related work is comprehensive.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Constant factor in approximation is small:** While a constant-factor guarantee is achieved, the constant (0.149) is relatively small, raising questions about the practical benefit in certain scenarios.  The dependence on `dlog k` in the constant further limits its applicability as `k` grows.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The choice of parameter `w` (memory parameter) impacts performance. The theoretical bound requires `w` to scale with `m/k`, which might be overly conservative in practice (as the experiments suggest `w=1` performs well).  Further investigation into the sensitivity of the algorithm to `w` is warranted.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis assumes knowledge of `f(OPT(k, V \ E))`. While the authors address this by proposing a parallel variant (Theorem 4.5), this adds computational overhead. The assumption, even with the workaround, feels somewhat limiting.
* **Limited comparison to state-of-the-art:** While SIEVE-STREAMING is a relevant baseline, a more thorough comparison to other robust submodular maximization algorithms (even those that aren't streaming) would strengthen the paper.

**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous analysis and well-supported claims. The theoretical results are a significant contribution. However, the small constant factor and assumptions slightly detract from the overall quality.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the key ideas.  The notation is consistent, and the explanations are clear.
* **Originality:** The partitioned thresholding approach is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field of streaming submodular maximization. The combination of this approach with robustness guarantees is unique.
* **Significance:** The problem addressed is important, and the proposed solution has the potential to be useful in various applications.  The practical demonstration of the algorithm's effectiveness further enhances its significance. The interactive aspect (reusing the summary for different removals) is a valuable contribution.



**Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a challenging and important problem.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound solution.
* Demonstrates competitive performance through experiments.
* Well-written and clearly organized.

**Con:**

* The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
* Theoretical assumptions regarding `f(OPT(k, V \ E))` are limiting.
* Limited comparison to a broader range of related work.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of submodular maximization. While the small constant factor and some theoretical assumptions are weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, strong theoretical guarantees, and positive empirical results outweigh these concerns. The problem addressed is important, and the proposed solution has the potential to be useful in a variety of applications.

**Recommendation:** **Accept (with minor revisions)**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to the parameter `w` more thoroughly.
2.  Provide a more comprehensive comparison to other robust submodular maximization algorithms.
3.  Acknowledge the limitations of the small constant factor and discuss potential avenues for improvement in the conclusion.
