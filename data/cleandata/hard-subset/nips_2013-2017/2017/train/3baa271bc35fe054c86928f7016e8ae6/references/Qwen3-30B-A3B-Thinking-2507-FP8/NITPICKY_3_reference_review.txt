

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-stage streaming algorithm (STAR-T) for robust submodular maximization. In the streaming stage, it constructs a summary set S that is robust against removal of up to m elements. In the query stage, it uses a greedy algorithm on S \ E to produce a solution of size at most k. The authors claim a constant-factor approximation guarantee with a summary size of O((k + m log k) log k). The paper compares its approach against SIEVE-STREAMING in two applications: dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in large-scale machine learning: robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. The partitioned thresholding approach is novel, and the experimental results show competitive performance against SIEVE-STREAMING. The paper makes a clear distinction between the streaming and query stages, which is a valuable conceptual contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Technical Inconsistency**: The paper states that buckets in partition i have threshold τ/2^i (Section 3), but the algorithm condition (Algorithm 1, line 5) uses τ/min{2^i, k}. These are inconsistent - the threshold should either be τ/2^i or τ/min{2^i, k}, but not both. This appears to be a major error that would invalidate the analysis.

2. **Misleading Memory Claim**: The paper claims "the memory requirement of their method increases linearly with k, in the case of our algorithm this dependence is logarithmic" (Section 2), but the bound O((k + m log k) log k) is linear in k, not logarithmic. This is a significant factual error that undermines the paper's main contribution.

3. **Poorly Defined Parameters**: The parameter w is defined as "w ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem k ⌉" (Theorem 4.1), where "dlog ke" is undefined and should be "⌊log k⌋" or similar. The expression "4dlog kem k" is mathematically nonsensical (dimensionally inconsistent, as m and k are both cardinalities).

4. **Constant Factor Justification**: The approximation constant c = 0.149 is extremely low compared to standard submodular maximization results (which are typically around 0.63). The paper fails to adequately justify why this constant is acceptable, especially when compared to the 0.5-ε approximation of SIEVE-STREAMING.

5. **Inadequate Theoretical Justification**: The proof sketch in Section 4 is too high-level to verify. The paper claims "the more detailed versions of our proofs are given in the supplementary material" but provides no specific details that would allow verification of the key lemmas (4.2-4.4).

6. **Experimental Weaknesses**: The paper claims "STAR-T-GREEDY slightly outperforms the other two" but provides no statistical significance testing for these claims (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals). The experimental results in Section 5.1 show comparable performance, but the paper doesn't explain why the slight edge is meaningful.

7. **Formatting Issues**: 
   - The footnote contains inconsistent asterisk symbols (∗, †, ‡, §, ¶)
   - The footnote incorrectly states "st Conference" instead of "28th Conference" (NIPS 2017 was the 31st conference)
   - The paper uses "dlog ke" throughout, which is not standard notation

8. **Misrepresentation of Related Work**: The paper cites [18] as having "a 1/2 - ε approximation guarantee" but fails to clarify how this compares to their 0.149 constant. The paper also incorrectly claims [18] uses "O(mk log k/ε) memory" without defining ε or explaining its relationship to the problem.

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and contains some novel ideas, the technical inconsistencies (particularly the threshold definition error), misleading claims about memory complexity, and extremely low approximation constant undermine the paper's core contribution. The paper's fundamental flaw in the threshold definition (τ/2^i vs. τ/min{2^i, k}) makes the theoretical analysis questionable. The experimental results are not sufficiently supported by statistical evidence, and the paper fails to adequately justify its extremely low approximation constant.

The paper would require significant revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. As it stands, the technical flaws are too substantial to overlook, despite the paper's conceptual novelty.

**Recommendation: Reject**
