### Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting. The authors formulate a practical two-stage problem: first, a streaming algorithm processes a large dataset to create a small, robust summary `S`. Second, after an arbitrary set `E` of up to `m` elements is removed from this summary, a query algorithm must efficiently find a near-optimal `k`-element solution from the remaining elements `S \ E` without accessing the original dataset.

The core contribution is the STAR-T algorithm for the streaming stage. It introduces a novel "partitioned thresholding" structure where the summary is organized into `log k` partitions. Partitions with a higher index use exponentially lower marginal gain thresholds for admitting new elements but allow for exponentially larger "buckets" of elements. This elegant structure is designed to capture both high-value elements (in early partitions) and a diverse set of elements for robustness (in later partitions). For the query stage, the authors propose simply running the standard GREEDY algorithm on the remaining summary.

The paper provides a theoretical analysis proving that this two-stage procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The memory complexity of the summary `S` is `O((k + m log k) log k)`, which is notably efficient due to its logarithmic (rather than linear) dependence on `k`. The authors validate their method on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, showing that it performs comparably to, and sometimes better than, the non-robust SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm, even when the latter is given the unrealistic advantage of knowing the removed elements in advance.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a new and highly relevant problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization. As machine learning systems are deployed in dynamic environments, ensuring that summaries built from massive data streams are resilient to failures, attacks, or shifting requirements is a critical challenge. This work provides a formal framing and the first principled solution to this problem, opening a promising direction for future research.

2.  **Elegant and Novel Algorithmic Design:** The STAR-T algorithm's core idea—a hierarchy of partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds and increasing bucket capacities—is a bold and clever concept. It provides an intuitive mechanism for balancing the immediate objective value with the diversity needed for robustness. This is a non-trivial and creative synthesis of ideas from streaming (thresholding) and robust non-streaming methods (partitioning), resulting in a genuinely new algorithm.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper establishes a constant-factor approximation guarantee for this challenging setting. While the constant itself (0.149) may seem modest, achieving *any* constant factor for a problem combining the constraints of single-pass streaming and robustness against arbitrary removals is a significant theoretical accomplishment. Furthermore, the memory complexity, particularly its logarithmic dependence on `k`, represents a substantial improvement over concurrent work and is a key theoretical result.

4.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is well-designed and compelling. The choice of baseline—allowing SIEVE-STREAMING to run on the dataset *after* removals are known—is an exceptionally strong "oracle" competitor. The fact that the proposed method remains competitive against this baseline strongly validates the quality of the robust summary `S`. The interactive movie recommendation scenario is an excellent illustration of the practical utility of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Modest Approximation Constant:** The proven approximation ratio of 0.149 is not particularly high. While this is likely a byproduct of a complex analysis for a difficult problem, and the empirical results suggest much stronger practical performance, it does leave significant room for theoretical improvement. This is a minor point, however, as the existence of a constant-factor guarantee for this new problem is the main takeaway.

2.  **Dependence on Optimal Value:** The algorithm, in its basic form, requires an estimate `τ` of the optimal solution value `f(OPT)`. The authors address this with the standard technique of running multiple instances in parallel, which is a sound and accepted workaround in the streaming literature. Nonetheless, it adds a layer of complexity and a logarithmic factor to the space requirement, which is a slight practical drawback.

**Recommendation**

This paper represents a significant step forward in the field of large-scale submodular optimization. It introduces a novel, important problem and presents an elegant and creative algorithmic solution, backed by both a solid theoretical guarantee and strong empirical evidence. The core idea of partitioned thresholding is a powerful concept that is likely to inspire follow-up work.

While the theoretical approximation constant could be improved and the reliance on guessing the optimal value is a minor inelegance, these points do not detract from the paper's main achievement. This is precisely the type of work NIPS should champion: it is bold, opens up a new research direction, and provides a complete contribution that is both theoretically and practically compelling. I strongly recommend acceptance.
