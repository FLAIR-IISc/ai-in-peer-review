Summary
The paper studies robust submodular maximization in the streaming model, where after a single pass we must retain a compact summary S that, for any subsequent deletion of up to m elements, supports returning a size-k set Z with a constant-factor approximation to OPT(k, V\E). The proposed STAR-T algorithm arranges elements into exponentially sized partitions of buckets, each governed by exponentially decreasing thresholds, and stores at most roughly O((k + m log k) polylog k) elements. At query time, the method runs GREEDY on S\E. The analysis shows a constant-factor guarantee (≈0.149 asymptotically in k) assuming a threshold parameter τ that is an α-approximation to f(OPT(k, V\E)), and extends to the unknown-τ case via multiple parallel instances. Experiments on dominating set and a personalized recommendation objective show performance comparable to SIEVE-STREAMING (even giving the latter the advantage of knowing E).

Strengths
- Tackles an important, timely problem: robust submodular maximization under a strict streaming constraint, relevant to “right to be forgotten” and large-scale summarization.
- Algorithmic idea—partitioned, exponentially sized buckets with per-partition thresholds—is natural and appealing, and connects clearly to SIEVE-STREAMING and non-streaming robust schemes (e.g., PRO).
- The memory dependence on m is O(m log^2 k) (vs. O(mk) of deletion-robust streaming), which is a notable improvement when k is large.
- Query-stage decoupling is practical: reuses a single precomputed summary for many different deletions.
- Empirical results on real datasets demonstrate reasonable robustness and practicality.

Weaknesses and issues (many are minor but numerous; some are more serious)
Technical correctness/clarity
- τ depends on unknown E: The main algorithm and Theorem 4.1 assume τ is an α-approximation to f(OPT(k, V\E)), yet E is unknown at streaming time. The resolution is deferred to Section 4.1 via multiple parallel instances, but the exposition of Theorem 4.1 still quantifies “for any E” while τ depends on that E. Please clarify the precise statement: with τ chosen independent of E (e.g., via a grid on value guesses), can one select the appropriate summary after E is revealed? What is the exact query-time selection rule and overhead?
- In Theorem 4.1, the definition “τ = 1 + (1−e−1)(1−e−1/3)(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉) f(OPT(…))” appears dimensionally inconsistent (the “1 +” term). Likely a missing division; please correct.
- Inconsistency in memory bounds: the abstract/contributions and conclusion state O((k + m log k) log^2 k), while the proof (Eq. (3)) yields O((k + m log k) log k). Section 4.1 adds a multiplicative log1+ factor. Please reconcile and present a single, precise memory bound (streaming and total, with/without τ-guessing).
- Time complexity is not analyzed. The per-element processing may be O(∑i w⌈k/2^i⌉) = Θ(wk), which can be large; SIEVE-STREAMING is O(log k) per element. This omission is critical for streaming practicality. Please provide a bound on marginal evaluations per element and empirical runtime.
- The approximation constant is low (~0.149) and further reduced by the (1+ε) factor in Section 4.1. Discussion of tightness or room for improvement would help, as well as comparison to deletion-robust streaming [18] in practice.
- Several proof steps are only sketched with appeals to “on average” (e.g., Lemma 4.2 “on average |Bi,j∩E| is relatively small”), yet E can be adversarial. The argument presumably uses pigeonhole principle over many buckets; please make this explicit in the main text and ensure constants align with w ≥ ⌈4⌈log k⌉ m/k⌉.
- Eq. (5) appears malformed: “12 α2(1−γ) + 1 α1”. Please fix typesetting and show the algebra leading to 0.149.
- Algorithmic threshold description oscillates: prose says τ/2^i, Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i,k}. They’re equal for i ≤ ⌈log k⌉, but please state this clearly to avoid confusion.

Experimental evaluation
- No comparison with the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18], which achieves 1/2−ε with O(mk log k/ε) memory. Even a small-scale empirical comparison would strengthen the paper.
- Limited datasets and metrics. No reporting of memory usage, number of oracle calls, or throughput; no variance bars; unclear stream ordering and seeds. Ablations (vary w, m, k) are missing. The choice w=1 often violates the theoretical condition on w; please show sensitivity and regimes where guarantees plausibly hold.
- In dominating set, E is sampled from S (the summary), which disadvantages the method, but it also does not reflect adversarial deletions from V; please also test deletions sampled from V (random and adversarial).
- STAR-T-SIEVE vs. SIEVE-STREAMING: the former runs on S\E, the latter on V\E. This is fine to showcase “summarization power,” but please also include a baseline that runs SIEVE-STREAMING on the full stream without E (to show end-to-end cost and memory) and report the memory/time used by STAR-T.

Writing/formatting (numerous minor issues)
- Typos/grammar: “refered”→“referred”; “peformance”; “sizem”; “st Conference on NIPS”; inconsistent capitalization (GREEDY vs. Greedy), spacing (N (Z)), punctuation, and math typesetting (ceil/floor as d·e).
- Some references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted; check years, venues, page ranges.
- Notation: define log’s base; consistently use ⌈log2 k⌉ if that’s intended.

Originality and significance
- The partitioned-thresholding streaming design for robust submodular maximization appears novel and interesting, with a favorable memory dependence on m (logarithmic in k). The approximation factor is weaker than in related centralized robust work but comparable in spirit to streaming trade-offs. The empirical evaluation is promising but currently too thin to establish practical impact.

Pros
- Addresses an important robust streaming problem with a clean algorithmic idea.
- Improved memory scaling in m relative to prior deletion-robust streaming work.
- Reusable summaries for multiple deletions; consistent with practical needs.

Cons
- Expositional and technical inconsistencies (τ’s dependence on E; memory bounds; malformed equations) that obscure correctness.
- Missing per-element time analysis; potential Θ(wk) marginal evaluations per element could be prohibitive.
- Limited and somewhat unbalanced experiments; no comparison to [18]; no runtime/memory reporting; weak ablations.
- Many minor writing/formatting issues.

Recommendation: Reject (weak). The core idea is promising and could be significant, but the paper needs a careful pass to fix technical/expositional inconsistencies, include time complexity, reconcile bounds, and substantially strengthen experiments (including comparison to [18] and sensitivity to w, m, k). I encourage resubmission after addressing these points.

Confidence: High.
