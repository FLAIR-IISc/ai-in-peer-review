This paper tackles the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, a relevant and challenging task with applications in data summarization and related areas. The authors propose a two-stage procedure, STAR-T, combining a streaming algorithm with a greedy algorithm for the query stage. The core idea is to construct a robust summary using partitioned buckets with decreasing greedy thresholds.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem statement, proposed approach, and theoretical results. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and its importance, connecting it to various applications. The problem formulation is precise, and the authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant papers in robust submodular maximization and streaming algorithms. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, with a theorem providing approximation guarantees. The experimental evaluation, though limited in scope, provides evidence supporting the algorithm's effectiveness and compares it favorably with existing methods like SIEVE-STREAMING. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** The problem of robust streaming submodular maximization is well-motivated and addresses a critical need in large-scale data analysis.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide theoretical bounds on the performance of their algorithm, demonstrating its approximation quality.
*   **Novelty:** The use of partitioned buckets with decreasing thresholds in the streaming stage is a novel contribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide some empirical evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness in practical scenarios.
*   **Interactive aspect:** The ability to answer multiple queries after a single pass over the data (using the precomputed summary) is a valuable practical benefit.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm's performance seems to be sensitive to the choice of the parameter *w*, which controls the memory usage. The paper provides some guidance on setting *w*, but a more detailed analysis of its impact on performance would be beneficial.
*   **Constant Factor:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149) is relatively small. While theoretically sound, it raises concerns about the practical utility of the algorithm compared to other methods.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation is limited to two datasets and a few specific scenarios. A more comprehensive evaluation with a wider range of datasets and tasks would strengthen the empirical validation.
*   **Comparison with PRO:** The paper mentions the PRO algorithm [11], but a direct comparison with it in the experiments would be valuable.
*   **Limited explanation of parameter approximation:** While section 4.1 describes the mechanism of approximating f(OPT(k,V\E)), the description lacks details making it hard to understand exactly how the approximation is achieved in practice.
*   **Memory Complexity:** Although logarithmic in *k*, the memory complexity, O((k + m log k) log k), can still be substantial for very large values of *k* and *m*. A discussion of memory optimization techniques or alternative data structures could be added.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a challenging and relevant problem with a novel approach.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the algorithm's performance.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness and competitiveness.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
*   The parameter sensitivity and memory complexity could pose practical challenges.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee and the limited scope of the experimental evaluation are concerns, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the clear presentation make this a worthwhile submission. Given the importance of the problem and the potential impact of the proposed solution, I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should provide more detailed analysis on the selection of the parameter *w*, explore the possibility of improving the constant factor in the approximation guarantee, and expand the experimental evaluation to include more datasets and tasks, and a direct comparison against the PRO algorithm. Additionally, a more detailed description of the approximation mechanism from section 4.1 would be very helpful.
