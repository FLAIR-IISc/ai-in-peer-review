- Paper addresses robust submodular maximization in one-pass streaming with sublinear space to produce a summary S that supports extraction of a size-k solution after any m deletions.  
- Method: STAR-T streaming algorithm organizes elements into exponentially sized partitions with decreasing marginal-gain thresholds; query stage runs GREEDY on S\E.  
- Main theoretical result: constant-factor approximation with space O(k log k + m log^2 k) when f(OPT) known, and O((k + m log k) log^2 k) via multi-guess when unknown; approximation constant approaches ~0.149 as k grows.  
- Experiments on dominating set and MovieLens show STAR-T+GREEDY matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows E) and is close to full GREEDY in small-scale settings.  
- Relation to prior work: builds on SIEVE-STREAMING (non-robust streaming) and PRO/Orlin et al. (robust non-streaming), and trades approximation for much better k-dependence compared to concurrent deletion-robust streaming [Mirzasoleiman et al.].  
- Novelty: principled combination of partitioned summaries and exponentially decreasing thresholds tailored for robustness with nontrivial analysis balancing full-bucket vs. non-full cases.  
- Theoretical strength: one-pass algorithm with provable constant-factor guarantee and only logarithmic dependence on k in the robust memory term.  
- Practicality: simple streaming rule and implementable query stage that avoids re-scanning the stream.  
- Empirical strength: solid results on real datasets and a compelling interactive recommender use case.  
- Main weakness: modest approximation factor (~0.149); discussion of tightness or improvement avenues is missing.  
- Presentation inconsistency: space bounds differ across sections (intro/conclusion vs. Theorem 4.1); multi-guess extra log factor should be made explicit early.  
- Threshold description mismatch: text says τ/2^i but Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i,k}; need to reconcile and explain min for last partition.  
- τ formatting and specification unclear in Theorem 4.1; experiments lack clarity on how τ or guesses and ε/grid are chosen.  
- Runtime concern: per-element cost may be Θ(wk) in worst case due to scanning buckets; practical runtime/scalability and sensitivity to w need discussion.  
- Missing empirical baseline: concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18] is not compared experimentally; a comparison or justification for omission is needed.  
- Experimental reproducibility: missing exact values for w, ε, number of τ guesses, stopping criteria, and wall-clock/memory usage; figures need clearer labeling.  
- Quality: theoretical development is sound with coherent case analyses and supplementary proofs; minor presentation fixes required.  
- Clarity: generally well-written and organized, but needs fixes for typos, notation/formatting issues, and more implementation detail.  
- Originality: novel combination of known ideas applied to robust streaming, yielding favorable space scaling in k.  
- Significance: addresses an important practical problem (robust summaries under deletions) with an attractive space–approximation trade-off likely to influence follow-up work.  
- Pros summary: one-pass robust streaming algorithm, favorable space dependence on k, simple algorithm and query stage, and solid empirical support.  
- Cons summary: relatively low constant factor, presentation inconsistencies, missing experimental comparison to [18], and potential high per-element cost for large k if w>1.  
- Questions for authors: clarify τ formula and experimental selection, report per-element processing time and heuristics to prune scans, provide memory/runtime vs. k and m and w ablations, and discuss adaptation to sliding-window/turnstile models.  
- Recommendation: Accept (borderline to clear) pending clarity fixes and additional experimental details.  
- Reviewer confidence: high for theory assessment; moderate for empirical evaluation due to limited experimental detail.
