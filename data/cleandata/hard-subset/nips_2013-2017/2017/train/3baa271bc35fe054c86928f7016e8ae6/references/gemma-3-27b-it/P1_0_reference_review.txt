## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm (STAR-T) that first constructs a robust summary of the data in a single pass, and then uses a greedy algorithm on this summary to respond to queries involving the removal of up to *m* elements. The core contribution lies in a novel partitioned thresholding scheme during the streaming phase, enabling a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The authors also provide theoretical bounds on the size of the retained summary and demonstrate empirically that their algorithm performs competitively with, and sometimes outperforms, existing methods like SIEVE-STREAMING.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a rich history of submodular maximization literature. It acknowledges the limitations of greedy algorithms in streaming settings and leverages ideas from prior work like SIEVE-STREAMING for efficient streaming. It also connects to recent research on robust submodular maximization, citing works by Krause et al., Orlin et al., and Bogunovic et al., drawing inspiration from partitioning approaches like PRO, but introduces a different thresholding scheme and analysis. The authors clearly position their work within the context of interactive and adaptive submodular optimization, highlighting the applicability of their approach to scenarios where removals are not known beforehand.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a relevant and challenging problem:** Robustness is crucial for real-world applications of submodular maximization, and the streaming setting adds significant practical constraints.
*   **Novel algorithmic approach:** The partitioned thresholding scheme offers a clever way to balance robustness and space complexity. The exponentially decreasing thresholds are a key insight.
*   **Strong theoretical guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee, while potentially loose (see weaknesses), provides a solid foundation for the algorithm’s performance. The bounds on memory usage are also valuable.
*   **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The paper includes experiments on two different datasets (social network and movie recommendation) and compares against relevant baselines (SIEVE-STREAMING, RANDOM, and STAR-T-SIEVE). The results demonstrate competitive performance.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, and the figures effectively illustrate the algorithmic approach.
*   **Interactive component:** The ability to efficiently respond to multiple removal queries using a single precomputed summary is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Constant factor in approximation guarantee:** While a constant-factor guarantee is provided, the constant (0.149) is relatively small. This raises questions about the practical tightness of the bound and the potential for improvement.  The dependence on `1/dlog ke` further diminishes the guarantee as k grows.
*   **Parameter setting:** The algorithm relies on setting parameters like *w* and *τ* appropriately. The paper mentions using w ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem / k ⌉ and an approximation of f(OPT(k,V\E)) for *τ*. Estimating *τ* accurately without knowing the optimal solution beforehand requires careful consideration and could be a practical challenge. The theorem 4.5 addresses this, but adds to complexity.
*   **Experimental setup limitations:**  The experiments primarily compare against SIEVE-STREAMING, which is given an unfair advantage in certain scenarios (knowing the removed elements beforehand). While the authors acknowledge this, it's important to consider how the algorithm would perform against a fully informed adversary. The use of a relatively small dataset (MovieLens 1M) for the recommendation task limits the scalability demonstration.
*   **Complexity of analysis:** The theoretical analysis is quite involved, making it difficult to fully grasp the intricacies of the proof. More intuitive explanations of the key analytical steps would be beneficial.
* **Lack of discussion on the impact of *w*:** While the authors state w = 1 works well in practice, they don't adequately discuss the trade-offs involved in choosing different values of *w*. How does increasing *w* affect performance and memory usage?



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and theoretically grounded approach.
*   The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance in realistic scenarios.
*   The work has the potential to be valuable to researchers and practitioners working with large-scale data summarization and robust machine learning.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The relatively small constant factor in the approximation guarantee raises concerns about practical performance.
*   The algorithm's reliance on parameter tuning and the complexity of the analysis could hinder its widespread adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of submodular maximization. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is a concern, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical analysis, and the experimental results are strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper is well-written and clearly presents its ideas.  However, I recommend the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly regarding parameter tuning and a more thorough discussion of the theoretical guarantees.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of how to practically estimate *τ* in scenarios where the optimal solution is unknown.
2.  Elaborate on the trade-offs involved in choosing different values of *w*.
3.  Consider adding experiments with larger datasets to demonstrate the algorithm's scalability.
4.  Provide more intuitive explanations for the key steps in the theoretical analysis.
