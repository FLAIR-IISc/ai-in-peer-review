Summary
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, where a single-pass algorithm must construct a small summary S that remains useful after the adversarial deletion of up to m items. The proposed approach, STAR-T, organizes S into log k + 1 partitions of exponentially increasing bucket sizes, and uses exponentially decreasing marginal-gain thresholds to decide where to place each arriving element. At query time, after a deletion set E (|E| ≤ m) is revealed, the method runs GREEDY on S \ E to return a k-size solution. The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation guarantee f(Z) ≥ c · f(OPT(k, V \ E)) with c ≈ 0.149 as k → ∞, using one pass and space O(k log k + m log^2 k) (modulo a minor inconsistency noted below). The paper also gives a standard “guessing τ” variant (à la SIEVE-STREAMING) when OPT is unknown, and empirical results on dominating set and a facility-location–like personalized recommendation task.

Relation to prior work
- Streaming submodular maximization: Builds on greedy-thresholding ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al. 2014) and fast greedy variants (Kumar et al. 2015), extending them to robustness against deletions.
- Robust submodular maximization (offline): Relates to Krause et al. (2008) and Orlin et al. (2016), and the partitioned non-streaming PRO approach (Bogunovic et al. 2017). The partitioned, exponentially increasing buckets come from this lineage, but the streaming assignment via thresholds is new.
- Concurrent streaming robustness: Mirzasoleiman et al. (ICML 2017) give a 1/2 − ε approximation but with memory Θ(mk log k/ε). The present work trades a lower constant (≈0.149) for much lower memory dependence on k (logarithmic), scaling as O(m log^2 k + k log k).

Strengths
- Novelty and technical ideas: The “partitioned thresholding” design is a clean and plausible way to obtain deletion-robustness in one pass, and the analysis that balances two regimes (many full buckets versus none) is insightful. The pigeonhole-style argument via w replicated buckets per partition to control |Bi ∩ E| is elegant.
- Theory: One-pass, oracle-based algorithm; adversarial deletions; memory scaling nearly linear in k plus m log^2 k; constant-factor guarantee independent of m (given sufficient memory). The parallel-τ construction for unknown OPT is standard and appropriate.
- Practicality: Simple query phase relying on GREEDY over S \ E; empirical evidence suggests the summaries are useful even beyond the theoretical m; STAR-T competes with a clairvoyant SIEVE-STREAMING baseline that knows E in advance.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Space bound inconsistency: The abstract/introduction and conclusion claim O((k + m log k) log^2 k), while Theorem 4.1 and Eq. (3) give |S| ≤ (log k + 5)wk, with w ≈ Θ((m log k)/k), implying O(k log k + m log^2 k). Please reconcile these; if the true bound is O((k + m log k) log k) versus O((k + m log k) log^2 k), this materially changes the headline.
- Threshold τ depends on f(OPT(k, V \ E)): Since E is only revealed at query time, the dependence on OPT(k, V \ E) is especially delicate. The parallel-τ remedy (Section 4.1) is plausible, but the paper does not clearly explain: (i) the range and initialization of τ guesses independent of E, (ii) how S is selected at query time (e.g., running GREEDY over O(log_{1+ε} k) instances and picking the best), and (iii) the additional query-time cost. Please make this explicit.
- Approximation constant: 0.149 is quite conservative compared to 1/2 for non-robust streaming and 0.387 for robust non-streaming. While the memory–approximation trade-off is compelling versus [18], a discussion of whether the constant can be improved (even modestly) or whether there are lower bounds would help.
- Time complexity per stream element: Each element checks up to sum_i w·⌈k/2^i⌉ ≈ O(wk) buckets; with w ≈ Θ((m log k)/k), the per-element work is O(m log k), which could be large when m is a substantial fraction of k. This is not discussed in the paper; empirical throughput numbers or an implementation note would help.
- Clarity/notation issues:
  - Threshold rule: Text says τ/2^i but Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}; the latter is correct but should be stated consistently.
  - τ in Theorem 4.1 appears garbled; please fix parentheses and constants.
  - The “on average” phrasing in Lemma 4.2’s proof sketch is misleading; the argument is deterministic via pigeonhole-style counting. Please clarify.
  - Several small typographical issues in references and equations.
- Experimental evaluation:
  - No comparison to [18] (Deletion-robust submodular maximization), even on small k where memory is manageable. A qualitative discussion of why it’s impractical here would help if a direct comparison is infeasible.
  - The choice w=1 in practice departs from the theoretical requirement w ≥ ceil(4 m log k / k). While this is common, it would be valuable to show sensitivity to w and m, and quantify when guarantees plausibly break down.
  - More details on how τ is handled in practice (parallel instances? τ scheduling?).

Questions for the authors
1) Which space bound is correct: O(k log k + m log^2 k) (from Eq. 3) or O((k + m log k) log^2 k) (stated elsewhere)?
2) How are τ guesses initialized without knowing E? What bounds on OPT do you use, and how do you select among parallel instances at query time? What is the query-time overhead?
3) What is the per-element processing time in your implementation, and how does it scale with m and k in practice?
4) Can your analysis be tightened to improve the 0.149 constant? Any empirical ratio estimates?
5) Is the approach extendable to matroid or knapsack constraints?

Pros
- Clear and novel algorithmic idea; solid analysis for adversarial deletions.
- Strong memory–approximation trade-off versus concurrent work.
- Simple query mechanism and practical experiments on two tasks.
- Potentially broadly useful for “right to be forgotten” and interactive recommendation.

Cons
- Space bound inconsistency; some clarity/notation issues.
- Low theoretical constant; lack of runtime analysis.
- Missing empirical comparison to the most relevant concurrent robust streaming baseline.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a meaningful and original advance on an important problem with a sound analysis, and the empirical evidence is promising. Addressing the clarity and evaluation issues above would strengthen it further.
