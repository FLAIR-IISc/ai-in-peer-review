

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper proposes a streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, extending the SIEVE-STREAMING approach to handle cases where up to m elements may be removed from the summary. The authors introduce a partitioned thresholding structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds to achieve robustness against element removals.

**Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work**

The paper addresses a meaningful extension of submodular maximization to a robust setting, where the summary must remain effective after arbitrary removal of up to m elements. The authors build upon the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8] and previous robust submodular maximization work [9, 10, 11]. The key technical contribution is a partitioned thresholding approach where the summary is organized into buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds, and the theoretical analysis combines properties of this structure with the greedy thresholding rule.

**Strengths**

1. The problem is well-motivated with relevant applications in social network analysis and personalized recommendation systems.
2. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, providing a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for the robust setting.
3. The experimental validation in two distinct domains (dominating set and movie recommendation) demonstrates practical relevance.
4. The paper addresses the practical challenge of not knowing f(OPT(k, V\E)) in advance by proposing a parallel variant (Theorem 4.5).

**Weaknesses**

1. **Theoretical Guarantee is Disappointingly Low**: The key theoretical result (Theorem 4.1) provides a constant-factor approximation of 0.149, which is significantly lower than the standard greedy algorithm's 0.632 guarantee for non-robust submodular maximization. This is a major drawback that the paper does not adequately address. The authors claim their method "matches (and in some cases outperforms)" SIEVE-STREAMING, which achieves a 0.5-ε approximation, yet their theoretical guarantee is substantially worse. This discrepancy between theory and practice needs clearer explanation.

2. **Practical Significance Questionable**: The low approximation guarantee (0.149) raises questions about the practical utility of the method. Given that standard greedy methods achieve ~0.632 without any robustness guarantees, and SIEVE-STREAMING achieves ~0.5, the marginal improvement in robustness comes at a significant cost in approximation quality. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate why this trade-off is worthwhile for practitioners.

3. **Incomplete Comparison with Robust Baselines**: The paper compares primarily against SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows removals in advance), but doesn't adequately compare against other robust submodular maximization approaches. For instance, [11] provides a centralized robust algorithm with a 0.387 approximation guarantee. It's unclear why the proposed streaming method would be preferable to applying [11] offline when robustness is required.

4. **Theoretical Assumption vs. Practical Implementation**: The algorithm assumes knowledge of f(OPT(k, V\E)) (τ in the algorithm), which is not known a priori. While the paper addresses this by proposing a parallel variant (Theorem 4.5), this increases space complexity by a factor of log1+ k, which could be prohibitive for large-scale applications. The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to select an appropriate τ in practice.

5. **Experimental Results Lack Depth**: The experiments show that the method performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING, but don't clearly demonstrate the value of the robustness property. For example, when the authors remove elements "greedily" (by iteratively removing the element that reduces the objective value the most), the performance difference between STAR-T-GREEDY and SIEVE-STREAMING is minimal, suggesting the robustness property may not provide significant benefits in practice.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents a technically sound extension to the streaming submodular maximization problem, but the practical significance is questionable due to the low approximation guarantee (0.149) compared to standard methods. The theoretical contribution appears to be incremental rather than transformative, and the experimental results don't convincingly demonstrate that the robustness property provides meaningful benefits over simpler approaches.

I recommend rejection. The paper would need to substantially improve the approximation guarantee, provide a clearer explanation of why the trade-off between robustness and approximation quality is worthwhile, and demonstrate a more compelling advantage over existing robust submodular maximization methods to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The current work falls short of meeting NIPS' high standards for methodological soundness and practical significance, particularly given the established nature of submodular optimization and the relatively modest improvements offered by this approach.
