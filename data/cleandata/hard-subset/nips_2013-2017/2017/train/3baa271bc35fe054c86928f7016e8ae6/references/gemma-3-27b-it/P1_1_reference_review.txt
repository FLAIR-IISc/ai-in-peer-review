## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm, STAR-T, designed to efficiently find a representative subset of data that is resilient to the removal of a fixed number of elements. The core idea involves partitioning the data into buckets with exponentially increasing sizes and associated decreasing greedy thresholds during the streaming phase.  A simple greedy algorithm is then applied in a query stage on the remaining elements after a removal set is specified. The authors provide theoretical guarantees, demonstrating a constant-factor approximation, and empirically validate their approach on dominating set and personalized recommendation tasks, showing competitive performance with existing streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING, even when the latter has prior knowledge of the removals.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon established work in submodular maximization (Nemhauser et al., 1978), streaming algorithms (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014), and robust submodular maximization (Krause et al., 2008, Orlin et al., 2016, Bogunovic et al., 2017). It correctly positions itself relative to SIEVE-STREAMING, highlighting its advantage in logarithmic memory dependence compared to concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017) which has linear dependence on *k*.  The work extends ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING like greedy thresholding to incorporate robustness, and leverages a similar partitioning structure to PRO (Bogunovic et al., 2017) but differs in its bucket filling strategy and analysis.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Robustness is a critical consideration in many real-world applications of submodular maximization, and this paper tackles it effectively in the challenging streaming setting.
* **Novel Algorithm Design:**  The partitioned thresholding scheme is a clever approach to balance robustness and efficiency. The exponential increase in bucket size and decreasing thresholds seem well-motivated.
* **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide a constant-factor approximation guarantee, demonstrating a solid theoretical foundation for the algorithm.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic (dominating set) and real-world (movie recommendation) datasets support the theoretical findings and demonstrate competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art algorithms.  The interactive recommendation scenario showcases a practical application of the approach.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper is generally well-organized and logically presented. The algorithms are clearly described, and the notation is generally consistent.
* **Handles Unknown OPT:** The authors address the practical difficulty of knowing `f(OPT(k, V \ E))` *a priori* with a parallel instance approach, further enhancing the practicality of the solution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Constant Factor is Small:** The constant factor (0.149) in the approximation guarantee is rather small, and the theoretical results show it decreases as k grows. While theoretically sound, this could limit the practical performance compared to algorithms with better constants.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the choice of parameters like `w` and `τ`, although the paper does provide guidance on setting `w`.  Further exploration of parameter tuning and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Proofs:**  The proofs, while correct, are quite dense and could benefit from more detailed explanations and intermediate steps to enhance readability.  Some proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, which can hinder understanding.
* **Limited Comparison:**  While comparison to SIEVE-STREAMING is present, a more thorough comparison to other relevant streaming algorithms, particularly those focusing on robustness, would strengthen the paper.
* **Discussion of Practical Implications:** While the interactive recommendation scenario is promising, a deeper discussion of the practical implications and limitations of the algorithm in real-world deployments would be valuable. For example, how does the cost of maintaining the pre-computed summary compare to the cost of re-optimization?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for a significant problem (robust submodular streaming).
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate competitive performance.
* The authors address a practical challenge (unknown OPT) and offer a solution.
* The work builds upon existing research in a meaningful way and contributes to the broader field of submodular optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The small constant factor in the approximation guarantee is a concern.
* The proofs are complex and could be more accessible.
* A more thorough comparison to related work would strengthen the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**.  While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is a limitation, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical soundness, and the promising empirical results make it a valuable contribution to the field. The authors have addressed an important and challenging problem with a well-designed algorithm.  The weaknesses are primarily related to clarity and completeness of the presentation, and could be addressed with minor revisions.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors focus on:

*   Clarifying the proofs and potentially adding more intermediate steps.
*   Expanding the experimental evaluation to include comparisons to more relevant algorithms.
*   Providing a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the algorithm, including parameter tuning and computational cost.
