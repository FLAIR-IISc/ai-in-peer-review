Summary
The paper studies robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting with a two-stage approach. In stage 1, the proposed STAR-T algorithm stores a summary S using a partitioned bucket structure with exponentially increasing bucket capacities and exponentially decreasing thresholds. Elements are inserted into the first non-full bucket whose marginal exceeds the partition threshold. In stage 2 (query), after removal of up to m arbitrary elements E, the algorithm runs GREEDY over S\E to produce a k-sized set. The main theorem asserts a constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉) using space O((k + m log k) log k) assuming access to a threshold τ tied to OPT; a parallelized variant removes this assumption at an extra log factor in space. Experiments on dominating set and personalized recommendation show performance comparable to SIEVE-STREAMING (which is allowed to know E in advance).

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on the streaming thresholding paradigm of SIEVE-STREAMING and the robust partitioning ideas of PRO (non-streaming). Independently concurrent with this work, deletion-robust streaming submodular maximization (Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17) achieves a 1/2−ε approximation with space O(mk log k/ε). The present paper trades approximation for substantially reduced dependence on k in memory, using O((k + m log k) polylog k).

Strengths
- Addresses an important, timely variant: robustness to deletions in the true streaming model with one pass and sublinear space.
- Clear high-level idea: partitioned buckets plus decreasing thresholds; the structure is simple to implement.
- Nontrivial analysis combining cases (many full buckets vs. few) to obtain a constant-factor guarantee.
- Memory bound has only logarithmic dependence on k beyond the k term, and linear in m up to a log k factor; attractive when m is moderate.
- The two-stage “summary then multiple queries” mode is practically useful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Inconsistencies and clarity issues:
  - Space bound is stated inconsistently: abstract/intro and conclusion say O((k + m log k) log^2 k), while Theorem 4.1 gives O((k + m log k) log k) under known τ, and Theorem 4.5 adds another log factor. This should be unified and made precise for both known and unknown τ.
  - Threshold description conflicts: text says partition threshold τ/2^i; Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Please reconcile and justify the exact threshold schedule used in the proofs and implementation.
- Approximation factor is small (≈0.149), significantly below 0.5−ε of SIEVE-STREAMING in the non-robust case and of the concurrent deletion-robust work (albeit with much higher memory). The paper should discuss whether this constant is improvable or fundamental given the memory profile.
- Empirical validation is limited and misaligned with the theory:
  - The key parameter w, which controls robustness vs memory, is fixed to w=1 in all experiments, even when removing 2k elements. This violates the theoretical setting (w ≥ 4⌈log k⌉ m/k) and leaves the main trade-off untested. There is no sensitivity analysis over m and w, no reporting of stored summary size vs the bound, and no timing/space measurements.
  - The unknown τ case (parallel instances) is not evaluated; the extra memory/log factors and overhead are not characterized empirically.
  - Baselines are incomplete. The most relevant robust-streaming baseline (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2017) is not compared. Also, a simple baseline that over-allocates capacity (e.g., run SIEVE-STREAMING for k+m and then GREEDY on S\E) is not included.
  - The streaming nature is not stressed at scale. The graphs (Twitter, Amazon) are moderate; wall-clock, pass count, and oracle call complexity are not reported.
- Some proof sketch steps rely on average-case arguments over E within a partition (e.g., Lemma 4.2 intuition “on average, |B∩E| is relatively small”) but the claims are for worst-case E; the supplemental presumably closes this gap, but the main text could be clearer about how worst case is handled (via pigeonhole/min-cut style arguments with w).
- Notation and presentation rough edges: frequent use of ⌈log k⌉ in constants, occasional typographical issues, and reliance on supplementary for central arguments may impede reproducibility.

Quality
Technically plausible with an interesting design and a careful case analysis, but there are inconsistencies (thresholds, space bound) and the experiments do not stress or validate the core robustness-memory trade-offs the theory claims. The approximation factor is also quite conservative.

Clarity
Readable at a high level, but key inconsistencies and missing implementation details (τ estimation, w tuning, actual memory used) detract. Recommend tightening the algorithm description vs proofs, unifying space bounds, and adding guidance on setting w and τ in practice.

Originality
Moderate. The partitioned buckets idea is adapted from prior robust non-streaming work; the streaming thresholding is adapted from SIEVE-STREAMING. Their combination for robust streaming is novel, and the memory-approximation trade-off differs from concurrent work.

Significance
Potentially useful if the memory savings are critical and m is moderate. However, given the small constant and limited experimental evidence, the current impact is uncertain.

Questions for the authors
- Please reconcile the threshold schedule and space bounds across sections. Which variant is analyzed and implemented?
- How should practitioners set w and τ without prior knowledge of m and OPT? What is the overhead of the parallel τ scheme in practice?
- Can you add experiments varying m and w, report summary sizes, and compare to a naive k+m over-allocation and to deletion-robust ICML’17 if feasible?
- Is the 0.149 constant inherent to your framework, or can it be improved with refined analysis?

Pros
- Addresses an important robust streaming problem.
- Simple, implementable algorithmic structure.
- Theoretical constant-factor guarantee with memory sublinear in n and only logarithmic dependence on k (beyond the k term).
- Potentially valuable two-stage “summarize once, answer many” paradigm.

Cons
- Inconsistent statements of thresholds and memory; small approximation constant.
- Experiments do not validate the key robustness-memory trade-offs; w fixed to 1 regardless of m.
- Missing key baselines and resource measurements.
- Clarity issues and reliance on supplementary for core arguments.

Recommendation: Weak reject

Confidence: High
