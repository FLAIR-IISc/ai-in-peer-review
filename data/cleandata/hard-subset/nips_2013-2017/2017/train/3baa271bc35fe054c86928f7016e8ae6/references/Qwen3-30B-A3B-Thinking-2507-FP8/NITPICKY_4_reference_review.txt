

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-stage streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization. The streaming stage constructs a robust summary using a partitioned thresholding approach, while the query stage uses a simple greedy algorithm on the remaining elements after removals. The authors claim a constant-factor approximation guarantee and demonstrate competitive performance against SIEVE-STREAMING in experiments.

## Major Weaknesses

### 1. Critical Inconsistency in Algorithm Description (Most Serious Issue)
The paper contains a fundamental inconsistency between Algorithm 1 and its textual description. Line 5 of Algorithm 1 states: "f(e | Bi,j) ≥ τ/min{2i, k}", while the text claims "buckets in the first partition will only store elements that have marginal value at least τ" (with thresholds decreasing as τ/2^i). These are mathematically inconsistent - the threshold should be τ/2^i, not τ/min{2i, k}. This inconsistency undermines the entire algorithm design and would likely cause implementation errors. As a reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of a paper with such a critical methodological flaw.

### 2. Weak Theoretical Guarantee
The claimed approximation ratio of 0.149 (approaching 0.149 as k→∞) is exceptionally weak for submodular maximization. Standard greedy algorithms achieve 1-1/e ≈ 0.632, and even the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm achieves a (0.5-ε)-approximation. The authors' constant is more than 4x smaller than the standard greedy guarantee, making the theoretical contribution significantly less valuable than claimed.

### 3. Unfair Experimental Comparison
The experiments compare against SIEVE-STREAMING "that knows in advance which elements will be removed." This is not a realistic comparison for a streaming algorithm, as in practice the removals would not be known in advance. The paper should compare against a fair baseline that does not have prior knowledge of removals.

### 4. Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies
- "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" should be "The Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)"
- Multiple instances of inconsistent notation: "dlog ke" vs. "⌈log k⌉"
- Reference formatting errors: "Learning mixtures of submodular functions for image collection summarization, S. Tschiatschek, R.K. Iyer, H. Wei, J.A. Bilmes, Advances in neural information processing systems, 2014, pp. 1413–1421., , " (double comma)
- Title contains "Thresholding" instead of "Thresholding" (typo)

### 5. Theoretical Analysis Gaps
The proof sketch of Theorem 4.1 contains logical gaps. Lemma 4.2 claims f(Z) ≥ (1-e^-1)(1-4m/wk)τ, but τ is defined as 1/(1-(1-e^-1)(1-e^-1/3))(1-1/dlog ke)f(OPT(k, V\E)), which is an incorrect expression. The constant factor derivation appears flawed, and the paper does not adequately justify why the chosen τ value leads to the claimed approximation ratio.

## Minor Weaknesses

- The paper claims "our algorithm matches (and in some cases outperforms) the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm" but does not provide statistical significance testing for these claims.
- The memory complexity is stated as O((m log k + k) log² k), but the proof in Theorem 4.1 shows O((k + m log k) log k), suggesting a possible error in the complexity statement.
- The paper cites [18] but does not adequately differentiate their approach from the concurrent work, which also provides a streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization.

## Strengths

- The problem of robust submodular maximization is well-motivated and important for large-scale applications.
- The partitioned thresholding approach is novel in the context of robust streaming algorithms.
- The two-stage framework is intuitive and could be valuable for interactive applications.

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses an important problem with a novel algorithmic approach, it contains multiple serious flaws that undermine its contribution. The critical inconsistency in the algorithm description, the extremely weak theoretical guarantee (0.149 vs the standard 0.632 for submodular maximization), and the unfair experimental comparison are substantial issues that cannot be overlooked. The formatting errors and notational inconsistencies further suggest a lack of attention to detail.

As a perfectionist reviewer who prioritizes technical rigor and precision, I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper. The flaws are too significant to be addressed through minor revisions. The paper would benefit from a complete reworking of the theoretical analysis, a correction of the algorithm description, and a fairer experimental comparison before it could be considered for publication.

**Recommendation: Reject**
