

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm: a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary in one pass, and a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that, given any removal of up to m elements from the summary, returns a near-optimal solution using only the precomputed summary. The work builds upon SIEVE-STREAMING [8] for submodular maximization and extends it to handle robustness against element removals.

The paper makes a clear connection to prior work. It references the robust submodular maximization problem introduced in [9], the centralized algorithm PRO [11] that achieves robustness with a similar partition structure, and the streaming algorithm SIEVE-STREAMING [8] for standard submodular maximization. The authors note that their work differs from [18] by achieving a memory complexity of O((k + m log k) log k) compared to O(mk log k/ε), with a better constant factor in the approximation guarantee.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical soundness:** The theoretical analysis is thorough, with multiple lemmas supporting the main result. The paper proves that STAR-T constructs a summary of size O((k + m log k) log k) and that STAR-T-GREEDY achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of approximately 0.149 for the robust problem. The proof strategy, which considers two cases (partitions with mostly full buckets versus partitions with mostly empty buckets), is well-structured and addresses the challenge of handling arbitrary element removals.

2. **Novelty:** The key innovation is the partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds across partitions. This differs from prior work that fills buckets greedily one after another [11] or uses a single threshold [8]. The authors successfully extend the streaming paradigm to the robust setting while maintaining a reasonable memory footprint.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-written with clear problem formulation, algorithm descriptions (with pseudocode), and theoretical results. The figures (e.g., Figure 1) help illustrate the partitioned thresholding structure. The connection between the theoretical results and practical applications is well-established in the experimental section.

4. **Experimental validation:** The authors demonstrate their approach on two applications: dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation. The experiments show that their method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed) in terms of objective value after removals, despite having to handle arbitrary removals. The results are statistically significant (averaged over 100 iterations) and cover both random and greedy removal scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Approximation factor:** The theoretical guarantee of approximately 0.149 is significantly lower than the (1-e^-1)≈0.632 guarantee for standard submodular maximization. While the authors acknowledge this as a trade-off for robustness, the paper could better contextualize this limitation and discuss whether such a low constant is practically acceptable for the applications they consider.

2. **Theoretical-practical disconnect:** The theoretical analysis requires w ≥ ⌈4dlog kem/k⌉, but the experiments use w=1, which is much smaller than the theoretical bound. The paper should more clearly explain why w=1 works well in practice despite the theoretical requirement, or provide additional analysis for the practical parameter choice.

3. **Limited experimental scope:** The experiments focus on two applications with limited parameter variations. More comprehensive experiments across different datasets, problem sizes, and robustness parameters would strengthen the practical validation. The paper does not compare against other robust submodular maximization methods beyond SIEVE-STREAMING.

4. **Memory complexity:** The paper claims O((k + m log k) log k) memory complexity, but the analysis shows O((k + m log k) log^2 k) in Theorem 4.1 (the log^2 k term appears in the problem statement but not in the theorem statement). This inconsistency needs clarification.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Addresses an important practical problem (robustness against arbitrary element removals) in large-scale submodular optimization.
- Presents a theoretically sound approach with a clear two-stage structure that enables efficient query responses.
- Demonstrates competitive empirical performance compared to strong baselines that have knowledge of removals.
- Builds logically on prior work while introducing a novel partitioned thresholding scheme.

*Con:*
- The approximation factor (0.149) is relatively low compared to standard submodular maximization guarantees.
- The theoretical analysis has some inconsistencies (e.g., log^2 k vs log k in the memory complexity).
- The experimental validation is limited to two applications with insufficient parameter exploration.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of streaming submodular maximization by addressing the important practical challenge of robustness against element removals. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the two-stage approach is well-motivated. The experimental results provide good evidence of practical utility, particularly given that the proposed method matches or outperforms baselines that have knowledge of future removals.

The main limitation is the relatively low approximation factor, which is a necessary trade-off for the robustness requirement. However, the paper clearly frames this as a trade-off and demonstrates that the approach remains practically useful. The memory complexity advantage over concurrent work [18] is also a significant contribution.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and properly contextualizes its contribution within the relevant literature. While the approximation factor is lower than standard results, the authors provide a compelling argument for why this is an acceptable trade-off for the robustness requirement. The experimental validation, while limited, is sufficient to demonstrate the practical value of the approach.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a significant step forward in robust submodular optimization for large-scale applications and makes a clear technical contribution to the field. The work is novel, technically sound, and addresses an important practical problem with meaningful theoretical and empirical results.
