This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure, STAR-T, combining a novel partitioned thresholding algorithm for the streaming stage with a simple greedy algorithm for query answering after element removals. The problem is well-motivated, with clear applications in data summarization, influence maximization, and recommendation systems.

The core idea of partitioning the data stream into buckets with decreasing thresholds is interesting and appears to be a key component enabling the robustness guarantee. The theoretical analysis is thorough and provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee under reasonable assumptions. The empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach on two different datasets, comparing favorably with existing algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING.  The authors also convincingly demonstrate the benefit of a single robust summary for multiple query scenarios.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Relevance:** The problem of robust streaming submodular maximization is important and practically relevant, particularly in dynamic environments where data is constantly changing.
* **Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach and the combination with a greedy algorithm in the query stage offer a novel solution to the problem.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with a constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is a significant achievement.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm and its competitiveness compared to existing approaches.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the algorithm and theoretical results.  Figures 1 and 2 are helpful for understanding the approach.
* **Interactive Setting:** The discussion of using the robust summary for multiple query scenarios (e.g., different user preferences) highlights a practical advantage of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to parameters like `w` (the memory parameter), which requires careful tuning. The paper acknowledges this, but further discussion on practical guidelines for setting this parameter would be helpful.
* **Constant Factor:** While a constant-factor approximation guarantee is established, the constant itself (0.149) is quite small.  Exploring possibilities for improving this constant would be valuable.
* **Theoretical Complexity:** The analysis, while rigorous, is dense and may be challenging for some readers to fully grasp. More intuitive explanations of the key analytical steps could improve accessibility.
* **Comparison to PRO:** The paper mentions the PRO algorithm [11] but only briefly compares its performance. A more detailed comparison, particularly regarding memory usage and computational complexity, would be beneficial.  It’s noted that PRO achieves the same approximation result; a discussion of the trade-offs (e.g., ease of implementation, streaming capability) would be useful.
* **Limited Baselines:**  While comparisons to SIEVE-STREAMING and RANDOM are provided, exploring other relevant baselines, especially for the interactive recommendation application, might strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound solution to an important problem.
* The theoretical guarantees are strong and provide confidence in the algorithm's performance.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
* The algorithm’s performance might be sensitive to parameter tuning.
* The theoretical analysis is complex and might be difficult for some readers to follow.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The proposed algorithm is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically validated. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee could be improved, and the parameter sensitivity needs consideration, the overall quality of the work is high.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Provide more practical guidance on choosing the parameter `w`.
* Investigate strategies for improving the constant factor in the approximation guarantee.
* Offer more intuitive explanations of the key analytical steps.
* Include a more detailed comparison to the PRO algorithm [11].
* Explore additional baselines for the interactive recommendation application.
* Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the approach.
