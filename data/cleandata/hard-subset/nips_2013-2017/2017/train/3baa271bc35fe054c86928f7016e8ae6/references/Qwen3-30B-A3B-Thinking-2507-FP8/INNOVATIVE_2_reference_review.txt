

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper introduces a novel two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors propose a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary of size O((k + m log k) log² k) in a single pass, followed by a query stage (STAR-T-GREEDY) that efficiently handles arbitrary removals of up to m elements from the summary. The core innovation lies in their "partitioned thresholding" approach, where the constructed summary is organized into partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While previous work on robust submodular maximization (e.g., [9, 10, 11]) focused on centralized settings, and streaming submodular maximization (e.g., [8]) addressed the non-robust case, this paper is the first to combine both streaming and robustness requirements in a single-pass algorithm. The partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a bold and creative solution that enables the algorithm to handle arbitrary removals while maintaining a compact summary. The theoretical analysis is particularly impressive, as it carefully combines properties of the robust structure with the decreasing thresholding rule to establish a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149(1-1/log k)).

The paper's significance is clear: robustness is a critical requirement in many real-world applications (e.g., social network influence maximization, personalized recommendations), but previous approaches either required multiple passes over data or could not handle arbitrary removals efficiently. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (a leading streaming algorithm) even when SIEVE-STREAMING knows in advance which elements will be removed, which is a strong practical validation of their theoretical claims.

The theoretical contribution is particularly strong. Theorem 4.1 provides a rigorous constant-factor approximation guarantee for the robust streaming setting, which is a non-trivial extension of previous work. The paper's handling of the unknown f(OPT(k, V\E)) parameter via parallel instances (Theorem 4.5) is elegant and practical. While the approximation constant (0.149) is lower than the (1-1/e) guarantee for standard submodular maximization, this is an expected trade-off for the added robustness requirement, and the authors clearly establish why this is necessary.

The experimental validation is convincing. In the dominating set problem on Twitter and Amazon networks, STAR-T-GREEDY matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, even when SIEVE-STREAMING knows in advance which elements will be removed. In the movie recommendation application, the approach performs within a few percent of the optimal greedy solution across multiple practical scenarios (genre-based filtering, already-seen movies), demonstrating the practical utility of the robust summary. The fact that the same precomputed summary can handle multiple different removal scenarios is particularly impressive and shows the approach's real-world applicability.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement, algorithm descriptions, and theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail, and the experimental section provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness. The authors properly contextualize their work against related contributions, including the recent independent work by [18], and clearly articulate how their approach differs.

While there are minor limitations—such as the relatively low approximation constant compared to non-robust settings, and the theoretical dependence on m in the memory complexity—the novelty and significance of the approach far outweigh these concerns. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization by introducing a completely new framework for handling robustness in this setting.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be highly significant. It introduces a bold new approach to a meaningful problem, provides rigorous theoretical guarantees, and demonstrates practical effectiveness. The partitioned thresholding structure is a genuinely novel contribution that opens up new directions for future research in robust streaming algorithms.

The paper's contribution is clearly distinct from previous NIPS work, particularly in its combination of streaming and robustness requirements. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and will likely inspire further work in this area. While the approximation constant is not optimal, the authors provide a clear theoretical explanation for this trade-off, and the practical performance results more than compensate for this.

In summary, this paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective approach to a challenging problem in streaming submodular optimization. The partitioned thresholding approach is a significant conceptual advance that deserves recognition. I strongly recommend acceptance.
