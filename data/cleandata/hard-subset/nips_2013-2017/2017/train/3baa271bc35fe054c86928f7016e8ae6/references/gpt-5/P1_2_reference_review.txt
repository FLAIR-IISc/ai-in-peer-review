Summary
The paper studies robust submodular maximization in the one-pass streaming setting. The goal is to build, during a single pass over a very large ground set V, a compact summary S such that after the adversarial deletion of up to m elements (E ⊆ V, |E| ≤ m), one can extract from S\E a size-k solution whose value approximates OPT(k, V\E), without revisiting the stream. The authors propose a two-stage approach:

- Streaming stage (STAR-T): Maintain a partitioned data structure consisting of w·⌈k/2^i⌉ buckets in partition i=0,…,⌈log k⌉, with bucket capacities min{2^i,k} and thresholds τ/min{2^i,k} that decrease exponentially with i. Each arriving element is placed into the first non-full bucket whose marginal gain exceeds the bucket’s threshold.
- Query stage (STAR-T-GREEDY): After any deletion set E (|E|≤m), run standard GREEDY on S\E to output a size-k set.

Theory: With w ≥ ⌈4⌈log k⌉m/k⌉ and a suitable τ that depends on OPT, the method stores O((k + m log k) log k) elements and guarantees a constant-factor approximation f(Z) ≥ c·f(OPT(k,V\E)) with c→0.149 as k→∞. When OPT is unknown, the paper follows the SIEVE-STREAMING idea of running multiple τ-guess instances in parallel, incurring an extra O(log k) factor in space and a (1+ε) loss, yielding space O((k + m log k) log^2 k) and the same constant (up to 1/(1+ε)). The analysis relies on: (i) a “many full buckets” case, where some bucket maintains value near τ even after deletions, and GREEDY on S\E inherits a (1−1/e) factor; (ii) an “all buckets sparse” case, where a trade-off between the value of a minimally hit bucket in the last partition and the residual value covered by elements placed earlier yields two complementary bounds that are balanced to derive the final constant. Empirically, on dominating set (Twitter, Amazon) and facility-location-style recommendation (MovieLens), the approach matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter is given knowledge of E; it also enables interactive, multiple-query reuse of S.

Relation to prior work: The approach blends ideas from robust non-streaming methods (e.g., PRO’s partitioned structure [11]) and streaming thresholding (SIEVE-STREAMING [8]). It contrasts with the concurrent deletion-robust streaming work of Mirzasoleiman et al. [18], which achieves a 1/2−ε approximation but requires O(mk log k/ε) memory; the proposed method sacrifices approximation constant for significantly reduced memory: O((k + m log k) log^2 k), only logarithmic in k for the m-dependent part.

Quality
- Theory: The main theorems are technically plausible and carefully structured. The memory/approximation trade-off is clearly articulated, and the removal of OPT-dependence via multi-τ instances follows established practice [8]. The constants (0.149) are modest but constant, and the dependence on m appears near-linear in m up to log factors, which is attractive. The “case” analysis (full vs. sparse partitions) and balancing is standard but nontrivial in the robust setting.
- Correctness/precision: There is a small inconsistency in space claims across sections: Theorem 4.1 yields O((k+m log k) log k) when τ is known; the intro and conclusion state O((k + m log k) log^2 k). This is later justified by Theorem 4.5 (unknown OPT), which incurs an extra log k factor; please state this alignment explicitly upfront. Also, in the algorithm description the narrative says the threshold in partition i is τ/2^i, while the pseudocode uses τ/min{2^i,k}; these are equivalent for i≤⌈log k⌉ but the paper should state the min explicitly in the text for consistency.
- Runtime: A significant practical concern is per-element processing time. The number of buckets across all partitions is Θ(wk); in the worst case, an element may be tested against many buckets before being inserted or discarded, implying up to O(wk) marginal evaluations per element. This is much higher than SIEVE-STREAMING’s O(log k) checks and deserves discussion, empirical timing, and/or heuristics (e.g., skipping full buckets quickly, random bucket choice within a partition, capping checks, or lazy evaluation).
- Experiments: The tasks (coverage-style dominating set and facility-location recommendation) are appropriate. It is commendable that SIEVE-STREAMING is given the advantage of knowing E. However, the experimental methodology often removes elements from S (not from V), which disproportionately hurts the proposed method; while this demonstrates robustness of S, it would be helpful to also report experiments where E is sampled from V (or determined greedily over V) to provide a complementary perspective and to more directly compare against baselines. Further, please report: (i) sensitivity to w and m; (ii) memory usage (|S|) and per-element processing time; (iii) comparison to [18] if feasible (or a discussion clarifying why it is impractical to include).

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and readable. The partitioned structure and thresholds are clearly explained; Figure 1 (referenced) helps. Proof sketches convey the key ideas; full proofs in the supplement are appropriate.
- Please clarify: (1) how τ is chosen in experiments (grid of τ? which ε?); (2) a brief pseudocode/description for the multi-τ parallelization and its implementation footprint; (3) the role of parameter choices (partition growth factor 2, threshold schedule) and whether other choices could improve constants.
- Notation: Use min{2^i,k} consistently in both text and code. Check minor typographical issues (e.g., dlog ke spacing).

Originality
- The partitioned-thresholding design that combines exponentially growing capacities with exponentially decreasing thresholds is novel in the streaming robust setting. The analysis that couples “full-bucket” robustness with “sparse-bucket” residual coverage is original and informative. The work is clearly distinguished from PRO [11] (centralized, greedy bucket filling) and SIEVE-STREAMING [8] (non-robust, fewer instances), and positioned relative to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [18].

Significance
- Robustness to deletions is central for “right to be forgotten,” failures, and interactive recommendation scenarios. The method offers a compelling space-approximation trade-off that is markedly better in memory than [18] for large k, with good empirical performance. The reusable summary enabling many queries is practically valuable. The relatively low constant (≈0.149) may limit purely theoretical appeal, but in practice the method performs competitively.

Pros
- Addresses an important, timely problem (robust streaming submodular maximization).
- Elegant algorithmic design with clear intuition; one-pass streaming with reusable summary.
- Strong theoretical guarantees with near-optimal space scaling in m (up to logs) and independence from k in the m term (logarithmic factor only).
- Solid empirical validation on two relevant applications; competitive with advantaged baselines.

Cons
- Potentially high per-element computational cost (O(wk) checks) not analyzed or benchmarked.
- Experiments omit comparison to the concurrent 1/2−ε algorithm [18] and lack sensitivity analyses (w,m, runtime/memory).
- Minor clarity issues around thresholds and space bounds (known vs unknown OPT).
- Approximation constant is relatively small.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include time-per-element and memory usage plots; analyze the impact of w and m on accuracy and cost.
- Discuss and/or implement practical heuristics to reduce the number of bucket checks per element.
- Clarify τ selection in experiments and provide a brief algorithm box for the parallel-τ variant.
- If possible, add a comparison (empirical or simulated memory–quality trade-off) to [18].
- Consider discussing whether changing the partition growth factor or threshold schedule can improve constants.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a novel and practically relevant algorithm with solid theory and promising experiments. Addressing the runtime concerns and clarifying the noted issues would further strengthen the submission.
