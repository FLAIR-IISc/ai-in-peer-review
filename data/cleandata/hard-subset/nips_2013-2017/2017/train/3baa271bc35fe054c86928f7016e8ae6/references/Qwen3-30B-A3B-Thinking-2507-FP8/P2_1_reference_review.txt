

# Review: Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. The authors propose a two-stage approach: (1) a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary set S in one pass over the data, and (2) a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that, after any m elements are removed from S, computes a near-optimal solution from S \ E. The key innovation is a partitioned thresholding structure where the summary is arranged into partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds, allowing for robustness against m element removals while maintaining sublinear memory usage. The paper builds upon SIEVE-STREAMING [8] and recent robust submodular maximization work [11], extending them to the streaming setting with a novel analysis.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with a rigorous proof of the approximation guarantee (Theorem 4.1). The proof carefully handles two cases: when a partition has many full buckets versus when all partitions have mostly empty buckets. The authors also properly address the practical issue of not knowing f(OPT(k, V \ E)) a priori by providing a parallel variant (Theorem 4.5) that achieves a (1+ε)-approximation with modest memory overhead. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows the removed elements in advance) and other baselines across two distinct applications (dominating set and movie recommendation). The results consistently show that STAR-T-GREEDY matches or outperforms the baselines, even when the removed elements are adversarial.

However, the theoretical approximation guarantee of 0.149 (approaching this as k → ∞) is substantially lower than the standard greedy algorithm's 1-1/e ≈ 0.632 guarantee for non-robust submodular maximization. The paper doesn't adequately explain why this reduction is necessary or provide intuition about the gap between robust and non-robust cases. This is a significant weakness that weakens the theoretical contribution.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement, contributions, and algorithm descriptions are precise and easy to follow. The proof sketches provide helpful intuition for the main theoretical results, and the experimental section is well-structured with meaningful comparisons. The authors effectively use Figure 1 to illustrate the partitioned thresholding structure. The paper is well-suited for the NIPS audience, with sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

## Originality
The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature. The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds is novel for the robust submodular maximization problem in streaming settings. While previous work [11] has considered a centralized robust algorithm with a similar partitioned structure, the authors' streaming adaptation with a different bucket-filling strategy is a substantial innovation. The paper also properly situates its contribution relative to concurrent work [18], noting that their memory complexity (O((k + m log k) log²k)) is superior to [18]'s O(mk log k/ε) dependence on k.

## Significance
The problem of robust submodular maximization is highly relevant to real-world applications like recommendation systems, social network analysis, and data summarization where element removals are common. The two-stage approach (precomputing a robust summary, then quickly responding to removals) is particularly valuable for interactive applications. The experimental results demonstrate that the approach matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows the removed elements in advance), showing practical viability. This is a significant contribution as it provides a practical solution for large-scale robust optimization where reoptimization after removals would be prohibitively expensive.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical contribution with rigorous analysis
- Novel partitioned thresholding approach for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Comprehensive experimental validation across two distinct applications
- Practical two-stage framework that enables interactive applications
- Better memory complexity than concurrent work [18]

**Weaknesses:**
- The 0.149 approximation guarantee is substantially lower than standard submodular maximization results (1-1/e ≈ 0.632), without sufficient explanation
- The paper doesn't adequately address why the robustness requirement leads to such a significant approximation gap
- The theoretical bound depends on 1/dlog k, which approaches 1 as k increases, but the paper doesn't clearly explain the practical implications of this bound

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on submodular maximization, addressing an important practical problem with a novel algorithmic approach. While the theoretical approximation guarantee is lower than standard results, the paper provides a compelling practical solution that matches or outperforms baselines in experiments, which is particularly valuable for large-scale applications. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

The main weakness is the low approximation guarantee, but this is partially offset by the strong experimental results showing competitive performance in practice. The authors should consider addressing this gap more explicitly in the revision (e.g., by providing intuition for the approximation gap or discussing how the robustness requirement inherently limits the approximation ratio). Nevertheless, the paper's theoretical and practical contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS.
