This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The core idea of partitioning the data and using decreasing thresholds for element inclusion is conceptually sound, building upon existing work in submodular streaming and robustness. However, I find the presentation lacking in clarity, and the theoretical guarantees, while present, are rather weak, particularly considering the complexity added by the robustness requirement. The empirical validation, while demonstrating comparable performance to SIEVE-STREAMING, doesn’t convincingly showcase a significant advantage, and relies on a somewhat unfair comparison where SIEVE-STREAMING *knows* the removals beforehand.

The paper’s main strength lies in tackling a genuinely challenging problem – combining streaming, robustness, and submodularity. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing greedy approaches in large-scale settings and attempt to address them. The connection to previous work, particularly SIEVE-STREAMING and the robustness literature (e.g., [9, 10, 11]), is reasonably well established.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall contribution. First, the notation and algorithmic description (Algorithms 1 & 2) are difficult to follow. The rationale behind the choice of partition sizes and thresholds isn't sufficiently motivated. Why exponentially decreasing thresholds? What is the intuition behind the `w` parameter? These are critical aspects of the algorithm, and the explanation feels superficial. Second, the theoretical analysis, while technically correct, yields a rather pessimistic constant factor (0.149 as k goes to infinity) which significantly limits the practical applicability of the algorithm.  The authors acknowledge this, but it's a serious issue. The dependence on `dlog ke` is also concerning as it potentially impacts scalability. Third, the experimental setup, particularly in the dominating set problem, feels skewed. Comparing against SIEVE-STREAMING when it has prior knowledge of removals is not a fair comparison.  A more convincing demonstration would be to simulate a removal *after* the summary has been created, without informing SIEVE-STREAMING. Moreover, the experimental section lacks a comparison against simpler baselines, such as a uniform random selection with robustness constraints. Fourth, the claim of matching or outperforming SIEVE-STREAMING feels overstated given the experimental results, which show comparable performance at best. The interactive movie recommendation experiments are somewhat more convincing, but still lack a strong justification for why this algorithm is preferred over existing methods.  Finally, the discussion of practical resilience exceeding theoretical bounds feels anecdotal without more robust supporting evidence.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*   Offers a novel algorithmic approach combining streaming, robustness, and submodularity.
*   Provides a theoretical analysis, albeit with a weak constant factor.
*   Demonstrates comparable performance to a strong baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING), although under potentially biased conditions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in presentation and algorithmic description.
*   Weak theoretical guarantees with a limited constant factor.
*   Experimental setup is potentially biased and lacks comprehensive baselines.
*   The claimed improvements over existing methods are not convincingly demonstrated.
*   The parameter choices and their rationale aren’t sufficiently explained.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is borderline. While the problem is important and the ideas are interesting, the lack of clarity, weak theoretical guarantees, and somewhat unconvincing empirical validation raise significant concerns. The paper feels incomplete and insufficiently polished for NIPS. I would lean towards rejection, but would be open to reconsidering with a major revision addressing the clarity and experimental issues, and ideally, strengthening the theoretical bounds.  The authors should focus on providing a more compelling justification for their approach and demonstrating a clear advantage over existing methods. The experimental comparisons need to be more rigorous and fair.
