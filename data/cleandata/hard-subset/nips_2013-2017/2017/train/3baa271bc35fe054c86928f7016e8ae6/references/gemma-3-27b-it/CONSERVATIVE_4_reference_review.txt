This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The motivation is well-articulated, and the problem formulation is clear and relevant to several applications. However, despite a seemingly novel partitioning and thresholding approach, I find the paper lacking in convincing evidence of significant advancement over existing methods. My primary concern revolves around the extensive reliance on asymptotic analysis and a rather limited empirical evaluation.

The core idea – partitioning the data stream and using decreasing thresholds – is intuitively reasonable, building on ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING. However, the theoretical guarantees presented (Theorem 4.1) are weakened by the dependence on `dlog k`, ultimately leading to a constant factor approximation that approaches 0.149 as *k* grows. This is a rather small constant, and while the authors acknowledge this, the practical implications aren’t fully addressed. The assumptions required to achieve this bound (specifically, the choice of *w* and *τ*) seem stringent and may not hold in many real-world scenarios. The extension to handle unknown `f(OPT(k, V \ E))` (Theorem 4.5) adds complexity but doesn't fundamentally alter the overall theoretical result.

The clarity of the presentation is acceptable, but could be improved. The description of Algorithm 1 and 2, while correct, feels somewhat terse. More detailed explanations of the intuition behind the partitioning scheme and threshold selection would be beneficial. The notation is generally consistent, but the many variables and lemmas contribute to a dense reading experience.

The originality of the work is incremental. The partitioning strategy and decreasing thresholding rule are novel combinations, but they feel like natural extensions of existing techniques, particularly SIEVE-STREAMING. The paper doesn’t present a radical departure from established approaches. The related work section adequately cites relevant papers, but fails to deeply engage with the strengths and limitations of these existing approaches in the context of robustness.

The significance of the work is questionable. The experiments, while demonstrating that the proposed algorithm *matches* SIEVE-STREAMING, do not show any clear advantage. The claim of *outperforming* SIEVE-STREAMING in some cases is weak, as it’s not consistently observed. The synthetic datasets used in the experiments are not fully representative of real-world streaming data. The movie recommendation example, while illustrative, lacks a thorough comparison against state-of-the-art recommendation algorithms. The observation that the same summary can be used for multiple removals is interesting, but isn’t a particularly groundbreaking contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem is important and well-motivated.
*   The proposed algorithm is a reasonable extension of existing techniques.
*   The theoretical analysis, while complex, is mathematically sound.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical guarantees are weak (a constant factor of 0.149).
*   The empirical evaluation is limited and doesn’t demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The originality is incremental.
*   The asymptotic analysis heavily influences the results, making it difficult to assess practical performance.
*   The experiments lack diversity and depth.



**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards **reject** based on the limited practical benefits demonstrated and the weakness of the theoretical guarantees. The paper feels more like a theoretical exploration of a natural extension of existing ideas than a significant breakthrough. While the work is technically sound and the authors have clearly invested considerable effort, it doesn't meet the NIPS standard for originality and significance. The authors would significantly strengthen their submission by:

1.  Conducting more extensive experiments with diverse, real-world datasets.
2.  Demonstrating a clear and consistent advantage over SIEVE-STREAMING (or other relevant baselines) in these experiments.
3.  Providing a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical results, including the sensitivity to the choice of parameters *w* and *τ*.
4.  Investigating alternative partitioning strategies and thresholding rules that might lead to stronger theoretical guarantees.
