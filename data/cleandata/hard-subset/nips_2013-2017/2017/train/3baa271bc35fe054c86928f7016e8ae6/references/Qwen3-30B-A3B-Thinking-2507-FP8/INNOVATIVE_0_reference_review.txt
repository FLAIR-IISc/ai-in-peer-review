

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper introduces a novel two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The authors propose a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary in a single pass, followed by a query stage where a simple greedy algorithm operates on the remaining elements after any m elements have been removed. The key innovation lies in their partitioned thresholding approach, where the constructed summary is arranged into partitions with buckets whose sizes increase exponentially with the partition index, and buckets in different partitions are associated with greedy thresholds that decrease exponentially with the partition index.

The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a meaningful problem: robust data summarization for large-scale applications where reoptimization after element removal is infeasible. While prior work (e.g., [9, 10, 11]) has studied robust submodular maximization, this paper is the first to provide a streaming solution with theoretical guarantees. The authors build upon SIEVE-STREAMING [8] but introduce a novel exponentially decreasing thresholding scheme that enables robustness against any number of removals m.

The theoretical contribution is solid, with Theorem 4.1 establishing a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for the robust streaming problem. The analysis cleverly combines properties of the robust structure with the decreasing greedy thresholding rule, and the authors provide a comprehensive proof sketch along with references to detailed proofs in the supplementary material. While the approximation constant (0.149) is lower than the 0.5-ε guarantee of SIEVE-STREAMING, it's important to note that the paper solves a fundamentally different problem (robust vs. standard submodular maximization), and the authors provide the first constant-factor approximation for the robust streaming problem.

The experimental validation is convincing. In both the dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation applications, STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed), and often outperforms a random baseline. The results demonstrate that the same robust summary can be used to regenerate recommendations for multiple different removals, which is a compelling practical advantage.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and provides a thorough comparison with related work. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the lower approximation ratio) and provide a parallel variant (Theorem 4.5) that addresses the practical issue of not knowing f(OPT(k, V\E)).

**Strengths:**
- Novel partitioned thresholding approach for robust submodular maximization
- First constant-factor approximation for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Strong theoretical guarantees with clear analysis
- Practical experiments demonstrating competitive performance
- Clear two-stage framework that enables efficient query processing after element removal

**Weaknesses:**
- The approximation constant (0.149) is lower than standard streaming algorithms (0.5-ε)
- Memory requirement O((k + m log k) log² k) is higher than SIEVE-STREAMING's O(k log k)
- The theoretical guarantee requires knowing f(OPT(k, V\E)), though the authors address this with a parallel variant

**Novelty Assessment:**
This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by introducing a new approach to robust submodular optimization in the streaming setting. The partitioned thresholding structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a bold and novel idea that fundamentally differs from previous approaches. While prior work has studied robust submodular maximization, this is the first to provide a streaming solution with theoretical guarantees for the robust problem.

**Significance Assessment:**
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in large-scale machine learning where robustness against element removal is critical, and where reoptimization is infeasible. The two-stage framework they propose (streaming summary followed by query processing) is a valuable new direction that could inspire future work in robust data summarization. The practical results showing competitive performance against SIEVE-STREAMING (which has better theoretical guarantees but doesn't handle robustness) demonstrate the practical relevance of the approach.

**Conclusion:**
As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper to be highly valuable. The partitioned thresholding approach is a significant conceptual advance, and the two-stage framework for robust submodular maximization represents a new direction in the field. While the approximation constant is lower than some previous work, this is expected for the robust problem, and the authors provide the first constant-factor approximation for this setting. The theoretical guarantees are sound, the experimental results are convincing, and the paper is well-written.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. The novelty and conceptual contribution outweigh the minor weaknesses in the approximation ratio and memory requirements. This work opens up new possibilities for robust data summarization in large-scale settings and has the potential to influence future research in submodular optimization.
