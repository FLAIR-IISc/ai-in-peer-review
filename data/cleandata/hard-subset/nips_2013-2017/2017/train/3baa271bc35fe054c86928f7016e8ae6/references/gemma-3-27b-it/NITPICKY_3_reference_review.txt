This paper presents a two-stage streaming algorithm, STAR-T, for robust submodular maximization. The core idea is to partition the elements into buckets with exponentially increasing sizes and decreasing thresholds, enabling efficient robust summarization. While the problem setting and proposed approach are relevant to the NIPS community, and the theoretical analysis appears rigorous, a multitude of minor flaws and inconsistencies detract significantly from the overall impact and justification for acceptance.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:** The paper tackles the important problem of robust data summarization in a streaming setting, motivated by applications like influence maximization and recommendation systems. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature on submodular maximization, streaming algorithms (SIEVE-STREAMING), and robust submodular function maximization.  The connection to PRO [11] is appropriately highlighted, and the difference – prioritizing logarithmic memory usage over a potentially better approximation guarantee – is clearly stated. The novelty lies in the partitioning and thresholding scheme coupled with the analysis to guarantee robustness.

**Strengths:**
* **Relevant Problem:** Robustness in streaming submodular maximization is a genuinely important and timely problem.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The analysis appears detailed and attempts to provide a concrete approximation guarantee. The proof sketch, while brief, suggests a careful consideration of the different cases.
* **Practical Considerations:** The comparison with SIEVE-STREAMING and the demonstration of performance in practical scenarios are valuable.
* **Interactive aspect:** The ability to regenerate summaries for different removal sets is a useful feature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Notational Inconsistencies & Clarity Issues:** Numerous minor notational inconsistencies hinder readability. For example, the definition of *w* is presented differently in the text (w ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem/k ⌉) compared to the equation (3), where it appears as a simple coefficient. The usage of 'dlog ke' throughout the paper is unclear; it’s defined as a number of partitions but is often used as a divisor, without a clear explanation of how it relates to *k*. 
2. **Parameter Sensitivity & Practicality of τ:** The dependence on the unknown f(OPT(k, V \ E)) via the parameter τ is a significant practical limitation. While Theorem 4.5 addresses this by suggesting multiple parallel instances, it drastically increases memory usage, negating one of the key advantages of the streaming approach. The discussion on approximating τ feels cursory.
3. **Scaling of w:** The theoretical requirement w ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem/k ⌉ suggests w could be very large, potentially undermining the sublinear space complexity claims. While experiments show w=1 works well, this is not justified theoretically and seems to contradict the analysis.
4. **Approximation Ratio:** The constant factor approximation of 0.149 (as k → ∞) is exceedingly small and raises concerns about the practical usefulness of the algorithm. Although achieving robustness often comes at the cost of approximation quality, this value seems remarkably low.
5. **Experimental Setup & Baseline Comparison:** The comparison with SIEVE-STREAMING is not entirely fair. SIEVE-STREAMING is given the benefit of knowing the removed elements *a priori*, which is a substantial advantage.  The RANDOM baseline, while useful, is not a strong enough competitor. A comparison against a centralized, non-streaming robust maximization algorithm (e.g., PRO [11]) would provide more insightful results.  The experimental sections lack details regarding parameter tuning and statistical significance. Why was α = 0.95 chosen? What is the variance of the results?
6. **Minor Typographical Errors:**  Several minor typos and grammatical errors are present throughout the paper (e.g., “refered to”, “αapproximation”).
7. **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The paper contains several redundant statements and could benefit from more concise writing.

**Arguments Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
* Presents a novel algorithmic approach with theoretical guarantees.
* Demonstrates empirical performance on real-world datasets.

**Con:**
* Very low approximation ratio.
* Notational inconsistencies and lack of clarity.
* Significant practical limitations due to parameter τ and potentially large w.
* Unfair comparison to SIEVE-STREAMING.
* Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting ideas presented in this paper, the numerous minor flaws and the extremely weak approximation ratio make it difficult to recommend acceptance. The lack of clarity and the sensitivity to parameters like τ significantly limit its practical applicability. The paper feels incomplete; more rigorous analysis on the scaling of 'w', exploration of alternative approaches to estimating τ, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation are needed.  

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. While the problem is important and the authors have made an effort, the current version of the paper is not of sufficient quality for NIPS. I encourage the authors to address the concerns raised above and resubmit in the future. The potential for interesting work here exists, but significant revisions are necessary.
