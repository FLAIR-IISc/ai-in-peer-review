This paper tackles the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage framework. In the first stage, a single-pass streaming algorithm, STAR-T, processes a stream of data to build a robust summary `S`. In the second, query stage, after an arbitrary set `E` of up to `m` elements is removed from the summary, a simple greedy algorithm is run on the remaining elements `S \ E` to produce a solution `Z` of size at most `k`. The goal is for `f(Z)` to approximate `f(OPT(k, V \ E))`, the optimal value achievable from the original ground set `V` after the removals.

The proposed streaming algorithm, STAR-T, is based on a novel partitioned thresholding approach. The summary `S` is structured into partitions, each with an exponentially decreasing greedy threshold. An incoming element is added to the first available bucket where it meets the marginal gain threshold. This design is inspired by, and effectively combines, ideas from streaming submodular maximization (e.g., the thresholding in SIEVE-STREAMING by Badanidiyuru et al., 2014) and non-streaming robust submodular maximization (e.g., the partitioned structure in PRO by Bogunovic et al., 2017). The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this procedure yields a constant-factor approximation (approaching 0.149 as `k` grows) while maintaining a summary of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The paper also provides strong empirical validation on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, showing that the proposed method is competitive with a strong baseline that has advance knowledge of which elements will be removed.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and practical problem. As datasets grow, streaming algorithms are essential, and ensuring the robustness of their outputs against failures, attacks, or evolving constraints is a critical challenge. The proposed STAR-T algorithm is a novel and elegant combination of a partitioned data structure and an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule, representing a significant contribution to the literature on both streaming and robust submodular optimization.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is solid and provides the first constant-factor approximation guarantee for this problem setting with a memory complexity that is polylogarithmic in `k`. This is a notable improvement over concurrent work (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2017), which has a memory complexity linear in `k`. The proof technique, which cleverly handles two distinct cases based on whether partitions are mostly full or not, is insightful.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The problem statement is precise, the algorithms are presented clearly, and the connection to related work is well-established. The illustrative examples (influence maximization, movie recommendation) effectively ground the abstract problem in real-world applications.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. Comparing STAR-T against SIEVE-STREAMING provided with an oracle (i.e., knowledge of the removed set `E` beforehand) is a very strong form of validation. The fact that STAR-T performs comparably, and sometimes even better, underscores the effectiveness of the proposed robust summary structure. The experiments on both random/adversarial removals and realistic application-driven removals (e.g., selecting a movie genre) convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the method.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas that could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Results:**
    *   There appears to be a minor discrepancy in the memory complexity. The abstract states a size of `O((m log k + k) log^2 k)`, whereas the derivation following Equation (3) in the proof of Theorem 4.1 suggests a bound of `O((k + m log k) log k)`. Could the authors please verify and unify these statements? The latter bound seems correct based on the derivation provided.
    *   The constant approximation factor of 0.149, while theoretically significant, is modest. It would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss if this is a limitation of the analysis technique or potentially inherent to the problem. A short comment on potential avenues for tightening this bound would be appreciated.
    *   There is a small inconsistency between the text and Algorithm 1. In Section 3, the threshold for partition `i` is described as `τ/2^i`, while in Algorithm 1 (line 5), it is `τ/min{2^i, k}`. The algorithm's version seems more correct, as bucket sizes are capped at `k`, but this should be made consistent.

2.  **Connection between Theory and Practice:**
    *   The theory requires the memory parameter `w` to be at least `O(m log k / k)`, yet the experiments are run with `w=1`. The paper rightly notes that the method performs well in practice even with this setting, suggesting the theoretical bound may be loose. This is a positive finding, but the paper would be strengthened by a more direct exploration of this gap. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance (both objective value and summary size) varies with `w` would provide valuable insight and better link the theoretical and empirical results.
    *   The algorithm requires a parameter `τ` that is an estimate of the unknown optimal value. Section 4.1 describes a standard method of running parallel instances to handle this, but the experimental section does not specify how `τ` was set in practice. Clarifying whether this parallelization was used, and with how many instances, would improve the reproducibility of the results.

3.  **Comparison to Concurrent Work:**
    *   The paper correctly identifies the key advantage over the concurrent work of Mirzasoleiman et al. [18]—namely, the better dependence of memory on `k`. However, the comparison could be slightly expanded. The other work achieves a higher approximation factor (`1/2 - ε`). A brief discussion of the trade-off would be helpful. For instance, clarifying the regimes of `m` and `k` where STAR-T's memory advantage is most pronounced would provide a more complete picture for practitioners choosing between the two methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a well-motivated, significant problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms, submodular optimization, and robustness.
*   Proposes a novel and technically sound algorithm with the first constant-factor guarantee for this problem with polylogarithmic memory dependence on `k`.
*   The empirical validation is extensive and strong, using a powerful oracle baseline to demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly positions its contributions within the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The proven constant in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
*   Minor clarity issues exist regarding the theoretical bounds and parameter settings, as noted above.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions to an important and growing area of research. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be readily addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
