This paper introduces a two-stage streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, addressing a critical need in large-scale data summarization applications where robustness to element removal is paramount. The core idea – partitioning the summary set with exponentially decreasing thresholds – is novel and cleverly designed to balance space efficiency and robustness guarantees. This is a bold approach, and I find the proposed solution quite appealing.

**Summary:** The authors tackle the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, where the goal is to maintain a representative subset of data that remains useful even after some elements are removed. They propose STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm constructing a partitioned summary, coupled with STAR-T-GREEDY, a greedy algorithm operating on the summary during a query stage after removals. They provide theoretical guarantees showing a constant-factor approximation and analyze the space complexity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach is a genuinely new idea in the context of robust submodular maximization. The decreasing threshold scheme is insightful and appears well-motivated.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The two-stage setting directly addresses the challenges of real-world applications like influence maximization and recommendation systems where dynamic data and potential element failures are common.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** Providing a constant-factor approximation guarantee in this setting is significant, even if the constant is relatively small and decreases with increasing *k*.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks supports the claims and demonstrates competitive performance against SIEVE-STREAMING, even in scenarios where SIEVE-STREAMING has access to prior removal information. The experiments demonstrating the utility of a single pre-computed summary for multiple removals are particularly compelling.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and the ideas are presented in a reasonably clear manner, although some sections are dense.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Constant Factor:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is small and diminishes as *k* grows. While a constant-factor guarantee is valuable, the practical impact of a very small constant is questionable.
*   **Space Complexity:** Although the space complexity of O((k + m log k) log k) is acceptable, the dependence on *m* could be limiting in certain applications where *m* is very large relative to *k*.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The algorithm introduces several parameters (w, τ) that require careful tuning. The paper offers some guidance, but more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Theoretical Exploration:** The theoretical analysis, while solid, focuses on asymptotic bounds. Exploring tighter bounds or addressing finite-sample performance would strengthen the work.
*    **Comparison scope:** The experiments comparing with sieve-streaming do not assess the complexity of the precomputed summary.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to a challenging problem.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a foundation for understanding the algorithm's performance.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical viability of the approach.
*   The authors clearly identify the limitations of their work and suggest avenues for future research.
*   The concept of partitioned thresholding has the potential to inspire further work in streaming submodular optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
*   The space complexity could be a limitation in certain applications.
*   The algorithm’s performance is highly dependent on careful parameter tuning.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of robust submodular maximization. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is not ideal, the novelty of the approach, the practical relevance of the problem, and the promising experimental results outweigh this limitation. I believe this paper is a good fit for NIPS, pushing the boundaries of streaming algorithms and providing a new direction for research in robust machine learning. I lean heavily towards acceptance. The authors have convincingly demonstrated a novel and promising approach to a difficult problem. Minor improvements to parameter sensitivity analysis and perhaps exploration of tighter bounds would further strengthen the work, but are not critical for acceptance.
