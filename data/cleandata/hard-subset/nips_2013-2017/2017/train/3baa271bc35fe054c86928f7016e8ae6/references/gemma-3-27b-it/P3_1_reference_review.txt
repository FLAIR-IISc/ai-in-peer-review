## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm consisting of a streaming phase that constructs a robust summary and a query phase that leverages this summary to find a solution even after elements are removed. The key innovation lies in a partitioned thresholding scheme during the streaming phase, resulting in a compact, robust summary. The authors provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating a constant-factor approximation ratio and validate their approach with experiments on both a graph-based dominating set problem and a personalized movie recommendation task. 

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the growing body of work on submodular optimization, specifically highlighting the challenges posed by large datasets and the need for robustness. It appropriately references prior work on streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular maximization (both centralized and streaming). The connection to related areas like influence maximization and interactive machine learning is also well noted.  The authors do a good job acknowledging related work and differentiating their contribution.  The mention of recent concurrent work by [18] is appreciated, and the discussion of the memory trade-offs is insightful.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Formulation:** The two-stage streaming/query setup is a realistic and practical framing of the problem, especially relevant for dynamic data streams where re-optimization over the entire dataset is infeasible.
* **Algorithmic Innovation:** The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a clever idea, balancing the need for a compact summary with robustness to removals. 
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee, while modest (0.149 in the limit as k increases), is a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm. The analysis appears rigorous and combines elements from related work effectively. 
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments on two diverse applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) lend credibility to the practical effectiveness of the approach.  The comparisons to SIEVE-STREAMING and other baselines are well-conducted and demonstrate competitive performance.  The demonstration of the interactive nature, i.e. using the same summary for multiple removal scenarios, is a strong point.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the core ideas and technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Constant Factor:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149) is quite low. While theoretically sound, it raises questions about the practical benefit of the approximation compared to simpler heuristics or alternative approaches. This is especially true given the overhead of the more complex partitioning scheme.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance is dependent on the choice of parameters like *w* and *τ*. While the paper provides some guidance on setting *w*, the choice of *τ* requires estimating *f(OPT(k, V \ E))*, which is itself a difficult problem. The proposed workaround in Section 4.1 (parallel instances) adds significant computational cost.
* **Scalability of Parallel Approach:** While the parallel approach (Theorem 4.5) addresses the *τ* estimation issue, it introduces a multiplicative factor of log(1+k) in the space complexity, potentially negating the benefits of the streaming algorithm for very large values of *k*.
* **Limited Exploration of Parameter *w*:**  The experiments mention that *w*=1 works well in practice, but a more systematic exploration of how *w* impacts performance (and memory usage) would strengthen the empirical evaluation.  Specifically, it would be valuable to see performance curves as *w* increases, illustrating the trade-off between approximation quality and memory consumption.
* **Comparison to PRO:** While the paper references PRO [11], a more detailed comparison (beyond stating that STAR-T is similar in structure) would be beneficial, especially given that PRO also achieves a constant-factor approximation with a different parameter trade-off.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and important problem in large-scale machine learning.
* Presents a novel algorithmic approach with a solid theoretical foundation.
* Validates the approach with well-designed experiments.
* Provides insights into the practical trade-offs of robust streaming submodular maximization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively low, raising concerns about practical utility.
* The parameter sensitivity and the overhead of the parallel approach for *τ* estimation add complexity.
* The limited exploration of parameter *w* and the lack of a detailed comparison to PRO leave some open questions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The proposed algorithm is innovative and theoretically well-grounded. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is a limitation, the practical experiments demonstrate competitive performance. I believe the paper is of sufficient quality for NIPS, but recommend that the authors address the weaknesses identified above in a revision. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of the parameter sensitivity and a more comprehensive comparison to related work (particularly PRO) would significantly strengthen the paper. I rate this paper as **Accept with Minor Revisions**.
