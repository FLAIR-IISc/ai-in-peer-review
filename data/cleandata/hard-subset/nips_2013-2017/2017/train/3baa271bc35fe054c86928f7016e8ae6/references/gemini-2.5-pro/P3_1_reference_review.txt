This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure where a streaming algorithm first creates a robust summary `S`, and then, after an adversarial removal of up to `m` elements, a simple greedy algorithm can be run on the remaining summary to find a high-quality solution of size `k`. The core contribution is a novel streaming algorithm, STAR-T, which elegantly combines a partitioned data structure with an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule.

The proposed method is well-situated within the literature, drawing inspiration from two key lines of work. From the streaming submodular maximization literature, it adapts the thresholding idea of SIEVE-STREAMING [8]. From the (non-streaming) robust submodular maximization literature, it uses a partitioned structure similar to that of PRO [11]. The novelty lies in the synthesis of these ideas into a new algorithm for the combined robust and streaming setting, along with a new analysis to prove its theoretical guarantees. The authors also correctly identify and compare their work to the concurrent paper by Mirzasoleiman et al. [18], noting the key difference in memory complexity.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The problem formulation is highly relevant. As datasets grow, single-pass streaming algorithms are essential, and the need for robustness against removals (due to failures, privacy constraints, or adversarial attacks) is a practical and pressing concern. This paper is one of the first to provide a formal treatment of this combined problem with theoretical guarantees. The proposed two-stage framework is a natural and practical approach.

2.  **Algorithmic Contribution:** The STAR-T algorithm is clever and well-designed. The structure of partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing thresholds is a powerful mechanism. It allows the algorithm to capture elements that provide high marginal gain early on (in low-index partitions with high thresholds) as well as elements with smaller gains that might be crucial for coverage (in high-index partitions with low thresholds). This multi-scale approach is key to building a summary that is both high-quality and robust.

3.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, culminating in a constant-factor approximation guarantee (Theorem 4.1). The proof sketch, which involves a case analysis based on whether partitions have a sufficient number of full buckets, is non-trivial and requires careful balancing of multiple technical lemmas. A key strength of the result is the memory complexity of the summary `S`, which is `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The logarithmic dependence on `k` is a significant advantage over the linear dependence in the concurrent work [18], making the approach more scalable for large `k`. The standard technique for handling the unknown optimal value (Section 4.1) is correctly applied and rounds out the theoretical contribution.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-executed and convincing. The choice of comparing against SIEVE-STREAMING, but giving it the oracle knowledge of which elements will be removed, is a strong methodological choice that clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed robust summary. The fact that STAR-T performs competitively or even outperforms this powerful baseline is impressive. The applications—dominating set and interactive movie recommendation—are well-chosen and highlight practical scenarios where this method would be valuable. The interactive recommendation experiment, in particular, effectively illustrates the utility of pre-computing a single summary that can handle diverse, ad-hoc removal queries.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Approximation Constant:** The proven approximation constant of `c ≈ 0.149` is quite low. While constant-factor approximations in this area are often loose and primarily of theoretical interest, it would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss if this is an artifact of the proof technique (e.g., from balancing multiple worst-case bounds in Eqs. 4 and 5) or if there is a fundamental reason for this value.

2.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice for `w`:** The theory requires the memory parameter `w` to scale with `m` and `log k` (`w ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem / k ⌉`). However, the experiments show excellent performance with `w = 1`. This is a significant gap. While the paper notes this, a deeper discussion would improve the paper. Does the theoretical requirement for `w` arise from a worst-case construction of the removal set `E` that is unlikely in practice? Providing some intuition for why `w=1` is sufficient for the tested scenarios would add valuable insight for practitioners.

3.  **Clarity of Thresholding Rule:** There is a minor ambiguity between the text and Algorithm 1 regarding the threshold. The text describes the threshold for partition `i` as `τ/2^i`, while Algorithm 1 (line 5) uses `τ/min{2^i, k}`. For most partitions (`i < log k`), these are identical. However, for the last partition, the algorithm uses `τ/k` if `2^i > k`. This is a more precise description and the text should be updated to reflect it for full clarity.

4.  **Comparison to Concurrent Work:** The comparison with Mirzasoleiman et al. [18] is brief. While the memory advantage is correctly highlighted, it would be useful to also explicitly state the trade-off with their approximation factor (1/2 - ε), which is better than the 0.149 proven here. A more explicit discussion of this approximation-memory trade-off would better situate the paper's contribution.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper tackles a novel, important, and well-motivated problem. The proposed algorithm is elegant and its analysis provides the first constant-factor guarantee for this setting with a memory complexity that is only logarithmic in `k`. The experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the method's practical utility.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The theoretical approximation guarantee is modest. There is a notable gap between the theoretical requirements on the parameter `w` and its effective setting in practice, which could be discussed in more detail.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a technically sound and complete piece of work that makes a significant contribution to the fields of submodular optimization and large-scale machine learning. The results are likely to be built upon by future researchers and used by practitioners. I strongly recommend acceptance.
