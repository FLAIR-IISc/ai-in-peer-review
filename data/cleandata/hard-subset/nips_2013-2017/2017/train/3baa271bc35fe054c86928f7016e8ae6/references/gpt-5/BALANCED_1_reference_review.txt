Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies robust submodular maximization in the single-pass streaming setting under a cardinality constraint. The goal is to precompute a compact summary S such that, after any subsequent removal E of up to m elements, one can extract a good size-k solution Z using only S\E. The authors propose STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that arranges elements into partitions of buckets with exponentially increasing capacities and exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds. Upon a query (i.e., a removal E), they run GREEDY over S\E (STAR-T-GREEDY). They prove a constant-factor approximation: as k grows, f(Z) ≥ 0.149·f(OPT(k, V\E)) using space O((k + m log k) log k) (plus a log(1+ε) factor for guessing f(OPT)). Empirically, STAR-T variants are competitive with SIEVE-STREAMING [8] that is allowed to know E in advance, on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks. The approach is related to PRO [11] (non-streaming robust partition structure) and extends the thresholding/sieve ideas of SIEVE-STREAMING [8] to a robust setting. The paper also discusses concurrent work [18] which attains a 1/2 − ε approximation but with memory O(mk log k/ε), i.e., linear in k, whereas STAR-T’s dependence on k is only logarithmic (outside the baseline k term), offering a different point on the memory–approximation trade-off.

Strengths:
- Novel algorithmic design that blends partitioned bucket structures (as in PRO [11]) with exponentially decreasing thresholds, yielding a robust, single-pass streaming method.
- Theoretical guarantee: constant-factor approximation after arbitrary removal of up to m elements, with memory O((k + m log k) log k), improved (logarithmic) dependence on k compared to [18].
- Practical relevance: robust summarization is important for applications like influence maximization and recommendations with “right-to-be-forgotten” constraints.
- Empirical results on real datasets show performance comparable to strong baselines; notably, STAR-T is competitive even when SIEVE-STREAMING is advantaged by knowing E.
- The two-stage framework is flexible: a single summary S supports many different removals E.

Weaknesses:
- Clarity/inconsistency issues:
  - The space bound varies across the paper: O((m log k + k) log^2 k) in the intro/conclusion vs O((k + m log k) log k) in Theorem 4.1 and O((k + m log k) log k log_{1+ε} k) in Theorem 4.5. These need reconciliation.
  - The expression for τ in Theorem 4.1 appears malformed (dimensionality: “τ = 1 + … f(OPT)”). Please correct and clearly state the required range for τ and how the parallel guessing attains it.
  - The text mentions thresholds τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 line 5 uses τ/min{2^i, k}; clarify this (it is consistent when 2^i≥k, but the narrative should match the code).
- The constant 0.149 is quite small; while acceptable for theory, discussion of whether tighter analysis or minor algorithmic tweaks can improve it would be valuable.
- Computational cost per stream element may be high: each element can be checked against O(w Σ_i ceil(k/2^i)) = O(wk) buckets. This is substantially larger than SIEVE-STREAMING’s O(log k) thresholds. The paper does not analyze per-element evaluation cost nor propose engineering to reduce it.
- Experiments are informative but limited:
  - No wall-clock or function-evaluation counts; no memory usage measurements to demonstrate the claimed space advantage.
  - Limited sweep over m and w; in practice, w=1 is used even when |E|>k. It would help to show accuracy vs. m, and how choosing w as suggested by theory impacts space/performance.
  - No empirical comparison to [18], even if only to discuss trade-offs (if implementation is unavailable, a discussion-based comparison would help).

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: The analysis is nontrivial and appears sound at a high level, combining two complementary cases. However, some presentation inconsistencies (τ definition, space bounds) should be fixed. The empirical validation is positive but would benefit from runtime/space reporting and broader parameter sweeps.
- Clarity: The main ideas are well-motivated and the algorithms are easy to follow, but the noted inconsistencies and minor notation mismatches detract. The proof sketch is clear; full proofs are in the supplement. Please proofread (e.g., typos like “st Conference”) and unify notation/bounds.
- Originality: The combination of partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds for robust streaming is new to my knowledge. The work clearly differentiates itself from prior non-streaming robust methods [10,11] and streaming non-robust [8].
- Significance: Robust streaming summarization is important; the memory–approximation trade-off distinct from [18] is valuable. While the approximation constant is modest, the memory scaling and single-pass nature are attractive, and the empirical results suggest practical utility.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Unify and carefully state the space complexity:
  - For a single τ: O((k + m log k) log k).
  - With τ-guessing: O((k + m log k) log k · log_{1+ε}(range)), and specify the “range” as in [8].
  - Ensure the abstract, intro, theorem statements, and conclusion match.
- Clarify the exact requirement on τ and fix the formula in Theorem 4.1. Summarize the parallel guessing scheme, its number of instances, and the effect on space/time.
- Analyze per-element processing time and suggest heuristics (e.g., early stopping, maintaining per-partition candidate pointers, grouping buckets with identical thresholds) to reduce the number of marginal evaluations.
- Expand experiments:
  - Report memory footprint, number of function evaluations, and runtime vs. SIEVE-STREAMING for various k, m, w.
  - Vary m systematically and study how performance degrades; include cases where w meets the theoretical bound.
  - If possible, add a discussion or synthetic experiment contrasting your memory scaling with [18], even absent an implementation.
- Discuss whether the constant can be improved (e.g., tuning thresholds, using lazy greedy in the query) and whether non-monotone extensions are feasible.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, contemporary problem with a novel algorithmic design and nontrivial theory.
- Presents a useful point in the trade-off space: single pass, low memory (logarithmic in k), robustness to deletions, and practical competitiveness.
- Empirical results on real data are strong.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Clarity and consistency issues in key bounds/parameters.
- Low approximation constant and lack of runtime/space measurements.
- Limited exploration of parameter m and the memory parameter w; no empirical comparison to concurrent [18].

Recommendation: Weak accept. The contribution is original and relevant, with solid theory and promising experiments. With the suggested revisions (cleaning up bounds/parameters, adding efficiency measurements, and expanding experimental analysis of m and w), the paper would make a valuable addition to NeurIPS.
