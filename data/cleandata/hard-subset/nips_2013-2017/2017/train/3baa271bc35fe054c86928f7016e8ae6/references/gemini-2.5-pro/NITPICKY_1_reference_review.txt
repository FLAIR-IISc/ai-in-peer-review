### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting. The goal is to select a small, robust summary set `S` from a data stream, such that even after an adversary removes up to `m` elements from `S`, one can still extract a high-quality solution of size `k` from the remaining elements without re-accessing the original stream. The authors propose a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a streaming algorithm named STAR-T constructs the summary `S` using a novel partitioned structure. This structure consists of partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds, an approach that synthesizes ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING [8] for streaming maximization and PRO [11] for non-streaming robust maximization. In the second stage, after `m` elements are removed from `S`, a standard GREEDY algorithm is run on the remainder to find the final solution. The main theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee for this procedure, with a summary set `S` of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The authors validate their approach on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, showing performance comparable to or better than SIEVE-STREAMING, even when the latter is given oracle knowledge of the elements to be removed.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a well-motivated and practically important problem. Combining the challenges of large-scale streaming data with the need for robustness against removals or failures is highly relevant for many modern machine learning applications.

2.  **Novel Algorithmic Design:** The STAR-T algorithm presents a clever synthesis of existing ideas. The combination of the partitioned structure from robust non-streaming work [11] with the thresholding mechanism from streaming algorithms [8] is a novel and non-trivial contribution to solve the robust streaming problem.

3.  **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a formal, constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is a significant theoretical result. The analysis, which must handle the complex interplay between the partitioned structure, the thresholds, and the adversarial removals, appears to be technically involved.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed. The comparison against SIEVE-STREAMING with foreknowledge of the removed set `E` is a particularly strong baseline, and the fact that the proposed method is competitive demonstrates its practical effectiveness. The interactive movie recommendation scenario is a compelling use case that highlights the utility of the approach.

### **Weaknesses**

Despite the paper's strengths, my review identifies a number of inconsistencies, points of unclarity, and minor errors that, in aggregate, detract from the quality of the submission and raise concerns about its technical precision.

1.  **Inconsistency in Memory Complexity:** There is a critical discrepancy in the stated memory complexity. The abstract claims the summary size is `O((m log k + k) log^2 k)`, whereas Theorem 4.1 claims a size of `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The proof sketch in Eq. (3) derives a bound of `(log k + 5)wk`, which, with the chosen `w`, leads to the `O((k + m log k) log k)` result. This inconsistency between the abstract and the main theorem is a major oversight and must be rectified.

2.  **Inconsistency in Algorithm Description:** There is a mismatch between the textual description of the thresholds and their implementation in Algorithm 1. The text (page 3) states that partitions have "a corresponding threshold that is exponentially decreasing with the partition index i as τ/2^i". However, line 5 of Algorithm 1 uses a threshold of `τ/min{2^i, k}`. For `i` such that `2^i >= k`, this threshold becomes constant at `τ/k`, not exponentially decreasing. This is a subtle but significant detail that affects the algorithm's behavior and the subsequent analysis.

3.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice for Parameter `w`:** Theorem 4.1 requires `w >= ceil(4dlog_k e * m / k)`. In the experiments (e.g., Fig 2(b,d)), `m` can be `2k`, which would require `w` to be on the order of `O(log k)`. For `k=500`, this would imply `w` should be roughly `ceil(8 * log_k e) = ceil(8 * 1/ln(500))`, which is not right. Let's re-examine `dlog_k e`. This notation is non-standard and unclear. Assuming it's a typo for `dlog_2 k e`, which is also unclear, let's assume `dlog ke` means `log k`. The requirement would be `w >= ceil(4m log k / k)`. For `m=2k`, this is `w >= 8 log k`. However, the experiments state that `w=1` "works well in practice". There is no discussion of this significant gap. Does `w=1` meet the theoretical condition in the experimental settings? If not, the practical performance may not be supported by the provided theory, a point which the authors must address.

4.  **Clarity of Theoretical Analysis:** The proof sketch for Theorem 4.1 is difficult to follow. The step leading to Eq. (5) by "balancing out the two inequalities" from Lemma 4.2 and Eq. (4) is hand-wavy and omits the actual derivation, which is central to obtaining the final approximation constant. While details may be in the appendix, the main paper should provide a clearer path. Furthermore, the non-standard notation `dlog ke` appears multiple times and is never defined. This appears to be a typo for `ceil(log_2 k)`, but such sloppiness impedes readability and assessment of the proofs.

5.  **Minor Formatting and Presentation Issues:**
    *   The footer on the first page reads "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems", which is improperly formatted.
    *   The reference list is not uniform. For example, reference [7] includes volume and page numbers, while [8] does not. Reference [22] ends with an extraneous "10,".
    *   The constant `c = 0.149 (1 - 1/dlog ke)` is presented as approaching 0.149 as `k -> infinity`. Assuming `dlog ke` is `log k`, the term `1/log k` indeed goes to zero. However, the initial constant of 0.149 is quite small, which could be noted as a limitation of the theoretical result compared to, for instance, the 0.5-ε of non-robust streaming algorithms.

### **Recommendation**

This paper presents a novel and interesting approach to a significant problem, with strong empirical results. The core ideas are valuable. However, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, including inconsistencies in key theoretical claims (memory complexity), mismatches between algorithm descriptions and pseudocode, a notable gap between theoretical parameter settings and practical implementation, and a general lack of precision in notation and formatting. While any single one of these might be excusable, their cumulative presence undermines my confidence in the work's carefulness and rigor. Given my role as a perfectionist reviewer, these issues overshadow the broader contributions. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.

**Decision: Weak Reject**

The paper has a strong foundation, but it requires a major revision to address the numerous inconsistencies and clarity issues before it can meet the high standards of NIPS. If the authors can thoroughly correct these points, a future submission would be significantly more compelling.
