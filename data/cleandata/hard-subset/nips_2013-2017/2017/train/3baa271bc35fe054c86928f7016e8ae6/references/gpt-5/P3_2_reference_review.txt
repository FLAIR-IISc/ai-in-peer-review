Summary
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in the single-pass streaming setting. The goal is to precompute a compact summary S such that, for any subsequent deletion of up to m elements (adversarially chosen), running a query-stage algorithm on S \ E returns a near-optimal size-k solution for V \ E without revisiting the full stream. The proposed approach, STAR-T, organizes the retained elements into partitions with exponentially increasing bucket capacities and exponentially decreasing marginal-gain thresholds. Each incoming element is placed into the first non-full bucket in which it exceeds the partition’s threshold; otherwise it is discarded. At query time, a standard GREEDY run over S \ E returns the final solution. The authors prove a constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149 (as k grows), with space O((k + m log k) log k) when an estimate of OPT is available, and O((k + m log k) log^2 k) using the standard parallel-threshold trick to guess OPT. Empirically, STAR-T is competitive with SIEVE-STREAMING (even when the latter is told deletions in advance) on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks.

Relation to prior work and novelty
- Builds on SIEVE-STREAMING-style thresholding and on the non-streaming robust structure PRO (non-uniform exponentially sized partitions) to achieve robustness in streaming.
- Compared to Mirzasoleiman et al. (ICML’17) on deletion-robust streaming submodular maximization, STAR-T trades a weaker approximation constant (~0.149 vs 1/2 − ε) for significantly improved space: O(m log^2 k) robustness overhead rather than O(mk log k/ε), i.e., only logarithmic dependence on k in the robustness term.
- Extends the “parallel guesses for OPT” idea to the robust setting with a precise complexity blow-up.

Technical quality
Strengths:
- Clear, single-pass streaming algorithm with a plausible and elegant partitioned-threshold design.
- Nontrivial analysis: the two-case argument (many full buckets vs. none) with balancing lemmas is sound in spirit. The robustness derives from spreading the “risk” of deletions across many buckets (via w) and from enforcing quality via thresholds that scale with partition size.
- Space bound derivation is transparent for the known-OPT case; the additional log factor for unknown OPT is standard.
- Query-stage guarantee compares against OPT(k, V \ E), which is the right benchmark.
- Experiments, while limited, use real datasets and reasonable tasks; the comparison to a strong baseline advantaged by knowing E is a plus.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Approximation constant: 0.149 is modest. The paper should better justify this tradeoff, e.g., by clarifying whether this is an artifact of proof looseness (use of 1 − e−1/3, union bounds), or inherent to the approach. Could tighter analysis (e.g., refined charging or adaptive threshold schedules) improve the constant?
- Threshold inconsistency: The text says the partition threshold is τ/2^i, but Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. The analysis (e.g., Lemma 4.4’s τ/k) suggests the latter is intended. Please reconcile throughout and explain why min{2^i, k} is required for correctness and constants.
- τ expression in Theorem 4.1 appears malformed: “τ = 1 + (1−e−1)(1−e−1/3)(1 − 1/log k) f(OPT(…))”. Presumably τ = f(OPT)/[1 + (1−e−1)(1−e−1/3)(1 − 1/log k)]. Please fix.
- Space bounds are stated inconsistently: early sections and the conclusion mention O((k + m log k) log^2 k) while Theorem 4.1 gives O((k + m log k) log k). It seems the base algorithm is O((k + m log k) log k), and the unknown-OPT variant (Theorem 4.5) incurs an extra ×log_{1+ε} k factor (hence log^2 k). Please harmonize and state both cases explicitly in the abstract and conclusions.
- Dependence on m: The algorithm requires w ≥ ceil(4 m log k / k), i.e., prior knowledge of m to set memory. How should practitioners proceed if m is unknown at stream time or varies per query? Is a multi-w scheme feasible, and what is the resulting space blow-up?
- Empirical evaluation:
  - No implementation/benchmark of the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18]. Given the core comparison in theory is approximation vs. memory, empirical curves (objective vs. memory) would help substantiate the tradeoff.
  - Lack of runtime/throughput measurements and sensitivity to w. The paper uses w = 1 in practice (much smaller than theory when m is large), which is encouraging but warrants a sensitivity study to the m/w/k relationship.
  - In the dominating-set experiments, E is sampled as a subset of S. While adversarial to STAR-T (since removing outside S has no effect), it is not a standard model of deletions in V. Please also report results where E ⊆ V is chosen without conditioning on S.
- Minor: Some proofs are deferred; the main text would benefit from more intuition behind Lemma 4.3/4.4 (e.g., a short sketch of the “balancing” step that yields the final constant).

Significance and impact
- Addresses a practically relevant model: precompute once, answer many deletion-queries without rescanning the stream. This is increasingly important (“right to be forgotten”, privacy deletions, robustness).
- The memory-approximation tradeoff offers a distinct point in the design space. For large k and moderate m, the improved memory scaling may be decisive.
- The structure (partitioned thresholds plus width w) is conceptually simple and likely to be reused.

Clarity and presentation
- Generally well written and organized. However, the threshold definition, τ formula, and space-bound statements need correction and consistent notation.
- Please explicitly state the final space for both known-OPT and unknown-OPT variants in the theorem statements or a corollary.
- Add a brief “algorithmic intuition” subsection to guide readers through the two-case analysis.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, simple algorithmic structure with a nontrivial robust streaming guarantee.
- Clear memory advantage over concurrent robust streaming work, with single-pass operation.
- Empirical results on real datasets; competitive to strong baselines even under unfavorable evaluation.

Cons for acceptance
- Weak approximation constant and lack of discussion on tightness/improvability.
- Some inconsistencies/typos in formal statements; threshold rule ambiguity.
- Limited empirical comparison set; no direct comparison to [18]; no runtime/memory-throughput analysis or sensitivity to w/m.

Questions for authors
1) Can the constant be improved with a refined analysis or alternative query algorithm (e.g., lazy/thresholded greedy) on S \ E?
2) How to handle unknown or varying m at stream time? Any adaptive scheme for w?
3) Could you report objective vs. memory tradeoffs (including [18]) and runtime?
4) Please clarify and fix the threshold definition and τ expression.

Overall assessment
A solid and thoughtful contribution to robust submodular streaming with a distinct memory-approximation tradeoff, a simple algorithmic design, and reasonable empirical support. With the noted fixes and expanded experiments, this would make a valuable addition. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the clarity issues and strengthening the empirical comparisons. Confidence: high.
