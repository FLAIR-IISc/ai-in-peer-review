

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper introduces a novel two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The core contribution is a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary of size O((k + m log k) log² k) in one pass, followed by a query stage algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that provides a constant-factor approximation after any removal of up to m elements from the summary.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature: while robust submodular maximization has been studied in non-streaming settings (e.g., [9, 10, 11]), and streaming submodular maximization has been studied (e.g., [8]), the combination of both robustness and streaming constraints has not been adequately addressed. The authors' key insight is a partitioned thresholding structure where the summary is organized into partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds. This design enables the algorithm to maintain robustness against arbitrary removals of up to m elements while operating in a single pass.

The theoretical contribution is strong, with Theorem 4.1 establishing a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for the robust setting. The analysis is novel in its combination of the partitioned structure with the exponentially decreasing thresholding rule, which is a significant departure from previous approaches that filled buckets greedily one after another. The paper also handles the practical limitation of not knowing f(OPT(k, V\E)) by providing a parallel variant (Theorem 4.5) that achieves a (1+ε)-approximation at the cost of a logarithmic factor in space.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates the practical value of the approach across two applications: dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation. The results are compelling - STAR-T-GREEDY matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed), and the method successfully handles multiple different removal scenarios using a single precomputed summary. The experiments are well-designed, and the results clearly support the theoretical claims.

This paper stands out for its novel approach to a challenging problem. The partitioned thresholding structure is elegant and represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work. While the approximation factor (0.149) is lower than the standard greedy's (1-1/e), this is expected for a robust setting, and the paper clearly demonstrates that the trade-off is justified by the robustness properties.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a thorough comparison to related work that highlights the novelty of the approach. The authors acknowledge limitations and provide solutions (e.g., for the τ parameter), demonstrating careful consideration of the problem's challenges.

In terms of significance, this work addresses an important practical challenge in large-scale data processing where robustness against data removal is critical (e.g., in social networks or recommendation systems). The two-stage approach enables efficient processing of massive datasets where reoptimization after removals is infeasible. The paper advances the state of the art by providing the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization with theoretical guarantees.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper's contribution highly valuable. It doesn't merely incrementally improve upon existing methods but introduces a fundamentally new algorithmic approach to a challenging problem. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical implications are significant.

The minor weaknesses (the need to approximate τ, the lower approximation factor than non-robust approaches) are well-justified by the problem's inherent difficulty and are addressed in the paper. These do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution.

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of submodular maximization, particularly for streaming applications requiring robustness. The approach is conceptually bold, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. It represents a meaningful advance beyond previous work and provides a new direction for future research in robust streaming algorithms.

I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to NIPS. The paper exemplifies the kind of novel, impactful work that NIPS aims to publish.
