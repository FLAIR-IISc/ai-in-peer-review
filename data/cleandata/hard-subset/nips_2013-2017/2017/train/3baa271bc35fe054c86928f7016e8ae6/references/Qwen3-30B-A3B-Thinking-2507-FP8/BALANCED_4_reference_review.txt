

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper proposes a two-stage algorithm for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. The authors address the challenge of creating a representative subset that remains effective even after the removal of up to m elements, which is particularly relevant for large-scale applications where re-optimization is infeasible. The first stage (STAR-T) constructs a robust summary set S using a partitioned thresholding approach in a single pass, while the second stage (STAR-T-GREEDY) applies a simple greedy algorithm on the remaining elements after m removals. The authors prove a constant-factor approximation guarantee with O((k + m log k) log²k) space complexity, extending previous work on streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]) and robust submodular maximization [9, 10, 11].

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by providing the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization with a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds is novel and elegantly designed to handle robustness requirements. The analysis is thorough, with multiple lemmas supporting the main result (Theorem 4.1), and the authors properly address the limitation of requiring knowledge of f(OPT(k, V \ E)) through Theorem 4.5.

2. **Practical Validation:** The experimental section demonstrates the practical relevance of the approach through two compelling applications: dominating set on social networks and personalized movie recommendations. The results show that the proposed method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which has knowledge of removed elements), suggesting strong practical utility.

3. **Clear Differentiation from Prior Work:** The paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work, particularly [11] and [18], by providing a more memory-efficient solution (logarithmic in k vs. linear). The comparison with [18] is particularly strong, as it highlights the memory advantage of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practical Implementation Consideration:** While Theorem 4.5 addresses the need for an approximation of f(OPT(k, V \ E)), the space complexity increase by a factor of log1+ k could be significant for very large datasets. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of how this affects real-world deployment, especially for applications where memory constraints are tight.

2. **Experimental Depth:** The experimental validation is somewhat limited. The paper compares against SIEVE-STREAMING (which is not designed for robustness) and a random baseline, but would benefit from comparison against other robust algorithms (if available) or analysis of how performance degrades with varying m beyond the theoretical bounds. The paper also doesn't explore how the choice of w affects performance in practice.

3. **Computational Complexity:** The paper focuses on space complexity but doesn't provide detailed analysis of the time complexity of the algorithms. For a streaming algorithm, the running time per element is an important practical consideration that should be addressed.

4. **Theoretical Bound Tightness:** The approximation guarantee of 0.149(1 - 1/log k) approaches 0.149 as k increases, but the paper doesn't discuss whether this bound is tight or if it can be improved. A discussion of the limitations of the current bound would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution with clear practical relevance. The two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental validation provides compelling evidence of the approach's practical value.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the following points in their revision:

1. Expand the discussion of Theorem 4.5, particularly the practical implications of the increased space complexity (log1+ k factor) for large-scale applications.

2. Provide more comprehensive experimental results, including performance across a wider range of m values and comparison against alternative robust algorithms if available.

3. Add a brief analysis of the time complexity of the algorithms, as this is crucial for streaming applications.

4. Discuss the tightness of the approximation bound (0.149) and whether it can be improved, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of streaming submodular maximization, addressing an important practical challenge (robustness) with a theoretically sound and practically validated approach. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent fit for NeurIPS.
