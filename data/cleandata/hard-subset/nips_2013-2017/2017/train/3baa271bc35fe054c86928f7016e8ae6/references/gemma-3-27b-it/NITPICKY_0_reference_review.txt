This paper proposes a two-stage streaming algorithm, STAR-T, for robust submodular maximization. The core idea is to partition the data stream into buckets with exponentially increasing sizes and associated decreasing thresholds. The algorithm aims to construct a robust summary resistant to the removal of *m* elements, followed by a greedy step on the remaining set to achieve a constant-factor approximation. While the problem addressed is relevant and the proposed approach has potential, the paper suffers from numerous minor flaws – particularly in presentation, analytical rigor, and experimental validation – that collectively weaken its impact and raise concerns about its overall quality.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:** The paper correctly identifies the need for robust streaming submodular maximization, building upon existing work in both streaming algorithms (SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular maximization ([9, 10, 11]). The partitioning and thresholding strategy seem inspired by [11], but adapted for the streaming setting. The proposed two-stage approach is well-motivated.

**Strengths:**
*   **Relevant Problem:** Robustness in data summarization is a critical consideration, particularly in dynamic environments.
*   **Novel Combination:** Adapting the partitioned thresholding idea to a streaming setting is a potentially interesting direction.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical bounds on the approximation ratio, although, as discussed below, these bounds require careful scrutiny.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper suffers from numerous instances of imprecise language and lacks sufficient detail in several places. For example, the description of Algorithm 1 is somewhat vague. What happens when an element *doesn't* fit into any bucket? Is it simply dropped? The explanation of 'w' is also unclear, initially presented as a memory parameter but then used in asymptotic analyses. Figure 1, while illustrative, is of limited value without explicit labelling of partitions and buckets. The notation is not always consistent (e.g., using both *E* for removed elements and sometimes for errors).
*   **Analytical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis relies heavily on several lemmas (4.2, 4.3, 4.4). Lemma 4.3 and 4.4 derive bounds based on the value of f(Bdlog ke) and do not seem to be logically linked or explained clearly. The constant factor approximation (0.149) is uncomfortably small. The argument regarding the applicability of the theorem as *k* approaches infinity is weak; a small constant *c* is not a compelling result. The derivation of the size bound (O((k + m log k) log k)) contains a potential error in step (3) and requires careful re-examination. Furthermore, the claim that the algorithm’s dependence on k is logarithmic while [18] has linear dependence is misleading since the paper does not state the dependency of [18]'s algorithm in a comparable way.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental setup is somewhat limited. While the comparisons with SIEVE-STREAMING and RANDOM are helpful, the choice of datasets (dominating set and movie recommendation) feels rather narrow. The discussion of the parameter *w* is frustrating. The authors state it works well in practice at *w*=1, contradicting the asymptotic theory requiring *w* ≥ ⌈4dlog kem / k⌉. This discrepancy deserves a thorough explanation. The evaluation lacks sensitivity analysis. How does performance change with different values of *m* and *k*? The interactive component described in section 5.2 is intriguing but the empirical results are not as clear cut as they ought to be. Reporting only average objective values without any standard deviation or confidence intervals hinders interpretation.
* **Related Work:** The citations in the Related Work section are incomplete. Several relevant papers on robust submodular maximization and streaming algorithms are missing. The discussion of [18] is also brief and dismissive, without a proper comparison of the algorithmic trade-offs.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*   Presents a novel algorithmic approach with theoretical guarantees.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance against existing methods in limited experiments.

**Con:**
*   Presentation lacks clarity and detail.
*   Theoretical analysis is not entirely convincing and yields a weak approximation guarantee.
*   Experimental validation is limited and lacks thorough analysis.
*   Numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite addressing an important problem and presenting a potentially interesting algorithmic approach, this paper is ultimately not ready for acceptance at NIPS. The numerous minor flaws in presentation, analysis, and experimentation raise significant concerns about its overall quality. The weak constant factor in the approximation guarantee and the discrepancies between the theoretical and empirical results further diminish its impact. The paper requires substantial revisions to address these issues before it can be considered for publication. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of their presentation, strengthening their theoretical analysis, and conducting more comprehensive experiments to validate their claims. Addressing the points raised above would significantly enhance the value of this work.
