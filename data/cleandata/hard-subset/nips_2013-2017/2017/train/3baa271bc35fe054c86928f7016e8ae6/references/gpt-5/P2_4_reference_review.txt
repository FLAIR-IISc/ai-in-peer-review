Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies robust submodular maximization in the streaming model under a cardinality constraint. The goal is to build, in one pass and sublinear memory, a summary S that remains useful even if up to m elements are removed; at query time, after an arbitrary removal set E (|E| ≤ m), one should output a size-k set Z ⊆ S \ E with constant-factor approximation to OPT(k, V \ E).

The authors propose STAR-T, which organizes the summary into partitions i = 0..⌊log k⌋, each with exponentially many buckets whose capacities grow as min{2^i, k}. Incoming elements are inserted into the first non-full bucket where the marginal gain exceeds a partition-dependent threshold τ/2^i (Algorithm 1 shows τ/min{2^i, k}, consistent for i ≤ log k). At query time, STAR-T-GREEDY runs standard GREEDY on S \ E to produce Z.

The main theorem shows: with w ≥ ⌈(4 m log k)/k⌉ buckets per “copy,” STAR-T stores |S| = O(k log k + m log^2 k) elements and, for any removal E, STAR-T-GREEDY achieves f(Z) ≥ c f(OPT(k, V \ E)) with c ≈ 0.149(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉). Since τ depends on f(OPT), the authors provide a standard parallel-guessing variant (as in SIEVE-STREAMING) that multiplies space by O(log_{1+ε} k) with a (1+ε)-factor loss.

This builds on: (i) SIEVE-STREAMING (greedy thresholding for non-robust streaming), (ii) robust non-streaming constructions using partitioned buckets (e.g., PRO), and (iii) concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [18], which attains 1/2 − ε but with O(mk log k/ε) memory. The novelty here is a streaming algorithm with partitioned thresholds yielding memory logarithmic in k (vs linear in [18]) at the cost of a worse approximation constant.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Theory: The analysis is careful, with two complementary cases: (1) some partition has ≥ half full buckets, yielding a direct bound via GREEDY’s (1 − 1/e) property and limited damage from deletions; (2) no partition has many full buckets, leveraging the last partition’s not-full bucket to sandwich f(Z) via Lemmas 4.3 and 4.4 and a balancing argument. The constants and dependencies are explicitly derived; the use of w to control deletion-robustness is clear.
- Assumptions and practicality: The need for τ ≈ f(OPT) is addressed via parallel guesses; the resulting extra logarithmic factor is standard. The robustness guarantee holds for any E, and memory scales as O(k log k + m log^2 k) (or with an extra log factor when guessing τ).
- Minor issues to fix:
  - Space bound consistency: Theorem 4.1 and Eq. (3) imply |S| = O(k log k + m log^2 k). Elsewhere (intro/conclusion) the paper states O((k + m log k) log^2 k), which is looser; please harmonize.
  - Clarify how τ was set in experiments (single τ vs parallel guesses, value of ε, number of instances).
  - Typographical glitches (e.g., “st Conference on NIPS”).
Overall, the technical contribution appears sound, with a coherent proof strategy.

Clarity
- The algorithmic ideas (partitioned buckets with decreasing thresholds) are well-motivated and intuitive. Pseudocode is provided.
- The proof sketch is readable; the roles of the key lemmas are clear. Constants and parameter choices are spelled out.
- Missing experimental details (τ selection, per-dataset memory |S|, runtime) reduce reproducibility; adding these would improve clarity. Unify the space bounds across sections.

Originality
- Streaming-robust formulation with a partitioned thresholding structure is novel. The approach meaningfully combines ideas from PRO (non-streaming, partitioned buckets) and SIEVE-STREAMING (thresholding) and adapts them to robustness under deletions in a one-pass model.
- The memory vs. approximation trade-off compared to concurrent [18] is a distinct and valuable angle: logarithmic dependence on k (vs linear) with a worse constant factor.
- Related work is adequately covered; the differences to [11], [8], and [18] are discussed.

Significance
- Robust, one-pass submodular maximization is practically important for large-scale and “right to be forgotten” scenarios. The ability to precompute a summary once and answer many deletion queries is impactful.
- The theoretical guarantee advances the state of the art in the memory regime where k is large, and m is nontrivial; practitioners may accept a smaller constant for sizable space savings.
- Experiments on influence-like dominating set and recommendation illustrate the interactive/query-time use. However:
  - There is no empirical comparison to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming algorithm [18] (even approximate surrogate). Including it (or a discussion of infeasibility) would strengthen significance claims.
  - The empirical section does not explore the m–w–memory–accuracy trade-offs; w is set to 1 in practice even when m is large, which is interesting but would benefit from a systematic study (vary m and w, report |S|, quality).
  - Report actual memory (|S|) and runtime vs SIEVE-STREAMING to quantify practical benefits.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated algorithm with one-pass guarantee.
- Solid theory: sublinear memory O(k log k + m log^2 k) and constant-factor approximation independent of m and n.
- Conceptually elegant partitioned thresholding; analysis covers all cases.
- Practical demonstration on two applications; shows interactive robustness without reprocessing.

Weaknesses
- Approximation constant (~0.149) is modest; discussion of whether it can be improved (or a tighter analysis) would help.
- Inconsistent space bounds across sections; missing details on τ selection and resource usage in experiments.
- Empirical evaluation lacks comparisons to concurrent robust streaming methods and lacks a thorough ablation on w, m, and τ guessing.
- Some presentation rough edges (typos, minor inconsistencies).

Questions for authors
- How is τ chosen in practice? Do you run the parallel-guessing variant; if so, with what ε and how many instances? What is the observed |S| and runtime overhead?
- Can the constant factor be improved with a refined balancing or alternative thresholds?
- How sensitive is performance to w; can you provide plots of quality vs w and memory?
- Is there a reason you did not include [18] in experiments (e.g., unavailable code or prohibitive memory)?

Recommendation
- Leaning accept (weak accept). The paper offers a novel and theoretically solid streaming-robust algorithm with appealing memory guarantees and demonstrates practical usability. Addressing the clarity issues and strengthening experiments (or at least reporting memory/runtime and τ details) would make it a strong accept.

Confidence: High (familiar with submodular streaming and robust maximization literature).
