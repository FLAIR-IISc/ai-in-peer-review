

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary
This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure: (1) a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that creates a robust summary of the data in a single pass, and (2) a query stage (STAR-T-GREEDY) that, after any removal of m elements from the summary, produces a solution of size at most k. The key novelty lies in their partitioned thresholding approach, where the summary is arranged into partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds. The paper establishes theoretical guarantees showing the summary size is O((k + m log k) log² k) and the query stage achieves a constant-factor approximation (0.149(1-1/log k)).

The work builds upon and extends previous streaming submodular maximization algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING [8], while addressing the robustness requirement that has been studied in non-streaming settings [9, 10, 11]. The paper demonstrates the practical effectiveness of their approach through experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, showing competitive performance compared to SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows in advance which elements will be removed.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The main result (Theorem 4.1) is well-motivated and proven through careful case analysis, considering both scenarios where partitions have many full buckets versus those with mostly empty buckets. The proof sketch is clear and the full proofs in the supplementary material provide sufficient detail. The authors properly address the practical limitation of needing an approximation of f(OPT(k, V\E)) by extending the approach of [8] to maintain multiple parallel instances, providing a (1+ε)-approximation.

The experimental validation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare against relevant baselines (SIEVE-STREAMING, RANDOM, and STAR-T-SIEVE) across two different applications, showing that their approach matches or outperforms the baselines. The experiments are well-justified and the results align with the theoretical guarantees. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the small constant factor 0.149) and properly positions their work against related contributions.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is well-motivated with concrete examples (influence maximization, movie recommendation). The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1 and 2) are precise and accompanied by pseudocode. The theoretical results are presented in a logical sequence, with proof sketches that help guide the reader. The figures (particularly Figure 1 illustrating the partitioned structure) effectively complement the text.

The paper is self-contained and provides sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The writing is concise yet comprehensive, avoiding unnecessary jargon while maintaining technical precision. The related work section is thorough and properly contextualizes the contribution.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization by addressing the robustness requirement in the streaming setting. While robust submodular maximization has been studied in non-streaming settings [9, 10, 11], and streaming submodular maximization has been studied [5, 8, 12, 13], the combination of these two problems in the streaming setting is novel.

The key innovation is the partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds, which differs from previous work that fills buckets greedily one after another [11]. The authors' analysis technique, which combines the properties of the robust structure with the decreasing thresholding rule, is also novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from concurrent work [18] by showing a more favorable memory dependence (logarithmic in k versus linear).

## Significance
The problem addressed is highly significant for large-scale machine learning applications where data is too large to fit in memory, and robustness to element removal is required. The practical implications span multiple domains including image/document summarization, influence maximization, facility location, and recommender systems. The theoretical guarantees (constant-factor approximation) and practical results (matching or outperforming SIEVE-STREAMING even when it has additional information) demonstrate clear advancement over the state of the art.

The paper makes a concrete contribution to the streaming submodular maximization literature, which is a prominent topic at NIPS. The two-stage approach enables interactive applications (as noted in Section 5.2) where a single precomputed summary can be used for multiple query scenarios, which is particularly valuable in real-world applications where data is produced rapidly.

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong NIPS submission that makes a well-motivated, technically sound, and significant contribution to the field. The paper excels in quality and clarity, with a clear demonstration of novelty and significance. While the constant factor (0.149) is somewhat smaller than the (1-1/e) approximation for standard submodular maximization, the robustness requirement inherently makes this a more challenging problem.

The paper's main limitation is the small constant factor, but this is inherent to the robustness constraint and the authors properly acknowledge this. The practical results, where the algorithm matches or outperforms baselines even when they have additional information, strongly support the theoretical claims.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization with well-supported theoretical results and compelling experimental validation. It addresses an important practical problem with a novel and effective approach that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The work is well-written, well-organized, and properly contextualized within the existing literature.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria at a high level, with particular strength in quality and significance. It represents a solid contribution to the field that will be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning.
