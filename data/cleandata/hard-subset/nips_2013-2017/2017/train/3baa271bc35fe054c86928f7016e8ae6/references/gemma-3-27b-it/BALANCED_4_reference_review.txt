This paper tackles the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure, STAR-T, utilizing partitioned thresholding to achieve a constant-factor approximation guarantee when elements are removed from the initially selected set. The problem is well-motivated, addressing a critical need for robustness in large-scale data summarization applications.

The paper builds upon prior work in submodular maximization (Nemhauser et al., 1978), streaming algorithms (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014), and robust submodular optimization (Krause et al., 2008; Orlin et al., 2016; Bogunovic et al., 2017). It correctly positions itself within the existing literature, clearly differentiating its contribution from previous approaches like SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014) and PRO (Bogunovic et al., 2017). The introduction provides a compelling justification for the need for both streaming and robustness, highlighting practical applications like dominating set problems and personalized recommendation systems. The two-stage setting is clearly defined.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Relevance:** The problem of robust streaming submodular maximization is highly relevant to modern large-scale machine learning applications.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm appears technically sound, with a well-defined procedure and a theoretical guarantee. The partitioning structure and decreasing thresholding rule seem like a sensible approach to balancing summarization and robustness.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing a constant-factor approximation guarantee under specific conditions. The proof sketch outlined in Section 4 provides a good overview of the key ideas.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on both the dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation demonstrate the effectiveness of STAR-T, achieving comparable or better performance than SIEVE-STREAMING, even with the advantage given to the latter.  The demonstration of performance on multiple removal scenarios highlights the practical benefits of robustness.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm descriptions are clear, and the motivation for each step is well explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm requires careful tuning of parameters like 'w' and 'τ'. While the paper provides some guidance on setting 'w', the practical implications of choosing different values for these parameters are not fully explored.
* **Constant Factor:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149) is quite small. Although theoretically sound, it raises questions about the practical effectiveness of the algorithm compared to simpler heuristics, especially when dealing with large datasets where constant factors can become significant.  The analysis shows it approaches 0.149 as k -> infinity, but that is an asymptotic result.
* **Space Complexity:** While the space complexity O((k + m log k) log k) is reasonable, it’s worth discussing the practical implications for very large values of k and m. A more detailed analysis of the memory footprint in different scenarios would be beneficial.
* **Assumption on Knowing f(OPT(k, V\E)) initially:** Though addressed in Theorem 4.5, the initial presentation of the algorithm assumes knowledge of f(OPT(k, V\E)), which is unrealistic. The extension to approximating this value adds complexity, and it could be more elegantly integrated into the main presentation.
* **Limited Comparison to PRO:** While SIEVE-STREAMING is a strong baseline, a comparison with PRO (Bogunovic et al., 2017) would be valuable, as it’s another relevant algorithm for robust submodular maximization. The authors briefly mention PRO in the related work, but a direct empirical comparison would strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and practically relevant problem.
* Presents a novel and technically sound algorithm with a theoretical guarantee.
* Provides compelling experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
* Parameter tuning might be challenging in practice.
* Comparison to other state-of-the-art algorithms (particularly PRO) is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The problem is well-motivated, the proposed algorithm is technically sound, and the experimental results are encouraging.  While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is a concern, the overall quality of the paper and the practical relevance of the problem outweigh this limitation.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Provide more guidance on parameter tuning, including sensitivity analysis of 'w' and 'τ'.
2.  Discuss the practical implications of the space complexity for very large datasets.
3.  Include a comparison with the PRO algorithm in the experimental evaluation.
4.  Integrate the discussion of approximating f(OPT(k, V\E)) more seamlessly into the main body of the paper, rather than presenting it as a separate extension.
