Summary
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting under a cardinality constraint. The goal is to precompute, in one pass and sublinear space, a robust summary S such that after the removal of any m elements, a high-quality size-k solution can be extracted using only S (not the full stream). The authors propose a two-stage approach: (i) a streaming algorithm, STAR-T, that organizes stored elements into exponentially sized partitions of buckets and uses exponentially decreasing marginal-gain thresholds to accept elements; (ii) a query stage that runs GREEDY on S\E after any deletions E of size ≤ m. The main theoretical result shows that with space O(k log k + m log^2 k) when the value f(OPT) is known—and O((k + m log k) log^2 k) using a standard multi-guess trick for unknown f(OPT)—the procedure guarantees a constant-factor approximation: f(Z) ≥ c f(OPT(k, V\E)) with c → 0.149 as k grows. Experiments on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation demonstrate performance comparable to SIEVE-STREAMING (which is given the deleted set E in advance) and close to GREEDY in the small-scale recommendation setting.

Relation to prior work
- Non-robust streaming submodular maximization: SIEVE-STREAMING [Badanidiyuru et al. ’14] achieves 0.5−ε with O(k log k) memory.
- Robust (non-streaming) submodular maximization: Orlin et al. ’16 and PRO [Bogunovic et al. ’17] achieve constant factors (≈0.387) with bucketed/partitioned constructions, but require multiple passes or full data access.
- Concurrent/independent robust streaming: Mirzasoleiman et al. ’17 (“Deletion-robust submodular maximization”) obtain a 1/2−ε guarantee with O(mk log k/ε) memory. The present work trades a smaller approximation constant (~0.149) for substantially better space scaling in k (logarithmic rather than linear).

Strengths
- Novelty: A principled combination of partitioned summaries (as in PRO) with exponentially decreasing thresholds (as in SIEVE-STREAMING) tailored for robustness. The analysis that balances cases (many full buckets vs. none) is nontrivial and well-motivated.
- Theory: One-pass algorithm with a constant-factor robust guarantee; space O(k log k + m log^2 k) when f(OPT) is known, and O((k + m log k) log^2 k) via parallel guesses. The dependence on k in the “robust” memory term is only logarithmic, contrasting with O(mk) in the concurrent work.
- Practicality: Simple streaming rule; the query stage uses GREEDY on S\E, which is implementable and avoids rescanning the stream.
- Empirics: On two real graphs and MovieLens, STAR-T + GREEDY matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (even when the latter knows E), and is close to full GREEDY in the small-scale setting. The “interactive” recommender use case is compelling and aligns with the robust objective.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Approximation factor: 0.149 is modest. While this is understandable given robustness in streaming, a brief discussion of whether this constant is tight for the given architecture (or how it might be improved) would be valuable.
- Space/time complexity exposition: There is a minor inconsistency between the introduction/conclusion O((k + m log k) log^2 k) and Theorem 4.1’s O(k log k + m log^2 k). It seems the extra log factor arises from the multi-guess variant (Theorem 4.5). Please make this explicit where space bounds are first stated to avoid confusion.
- Threshold definition: Early text says thresholds are τ/2^i, whereas Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. The latter is important for the last partition; please reconcile the descriptions and emphasize why the min is needed for correctness.
- τ specification: The expression for τ in Theorem 4.1 is hard to parse in the current typography; it appears to be f(OPT) divided by a constant depending on e^{-1}, e^{-1/3}, and 1 − 1/log k. Please retypeset clearly. Also, in experiments, how is τ (or the set of guesses) chosen, and what ε (or grid) is used?
- Runtime: The per-element processing cost scans buckets across partitions; worst-case work per element is Θ(wk) (sum over i of w⌈k/2^i⌉), which could be large when k is large (though often w is small). A discussion of practical runtime and scalability (e.g., throughput vs. k, sensitivity to w) would help practitioners.
- Empirical baselines: The concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18] is cited but not empirically compared. Even a small-scale comparison (or a clear argument why it was impractical to implement due to memory) would strengthen the empirical section.
- Experimental details: More specifics are needed for reproducibility: exact values of w, ε, number of τ guesses, stopping criteria, and wall-clock/memory usage. Figures referred to (2a–2f) should be clearly labeled and explained; currently we cannot verify them from the text.

Quality
- The theoretical development appears sound, with clear case analyses (full-bucket vs. not) leading to Lemmas 4.2–4.4 and the final bound. Some proofs are sketched, with full details in the supplement; from what is presented, the logic is coherent. Minor presentation issues (τ formatting, threshold/min clarification, space-bound consistency) should be fixed.
- The experiments support claims of practicality and robustness, but would benefit from runtime/memory reporting and an additional baseline [18].

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The STAR-T structure and intuition are easy to follow. Please fix minor typos and the notational/formatting issues noted above, and add more implementation details.

Originality
- Combines known ideas (partitioning and thresholding) in a novel way for the robust streaming setting, with a new analysis yielding favorable space scaling in k compared to concurrent work.

Significance
- Addresses a timely and practically important problem (robust summaries under deletions/right-to-be-forgotten). The space-approximation trade-off is attractive; likely to influence follow-up work on robust streaming and sketching for submodular optimization.

Pros
- One-pass robust streaming algorithm with provable constant-factor guarantee.
- Space nearly linear in k only up to a log, and only log^2 dependence in m term.
- Clean, simple algorithm; practical query stage.
- Solid empirical results on real data; interactive recommender use case.

Cons
- Constant factor is relatively low.
- Some presentation inconsistencies and missing experimental details.
- No empirical comparison to concurrent robust streaming work [18].
- Potentially high per-element processing cost for large k if w > 1.

Questions for authors
- Please clarify τ’s exact formula in Theorem 4.1 and how τ is chosen in experiments (number of parallel guesses, ε).
- What is the per-element processing time in practice for your datasets? Any heuristics to prune bucket scans?
- Can you report memory and runtime vs. k and m, and ablate w?
- Is there a straightforward way to adapt STAR-T to sliding-window or turnstile streaming models?

Recommendation
Accept (borderline to clear). The paper offers a principled and practically relevant contribution to robust streaming submodular maximization, with novel analysis and improved space guarantees. Addressing clarity issues and adding experimental details would strengthen the final version.

Confidence
High for the theoretical assessment; moderate for empirical evaluation due to limited detail in the current draft.
