### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The goal is to make a single pass over a large dataset to select a summary set `S` such that, even after an adversary removes up to `m` elements from `S`, one can still find a `k`-sized solution within `S` that has a good objective value. The authors propose a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a novel streaming algorithm called STAR-T constructs the robust summary `S`. Its key idea is to organize elements into partitions, where partitions have exponentially decreasing thresholds and contain buckets of exponentially increasing size. In the second (query) stage, after `m` elements are removed, a simple greedy algorithm is run on the remainder of `S` to produce the final solution. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this procedure yields a constant-factor approximation guarantee, while the summary set `S` has a size of `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The work is supported by strong experimental results on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, where it performs comparably to or better than a strong baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING) that is given oracle knowledge of the elements to be removed.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Formulation and Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem. Combining the challenges of streaming data (single pass, sublinear memory) with the need for robustness is a natural and important extension of submodular maximization research. The two-stage setup, where a summary is pre-computed and then queried multiple times under different removal scenarios, maps well to real-world interactive applications like the movie recommendation example provided.

2.  **Algorithmic Originality:** The proposed STAR-T algorithm is a clever and non-trivial extension of the thresholding idea from SIEVE-STREAMING [8] to the robust setting. The core novelty lies in the partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds. This structure is inspired by recent non-streaming robust work [11], but its adaptation to the streaming context via a thresholding mechanism is new and well-executed. This design elegantly balances the need to capture high-value elements (low-index partitions with high thresholds) and maintain diversity and redundancy for robustness (high-index partitions with low thresholds).

3.  **Technical Quality and Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a solid theoretical contribution. Achieving a constant-factor approximation (0.149) for this challenging problem is a significant result. The analysis, sketched via Lemmas 4.2-4.4, is intricate, correctly identifying and balancing different cases (e.g., whether partitions get filled or not). The memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)` is also noteworthy. In particular, the logarithmic dependence on `k` is a significant improvement over the concurrent work of [18], which has a linear dependence on `k` (`O(mk log k)`), making the proposed method much more scalable for large `k`.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. Comparing against SIEVE-STREAMING, which knows the removed set `E` in advance, is a very strong baseline. The fact that STAR-T often matches or even outperforms this oracle-endowed baseline strongly validates the effectiveness of the robust summary it constructs. The use of both random and adversarial (greedy) removal strategies further strengthens the empirical claims. The movie recommendation experiment effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed interactive framework.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of the Analysis:** While the proof sketch presents the key lemmas, the high-level intuition connecting them could be improved. The argument proceeds by balancing two different lower bounds on `f(Z)` (from Lemma 4.2 and the combination of 4.3/4.4). A more intuitive explanation of why these specific cases cover all possibilities and why their trade-off leads to the final guarantee would make the technical contribution more accessible. The derivation feels somewhat like algebraic manipulation, and the reader is left wanting a clearer narrative for the proof strategy.

2.  **Value of the Approximation Constant:** The theoretical constant of `c ≈ 0.149` is quite low. While obtaining any constant factor is a strong theoretical achievement, this specific value may not be practically reassuring on its own. The authors rightly show in experiments that the empirical performance is much better, which highlights a significant gap between the worst-case theoretical bound and average-case performance. While common in this field, a brief discussion on the sources of looseness in the analysis could be beneficial.

3.  **Gap between Theoretical and Practical Parameters:** There is a large discrepancy between the theoretical requirement for the memory parameter `w` (`w ≥ ⌈4dlog kem / k⌉`) and the value used in experiments (`w = 1`). In practice, `w=1` is shown to be robust to removals far exceeding the theoretical limit (e.g., `|E| >> k`). This suggests the theoretical analysis is quite conservative. While this is a positive finding for the algorithm's practical performance, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of why this gap might exist.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   There appears to be a minor inconsistency in the description of the threshold. In the text (end of Section 3), it is stated as `τ/2^i`. However, in Algorithm 1 (line 5), the condition is `f(e|B_i,j) >= τ/min{2^i, k}`. The latter makes more sense as it normalizes by the maximum size of a bucket in the partition. This should be clarified.
*   The conclusion contains a small typo in the memory complexity, stating `O((k + m log k) log^2 k)`. The derivation in the proof of Theorem 4.1 correctly shows `O((k + m log k) log k)`, which should be used consistently.
*   The standard technique of running parallel instances to guess `f(OPT)` is used, which is acceptable. However, this adds a `log_{1+ε} k` factor to the memory. It would be helpful to explicitly state the final memory complexity of the full, practical algorithm in the main text, not just the appendix.

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality paper that makes significant contributions to the field of large-scale submodular optimization. It introduces a novel and well-motivated problem, proposes an elegant and original algorithm, provides strong theoretical guarantees that improve upon concurrent work, and validates the approach with compelling experiments. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to the clarity of the presentation of the analysis and the typical gaps between theory and practice in this area. The work is technically sound, significant, and likely to inspire follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
