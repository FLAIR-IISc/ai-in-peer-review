

# Review: Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach

This paper proposes a two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. The authors address a practical challenge in large-scale machine learning where data summarization must be robust against element removals. They introduce STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm that constructs a robust summary of size O((k + m log k) log²k), followed by STAR-T-GREEDY, a query stage that returns a solution of size at most k with a constant-factor approximation guarantee after any removal of up to m elements from the summary.

The work builds upon prior submodular maximization research, particularly extending the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8] to the robust setting. The authors note that while [11] and [18] have addressed robust submodular maximization, their approach differs significantly: STAR-T uses a partitioned thresholding structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds (τ/2ⁱ) rather than greedy bucket construction, achieving a logarithmic dependence on k in memory requirements (O((k + m log k) log²k)) versus the linear dependence (O(mk log k/ε)) of [18].

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with a detailed proof of the main theorem (Theorem 4.1), showing a constant-factor approximation guarantee of approximately 0.149. The analysis correctly handles two cases (when partitions have full buckets vs. when they don't) and provides appropriate lemmas (4.2-4.4) to support the main result. The experimental validation on two applications (dominating set and personalized movie recommendation) demonstrates that the approach matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows removals in advance) while requiring only a single precomputed summary for multiple removal scenarios. The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need for τ approximation (handled in Theorem 4.5) and the lower approximation factor compared to non-robust methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement precisely defines robustness requirements, and algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode (Algorithms 1-2). The theoretical analysis, while complex, is logically structured and well-motivated. The experimental section provides sufficient detail about datasets, baselines, and evaluation metrics. The connection between the theoretical analysis and practical performance is well-explained, particularly in the "Interactive Personalized Movie Recommendation" section.

**Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds represents a significant technical novelty. The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from prior work [11, 18], particularly in achieving a logarithmic dependence on k in memory requirements rather than linear. The two-stage framework (streaming then query) is novel for robust submodular maximization, as prior work [9, 10] typically focuses on a single-stage solution. The analysis techniques combining properties of the robust structure with decreasing thresholding are also new.

**Significance:** The problem of robust submodular maximization has significant practical importance in applications where data may be removed (e.g., network failures, user preferences). The two-stage approach enables efficient processing of large datasets where re-optimization after removals would be prohibitively expensive. The experimental results demonstrate clear practical utility in real-world applications, and the theoretical guarantees advance the state of the art in streaming submodular optimization.

**Strengths:**
- Novel partitioned thresholding approach for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Theoretical guarantees with constant-factor approximation
- Practical validation across two real-world applications
- Clear distinction from related work [11, 18] with improved memory complexity
- Demonstrated ability to handle multiple removal scenarios with a single precomputed summary

**Weaknesses:**
- The approximation factor (0.149) is lower than the (0.5-ε) factor for non-robust streaming submodular maximization, though this trade-off is expected
- The requirement for τ (a function of f(OPT(k, V\E))) necessitates the parallel algorithm (Theorem 4.5), increasing space complexity by a factor of log₁₊ᵋ k
- The theoretical analysis is complex, potentially limiting accessibility for some readers
- Limited comparison with other robust submodular maximization algorithms beyond SIEVE-STREAMING

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to robust streaming submodular maximization with a novel algorithmic approach and strong theoretical guarantees. The practical experiments validate the theoretical claims, and the two-stage framework provides a valuable solution to a practically important problem. The work clearly advances the state of the art in a well-defined direction.

*Con:* The approximation factor is substantially lower than non-robust methods, which may limit practical appeal. The space complexity increase (log₁₊ᵋ k factor) for handling unknown τ could be problematic for extremely large datasets.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization with a novel algorithmic approach that addresses an important practical challenge. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the work clearly advances the state of the art. The trade-offs are well-understood and appropriately discussed. The paper meets all NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, novelty, and significance.
