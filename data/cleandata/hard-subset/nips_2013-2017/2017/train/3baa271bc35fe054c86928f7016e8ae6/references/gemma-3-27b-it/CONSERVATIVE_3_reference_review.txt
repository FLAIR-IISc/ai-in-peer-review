This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The core idea – partitioning the data stream and using decreasing thresholds – is intuitively reasonable and aims to balance efficiency with robustness. However, despite the clear presentation and thorough experimental evaluation, I remain unconvinced of the novelty and significance of this work, and have substantial reservations about the theoretical guarantees.

The framing of the problem is well-motivated, and the two-stage setting reflects a practical need. However, the connection to existing work feels underdeveloped. While the authors cite related work on robust submodular maximization ([9, 10, 11]) and streaming algorithms ([5, 8, 12, 13]), the incremental contribution isn’t sufficiently highlighted. The connection to SIEVE-STREAMING [8] appears particularly strong, and the authors’ claim of matching or outperforming it, even when SIEVE-STREAMING *knows* the removals, feels somewhat overstated without a more rigorous comparison across a wider range of parameters (m, k, dataset sizes). The authors acknowledge this in the experimental section, noting that SIEVE-STREAMING outperforms them for some values of k, but downplay its significance.

My primary concern lies with the theoretical bounds. The conditions required to achieve the constant factor approximation seem quite restrictive, particularly the dependence on `dlog k` and the need for a large value of `w` (related to `m`). While the authors acknowledge that `w=1` works well in practice, this is concerning given that the theoretical analysis *relies* on a larger `w` to guarantee the approximation ratio. The fact that the approximation ratio *decreases* as `k` increases is also troubling. A constant factor approximation is only useful if it holds across a reasonable range of `k` values, and the presented bound diminishes as `k` grows. The analysis also appears complex and potentially relies on several simplifying assumptions that might not hold in real-world scenarios.

The paper's clarity is good; the algorithms are clearly presented, and the notation is consistent. However, the lengthy proofs, relegated to the supplementary material, make it difficult to fully assess the validity of the theoretical claims. A more concise and insightful overview of the proof strategy within the main paper would be beneficial. 

Regarding originality, the combination of partitioning and thresholding, while not inherently uninteresting, doesn't strike me as a major breakthrough. These are well-established techniques in streaming algorithms and submodular optimization, respectively. The novelty seems to reside in their specific combination within this robust streaming context, but the practical impact of this combination feels limited, particularly given the theoretical constraints.

The experimental results are a strength of the paper. The application to both the dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation demonstrates the versatility of the approach. However, the experimental setup isn’t entirely convincing. Comparing against a uniformly random selection (RANDOM) is a weak baseline. More compelling comparisons against other robust streaming algorithms (if they exist) or more sophisticated heuristics would strengthen the evaluation. The interactive movie recommendation setting is interesting, and the ability to reuse the precomputed summary for different removal scenarios is a practical advantage.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Clear presentation and well-defined problem statement.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation with real-world datasets.
*   Demonstrated practical applicability in relevant domains.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited theoretical novelty and restrictive approximation guarantees.
*   Dependence on a large parameter `w` for theoretical validity that doesn’t appear necessary in practice.
*   Decreasing approximation ratio with increasing `k`.
*   Incremental contribution compared to existing work on streaming and robust submodular maximization.
*   Insufficient comparison to strong baselines beyond SIEVE-STREAMING.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid piece of work, but falls short of the bar for acceptance at NIPS. While technically sound and well-presented, the limited theoretical novelty, restrictive guarantees, and incremental contribution do not justify publication at this highly competitive conference. I would recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to consider submitting a revised version to a more applied conference. A stronger focus on practical performance, more rigorous comparison to relevant baselines, and a more streamlined theoretical analysis would significantly improve the paper's impact.
