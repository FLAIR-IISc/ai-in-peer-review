Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting under a cardinality constraint. The goal is to precompute a compact summary S that is robust to any post-hoc deletion of up to m elements, and then, at query time, return a size-k set from S \ E with a constant-factor approximation to OPT(k, V \ E), without revisiting the stream. The proposed two-stage approach consists of: (1) STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm that arranges elements into partitions of exponentially growing bucket sizes with exponentially decreasing thresholds; (2) STAR-T-GREEDY, which runs GREEDY on S \ E at query time.

The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation guarantee c ≈ 0.149(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉) for the query solution, with space O((k + m log k) log k) elements (and O((k + m log k) log k · log1+ε k) with parallel guessing of τ when OPT is unknown). The analysis hinges on two complementary cases (many full buckets vs. many non-full buckets) and balances their bounds. Empirically, the method matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (even when the latter knows the deletions), on influence/dominating-set tasks and a personalized recommendation task.

This work bridges robust submodular maximization (e.g., Orlin et al. 2016; PRO [Bogunovic et al. 2017]) with submodular streaming (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [Badanidiyuru et al. 2014]) via novel partitioned thresholding. It compares to concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [Mirzasoleiman et al. 2017], offering better memory scaling in k at the cost of a weaker approximation constant.

Quality:
- Theory: The proof strategy is sound and clearly outlined: the partitioned buckets with thresholds allow bounding marginal gains and deletion impact, controlled by a memory parameter w that trades space for robustness. The two-case analysis (half full vs. not) and the balancing argument to obtain c ≈ 0.149 are convincing at a high level. The memory bound derivation (Eq. 3) is straightforward and ties to setting w = Θ((m log k)/k).
- Issues to fix/clarify:
  - Consistency of thresholds: The text states thresholds τ/2^i per partition, while Algorithm 1 line 5 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Please clarify and use a single, correct rule (likely τ/min{2^i,k} to cap threshold when 2^i > k).
  - Space bound inconsistencies: Different parts state O((k + m log k) log k) vs O((k + m log k) log^2 k). From Eq. (3) and w = Θ((m log k)/k), I infer |S| = O(k log k + m log^2 k). Please unify this throughout the paper (including abstract/conclusion).
  - τ specification in Theorem 4.1 appears garbled (missing ε and parentheses). Please restate the precise constant and dependence cleanly.
  - Per-element time: STAR-T loops over all partitions and up to w·(k/2^i) buckets, potentially O(w k log k) marginal evaluations per element in the worst case. Some discussion on expected/practical cost and potential implementation optimizations (e.g., early stopping, indexing) would strengthen the practicality claim.
- Experiments: The setups are sensible and demonstrate robustness in practice. It is nice that STAR-T matches baselines even when they know deletions. However:
  - The parameter w is set to 1 in all experiments, which may be below the theoretical prescription for some m, yet still works. It would be helpful to plot performance vs. w and m to illuminate the memory–robustness trade-off.
  - Since [18] is concurrent, a direct empirical comparison may not have been possible; still, a discussion on when each approach is preferable (in terms of m, k, memory) would aid readers.
  - Reporting running times and number of function evaluations would help assess streaming practicality.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear high-level algorithmic picture and proof roadmap. The above inconsistencies (threshold rule, space bound, τ expression) do detract; fixing them, and tightening notation (e.g., use either log k or ⌈log k⌉ consistently; define ε in Section 4.1; fix “st Conference” typo) would improve clarity. A brief intuitive explanation of why exponentially decreasing thresholds and exponentially increasing bucket sizes together yield robustness to deletions would also help readers unfamiliar with PRO.

Originality:
The partitioned thresholding design for robust streaming is novel and nontrivial. While inspired by PRO’s partitioned structure and SIEVE-STREAMING’s thresholding, their combination here, together with a new analysis yielding O(m log^2 k) memory dependence and constant-factor robustness, appears original. The independent concurrent work [18] emphasizes a different trade-off (better approximation, worse space); the distinctions are adequately acknowledged.

Significance:
The robust streaming setting is important for large-scale summarization with deletion/forgetting constraints. The memory improvement from O(mk · polylog) to O(m log^2 k + k log k) elements can be substantial when k is large, making this contribution potentially impactful. The constant 0.149 is modest compared to 0.5−ε, but the empirical results suggest that the practical performance is much better than the worst-case bound. I expect both researchers and some practitioners to build on these ideas.

Pros:
- Addresses an important and timely problem (robustness in streaming).
- Elegant algorithmic design (partitioned buckets + thresholds) with a clean one-pass summary and simple query procedure.
- Strong theoretical guarantees with markedly improved space scaling in k.
- Solid empirical validation across two domains.
- Clear relation to prior work; concurrent work acknowledged.

Cons:
- Approximation factor is small (≈0.149) and includes a mild finite-k degradation.
- Several presentation inconsistencies/typos (threshold rule, space bound, τ expression) need correction.
- No time/efficiency measurements; per-element processing could be nontrivial.
- Limited exploration of the w–m–memory trade-off in experiments; w fixed to 1.

Overall evaluation:
A technically sound and original contribution with meaningful theoretical and practical implications for robust submodular streaming. Despite some clarity issues and a conservative constant, the work advances the state of the art on an important problem. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on fixing the noted inconsistencies and adding brief discussion of runtime and parameter trade-offs.
