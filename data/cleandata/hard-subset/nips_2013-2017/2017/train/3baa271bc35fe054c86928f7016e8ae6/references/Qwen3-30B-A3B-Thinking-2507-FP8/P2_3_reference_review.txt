

# Review: "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary
This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. The authors propose a two-stage approach: (1) a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary set S of size O((k + m log k) log k), and (2) a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that, after any removal of up to m elements from S, returns a set of size at most k with a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The key innovation is a partitioned thresholding approach where the summary is arranged into partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds. The paper builds on SIEVE-STREAMING [8] but extends it to the robust setting with a novel thresholding scheme.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness with comprehensive theoretical analysis. The main result (Theorem 4.1) is well-proven, with detailed lemmas covering different cases of the partition structure. The authors carefully address the challenge of unknown parameter τ by introducing a parallel variant (Theorem 4.5) that increases memory by a factor of log1+ k while maintaining a (1+ε)-approximation. The experimental validation on two distinct applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) provides strong empirical support for the theoretical claims, showing that their method performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed).

The main weakness in quality is the relatively low approximation factor (0.149) compared to standard streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING (which achieves 0.5-ε). While the authors correctly argue that robustness comes at a cost, this limitation should be more explicitly discussed in terms of when their approach would be preferred over standard methods. The paper also doesn't thoroughly compare with other robust submodular maximization approaches beyond SIEVE-STREAMING, particularly [11] and [18] which are mentioned but not fully contrasted.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem statement to algorithm design to theoretical analysis to experiments. The pseudocode for STAR-T and STAR-T-GREEDY is precise and well-explained. The theoretical proofs are detailed, though some parts (particularly Lemma 4.3 and 4.4) could benefit from more intuitive explanations before the formal proofs.

The paper would be improved by:
1. Providing more detail on how τ is approximated (Section 4.1) beyond "we show how to extend the idea of [8]"
2. Adding a brief discussion of how the algorithm parameters (like w) should be set in practice
3. Clarifying the relationship between their partitioned thresholding approach and the greedy bucket filling in [11]

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the originality category. The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds represents a novel algorithmic design that differs substantially from previous robust submodular maximization approaches. The key distinction from [11] is that they don't fill buckets greedily one after another but instead place elements in the first bucket where the marginal gain exceeds the exponentially decreasing threshold. The authors properly distinguish their work from [18] which uses O(mk log k/ε) memory (linear in k) versus their O((k + m log k) log k) (logarithmic in k).

The paper builds on SIEVE-STREAMING [8] but extends it to the robust setting with a fundamentally different approach to thresholding, which is a meaningful contribution to the literature on streaming submodular maximization.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial for large-scale machine learning applications where data is too large to store and robustness against element removal is required. The two-stage approach provides a practical solution for scenarios where re-optimization after removals is infeasible, which is common in real-world applications like social network analysis and personalized recommendation systems.

The experimental results are particularly compelling: in both the dominating set and movie recommendation applications, their method performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING (which has the advantage of knowing in advance which elements will be removed). This demonstrates that the robustness guarantee doesn't come at a significant cost in practical performance.

The paper addresses an important gap in the literature: most streaming submodular maximization algorithms don't account for robustness against element removal, which is crucial for many real-world applications. The authors have shown that their approach can provide robustness with only a modest increase in memory requirements (logarithmic in k versus linear for the competing method [18]).

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of streaming submodular maximization. The theoretical results are sound, the algorithmic design is novel, and the experimental validation is convincing. While the approximation factor (0.149) is lower than some existing methods, the robustness guarantee in a streaming setting is valuable for specific applications where re-optimization after removals is infeasible.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a significant contribution to the literature. The main limitation is the relatively low approximation factor, but the authors have demonstrated that their method performs well in practice, which is a strong practical validation of their theoretical claims.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It makes a meaningful contribution to the field with a novel algorithmic approach that solves an important practical problem. The theoretical guarantees are well-supported, and the experimental results demonstrate the practical viability of the approach.

The paper is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS papers on submodular maximization (e.g., [8], [12], [13]) by addressing the robustness aspect in a streaming setting. It advances the state of the art by providing the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization with a constant-factor approximation guarantee and a memory complexity that has logarithmic dependence on k (unlike [18] which has linear dependence).

While the approximation factor could be improved, the robustness guarantee is a valuable trade-off for many applications, and the authors have provided strong evidence that their method performs well in practice. This paper should be of interest to the NIPS community working on large-scale machine learning and submodular optimization.
