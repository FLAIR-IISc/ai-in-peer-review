This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The core idea of using partitioned buckets with decreasing thresholds is interesting and, in principle, allows for a trade-off between memory usage and robustness. However, despite the novelty of the approach and potential significance of the problem, a multitude of small, yet persistent, issues detract from the overall quality and clarity of the work, leading me to recommend rejection.

**Summary of Ideas:** The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm for robust streaming submodular maximization. Stage 1 constructs a robust summary S using a partitioned thresholding scheme. Stage 2 employs a greedy algorithm on S \ E (where E is the removed set) to find a solution. The theoretical analysis aims to provide approximation guarantees, and experiments demonstrate competitive performance with SIEVE-STREAMING.

**Strengths:**
*   **Problem Formulation:** The problem of robust streaming submodular maximization is well-motivated and relevant to several applications.
*   **Partitioned Thresholding:** The core idea of partitioning the summary with decreasing thresholds is conceptually sound.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation provides some evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness, showing comparable performance to a strong baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING).

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity & Notation:** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity and inconsistent notation. For example, the meaning of *w* is unclear for a substantial portion of the paper – it is introduced as a “memory parameter” but its precise impact on memory isn't immediately obvious. The explanation of the partitions and buckets feels convoluted, and Figure 1, while helpful, doesn’t fully clarify the arrangement.
2.  **Theoretical Rigor & Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis is dense and relies on several simplifying assumptions.  The assumption of knowing *f(OPT(k, V \ E))* a priori, even with the attempt to circumvent it in Section 4.1, is a significant limitation. While the parallel approach outlined in Theorem 4.5 addresses this, the logarithmic factor in memory usage raises practical concerns. The constant factors within the approximation bounds (e.g., 0.149) are quite small and diminish the practical appeal of the theoretical guarantee.
3.  **Algorithm Presentation:** Algorithm 1 lacks sufficient detail.  Specifically, the condition `f (e | Bi,j) ≥ τ/min{2i, k}` is not clearly explained; is this a marginal gain *relative* to the current bucket or the empty set? Furthermore, the break statement within the inner loop isn’t thoroughly described – does it immediately proceed to the next element, or are subsequent buckets considered?
4.  **Experimental Setup:** The experimental section feels incomplete. While results are presented for dominating set and movie recommendation, the parameter settings (e.g., α in movie recommendation) are not justified. The choice of *w* = 1 in the experiments, despite the theory suggesting *w* ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem/k ⌉, feels ad-hoc and potentially undermines the theoretical guarantees.  More thorough ablation studies and sensitivity analyses are needed to understand the algorithm’s behavior.
5.  **Related Work Discussion:** The related work section, while covering relevant papers, lacks critical engagement. The authors mention [18] but only briefly discuss the differences in memory requirements. A more in-depth comparison and discussion of the trade-offs would be beneficial. The discussion of [8] could also be expanded to highlight specific advantages and disadvantages relative to STAR-T.
6.  **Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:** Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies exist throughout the paper. For example, the sentence "Hence, the buckets are disjoint sets that in the end (after one pass over the data) can have a smaller number of elements than specified by their corresponding cardinality constraints, and some of them might even be empty" seems redundant, and potentially confusing, given the algorithm's description. Additionally, several citations are missing details (e.g., missing volume/page numbers).

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The problem is important, the core idea is interesting, and the empirical results show competitive performance.
*   **Con:** The lack of clarity, theoretical limitations, algorithmic presentation issues, insufficient experimental detail, and numerous minor errors collectively undermine the paper’s overall quality. The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is too small to be practically useful.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper addresses a relevant problem and proposes a novel approach, the numerous shortcomings significantly detract from its overall quality. The paper feels underdeveloped and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. The persistent lack of clarity and the multitude of minor errors create a frustrating reading experience. I believe that substantial revisions are necessary to address these issues before this work would be suitable for publication at NIPS. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**.
