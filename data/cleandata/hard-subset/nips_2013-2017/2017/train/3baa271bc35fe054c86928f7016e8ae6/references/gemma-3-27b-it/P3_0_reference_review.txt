## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm, STAR-T, which first constructs a robust summary in a single pass over the data using partitioned buckets and decreasing thresholds, and then leverages this summary with a greedy algorithm to respond to removal queries. The theoretical contributions include a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the robustness and an analysis of the memory complexity. The authors also provide empirical validation demonstrating competitive performance against existing streaming algorithms, even when those algorithms have prior knowledge of removals.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon the foundational work on submodular maximization (Nemhauser et al., 1978) and streaming submodular maximization (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., 2014). It addresses the gap between the theoretical robustness results of Orlin et al. (2016) and Bogunovic et al. (2017) and the practical efficiency of streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014).  The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, highlighting the advantages of their approach over existing methods in terms of memory usage and theoretical guarantees. The connection to interactive and adaptive submodular optimization (Golovin & Krause, 2011) through the query stage is also well-noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The partitioned thresholding scheme is a clever idea for constructing a robust summary in a streaming manner. The exponentially decreasing thresholds and bucket structure appear well-motivated and crucial for achieving the theoretical guarantees.
* **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee for robustness is a significant contribution.  The analysis appears rigorous and addresses the complexities of the streaming and query stages.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem of robust summarization is highly relevant to many real-world applications. The two-stage framework allows for efficient adaptation to removals, making the approach practical.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both the dominating set and movie recommendation tasks demonstrate the competitive performance of STAR-T against SIEVE-STREAMING, even with SIEVE-STREAMING knowing the removals in advance.  The observation of performance exceeding theoretical bounds (w=1 in practice) is interesting.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear presentation of the problem, the proposed algorithm, and the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The theoretical guarantees rely on a carefully chosen threshold `τ`. The paper discusses approximating `f(OPT(k, V \ E))`, but the practical challenges of doing so effectively could limit the algorithm’s performance in real-world scenarios. The sensitivity of the performance to `τ` isn’t deeply explored empirically.
* **Constant Factors:** While the approximation guarantee is constant-factor, the constant (0.149) is relatively small. This could be a limiting factor in applications where a stronger approximation is desired.  It would be valuable to discuss the tightness of this bound and potential avenues for improvement.
* **Complexity of Analysis:** The analysis appears complex, making it challenging for readers to fully grasp the nuances of the guarantees. A more intuitive explanation of the key analytical steps could enhance understanding.
* **Limited Comparison to PRO:** While the authors mention PRO (Bogunovic et al., 2017) in the related work, a more direct comparison – even a brief one with experimental results – would be helpful. The authors claim their memory requirement is logarithmic in *k*, whereas PRO's is linear; showcasing this difference empirically would strengthen the claim.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments, while solid, primarily focus on two applications. Exploring the performance of STAR-T on a wider range of datasets and submodular functions would increase confidence in its generalizability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a significant theoretical contribution.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate competitive performance.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented.
* The problem addressed is highly relevant to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The small constant factor in the approximation guarantee could be a limitation.
* The analysis is complex and may be difficult for some readers to fully understand.
* The sensitivity to parameter tuning (especially `τ`) needs further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The proposed algorithm is novel, theoretically well-supported, and demonstrates promising empirical performance. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh them.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions).

I recommend accepting this paper, with the following suggestions for improvement:

*   **Discuss the practical challenges of estimating `τ` and explore robust strategies for setting it.**
*   **Provide a more intuitive explanation of the key analytical steps.**
*   **Include a brief comparison to the PRO algorithm, either analytically or experimentally.**
*   **Consider expanding the experimental evaluation to include additional datasets and submodular functions.**
