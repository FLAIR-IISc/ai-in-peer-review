Summary
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The goal is to precompute, in a single pass and sublinear memory, a summary S from which one can extract a near-optimal size-k subset even after the removal of any set E of up to m elements, without revisiting the stream. The authors propose a two-stage procedure:

- Streaming stage (STAR-T): maintain dlog ke + 1 partitions; partition i has w·⌈k/2^i⌉ buckets of capacity min{2^i, k}. Each partition i uses a (decreasing) threshold τ/2^i (or τ/min{2^i,k} in the algorithm). An incoming element is placed into the first non-full bucket where its marginal gain w.r.t. that bucket exceeds the partition threshold. This yields a summary of size O((k + m log k) log k), or O((k + m log k) log k log_{1+ε} k) when τ is unknown and parallel thresholds are used.

- Query stage (STAR-T-GREEDY): given any E, run GREEDY on S\E and output a size-k set Z.

The main theorem shows a constant-factor approximation: f(Z) ≥ c f(OPT(k, V\E)) with c ≈ 0.149(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉), using w = ⌈4·⌈log k⌉·m/k⌉. The analysis considers two complementary cases (some partition has many full buckets vs. none do) and balances bounds from lemmas based on bucket filling and submodularity.

The approach extends ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING (thresholding) and non-streaming robust methods like PRO (partitioned structure) to a robust streaming algorithm with better memory dependence on k than concurrent work on deletion-robust streaming (which achieves 1/2 − ε but uses O(mk log k/ε) memory). Experiments on dominating set (Twitter and Amazon co-purchasing graphs) and MovieLens recommendation show performance comparable to SIEVE-STREAMING that is allowed to know E in advance, and near GREEDY in smaller-scale settings.

Quality
- Theory: The algorithm is well-motivated and the analysis is careful. The two-case argument (many full buckets vs. none) and use of exponentially increasing bucket sizes/decreasing thresholds are sound. The constant 0.149 is modest but constant-factor guarantees for robust streaming with memory O(m log k polylog k + k polylog k) are non-trivial. The parallel-threshold approach to remove τ’s dependence on unknown OPT mirrors SIEVE-STREAMING and is standard.
- Completeness: Proof sketches are provided; full proofs in the supplement (not included here) are referenced. The memory bound is derived cleanly. One concern is the lack of explicit time/compute complexity per streamed element; worst-case per-element work seems to be O(wk log k) marginal evaluations, which may be non-trivial for expensive oracles.
- Empirics: The experiments demonstrate robustness and practicality on two tasks. Comparisons are fair in that the baseline is allowed to know E. However, the empirical section would benefit from reporting wall-clock time and memory usage, exploring the effect of m and w, and including sensitivity to α in the recommendation task. Also, the experiments sometimes remove more than k or a large fraction of V, beyond the theory’s m-bound; while this is encouraging, it would be useful to also show results within the theoretical regime across varying m.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The main ideas, algorithms, and proof structure are clear.
- Inconsistencies to fix:
  - The introduction and conclusion state O((k + m log k) log^2 k) space, whereas Theorem 4.1 and its derivation show O((k + m log k) log k) (and O((k + m log k) log k log_{1+ε} k) with parallel thresholds). Please reconcile.
  - Threshold notation: earlier text says threshold τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Align and explain the choice.
  - Minor typographical issues (e.g., “st Conference”).
- Provide per-element and total time complexity (number of oracle calls) and discuss practical optimizations (early stopping, bucket indexing).

Originality
- Novel combination of partitioned bucket structure (inspired by offline PRO) with streaming thresholding (SIEVE-STREAMING) to address robust deletions.
- Memory-approximation trade-offs differ from concurrent deletion-robust streaming work: your memory dependence on k is logarithmic vs. linear there, at the expense of a smaller approximation constant. This distinction is meaningful and well-argued.
- To the best of my knowledge, the specific partitioned thresholding design and analysis for robust streaming are new.

Significance
- Robust streaming submodular maximization is important for large-scale, dynamic applications where reprocessing the stream is infeasible and deletions/adversarial removals occur (privacy, failure, user feedback).
- The memory bound O((k + m log k) polylog k) is attractive, and the simple GREEDY query mechanism is practical.
- The approximation constant is modest, but the empirical results indicate good practical performance.
- Likely to be used as a building block and extended (e.g., to matroid constraints or non-monotone objectives, if possible).

Pros
- One-pass, sublinear memory algorithm with explicit robustness to m deletions.
- Clear, principled structure (exponential partitions + thresholds) and rigorous analysis.
- Better memory scaling in k than concurrent robust streaming methods.
- Practical two-stage workflow; competitive empirical results against strong baselines.

Cons
- Approximation factor (≈0.149) is relatively low.
- Missing computational complexity analysis; potential high per-element oracle cost.
- Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive (runtime/memory, varying m and w, additional datasets/objectives).
- Minor clarity inconsistencies in bounds and thresholds.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide per-element amortized number of oracle calls and any data structures that reduce the worst-case O(wk log k) scans.
- Empirically study the effect of w and m: how does increasing w improve robustness/utility vs. memory?
- Reconcile the space complexity statements (log k vs. log^2 k) throughout the paper.
- Clarify and unify the threshold definition across text and Algorithm 1.
- If possible, include a comparison (even small-scale) to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18], or at least a more detailed discussion of trade-offs.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept
A solid theoretical and algorithmic contribution addressing an important problem, with a novel design and better memory scaling than concurrent work. Some clarity issues and modest constant, and experiments could be strengthened, but overall a valuable addition to the NeurIPS literature on submodular streaming and robustness.
