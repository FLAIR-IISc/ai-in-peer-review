

# Peer Review: "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary and Context
This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, where the goal is to create a summary set that maintains good performance even after arbitrary removal of up to m elements. The authors propose a two-stage approach: a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary S in a single pass, followed by a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that handles removals by running greedy on S. The key innovation is a partitioned thresholding structure where buckets in different partitions have exponentially decreasing thresholds.

The work builds on SIEVE-STREAMING [8] for streaming submodular maximization and extends it to the robust setting. It relates to the robust submodular maximization problem introduced in [9], and the partitioning structure resembles the PRO algorithm [11], but with a novel thresholding scheme.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that STAR-T constructs a summary of size O((k + m log k) log k) and that STAR-T-GREEDY achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for the robust problem. This is a significant advance over prior work.

2. **Novel Algorithmic Design**: The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds is elegant and effectively handles the robustness requirement. The analysis carefully combines properties of the robust structure with the thresholding rule, which is a non-trivial technical contribution.

3. **Practical Validation**: The experimental results on two distinct applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) convincingly demonstrate that the approach matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows removals in advance), showing the practical viability of the method.

4. **Space Complexity Advantage**: The space complexity O((k + m log k) log k) is superior to concurrent work [18] (O(mk log k/ε)), with a logarithmic dependence on k rather than linear.

## Critical Analysis

1. **Practical Implementation Challenge**: The main theoretical result assumes τ = f(OPT(k, V\E)) is known, which is unrealistic. While Section 4.1 addresses this with a parallel algorithm (Theorem 4.5), the space complexity increases by a factor of log₁₊ᵋ k. For large k, this could significantly impact practicality. The authors should better justify why this overhead is acceptable compared to the constant factor improvement.

2. **Constant Factor Comparison**: The approximation ratio (0.149) is substantially lower than SIEVE-STREAMING's (0.5-ε) for the non-robust problem. The paper claims their method "matches (and in some cases outperforms) SIEVE-STREAMING," but this is only true when SIEVE-STREAMING is given advance knowledge of removals. For fair comparison, the authors should show how their method compares to a robust version of SIEVE-STREAMING.

3. **Comparison with Concurrent Work**: The paper mentions [18] but does not sufficiently contrast their approach with this concurrent work. A more detailed comparison of the tradeoffs (e.g., constant factor vs. space complexity) would strengthen the paper. The claim that "their algorithm uses O(mk log k/ε) memory" while "our algorithm has logarithmic dependence on k" is accurate but lacks context about practical memory requirements.

4. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The analysis assumes normalized monotone submodular functions, which may not cover all practical applications. The authors should discuss how their approach might extend to other function classes.

5. **Experimental Design**: The experiments in Section 5.1 show that STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING when SIEVE-STREAMING knows removals in advance, but this is not a fair comparison to a truly robust method. The paper would be stronger if it compared against a robust version of SIEVE-STREAMING (e.g., one that builds a robust summary using similar techniques).

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the growing literature on streaming submodular maximization. The partitioned thresholding approach is novel and well-motivated, and the analysis is technically sound. The practical experiments provide good validation of the method's utility.

The main weakness is the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical implementation due to the τ assumption, though the authors address this with a parallel algorithm. The constant factor (0.149) is lower than non-robust streaming algorithms, but the authors convincingly argue that this is a necessary trade-off for robustness.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, advancing the state of the art in robust streaming submodular maximization. The work is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and has practical implications for large-scale data summarization applications.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The partitioned thresholding technique is elegant and effectively handles the robustness requirement while maintaining sublinear space complexity. The experimental results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical viability.

The main concerns about the τ assumption and constant factor are addressed adequately in the paper, and the contribution is sufficiently distinct from prior work to warrant publication. The paper would be strengthened by more detailed comparison with concurrent work [18] and a more thorough discussion of the constant factor trade-off, but these do not undermine the core contribution.

The work is relevant to NIPS, addresses an important problem in large-scale machine learning, and makes a demonstrable advance over prior research.
