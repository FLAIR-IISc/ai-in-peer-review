Summary
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting via a two-stage framework. In the streaming stage, STAR-T constructs a compact, deletion-robust summary S using a novel partitioned-thresholding design: partitions have exponentially increasing bucket capacities and exponentially decreasing acceptance thresholds; arriving elements are inserted into the first non-full bucket whose marginal gain exceeds the partition threshold. In the query stage, after an arbitrary removal E of up to m elements, running GREEDY on S\E returns a size-k solution with a constant-factor approximation. The main guarantee (Theorem 4.1) gives f(Z) ≥ 0.149(1 − 1/log k)·OPT with a single pass and space roughly O((k + m log k) log k) (with an additional log(1+ε) factor when OPT is unknown). Empirically, on dominating-set tasks (Twitter ego-networks, Amazon co-purchase) and MovieLens recommendation, the method matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter is given the removed set E in advance.

Relation to prior work
The design bridges PRO-style non-uniform partitioning (Bogunovic et al., ICML’17) with streaming thresholding from SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., KDD’14), but replaces sequential greedy bucket filling by a streaming threshold rule tailored to robustness. Compared to concurrent deletion-robust streaming work (Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17), the paper trades a smaller constant (≈0.149 vs 1/2−ε) for dramatically better memory dependence (log k vs linear in k; and linear in m only via a modest factor), which is compelling for large k. The problem setting—build once, answer many robust queries—is timely and useful.

Strengths
- Novel algorithmic structure: partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds for robustness in streaming.
- Theoretical contribution: first constant-factor approximation for robust streaming with space O((k + m log k) log k) (plus a standard log(1+ε) factor when OPT is unknown). The analysis (two complementary cases: many full buckets vs mostly non-full) is neat and leverages both the robust structure and thresholding.
- Practical relevance: two-stage design naturally supports multiple post-hoc removals without access to the stream.
- Empirical results: competitive with SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows E; demonstrations on graphs and recommendation.

Weaknesses and clarity issues
- Constants and statements are sometimes inconsistent or confusing:
  - The intro and theorem alternately state O((k + m log k) log^2 k) vs O((k + m log k) log k) space; please unify.
  - Algorithm 1 line 5 uses τ/min{2^i,k} but the text describes thresholds τ/2^i; clarify the intended thresholding rule.
  - The specification of τ in Theorem 4.1 appears malformed (missing a denominator or parentheses).
- Practicality details missing: per-element processing time can be high if many buckets are scanned; no runtime or throughput measurements are reported. How is insertion implemented to avoid O(wk) per element in the worst case?
- The core constant 0.149 is modest; while acceptable from a theory standpoint, some discussion on tightening it (or typical-case behavior) would help.
- Experimental evaluation:
  - No direct empirical comparison to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18] (even if only discussion/estimates), or to a strong non-streaming robust baseline when feasible.
  - Robustness parameterization: many experiments remove k or 2k elements with w=1, which exceeds the theory’s m bound; while it’s nice that performance remains strong, it would be useful to also show curves varying w and m to validate the prescribed scaling.
  - Limited diversity of objectives; beyond coverage-like objectives, consider a feature-based submodular objective (e.g., facility location with diversity) in a large streaming setting.

Quality
The core proofs appear sound and the high-level argument is clear: either we harvest value from a full bucket robust to a bounded number of deletions, or we leverage the largest not-full bucket together with GREEDY’s guarantee. The extension to unknown OPT via parallel instances follows SIEVE-STREAMING practice. The main gaps are clarity (notation/typos) and a lack of runtime analysis/measurements.

Originality
High. The partitioned thresholding construction for robust streaming is, to my knowledge, new, and the memory/approximation trade-off is a substantial advance over prior streaming baselines in the robust setting.

Significance
Meaningful. Robustness to deletions is increasingly important (privacy/right-to-be-forgotten, failures, changing preferences). The two-stage summary-and-query paradigm, with strong space dependence and single-pass processing, is of broad interest to the NeurIPS community.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Clarify the exact threshold per partition (τ/2^i vs τ/min{2^i,k}) and fix τ’s definition in Theorem 4.1. Unify the space bound across the paper.
- Provide per-element amortized processing time bounds and practical implementation details (e.g., early stopping, pointers to first non-full bucket).
- Report wall-clock and memory usage vs SIEVE-STREAMING, and include sensitivity to τ (when approximated) and w.
- Add experiments varying m and w to empirically validate the space–robustness trade-off.
- Discuss extensions to other constraints (e.g., matroids) and whether the analysis can generalize.

Pros
- Novel and elegant algorithmic idea with rigorous guarantees.
- Strong memory efficiency in k and m; single pass.
- Practical two-stage interface; multiple queries supported.
- Solid empirical showing vs a strong streaming baseline that knows deletions.

Cons
- Presentation issues (inconsistent thresholds/space, τ formula).
- Low constant; no runtime evaluation.
- Limited experimental breadth; no direct comparison to [18].

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). Despite some presentation and evaluation gaps, the algorithmic idea is fresh and promising, with clear theoretical value and practical potential. Tightening the exposition and adding runtime analysis would further strengthen the paper.
