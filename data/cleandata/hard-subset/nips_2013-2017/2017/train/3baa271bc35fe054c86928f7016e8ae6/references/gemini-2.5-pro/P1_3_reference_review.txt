### Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The goal is to select a small, representative summary of a large dataset in a single pass, such that the summary remains effective even after an adversary removes up to `m` of its elements. The authors propose a two-stage framework. In the first (streaming) stage, their algorithm, STAR-T, processes the data stream to build a robust summary `S`. In the second (query) stage, after an arbitrary set of `m` elements `E` is removed from `S`, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining set `S \ E` to find a `k`-sized solution.

The core contribution is the STAR-T algorithm, which builds the summary `S` using a novel partitioned structure. The summary consists of multiple partitions, each containing a number of buckets. The key ideas are that (i) partitions are associated with exponentially decreasing quality thresholds, and (ii) buckets within partitions have exponentially increasing size limits. An incoming element is placed into the first available bucket for which its marginal gain exceeds the corresponding partition's threshold.

Theoretically, the authors prove that this method yields a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the final solution obtained in the query stage. The required summary `S` has a size of `O((k + m log k) log k)`, which notably has a favorable logarithmic dependence on `k`, unlike concurrent work. The paper also validates the approach empirically on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, showing that it performs comparably to, and sometimes better than, the non-robust SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm, even when the latter is given the unfair advantage of knowing which elements will be removed in advance.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is the main strength. The authors provide a complete proof sketch for their constant-factor approximation guarantee (c ≈ 0.149). The analysis is non-trivial, cleverly handling two distinct cases: one where many buckets in the summary are full, and another where they are not. The resulting memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)` is a significant result, particularly its logarithmic dependence on `k`, which compares favorably to the linear dependence in the concurrent work of Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017). The experimental evaluation is also strong. Comparing against an oracle-assisted version of SIEVE-STREAMING is a very challenging baseline, and the fact that STAR-T performs competitively demonstrates the practical utility of its robust structure.
*   **Weaknesses:** The derived approximation constant of 0.149 is quite low. While achieving any constant factor in this challenging setting is a significant achievement, the practical implications of such a loose bound are limited. The paper is honest about this, but it remains a theoretical weakness.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
*   **Strengths:** The problem statement is clearly motivated with compelling examples. The two-stage procedure is well-defined. The high-level description of the STAR-T algorithm's structure (partitions, buckets, thresholds) provides good intuition. The proof sketch, while dense, outlines the key lemmas and the overall logical flow of the argument.
*   **Weaknesses:** There is a minor inconsistency in the description of the thresholding rule. The text in Section 3 states that the threshold decreases as `τ/2^i`. However, Algorithm 1 (line 5) defines the condition as `f(e | Bi,j) ≥ τ/min{2^i, k}`. This normalization by the bucket size is a standard and necessary detail for such thresholding algorithms, but the text's simplification could cause confusion. Clarifying this would be helpful. Additionally, while the gap between the theoretical requirement for the parameter `w` (`w` >= `O(m log k / k)`) and its practical setting (`w=1`) is mentioned in passing, a more detailed discussion on why the algorithm is so effective in practice even with this simplified setting would strengthen the experimental section.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
*   **Strengths:** The problem formulation combining the challenges of streaming processing and robustness to adversarial removals is novel and timely. While building on ideas from streaming submodular maximization (like SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust offline maximization (like the partitioned structure in Bogunovic et al., 2017), the proposed "Partitioned Thresholding" approach is a novel synthesis that is specifically tailored to the robust streaming setting. The analysis technique, which balances multiple lower bounds derived from different scenarios, is also innovative. The paper clearly situates itself with respect to related and concurrent work, highlighting its unique contributions, especially the improved memory complexity.

**Significance:** The paper has high significance for both researchers and practitioners.
*   **Strengths:** Data summarization is a fundamental problem in large-scale machine learning, and the need for both streaming-friendliness and robustness is increasingly prevalent. This work provides the first practical, theoretically-grounded algorithm for this combined setting. The two-stage model is particularly relevant for applications where a system must pre-process a massive data stream to create a summary that can later be queried interactively and robustly without re-accessing the original data (as demonstrated in the movie recommendation experiment). The ideas presented here are likely to inspire follow-up work on robust optimization in streaming and other resource-constrained environments.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
1.  **Novel Problem:** Addresses the important and challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** Provides the first constant-factor approximation guarantee for this problem with a favorable memory complexity (`log k` dependence).
3.  **Innovative Algorithm:** The partitioned thresholding algorithm is a novel and clever combination of existing ideas tailored for this new setting.
4.  **Convincing Experiments:** The empirical results are strong, showing the algorithm is competitive with a powerful, oracle-assisted baseline, confirming its practical relevance.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Cons):**
1.  **Low Approximation Constant:** The theoretical guarantee of 0.149, while a constant, is small.
2.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** Small inconsistencies in the algorithm description and a lack of discussion about the theory-practice gap for parameter `w` slightly detract from the paper's polish.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions to the field of submodular optimization. It tackles a novel, well-motivated, and difficult problem and provides a strong solution backed by both rigorous theory and compelling experiments. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the problem and the quality of the theoretical and empirical results, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper is a clear advance in the state of the art and is very likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
