This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The goal is to select a small, robust summary `S` from a large data stream in a single pass, such that even after an adversary removes up to `m` elements from `S`, a near-optimal solution of size `k` can still be recovered from the remaining elements without accessing the original data stream. The authors propose a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a novel streaming algorithm called STAR-T constructs the summary `S`. This algorithm is inspired by the thresholding approach of SIEVE-STREAMING [8] and the partitioned structure of the non-streaming robust algorithm PRO [11]. It organizes the summary into partitions with exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds and buckets of exponentially increasing size. In the second stage, after `m` elements are removed from `S`, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining elements to find the final solution `Z`.

The work is well-positioned within the literature. It effectively combines ideas from streaming submodular maximization [8, 12] and robust submodular maximization [9, 10, 11]. The key novelty lies in adapting the partitioned structure, previously used in a centralized setting [11], to the streaming context by integrating an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. This is a non-trivial extension that requires a new analysis. The paper also appropriately compares itself to concurrent work [18], highlighting a key advantage in memory complexity (logarithmic vs. linear dependence on `k`).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of robust summarization for streaming data is highly relevant to modern large-scale machine learning, where data is massive and systems can be brittle or subject to adversarial manipulation. The proposed STAR-T algorithm presents a novel and elegant combination of existing ideas (partitioning and thresholding) to solve this important problem. The design of the algorithm, particularly the interplay between partitions, bucket sizes, and thresholds, is clever and well-motivated.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving that the proposed procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The proof sketch is well-structured, breaking down the analysis into two main cases (many full buckets vs. few full buckets) and providing clear lemmas for each. A key theoretical contribution is the memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)`, which is substantially better than the `O(mk log k)` of concurrent work [18], especially for large `k`. This logarithmic dependence on `k` makes the approach much more scalable.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement is motivated with compelling examples. The algorithms (STAR-T and STAR-T-GREEDY) are presented cleanly, and the intuition behind the partitioned structure is explained well. The experimental section is also clear, with well-defined baselines and evaluation scenarios that effectively demonstrate the algorithm's practical performance.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation are convincing. The comparison against SIEVE-STREAMING, a strong baseline that is given the advantage of knowing the removals in advance, is particularly powerful. The fact that STAR-T performs comparably or even slightly better in some cases highlights its effectiveness. The movie recommendation experiment demonstrates the practical utility of the two-stage approach, where a single pre-computed summary can handle various interactive queries (e.g., filtering by genre), showcasing its flexibility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Approximation Constant:** The theoretical approximation guarantee of `c ≈ 0.149` is quite low. While obtaining any constant factor in this challenging setting is a significant achievement, there is a noticeable gap between this worst-case bound and the strong empirical performance, which appears to be on par with non-robust algorithms. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss this gap. Is the constant a limitation of the analysis technique, or is it inherent to the algorithm's structure? Exploring this could provide deeper insights.

2.  **Practicality of Algorithm Parameters:**
    *   The algorithm requires an estimate `τ` of the unknown optimal value. The proposed solution is to run multiple instances in parallel, which is a standard technique but increases memory by a `log(k)/ε` factor and adds implementation complexity. The experiments section does not specify how `τ` was set. Was the parallel version implemented, or was `τ` estimated heuristically? Clarifying this and commenting on the sensitivity to `τ` would strengthen the paper's practical guidance.
    *   The memory parameter `w` is required to be `Ω(m log(k) / k)` in the theory, but experiments show `w=1` works very well, even for cases where `m > k`. This is a positive practical finding but suggests the theoretical analysis might be loose. A brief discussion on why this might be the case (e.g., the adversarial removal of `m` elements is much worse than typical random or targeted removals) would be insightful.

3.  **Comparison to Concurrent Work:** The paper rightly points out its superior memory complexity compared to [18]. However, [18] achieves a better approximation factor of `1/2 - ε`. A more explicit discussion of this trade-off (better memory vs. better approximation guarantee) would provide a more complete picture for readers and practitioners choosing between the methods.

4.  **Minor Clarification in Proof Sketch:** In the proof sketch for Lemma 4.4, the argument relies on bounding the marginal gain `f(e | Bdlog ke)` for elements `e` in the optimal solution that were not stored. The reason they were not stored is that they failed to meet the threshold for *any* available bucket, not just `Bdlog ke`. While the resulting bound `f(e | Bdlog ke) < τ/k` is likely correct due to the decreasing thresholds and submodularity, stating the argument more precisely would improve clarity.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper addresses a significant and timely problem. The proposed algorithm is novel, combining ideas from different sub-fields in a non-trivial way. The theoretical results are solid, with a particularly strong result on memory complexity. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate practical effectiveness against strong baselines.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The theoretical approximation constant is low. The practical implementation details regarding key parameters (`τ`, `w`) could be clarified further.

**Decision:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization and large-scale machine learning. The strengths—in particular, the novelty of the algorithm, the practical relevance of the problem, and the excellent memory complexity—far outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses identified are primarily suggestions for clarification and future work rather than fundamental flaws. I recommend accepting this paper.
