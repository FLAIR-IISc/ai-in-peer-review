Summary
This paper tackles robust submodular maximization in the single-pass streaming setting with post-hoc deletions. It proposes STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that builds a “partitioned thresholding” summary S comprising exponentially sized buckets across partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds. Upon a query with any deletion set E of size ≤ m, a second-stage procedure (GREEDY on S\E) returns a k-set with a constant-factor guarantee. The main theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation (c ≈ 0.149 asymptotically) with memory O((k + m log k) polylog k), specifically O(m log^2 k) when parameters are set tightly, and a parallel-instance variant to eliminate dependence on f(OPT). Empirically, on influence/dominating set and personalized recommendation, the method matches or beats SIEVE-STREAMING even when SIEVE is allowed to know E.

Relation to prior work
The approach combines ideas from: (i) partitioned structures used in robust non-streaming algorithms (e.g., PRO) and (ii) streaming thresholding (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING), but departs from both by placing incoming items into the first bucket surpassing a decreasing partition threshold rather than filling buckets greedily. Compared to concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [18] (1/2−ε approximation, O(mk log k/ε) space), STAR-T trades a smaller constant (≈0.149) for dramatically better space (logarithmic vs. linear in k) and a clean one-pass design. This is a meaningful and novel trade-off.

Quality
- Theory: The lemmas and the two-case analysis (many full buckets vs. few) are plausible and technically nontrivial. The averaging argument to ensure a bucket with low overlap with E is standard and appropriate for adversarial removals. The balancing of bounds to obtain c is clear conceptually. The parallel-threshold construction to remove dependence on f(OPT) is standard yet necessary and is correctly accounted for in space.
- Experiments: The empirical section is focused and relevant. Using SIEVE-STREAMING with knowledge of deletions as a strong baseline is a conservative/even adversarial choice against the proposed method; STAR-T matching it is impressive. The recommendation experiments are compelling and demonstrate the interactive re-use of a single summary for multiple queries.
- Caveats: The constant 0.149 is modest; while acceptable for a streaming robust setting, discussion of whether better constants are achievable with the same structure would be useful. Runtime/memory measurements are missing; given the paper’s motivation, reporting per-element processing time and empirical memory usage would strengthen the case. Sensitivity to w and m (beyond w=1) is not extensively explored.

Clarity
Overall well-written and organized. However, there are a few places where clarity suffers and should be fixed:
- Threshold inconsistency: Earlier text says thresholds are τ/2^i, whereas Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i,k}. Please reconcile and explain why the min{k,2^i} normalization is needed.
- Space bound inconsistencies: The abstract/intro mention O((m log k + k) log^2 k), Theorem 4.1 says O((k + m log k) log k), while Eq. (3) suggests (log k + 5) w k leading to O(m log^2 k) when w is set to its minimum. Please unify these bounds and clearly state the dominant terms.
- The τ definition in Theorem 4.1 appears garbled; please fix notation/parentheses so readers can verify the constants.
- Several typographical issues (e.g., dlog ke notation) impede readability.

Originality
High. The partitioned thresholding scheme is a fresh algorithmic idea that blends two lines of work in a nontrivial way and yields new guarantees (in particular, logarithmic dependence on k in memory under deletions). The analysis is tailored to this structure and not a routine adaptation.

Significance
High for both theory and practice:
- Theory: Provides an important point in the robustness-memory trade-off landscape for streaming submodular maximization. The result is likely to be a reference for future deletion-robust streaming algorithms.
- Practice: The ability to precompute a single summary and answer many deletion queries is valuable in domains with “right to be forgotten,” personalization constraints, or failures. The experiments demonstrate feasibility on nontrivial graphs and recommendation data.

Pros
- Novel and elegant algorithmic structure (partitioned, exponentially decreasing thresholds).
- One-pass, small-space robust summaries with memory only logarithmic in k (for fixed m).
- Constant-factor guarantee with post-hoc GREEDY, plus a practical multi-instance trick to remove dependence on f(OPT).
- Empirical results that are conservative yet favorable (competing with a baseline that knows the deletions).

Cons
- Approximation constant is relatively small.
- Presentation issues: threshold and space bounds inconsistencies; τ definition garbled.
- Experiments lack runtime/memory profiling and deeper sensitivity to w and m.
- Limited comparison to PRO or other non-streaming robust methods to quantify the loss from streaming.

Questions/requests for the authors
- Please clarify the exact thresholds used per partition and the rationale for τ/min{2^i,k} vs. τ/2^i.
- Unify the memory bounds and provide a succinct theorem with tight leading terms (in k, m, and log k).
- Can the constant be improved with minor tweaks (e.g., different partition factors or thresholds)?
- Provide sensitivity plots over w and m, and report empirical memory footprint and throughput.
- Consider adding a comparison to PRO (centralized) to quantify the effect of streaming on solution quality.

Recommendation
Accept. This paper introduces a genuinely new algorithmic design for a timely and important problem, with a compelling memory–approximation trade-off and solid empirical validation. Despite some clarity issues and a modest constant, the novelty and potential impact justify acceptance.

Confidence
High.
