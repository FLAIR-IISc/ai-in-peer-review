

# Peer Review: "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where the goal is to create a small representative summary that remains effective even after the removal of up to m elements. The authors propose a two-stage procedure: (1) a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary in one pass, and (2) a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that efficiently computes a solution after element removals. The key innovation is a partitioned thresholding approach where the summary is structured into exponentially increasing buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds.

The paper builds upon prior work on streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., [9, 10, 11, 18]). Unlike previous robust algorithms that require multiple passes or have higher memory requirements, their approach maintains a single-pass streaming property while achieving a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the robust problem. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that the algorithm achieves an approximation ratio of approximately 0.149 for large k, with a memory complexity of O((k + m log k) log k).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution with a novel partitioned thresholding approach and a rigorous analysis that combines properties of the robust structure with decreasing greedy thresholding. The analysis is comprehensive, addressing both cases where partitions have many full buckets and where most buckets are not full.

2. **Practical Relevance**: The two-stage framework is highly practical for real-world applications where data is produced rapidly and storage is limited. The authors demonstrate this with compelling experiments on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments are well-designed and show that their approach matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed). This is particularly impressive given that their method does not have this prior knowledge.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear pseudocode for both algorithms, well-structured theoretical results, and thorough experimental validation. The supplementary material provides detailed proofs, making the work reproducible.

5. **Novelty**: The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds represents a meaningful innovation that extends SIEVE-STREAMING to the robust setting while maintaining one-pass streaming properties.

## Weaknesses

1. **Approximation Ratio**: The approximation ratio of approximately 0.149 is substantially lower than the 0.5-ε guarantee of SIEVE-STREAMING. While the authors acknowledge this is a trade-off for robustness, it would be helpful to better quantify this trade-off in terms of practical performance versus theoretical guarantees.

2. **Parameter Dependency**: The algorithm requires knowledge of f(OPT(k, V\E)) for the threshold parameter τ. While the paper provides a solution using parallel instances (increasing space by log1+ k), this feels somewhat ad hoc compared to the elegance of the main algorithm.

3. **Memory Complexity Claim**: The paper claims their memory complexity has logarithmic dependence on k versus linear dependence in [18], but the exact comparison needs more clarification. Specifically, [18] uses O(mk log k/ε) memory, while the authors claim O((k + m log k) log k). The relationship between these two expressions (particularly for varying m and k) could be more clearly explained.

4. **Theoretical vs. Practical Parameters**: The theoretical analysis requires w ≥ ⌈4dlog kem/k⌉, but the experiments show w=1 works well in practice. The authors don't adequately explain why the theoretical bound is so much larger than what works in practice, which could be a point of confusion for readers.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of robust submodular maximization in streaming settings. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical relevance is clear. The two-stage framework provides a genuinely useful solution to a challenging problem with real-world applications.

The main concerns are the relatively low approximation ratio compared to non-robust streaming algorithms and the parameter dependency on f(OPT(k, V\E)). However, these are reasonable trade-offs for the robustness property, which is the paper's main contribution. The empirical results demonstrating that their method outperforms or matches SIEVE-STREAMING (which has prior knowledge of removals) strongly support the practical value of their approach.

The paper is well-written, well-structured, and makes a clear contribution that advances the state of the art in streaming submodular optimization. It is clearly different from previous NIPS work on submodular maximization and robust optimization, and the problem it addresses is important and timely.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with practical implications for large-scale machine learning applications. The authors have carefully addressed the challenges of robustness in streaming settings and demonstrated both the theoretical guarantees and practical effectiveness of their approach. While the approximation ratio is lower than some non-robust methods, the robustness property is valuable and the paper convincingly shows that their method performs well in practice.

**Pro for Acceptance**:
- Novel partitioned thresholding approach for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with constant-factor approximation guarantee
- Strong empirical validation showing competitive performance against baselines
- Clear, well-structured presentation of the problem and solution

**Con for Acceptance**:
- Lower approximation ratio compared to non-robust methods (though this is a necessary trade-off for robustness)
- Parameter dependency on f(OPT(k, V\E)) requires additional complexity (though the paper provides a reasonable solution)

This paper represents a solid contribution to the field of streaming algorithms and submodular optimization, with clear theoretical and practical value. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
