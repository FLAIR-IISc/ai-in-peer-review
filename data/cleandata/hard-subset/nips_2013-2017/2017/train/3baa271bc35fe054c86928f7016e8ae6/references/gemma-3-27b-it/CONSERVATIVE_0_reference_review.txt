This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure, STAR-T, based on partitioned thresholding, and provide theoretical guarantees. While the problem setting is well-motivated and the attempt to address robustness in a streaming context is commendable, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing due to a lack of compelling empirical validation and several theoretical concerns.

The core idea of partitioning the data stream and using decreasing thresholds is reasonable. However, the theoretical results, while mathematically presented, are heavily reliant on parameters (w, τ) that are themselves functions of unknown quantities (f(OPT(k, V \ E))). While the authors address approximating this unknown value, the resulting complexity and the dependence on *multiple* parallel instances (Theorem 4.5) significantly diminish the practical appeal. The theoretical approximation guarantee, while a constant factor, is quite small (0.149 in the limit) and the dependence on `dlog ke` suggests limited scalability.

A major weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The comparison to SIEVE-STREAMING is reasonable, but the experimental setup feels somewhat contrived. The authors specifically emphasize that SIEVE-STREAMING is allowed knowledge of the removal set, yet the experiments don’t fully demonstrate a clear benefit of STAR-T *despite* the increased theoretical complexity. Furthermore, the application to movie recommendation feels rather limited. The setup involving genre and already-seen movies is a reasonable attempt to create robustness scenarios, but lacks broader context and isn't representative of a real-world recommendation system's complexities. The claim that STAR-T matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, even in these limited scenarios, isn't convincingly demonstrated; differences are often within a few percent. The selection of w=1 in experiments, despite the theoretical requirement of w ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem/k ⌉, raises concerns about the validity of the experimental results compared to the theoretical guarantees. The authors mention additional results in the supplementary material but these should be included in the main text to permit a thorough assessment.

I'm also skeptical of the claim that the algorithm is more resilient in practice than the theoretical bounds suggest. This needs further investigation and justification. The dependency on `log k` in the space complexity is also a concern – while logarithmic, it can become significant for very large values of k.

**Strengths:**

*   Well-motivated problem: Robust submodular maximization is a relevant and challenging problem.
*   Clear problem statement and formulation.
*   Theoretical analysis provides some guarantees, albeit limited.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Theoretical guarantees rely on unknown quantities and require multiple parallel instances.
*   Small constant approximation factor (0.149).
*   Limited and somewhat contrived experimental evaluation.
*   Practical implications of the theoretical results are unclear.
*   Experimental setup and parameter choices raise concerns regarding the validity of the results.
*   The impact of setting w=1 despite the theoretical requirement.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and open problem.
*   Provides a theoretical framework for robust streaming submodular maximization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical guarantees are weak and complex.
*   Experimental evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   Limited practical relevance due to computational overhead and dependence on unknown parameters.
*   The experimental results do not clearly demonstrate superior performance over existing methods, even under favorable conditions.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper tackles an important problem, the theoretical contributions are overshadowed by their complexity and limited practical implications. The experimental validation is insufficient to justify the added complexity of the proposed algorithm. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and impactful contribution. I would encourage the authors to focus on more extensive empirical validation, addressing the parameter sensitivity, and demonstrating a clear advantage over existing methods in realistic scenarios before resubmitting.
