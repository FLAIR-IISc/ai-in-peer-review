

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper proposes a streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, with the goal of finding a summary set S that remains useful even after the removal of up to m elements. The authors present a two-stage approach: a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary, followed by a query stage (STAR-T-GREEDY) that finds a solution from the remaining elements.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in large-scale data summarization, where robustness to element removal is critical. The algorithmic approach using partitioned thresholding is novel, and the theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful proof sketches. The experimental results demonstrate that the method performs comparably to SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed) across two applications.

## Critical Weaknesses

1. **Fundamental Error in Problem Formulation**: The paper defines a set S as robust if "for any set E ⊆ V such that |E| ≤ m, there is a subset Z ⊆ S \ E of size at most k such that f(Z) ≥ c f(OPT(k, V \ E))". This is incorrect because if E ⊆ V but E ∩ S = ∅, then S \ E = S, which is trivial. Robustness should be defined for E ⊆ S, not E ⊆ V. This fundamental error undermines the entire theoretical framework and makes the approximation guarantee meaningless.

2. **Inconsistent Threshold Definition**: Algorithm 1 states in line 5 that "f(e | Bi,j) ≥ τ/min{2i, k}", but the paper's explanation states "the marginal value f(e | Bi,j) is at least τ/2i". These are inconsistent - the threshold should be τ/2i, not τ/min{2i, k}. This error propagates through the analysis and affects the correctness of the proof.

3. **Inconsistent Memory Complexity Claims**: The paper title claims "O((m log k + k) log² k)" memory, but Theorem 4.1 states "O((k + m log k) log k)" memory. The proof shows |S| = O((k + m log k) log k), making the title's claim incorrect. This is a serious inconsistency that misrepresents the algorithm's efficiency.

4. **Misleading Comparison**: The paper compares against SIEVE-STREAMING "that knows in advance which elements will be removed." This is not a fair comparison, as SIEVE-STREAMING is not designed for robustness. A fair comparison would be against a standard greedy algorithm run on the remaining elements after removal, not against a version of SIEVE-STREAMING with prior knowledge of removals.

5. **Low Approximation Constant**: The approximation ratio approaches 0.149 as k → ∞, which is significantly lower than the standard greedy algorithm's (1-1/e) ≈ 0.632 guarantee. The paper does not adequately justify why this low constant is acceptable for practical applications.

## Minor Issues

- Theorem 4.5 states the approximation guarantee as 0.149/(1+ε) (1 - 1/dlog ke), but the proof sketch suggests a different constant.
- Inconsistent notation: "dlog ke" appears throughout instead of standard floor notation ⌊log k⌋.
- Minor typo in the abstract: "st Conference" should be "The Conference".
- Inconsistent use of "dlog ke" vs. "log k" in the text.
- The paper claims a "two-stage procedure," but the query stage is simply a standard greedy algorithm, which is not truly novel.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses an interesting problem and contains some novel algorithmic ideas, the fundamental error in the problem formulation (defining E ⊆ V instead of E ⊆ S) is severe and undermines the theoretical contribution. The inconsistent threshold definition and memory complexity claims further erode confidence in the results. The low approximation constant (0.149) is not adequately justified, and the experimental comparison is misleading.

The paper would benefit from a complete reworking of the theoretical framework, with careful attention to the problem definition, consistent notation, and accurate complexity claims. Without these corrections, the paper's main contributions cannot be properly evaluated.

Given the severity of the fundamental error in the problem formulation and the other significant technical inconsistencies, I recommend rejection. The paper's core idea may be promising, but the current presentation contains critical flaws that prevent it from being considered a valid contribution to the field.
