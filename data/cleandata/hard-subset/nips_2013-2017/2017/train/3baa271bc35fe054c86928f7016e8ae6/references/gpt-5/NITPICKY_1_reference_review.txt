Summary
The paper studies streaming robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint. It proposes STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm that builds a summary S via a partitioned, exponentially growing bucket structure with decreasing thresholds, and a query-time recovery procedure (STAR-T-GREEDY) that runs GREEDY on S\E after arbitrary removal of up to m elements. The main guarantee: using space roughly O(k log k + m log^2 k), the query stage returns a k-set with a constant-factor approximation to OPT(k, V\E), with constant ≈0.149 asymptotically. When OPT is unknown, the paper sketches the standard “multiple guesses” trick increasing space by a log factor. Experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation show performance comparable to SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter is granted knowledge of E.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on classic GREEDY [Nemhauser et al.], streaming SIEVE-STREAMING [Badanidiyuru et al.], and robust (non-streaming) submodular maximization [Orlin et al., Bogunovic et al. (PRO)]. The novelty is adapting the non-uniform partition structure to streaming via thresholding. The paper also discusses concurrent work [Mirzasoleiman et al. 2017] with 1/2−ε approximation but higher memory O(mk log k/ε), highlighting a different space–approximation tradeoff.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical gap: robust selection in streaming with limited memory and no re-access to raw data.
- Conceptual synthesis: combining non-uniform partitioning (from PRO) with streaming thresholding.
- Theoretical contribution: constant-factor recovery after arbitrary deletions using only the stored summary; memory scaling O(k log k + m log^2 k).
- Practical: experiments on two applications; shows that a single summary supports multiple queries E; performance near GREEDY in small-scale recommendation; competitive with SIEVE-STREAMING.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Despite the interesting idea, there are many inconsistencies, unclear derivations, and presentation issues that collectively undermine confidence.

Theoretical clarity and correctness
- Threshold τ in Theorem 4.1: As written, “τ = 1 + (1−e−1)(1−e−1/3)(1−1/⌈log k⌉) f(OPT(…))” is dimensionally nonsensical (summing 1 and a function value). It appears a division is missing, presumably τ = f(OPT)/( (1−e−1)(1−e−1/3)(1−1/⌈log k⌉) ), but this must be fixed. The exact scaling of τ is critical to both algorithm and proof.
- Threshold schedule inconsistency: The prose says partition i uses threshold τ/2^i. Algorithm 1, line 5, uses τ/min{2^i,k}, and later proofs (Lemma 4.4) rely on a τ/k condition in the last partition. These need to be reconciled and consistently justified. If min{2^i,k} is intended, it should be used consistently throughout (including the description and lemmas).
- Space bound inconsistency: The paper states O((m log k + k) log^2 k) in the intro and conclusion, but Theorem 4.1 and Eq. (3) imply O(k log k + m log^2 k). These are materially different; please unify and state the tightest bound consistently.
- Dependence on k in constants: c = 0.149(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉) degrades and is undefined for k ≤ 1. The small-k regime (e.g., k=1,2) should be handled or excluded explicitly to avoid divide-by-zero and to clarify guarantees for moderate k.
- Lemma 4.2 sketch is hand-wavy (“on average |B∩E| is relatively small”), but E is adversarial. The argument should be stated precisely (e.g., via pigeonhole principle over the number of full buckets per partition and total deletions m, carefully accounting for w and the number of buckets).
- The “balancing inequalities” step yielding Eq. (4) and then Eq. (5) is terse; the constants α1, α2, and γ, and the choice of τ should be made explicit with exact inequalities and monotonicity arguments. As written, it is difficult to verify.
- The “unknown OPT” variant: the space blow-up is stated as log_{1+ε} k, but the usual range of guesses depends on v_max and k v_max, which yields a factor O(log_{1+ε} k). Please specify the precise range, initialization of guesses, and how τ mapping from guesses aligns with the earlier (corrected) τ formula.

Algorithmic details
- STAR-T-SIEVE at query time: SIEVE-STREAMING itself uses multiple guesses and thresholds; details of its instantiation on S\E (e.g., how thresholds are set, whether it’s single- or multi-guess) are unspecified.
- Runtime/Query efficiency: GREEDY on S\E costs O(k|S|) oracle calls; when m is large, S = Θ(k log k + m log^2 k), which may be large. There is no runtime or throughput data (streaming ingestion rate, per-element processing cost), which is critical for streaming claims.

Empirical evaluation
- Baselines: Not comparing to the concurrent deletion-robust method [18] (even with approximate re-implementation) weakens the empirical case.
- Fairness: In the dominating set experiments, E is sampled from S (not V), which is adversarial to the proposed method. While this can show robustness, it complicates fairness relative to SIEVE-STREAMING on V\E. A complementary experiment where E is sampled from V (and SIEVE-STREAMING is either given E or not) would be informative.
- Sensitivity to m and w: Experiments fix w=1 while the theory requires w ≥ ⌈4⌈log k⌉ m/k⌉. There is no study varying m and w to validate the memory-robustness tradeoff, nor reporting actual memory footprint vs. k and m.
- Metrics: No wall-clock, per-element processing time, or memory curves; only objective values. For streaming work, resource usage is essential.
- Statistical reporting: Only averages are shown; confidence intervals or variance across runs (for random E) are not reported.

Clarity and presentation issues (numerous)
- Notation and formatting:
  - Repeated inconsistent use of ⌈log k⌉ vs dlog ke; at times “log2 k” seems to mean (log k)^2 but is ambiguous as “log base 2”.
  - Section heading “.1 Algorithm without access …” misnumbered.
  - “STreAming Robust - Thresholding” capitalization and hyphenation inconsistent.
  - Stray header “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems…”.
  - References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing venues, years, extraneous commas).
- Algorithm 1: input line has punctuation glitches, and the “break: proceed …” is unconventional; adopt standard pseudocode.
- Several equations lack proper context and assumptions (e.g., base of logarithms, normalization assumptions beyond f(∅)=0).

Questions for the authors
1. Please provide the correct, fully parenthesized expression for τ in Theorem 4.1 and reconcile all threshold definitions (text, Algorithm 1, Lemma 4.4).
2. Unify and prove the claimed space bound, and state it consistently throughout.
3. Can you give a precise, adversarial proof of Lemma 4.2 without “on average” language?
4. Provide runtime and memory measurements, a sensitivity analysis over m and w, and a comparison to [18] if possible.
5. Clarify the small-k behavior (k=1,2) and whether the final bound applies.

Overall assessment
The idea is promising and potentially useful, and the high-level approach is interesting. However, the paper currently suffers from many technical inconsistencies, unclear or possibly incorrect formulae (notably τ), and insufficient empirical validation of the claimed memory–robustness tradeoff. Given the accumulation of issues—several of which affect the core algorithm and guarantees—I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. A revised version that fixes the theoretical inconsistencies, clarifies proofs, and provides stronger and fairer experiments could be much stronger.
