### Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting. The goal is to select a small, robust summary `S` from a massive data stream, such that even after an adversary removes up to `m` elements from `S`, one can still efficiently extract a `k`-sized solution that provides a good approximation to the optimal solution from the original (post-removal) data.

The authors propose a novel two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a streaming algorithm called STAR-T processes the data in one pass to build the robust summary `S`. The core idea of STAR-T is a partitioned structure where elements are assigned to buckets based on their marginal gain against exponentially decreasing thresholds. This elegantly combines ideas from the non-streaming robust algorithm PRO [11] (the partitioned structure) and the streaming algorithm SIEVE-STREAMING [8] (thresholding). In the second, query stage, after `m` elements are removed from `S`, a simple greedy algorithm is run on the remainder of the summary to produce the final solution. The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation guarantee (≈0.149) for this procedure, with a summary size of `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The paper validates this approach on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, showing competitive performance against a strong baseline.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its bold and novel formulation of the *streaming robust submodular maximization* problem. This is a timely and critical problem, as many large-scale machine learning systems must not only handle massive data streams but also be resilient to failures, changing conditions, or adversarial attacks. The proposed two-stage framework (build-once, query-many-times) is a practical and powerful paradigm for such settings, where re-streaming the data is infeasible. This work carves out a new and important direction that sits at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization.

2.  **Elegant Algorithmic Contribution:** The STAR-T algorithm is a clever and non-trivial synthesis of existing ideas into a new, powerful whole. The combination of a partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a significant conceptual leap. It is not merely an extension of SIEVE-STREAMING to the robust setting; it requires a fundamentally new structure and a more complex analysis to handle the interplay between partitions, thresholds, and adversarial removals. This is a creative and insightful approach to a difficult problem.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** Establishing a constant-factor approximation guarantee in this challenging setting is a major theoretical achievement. While the constant itself (0.149) is modest, its existence provides a firm theoretical grounding for the approach. The analysis, which involves reasoning across two distinct cases (partitions being mostly full vs. mostly not-full) and balancing the resulting bounds, appears sound and technically deep. Furthermore, the resulting space complexity, particularly its logarithmic dependence on `k`, is a strong point and compares favorably to concurrent work.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Modest Approximation Constant:** The proven approximation guarantee of 0.149, while a significant theoretical result, is quite low from a practical standpoint. While the experiments suggest performance is much better in practice, the theoretical gap remains large. This is a common trade-off in papers that break new ground, but it is a limitation nonetheless.

2.  **Dependence on Optimal Value:** The algorithm, in its basic form, requires an estimate of the optimal objective value `f(OPT)` to set its initial threshold `τ`. The authors address this with the standard technique of running multiple instances in parallel, which is a correct and accepted solution. However, this increases space complexity by a logarithmic factor and adds a layer of machinery that slightly detracts from the elegance of the core algorithm.

3.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are well-chosen and demonstrate the viability of the approach against a strong baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING with oracle knowledge of removals). The interactive movie recommendation scenario is particularly compelling. However, the validation could be strengthened. For instance, the experiments focus on `w=1` while the theory requires `w` to grow with `m`, and an ablation study on the effect of `w` would have been insightful. Similarly, the exploration of the robustness parameter `m` is somewhat limited. While the current results are promising, a more extensive experimental section would make the practical case for the algorithm even more convincing.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

This work tackles a new, important, and difficult problem with a novel and elegant solution. Its main contribution is conceptual: it provides the first theoretically-grounded framework for performing robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming context. This is precisely the kind of bold, forward-looking research that NIPS should champion.

The paper’s weaknesses—a low theoretical constant and a need for more exhaustive experiments—are characteristic of initial work in a new domain. I am more than willing to overlook these "methodological imperfections" in light of the significance of the new direction being established. The paper is well-written, the core ideas are clear, and the theoretical and empirical results are sufficient to demonstrate the value of the contribution. This work is highly likely to inspire follow-up research on robust streaming optimization.
