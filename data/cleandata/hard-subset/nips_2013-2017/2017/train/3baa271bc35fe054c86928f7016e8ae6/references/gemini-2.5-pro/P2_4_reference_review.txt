### Summary of the Paper
This paper tackles the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The goal is to select a small, robust summary set `S` from a large data stream in a single pass. This summary `S` should be resilient to the removal of up to `m` adversarial elements. In a second, "query" stage, given any removed set `E ⊆ S` of size at most `m`, the algorithm must efficiently produce a `k`-sized solution `Z ⊆ S \ E` that approximates the optimal solution obtainable from the entire dataset minus `E`.

The authors propose a two-stage procedure. The first stage, STAR-T, is a streaming algorithm that constructs the robust summary `S`. Its key innovation is a partitioned structure where elements are assigned to buckets based on a series of exponentially decreasing thresholds. Partitions with high thresholds capture high-value elements, while later partitions with lower thresholds capture elements contributing to diversity and robustness. The second stage, STAR-T-GREEDY, simply runs the standard greedy algorithm on the remaining elements `S \ E` to find the final solution. The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation guarantee (≈0.149) for the final solution, with the summary `S` having a memory footprint of `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The paper also provides strong empirical validation on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, showing that the proposed method is competitive with, and sometimes superior to, a strong baseline (`SIEVE-STREAMING`) that has the unfair advantage of knowing the removed set `E` in advance.

### Strengths
1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and practical problem. Combining the challenges of submodular maximization with both the streaming constraint (single-pass, low memory) and the robustness requirement (resilience to adversarial removals) is a novel and important direction. This two-stage "summarize-then-query" framework is well-motivated for applications where re-scanning the entire dataset is infeasible after a failure or change in constraints.

2.  **Technical Quality (Algorithm and Theory):** The proposed STAR-T algorithm is an elegant and clever extension of the thresholding idea from `SIEVE-STREAMING`. The use of multiple partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds and increasing bucket sizes is a novel design that intuitively balances capturing high-value elements (in early partitions) with maintaining diversity for robustness (in later partitions). The theoretical analysis, leading to a constant-factor approximation guarantee, is a significant contribution. The memory bound of `O((k + m log k) log k)` is particularly noteworthy for its logarithmic dependence on `k`, which compares favorably to the linear dependence in concurrent work [18].

3.  **Technical Quality (Experiments):** The experimental evaluation is a major strength of this paper. The authors make a very strong choice for a baseline comparison: they run `SIEVE-STREAMING` on the data stream *after* removing the adversarial set `E`. This provides the baseline with an oracle, making it an exceptionally tough competitor. The fact that STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably, and in some cases better, than this advantaged baseline provides compelling evidence for the quality of the robust summary `S` that STAR-T constructs. The choice of diverse applications (dominating set and personalized movie recommendation) and adversarial scenarios (random vs. greedy removals, genre filtering) further strengthens the empirical claims.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The problem statement is clear, and the proposed algorithms are presented with sufficient detail in the pseudocode. The intuition behind the partitioned thresholding structure is conveyed effectively. The authors are also upfront about related and concurrent work, clearly positioning their contribution in the literature.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement
1.  **Approximation Constant:** The proven approximation constant of c ≈ 0.149 is quite low. While constant-factor guarantees in this domain are often small and technically challenging to obtain, it would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss whether this constant is an artifact of their proof technique or if they believe it reflects a fundamental limitation of the problem.

2.  **Clarity on Thresholds in Pseudocode:** There is a minor discrepancy between the description of the threshold in the text and in Algorithm 1. The text states the threshold for partition `i` is `τ/2^i`, whereas Line 5 of the algorithm uses `τ/min{2^i, k}`. While these are identical for most partitions (`2^i < k`), they differ for the last partition where `min{2^i, k} = k`. This should be clarified to ensure perfect reproducibility.

3.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice for `w`:** The theoretical analysis requires the memory parameter `w` to scale with `m` and `log k` (`w ≥ ⌈4dlog kem / k⌉`). However, the experiments are run with `w=1` and achieve excellent results, even in settings where the number of removals `|E|` is large. This suggests the practical performance is much better than the worst-case theoretical bound implies. While this is a positive result, the paper would be strengthened by a short discussion on this gap. Acknowledging this and perhaps providing some intuition for why `w=1` works so well in practice would be valuable for the reader.

4.  **Experimental Details:** For reproducibility, it would be helpful to provide more detail on how the initial threshold `τ` was estimated in the experiments, as Section 4.1 describes a parallel-instance approach to handle the unknown `f(OPT)`. Clarifying which value or range of values was used would be beneficial.

### Justification for Decision
Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to large-scale machine learning. It introduces a novel algorithm for a challenging and practically motivated problem. The theoretical results are solid, and the memory complexity is a key advantage over concurrent work. The experimental evaluation is particularly compelling and provides strong evidence that the proposed method is effective in practice. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor, consisting mostly of small points of clarification and potential avenues for discussion that would further improve the paper but do not detract from its core contributions. The work is novel, technically sound, and likely to be influential for researchers working on submodular optimization, streaming algorithms, and robust machine learning.

**Recommendation: Accept**
