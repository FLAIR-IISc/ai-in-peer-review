- Tackles robust submodular maximization in streaming settings via STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm that builds a single-pass robust summary then runs greedy on it.  
- Core contribution: a novel partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholding and a constant-factor approximation guarantee.  
- Empirical validation on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks shows STAR-T is competitive with SIEVE-STREAMING, even when the latter knows removals.  
- Positions work well within prior submodular, robust, and streaming literature and compares memory usage to recent concurrent work.  
- Problem formulation is well-motivated and practically relevant for streaming robustness.  
- Algorithmic innovation: partitioned thresholding effectively balances memory use and robustness.  
- Theoretical guarantees are strong: provides a constant-factor approximation with rigorous analysis.  
- Experiments are convincing and the interactive movie recommendation demonstration is compelling.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized.  
- Performance is sensitive to parameters w and τ, with limited practical guidance for choosing them.  
- Approximating τ via multiple parallel instances is memory-intensive; a single-pass practical estimator is lacking.  
- Scalability to very large datasets and runtime behavior are not thoroughly explored.  
- Stage-2 reliance on a simple greedy algorithm may underutilize the summary; more sophisticated query-stage methods could help.  
- Claims that STAR-T outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING are somewhat overstated and need nuance about conditions.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, promising empirical results, and well-written.  
- Against acceptance: parameter sensitivity, τ estimation practicality, limited scalability analysis, and potentially suboptimal query-stage design.  
- Overall recommendation: strong contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: provide parameter-tuning guidance, scalability/runtime analysis, explore alternative query-stage algorithms, and temper claims about outperforming SIEVE-STREAMING.
