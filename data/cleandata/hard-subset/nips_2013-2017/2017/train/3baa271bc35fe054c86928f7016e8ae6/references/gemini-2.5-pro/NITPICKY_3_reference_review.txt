### Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a novel streaming algorithm called STAR-T processes the data stream to create a summary set `S`. This algorithm is designed to be robust against the removal of up to `m` elements. In the second stage, after an arbitrary set `E` of at most `m` elements is removed, a standard greedy algorithm is run on the remaining summary (`S \ E`) to produce a solution of size `k`.

The STAR-T algorithm cleverly combines two existing lines of work: the partitioned structure for robust summaries from Bogunovic et al. [11] and the thresholding technique for streaming maximization from Badanidiyuru et al. [8]. The summary `S` is organized into partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds, and buckets within partitions have exponentially increasing sizes. The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation guarantee (`~0.149`) for the solution obtained in the second stage, with a memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)`. The authors also provide an experimental evaluation on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, showing that their method performs comparably to or better than the non-robust SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm, even when the latter is given oracle knowledge of the elements to be removed.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of building robust summaries from data streams is both challenging and highly relevant to modern large-scale machine learning. This paper, along with concurrent work [18], appears to be among the first to provide a formal solution with provable guarantees. The algorithmic approach, which synthesizes ideas from non-streaming robust optimization and streaming submodular maximization, is novel and well-motivated.

2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a complete theoretical analysis, culminating in a constant-factor approximation guarantee. A key strength of this result is the space complexity's dependence on `log k`, which compares favorably to the linear dependence in `k` of the concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. [18]. This makes the proposed algorithm more scalable for problems where `k` is large.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are promising. The proposed method demonstrates strong performance, often matching or outperforming a powerful baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING with an oracle). The interactive movie recommendation experiment is a compelling use case that showcases the practical utility of the proposed two-stage framework.

**Weaknesses and Concerns**

Despite its strengths, the paper suffers from numerous issues in clarity, presentation, and technical detail that, in aggregate, significantly detract from its quality.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:**
    *   **Inconsistent Algorithm Description:** There is a critical inconsistency between the textual description of the algorithm and its formal specification in Algorithm 1. The text states that partition `i` has a threshold of `τ/2i`, but Line 5 of Algorithm 1 uses a threshold of `τ/min{2i, k}`. These are not equivalent, and this ambiguity is fundamental to understanding the algorithm's mechanics.
    *   **Confusing Notation:** The notation `dlog ke` is used throughout the paper without definition. Assuming it means `ceil(log_2 k)`, this should be explicitly stated. Similarly, the expression `wdk/2ie` in the proof of Theorem 4.1 is inconsistent with the definition of the number of buckets, `wdk/2ie`, in the algorithm description. Such notational sloppiness makes the theoretical sections difficult to parse and verify.
    *   **Missing Figure:** The text explicitly refers to "Figure 1 for an illustration" of the set `S`, but no such figure is included in the paper. This is a major omission.
    *   **Opaque Theorem Statement:** The definition of `τ` in Theorem 4.1 is presented in a highly convoluted and un-intuitive manner. A simpler statement, such as `τ = C · f(OPT(k, V \ E))` for a specified constant `C`, would be far clearer. The current form obfuscates the core requirement.
    *   **Formatting and Typos:** The paper contains several formatting errors, such as the title of Section 4.1 being ".1 Algorithm...", inconsistent formatting in the references (e.g., the trailing "10," in reference [22]), and an unusual footnote on the first page. While minor individually, their abundance suggests a lack of careful preparation.

2.  **Gaps in Technical Justification and Evaluation:**
    *   **Discrepancy in Parameter `w`:** The theory requires the memory parameter `w` to be at least `⌈ 4dlog kem / k ⌉`. This implies `w` must grow with `m`. However, all experiments are conducted with `w=1`. The paper notes this works well but fails to reconcile this with the theory. The claim that `w=1` "theoretically allows for up to k removals" is unsubstantiated and appears to contradict the paper's own derived lower bound for `w`. This disconnect between theory and practice is a serious concern.
    *   **Mismatch in Removal Model:** The theoretical guarantee holds for the removal of any set `E ⊆ V`. The experiments, however, test a much stronger adversarial model where the removed set `E` is a subset of the algorithm's summary `S`. The authors acknowledge this ("which hurts the performance of our algorithm more"), but this fundamental mismatch makes it difficult to assess how well the experiments validate the theoretical claims.
    *   **Missing Baseline:** A natural and important baseline is absent: running a non-robust streaming algorithm (like SIEVE-STREAMING) on the full stream `V` to generate a summary `S'`, and then evaluating the performance of `GREEDY(k, S' \ E)`. This would directly measure the robustness inherent in standard streaming methods and provide a better context for the contribution of the proposed robust structure.
    *   **Low Approximation Constant:** The derived approximation constant of `0.149` is quite low, especially compared to the `0.5 - ε` guarantees common in related (albeit different) problems. The paper offers no discussion on whether this constant is an artifact of a loose analysis or an inherent limitation of the approach.

**Conclusion and Recommendation**

This paper tackles an important and timely problem with a novel and promising approach. The core ideas are strong, and the improved memory complexity over concurrent work is a significant contribution. However, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication. It is plagued by a substantial number of clarity issues, notational inconsistencies, and unresolved discrepancies between the theoretical analysis and the experimental setup. These issues are not merely cosmetic; they impede a clear understanding of the algorithm and undermine the confidence in the presented results.

Given the stringent standards of NIPS and my detailed examination, I must recommend **rejection**. The paper requires a major revision to address the numerous flaws outlined above before it can be considered a complete and carefully presented piece of scientific work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important, practical problem in large-scale machine learning.
*   Presents a novel algorithm combining ideas from robust and streaming optimization.
*   Provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee with superior memory complexity (`log k` factor) compared to concurrent work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Significant clarity issues, including a critical inconsistency in the algorithm's definition and undefined/inconsistent notation.
*   A major omission (missing Figure 1) and poor formatting throughout.
*   A crucial disconnect between the theoretical requirements for the parameter `w` and its setting in the experiments.
*   A mismatch between the removal model in the theory versus the experiments.
*   Absence of a key baseline to properly contextualize the algorithm's performance.
