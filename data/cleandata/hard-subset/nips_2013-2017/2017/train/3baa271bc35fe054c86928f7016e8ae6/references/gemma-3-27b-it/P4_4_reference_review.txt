This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose a two-stage procedure, STAR-T, consisting of a streaming algorithm for creating a robust summary and a greedy algorithm for querying this summary after element removals. The key contribution lies in the design of a partitioned thresholding scheme within the streaming algorithm and a corresponding theoretical analysis guaranteeing a constant-factor approximation. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing greedy algorithms for large-scale datasets and the need for robustness against element removals. The problem statement is precise and the proposed two-stage setting is a reasonable approach. The authors carefully relate their work to existing literature, citing relevant papers on submodular maximization, streaming algorithms, and robust optimization. The experimental validation is thorough, comparing STAR-T against SIEVE-STREAMING and other baselines in two practical applications – dominating set and movie recommendation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach combined with the robust analysis is a significant contribution. The analysis appears non-trivial and addresses the challenges of maintaining robustness in a streaming environment.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is a strong theoretical result for this problem.
* **Practical Relevance:** The two-stage approach is well-suited for applications where data arrives in a stream and robustness is crucial. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in realistic scenarios.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The algorithm descriptions are clear and concise, and the theoretical analysis is presented in a logical manner.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are well-designed and provide compelling evidence for the performance of the proposed algorithm.  The comparison against SIEVE-STREAMING and other baselines is particularly valuable.
* **Interactive Component:** The discussion of using the precomputed summary for multiple removal scenarios highlights the practical utility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm relies on parameters like *w* and *τ* that require careful tuning. While the paper discusses setting *w*, a more detailed discussion of parameter selection in practice would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:**  While the space complexity is well-analyzed, the time complexity of the streaming stage, especially related to finding the appropriate bucket for each element, could be discussed in more detail.
* **Constant Factor:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149) is relatively small. While theoretically significant, it may limit the practical performance in some cases.
* **Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on the assumption of a normalized monotone submodular function.  The paper doesn't discuss the implications of relaxing these assumptions.
* **Limited Scope of Baselines:** Though a good set of baselines are tested, including more recent and specialized robust submodular maximization algorithms, if available, could strengthen the empirical evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for a challenging problem.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in practical applications.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and comprehensive.
* The contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
* The parameter sensitivity and computational complexity could be potential limitations in practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The proposed algorithm is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically validated. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee and the parameter sensitivity are potential concerns, they do not outweigh the overall strengths of the paper.  I recommend acceptance.
