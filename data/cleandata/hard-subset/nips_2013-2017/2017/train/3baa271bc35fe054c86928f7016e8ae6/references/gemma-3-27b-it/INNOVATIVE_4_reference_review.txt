This paper tackles the important problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, a challenging combination of large-scale data processing, robustness to failures, and theoretical guarantees. The authors propose a two-stage approach – STAR-T for streaming a robust summary and STAR-T-GREEDY for query-time subset selection – and provide a theoretical analysis showing a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The core idea of partitioned thresholding is novel and well-motivated, and the analysis, while complex, appears sound.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of streaming, robustness, and submodular maximization is a compelling problem, and the proposed partitioned thresholding approach is a genuinely novel way to tackle it. The exponential decay of thresholds within partitions is a clever design choice.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** A constant-factor approximation guarantee, even with the stated caveats, is a significant achievement for this problem. The analysis appears rigorous (though a deeper dive would be ideal).
* **Practical Relevance:** The motivating examples (dominating set, personalized recommendation) are relevant and clearly illustrate the practical benefits of a robust streaming solution. The interactive component, where the same summary can be reused for multiple removal scenarios, is particularly appealing.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while not exhaustive, demonstrate that the proposed method performs competitively with, and in some cases outperforms, existing approaches like SIEVE-STREAMING. The fact that performance is maintained even when removing *more* elements than the theoretical robustness parameter is encouraging.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithmic descriptions are clear, and the problem statement is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Constant Factor:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149) is quite small. While theoretically interesting, it raises questions about the practical utility of the guarantee.  It’s unclear how much this constant is driven by the analysis versus inherent limitations of the approach.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The parameter ‘w’ (determining the number of buckets per partition) seems crucial, and the theoretical justification (⌈ 4dlog kem/k ⌉) might be overly conservative.  The experiments suggest w=1 works well, but the theoretical implications of this choice are not fully explored.  The dependence on *m* in *w* is also concerning for very large robustness requirements.
* **Approximation of f(OPT):**  The need to approximate f(OPT(k, V \ E)) initially feels a bit awkward. While addressed in Theorem 4.5, the overhead of maintaining multiple parallel instances to get a good approximation feels potentially costly and could diminish the streaming benefits.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited in scope. More diverse datasets and a more comprehensive comparison with alternative robust summarization methods would strengthen the empirical validation.  The choice of removal strategies (random, greedy) is reasonable but could be expanded.
* **Logarithmic Dependence:** The O((k + m log k) log k) space complexity, while polynomial, may become a bottleneck for extremely large *k* and *m*.  



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is genuinely novel and presents a significant step forward in the area of robust streaming submodular maximization.
* The theoretical analysis, despite the small constant factor, establishes a solid foundation for the approach.
* The empirical results provide encouraging evidence of practical viability.
* The paper addresses an important problem with relevance to a broad range of applications.
* The authors have clearly articulated the limitations of their work and suggested directions for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The small constant factor in the approximation guarantee limits the practical impact of the theoretical result.
* The parameter sensitivity and dependence on *m* in *w* raise concerns about scalability.
* The experimental validation, while positive, is not exhaustive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a significant contribution to the field. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is a concern, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the promising experimental results outweigh this drawback. The authors have tackled a challenging problem with a creative solution. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, prioritizing the innovative approach over absolute methodological perfection.  Further work to improve the constant factor and explore more efficient parameter settings would be valuable.



**Rating:** Accept.
