## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm that first constructs a robust summary of the data in a single pass, and then utilizes a greedy algorithm on this summary to find a representative subset even after a specified number of elements have been removed. The core contribution lies in a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding scheme for the streaming stage, coupled with a theoretical analysis guaranteeing a constant-factor approximation. The paper also provides empirical validation on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, demonstrating competitive performance against existing streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING, even when the latter has foreknowledge of removals.

**Related Work:**

The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature on submodular maximization, robust submodular maximization, and streaming algorithms. It correctly references key works like Nemhauser et al. (1978), Krause et al. (2008), Badanidiyuru et al. (2014), and recent advancements in robust maximization (Orlin et al. 2016, Bogunovic et al. 2017). The comparison to SIEVE-STREAMING and highlighting the difference in memory usage compared to other recent work (e.g., the concurrent work by [18] mentioned in the paper) is particularly valuable.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Formulation:** The problem addressed is both theoretically interesting and practically relevant. The two-stage setting and the need for robustness in streaming scenarios are well-motivated.
* **Algorithmic Innovation:** The partitioned thresholding approach is a clever way to balance memory usage with robustness. The exponentially decreasing thresholding scheme appears well-designed for this purpose.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a strong result, particularly in the streaming setting. The analysis appears rigorous, although the details are relegated to the supplementary material.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide convincing evidence that STAR-T performs well in practice, matching or exceeding the performance of SIEVE-STREAMING on relevant tasks. The interactive movie recommendation scenario is a compelling demonstration of the algorithm’s utility.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem statement and contributions are clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of STAR-T seems highly dependent on the choice of parameters, particularly *w* and *τ*. The paper doesn’t provide clear guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice, beyond suggesting *w* >=  ⌈ 4dlog kem/k ⌉. This could be a barrier to adoption.
* **Practicality of *τ* Approximation:** While the paper acknowledges the need to approximate *τ* (f(OPT(k, V \ E))), the proposed solution of using multiple parallel instances (Theorem 4.5) significantly increases memory usage. A more practical approach for estimating *τ* in a single pass would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the theoretical memory complexity is stated, the practical scalability of STAR-T to truly massive datasets is not thoroughly discussed. The experiments are conducted on reasonably sized datasets; it would be helpful to analyze the runtime performance with larger instances.
* **Focus on Greedy in Stage 2:**  The choice of using a simple greedy algorithm in the query stage seems somewhat limiting.  While it simplifies the analysis, it might not fully exploit the information contained in the robust summary S. Investigating the use of more sophisticated algorithms in stage 2 could potentially yield improved performance.
* **Overly Strong Claims:** The claim that STAR-T outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING "in some cases" is slightly underwhelming given the thorough experimental setup.  A more nuanced discussion of the conditions under which each algorithm performs best would be more informative.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a strong result.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the practical utility of the algorithm.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the core ideas and contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The parameter sensitivity and the practical difficulty of estimating *τ* could limit the algorithm’s real-world applicability.
* The scalability to very large datasets is not fully explored.
* The reliance on a simple greedy algorithm in the query stage might be suboptimal.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of submodular optimization and streaming algorithms.  The authors have presented a well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated solution to a challenging problem. While there are some weaknesses, they do not fundamentally detract from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Recommendations for Revision:**

* **Parameter Tuning:** Provide more detailed guidance on how to choose the parameters *w* and *τ* in practice, perhaps based on dataset characteristics or heuristics.
* **Scalability Analysis:** Discuss the runtime performance of STAR-T with larger datasets and explore potential optimizations for improving scalability.
* **Alternative Query Stage Algorithms:** Briefly investigate the performance of more sophisticated algorithms (e.g., a limited-budget greedy search) in the query stage.
* **Nuance Claims:**  Refine the claims about outperforming SIEVE-STREAMING to more accurately reflect the experimental results.
