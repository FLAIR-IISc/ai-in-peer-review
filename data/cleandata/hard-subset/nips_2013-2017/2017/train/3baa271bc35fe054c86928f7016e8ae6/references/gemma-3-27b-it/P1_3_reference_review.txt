## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of robust streaming submodular maximization. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm, STAR-T, which first constructs a robust summary of the data in a single pass, and then uses a greedy algorithm on this summary to find a solution even after elements have been removed.  The core innovation lies in a partitioned thresholding scheme used during the streaming phase, leading to a provable constant-factor approximation guarantee. The work builds on existing streaming submodular maximization techniques (like SIEVE-STREAMING) and recent advances in robust submodular maximization, offering improvements in memory complexity compared to concurrent work.  The paper includes both theoretical analysis and empirical validation demonstrating competitive performance on real-world datasets.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the limitations of greedy algorithms for large datasets, motivating the need for streaming approaches. It clearly references prior work on submodular maximization, streaming algorithms (SIEVE-STREAMING), and robust submodular maximization (Krause et al. 2008, Orlin et al. 2016, Bogunovic et al. 2017), and differentiates its contributions effectively.  The comparison with the concurrent work by [18] is a strength, highlighting the advantage of the proposed algorithm in terms of memory usage.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding scheme and its accompanying analysis represent a significant contribution.  The combination of these two elements is crucial for achieving the theoretical guarantees.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a strong result, demonstrating the quality of the proposed solution.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant in many large-scale machine learning applications, as highlighted by the illustrative examples (dominating set, recommendation systems).
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both the dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation demonstrate competitive performance against strong baselines, including SIEVE-STREAMING. The interactive experiment showing robustness to different removal scenarios is particularly compelling.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.  The algorithm descriptions are clear.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge and address potential limitations, such as the dependence on knowing or approximating `f(OPT(k, V \ E))`, and provide a solution in Section 4.1.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Constant Factor:** While a constant-factor approximation is achieved, the constant (0.149) is rather small.  This could limit the practical applicability of the algorithm in scenarios where a higher approximation ratio is desired. Investigating ways to improve this constant would be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the choice of `w` (the memory parameter). While the paper provides a theoretical bound for `w`, the experimental results show that `w=1` works well in practice. A more detailed discussion of the impact of `w` and guidelines for choosing it in different scenarios would be helpful.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation, while solid, could be expanded.  Exploring additional datasets and applications would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
* **Proof Details:** While the overall proof sketch is provided, some details are deferred to the supplementary material.  A more concise summary of the key ideas behind the proofs within the main paper would be beneficial for readers less familiar with submodular optimization.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for a challenging problem.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate competitive performance.
* The work builds on and extends existing research in a meaningful way.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
* The algorithm’s performance may be sensitive to parameter tuning (specifically ‘w’).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is a limitation, the theoretical and empirical results are impressive. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and tackles a problem of significant practical relevance.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above (specifically expanding the discussion on parameter selection and summarizing the key proof ideas).

**Confidence:** 8/10
