### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting. The goal is to design an algorithm that processes a massive dataset in one pass using sublinear memory to produce a small, robust summary set `S`. In a subsequent query stage, given an arbitrary removal of up to `m` elements from `S`, the algorithm must efficiently find a `k`-sized solution from the remaining elements that provides a good value for a given monotone submodular function `f`.

The authors propose a two-stage procedure. For the streaming stage, they introduce the STAR-T algorithm, which builds the summary `S`. This summary has a specific structure composed of `log k` partitions. Each partition `i` contains multiple buckets, and is associated with an exponentially decreasing quality threshold `τ/2^i`. Arriving stream elements are placed into the first available bucket for which their marginal gain exceeds the corresponding threshold. For the query stage, the authors propose STAR-T-GREEDY, which simply runs the standard greedy algorithm on the summary `S` after the `m` elements have been removed.

The main theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee of approximately 0.149 for this problem. The STAR-T algorithm produces a summary of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`, which is notably sublinear in the size of the ground set and has a poly-logarithmic dependence on the solution size `k`. The authors also show how to handle the practical issue of not knowing the optimal value a priori by running parallel instances of their algorithm, following prior work. The proposed approach cleverly combines the thresholding idea from streaming submodular maximization (e.g., Sieve-Streaming [8]) with a partitioned summary structure from non-streaming robust submodular maximization (e.g., PRO [11]). The work is validated empirically on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, where it shows strong performance, often matching or exceeding a Sieve-Streaming baseline that is given oracle access to the set of removed elements.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-supported piece of work.
*   **Strengths:** The primary strength is the theoretical guarantee. This is the first constant-factor approximation for the streaming robust submodular maximization problem with memory that depends only poly-logarithmically on `k`. The proof sketch, while dense, follows a logical structure by analyzing two complementary cases and balancing the resulting bounds. The experimental evaluation is strong; comparing against an oracle-endowed Sieve-Streaming algorithm is a high bar, and the proposed method's competitive performance is compelling evidence of its practical utility.
*   **Weaknesses:** The constant approximation factor of 0.149 is quite small from a theoretical standpoint, though the empirical results suggest much better real-world performance. The analysis relies on a careful setting of the parameter `w`, which depends on `m`, `k`, and `log k`, while experiments show `w=1` works well. This gap between the theoretical requirement and practical setting is not discussed.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.
*   **Strengths:** The motivation and problem statement are presented very clearly, and the two-stage setting is easy to understand. The high-level description of the STAR-T algorithm and its partitioned structure (aided by Figure 1) is intuitive. The related work section effectively contextualizes the paper's contribution.
*   **Weaknesses:** The proof sketch in Section 4 is challenging to follow without the supplementary material. The derivation of the final approximation guarantee by "balancing out the two inequalities" is opaque, and the connection between this balancing act and the specific value of `τ` presented in Theorem 4.1 is not explained. There is a minor but potentially confusing inconsistency in the definition of the threshold in Algorithm 1 (line 5: `τ/min{2^i, k}`) versus the prose in Section 3 (paragraph 3: `τ/2^i`). This should be unified.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel and important contribution.
*   **Strengths:** The paper tackles a new and challenging problem at the intersection of streaming, robustness, and submodular optimization. While the building blocks (partitioned summaries, thresholding) are inspired by prior work [8, 11], their synthesis into a single algorithm for this new setting is non-trivial and original. The analysis required to handle the exponentially decreasing thresholds across partitions appears novel. Crucially, the achieved memory complexity is a significant advantage over concurrent work [18].
*   **Weaknesses:** The approach is an adaptation and combination of existing techniques. This is a minor point, however, as the combination is elegant and effective for solving a new problem.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are likely to be influential.
*   **Strengths:** The problem setting is of high practical importance. As datasets grow, the need for single-pass algorithms that produce robust summaries is paramount in numerous applications (e.g., recommender systems, sensor networks, influence maximization). This paper provides the first theoretically-grounded and practical algorithm for this task. Its low memory requirement (poly-log in `k`) makes it particularly attractive. Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale data summarization are likely to use and build upon these ideas.
*   **Weaknesses:** The small theoretical constant might deter some, but the strong empirical results should alleviate these concerns.

### Arguments for Acceptance
1.  **Novel Problem and Setting:** Addresses the important and previously unsolved problem of robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming model.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Result:** Provides the first constant-factor approximation guarantee for this problem.
3.  **Efficient Memory Usage:** The algorithm's memory complexity is a key strength, with only a poly-logarithmic dependency on `k`, making it highly scalable and superior to concurrent work.
4.  **Compelling Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate that the algorithm is practical and competitive with a strong, oracle-aware baseline.
5.  **Elegant Algorithmic Design:** The STAR-T algorithm is an intuitive and clever combination of state-of-the-art ideas from related fields.

### Arguments for Rejection
1.  **Low Approximation Constant:** The theoretical guarantee of `~0.149` is modest, though this is common for worst-case analyses in this area and is offset by strong empirical performance.
2.  **Clarity of Theoretical Sketch:** The proof sketch in the main text is difficult to fully parse, particularly the final steps of the derivation. More intuition could be provided.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement
*   Please clarify the threshold definition used in the algorithm. Is it `τ/2^i` or `τ/min{2^i, k}`? Based on the analysis, the latter seems more likely, but the text should be consistent with the algorithm box.
*   The proof sketch would be significantly improved by adding a sentence of intuition about the final step. Specifically, explain that the bounds from the "full buckets" case and the "not-full buckets" case are functions of `τ`, and the optimal worst-case guarantee is found by choosing `τ` to balance these two competing bounds. Explaining how the value of `τ` in Theorem 4.1 arises from this balancing would make the theorem feel less "out of the blue."
*   It would be valuable to add a brief comment on the discrepancy between the theoretical requirement for `w` (which can be large) and the practical success with `w=1`. This could offer insight into the tightness of the analysis, perhaps suggesting that the worst-case adversarial removals are rare in practice.
*   The memory complexity in Eq. (3) leads to `O(wk log k)`. With `w`'s definition, this seems to be `O(k log k + m log^2 k)`. The paper states `O((k + m log k) log k)`. While likely not a major difference, clarifying the precise bound and its derivation would strengthen the paper.

Overall, this is a very strong paper that makes a significant contribution to an important and timely problem. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
