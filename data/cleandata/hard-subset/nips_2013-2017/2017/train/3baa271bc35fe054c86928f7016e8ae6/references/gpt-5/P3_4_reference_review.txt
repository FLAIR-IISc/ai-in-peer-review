Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting with a cardinality constraint. The authors propose a two-stage approach. In stage 1, STAR-T (Streaming Robust submodular algorithm with Partitioned Thresholding) makes one pass and stores a structured summary S composed of partitions of buckets. Partition i has buckets of capacity min{2^i, k} and uses a threshold that decreases with i; arriving elements are placed into the first non-full bucket whose marginal gain exceeds the partition’s threshold. In stage 2, given any deletion set E of size at most m, STAR-T-GREEDY runs GREEDY on S\E to produce a size-k solution.

The main theoretical result: assuming a guess τ within a constant factor of f(OPT(k, V\E)), STAR-T stores O((k + m log k) log k) elements and, for any |E| ≤ m, STAR-T-GREEDY achieves f(Z) ≥ c f(OPT(k, V\E)) with c ≈ 0.149(1 − 1/⌈log k⌉). When OPT is unknown, a standard geometric guessing scheme (as in SIEVE-STREAMING) yields total memory O((k + m log k) log k log_{1+ε} k), i.e., O((k + m log k) log^2 k) up to constants, and a (c/(1+ε))-approximation.

This extends two lines of work: (i) streaming submodular maximization via thresholding (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING, 0.5−ε with O(k log k) memory), and (ii) robust (non-streaming) submodular maximization via partitioned structures (e.g., PRO [Bogunovic et al. 2017] and Orlin et al. 2016). The independent concurrent streaming-robust paper [Mirzasoleiman et al. 2017] attains a better approximation (≈1/2−ε) but with O(mk log k/ε) memory; this paper trades a smaller constant factor for dramatically improved memory that depends only polylogarithmically on k.

Strengths
- Novel streaming-robust design: the partitioned bucket structure plus decreasing thresholds is a clean extension of sieve-style methods that enables robustness with one pass and sublinear memory.
- Strong memory–approximation trade-off: O((k + m log k) log^2 k) memory (with unknown OPT) vs O(mk) in concurrent work is a substantial improvement when k is large.
- Clear two-stage model with multiple queries: compute S once and answer many deletion queries via GREEDY; this is practically important.
- Solid, careful analysis: the proofs combine (i) guarantees when a partition is mostly full, (ii) complementary bounds tied to the last partition’s not-full bucket, and (iii) a balancing argument on τ, leading to a constant-factor guarantee against adversarial deletions.
- Empirical evidence on two applications (dominating set and personalized recommendation) shows performance comparable to SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows E in advance, and demonstrates interactive reuse of S across different deletions.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Small constant: The approximation factor ≈0.149 is notably below the 0.5−ε of SIEVE-STREAMING (non-robust) and the concurrent robust streaming result, although the latter uses much more memory. It would help to discuss whether analysis is loose (e.g., the 1−e^{-1/3} term) and if stronger constants are plausible.
- τ specification and clarity: Theorem 4.1 states τ using a malformed expression (“τ = 1 / + (1−e−1)(1−e−1/3)(1−1/⌈log k⌉) f(OPT(…))”), which appears to be a typesetting error and obscures how τ should be set to balance the bounds. Please correct and explicitly state the precise τ used in the proof and its intuition.
- Threshold inconsistency: The text states partition threshold τ/2^i, whereas Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Since i goes up to ⌈log k⌉, the last partition differs by a factor ≈2. Please reconcile and explain the intended design; if τ/min{2^i, k} is essential for the analysis (e.g., to ensure f(B) ≥ τ for full buckets), say so earlier.
- Use of w in practice: The theory requires w ≥ ⌈4⌈log k⌉ m/k⌉, linear in m/k, but experiments use w = 1. While the empirical robustness is encouraging, the paper should discuss the practical choice of w, its effect on memory/runtime, and sensitivity to m. Otherwise the gap between theory and practice is unclear.
- Experimental scope and reporting: 
  - Missing comparison to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method [18] (even if independent), at least in a post-deadline note or discussion.
  - No reporting of memory size |S| and query-time cost (number of oracle calls) vs baselines. Since the main contribution is a memory–approximation trade-off, actual space/runtime plots would strengthen the empirical case.
  - The evaluation sometimes samples E as a subset of S, which stresses the method but does not mirror typical deletions in V; present both settings. Also include adversarial E on V.
  - More datasets or larger k would help demonstrate scalability; runtime wall-clock numbers would be useful.
- Proof sketch gaps: The origin of the 1 − e^{-1/3} factor in Lemma 4.3 deserves more intuition (e.g., effective number of “good” selections remaining). Also, the dependence on γ = 4m/(wk) and the balancing argument that yields the final constant could be explained more transparently.

Minor comments and typos
- Notation: clarify whether E ⊆ V or E ⊆ S at query time; the guarantee is stated for E ⊆ V, but Algorithm 2 only sees S\E. Make explicit that the bound compares to OPT(k, V\E).
- Equation (3) uses inequalities that would benefit from a small derivation in the main text.
- In the introduction, the stated memory O((m log k + k) log^2 k) vs Theorem 4.1’s O((k + m log k) log k) can confuse readers; clarify that the extra log factor comes from guessing τ when OPT is unknown (Theorem 4.5).

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important and timely problem (robust streaming submodular maximization) with a principled, simple algorithm.
- Provable constant-factor guarantee against adversarial deletions with near-optimal one-pass and low memory.
- Clear advancement in memory scaling compared to concurrent robust streaming work.
- Practical two-stage workflow with reusable summaries; convincing qualitative experiments.

Cons:
- Low approximation constant and several clarity issues in algorithm/parameter specification.
- Limited empirical evaluation (missing space/time reporting and comparison to concurrent robust streaming baseline).
- Theory–practice gap in the choice of w is not discussed.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a meaningful theoretical and algorithmic contribution with a distinctive memory–approximation trade-off for a problem of clear interest to the NeurIPS community. Addressing the clarity issues (τ expression, threshold definition), discussing the choice of w, and expanding empirical reporting would significantly strengthen the submission.

Reviewer confidence: High.
