This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a challenging setting that combines two practical constraints: the data arrives in a single pass (streaming), and the solution must be robust to the removal of `m` items from the computed summary. The work proposes a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a novel streaming algorithm, STAR-T, processes the data to build a robust summary `S`. In the second stage, after an arbitrary set `E` of `m` items is removed from `S`, a standard greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining summary `S \ E` to produce the final solution.

The core contribution is the STAR-T algorithm, which cleverly constructs the summary `S` using a partitioned structure. The summary is divided into `O(log k)` partitions, where each partition `i` contains multiple buckets and is associated with an exponentially decreasing threshold `τ/2^i`. An incoming stream element is placed into the first available bucket where its marginal gain exceeds the corresponding partition's threshold. This design combines ideas from the streaming literature (thresholding, as in SIEVE-STREAMING) and recent centralized robust optimization work (partitioned structures).

The paper provides strong theoretical backing for this approach. It proves that for a summary of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`, the final solution produced by STAR-T-GREEDY achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of approximately 0.149. The analysis adeptly handles the unknown optimal value by running parallel instances of the algorithm, a standard technique in this area. The work is validated empirically on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, demonstrating that the proposed method performs comparably to or better than the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm, even when the latter is given the advantage of knowing the removed set in advance.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis, with a clear proof sketch provided in the main text and full details in the supplement. The work is presented as a complete contribution, from algorithmic design and theoretical guarantees to empirical validation. The authors are careful in their evaluation, for instance, by noting that the experimental removal strategy is chosen to be particularly challenging for their own method.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The problem statement, algorithms, and theoretical results are presented precisely and are easy to follow. The motivation is compelling, and the illustration of the summary structure (Figure 1) is helpful. The writing provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the work's contributions and methodology.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. It is one of the first to tackle the important problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The algorithmic design, which synthesizes partitioned structures with an exponentially decreasing thresholding scheme, is novel and non-trivial. The paper does an excellent job of positioning its contribution with respect to existing work in streaming submodular optimization, robust optimization, and a concurrent paper [18]. The memory complexity, which depends logarithmically on `k`, is a notable improvement over the linear dependence in the concurrent work.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is of significant practical importance. As machine learning systems increasingly operate on massive data streams, the need for both efficiency (single-pass processing) and resilience (robustness to removals or failures) is paramount. The proposed algorithm is conceptually simple and demonstrably effective, making it likely that these ideas will be used by practitioners and built upon by other researchers. The results meaningfully advance the state of the art in large-scale submodular optimization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel, well-motivated, and challenging problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization.
*   Proposes an elegant and novel algorithm (STAR-T) that effectively combines ideas from different subfields.
*   Provides strong theoretical results, including a constant-factor approximation guarantee and an efficient memory footprint.
*   Presents convincing experimental results on real-world applications that validate the practical utility of the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The proven approximation constant (0.149) is somewhat low, though obtaining any constant factor in this difficult setting is a significant achievement.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is strong, but could be further improved with minor revisions. These are suggestions, not essential changes for acceptance.

1.  **Context on the Approximation Constant:** The theoretical constant of 0.149 is modest, whereas the empirical results are very strong (often near-optimal). It could be beneficial to add a brief discussion on this potential gap. Is the constant a limitation of the analysis technique, or does it reflect a true worst-case scenario?

2.  **Comparison with Concurrent Work:** The paper correctly identifies memory complexity as a key advantage over the concurrent work of Mirzasoleiman et al. [18]. To provide a more complete picture, it would be valuable to also explicitly mention that the approximation factor in [18] (1/2 - ε) is higher. This would give the reader a fuller understanding of the trade-offs between the two approaches.

3.  **Experimental Details:** For maximum clarity and reproducibility, a few experimental details could be clarified.
    *   In the greedy removal experiment (Figures 2(b,d)), it should be specified from which set the elements are greedily removed (e.g., from the summary `S`, or from the solution obtained on `S`?).
    *   The experiments demonstrate impressive robustness with `w=1`, even when the number of removals `m` is large. This suggests the theoretical requirement on `w` might be conservative. A brief comment on this observation would be insightful.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of submodular optimization. It tackles a practical and important problem with a novel algorithm supported by strong theory and experiments.
