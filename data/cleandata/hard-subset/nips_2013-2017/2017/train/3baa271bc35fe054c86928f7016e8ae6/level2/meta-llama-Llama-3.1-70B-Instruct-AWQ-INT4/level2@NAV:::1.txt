This paper presents a novel streaming algorithm, STAR-T, for robust submodular maximization. The algorithm makes one pass over the data and retains a set of size O((k + m log k) log k) elements, where k is the cardinality constraint and m is the robustness parameter. The authors prove that after the removal of any m elements, a simple greedy algorithm that runs on the obtained set achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee for robust submodular function maximization.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm in two different data summarization applications: dominating set and personalized movie recommendation.

The main claims of the paper are:

1. The algorithm STAR-T achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee for robust submodular function maximization.
2. The algorithm makes one pass over the data and retains a set of size O((k + m log k) log k) elements.
3. The algorithm outperforms existing greedy and streaming methods in two different data summarization applications.

The support for these claims is provided through theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors prove the theoretical guarantees of the algorithm and demonstrate its effectiveness in practice through experiments on real-world datasets.

The usefulness of the ideas presented in the paper is evident in the context of large-scale machine learning applications, where data summarization is a crucial task. The algorithm's ability to provide a robust summary that can withstand the removal of up to m elements makes it particularly useful in applications where data is noisy or incomplete.

The paper reflects common knowledge in the field of submodular optimization and machine learning. The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work and clearly explain the contributions of their paper.

The originality of the paper lies in the novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule used in the algorithm. The authors also provide a new analysis that allows them to be robust to any number of removals m as long as they are allowed to use O(m log k) memory.

The completeness of the paper is good, and the authors provide sufficient details for reproducibility. However, some of the proofs are relegated to the supplementary material, which may make it difficult for readers to follow the complete proof.

The limitations of the paper are acknowledged by the authors, who note that the algorithm's performance may degrade if the robustness parameter m is too large. However, the authors provide a solution to this problem by introducing a parallel variant of the algorithm that can handle larger values of m.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the algorithm and its theoretical guarantees. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm in practice, and the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization and machine learning.
