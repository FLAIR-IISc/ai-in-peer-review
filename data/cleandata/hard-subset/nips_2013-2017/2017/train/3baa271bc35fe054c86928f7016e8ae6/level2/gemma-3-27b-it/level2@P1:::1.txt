This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where elements arrive sequentially and a subset of the selected elements may be removed. The authors propose STAR-T, an algorithm based on partitioned thresholding, and provide theoretical guarantees on its performance. The paper is well-written and tackles a relevant and challenging problem.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:** The paper builds upon the existing literature on submodular maximization, particularly streaming algorithms (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular maximization. It correctly identifies the gap between these areas – the need for a streaming algorithm that produces a robust summary. The authors position their work effectively with respect to existing methods like [8, 9, 10, 11] and highlight the benefits of their approach. Specifically, they emphasize a different memory dependence on *k* compared to recent work [18].  The motivation stemming from data summarization, influence maximization, and recommendation systems is clear and well-articulated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of partitioned thresholding and an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule seems novel. The design choices appear well-motivated and geared towards achieving robustness.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the robust streaming submodular maximization problem.  The proof sketch is provided, and the theoretical results are clearly stated.
*   **Experimental Validation:**  The empirical evaluation on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks demonstrates competitive performance against existing algorithms, even when those algorithms have access to prior knowledge about the removed elements.  The exploration of different removal scenarios (random vs. greedy removal) is a strong point.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its analysis. The notation is consistent, and the problem statement is clearly defined.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of the theoretical analysis (requiring knowledge of *f(OPT(k, V \ E))* and propose a parallel approach to approximate it (Theorem 4.5).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The parameter *w*, which controls the memory usage, appears to have a significant impact on the performance. While the theoretical analysis provides a lower bound for *w*, its optimal value in practice might be difficult to determine.  The experiments suggest *w* = 1 works well, but a more thorough investigation of the sensitivity to this parameter would be valuable.
*   **Constant Factors:** While a constant-factor approximation guarantee is provided, the constant itself (0.149) is relatively small.  It might be worthwhile to explore techniques to improve this constant, even if it comes at the cost of increased complexity.
*   **Scalability of Experiments:**  The experiments are conducted on relatively small datasets. While the results are promising, it would be beneficial to evaluate the algorithm on larger, more realistic datasets to assess its scalability.
*   **Connection to Related Work could be stronger:** The paper mentions related work, but the discussion could be more detailed. For example, the connection between the partitioned structure and approaches in [10, 11] could be elaborated upon. A discussion of the trade-offs between the proposed method and other robust maximization techniques beyond the specific papers cited would be beneficial.
*   **Proof Details:** While a proof sketch is provided, the full proofs are relegated to supplementary material. It would be helpful to include more details of the critical steps in the main body of the paper, at least for key lemmas.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a challenging and important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   The theoretical guarantees are strong and demonstrate the quality of the proposed algorithm.
*   The experimental results validate the theoretical findings and demonstrate competitive performance against existing methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
*   The sensitivity to the parameter *w* and the scalability to larger datasets are potential concerns.
*   The connection to related work could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of robust submodular maximization. The proposed algorithm is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider: 1) providing more guidance on selecting the parameter *w* in practice, 2) briefly discussing potential techniques for improving the approximation constant, and 3) elaborating on the connections to related work.
