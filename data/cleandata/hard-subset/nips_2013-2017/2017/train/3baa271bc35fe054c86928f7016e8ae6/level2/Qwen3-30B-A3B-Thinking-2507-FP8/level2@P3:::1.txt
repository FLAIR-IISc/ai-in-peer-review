

This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, where the goal is to find a small representative subset of elements that remains effective even after m elements are removed from it. The authors propose a two-stage approach: a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that makes one pass over the data to construct a robust summary S of size O((k + m log k) log^2 k), followed by a query stage (STAR-T-GREEDY) that runs a simple greedy algorithm on the remaining elements after any m removals. The key theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for the robust streaming problem, achieved through a novel partitioned thresholding structure where buckets in different partitions have exponentially decreasing thresholds.

The paper makes several significant contributions. Theoretically, it extends the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm to the robust setting with a new analysis that allows for robustness against any number of removals m as long as memory scales as O(m log^2 k). The partitioned thresholding approach, where elements are assigned to buckets based on exponentially decreasing thresholds, is a novel technical contribution that enables the robustness guarantee. The experimental evaluation on two data summarization tasks (dominating set and personalized movie recommendation) demonstrates that STAR-T matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, even when SIEVE-STREAMING is allowed to know in advance which elements will be removed. This is a strong empirical validation of the theoretical claims.

The paper is well-written, with clear algorithm descriptions and a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to experimental validation. The connection to previous work is well-established, particularly with respect to robust submodular maximization [9, 10, 11] and streaming submodular optimization [8, 12, 13]. The authors effectively position their work as extending the SIEVE-STREAMING approach to the robust setting with a novel analysis.

There are several strengths that make this work compelling. The problem is well-motivated by practical applications where robustness against element removal is essential (e.g., network failures, user preferences). The two-stage approach is elegant in its simplicity, allowing a single robust summary to be used for multiple different removal scenarios. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful consideration of both the case where a partition has many full buckets and the case where all partitions have mostly empty buckets. The experimental results are convincing and address a practical concern: the robust summary can handle more removals than theoretically guaranteed (e.g., |E| > k in the movie recommendation scenario).

The paper has a few weaknesses that could be addressed. The approximation guarantee (0.149) is weaker than the (1-1/e) guarantee of standard greedy algorithms for the non-robust problem, but the paper does not provide a clear discussion of the tradeoff between robustness and approximation quality. While the paper mentions [18] which has a 1/2 - ε approximation with O(mk log k/ε) memory, it would be helpful to have a more concrete comparison of the memory vs. approximation tradeoff. Additionally, the paper states the memory complexity as O((k + m log k) log^2 k) in the abstract and conclusion, but Theorem 4.1 shows O((k + m log k) log k). This discrepancy should be clarified in the main text.

The significance of the work is high, as it addresses a practical challenge in large-scale data processing where robustness to element removal is increasingly important. The two-stage approach enables a single precomputation that can handle multiple different removal scenarios, which is valuable for interactive applications. The method provides a practical solution to a problem where re-optimization after removals is infeasible, and the experiments show it performs well even when compared to methods with prior knowledge of the removals.

Overall, this paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the field of submodular optimization with a practical algorithm that addresses a relevant problem. The quality of the theoretical analysis is strong, the clarity of the presentation is good, the originality is evident in the partitioned thresholding approach, and the significance is high for applications requiring robustness in streaming settings. The minor weaknesses noted above are relatively small and could be addressed in a revision. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission.
