- Paper proposes STAR-T: a two-stage streaming robust submodular maximization method that builds a compact partitioned summary S in one pass and runs greedy on S\E after up to m deletions.

- Main theoretical guarantee: constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149 (asymptotic in k) with space O((k + m log k) log k) when τ (approx. value of OPT) is known; unknown τ costs an extra log_{1+ε}k factor and (1+ε) loss (stated as O((k + m log k) log^2 k) for constant ε).

- Experiments (dominating set, personalized recommendation) show STAR-T-GREEDY matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when SIEVE is given oracle knowledge of E.

- Methodologically blends partition-based robust selection (e.g., PRO) with streaming thresholding (SIEVE-STREAMING), trading memory dependence for approximation quality.

- Compared to non-streaming robust methods, STAR-T is one-pass and memory-bounded; compared to concurrent deletion-robust streaming, it reduces memory dependence (logarithmic in k vs linear) at the cost of a weaker approximation.

- Theoretical analysis is careful and novel: decreasing-threshold partitioned buckets yield a dichotomy (many full buckets vs sparse last partition) that produces the 0.149 constant.

- Memory and approximation scale reasonably with m; handling unknown τ via parallel guesses is standard and quantified in Theorem 4.5.

- Empirically shows robustness to both random and adversarial deletions and that a single summary can serve multiple post-hoc queries E.

- Inconsistency in reported space bounds: abstract/conclusion claim O((k + m log k) log^2 k) while Theorem 4.1 gives O((k + m log k) log k); the known-τ vs guessed-τ distinction should be made consistent.

- Contradiction about w=1: claim that w=1 allows up to k removals conflicts with the requirement w ≥ ⌈4⌈log k⌉ m/k⌉ (which implies m ≲ k/(4 log k) for w=1).

- Missing runtime/per-element processing analysis: worst-case incoming element may probe many buckets (≈ w k log k); need amortized/worst-case update cost and empirical throughput.

- Concurrent method [18] is only compared theoretically; an empirical comparison (or approximate subset) is needed to substantiate the memory-vs-approximation trade-off claim.

- The 0.149 approximation constant is modest; paper should characterize regimes where practical performance approaches or exceeds non-robust streaming baselines and report sensitivity to m, w, and τ-guess granularity.

- Minor clarity issues: make explicit when τ is assumed known vs unknown and reconcile O(log k) vs O(log^2 k) across sections.

- Algorithm detail discrepancy: Algorithm 1 uses threshold τ/min{2^i, k} while text says τ/2^i; clarify equivalence when i ≥ ⌈log k⌉.

- Request to provide brief cost analysis: space, per-element time, and number of f evaluations; fix typos and harmonize notation.

- Originality: the partitioned thresholding scheme in a streaming robust setting and the balancing-analysis technique are novel and interesting.

- Significance: addresses a timely problem (deletions/right-to-be-forgotten), offers an attractive memory-vs-approximation trade-off and one-pass practicality; empirical results support practicality beyond theoretical bounds.

- Pros: novel, well-motivated design; solid theoretical guarantees; convincing empirical robustness; practical two-stage workflow for multiple queries.

- Cons: relatively small approximation constant; lack of empirical comparison to the concurrent 1/2−ε method; minor inconsistencies and missing runtime/sensitivity analyses.

- Suggestions: unify and clearly separate space/approximation claims for known-τ vs guessed-τ; correct w=1/m relationship; add per-element complexity and empirical throughput; include sensitivity studies over m and w; add empirical comparison to [18] or detailed practical memory-vs-approx discussion; move τ-guessing scheme details into main text.
