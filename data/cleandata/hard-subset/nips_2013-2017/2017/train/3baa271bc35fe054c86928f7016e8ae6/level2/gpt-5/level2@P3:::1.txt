Summary
The paper studies monotone submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint k in a challenging setting that combines streaming access with robustness to the deletion of up to m items after the stream. The proposed two-stage procedure consists of (1) a single-pass streaming algorithm, STAR-T, that builds a compact, deletion-robust summary using a novel partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds; and (2) a query-time phase, STAR-T-GREEDY, that runs GREEDY on the remaining summary after any set E of up to m elements is removed, returning a size-k solution. The analysis shows a constant-factor approximation to OPT(k, V \ E) after arbitrary removals, with overall space O((k + m log k) polylog k). A parallel-guess variant removes the need to know OPT a priori with only logarithmic overhead. Empirically, on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, the approach matches or outperforms strong streaming baselines, including a SIEVE-STREAMING variant that is advantaged by knowing the deleted set in advance.

Relation to prior work
- Streaming submodular maximization without robustness has been addressed by SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., KDD’14), achieving ~0.5-approximation in one pass with O(k log k) space.
- Robust submodular maximization in offline/centralized settings has been explored by Orlin et al. (IPCO’16) and PRO (Bogunovic et al., ICML’17), with partition-based constructions but without streaming constraints.
- Concurrent to this work, a deletion-robust streaming algorithm (Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17) attains 1/2 − ε approximation using O(m k log k/ε) memory; the current paper trades approximation constant for significantly better memory scaling (logarithmic in k and linear in m up to log factors).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Extends greedy-thresholding ideas to a robust streaming setting via a partitioned bucket design with exponentially decreasing thresholds; this combination appears new relative to both SIEVE-STREAMING and offline robust methods.
- Theoretical contribution: A constant-factor guarantee (asymptotically ≈0.149) for any deletions up to m, using one pass and space O((k + m log k) polylog k), with only a logarithmic blowup to remove dependence on OPT. The analysis neatly handles two complementary cases (many full buckets vs. none) and balances bounds to derive the final constant.
- Practical relevance: The two-stage summary enables multiple post-hoc queries for different removals E without re-accessing the stream, aligning with “right to be forgotten” and interactive personalization scenarios.
- Empirical validation: On two real tasks, performance is comparable to or better than SIEVE-STREAMING that knows the deletions in advance, and robust even when |E| exceeds k.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Inconsistencies in threshold specification: The text describes partition thresholds τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Please reconcile and justify the exact threshold rule; this affects both analysis and reproducibility.
- Space bound discrepancy: Section 4 suggests O((k + m log k) log k) elements (Eq. 3 with w ≈ (m log k)/k), whereas the abstract and conclusion state O((k + m log k) log^2 k). Clarify which bound is tight, and where the extra log factor arises (e.g., from parallel guesses for τ).
- τ definition in Theorem 4.1 appears dimensionally unclear due to missing parentheses; it reads as “τ = 1 + (…) f(OPT(…))”. Please provide the exact expression and its derivation.
- Experimental reporting: While quality metrics are strong, the study lacks explicit measurements of memory footprint (|S|) and running time/objective evaluations, and sensitivity to m, k, and the memory parameter w. Since the main claims are about robustness and space, these metrics are critical.
- Baseline coverage: The paper favorably compares to SIEVE-STREAMING (with knowledge of E) and random, and also reports STAR-T-SIEVE. A discussion or empirical comparison to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming approach (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2017) would contextualize the trade-off between approximation factor and memory.
- Fairness/setup details: Experiments remove elements from S (harsher for the proposed approach), while SIEVE-STREAMING runs on V \ E with knowledge of E. This is commendably conservative; nonetheless, clearly documenting stream order, one-pass constraints, and space limits for each method would strengthen the empirical case.

Quality
The algorithm is well-motivated and the analysis is careful, with a clear two-case structure and use of standard GREEDY bounds. The parallel-guess construction is standard and appropriate. The primary technical caveats are the threshold and space-bound inconsistencies noted above.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear problem statement and algorithms. Addressing the noted formula inconsistencies and tightening descriptions of parameters (w, τ) and their practical settings would materially improve clarity and reproducibility. Including pseudocode for the parallel-guess variant and an explicit statement of time/space complexities would help.

Originality
The partitioned thresholding scheme for robust streaming, and its analysis yielding constant-factor robustness with near-linear space in m (up to logs) and logarithmic in k, appear novel relative to prior offline robust approaches and non-robust streaming algorithms.

Significance
The problem is timely and relevant; the approach enables practical robust summarization with single-pass constraints and multiple post-hoc queries. The theoretical and empirical results advance the state of the art, offering a useful new point in the memory–approximation trade-off.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- New algorithmic design for robust streaming with a provable constant-factor guarantee.
- Favorable memory scaling in k and m compared to concurrent robust streaming work.
- Single-pass summary supports multiple deletion queries without revisiting the stream.
- Solid empirical performance on two real tasks, despite a conservative evaluation setup.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Inconsistencies in the algorithm thresholds and space bounds need correction.
- Limited reporting on space/time and sensitivity; lack of comparison to concurrent robust streaming method.
- Approximation constant (≈0.149) is modest; discussion on practical impact vs. theory could be expanded.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Reconcile and correct the threshold rule (text vs. Algorithm 1) and provide the precise τ expression in Theorem 4.1.
- Clarify the asymptotic space bound (log vs. log^2 factors), separating core STAR-T space from the overhead of parallel guesses.
- Report empirical memory usage (|S| as a function of k, m, w) and running time/objective evaluations for all methods; include sensitivity analyses over m, k, and w.

Optional but beneficial:
- Provide a discussion and, if feasible, an empirical comparison with the concurrent deletion-robust streaming approach (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2017), highlighting the memory–approximation trade-off.
- Include ablations of STAR-T-GREEDY vs. STAR-T-SIEVE across tasks, and show performance for |E| varying around and beyond m.
- Describe how τ is instantiated in practice (parallel guesses, step sizes, stopping criteria), and whether adaptive thresholding without parallel copies is viable.
