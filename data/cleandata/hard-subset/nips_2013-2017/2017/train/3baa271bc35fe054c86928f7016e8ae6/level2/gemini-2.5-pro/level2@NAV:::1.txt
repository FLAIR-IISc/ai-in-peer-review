This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function under a cardinality constraint in a setting that is simultaneously streaming and robust to adversarial deletions. The authors propose a two-stage procedure: a single-pass streaming algorithm, STAR-T, which builds a robust summary, followed by a simple greedy algorithm, STAR-T-GREEDY, which selects the final k-element set from the summary after `m` elements have been removed. The core technical contribution is the design of STAR-T, which cleverly builds a summary organized into partitions with exponentially sized buckets and exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds. This work synthesizes ideas from streaming submodular maximization (e.g., the thresholding of SIEVE-STREAMING) and non-streaming robust maximization (e.g., the partitioning structure from Bogunovic et al., 2017).

The paper's strengths are significant. First, the problem formulation is timely and practical, as many large-scale data summarization tasks require both streaming processing and resilience against unforeseen changes or failures. Second, the primary theoretical contribution, Theorem 4.1, provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee for this challenging setting, supported by a non-trivial analysis. The memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)` is notably efficient, particularly its logarithmic dependence on `k`. Third, the theoretical claims are validated with compelling experiments on two distinct applications: dominating set and personalized movie recommendation. The comparison against SIEVE-STREAMING, an already strong baseline that is given the advantage of knowing the removed elements in advance, effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed robust summary structure. The paper is well-written and the algorithm is presented clearly.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses. The theoretical approximation constant of 0.149 is modest, although achieving any constant factor in this setting is a strong result. A brief discussion on the potential gap between this theoretical bound and the excellent empirical performance would be insightful. Furthermore, the main algorithm requires an estimate of the optimal value `f(OPT)`, a common issue in threshold-based methods. While the authors present a standard solution using parallel instances in Theorem 4.5, the main experiments do not appear to use this version. Discussing the practical overhead of this parallelization or its effect on the parameter `τ` in the experiments would strengthen the paper’s completeness.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The combination of a strong theoretical result with convincing empirical validation on a practical problem makes it a clear candidate for acceptance.
