Summary
The paper studies robust monotone submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in the streaming setting, where, after a single pass over the data, up to m items may be removed and one must still extract a good size-k solution using only the retained summary. The authors propose STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm that partitions a summary into exponentially sized buckets across partitions, with exponentially decreasing thresholds. An element is placed into the first non-full bucket whose marginal exceeds the bucket’s threshold. The resulting summary S has size O(k log k + m log^2 k). At query time, after any removal set E with |E| ≤ m, running GREEDY only on S \ E (STAR-T-GREEDY) achieves a constant-factor approximation: as k grows, f(Z) ≥ 0.149·f(OPT(k, V \ E)). When f(OPT) is unknown, a parallel version maintains multiple τ values (as in SIEVE-STREAMING), incurring a log1+ε k factor in space and a (1 + ε) loss. Experiments on dominating set (Twitter/Amazon graphs) and personalized movie recommendation show that STAR-T-GREEDY matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, even when the latter is told the removed set in advance, and that a single summary supports multiple removals.

Strengths
- Problem importance and fit: Robust summarization under deletions is practically motivated (failures, preferences, “right to be forgotten”) and aligns with NIPS interests in scalable submodular optimization.
- Algorithmic novelty: The partitioned, exponentially decreasing thresholding design is a clean streaming extension of partitioned robustness ideas (e.g., PRO) and thresholding (SIEVE-STREAMING).
- Theory: Single-pass, memory O(k log k + m log^2 k), and a constant-factor approximation after arbitrary removals are nontrivial. The analysis carefully handles two regimes (many full buckets vs. none) and balances complementary bounds to obtain the guarantee. The space dependence on k is only logarithmic for the m term (vs. linear in concurrent work).
- Practicality: Query-time reuse of the same summary for different removals is compelling. Experiments on two tasks demonstrate competitive or better performance versus strong baselines.
- Clarity of high-level ideas and relation to prior work: The paper situates itself well with classic greedy [Nemhauser et al.], streaming [Badanidiyuru et al.], robust non-streaming [Orlin et al.; Bogunovic et al.], and concurrent deletion-robust streaming [Mirzasoleiman et al.].

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Approximation constant: The 0.149 factor is modest (and degrades by 1/(1 + ε) in the τ-free variant). While constants are often pessimistic, a discussion of tightness or avenues for sharpening (e.g., refined analysis of the “not-full” case, or stronger post-removal selection than plain GREEDY) would help.
- τ selection and overhead: The practical procedure for maintaining multiple τ’s (ε choice, number of instances, computational overhead per element) is not detailed in experiments. Please report the number of parallel instances used, wall-clock overhead, and sensitivity to ε.
- Parameter w and robustness trade-offs: Theory requires w ≥ ⌈4 m log k / k⌉, yet experiments use w = 1, even when removing up to 2k elements. This is informative but leaves practitioners without guidance. Please add plots varying m and w to show memory–robustness–quality trade-offs and identify minimal w that preserves quality for given m.
- Baselines: The concurrent deletion-robust streaming algorithm [Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17] achieves a 1/2 − ε approximation with higher memory O(m k log k/ε). An empirical comparison (even on smaller instances due to memory) would strengthen the paper, or at least a more quantitative discussion of regimes where STAR-T’s memory advantage dominates despite the weaker constant.
- Clarity issues:
  • Threshold rule: The text says partition i uses threshold τ/2^i, whereas Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. It would help to unify the description and emphasize that the latter ensures a full bucket has value ≥ τ.
  • Space bound statements vary: the abstract/conclusion mention O((k + m log k) log^2 k), while Theorem 4.1 implies |S| = O(k log k + m log^2 k) = O((k + m log k) log k). Please standardize the expression and highlight the different scaling for the k and m terms.
  • Time complexity: State per-element update time. Scanning all buckets implies O(∑i w·(k/2^i)) = O(w k) = O(m log k) with the theoretical w, which is favorable. Reporting empirical throughput would be useful.
- Experimental details: Please clarify streaming order, τ instantiation in practice, whether lazy evaluations are used for GREEDY, and provide variance/error bars. Releasing code would enhance reproducibility.

Quality
The theoretical contributions are sound and carefully argued, with appropriate handling of edge cases. Assumptions are standard (monotone submodular). Experiments, while not exhaustive, are thoughtful and use realistic scenarios (including adversarial-style deletions via greedy removal).

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Address the noted notational/statement inconsistencies and expand on practical aspects (τ, w, complexity) to aid adoption.

Originality
The combination of partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds for streaming robustness appears novel. The analysis that yields memory O(k log k + m log^2 k) and a constant-factor post-deletion guarantee is a meaningful advance over prior streaming work.

Significance
The method offers a practical and theoretically grounded approach to robust streaming summarization with attractive space bounds. Given the breadth of submodular applications, this can influence practice, especially where post-hoc deletions are common.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple algorithm with single pass and robust guarantees
- Strong and novel theoretical analysis with favorable space dependence
- Practical query-time reuse; convincing experiments on two tasks
- Good positioning with respect to prior and concurrent work

Cons against acceptance
- Weak constant in the approximation factor
- Missing empirical comparison to the concurrent 1/2 − ε algorithm
- Limited guidance on parameter selection (τ, w) and computational overhead

Questions for authors
1) How many τ-instances were used in practice, and what was the resulting per-element processing time and memory? How sensitive is performance to ε?
2) Can STAR-T-GREEDY be replaced by a more efficient or stronger post-processing (e.g., lazy greedy, stochastic greedy) without affecting theory?
3) Could you provide experiments varying m and w to validate the theoretical scaling?
4) Is there a way to adaptively tune τ online (e.g., doubling/halving schemes) to reduce the number of parallel instances?

Overall recommendation
A solid and timely contribution to robust streaming submodular maximization with novel ideas, rigorous analysis, and promising empirical results. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarity and experimental-detail concerns.
