

This paper presents a novel approach to robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, addressing the problem of finding a summary set that remains effective even after m arbitrary elements are removed. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm: STAR-T for the streaming phase and STAR-T-GREEDY for the query phase. The key technical contribution is a partitioned thresholding structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds, which enables the algorithm to maintain a robust summary of size O((k + m log k) log^2 k) while providing a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) after any m removals.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough discussion of related work that properly contextualizes the contribution. The authors effectively connect their work to recent advances in streaming submodular maximization (SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular optimization (PRO, [11]), highlighting how their approach differs through the partitioned thresholding structure. The theoretical analysis is sound, with careful proof sketches for the main results, though the approximation factor of 0.149 is notably lower than the 0.5-ε guarantee of SIEVE-STREAMING, which may raise practical concerns.

The experimental validation in two applications (dominating set and personalized movie recommendation) is convincing, demonstrating that the method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows in advance which elements will be removed. This is a strong empirical contribution that validates the practical utility of the robustness guarantee. The interactive aspect of the method (using the same precomputed summary for different removals) is particularly compelling for real-world applications.

However, several issues need to be addressed before the paper can be accepted. First, the theoretical memory bound O((k + m log k) log^2 k) appears to be significantly larger than the O(k log k) bound of SIEVE-STREAMING, and the paper doesn't sufficiently justify this trade-off. The authors should provide a more detailed comparison of the memory requirements, especially since the paper claims "short but robust summary" but the bound has a log^2 k factor that seems excessive. 

Second, the paper mentions a concurrent work [18] with a similar problem formulation but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of the two approaches. The authors should clarify how their method differs from [18], particularly regarding the memory requirements (O(mk log k/ε) vs. O((k + m log k) log^2 k)) and the approximation guarantees (1/2-ε vs. 0.149).

Third, while the robustness guarantee is theoretically interesting, the paper doesn't sufficiently motivate why robustness against m removals is necessary in practice. The authors should provide more context about realistic values of m in real-world applications and how the robustness requirement affects the trade-off between memory usage and solution quality.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to a meaningful problem, but the practical significance is somewhat diminished by the relatively low approximation factor and the memory overhead compared to existing methods. The authors should strengthen the paper by:

1) Providing a more detailed comparison with the concurrent work [18] to clarify the relative advantages and disadvantages of each approach.
2) Explaining why the log^2 k factor in the memory bound is necessary and whether it can be improved.
3) Better justifying the practical relevance of the robustness requirement by discussing realistic values of m in application domains.

Overall, this is a well-executed theoretical contribution that addresses an important problem, but the practical significance could be strengthened through better contextualization of the theoretical guarantees and a more detailed comparison with related work. The experimental results are strong, but the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between robustness, memory usage, and approximation quality.
