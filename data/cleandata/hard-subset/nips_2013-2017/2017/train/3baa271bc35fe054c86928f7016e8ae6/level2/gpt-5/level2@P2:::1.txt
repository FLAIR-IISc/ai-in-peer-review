Summary
The paper addresses robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting with a two-stage procedure. In the streaming stage, STAR-T makes a single pass and stores a compact, robust summary S arranged into exponentially sized partitions of buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds. In the query stage, after an arbitrary removal E of up to m items from S, a standard greedy algorithm is run on S\E to produce a size-k solution. The main theoretical result shows a constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149 (asymptotically in k) using space O((k + m log k) log k) when an approximation τ to f(OPT(k, V\E)) is known; when τ is unknown, a parallel-guessing variant increases space by a factor log_{1+ε} k and loses a (1+ε) factor, leading to O((k + m log k) log^2 k) space for constant ε. Experiments on dominating set and personalized recommendation show that STAR-T-GREEDY matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, even when the latter is given the advantage of knowing E in advance.

Relation to prior work
The approach blends ideas from partition-based robust submodular selection (e.g., PRO [Bogunovic et al., ICML’17]) and streaming thresholding (SIEVE-STREAMING [Badanidiyuru et al., KDD’14]). Compared to non-streaming robust methods [Orlin et al., IPCO’16; Bogunovic et al., ICML’17], STAR-T is one-pass and memory-bounded. Compared to concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17], STAR-T trades a smaller memory dependence (logarithmic in k rather than linear) for a weaker approximation constant (≈0.149 vs 1/2−ε). The paper situates itself well in the submodular streaming and robustness literature and clearly articulates its memory/approximation trade-offs.

Quality
Theory:
- The analysis is careful and novel: the partitioned-bucket structure with decreasing thresholds enables a dichotomy—either one partition has many full buckets (yielding a good full bucket surviving deletions by an averaging argument), or the last partition is sparse and one can bound the loss via complementary lemmas. The resulting 0.149 constant is derived by balancing the two bounds.
- The memory and approximation results are reasonable and scale favorably with m relative to some prior robust methods.
- The dependence on an unknown τ is handled via parallel guesses in a standard manner; Theorem 4.5 quantifies the overhead.

Empirics:
- Two representative tasks demonstrate robustness to both random and adversarial (greedy) deletions. Comparisons to SIEVE-STREAMING (with oracle knowledge of E) are fair and informative; STAR-T often matches or slightly beats it.
- The experiments convincingly show that a single robust summary can serve multiple queries E.

Concerns / potential issues:
- Inconsistency in memory bounds across sections: abstract/conclusion state O((k + m log k) log^2 k) while Theorem 4.1 states O((k + m log k) log k). The intended distinction (known τ vs guessed τ) should be made explicit consistently.
- The statement in Section 5.2 that w=1 “theoretically allows for up to k removals” appears inconsistent with the requirement w ≥ ⌈4⌈log k⌉ m/k⌉ (which implies for w=1 that m ≲ k/(4 log k)). Please clarify.
- No runtime/per-element processing analysis: in the worst case, each incoming element may probe many buckets (on the order of wk log k). A discussion of the amortized and worst-case update cost (and empirical throughput) would strengthen the practical contribution.
- The concurrent method [18] is only discussed theoretically; an empirical comparison (even approximate or on a subset) would better support the claimed memory advantage trade-off.
- The constant 0.149, while nontrivial, is modest; it would help to characterize regimes where the practical performance approaches or exceeds non-robust streaming baselines and to report sensitivity to m, w, and τ-guess granularity.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The intuition behind the structure and thresholds is clear. Pseudocode is helpful. Minor clarity issues:
- Make explicit throughout when τ is assumed known; when unknown, consistently state the resulting space and approximation factors (and reconcile O(log k) vs O(log^2 k) factors across abstract, main text, and conclusion).
- In Algorithm 1, the threshold is τ/min{2^i, k}; earlier text says τ/2^i. Clarify equivalence for i ≥ ⌈log k⌉.
- Provide a brief cost analysis (space, per-element time, number of f evaluations).
- Fix typos (e.g., “st Conference …”, “peformance”) and harmonize notation.

Originality
The partitioned thresholding scheme in a streaming robust setting is, to my knowledge, novel and interesting, combining partition-based robustness with streaming sieve ideas in a nontrivial way. The analysis technique (balancing two complementary lemmas via the last partition vs many full buckets) is also original.

Significance
The problem is timely and relevant (robustness under deletions/right-to-be-forgotten, interactive recommendations). The memory-vs-approximation trade-off is attractive for large-scale systems, and the one-pass property is valuable. The empirical evidence suggests the method is practical and robust beyond the proven bounds.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Novel and well-motivated algorithmic design for robust streaming submodular maximization.
- Solid theoretical guarantees with clear memory-approximation trade-offs.
- Empirical results on real datasets showing strong performance versus competitive baselines, even under adversarial deletions.
- Practical two-stage workflow enabling multiple post-hoc queries E from a single summary.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Approximation constant is relatively small; no empirical comparison to the concurrent 1/2−ε robust streaming method.
- Minor inconsistencies and clarity issues on space bounds and parameter regimes (w, m).
- Lack of runtime analysis and ablation on sensitivity to m, w, and guessing granularity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Unify and clearly separate the space bounds and guarantees for known-τ vs guessed-τ variants across abstract, main text, and conclusion; correct the w=1/m relationship.
- Add per-element processing complexity and empirical throughput; report number of f evaluations.
- Include a sensitivity study varying m and w (including cases approaching the theoretical limit for w=1).
- If feasible, add an empirical comparison to [18], or at least a more detailed discussion of practical memory usage vs approximation in typical regimes.
- Move key aspects of the τ-guessing scheme (range, grid, stopping criteria) from appendix into the main text.

Overall, this is a strong and relevant contribution with a clear algorithmic idea, rigorous analysis, and convincing experiments. With minor clarifications and additional empirical characterization, it would make a solid addition to NIPS.
