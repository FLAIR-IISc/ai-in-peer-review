## Review of Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach

This paper tackles the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where elements arrive sequentially and a subset of the selected elements may be removed. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm employing a partitioned thresholding approach to maintain a robust summary of the data. This summary allows for efficient re-selection of a near-optimal subset even after elements are removed.

The core idea of partitioning the summary space and using exponentially decreasing thresholds is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is a significant contribution. The paper clearly articulates the problem, relates it to existing work in streaming submodular maximization and robustness, and positions its contribution effectively. The inclusion of experiments on both the dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation strengthens the paper, demonstrating practical applicability and competitive performance against existing methods like SIEVE-STREAMING. Notably, the experiments show STAR-T-GREEDY matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter has prior knowledge of the removed elements.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues. The notation is dense, and the explanation of the partitioning structure could be more intuitive. While the theoretical results are strong, the constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small, and the dependence on parameters like `dlog ke` might limit practical applicability for very large `k`. The discussion of the parameter `w` and its impact on performance could be expanded. Furthermore, the experimental section, while demonstrating good performance, lacks a detailed ablation study to understand the impact of different parameter settings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to robust streaming submodular maximization.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees (constant-factor approximation).
*   Empirically validated performance on relevant tasks.
*   Clear problem definition and connection to related work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity of explanation could be improved.
*   Small constant factor in the approximation guarantee.
*   Limited ablation study in the experimental section.

**Overall:**

This is a solid contribution to the field. While there are areas for improvement in terms of clarity and experimental analysis, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the promising experimental results make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand the experimental evaluation. The authors should consider providing more intuition behind the partitioning scheme and exploring the sensitivity of the algorithm to parameter choices.
