

This paper introduces a novel approach for robust submodular maximization in streaming settings, where the goal is to maximize a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint k, while ensuring the solution remains robust against the removal of up to m elements from the memory. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm: STAR-T for the streaming phase that constructs a robust summary, and STAR-T-GREEDY for the query phase that produces a solution from the remaining elements after removal. The theoretical analysis shows a constant-factor approximation guarantee (approaching 0.149 as k → ∞), with the summary size being O((k + m log k) log² k).

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, building on submodular streaming work (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., [11]). The key novelty lies in their partitioned thresholding structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule, which enables robustness to any number of removals m while maintaining logarithmic dependence on k in the memory requirement. This contrasts with concurrent work [18] that has linear dependence on k.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful handling of two cases (when a partition has many full buckets vs. when no partition has many full buckets). The proofs are well-structured and detailed in the supplementary material. The experimental validation in two practical applications (dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation) is convincing, demonstrating that the method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the baseline knows in advance which elements will be removed. The results are statistically significant (averaged over 100 iterations), and the paper provides a useful demonstration of how a single robust summary can serve multiple query scenarios.

Clarity is excellent. The paper is well-organized with clear notation, and the main algorithm (STAR-T) is explained with a helpful illustration (Figure 1). The partitioning structure and thresholding rules are well-explained. The supplementary material provides detailed proofs, which is valuable for readers seeking to verify the theoretical claims. The writing is accessible to the NIPS audience, with sufficient background on submodular functions and related work.

The originality is significant. While building on previous work, the paper introduces a novel partitioned thresholding approach that addresses the robust streaming submodular maximization problem in a fundamentally different way than prior approaches. The key insight is the exponentially decreasing thresholding rule that enables the robustness guarantee with minimal memory overhead. This represents a meaningful advance beyond the state of the art in robust submodular maximization.

The significance of the work is high. The problem of robust data summarization is increasingly important in real-world applications where representative sets must withstand element removals (e.g., network failures, user preferences). The authors demonstrate practical utility in two meaningful applications, and the theoretical guarantee of a constant-factor approximation with reasonable memory requirements makes this work valuable for practitioners dealing with large-scale data.

A few minor concerns: The approximation ratio of 0.149 is lower than non-robust streaming methods (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING's 0.5-ε), though this is expected given the added robustness requirement. The paper assumes knowledge of f(OPT(k, V\E)) for the threshold parameter τ, which is not practical. While the authors address this with a parallel variant (Theorem 4.5), the space complexity increases by a factor of log1+ k, which might be significant for very large datasets. It would be helpful to have more details on the runtime comparison between the proposed method and baselines.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with a novel solution. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. The authors have made a significant contribution to robust submodular maximization in streaming settings, with clear theoretical and practical implications. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
