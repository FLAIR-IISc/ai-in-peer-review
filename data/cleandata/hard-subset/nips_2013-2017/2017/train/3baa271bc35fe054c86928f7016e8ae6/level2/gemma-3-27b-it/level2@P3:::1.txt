This paper addresses the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, a challenging problem with applications in data summarization where robustness to element removal is crucial. The core contribution is the STAR-T algorithm, which utilizes a partitioned thresholding approach to maintain a robust summary of the stream, and a subsequent greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) to generate a solution after removing elements. The work provides theoretical guarantees on the approximation ratio and demonstrates competitive performance against existing methods on several datasets.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The problem statement is well-motivated, and the connection to real-world applications is convincingly made. The introduction effectively highlights the challenges of streaming and robustness, setting the stage for the proposed solution. The related work section provides a good overview of relevant research, positioning the contribution within the existing literature and accurately citing prior work, including recent results like those in [18]. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the experimental results are well-presented and support the claims made in the paper. The inclusion of experiments on both a graph-based problem (dominating set) and a recommendation task strengthens the evaluation.

A key strength of the work is the introduction of the partitioned thresholding structure and its associated analysis. The exponentially decreasing thresholds seem crucial for achieving the robustness guarantee. The algorithm’s ability to maintain a relatively small summary while providing robustness is commendable. Furthermore, the ability to re-use the summary for different removal sets is a significant advantage in interactive scenarios.

However, there are areas where the paper could be improved. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the constant factors involved in the approximation guarantee are relatively small (specifically, the 0.149 factor). Investigating methods to improve these constants or providing a more nuanced understanding of their implications could be valuable.  The discussion of the parameter ‘w’ could be clarified. While the paper states w ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem
k ⌉, the experiments demonstrate good performance with w=1, creating a potential disconnect.  An explanation of why w=1 works well in practice, despite the theoretical bound, would be insightful.

The presentation of the experimental results could also benefit from more detailed statistical analysis. Reporting confidence intervals or p-values for the performance comparisons would strengthen the conclusions drawn from the experiments.  The choice of baselines is appropriate, but a comparison to more recent or specialized algorithms for each application (e.g., more advanced influence maximization algorithms) could further demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.

Arguments for acceptance:
* The problem addressed is important and relevant to current research trends in machine learning.
* The proposed algorithm is novel and well-motivated.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the approximation ratio.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance against existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

Arguments against acceptance:
* The constant factors in the approximation guarantee are relatively small.
* The connection between the theoretical bound on 'w' and the observed performance in experiments (w=1) isn’t fully explained.
* The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more detailed statistical analysis. 

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work. Addressing the suggested improvements would further enhance the paper’s impact.  This reviewer recommends acceptance.
