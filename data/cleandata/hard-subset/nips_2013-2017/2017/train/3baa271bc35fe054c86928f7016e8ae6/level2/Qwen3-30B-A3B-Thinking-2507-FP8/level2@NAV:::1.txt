

This paper introduces a novel approach to robust submodular maximization in streaming settings, addressing the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint k, where elements arrive in a stream and m elements may be removed from the summary after processing. The authors propose STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm that constructs a robust summary of size O((k + m log k) log k), followed by STAR-T-GREEDY, a greedy algorithm that runs on the remaining elements after removals to achieve a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149).

The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the SIEVE-STREAMING framework to the robust setting through a partitioned thresholding approach. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with Theorem 4.1 providing a solid approximation guarantee that improves upon related work (e.g., [18]) by reducing the memory dependence on k from linear to logarithmic. The experimental results on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks demonstrate that STAR-T-GREEDY matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows the removed elements in advance, which is a strong practical validation of the approach.

The paper excels in technical quality, with well-structured theoretical proofs and comprehensive experiments. The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with clear algorithm descriptions and intuitive explanations of the partitioning structure. The novelty is evident in the partitioned thresholding mechanism, which is distinct from prior robust submodular maximization approaches [9, 10, 11].

The significance of this work is high, as it solves a practically important problem in large-scale machine learning where robustness against element removal is critical. The two-stage approach is particularly valuable for applications requiring precomputation of robust summaries that can handle multiple query scenarios.

One limitation is the relatively low approximation factor (0.149) compared to non-robust streaming methods (which achieve ~0.5). While the paper acknowledges this trade-off, it would benefit from more discussion on how this factor impacts practical applications. The authors could also provide more intuition about why their partitioning scheme achieves better robustness with lower memory requirements.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field, with well-supported claims, novel technical insights, and compelling experimental validation. The paper makes a significant step toward practical robust submodular maximization in streaming settings and should be accepted.
