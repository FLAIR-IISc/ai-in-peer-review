This paper introduces STAR-T, a streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization. The problem is motivated by data summarization scenarios where elements may be removed from a computed summary, and the goal is to maintain a good approximation even after such removals. The algorithm utilizes a partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds, ensuring a constant-factor approximation guarantee after removing up to *m* elements. 

The work builds upon existing literature on submodular maximization, streaming algorithms, and robustness. It correctly cites relevant papers like [7] on greedy algorithms, [8] on streaming submodular maximization (SIEVE-STREAMING), and [9, 10, 11] concerning robust submodular maximization. The problem setting, combining streaming and robustness, appears novel and addresses a practical challenge in many applications. The algorithm design, utilizing partitioning and thresholding, is also a contribution.  Related work is comprehensively discussed, including connections to influence maximization and interactive machine learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Problem Formulation:** The combination of streaming data arrival and the need for robustness against element removal is a well-motivated and novel problem setting.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is a strong result.
*   **Clear Algorithm Description:** The algorithm is described clearly and the partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is well-defined.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results in two applications (dominating set and personalized movie recommendation) demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness and compare favorably against existing methods, even those with prior knowledge of removals.  The use of multiple scenarios (random removals vs. greedy removals) strengthens the evaluation.
*   **Practical Considerations:**  The discussion of how to approximate the required threshold parameter without knowing the optimal solution beforehand (Theorem 4.5) adds to the practicality of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Space Complexity:** While theoretically O((k + m log k) log k), the space complexity can still be significant, especially when *m* is large. The reported practical performance with *w*=1 suggests efficient use of memory, but this might not hold for all datasets or parameter settings.
*   **Constant Factor:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149) is relatively small, suggesting potential for further improvement.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance likely depends on the choice of parameters, particularly *w* and the initial threshold τ. The paper provides guidance but a more thorough sensitivity analysis might be beneficial.
*   **Clarity of Proofs:** The proofs, while outlined, are referred to supplementary material, making it hard to quickly assess the technical validity of the results within the main paper.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and relatively unexplored problem.
*   Provides strong theoretical guarantees.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results on real-world datasets.
*   The algorithm is well-described and appears practical.
*   The work is well-connected to existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is small.
*   The space complexity, while polynomial, may be a limitation in some scenarios.
*   Detailed proof verification is difficult without access to the supplementary material.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The problem formulation is novel and relevant, the algorithm is well-designed, and the theoretical guarantees are strong. The empirical results are convincing. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee could be improved, and the space complexity is worth noting, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
