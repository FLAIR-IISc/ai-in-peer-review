### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a setting that is both **streaming** (elements arrive sequentially and memory is limited) and **robust** (the solution must be resilient to the removal of up to `m` elements). The authors propose a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a single-pass streaming algorithm called STAR-T constructs a robust summary `S` of the data. In the second stage, after an arbitrary set `E` of at most `m` elements is removed from `S`, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining summary `S \ E` to produce the final solution `Z`.

The core contribution is the design of STAR-T, which cleverly combines and extends ideas from prior work. It adapts the partitioned summary structure from recent non-streaming robust optimization work (Bogunovic et al., 2017) and integrates it with a greedy thresholding principle from streaming submodular maximization (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014). Specifically, STAR-T organizes its summary into partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds, where each partition contains multiple buckets of exponentially increasing size. An incoming element is placed into the first available bucket for which its marginal gain exceeds the corresponding threshold.

The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving that their method achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee (`c ≈ 0.149`) for the robust objective. Crucially, the size of the summary `S` is `O((k + m log k) log k)`, which notably has only a poly-logarithmic dependency on the cardinality constraint `k`. This is a significant improvement over concurrent work (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2017), which has a linear dependency on `k`. The method is validated empirically on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, where it demonstrates performance comparable or superior to strong baselines, including a streaming algorithm that is given the advantage of knowing the removed elements in advance.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Formulation and Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem. The combination of streaming data and the need for robust solutions is a common scenario in large-scale machine learning (e.g., fault-tolerant sensor placement, interactive recommendation systems). The two-stage formulation, where a robust summary is pre-computed to handle arbitrary future removals, is well-motivated and practical.

2.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The STAR-T algorithm is an elegant and novel synthesis of state-of-the-art techniques. While it builds upon the partitioned structure of PRO (Bogunovic et al., 2017) and the thresholding of Sieve-Streaming (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014), the combination is non-trivial and requires a new analysis. The introduction of exponentially decreasing thresholds across partitions is the key technical innovation that enables a single-pass, robust summary construction.

3.  **Technical Quality and Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides the first constant-factor approximation for this problem with a memory complexity that scales favorably with `k`. The theoretical analysis, sketched in the main paper, appears sound and handles the intricate interplay between the partitioned structure and the thresholding rule. The comparison with the concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017) is important and clearly highlights the key advantage of this paper's approach: the memory complexity is `O((k + m log k) log k)` versus `O(mk log k)`. For large `k`, this is a substantial improvement.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The choice of comparing against Sieve-Streaming on the ground set `V \ E` is a particularly strong baseline, as it represents an algorithm with full information about the removals. The fact that STAR-T-GREEDY matches or even exceeds its performance is a powerful testament to the quality of the robust summary it constructs. The interactive movie recommendation experiment serves as an excellent practical illustration of the method's utility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Value of the Approximation Constant:** The derived approximation constant is `c ≈ 0.149`. While achieving any constant factor is a significant theoretical result, this value is quite low. The paper does not discuss whether this is an artifact of the proof technique or potentially a lower bound for this problem setting. A tighter analysis or a discussion on the potential for improving this constant would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice for Parameter `w`:** The theory requires the memory parameter `w` to be at least `⌈4dlog(k)em/k⌉` to guarantee robustness against `m` removals. However, the experiments are conducted with `w=1` and show excellent empirical robustness, even for `m > k`. This suggests a significant gap between the worst-case theoretical requirement and practical performance. A discussion of this gap would be valuable. For instance, is the analysis of `w` loose, or do the chosen datasets possess structures that make the problem easier than the worst-case?

3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   There is a small inconsistency in the definition of the threshold in Section 3. The text states the threshold for partition `i` is `τ/2^i`, whereas Algorithm 1 uses `τ/min{2^i, k}`. The latter seems correct given that bucket sizes are also capped at `k`, but this should be clarified.
    *   The proof sketch is concise, which is necessary for a NIPS paper, but some steps could benefit from slightly more intuition. For example, the argument for bounding `f(Y)` in the sketch for Lemma 4.4 is very brief and requires careful reading to parse.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization. It introduces a novel and practical algorithm for the important problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The approach is technically sound, supported by a strong theoretical analysis that establishes a constant-factor approximation with superior memory complexity compared to concurrent work, and validated by compelling experimental results. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the core strengths of the work. The paper is well-written, clearly situated within the literature, and presents a complete piece of research. It is very likely to inspire follow-up work at the intersection of streaming, robustness, and submodular optimization.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
