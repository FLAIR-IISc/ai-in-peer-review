This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where elements arrive sequentially and a subset of the selected elements might be removed. The authors propose STAR-T, an algorithm based on partitioned buckets and exponentially decreasing thresholds, and provide theoretical guarantees for its performance, specifically a constant-factor approximation after the removal of *m* elements.

The problem statement is well-motivated and relevant to many applications involving data summarization and robustness. The introduction clearly outlines the challenges of dealing with large datasets, streaming data, and the need for robustness against element removal. The connection to related work, including streaming submodular maximization (Sieve-Streaming) and robust submodular maximization (previous centralized algorithms), is comprehensive and accurately placed.  The two-stage approach – streaming selection followed by a query stage with a greedy algorithm – is a sensible way to tackle the problem.  The motivation and examples (dominating set, movie recommendation) are well-chosen and help to illustrate the applicability of the proposed method.

The technical contribution is significant. The partitioning structure and decreasing thresholding rule are novel and seem well-designed for achieving robustness. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, providing a constant-factor approximation guarantee under reasonable assumptions. The analysis cleverly combines properties of the partitioning and thresholding.  Extending the algorithm to work without knowing the optimal value beforehand (Theorem 4.5) is a valuable addition, addressing a key practical concern. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets (dominating set and movie recommendation) are convincing, demonstrating that STAR-T performs competitively with, and often outperforms, existing methods, including Sieve-Streaming, even when the latter has prior knowledge of the removed elements. The interactive setting demonstrated in the movie recommendation experiments is particularly compelling.

However, there are a few areas for improvement. 

* **Clarity of Constants:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149) is quite small. While theoretically sound, it raises a question of practical significance. The paper could benefit from a discussion of how sensitive the performance is to this constant, and whether it’s possible to improve it with a different choice of parameters. The impact of the 'dlog ke' term on the approximation ratio is not fully clear, and could be further elaborated.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The choice of parameters, especially *w* (memory parameter) and *τ* (threshold), can significantly impact performance. The paper mentions a practical value of w=1 but doesn't provide a clear guideline for choosing these parameters in general. Further discussion on practical parameter tuning would be valuable.
* **Scalability and Memory Usage:** While the theoretical memory usage is stated as O((k + m log k) log k), the practical memory requirements could be a concern for very large *k* and *m*. A more detailed analysis of memory usage, along with potential optimization strategies, would strengthen the paper.
* **Comparison with PRO:** The paper references the PRO algorithm [11] but doesn’t provide a direct empirical comparison. Given that PRO achieves a similar approximation guarantee, a comparison would be valuable for understanding the trade-offs between the two approaches (e.g., streaming vs. centralized).
* **Proof Details:** The proofs are referred to as being in the supplementary material, which is standard. However, a more high-level summary of the key ideas behind the proofs within the main body of the paper would improve readability for readers who may not delve into the supplementary material.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of partitioned buckets and exponentially decreasing thresholds is a novel approach to robust streaming submodular maximization.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a strong theoretical result.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that the algorithm performs well in practice, matching or outperforming existing methods.
*   **Relevance:** The problem is well-motivated and has applications in various domains.
*   **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the key ideas and contributions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Small Constant Factor:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small, raising concerns about practical significance.
*   **Lack of Comparison to PRO:** The absence of a direct comparison to the PRO algorithm limits the ability to assess the relative merits of the two approaches.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of robust submodular maximization. While the constant factor in the approximation guarantee could be improved, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the strong empirical results make it a valuable addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above. Specifically, adding a discussion on the impact of the constant factor, and a comparison to the PRO algorithm, would significantly strengthen the paper.
