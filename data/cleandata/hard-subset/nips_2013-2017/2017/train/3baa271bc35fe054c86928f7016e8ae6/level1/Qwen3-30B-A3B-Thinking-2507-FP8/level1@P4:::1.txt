

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint k in a streaming setting with the additional requirement that the solution must remain robust to the removal of up to m elements after the stream finishes. The authors propose a two-stage procedure: a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary in one pass, and a query algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that produces a solution from the remaining elements after removals.

The work extends the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8] for submodular maximization to the robust setting. It builds upon the robust submodular maximization work of [9, 10, 11] by introducing a novel partitioned thresholding approach where the summary is structured into partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing thresholds. The paper also relates to the concurrent work by [18] on robust streaming submodular maximization, showing that their memory complexity (O((k + m log k) log k)) is better than [18]'s O(mk log k/ε).

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness and Quality**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis proving a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for the robust streaming submodular maximization problem. The proof is well-structured, with careful handling of different cases through multiple lemmas. The authors demonstrate how their algorithm handles the challenge of unknown f(OPT(k, V \ E)) by introducing a parallel variant that achieves a (1 + ε)-approximation with only a logarithmic increase in space complexity.

**Clarity and Presentation**: The paper is well-written with clear organization. The problem statement is precise, the algorithm is described with detailed pseudocode, and the theoretical results are presented logically. The experimental section effectively validates the theoretical claims with two practical applications (dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation), showing that the proposed method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows the removed elements in advance.

**Originality**: The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds represents a significant innovation. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work, particularly [18], by achieving better memory complexity (logarithmic in k vs. linear in k). The theoretical analysis combining properties of the robust structure with the thresholding rule is novel.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is highly relevant for large-scale machine learning applications where data is too large to store and robustness to element removal is required. The paper demonstrates practical utility through two compelling applications, showing that the robust summary can be reused for multiple removal scenarios. The fact that the method outperforms competitors even when they have the advantage of knowing the removed elements in advance is a strong point for significance.

## Weaknesses

**Approximation Factor**: The approximation factor (0.149) is lower than the 0.5 - ε achieved by SIEVE-STREAMING for the non-robust problem. While this is expected due to the additional robustness constraint, the paper could more thoroughly discuss the trade-offs between robustness and approximation quality.

**Comparison with Other Robust Methods**: The paper primarily compares with [18] but does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other robust submodular maximization methods beyond those mentioned in the related work section. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

**Experimental Details**: The experimental setup could benefit from more details about parameter choices (e.g., why w=1 works well in practice) and the specific implementation details of the baselines.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization in streaming settings with robustness requirements. The theoretical results are rigorous and the practical evaluation demonstrates the method's effectiveness. The memory complexity advantage over the concurrent work [18] is particularly noteworthy.

The paper meets all the NIPS quality criteria:
- **Quality**: The technical content is sound with rigorous proofs and experimental validation.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written.
- **Originality**: The partitioned thresholding approach represents a novel contribution.
- **Significance**: The problem is important for large-scale machine learning applications, and the method provides practical advantages.

The main weakness is the lower approximation factor compared to non-robust streaming methods, but this is a necessary trade-off for the robustness requirement. The paper's contribution is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Novel partitioned thresholding approach with exponential decreasing thresholds
- Rigorous theoretical guarantee for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Practical demonstration with two compelling applications
- Better memory complexity than concurrent work [18]
- Clear differentiation from related work

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Lower approximation factor (0.149) compared to non-robust streaming methods (0.5 - ε)
- Limited comparison with other robust submodular maximization methods
- Some experimental details could be more thorough

The paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization in streaming settings with robustness requirements. The theoretical results are well-supported, the practical evaluation is convincing, and the method represents a meaningful advancement over existing work. I recommend acceptance.
