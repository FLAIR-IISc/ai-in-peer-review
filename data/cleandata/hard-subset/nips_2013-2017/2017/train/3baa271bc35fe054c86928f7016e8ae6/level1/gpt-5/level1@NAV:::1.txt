This paper studies monotone submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in a streaming setting with adversarial deletions after the stream. The authors propose STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm that maintains a partitioned summary with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing thresholds, and a query-time procedure (STAR-T-GREEDY) that runs GREEDY on the surviving elements after up to m deletions. They prove a constant-factor approximation (c → 0.149 as k grows) using O((k + m log k) log k) space when the optimal value is known, and extend to the unknown-optimal setting by running parallel instances, leading to O((k + m log k) log2 k) memory and a (1+ε)-loss. Empirically, on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, STAR-T matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, even when the latter is given the removed set in advance.

Relation to prior work: This builds on streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING), robust (non-streaming) submodular maximization (Orlin et al.; PRO), and concurrent deletion-robust streaming work (Mirzasoleiman et al.), which attains ~1/2−ε approximation but requires O(mk log k) space. STAR-T trades a smaller constant for logarithmic dependence on k in memory.

Quality: The algorithmic design is sound and the analysis leverages the partitioned structure and thresholding cleverly, balancing two regimes via lemmas on full vs. non-full buckets. The memory–approximation tradeoff against [18] is well argued. Some clarity issues could be tightened: Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i,k} while the text states τ/2^i; please reconcile and explain the intent in the last partition. The abstract/conclusion emphasize O((k+m log k) log2 k) while Theorem 4.1 shows O((k+m log k) log k); make the role of parallel instances explicit earlier. Provide full proofs and conditions (e.g., γ<1) in the main text or a clear roadmap.

Clarity: Generally well-written and organized, with intuitive figures. Pseudocode and parameter choices (w, τ) need clearer guidance, and a practical recipe for τ-approximation would help reproducibility.

Originality: The partitioned thresholding scheme for robust streaming is novel and distinct from both bucket-by-bucket greedy PRO and SIEVE-STREAMING.

Significance: Addresses an important “right to be forgotten” setting. The improved space bound is meaningful for large k. Experiments are promising; however, adding comparisons to the concurrent deletion-robust streaming method and reporting sensitivity to w, m/k, and runtime would strengthen the case.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel robust streaming framework with principled analysis and constant-factor guarantee.
- Favorable space–approximation tradeoff versus prior/concurrent work.
- Practical performance on two applications; reusable summary across queries.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Weaker approximation constant than concurrent work; missing empirical comparison to it.
- Some inconsistencies/clarity gaps in thresholds, memory bounds, and parameterization.
- Limited evaluation breadth (e.g., varying m, scalability metrics, τ/w sensitivity).
