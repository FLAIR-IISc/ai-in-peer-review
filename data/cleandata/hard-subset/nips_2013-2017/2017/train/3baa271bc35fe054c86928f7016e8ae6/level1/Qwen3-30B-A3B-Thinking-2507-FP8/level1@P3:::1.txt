

# Peer Review: Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach

This paper addresses the important problem of robust submodular maximization in streaming settings, where the algorithm must produce a summary set that remains effective even after m arbitrary elements are removed. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm (STAR-T and STAR-T-GREEDY) that achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee with O((k + m log k) log²k) memory complexity.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon two well-established lines of work: streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., [9, 10, 11]). Previous robust approaches [11] were centralized with O(m² log k) memory, while streaming approaches [8] did not address robustness. The authors' key insight is extending the SIEVE-STREAMING framework with a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding to achieve robustness against any m removals. Their work is also related to concurrent work [18] which provides a 1/2-ε approximation but with O(mk log k/ε) memory complexity, whereas the authors' approach achieves logarithmic dependence on k.

**Strengths**

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a clean theoretical analysis with a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149(1-1/log k)). The proof is technically sound and carefully handles the two cases (partitions with many full buckets vs. partitions with few full buckets). The authors correctly identify that the main challenge is balancing the trade-off between the greedy approximation and the robustness requirement.

2. **Practical Algorithm**: The STAR-T algorithm is simple to implement and makes only one pass over the data. The partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is elegant and well-motivated by the need for robustness against multiple removals.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments convincingly demonstrate that STAR-T-GREEDY outperforms or matches SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows in advance which elements will be removed. The two application scenarios (dominating set and movie recommendation) are well-chosen and realistic. The interactive recommendation example particularly illustrates the practical value of their two-stage approach.

4. **Memory Efficiency**: The memory complexity O((k + m log k) log²k) is significantly better than [18]'s O(mk log k/ε), especially for large k. The paper correctly identifies this as a key advantage.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Comparison to Concurrent Work**: While the paper briefly mentions [18], it doesn't fully elaborate on the theoretical differences. The authors claim their memory complexity is better, but a more detailed comparison of the approximation guarantees and memory complexities would strengthen the paper. The paper should clarify whether their approach achieves a better approximation ratio or simply better memory complexity.

2. **Practical Memory Impact**: The log²k factor in memory complexity might be significant in practice. The paper doesn't provide empirical evidence of how this factor affects performance. For example, how does the memory usage scale with k in their experiments? The authors claim w=1 works well in practice, but this might not scale well for larger k.

3. **Approximation of τ**: The paper requires τ to be an approximation of f(OPT(k, V \ E)), which is not known a priori. While Theorem 4.5 addresses this with a parallel approach that increases space by log₁₊ε k, this could be substantial for large k. The paper doesn't provide empirical evidence of how this impacts performance in practice.

4. **Theoretical Tightness**: The approximation factor (0.149) is quite low compared to standard greedy (0.632) and SIEVE-STREAMING (0.5-ε). The paper doesn't discuss whether this is a fundamental limitation of the robust streaming setting or if it could be improved with a different approach.

**Recommendation**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization. The theoretical analysis is sound, the algorithm is practical, and the experimental results are convincing. While the approximation factor is lower than standard submodular maximization, this is a necessary trade-off for the robustness requirement in the streaming setting. The memory complexity advantage over concurrent work is particularly compelling.

The main weaknesses are the limited comparison to concurrent work and insufficient discussion of the practical impact of the log²k factor in memory complexity. Addressing these would strengthen the paper significantly.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a meaningful problem with clear practical motivation
- Theoretical analysis is sound and novel
- Memory complexity is better than competing approaches
- Experimental results demonstrate clear advantages
- The two-stage approach is well-motivated by real-world applications

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The approximation factor (0.149) is relatively low compared to standard submodular maximization
- The log²k factor in memory complexity might be practically significant
- Limited discussion of the trade-offs between robustness and approximation quality

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field. The theoretical results are novel and well-supported, and the experimental validation is convincing. The memory complexity advantage over concurrent work is particularly compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better compare with [18] and discuss the practical impact of the log²k factor.
