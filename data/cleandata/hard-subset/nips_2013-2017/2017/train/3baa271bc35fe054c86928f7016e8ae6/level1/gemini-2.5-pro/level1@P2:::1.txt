### Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a setting that is both streaming (elements arrive one by one) and robust (the solution must be resilient to the removal of `m` elements). The authors propose a two-stage framework. In the first stage, a single-pass streaming algorithm called STAR-T processes the data stream to create a summary `S` of size O((k + m log k) log k). This summary is structured into partitions, with each partition containing buckets of exponentially increasing size and associated with an exponentially decreasing quality threshold. In the second, query stage, after an arbitrary set `E` of `m` elements is removed from the summary, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining elements `S \ E` to produce a final solution of size `k`. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of approximately 0.149. The authors validate their approach empirically on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks, showing that it performs comparably to or better than the non-robust SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm, even when the latter is given the advantage of knowing the removed set `E` in advance.

The work builds upon two distinct lines of research. It extends the streaming submodular maximization framework of SIEVE-STREAMING [8] by adapting its thresholding idea to a more complex, partitioned structure. The concept of robustness and the partitioned summary structure are inspired by recent work in the non-streaming robust setting [10, 11]. This paper provides a novel synthesis of these ideas to tackle the combined streaming and robust challenge. The authors also clearly position their work against concurrent research [18], highlighting a key advantage in memory complexity (logarithmic vs. linear in `k`).

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and challenging problem. As datasets grow, single-pass streaming algorithms are essential, and in many real-world applications (e.g., sensor placement, recommender systems), ensuring the robustness of the selected subset against failures or adversarial removals is critical. The combination of the streaming and robust settings is novel and important. The proposed algorithm, STAR-T, is an original contribution that cleverly combines a partitioned structure with an exponentially decreasing thresholding scheme, and the analysis required to prove a guarantee for this combination is non-trivial.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, culminating in a constant-factor approximation guarantee (Theorem 4.1). While the constant (0.149) is modest, obtaining any constant factor in this challenging setting is a strong result. The proof sketch is well-structured, breaking the analysis into clear cases and balancing the resulting bounds. The authors are careful to address the practical issue of not knowing `f(OPT)` a priori by providing a standard parallel-instances approach (Theorem 4.5) to remove this assumption. The memory complexity of O((k + m log k) log k) is a significant strength, particularly its logarithmic dependence on `k`, which compares favorably to concurrent work.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. Comparing against SIEVE-STREAMING with an oracle (knowing `E` beforehand) is a strong baseline that effectively highlights the practical utility of the proposed robust summary. The results consistently show that STAR-T-GREEDY is competitive, demonstrating that the robust summary does not sacrifice much performance for its resilience. The movie recommendation application is an excellent use-case, illustrating how a single precomputed summary can handle diverse, interactive queries (e.g., filtering by genre), which is a powerful and practical feature.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of Theoretical Section:** While the overall paper is well-written, the proof sketch in Section 4 is quite dense. The transition from the individual lemmas (4.2, 4.3, 4.4) to the final balanced results in Equations (4) and (5) is abrupt and could benefit from more intuition. The main text relies heavily on the reader trusting the stated lemmas, and while full proofs are in the appendix, a bit more insight in the main paper would improve readability for those not digging into the supplement.

2.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** There appears to be a disconnect between the theoretical requirements and the practical implementation. The memory parameter `w` requires `w >= ceil(4 * log(k) * m / k)` for the theoretical guarantee to hold, which can be large. However, the experiments are conducted with `w=1` and still show excellent performance, even for `m` larger than `k`. While this is a positive empirical finding, the paper could benefit from a discussion on this gap. It suggests that the worst-case analysis might be pessimistic and that the algorithm is even more practical than the theory implies.

3.  **Minor Typo:** Throughout the paper, the notation `dlog ke` is used. This appears to be a typo for `ceil(log_2 k)`, which should be corrected for clarity.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and practically significant problem at the intersection of three key areas: submodular optimization, streaming algorithms, and robustness.
*   Proposes a new algorithm with a creative design that combines techniques from different sub-fields.
*   Provides the first constant-factor approximation guarantee for this problem with a memory complexity that is only logarithmic in `k`.
*   The empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness against powerful baselines.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical approximation constant is low, which might limit its theoretical impact.
*   The clarity of the theory section could be improved, particularly the intuition behind the main proof steps.

**Conclusion**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. It introduces and provides a strong solution to the important problem of robust streaming submodular maximization. The combination of theoretical novelty, a constant-factor guarantee, and compelling empirical results makes this a solid addition to the NIPS proceedings. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
