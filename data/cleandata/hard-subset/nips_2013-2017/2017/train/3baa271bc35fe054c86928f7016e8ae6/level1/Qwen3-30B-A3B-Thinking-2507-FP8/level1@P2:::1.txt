

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper addresses an important extension of submodular maximization: the robust streaming setting where elements arrive in a stream, and after the stream, up to m elements may be removed from the solution. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm that first constructs a robust summary in one pass, then applies greedy selection on the remaining elements after removal. The key innovation is a partitioned thresholding structure where buckets within partitions have exponentially decreasing thresholds, enabling a constant-factor approximation guarantee.

The paper builds upon two well-established lines of work: robust submodular maximization [9, 10, 11] and streaming submodular maximization [8, 12, 13]. Previous robust approaches were centralized (e.g., PRO [11]), while streaming approaches (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]) did not consider robustness. The authors' approach bridges these areas, providing the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization with a constant-factor guarantee. They also note the concurrent work [18], which achieves a 1/2-ε approximation but with O(mk log k/ε) memory, while their algorithm has O((k + m log k) log k) memory with logarithmic dependence on k.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with a rigorous proof of a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) for the robust streaming problem. The proof technique is novel, combining partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds to handle the robustness requirement. The authors carefully analyze both cases where partitions have full buckets versus not-full buckets.

2. **Practical Validation:** The experimental section demonstrates the algorithm's effectiveness in two real-world applications: dominating set problems and personalized movie recommendation. Crucially, the authors compare against SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed), showing their method matches or outperforms it. This is a strong empirical validation of the robustness claim.

3. **Memory Efficiency:** The paper achieves a memory complexity of O((k + m log k) log k), which is more efficient than the concurrent work [18] (O(mk log k/ε)) when m is small relative to k, and matches the memory complexity of SIEVE-STREAMING for the non-robust case (O(k log k)).

4. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the two-stage problem setup, which is well-motivated by practical applications like influence maximization and personalized recommendations.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Approximation Factor:** The approximation factor (0.149) is significantly lower than the 0.5-ε factor achievable by SIEVE-STREAMING in the non-robust case. While the paper acknowledges this trade-off, it might be a concern for applications where high approximation quality is critical.

2. **Parameter Dependency:** The algorithm requires knowing τ = f(OPT(k, V\E)), which is unknown. While the paper provides a solution using multiple parallel instances, this increases memory complexity by a log factor (O((k + m log k) log k log₁₊ᵋ k)), which could be prohibitive for large k.

3. **Proof Complexity:** The proof sketch in the main paper is quite dense, with the full proof relegated to supplementary material. This makes it challenging for readers to follow the technical details without consulting the appendix.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field by solving a meaningful problem at the intersection of robust optimization and streaming algorithms. The theoretical guarantees are sound, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the approach. While the approximation factor is lower than non-robust streaming methods, the robustness is a critical feature for many real-world applications where element removal is unavoidable.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria well:
- **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental validation is thorough, and the authors are careful to discuss limitations.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good motivation and explanation of the algorithm.
- **Originality:** The partitioned thresholding approach is novel for the robust streaming setting, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work.
- **Significance:** The problem addressed is important for large-scale machine learning applications, and the solution advances the state of the art in both robust optimization and streaming algorithms.

The main weakness is the lower approximation factor compared to non-robust methods, but this is an inherent trade-off for the robustness requirement. The memory efficiency compared to concurrent work [18] is a significant advantage, and the empirical results demonstrate that the practical performance is competitive even with the lower approximation factor.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance at NIPS 2017.
