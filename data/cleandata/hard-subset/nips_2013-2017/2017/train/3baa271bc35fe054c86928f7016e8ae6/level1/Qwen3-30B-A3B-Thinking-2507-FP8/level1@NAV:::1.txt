

This paper proposes a novel two-stage approach for robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, addressing the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint k, with the additional challenge that m items may be removed from the algorithm's memory after the stream is processed. The authors introduce STAR-T (Streaming Robust submodular algorithm with Partitioned Thresholding) for the streaming stage and STAR-T-GREEDY for the query stage. The key innovation is a partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholding rules, which allows the algorithm to make one pass over the data while retaining a summary of size O((k + m log k) log²k). The authors prove that after any m elements are removed, STAR-T-GREEDY achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee (c = 0.149(1-1/log k)).

The paper builds upon prior work on submodular streaming (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., [9, 10, 11]), but makes a significant contribution by combining these settings in a novel way. The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-motivated, with clear proof sketches and detailed supplementary material. The experimental results on two data summarization tasks (dominating set and movie recommendation) demonstrate that the proposed method matches or outperforms existing approaches, even when baselines know in advance which elements will be removed.

Strengths include the clear problem formulation, solid theoretical foundation, and compelling experimental validation. The algorithmic design is elegant and well-suited to the problem. The paper excels in quality (technically sound with strong theoretical guarantees) and originality (novel partitioning structure for robust streaming submodular maximization).

Weaknesses include the relatively low approximation factor (0.149) compared to standard submodular maximization algorithms (which achieve ~0.63), and limited discussion of how the performance scales with larger m values. While the paper acknowledges this trade-off, a more detailed analysis of the practical implications would strengthen the contribution.

The significance of the work is high, as it addresses a practically relevant problem in large-scale machine learning where data is too large to fit in memory and robustness to element removal is required. The method's ability to outperform baselines that know removals in advance is particularly impressive.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of submodular optimization. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the approximation factor discussion.
