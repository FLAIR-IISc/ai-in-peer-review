## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper tackles the important problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where elements arrive sequentially and a subset of the selected elements may be removed. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm utilizing a partitioned structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding. The core idea is to create a robust summary in the streaming phase, and then apply a simple greedy algorithm to this summary after potential removals. 

This work builds upon existing streaming submodular maximization algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING and extends the concept of robustness explored in prior work (e.g., [9, 10, 11]). The authors correctly identify the challenge of maintaining robustness without re-optimizing over the entire dataset, which is crucial for large-scale applications. The two-stage approach is a sensible way to address this.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of partitioned structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding for robust streaming submodular maximization appears novel. The analysis connecting these components to achieve a constant-factor approximation is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a theoretical guarantee for the approximation ratio of the proposed algorithm, which is essential for establishing its effectiveness.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both the dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendation demonstrate that STAR-T performs competitively with, and sometimes outperforms, existing methods, even when those methods have prior knowledge of the removed elements.
* **Practical Relevance:** The interactive movie recommendation scenario highlights the practical utility of the algorithm in real-world applications where user preferences and data availability change dynamically.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Analysis:** The theoretical analysis, while thorough, is quite dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with the specific techniques used.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithm likely depends on the choice of parameters like *w* and *τ*. While the paper provides guidance on setting *w*, a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning and its impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison to SIEVE-STREAMING is valuable, exploring the performance against other relevant baselines (e.g., algorithms specifically designed for robust submodular maximization in a non-streaming setting) would strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The theoretical guarantees and experimental results provide strong evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness.
* The work has the potential to impact various applications, including data summarization, influence maximization, and recommender systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the analysis might limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
* The parameter sensitivity and limited comparison to other baselines could raise concerns about the robustness of the results.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The novelty of the approach, combined with the theoretical guarantees and experimental validation, makes it a strong candidate for acceptance. While the analysis is complex and some aspects of the evaluation could be strengthened, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
