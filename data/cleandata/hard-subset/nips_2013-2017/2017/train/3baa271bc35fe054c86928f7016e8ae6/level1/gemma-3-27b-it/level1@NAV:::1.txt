## Review of Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach

This paper tackles the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, a relevant and challenging area with applications in data summarization, recommendation systems, and influence maximization. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm leveraging a partitioned structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding. In the streaming stage, STAR-T creates a robust summary, and in the query stage, STAR-T-GREEDY efficiently finds a solution even after removing elements from the summary. 

The work builds upon existing streaming submodular maximization techniques (like SIEVE-STREAMING) and extends them to the robust setting, drawing parallels from recent work on robust submodular maximization in static settings. It correctly identifies the need for robustness in scenarios where data is dynamic or subject to errors. The introduction does a good job of motivating the problem and outlining contributions. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of partitioned structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding for robust streaming submodular maximization appears novel.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the proposed algorithm, a significant achievement.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks demonstrate that STAR-T-GREEDY performs competitively with, and in some cases surpasses, existing methods, even when those methods have prior knowledge of removals.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The algorithm’s ability to handle removals and provide good solutions from a precomputed summary is appealing for real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The parameter *w* (related to memory allocation) requires careful tuning, and its optimal setting might depend on the specific problem instance. The explanation of how to choose *w* could be more detailed.
*   **Clarity of Proofs:** While the theorem is clearly stated, some parts of the proof sketches (particularly in Section 4) require more elaboration to be fully accessible.
*   **Comparison Depth:** While the experimental results are promising, the comparison with other algorithms could be more thorough. A more detailed ablation study examining the impact of the partitioning and thresholding parameters would be beneficial.
* **Space Complexity**: While the authors state O((k + m log k) log k) space complexity, this is a high space complexity for large *m*.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem is important and well-motivated.
*   The proposed algorithm is novel and provides a strong theoretical guarantee.
*   The empirical results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical usefulness.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The parameter sensitivity of the algorithm could limit its practical applicability.
*   The proofs are somewhat dense and could benefit from further clarification.
*   The space complexity can be high for very large *m*.



**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The algorithm is well-designed, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While there are areas for improvement (particularly in clarity and parameter tuning discussion), the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
