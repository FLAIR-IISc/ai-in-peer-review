Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in a single-pass streaming setting, with post-hoc adversarial deletions of up to m items from memory. The proposed two-stage procedure comprises: (i) STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm that maintains a small, robust summary S using a partitioned bucket structure with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing marginal-gain thresholds; and (ii) STAR-T-GREEDY, which, given any deletion set E with |E| ≤ m, runs greedy on S \ E to produce a k-sized solution. The authors prove that with space O(k log k + m log^2 k) (or variants stated elsewhere), the returned solution achieves a constant-factor approximation approaching 0.149 as k grows, and they provide a parallel-thresholding variant to remove dependence on unknown OPT. Empirically, the approach matches or improves over SIEVE-STREAMING (which is given the advantage of knowing E) on dominating set and personalized recommendation tasks.

This work builds on several NIPS/ML conference strands: streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING, KDD’14), robust submodular optimization (Krause et al. JMLR’08; Orlin et al. IPCO’16; Bogunovic et al. ICML’17-PRO), and large-scale summarization. The key novelty is adapting nonuniform partitioning ideas (cf. PRO) to a one-pass streaming setting via exponentially decreasing thresholds, plus an analysis that ensures robustness to deletions with only logarithmic dependence on k in memory overhead. The authors also discuss concurrent work [18] (Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17) that achieves 1/2 − ε approximation but requires memory linear in k and m.

Quality:
- Theory: The main guarantee (Theorem 4.1) gives a constant-factor approximation under deletions using a robust summary of size roughly O(k log k + m log^2 k) when w ≈ Θ(m log k / k), and a parallel version to eliminate OPT dependence at an extra log factor. The proof strategy is sound and intuitive: either many buckets are full (implying existence of a high-value, lightly-hit bucket), or many buckets are not full, in which case either the last-partition bucket is already valuable or the contributions from earlier buckets imply a large remaining value; the two resulting bounds are balanced to yield the 0.149 constant. The analysis highlights how replication across partitions/buckets and decreasing thresholds confer deletion resilience.
- Experimental validation: The experiments support the practical viability of the approach and its robustness, including scenarios where |E| > k. They are fair in giving SIEVE-STREAMING advance knowledge of E.
- Concerns/bugs to address:
  • Inconsistency in thresholds: The text describes thresholds τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k} and later proofs rely on τ/k in the last partition. Please reconcile and adopt one consistent definition (Algorithm 1’s version seems to align with Lemma 4.4).
  • Space complexity inconsistencies: The intro and conclusion claim O((k + m log k) log^2 k), Theorem 4.1 and Eq. (3) imply O(k log k + m log^2 k), and sometimes O((k + m log k) log k). Please unify these statements and clearly explain the role of w = max{1, ⌈4 m log k / k⌉}.
  • τ definition in Theorem 4.1 appears dimensionally inconsistent (“τ = 1 + (1−e−1)(1−e−1/3)(1− 1/dlog ke) f(OPT(·))”). Presumably τ should be proportional to f(OPT), not 1 plus a scaling; please correct and relate to the parallel-thresholding construction (Section 4.1).
  • Per-element oracle complexity: STAR-T checks buckets across partitions; worst-case per arriving element is O(∑i w·k/2^i) = O(wk). Please discuss/measure oracle calls and running time vs. SIEVE-STREAMING.

Clarity:
The high-level algorithm and intuition are well explained, and the proof sketches are informative. However, the noted inconsistencies (thresholding rule, memory bounds, τ) should be corrected. It would also improve clarity to:
- Include pseudo-code or a brief description for the parallel-thresholding variant and its memory factor (log1+ε k).
- Provide a short intuition for how replication across partitions limits the fraction of deletions that can concentrate on any single bucket.
- Report memory and wall-clock/runtime for experiments, including the effect of w, and the number of function evaluations.

Originality:
The combination of nonuniform partitioning with exponentially decreasing thresholding in a single-pass streaming algorithm for deletion robustness is novel relative to both SIEVE-STREAMING and PRO. The analysis technique that balances two complementary bounds to achieve a constant factor under adversarial deletions is also original.

Significance:
Robustness to deletions is increasingly important (privacy/”right to be forgotten”, failures, personalization constraints). The method offers a meaningful trade-off: substantially reduced space compared to concurrent 1/2 − ε methods (logarithmic vs. linear dependence on k), at the cost of a smaller constant (≈0.149). Given the practical performance (often close to greedy) and small memory, this is a useful contribution likely to be built upon.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and principled algorithmic design with a new analysis for deletion-robust streaming.
- Single-pass, small-space summary enabling multiple post-hoc queries.
- Solid theoretical guarantee under adversarial deletions with mild space.
- Empirical results on two applications, competitive with strong baselines even when handicapped.

Cons for acceptance:
- Several presentation inconsistencies/typos (threshold rule, memory bound, τ definition) that must be fixed.
- No runtime/memory usage reporting; per-element cost may be nontrivial.
- No empirical comparison to the concurrent deletion-robust work [18] (even a brief discussion of practical memory/runtime trade-offs would help).
- Approximation factor is modest (though acceptable given space advantages).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Reconcile and correct all inconsistencies (thresholding, τ, and complexity bounds) and provide a unified theorem statement.
- Add per-element oracle-call complexity, wall-clock time, and memory usage vs. SIEVE-STREAMING, including an ablation over w.
- Briefly discuss extensions/non-monotone objectives or matroid constraints as future work.
- If feasible, include a comparison or at least a detailed discussion with [18] on practical regimes where STAR-T’s memory advantage is decisive.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, technically sound paper with a novel and practically relevant contribution to robust streaming submodular maximization. With the presentation issues addressed, I lean toward acceptance.
