This paper addresses the problem of monotone submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint `k` in a challenging setting that combines streaming data access with a robustness requirement against the removal of `m` elements. The authors propose a two-stage procedure: a single-pass streaming algorithm, STAR-T, which builds a robust summary, followed by a simple greedy algorithm, STAR-T-GREEDY, which selects a `k`-element solution from the summary after `m` items are adversarially removed. The STAR-T algorithm is based on a novel partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds, cleverly merging ideas from the non-robust SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8] and recent centralized robust methods [11]. The main theoretical contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee for this problem, achieved using a summary of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`.

The paper’s key strength lies in its originality and significance. The problem setting is highly relevant for large-scale applications where data summaries must be resilient to failures or adversarial attacks, and this work is one of the first to provide a formal guarantee for the robust *streaming* submodular maximization problem. The paper is technically sound, with a complete theoretical analysis that culminates in a constant-factor approximation. Furthermore, the experimental validation is particularly compelling; demonstrating that the proposed method is competitive with a strong non-robust baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING) that has the unfair advantage of knowing the removed set in advance is a strong endorsement of the approach. The paper is also clearly written and well-organized, making the algorithm's design intuitive.

A potential weakness is that the approximation constant of 0.149 is modest, though obtaining high constants in robust settings is notoriously difficult. While the memory complexity's logarithmic dependence on `k` is a key advantage over concurrent work [18], the overall `O((k + m log k) log k)` space could still be large in practice, particularly for large `m`.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and important problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization.
*   Provides a sound theoretical analysis with the first constant-factor guarantee for this setting.
*   Features a strong and convincing experimental section that validates the practical utility of the algorithm.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical approximation guarantee is relatively low.
*   The reliance on multiple parallel instances to estimate the unknown optimal value (a standard technique) adds a logarithmic factor to memory usage.

In conclusion, the paper’s strengths in defining a new, important problem and providing a sound, well-validated solution significantly outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a solid and timely contribution to the field.
