This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where elements arrive sequentially and a subset of the chosen elements may be removed. The authors propose STAR-T, an algorithm based on partitioned buckets and exponentially decreasing thresholds, and provide theoretical guarantees for its performance. 

The problem setting is well-motivated – data summarization with robustness is increasingly relevant. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature on submodular maximization, streaming algorithms, and robust optimization. They clearly articulate the challenges of combining these aspects, especially the need for a solution that is both space-efficient and resilient to element removal. The contributions – a novel algorithm with theoretical guarantees and empirical validation – are significant. The connection to real-world applications like influence maximization and recommendation systems is compelling.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The partitioned thresholding approach is a clever way to balance space efficiency and robustness in a streaming setting. The exponentially decreasing thresholds appear crucial for achieving the theoretical guarantees.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee, despite the removal of *m* elements, is a strong result. The analysis appears rigorous, although a deeper dive into the proofs in the supplementary material would be needed for complete validation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem statement, algorithm description, and theoretical results are presented in a clear and concise manner. The use of notation is consistent.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that STAR-T performs well in practical scenarios, often matching or outperforming existing methods like SIEVE-STREAMING, even when SIEVE-STREAMING has prior knowledge of the removals.  The two applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) are well-chosen and representative of real-world use cases.
* **Interactive aspect**: The ability to use the same robust summary for multiple different removal scenarios, and the corresponding benefits, add to the practical value of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm relies on several parameters (k, m, τ, w). The choice of these parameters, especially τ, can significantly impact performance. The paper acknowledges the need to approximate f(OPT(k, V \ E)) but doesn’t provide extensive guidance on how to do so effectively in practice. The parallelization approach discussed in Theorem 4.5 increases the space complexity.
* **Constant Factor:** The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (0.149) is relatively small. While theoretically significant, this might limit the practical applicability of the algorithm in scenarios where a higher approximation ratio is desired.
* **Space Complexity:** While the space complexity is stated as O((k + m log k) log k), the constant factors hidden within the big-O notation could be substantial. A more detailed analysis of the actual memory usage in the experiments would be helpful.  The experiments suggest w=1 works well, but the theory requires w >= ⌈4dlog(k)em/k⌉, suggesting a potential discrepancy.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison with SIEVE-STREAMING is valuable, it would be beneficial to compare STAR-T with other relevant algorithms for robust submodular maximization, particularly those proposed in [10] and [11] (mentioned in the related work).  The comparison could be expanded to more diverse datasets and problem settings.
* **Proof Details:** The details of the proofs are relegated to the supplementary material. While this is standard practice, it makes it difficult to fully assess the validity of the theoretical claims without a careful review of the supplementary material.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the main ideas and contributions.
* The work represents a significant advancement in the field of robust submodular maximization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The constant factor in the approximation guarantee is relatively small.
* The space complexity and parameter sensitivity could be limitations in practice.
* The comparison with existing algorithms is somewhat limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS 2017. The contributions are significant, the presentation is clear, and the empirical results are promising. The weaknesses identified are not major enough to outweigh the strengths of the work. The theoretical guarantees and the practical performance make this a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization and data summarization.  I recommend the authors address the points raised in the weaknesses section – particularly regarding parameter tuning and a more thorough comparison with related work – in a revised version of the paper.  Checking the consistency between theoretical requirements for 'w' and empirical results would also be beneficial.
