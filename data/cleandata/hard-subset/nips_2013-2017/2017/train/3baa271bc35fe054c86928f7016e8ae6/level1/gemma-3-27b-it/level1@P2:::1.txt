This paper addresses the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, a challenging combination of well-studied areas. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm with a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule, offering a constant-factor approximation guarantee after arbitrary removals of *m* elements.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:** The paper tackles a practical problem: summarizing large datasets efficiently while ensuring robustness to data loss or changes. This is motivated by applications like influence maximization and recommendation systems. The work builds upon the well-established greedy algorithm for submodular maximization, acknowledging its limitations in streaming settings. It extends prior streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING and incorporates ideas from robust submodular maximization work (e.g., [9, 10, 11]). The contribution lies in adapting these techniques to the *streaming* and *robustness* requirements simultaneously, achieving a constant-factor approximation. The connection to existing work is well-articulated, and relevant papers are cited.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The approximation guarantee is clearly stated and appears rigorously justified (although a full verification requires detailed examination of the supplementary material). The two-stage approach is well-defined, and the algorithm's components (partitioning, thresholding) are clearly explained. The theoretical analysis seems robust, with careful consideration of parameters like *w* and *τ*. The experiments provide evidence supporting the theoretical claims and demonstrating the algorithm's performance relative to baselines. However, the choice of *w* being 1 in experiments despite the theoretical requirement of w ≥ ⌈ 4dlog kem/k ⌉ requires more explanation. The justification for this practical deviation is not entirely clear and may indicate sensitivity to parameter tuning. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The problem statement and motivation are clearly presented. The algorithm is described with sufficient detail, and the theoretical results are stated concisely. The notation is mostly consistent, although the abundance of variables could be slightly overwhelming. The experimental setup is clearly explained, and the results are presented in a comprehensible manner.  Minor improvements could be made by adding more intuitive explanations alongside the mathematical notation. For example, elaborating on the intuition behind the exponentially decreasing thresholds.

**Originality:** The combination of streaming, robustness, and submodular maximization is a novel contribution. While the individual components (streaming algorithms, robust optimization) are known, their integration in this specific way is original. The partitioning structure and thresholding scheme appear to be novel adaptations specifically tailored to the robust streaming setting. The experimental validation on relevant tasks further strengthens the originality of the work.

**Significance:** The results are significant. Robustness is a crucial requirement in many real-world applications. A streaming algorithm that provides robustness with a constant-factor approximation guarantee is valuable. The performance comparison with SIEVE-STREAMING, especially the demonstration of comparable performance even when SIEVE-STREAMING has access to removal information, is compelling. The potential for interactive applications (as demonstrated in the movie recommendation example) adds to the significance of the work. The demonstrated capability to use the same robust summary across multiple removal scenarios is a particularly strong contribution.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated problem addressing a practical need.
*   Technically sound algorithm with a rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   Strong experimental validation demonstrating competitive performance.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The seemingly contradictory use of *w* = 1 in experiments versus the theoretical requirement requires further justification.
*   The theoretical analysis, while seemingly sound, would benefit from further scrutiny (verification through supplemental material).
*   The approximation constant *c* = 0.149 is relatively small, indicating potential for improvement.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization by tackling a challenging and practically relevant problem. The algorithm is well-designed, rigorously analyzed, and empirically validated. The minor weaknesses mentioned above do not outweigh the strengths of the work. The paper meets the NIPS reviewing criteria and is of high quality.
