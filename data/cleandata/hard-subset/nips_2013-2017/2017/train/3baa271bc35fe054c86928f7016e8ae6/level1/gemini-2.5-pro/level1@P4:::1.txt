### Review

**Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a setting that is both streaming (single-pass, sublinear memory) and robust to adversarial removals. The problem is framed in a two-stage process: first, a streaming algorithm processes a data stream to create a summary `S`; second, after an adversary removes an arbitrary set `E` of up to `m` elements from `S`, a query-stage algorithm must efficiently find a high-quality solution of size `k` from the remaining elements `S \ E`.

The authors propose a novel two-stage procedure. In the first stage, the STAR-T algorithm makes a single pass over the data to build the robust summary `S`. The core idea of STAR-T is a new partitioned thresholding scheme. The summary `S` is structured into `log k + 1` partitions. Partitions with a smaller index `i` are associated with higher greedy thresholds (`τ/2^i`) and contain buckets `B_{i,j}` of smaller capacity (`2^i`). An incoming element is placed into the first non-full bucket for which its marginal gain exceeds the corresponding threshold. This design aims to capture both high-value elements (in early partitions) and a diverse set of elements for robustness (across all partitions).

In the second stage, after `m` elements are removed from `S`, the STAR-T-GREEDY algorithm simply runs the standard greedy algorithm on the remaining elements `S \ E` to produce the final solution.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of approximately 0.149, while requiring memory of size `O((k + m log k) log k)`. This work builds upon and extends key ideas from two distinct lines of research. It adapts the thresholding concept from streaming submodular maximization, notably the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8]. Simultaneously, it employs a partitioned summary structure conceptually similar to that used in recent *centralized* robust submodular maximization algorithms like PRO [11], but innovatively adapts it for the streaming context with a novel thresholding rule and analysis. The paper also compares itself to concurrent work [18], highlighting a key advantage in memory complexity, which scales logarithmically with `k` rather than linearly.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and challenging problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization. The formulation of robust summarization where deletions happen *after* the summary is built is practical for many real-world applications where data is ephemeral or too large to re-process. This work appears to be the first to provide a constant-factor approximation for this problem with provable guarantees. The proposed algorithm and its analysis are novel.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and non-trivial. The proof strategy, which carefully analyzes and balances two distinct cases (one where a partition is densely populated with "high-quality" buckets, and one where it is not), is clever and leads to the constant-factor guarantee. The authors also correctly handle the practical issue of the unknown optimal value `f(OPT)` by using a standard technique of running multiple instances in parallel, adapting the method from [8]. The memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)` is a strong result, particularly its logarithmic dependence on `k`, which is a significant improvement over concurrent work.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement is precise, the proposed algorithms are presented cleanly, and the main theoretical results are stated upfront. The partitioned structure of the summary `S` is well-explained and intuitive. The experimental setup and results are also presented in a clear and understandable manner.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are convincing and well-designed. The choice to compare against SIEVE-STREAMING, a strong non-robust baseline that is given the significant advantage of knowing the removed set `E` in advance, is a powerful way to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed robust summary. The fact that STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably to, and sometimes better than, this "clairvoyant" baseline is a strong empirical result. The use of two distinct applications (dominating set and personalized recommendation) and two removal strategies (random and adversarial) further strengthens the experimental claims.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Approximation Constant:** The proven constant factor of `c ≈ 0.149` is modest. While obtaining any constant factor in this challenging combined setting is a significant achievement, this value is relatively low compared to guarantees in simpler, non-robust or non-streaming settings (e.g., `1 - 1/e`). The authors are transparent about this value, but it represents a theoretical limitation.

2.  **Gap between Theory and Practice for Parameter `w`:** The theoretical analysis requires the memory parameter `w` to scale with `m` and `log k` (`w ≥ ⌈4dlog k * m / k⌉`). However, the experiments show excellent performance with `w = 1`, even in scenarios where the number of removed elements is large. While this is a positive practical finding, it suggests a potential looseness in the theoretical bounds. A brief discussion on this gap could be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and significant problem: robust submodular maximization in a single-pass streaming setting.
*   Proposes a novel algorithm (STAR-T) with an innovative partitioned thresholding structure.
*   Provides the first constant-factor approximation guarantee for this problem.
*   The theoretical analysis is sound and technically strong.
*   Achieves a favorable memory complexity with logarithmic dependence on `k`, improving upon concurrent work.
*   Empirical results are strong and demonstrate the algorithm's practical effectiveness against well-chosen baselines.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The proven approximation constant (≈0.149) is relatively low.
*   There appears to be a gap between the theoretical requirements on the parameters and the settings that work well in practice, suggesting the analysis might not be tight.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field. It introduces a novel and practical problem, provides an innovative algorithmic solution, and backs it up with both solid theoretical guarantees and compelling empirical evidence. The strengths, particularly the originality of the problem and solution and the quality of the technical contributions, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to inspire follow-up work.
