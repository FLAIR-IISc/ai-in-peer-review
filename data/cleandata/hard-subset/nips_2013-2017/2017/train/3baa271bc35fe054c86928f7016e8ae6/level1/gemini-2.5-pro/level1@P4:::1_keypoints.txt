- Problem: robust monotone submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in a single-pass streaming setting where up to m elements can be removed from the summary after it’s built.

- Approach: two-stage method—STAR-T builds a streaming summary S; STAR-T-GREEDY runs standard greedy on S \ E after removals.

- STAR-T design: novel partitioned thresholding with log k + 1 partitions, bucket capacities 2^i, and thresholds τ/2^i; elements placed into first non-full bucket exceeding its threshold.

- Theoretical guarantee: constant-factor approximation ≈ 0.149 with memory O((k + m log k) log k).

- Connection to prior work: adapts SIEVE-STREAMING thresholding and centralized PRO-style partitioned summaries, but novelly combines them for streaming with new analysis.

- Memory advantage: memory scales logarithmically with k, improving over concurrent work that scales linearly.

- Technical quality: sound, non-trivial analysis that balances dense vs. sparse partition cases and handles unknown f(OPT) via parallel instances.

- Clarity: paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents algorithms and results accessibly.

- Experimental validation: convincing experiments comparing to a clairvoyant SIEVE-STREAMING baseline; STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably or better across tasks (dominating set, recommendations) and removal strategies.

- Weakness: proven approximation constant (~0.149) is modest relative to non-robust or non-streaming guarantees.

- Weakness: theoretical requirement on parameter w (scaling with m and log k) seems loose versus empirical success with w = 1, indicating a gap between theory and practice.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel problem, first constant-factor guarantee in this setting, strong theory and experiments, and improved memory complexity.

- Arguments against acceptance: low approximation constant and looseness of theoretical parameter bounds.

- Conclusion: overall strong and timely contribution; strengths outweigh weaknesses and likely to inspire follow-up work.
