The paper "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach" presents a novel algorithm, STAR-T, for robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors tackle a challenging problem, where the goal is to find a small representative subset of elements that is robust against the removal of up to m elements. The algorithm makes one pass over the data and retains a set of size O((k + m log k) log k) elements, achieving a constant-factor approximation guarantee.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their algorithm and its theoretical guarantees. The use of a novel partitioning structure and an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule is innovative and allows for a robust solution. The analysis is thorough, and the authors provide a detailed proof of their main result.

The paper is related to previous work on submodular maximization, streaming algorithms, and robust optimization. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of the existing literature and clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions. In particular, they highlight the novelty of their approach in the streaming setting and the robustness requirement.

The experimental evaluation is convincing, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their algorithm in two different data summarization applications: dominating set and personalized movie recommendation. The results show that STAR-T-GREEDY, the greedy algorithm that runs on the obtained summary, performs comparably to or outperforms existing greedy and streaming methods, even when they are allowed to know the removed subset in advance.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel algorithm for robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of their algorithm and its theoretical guarantees.
* The experimental evaluation is convincing and demonstrates the effectiveness of the algorithm in two different applications.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes that the value f(OPT(k, V \ E)) is known, which might not be the case in practice. However, the authors provide a solution to this problem in Section 4.1.
* The algorithm requires a memory parameter w that depends on m, which might be difficult to set in practice.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and innovative solution to a challenging problem.
* The analysis is thorough, and the authors provide a detailed proof of their main result.
* The experimental evaluation is convincing and demonstrates the effectiveness of the algorithm in two different applications.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The assumption that the value f(OPT(k, V \ E)) is known might not be realistic in practice.
* The algorithm requires a memory parameter w that depends on m, which might be difficult to set in practice.

Overall, I believe that the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their algorithm and its theoretical guarantees. The experimental evaluation is convincing, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm in two different applications. I recommend accepting the paper.
