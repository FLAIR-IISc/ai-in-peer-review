Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in a single-pass streaming setting, where up to m items may be removed from memory after the stream. It proposes a two-stage method: (i) STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that stores a partitioned summary S composed of exponentially sized buckets with exponentially decreasing (greedy-style) thresholds; and (ii) STAR-T-GREEDY, which, given any removal set E of size at most m, runs GREEDY on S\E to return k items. The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation: for appropriate parameters, f(STAR-T-GREEDY) ≥ c·f(OPT(k, V\E)) with c → 0.149 as k grows, using memory O((k + m log k) log k) or, with the parallel-guess trick for unknown OPT, O((k + m log k) log k log_{1+ε} k). Empirically, the approach performs on par with or better than SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., KDD’14), even when the latter is given the removed set E in advance, on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks.

The paper builds on two lines of work: (a) robust (non-streaming) submodular maximization with partitioned constructions (e.g., PRO; Bogunovic et al., ICML’17) and (b) streaming submodular maximization via thresholding (SIEVE-STREAMING). It also relates to concurrent deletion-robust streaming work (Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17), which attains a 1/2 − ε approximation with memory O(mk log k/ε), versus this paper’s smaller memory scaling in k at the cost of a smaller constant.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The algorithmic design and analysis are coherent. The partitioned, exponentially sized buckets with decreasing thresholds create redundancy to withstand deletions, and the query-stage GREEDY leverages standard (1 − 1/e)-type bounds. The two case analyses (many full buckets vs. few) are reasonable and the constants are derived by balancing complementary lemmas. The extension to unknown OPT via parallel guesses is standard.
- Assumptions/parameters: The method needs a memory parameter w and a threshold τ that depends on f(OPT(k, V\E)). The latter is handled via parallel instances at extra log factor cost, which is acceptable. The required scaling w ≥ ⌈4 m log k / k⌉ for the theory is explicit; empirically w=1 often suffices, though more guidance on choosing w would help.
- Proof details: The main proof ideas are presented clearly; complete proofs are claimed in the supplement.
- Minor technical issues to fix:
  - Space complexity is stated as O((k + m log k) log k) in Theorem 4.1 but O((k + m log k) log^2 k) in the abstract and conclusion; please reconcile.
  - Threshold definition: The text describes thresholds τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Clarify which is intended and ensure the analysis matches the implementation.
  - Notation and constants (e.g., dlog ke) occasionally lead to small off-by-one factors; a polishing pass would improve precision.

Clarity:
- The paper is overall well written and organized: problem setup, algorithm descriptions, and the two-stage structure are clear. The figures (not shown here) reportedly illustrate the partitioned structure; algorithms are easy to follow.
- The description of the case analysis and how constants are obtained is helpful, though the above inconsistencies should be corrected. Some intuition for the role of w and its practical tuning would further aid readers.

Originality:
- The combination of partitioned robust summaries with streaming thresholding is novel. While inspired by PRO’s partitioning (non-streaming) and SIEVE-STREAMING’s thresholds, the specific exponentially decreasing threshold scheme across partitions, and the robust analysis yielding deletion tolerance with memory O(m log^2 k + k log k), are new. Compared to concurrent [18], the different trade-off (smaller memory dependence on k, worse constant) is meaningful.

Significance:
- Robust streaming summarization is practically relevant (deletions due to privacy, failures, or user feedback). The theoretical guarantee provides a constant-factor approximation with space sublinear in n and only logarithmic in k (for the m-dependent part), improving over prior streaming robust methods’ linear dependence on k.
- Empirically, matching SIEVE-STREAMING even when it knows E is compelling. The applications (dominating set; recommendations) are standard and appropriate; the “interactive” reuse of a single summary for different removals is a practical advantage.
- Limitations: The constant 0.149 is modest compared to 1/2 − ε in [18]; however, the memory trade-off can matter in large k regimes. Experiments do not compare to [18] or other deletion-robust baselines; runtime/space measurements and sensitivity to m and w are limited. Still, the contributions advance the state of the art.

Pros:
- New streaming algorithm with robust guarantee under deletions, using a principled partitioned-threshold design.
- Constant-factor approximation with space O((k + m log k) log k) (or with an extra log factor for unknown OPT).
- Clear two-stage pipeline enabling multiple post-hoc queries/removals without revisiting the stream.
- Solid empirical results on two tasks; competitive with strong streaming baselines even under an adversarial evaluation protocol.

Cons:
- Inconsistencies in space bound and threshold specification; needs clarification.
- Approximation factor (≈0.149) is relatively small.
- No experimental comparison to concurrent deletion-robust streaming work; limited analysis of sensitivity to m and w, and limited reporting of time/memory usage.
- Dependence on an approximation to f(OPT) requires multiple instances, inflating space by a log factor.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Reconcile the log vs. log^2 space claims and ensure the algorithm text matches the analysis (threshold rule).
- Add empirical comparisons to [18] if feasible, or at least a more detailed discussion of trade-offs.
- Report wall-clock time and memory usage, and sensitivity of performance to m and w.
- Provide guidance for choosing w in practice, possibly with adaptive schemes.

Overall evaluation:
The paper presents a well-motivated and technically nontrivial contribution to robust streaming submodular maximization, with novel algorithmic design, provable guarantees, and encouraging experiments. Despite minor presentation inconsistencies and a modest constant, the work meets NIPS quality standards in quality, clarity (subject to fixes), originality, and significance. I lean toward acceptance.
