

# Peer Review: "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper addresses a challenging variant of submodular maximization where elements arrive in a streaming fashion and m elements are removed from the algorithm's summary after the stream. The authors propose a two-stage algorithm (STAR-T for the streaming stage and STAR-T-GREEDY for the query stage) that achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee even after any m elements are removed from the summary. The key innovation is a novel partitioning structure where the summary is arranged into partitions with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds.

The paper builds on previous work in two important areas: streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., PRO [11]). While SIEVE-STREAMING achieves a (0.5-ε)-approximation with O(k log k) memory, and PRO provides robustness with a similar memory complexity, the authors introduce a partitioned thresholding approach that achieves robustness against any m removals with O((k + m log k) log k) memory. This is significantly better than concurrent work [18], which requires O(mk log k/ε) memory.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound algorithm with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The main result (Theorem 4.1) is well-supported by multiple lemmas (4.2-4.4) that carefully handle different cases of the partitioned structure. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach, particularly the need for an approximation of f(OPT(k, V \ E)), and provide a solution (Theorem 4.5) that maintains the constant-factor guarantee with only a logarithmic increase in memory. The experimental section is well-designed, demonstrating that the algorithm matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows in advance which elements will be removed. The experiments cover two distinct applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) with appropriate baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear, the algorithms are precisely described (with Algorithms 1 and 2), and the theoretical results are well-motivated. The proof sketches are helpful for understanding the main ideas, though the complete proofs are in the supplementary material. The experimental setup is clearly explained, and the results are presented with appropriate figures. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by combining partitioned structures with exponentially decreasing thresholds to solve the robust streaming submodular maximization problem. This approach is distinct from both previous streaming algorithms (which don't handle robustness) and previous robust algorithms (which don't handle streaming). The paper's main theoretical result is novel, as confirmed by the comparison with concurrent work [18]. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work, including the recent PRO algorithm [11] and the concurrent deletion-robust submodular maximization work [18].

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world applications where data is too large to store in memory and robustness against element removal is critical (e.g., social networks, recommendation systems). The two-stage approach allows precomputing a robust summary that can be used for multiple query scenarios, which is a practical advantage. The experimental results demonstrate that the method outperforms or matches existing methods even with the theoretical gap (0.149 vs. 0.5-ε), showing that the theoretical guarantee is not overly pessimistic. This work advances the state of the art in submodular optimization for large-scale applications.

**Strengths:**
- Novel partitioned thresholding approach that achieves robustness with better memory complexity than concurrent work
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with constant-factor approximation guarantee
- Strong experimental validation showing practical superiority over baselines
- Well-structured and clearly written paper with appropriate technical depth

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical approximation guarantee (0.149) is lower than the standard greedy algorithm's (1-1/e) and SIEVE-STREAMING's (0.5-ε), though the experiments show better practical performance
- The need for an approximation of f(OPT(k, V \ E)) requires additional memory (log1+ k factor), though this is well-handled in Theorem 4.5
- The parameter w might require tuning in practice, though the authors show w=1 works well

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of submodular optimization, addressing an important problem with both theoretical novelty and practical relevance. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the experimental validation is convincing. The paper is clearly written, and the authors have carefully considered related work, including concurrent research. The memory complexity advantage over [18] is significant, and the practical performance matches or exceeds existing methods.

I recommend acceptance. The paper advances the state of the art in robust submodular maximization and provides both theoretical guarantees and practical validation. The work is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with significant theoretical and practical contributions.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel partitioned thresholding approach for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Strong theoretical guarantee with rigorous proof
- Practical experiments demonstrating superior or comparable performance
- Clear writing and well-structured presentation
- Significant improvement in memory complexity over concurrent work

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Theoretical approximation guarantee (0.149) is lower than standard greedy
- Requires approximation of f(OPT(k, V \ E)) which increases memory by log1+ k
- The practical advantage over baselines might be attributed to the two-stage approach rather than the robustness itself

Overall, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and it makes a valuable contribution to the field.
