Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies robust streaming maximization of a monotone submodular function under a cardinality constraint k, when up to m items may be removed after the stream. The authors propose a two-stage framework: (i) a one-pass streaming algorithm, STAR-T, that builds a compact, robust summary S using a partitioned structure of buckets with exponentially increasing capacities and exponentially decreasing thresholds; and (ii) a post-removal query procedure, STAR-T-GREEDY, that runs GREEDY on the remaining elements S \ E to return a size-k solution. The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation f(Z) ≥ c f(OPT(k, V \ E)) with c ≈ 0.149(1 − 1/log k), while storing O((k + m log k) log k) elements (and O((k + m log k) log k log_{1+ε} k) when OPT is unknown and thresholds are guessed in parallel). Empirically, on dominating set and personalized recommendation tasks, STAR-T matches or outperforms strong streaming baselines even when those baselines are given the removed set in advance.

This work meaningfully extends streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [Badanidiyuru et al., KDD’14]) to a robust deletion setting, drawing inspiration from non-streaming robust constructions such as PRO [Bogunovic et al., ICML’17] and the earlier robust selection literature [Krause et al., JMLR’08; Orlin et al., IPCO’16]. It is contemporaneous with deletion-robust streaming work [Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17], which achieved a 1/2 − ε approximation at the cost of O(mk log k/ε) memory; here, the dependence on k is improved to logarithmic at the cost of a smaller approximation factor.

Quality:
- Theory: The analysis is careful and novel. The partitioned bucket design with exponentially decreasing thresholds and the case analysis (many full buckets vs. not) are sound and reminiscent of robust non-streaming constructions but adapted to one-pass streaming through thresholding. The main constant (≈0.149) is modest but nontrivial given deletion robustness and limited memory. The dependence on m is near-linear in m log k, which is attractive.
- Assumptions/parameters: The algorithm requires m (to set w) and an estimate τ ≈ f(OPT) during streaming; the parallel-threshold variant addresses the latter with a standard log-factor overhead. The need to know m in advance limits adaptivity if the removal budget is mis-specified.
- Completeness: The paper gives a clear proof sketch and references supplementary details. However, there are inconsistencies in space bounds across sections (see clarity below), and runtime complexity per element is not analyzed.

Clarity:
- The high-level idea and algorithmic description are clear. The partitioned structure and acceptance rule (first non-full bucket with marginal ≥ τ/min{2^i, k}) are well motivated.
- Minor inconsistencies/typos materially hinder precision:
  - Space bound is stated as O((m log k + k) log^2 k) in the abstract and conclusion, but Theorem 4.1 and Eq. (3) imply O((k + m log k) log k); the parallel-threshold variant adds another log_{1+ε} k factor. Please reconcile and state the exact bounds separately for the known-OPT and unknown-OPT settings.
  - The formula for τ in Theorem 4.1 appears garbled; specify τ as a clean constant multiple of f(OPT(k, V \ E)).
  - Early text says thresholds are τ/2^i; Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Clarify this (they coincide except in the last partition).
- The related work discussion is appropriate and positions the contribution well, including the concurrent work [18].

Originality:
- The combination of partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds in a streaming setting, and the robust analysis guaranteeing good performance after arbitrary deletions, appears novel. The construction is a principled streaming analogue of PRO’s non-uniform partitioning.
- The memory–approximation trade-off compared to [18] (logarithmic vs. linear in k) is a meaningful new angle.

Significance:
- Robust streaming summarization is practically important (e.g., “right to be forgotten,” fault tolerance, preference shifts). Achieving constant-factor robustness with one pass and space sublinear in n and only logarithmic in k (besides m log k) is valuable.
- The approximation constant is smaller than 1/2 − ε results but the improved memory scaling can matter in large-k applications. The empirical results showing parity with strong baselines even when they know E in advance strengthen the case.

Empirical evaluation:
- The two applications (dominating set on Twitter/Amazon graphs; MovieLens recommendations) are relevant. It is commendable that the baselines are advantaged (access to V \ E) while STAR-T uses only S \ E.
- Missing pieces:
  - No runtime measurements or per-element processing cost comparison. STAR-T potentially tests each arriving item against O(∑_i w·k/2^i) = O(wk) buckets; with parallel thresholds this could be multiplied by O(log k). SIEVE-STREAMING is typically O(log k) per element. Please report wall-clock time and per-element evaluations.
  - Sensitivity to m and w: Experiments fix w=1 even when theory suggests larger w for robustness level m; it would be useful to sweep w and m, report how performance degrades when m exceeds the designed robustness, and validate the predicted scaling.
  - Comparison to [18]: While concurrent, a post-hoc discussion or experiment contrasting memory–quality trade-offs would help.
  - Clarify how marginal gains are computed efficiently in the streaming setting for the facility location term; discuss any preprocessing and update costs.

Pros:
- Conceptually neat and simple two-stage robust streaming framework.
- Strong theoretical guarantees with one pass and space O((k + m log k) log k) (or plus an extra log factor without OPT).
- Clear advantage in memory dependence on k compared to concurrent robust streaming work.
- Empirical results competitive with strong baselines that are given more information.

Cons:
- Approximation constant is relatively small (≈0.149), and degrades for very small k.
- Requires prior knowledge of m and either τ or multiple parallel thresholds; computational overhead per element is not analyzed empirically.
- Some presentation inconsistencies (space bounds, τ definition).
- Experimental coverage could be broader (runtime, sensitivity to m/w, adversarial deletions targeting specific buckets).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Reconcile and clearly state the space bounds in all sections; fix the τ expression.
- Add a thorough runtime analysis and measurements, including per-element marginal evaluations and wall-clock comparisons to SIEVE-STREAMING.
- Provide sensitivity studies over m and w, and report robustness when m is mis-specified.
- Include a discussion and, if feasible, an experimental comparison to [18], highlighting the memory–quality trade-off.
- Consider whether query-time SIEVE-STREAMING vs. GREEDY changes guarantees; formalize if possible.
- Discuss practicality for large-scale facility-location-like objectives: data structures, incremental maintenance, and caching.

Overall recommendation: This paper presents a solid, well-motivated contribution to robust streaming submodular maximization with novel analysis and practical promise. With clarified bounds and stronger empirical evaluation (especially runtime and sensitivity), it would be a strong NIPS paper.
