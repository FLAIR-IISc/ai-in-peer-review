### Review

**Summary of the Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k`, but under the challenging combination of a streaming setting and a robustness requirement. The goal is to design a single-pass streaming algorithm that produces a small, robust summary `S` of the data. At a later query stage, after an arbitrary set of `m` elements is removed from this summary, a high-quality solution of size `k` must be constructed using only the remaining elements of `S`, without re-accessing the original data stream.

The authors propose a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a novel streaming algorithm called STAR-T constructs the summary `S`. The core idea of STAR-T is a carefully designed structure for `S`, which is organized into `~log k` partitions. Each partition `i` contains multiple buckets of size `min(2^i, k)` and is associated with a threshold that decreases exponentially with `i`. An incoming element is placed into the first available bucket whose partition's threshold is met by the element's marginal gain. In the second stage, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining summary `S \ E` to find the final solution.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of approximately 0.149. The summary `S` created by STAR-T requires `O((k + m log k) log k)` memory. The work builds upon key ideas in the submodular optimization literature. It adapts the thresholding technique from the non-robust streaming algorithm SIEVE-STREAMING [8] and combines it with a partitioned summary structure inspired by recent work on centralized robust submodular maximization [11]. The paper also appropriately positions itself against concurrent work [18], highlighting a key difference in memory complexity, which is logarithmic in `k` for the proposed method versus linear in `k` for [18], making it more suitable for scenarios with large `k`.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem. The "summarize-then-act" paradigm, where a summary must be robust to future removals or filtering (e.g., due to user feedback, system failures, or privacy requests), is a crucial challenge in large-scale machine learning. The combination of streaming constraints with this robustness model is novel and significant. The proposed algorithm and its analysis represent a strong original contribution to this area.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed STAR-T algorithm is elegant, and its design is well-motivated. The theoretical analysis provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is a strong result for such a constrained problem. The memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)` improves upon concurrent work, particularly in its dependence on `k`, which is an important theoretical advance. The paper also correctly handles the practical issue of not knowing the optimal value a priori by using a standard parallel-instances approach.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. It begins with a good motivation, a precise problem statement, and a concise overview of related work. The algorithm is presented clearly, and the proof sketch in the main paper provides good intuition for the main analytical steps.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are a major strength of the paper. They are conducted on real-world datasets for two compelling applications: dominating set selection and personalized movie recommendation. The comparison against strong baselines is particularly convincing; STAR-T performs comparably or even better than SIEVE-STREAMING, even when the latter is given the significant advantage of knowing the removed set `E` in advance. The interactive movie recommendation experiment is an excellent showcase of the practical utility of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity on Technical Details:** While generally clear, a few technical points could be clarified:
    *   **Memory Complexity:** There appear to be minor inconsistencies in the stated memory bound across the paper. The abstract and conclusion mention `O((...)log^2 k)`, while Theorem 4.1 gives `O((...)log k)`. A careful check and consistent reporting of the tightest bound would improve clarity.
    *   **Algorithm Threshold:** In Algorithm 1 (line 5), the threshold condition is `f(e | B_ij) >= τ/min{2^i, k}`, whereas the preceding text describes the partition `i` threshold as `τ/2^i`. These are only identical for partitions where `2^i < k`. This is a minor point but should be stated precisely for reproducibility.
    *   **Parameter `w`:** The theoretical requirement for the memory parameter is `w >= O(m log k / k)`. However, the experiments show that `w=1` works very well. A brief discussion on this theory-practice gap would be insightful. Is the theoretical analysis of `w` loose, or are the experimental removals not worst-case?

2.  **Approximation Constant:** The proven approximation ratio of `c ≈ 0.149` is somewhat low from a theoretical standpoint, although obtaining any constant factor is a significant achievement. The excellent empirical results suggest that this constant is likely an artifact of the proof technique (e.g., balancing multiple worst-case bounds). A brief comment on the potential tightness of this constant would be welcome.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a novel and important problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization, with clear real-world applications.
*   It presents an elegant and effective algorithm (STAR-T) with strong theoretical guarantees, including a constant-factor approximation and favorable memory complexity compared to related work.
*   The empirical validation is thorough and compelling, demonstrating that the method is not only theoretically sound but also practically effective, even when compared to oracle--based baselines.
*   The work represents a significant and complete scientific contribution to the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical approximation constant is modest.
*   There are minor clarity issues in the technical presentation that could be improved in a final version.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions to the literature on large-scale submodular optimization. The problem is novel and important, the proposed solution is technically sound and well-motivated, and the experimental results are strong. The weaknesses are minor and can be easily addressed in a camera-ready version. The paper's strengths, particularly its combination of theoretical novelty and practical relevance, far outweigh its weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
