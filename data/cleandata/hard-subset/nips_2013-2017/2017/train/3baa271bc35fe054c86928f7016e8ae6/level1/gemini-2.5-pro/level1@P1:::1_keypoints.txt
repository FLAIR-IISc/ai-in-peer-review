- Problem: maximize a monotone submodular function under cardinality k in a single-pass streaming setting where a small summary must be robust to removal of any m elements at query time.  
- Method: two-stage algorithm — STAR-T builds a partitioned summary S in one pass; STAR-T-GREEDY runs greedy on S \ E to produce the final solution.  
- STAR-T design: ~log k partitions with buckets of size min(2^i,k) and exponentially decreasing thresholds; elements added to first bucket meeting the threshold.  
- Theoretical guarantee: constant-factor approximation of about 0.149.  
- Memory: summary size/memory claimed as O((k + m log k) log k) (noted improvement over some concurrent work).  
- Relation to prior work: adapts thresholding from SIEVE-STREAMING and partitioning ideas from centralized robust submodular maximization; contrasts with concurrent work [18] having linear-in-k memory.  
- Strength — significance/originality: tackles a novel, practical "summarize-then-act" problem combining streaming and robustness.  
- Strength — technical quality: elegant algorithm with nontrivial analysis and favorable memory dependence on k; handles unknown OPT via parallel instances.  
- Strength — clarity/organization: well-written, motivated, with clear presentation and proof sketch.  
- Strength — experiments: strong empirical validation on real tasks (dominating set, movie recommendation) where STAR-T matches or beats baselines, including oracle-like ones.  
- Weakness — memory reporting: inconsistency in stated memory bound (abstract/conclusion mention log^2 k vs theorem stating log k) needs resolution.  
- Weakness — threshold description: minor mismatch between algorithm line (τ/min{2^i,k}) and textual description (τ/2^i); should be clarified.  
- Weakness — parameter w: theory requires w ≥ O(m log k / k) but experiments use w=1 successfully; discuss theory-practice gap or looseness of bound.  
- Weakness — approximation constant: proved constant ≈0.149 is modest and may be an artifact of analysis; a comment on potential tightness would help.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel and important problem, elegant algorithm with theoretical guarantees, improved memory scaling, and convincing empirical results.  
- Rejection arguments: relatively low proven approximation constant and minor technical clarity issues (fixable in final version).  
- Overall conclusion: strong paper with significant contributions and practical impact; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
