

This paper addresses streaming robust submodular maximization, proposing a two-stage algorithm (STAR-T and STAR-T-GREEDY) that constructs a summary set T of size O((k + m log k) log^2 k) such that after any m elements are removed, a simple greedy algorithm on the remaining elements achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the objective function.

The paper builds upon prior work on robust submodular maximization (Krause et al., 2008; Bogunovic et al., 2017) and streaming submodular maximization (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014). The key innovation lies in their partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds, which enables the algorithm to handle arbitrary element removals while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

The technical quality is strong - the algorithm design is non-trivial, and the analysis, while complex, appears correct. The theoretical results are well-supported, and the experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance against SIEVE-STREAMING in two practical applications (dominating set and movie recommendation), even when the baseline knows the removed elements in advance.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses. The motivation for robustness is superficial, providing only generic examples like "movie recommendations" without concrete application contexts where robustness is truly critical. The problem formulation is overly narrow, focusing solely on cardinality constraints rather than more complex constraints common in machine learning applications. This limits the paper's relevance to NIPS, which typically prioritizes work with strong connections to core machine learning problems and applications.

The significance is questionable - while the theoretical contribution is sound, the practical impact is unclear given the limited motivation and narrow problem scope. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how this robustness requirement arises naturally in real-world machine learning scenarios beyond the basic examples provided.

Pro: Strong technical contribution with non-trivial algorithm design and analysis; experimental results show promise.

Con: Weak motivation and applications; problem formulation too narrow; questionable relevance to NIPS focus areas.

Overall, while technically sound, the paper's narrow focus and weak motivation make it difficult to justify as a significant contribution to the NIPS community. The problem is not general enough to warrant publication in a top-tier machine learning conference.
