This paper studies robust submodular maximization with a cardinality constraint in a one-pass streaming setting, where up to m items may be removed after the stream. The authors propose a two-stage approach: (1) STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that builds a compact, robust summary using a partitioned bucket structure and exponentially decreasing thresholds; (2) a query-stage algorithm, STAR-T-GREEDY, that runs standard GREEDY on the remaining summary after any subset E of size ≤ m is removed. The main theoretical result (Theorem 4.1) proves a constant-factor approximation c ≈ 0.149 (as k → ∞), using memory O((k + m log k) log k) when τ is known, and O((k + m log k) log k log1+ k) when τ is approximated via parallel instances (Theorem 4.5). The analysis hinges on two cases: at least half of the buckets in some partition are full (Lemma 4.2), or all partitions are mostly not full (Lemmas 4.3–4.4), with guarantees balanced via τ. Experiments on dominating set (coverage-style influence) and personalized movie recommendation show that STAR-T-GREEDY matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter is allowed to know the removed set E in advance.

Relation to prior work. The streaming non-robust setting is well-studied (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]). Robust (non-streaming) submodular maximization has recent constant-factor algorithms via partitioned constructions (e.g., PRO [11], Orlin et al. [10]). This paper adapts partition ideas to streaming, combining them with thresholding akin to SIEVE-STREAMING. An independent contemporaneous work [18] gives a deletion-robust streaming algorithm with approximation 1/2 − ε but memory O(mk log k/ε) (linear in k), whereas STAR-T achieves polylog(k) dependence for the m term, improving memory in many regimes at the cost of a smaller approximation constant.

Quality
- Theory: The partitioned thresholding design and analysis are technically sound and original. The two-case analysis is clear, and the memory bound derivation is reasonable. The dependence on w ≥ ⌈4⌈log k⌉m/k⌉ is explicit.
- Assumptions and parameters: The method needs τ ≈ f(OPT(k, V\E)). The parallel-threshold trick is standard and handled appropriately (Theorem 4.5), though it increases memory/time by a log factor. The constant 0.149 is modest, but constant-factor robustness in streaming is nontrivial.
- Empirics: The experiments demonstrate robustness and practical competitiveness but are limited in scope. The influence experiments use coverage f(Z)=|N(Z)∪Z| rather than stochastic diffusion (IC/LT), limiting conclusions for classical influence maximization.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written, with clear algorithm descriptions and proof structure. The two-stage setting and robustness notion are explained well.
- Minor inconsistencies to fix:
  - Threshold description: text says threshold τ/2^i per partition, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Please reconcile.
  - Memory bound wording: the abstract states O((m log k + k) log^2 k); Theorem 4.1 gives O((k + m log k) log k); Theorem 4.5 adds a log1+ k factor. Unify these statements and clearly separate cases with/without τ-approximation.
- Practical guidance: Provide clearer directions for choosing w and τ (or the τ-grid) in practice, including sensitivities.

Originality
- To my knowledge, this is the first streaming algorithm tailored to robust submodular maximization with polylogarithmic dependence on k in the memory term for m, using a novel partitioned thresholding scheme. The analysis differs from non-streaming PRO [11] and extends SIEVE-STREAMING’s thresholding to a robust, partitioned setting.
- Independent concurrent work [18] addresses deletion-robustness but with different trade-offs (better approximation, worse memory). The comparison is acknowledged; more empirical and theoretical discussion would strengthen the positioning.

Significance
- Robustness to deletions is practically important (privacy/“right to be forgotten”, failures, personalization). The memory/time-footprint and single-pass property make the approach attractive at scale.
- The constant is small but practically the method performs comparably to strong baselines; the memory advantage relative to [18] can be impactful when k is large and m is moderate-to-large.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean two-stage method with a compact, reusable summary.
- Theoretical constant-factor guarantee under deletions with near-linear memory in k plus O(m log k log k) factors.
- Practical implementation is simple in the query phase (standard GREEDY).
- Empirical results show competitive performance even against baselines with knowledge of E.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope is limited; STAR-T-GREEDY does not consistently outperform SIEVE-STREAMING, and evaluations avoid standard IC/LT diffusion models.
- Parameterization: w is set to 1 in experiments, which violates the theoretical requirement for large m; more ablations vs m/k, w, and τ would be informative.
- Per-element processing time may be high: in the worst case, each streamed element checks O(wk log k) buckets; wall-clock measurements are missing.
- Approximation constant is modest; practical competitive edge is not conclusively demonstrated across tasks.
- Minor inconsistencies/clarity issues as noted above.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-analyzed streaming algorithm for a timely robust objective, with strong memory-approximation trade-off.
- Clean design and reusable robust summaries enable multiple post-hoc queries/removal scenarios.
- Solid theoretical contribution and promising empirical evidence.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental validation (no IC/LT, few datasets), and lack of consistent empirical gains over SIEVE-STREAMING.
- Practical guidance on parameters and runtime/memory costs is light; no comparisons against concurrent [18].
- Some clarity inconsistencies (threshold and memory statements) should be fixed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on stochastic diffusion (IC/LT) using standard influence maximization evaluation, and on larger-scale datasets; include time/memory breakdowns.
- Compare against [18] empirically and discuss regimes where each method is preferable.
- Provide ablations over w, τ-grid density, and m/k; include sensitivity plots and practical defaults.
- Report per-element processing cost and total runtime versus SIEVE-STREAMING and GREEDY; discuss implementation optimizations (e.g., early stopping, bucket indexing).
- Clarify threshold rule (τ/2^i vs τ/min{2^i, k}) and reconcile memory bounds across sections; add guidance for choosing w as a function of target m.

Overall: a solid theoretical advance with a clear algorithmic idea and plausible practical impact; the paper would be strengthened by broader, more canonical experiments and clarified presentation. I lean toward acceptance if the empirical and clarity issues can be addressed.
