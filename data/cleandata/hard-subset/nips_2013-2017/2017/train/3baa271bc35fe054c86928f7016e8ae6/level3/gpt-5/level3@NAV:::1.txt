This paper studies streaming robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint: after a single pass over a stream and construction of a summary T, the algorithm guarantees that even after deletion of any m items from T one can still extract a size-k set S with f(S) within a constant factor of OPT on the remaining elements. The proposed STAR-T algorithm uses a partitioned summary with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing thresholds; at query time, STAR-T-GREEDY runs standard greedy on T \ E. The main theorem shows a constant-factor (≈0.149) approximation with a one-pass algorithm that stores O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements, improving the dependence on k over a concurrent deletion-robust streaming approach. The work builds on and extends ideas from SIEVE-STREAMING (KDD’14) and robust non-streaming formulations (IPCO’16; ICML’17), and relates to NeurIPS work on submodular summarization and scalable optimization.

Quality: The technical development appears sound. The partitioned thresholding design and its careful case analysis are non-trivial, and the tradeoffs between bucket fullness and thresholds are convincingly handled. The dependence on unknown OPT is resolved via standard parallel guesses. Experiments on dominating set and MovieLens are reasonable and allow a strong baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING) advance knowledge of deletions; STAR-T remains competitive. However, empirical evaluation is limited in scope, with little sensitivity analysis (e.g., w, m, τ-grids), no oracle-query/runtime reporting, and no ablations to isolate the value of partitioning versus thresholds.

Clarity: Overall clear and well organized. Some constants and asymptotics are inconsistent across sections (O((k + m log k) log k) vs. O((m log k + k) log^2 k)); please standardize. The exposition around τ and the multi-instance scheme could be tightened, and pseudocode should match the described thresholds.

Originality: The combination of partitioned summaries with exponentially decreasing thresholds in a streaming-robust setting is novel, and the memory-vs-k improvement over concurrent work is interesting.

Significance: The theoretical contribution is meaningful for scalable summarization with deletions. That said, the robustness model (deletions from the summary, monotone f, cardinality only) feels narrow; stronger applications and broader constraints (matroid/knapsack, non-monotone, multiple deletion rounds) would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Non-trivial, correct-looking theory with improved memory dependence.
- Simple two-stage design; single pass; clear constant-factor guarantee.
- Competitive empirical results under strong baselines.

Cons against acceptance:
- Motivation and application stories are weak; evaluation limited.
- Narrow problem formulation; small constant and log^2 factors.
- Borderline fit for NeurIPS without stronger ML-facing evidence and insights.

Suggestions: Strengthen application sections with real deletion scenarios (e.g., right-to-be-forgotten), report time/query complexity, provide sensitivity/ablation studies, and discuss extensions beyond monotone cardinality.
