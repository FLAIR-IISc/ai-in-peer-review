Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in a single-pass streaming setting, where up to m items may be deleted from memory after the stream finishes. The authors propose a two-stage method: (i) STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that maintains a compact, robust summary S using a partitioned bucket structure with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing thresholds; and (ii) STAR-T-GREEDY, a query-time greedy procedure run on S \ E (for any deletion set E, |E| ≤ m) to return a k-element set. The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation guarantee: with memory O((k + m log k) log^2 k) elements and one pass, STAR-T-GREEDY yields f(Z) ≥ c f(OPT(k, V \ E)) with c ≈ 0.149 as k → ∞. When f(OPT) is unknown, they adopt the standard “multi-guess” strategy (as in SIEVE-STREAMING), incurring a log_{1+ε} k factor in space and a (1+ε) loss in approximation.

The work builds on the standard greedy and thresholding approaches for submodular maximization, most notably SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., KDD’14) for non-robust streaming, and the partitioning ideas used in offline robust methods such as PRO (Bogunovic et al., ICML’17) and earlier robust formulations (Orlin et al., IPCO’16). It is concurrent with Mirzasoleiman et al. (ICML’17), who give a deletion-robust streaming algorithm with a 1/2−ε guarantee but memory O(mk log k/ε). Compared to that, STAR-T trades a smaller approximation constant for significantly better dependence on k in memory (logarithmic vs. linear), which is attractive when k is large.

Strengths
- Novelty: To my knowledge, this is the first streaming algorithm with provable robustness to m adversarial deletions and a constant-factor guarantee, with memory scaling only logarithmically in k (up to log factors).
- Sound theory: The partitioned, decreasing-threshold structure is well-motivated and the analysis covers two complementary cases (many full buckets vs. mostly non-full buckets) via Lemmas 4.2–4.4, culminating in Theorem 4.1. The multi-guess extension is standard and sensible.
- Practicality: The query-time decoupling is useful in practice—one can reuse the same summary S for many different deletion sets E (“right to be forgotten” scenarios).
- Empirics: On two summarization tasks (dominating set proxy and movie recommendation), STAR-T-GREEDY performs on par with strong baselines, even when SIEVE-STREAMING is given the unfair advantage of knowing E in advance. The experiments also hint that w=1 can work well in practice.

Weaknesses
- Approximation constant: The proven constant (~0.149) is modest. While constants are often pessimistic, a discussion of whether this is close to the current analytical limit for this approach would help.
- Parameter dependence and clarity: STAR-T assumes τ ≈ f(OPT), and Section 4.1 replaces this with multiple guesses, increasing space by log_{1+ε} k. Practical guidance on ε and the number of instances is limited. Also, there is a small inconsistency between the prose (“threshold τ/2^i”) and Algorithm 1 (“τ/min{2^i, k}”). Please clarify the intended rule and its role in the proof (the latter seems necessary to ensure each full bucket attains value ≥ τ).
- Experimental scope: 
  - Influence maximization is evaluated via the dominating-set objective |N(Z) ∪ Z|, which is a coverage proxy, not the classical Kempe et al. diffusion models. This makes it hard to assess performance on true influence spread. 
  - No runtime/oracle-call measurements or memory usage profiles are reported. Given the per-element per-bucket threshold checks, practical throughput is important.
  - STAR-T-GREEDY does not consistently outperform SIEVE-STREAMING; results are largely parity. Ablations over w and the number of τ-guess instances are missing, and comparisons to Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017) are absent.
- Assumptions on m: STAR-T requires m (or at least an upper bound) to set w. The paper does not discuss strategies when m is unknown or varying.

Quality
- The theoretical development appears careful and technically sound, with a clear high-level proof strategy and supporting lemmas. The constant factor and memory bound are explicit. However, the thresholding rule needs to be stated consistently, and the complexity in terms of function oracle evaluations per element should be analyzed.
- Experimental design is reasonable for a first validation, but lacks breadth (true influence models), ablations, and efficiency metrics.

Clarity
- Overall, the paper is well-organized and readable. The partitioned-threshold idea is explained clearly with intuition. 
- Please fix the thresholding inconsistency and explicitly justify the choice τ/min{2^i, k} vs. τ/2^i in both the algorithm and in the analysis.
- Provide more implementation details: how τ guesses are selected; values of ε; actual memory used; number of buckets traversed per element on average; wall-clock times.

Originality
- The combination of partitioned buckets with decreasing thresholds in a single-pass robust streaming setting is novel. Positioning relative to PRO (offline) and Mirzasoleiman et al. (concurrent) is appropriate. The contribution is distinct from prior NIPS/KDD/ICML work on non-robust streaming (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING).

Significance
- Robustness to deletions is increasingly relevant (“right to be forgotten,” failures, personalization constraints). The improved memory scaling with k can be impactful for large-scale summarization. Stronger empirical validation (especially on true influence spread) would further substantiate significance.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- First constant-factor, single-pass robust streaming method with near-optimal memory dependence on k.
- Clean, reusable two-stage design; strong theory; matches strong baselines empirically.
- Potentially broad applicability in summarization and recommendation with deletions.

Cons:
- Small approximation constant; practical performance parity rather than clear wins.
- Experimental scope limited (no evaluation on diffusion models; no efficiency metrics/ablations).
- Minor clarity issues around thresholding; reliance on τ guesses; handling unknown m not addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the thresholding rule and ensure consistency across text/algorithms/proofs.
- Report runtime, number of oracle calls per element, and actual memory usage. Add ablations over w and ε.
- Evaluate on influence maximization under IC/LT models on moderate graphs (using standard simulators or scalable approximations), and compare with Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017) if possible.
- Discuss strategies when m is unknown (e.g., multiple STAR-T instances for geometrically increasing m or adaptive w).
- If space allows, analyze when STAR-T’s memory bound beats O(mk log k) baselines as a function of k and m, to give practical guidance.

Overall evaluation
A solid theoretical contribution introducing a practically motivated robust streaming approach with attractive memory scaling. While the empirical section could be stronger and some clarity issues remain, the paper advances the state of the art and is appropriate for NIPS. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the thresholding rule and strengthening the empirical evaluation.
