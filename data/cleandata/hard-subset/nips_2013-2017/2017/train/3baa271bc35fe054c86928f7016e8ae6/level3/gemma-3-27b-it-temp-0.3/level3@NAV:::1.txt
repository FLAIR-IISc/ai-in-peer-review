## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper tackles the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, where the goal is to maintain a representative subset of elements arriving in a stream, resilient to the removal of a fixed number of elements. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm utilizing a partitioned thresholding approach to construct a robust summary, followed by a greedy selection on the remaining elements after potential removals. 

The work builds upon existing research in submodular maximization, particularly streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING and robust maximization approaches. It correctly identifies the need for robustness in applications like recommendation systems and data summarization, where elements may become unavailable. The core contribution lies in the design of the partitioned thresholding structure and the associated analysis, which yields a constant-factor approximation guarantee with a summary size of O((m log k + k) log^2 k). The technical quality appears sound; the algorithm and its analysis are non-trivial and demonstrate a good understanding of submodular optimization techniques. 

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. While the motivation is stated, the application examples feel somewhat weak and lack concrete details. The connection to real-world scenarios could be strengthened by providing more compelling use cases and discussing the practical implications of the robustness parameter *m*. More significantly, the problem formulation feels restrictive. The assumption of a fixed number of removable elements *m* might not be suitable for all applications, and a more general formulation allowing for a distribution over removals could be considered.

A key concern is the relevance to NIPS. While technically sound, the problem setting feels somewhat specialized and the contribution, while novel, doesn't represent a significant paradigm shift in the field. The paper’s scope feels narrower than typical NIPS submissions. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, but it remains a valid point.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and provides a constant-factor approximation for a challenging problem.
*   Non-trivial algorithm and analysis demonstrate expertise in submodular optimization.
*   Presents a novel partitioning structure and thresholding rule.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak application motivation and limited discussion of practical implications.
*   Restrictive problem formulation with a fixed removal parameter *m*.
*   Questionable relevance to the core NIPS audience.



**Overall:** I lean towards **reject** due to the limited scope and questionable relevance to NIPS. While the technical contribution is valuable, the lack of compelling applications and the restrictive problem formulation diminish its impact. The authors should consider broadening the scope or providing stronger justification for the specific problem setting to appeal to a wider audience.
