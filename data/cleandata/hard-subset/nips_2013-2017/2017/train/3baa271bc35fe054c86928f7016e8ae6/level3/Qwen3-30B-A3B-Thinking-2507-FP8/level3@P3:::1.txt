

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. The authors consider the problem of constructing a small summary set T from a ground set E such that, after any m elements are removed from T, one can still select k elements S with high f(S). The paper proposes a two-stage algorithm: STAR-T (streaming stage) and STAR-T-GREEDY (query stage). STAR-T makes a single pass over the data and constructs a summary set T of size O((m log k + k) log² k), while STAR-T-GREEDY runs on the remaining elements after any m-element removal to achieve a constant-factor approximation guarantee.

The work builds on the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8] for non-robust streaming submodular maximization and extends it to the robust setting. It relates to the robust non-streaming work of Orlin et al. [10] and Bogunovic et al. [11], as well as the independent concurrent work of Mirzasoleiman et al. [18]. The authors introduce a novel partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds, which enables them to handle any number of removals m while maintaining a logarithmic dependence on k in their memory requirement.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with non-trivial algorithm design and analysis. The theoretical results are well-supported by careful proofs that handle the dual challenges of streaming constraints and robustness requirements. The authors prove a constant-factor approximation guarantee (c ≈ 0.149) for their approach, which is theoretically sound and non-trivial. The experimental results on dominating set and movie recommendation problems show that their method matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, even when the latter knows in advance which elements will be removed.

The paper has several strengths: it provides a complete theoretical analysis, the algorithm is simple to implement, and the experimental results are convincing. The technical contribution of the partitioned thresholding approach is novel and well-executed. The paper also demonstrates the practical utility of their method in two different data summarization tasks.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses. The motivation and applications are weak - the paper mentions movie recommendations as an application, but fails to provide compelling evidence of real-world need or practical impact. The problem formulation is not general enough, as it only considers removals from the summary set rather than from the entire ground set, which limits its applicability to many real-world scenarios.

The relevance to NIPS is questionable. While submodular optimization is a relevant topic, the paper's focus on a very specific variant of the problem (streaming with robustness against removals from the summary) doesn't align well with NIPS' broader focus on machine learning, neural networks, and their applications. The paper doesn't make strong connections to machine learning problems or demonstrate how this approach advances the state-of-the-art in ML applications.

The paper would benefit from stronger motivation, more general problem formulation, and better connections to machine learning applications. While the technical contribution is sound, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate why this specific variant of robust submodular maximization matters for machine learning practitioners.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical results with non-trivial analysis
- Correct and technically sound contribution
- Good experimental validation showing practical utility

Arguments against acceptance:
- Weak motivation and application examples
- Problem formulation too narrow and not general enough
- Questionable relevance to NIPS conference scope
- Limited connection to machine learning applications

In conclusion, while the paper presents a technically sound solution to a specific variant of the robust submodular maximization problem, its narrow focus, weak motivation, and limited relevance to NIPS' broader machine learning focus make it a marginal fit for the conference. The technical contribution is solid, but the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate why this particular problem is important for machine learning research. I recommend rejection as the paper doesn't advance the field in a way that aligns with NIPS' scope and priorities.
