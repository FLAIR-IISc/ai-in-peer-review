This paper addresses the problem of robust submodular maximization subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a streaming setting, where a solution must be resilient to the removal of up to `m` elements. The authors propose a novel two-stage procedure: a single-pass streaming algorithm, STAR-T, which uses a partitioned structure and a decreasing threshold rule to build a summary, followed by a standard greedy algorithm that runs on the summary after elements are removed. This work appears to be the first to formally tackle this robust problem in the streaming model, extending concepts from prior work like Sieve-Streaming to a more challenging setting.

**Strengths:**
The main strengths of this paper are its originality and theoretical depth. The problem itself is well-motivated, highly relevant, and has significant practical implications for large-scale data summarization tasks where robustness is critical. The proposed STAR-T algorithm is novel, and the accompanying theoretical analysis is sound, culminating in Theorem 4.1, which establishes a constant-factor approximation guarantee. This is a strong theoretical result for such a constrained problem. Furthermore, the authors thoughtfully address the practical challenge of setting the threshold parameter `τ` by providing an approximation scheme (Theorem 4.5), enhancing the algorithm's usability.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which is not expressive enough to fully substantiate the algorithm's practical advantages. A major concern is that the proposed STAR-T-Greedy method does not consistently outperform the Sieve-Streaming baseline across the experiments. This lack of a clear empirical win makes the practical contribution less compelling.

More critically, the experimental setup for the influence maximization task is questionable. The authors use a simplified objective function (`f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z|`) instead of the standard and more realistic diffusion models (e.g., Independent Cascade or Linear Threshold) established by Kempe et al. This choice makes it unclear how STAR-T would perform on the canonical version of the influence maximization problem, limiting the generalizability of these specific results.

**Summary:**
This paper introduces a theoretically strong and novel algorithm for an important, previously unstudied problem. However, the empirical validation is a significant weakness. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the experiments need to be more convincing, particularly by using standard problem formulations for key applications and demonstrating a more consistent performance gain over relevant baselines.
