

This paper introduces STAR-T, a novel algorithm for robust streaming submodular maximization, which is the first to jointly address both streaming and robustness constraints. The problem involves selecting k elements from a stream while ensuring the solution remains robust to the removal of up to m elements. STAR-T partitions the solution space into exponentially sized buckets with decreasing thresholds, achieving a summary size of O((m log k + k) log² k) and a 0.149 approximation guarantee. The authors demonstrate strong empirical performance on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks, even outperforming algorithms that know the removed elements in advance.

The work makes a significant contribution by combining two challenging settings (streaming and robustness) that have been studied separately. The theoretical result of achieving a constant-factor approximation for the combined problem is indeed strong, representing a meaningful advance over prior work on streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., PRO). The experimental validation across two different applications is thorough and well-presented.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The analysis is poorly explained despite the authors' claim of "innovative analysis"; the proof sketch is difficult to follow, and the claimed constant factor (0.149) is not derived from a clear theoretical foundation. The paper lacks non-trivial lower bounds to complement the upper-bound guarantee, making it difficult to assess the optimality of the approximation ratio. Additionally, the influence maximization experiments use a simplified neighborhood-size objective rather than the traditional network-dependent influence model, raising questions about the practical applicability of the approach.

The writing is unclear in several technical sections, particularly regarding the bucket assignment mechanism and thresholding rules. While the experimental results are promising, the theoretical contribution would be substantially strengthened by a clearer exposition of the analysis and a more realistic experimental setup for influence maximization.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and novel approach to a challenging problem with strong empirical results. However, the unclear analysis and lack of lower bounds significantly undermine the theoretical contribution, and the experimental setup for one key application is questionable. The paper would benefit substantially from clearer exposition and more rigorous theoretical justification.
