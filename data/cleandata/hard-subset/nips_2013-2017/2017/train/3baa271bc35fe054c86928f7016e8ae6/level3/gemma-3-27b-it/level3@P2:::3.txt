This paper introduces a novel algorithm, STAR-T, for robust streaming submodular maximization, a challenging problem combining the complexities of maintaining a representative subset from a data stream while guaranteeing performance even after a subset of elements are removed. The problem is motivated by applications like influence maximization and personalized recommendation where robustness to failures or changing user preferences is crucial. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating a constant-factor approximation guarantee and empirical validation on synthetic and real-world datasets, showcasing competitive performance against existing streaming and robust submodular maximization techniques.

The core idea of STAR-T is to maintain a partitioned summary of the stream, where elements are assigned to buckets within partitions based on a decreasing thresholding rule. This approach allows for a balance between retaining sufficient elements for good approximation and ensuring robustness to removals. The subsequent STAR-T-GREEDY algorithm efficiently extracts a solution from the remaining elements after removals. This work builds upon prior research in submodular maximization [7], streaming algorithms [8, 12, 13], and robust submodular maximization [9, 10, 11], but uniquely tackles the combined streaming and robustness constraints.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of streaming and robustness in submodular maximization is a significant contribution. Existing work typically focuses on one aspect or the other, and this paper directly addresses the intersection of both.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee, while relatively small (0.149), provides a strong theoretical foundation for the algorithm’s performance.
*   **Practical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that STAR-T performs competitively with, and in some cases outperforms, state-of-the-art algorithms in practical applications. The ability to leverage the precomputed robust summary for multiple removal scenarios is particularly valuable.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The problem formulation is well-motivated with relevant applications, and the authors clearly articulate the challenges addressed by their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Analysis:** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in the presentation of the algorithm's analysis. The "innovative analysis" mentioned in the contributions section is not adequately explained, and the provided proof sketch lacks sufficient detail to fully understand the theoretical guarantees. The numerous lemmas mentioned without much context within the main text further hinder understanding.
*   **Lack of Lower Bounds:** The paper only presents upper bounds on the approximation ratio. Establishing lower bounds for this combined problem would strengthen the contribution by demonstrating the theoretical limitations and the quality of the proposed solution.
*   **Experimental Setup:** While the experiments are well-executed, the influence maximization evaluation uses a simplified objective function based on neighborhood size. This raises concerns about the algorithm’s performance on more complex influence models that are commonly used in the literature. The relevance of this simplified objective to the broader field of influence maximization should be better discussed.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithm likely depends on the choice of parameters like `w` and `τ`. A more thorough investigation of the parameter sensitivity and guidelines for choosing appropriate values would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a novel and important problem in submodular maximization.
*   The theoretical results provide a solid foundation for the algorithm's performance.
*   The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on real-world datasets.
*   The contribution extends existing techniques in streaming and robust optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of clarity in the analysis and the absence of lower bounds weaken the theoretical contribution.
*   The simplified experimental setup in influence maximization limits the generalizability of the results.
*   The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of parameter sensitivity.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of submodular maximization by addressing the challenging problem of robust streaming maximization. Despite some weaknesses in clarity and completeness, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the positive empirical results make it a worthwhile contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should focus on improving the clarity of the analysis, adding a discussion of potential lower bounds, and providing a more detailed discussion of the experimental setup and parameter sensitivity. Expanding on the potential application to more realistic influence maximization models would also strengthen the paper.
