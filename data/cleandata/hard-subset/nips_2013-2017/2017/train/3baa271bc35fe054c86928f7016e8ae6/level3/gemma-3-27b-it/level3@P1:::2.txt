This paper introduces STAR-T, a novel two-stage algorithm for robust streaming submodular maximization. The problem addresses scenarios where data arrives sequentially and a representative subset must be maintained, while also being resilient to potential element removals. The authors present a streaming algorithm (STAR-T) that constructs a robust summary, followed by a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) applied to the summary after elements are removed.  STAR-T leverages a partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds to achieve robustness.  The core contribution lies in providing a constant-factor approximation guarantee for this robust streaming setting, a problem not previously addressed with a streaming algorithm.

The work builds upon prior research in submodular maximization, particularly the greedy algorithm [7] and streaming approaches like SIEVE-STREAMING [8].  It also draws inspiration from recent work on robust submodular maximization [9, 10, 11], specifically adapting ideas from partitioning and thresholding. However, unlike prior robust algorithms, STAR-T is designed for the streaming setting, making it applicable to large-scale datasets where repeated access is prohibitive. The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader context of data summarization [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6] and interactive machine learning [16, 17].

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper tackles a challenging and practically relevant problem – robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting – for which a dedicated solution was lacking. The proposed approach is a genuinely novel combination of techniques.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The authors provide a theoretical analysis (Theorem 4.1) demonstrating a constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is crucial for establishing the algorithm's effectiveness.  The breakdown of the proof using lemmas 4.2-4.4 provides insight into the approach's robustness.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The algorithm addresses practical concerns such as the need for a single-pass solution and limited memory. The analysis of the parameter τ and the suggestion that w=1 works well in practice are helpful.
*   **Versatility:**  Demonstrated application to both influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation showcases the algorithm's potential in diverse areas.
*   **Interactive Setting:** The work recognizes the interactive nature of real-world data summarization and shows how the robust summary can be used for multiple removal scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results, while demonstrating competitive performance, are not particularly compelling. The improvements over SIEVE-STREAMING are often marginal, and STAR-T-GREEDY does not consistently outperform it.  More comprehensive experiments with a wider range of datasets and parameters would strengthen the empirical validation.
*   **Objective Function in Experiments:** The choice of objective function *f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|* in the influence maximization experiment is somewhat unconventional.  It differs from the standard spread model [3] used in influence maximization, raising questions about the generalizability of the results to realistic influence propagation scenarios.
*   **Clarity of Parameters:** While the discussion of w=1 is helpful, the choice of τ and its approximation (Theorem 4.5) could benefit from more detailed explanation and guidance. It's not immediately clear how a user would effectively set these parameters in practice.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** An ablation study, examining the impact of the partitioning structure and thresholding rule independently, would provide a more granular understanding of the algorithm’s components.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a novel and important problem with a sound theoretical foundation.
*   The proposed algorithm is a significant contribution to the field of submodular optimization.
*   The experiments, although not conclusive, demonstrate promising results.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental results are not strongly convincing and lack a thorough comparison against strong baselines.
*   The choice of objective function in the influence maximization experiment may limit the generalizability of the findings.
*   The practical guidance on parameter selection could be improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The novelty of the problem addressed, the theoretical guarantees, and the clear presentation of the algorithm make it a worthwhile submission. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the paper represents a valuable addition to the literature and has the potential to inspire further research in robust streaming algorithms. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, primarily focused on expanding the experimental evaluation and providing clearer guidance on parameter tuning. I suggest that the authors consider adding experiments using the standard influence maximization objective function and performing a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to parameter settings.
