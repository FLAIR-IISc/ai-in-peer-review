This paper addresses the challenging problem of robust streaming submodular maximization – selecting a representative subset of data arriving in a stream while being resilient to the removal of a fixed number of elements. This is a novel and valuable combination, as prior work typically focused on either streaming or robustness in isolation. The authors propose STAR-T, an algorithm with a theoretical guarantee of a 0.149-approximation after *m* removals, retaining only O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements.

The problem statement is clearly articulated, and the motivation is well-grounded in applications like influence maximization and movie recommendation. The connection to prior work is reasonably well-established, citing relevant papers on streaming submodular maximization (e.g., [8]) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., [9, 10, 11]). The authors appropriately position their work as extending existing methods to handle both streaming and robustness simultaneously.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** Addressing streaming *and* robustness in submodular maximization is a significant contribution. Achieving a constant-factor approximation in this combined setting is a strong result, indicating a potentially valuable tool for large-scale data summarization in dynamic environments.
* **Practical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that STAR-T performs competitively with, and sometimes outperforms, existing algorithms, even those with the advantage of knowing the removals beforehand. The experiments on both dominating set and movie recommendation are encouraging.
* **Well-Motivated Problem:** The problem is relevant to many real-world applications, and the authors clearly articulate the motivations for tackling this challenge.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Lower Bounds:** The paper lacks any lower bounds to complement the upper-bound approximation guarantee. Establishing lower bounds would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution and provide a more complete understanding of the problem's inherent difficulty. Without lower bounds, it’s hard to assess how close the achieved approximation is to the optimal possible performance.
* **Clarity and Analysis:** The analysis and writing are often unclear. The claim of an “innovative analysis” feels unsubstantiated, as the provided proof sketch is difficult to follow. Key analytical steps aren't sufficiently explained, hindering the reader's ability to fully grasp the reasoning behind the approximation guarantee. More detailed and accessible explanations are crucial.
* **Influence Maximization Experiments:** The experiments in influence maximization utilize a simplified neighborhood-size objective. While this simplifies the evaluation, it raises concerns about the applicability of the results to more traditional, network-dependent influence objectives (e.g., expected cascade size).  The relevance to real-world influence maximization scenarios is therefore somewhat limited. The choice to use a simplified objective should be clearly justified, and the potential limitations acknowledged.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The dependence on the 'w' parameter in the theoretical bounds, and its impact on performance in practice, isn't thoroughly explored. While the paper notes w=1 works well empirically, a more systematic investigation of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. The combination of streaming and robustness is a significant step forward, and the experimental results are promising. However, the lack of lower bounds and the clarity issues in the analysis detract from the overall impact. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and significant problem formulation.
* Strong theoretical approximation guarantee.
* Compelling experimental results demonstrating practical performance.
* Well-motivated and relevant application scenarios.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Absence of lower bounds to contextualize the approximation guarantee.
* Unclear and difficult-to-follow analysis.
* Limited relevance of the influence maximization experiments due to the simplified objective function.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with major revisions. The authors should prioritize improving the clarity of the analysis and providing a more detailed explanation of the key analytical steps. Furthermore, an attempt to establish lower bounds, even if preliminary, would significantly strengthen the paper. Finally, addressing the limitations of the influence maximization experiments by either using a more realistic objective function or acknowledging the limitations more explicitly is crucial.  Addressing these points would elevate the paper to a stronger and more impactful contribution.
