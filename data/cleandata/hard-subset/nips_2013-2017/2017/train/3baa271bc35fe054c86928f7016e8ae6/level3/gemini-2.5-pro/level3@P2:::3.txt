This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint k, where elements arrive in a stream and the solution must be robust to the removal of up to m elements. This is a novel and challenging problem setting that combines two critical aspects of large-scale machine learning: single-pass data processing and solution robustness. The authors propose a two-stage approach. In the first stage, a streaming algorithm called STAR-T makes a single pass over the data to build a robust summary S. This algorithm is based on a novel partitioned structure of buckets with exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds, extending ideas from the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8] and robust non-streaming methods [11]. In the second stage, after an arbitrary set E of at most m elements is removed from S, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining elements S \ E to produce the final solution. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee of 0.149 while storing O((k + m log k) log k) elements. The authors validate their method on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper is the first to formally address and provide theoretical guarantees for the problem of submodular maximization that is simultaneously streaming and robust. This is a well-motivated and important problem, as many real-world applications require summarizing massive data streams in a way that is resilient to failures, adversarial removals, or dynamic changes in constraints. The work significantly advances the state of the art by providing a practical algorithmic framework for this challenging setting.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The primary contribution is a constant-factor approximation guarantee for this combined problem, which is a strong and non-trivial theoretical result. The STAR-T algorithm's design, which cleverly synthesizes the partitioned structure from robust optimization with the thresholding approach from streaming algorithms, is elegant and effective. The memory complexity, which scales logarithmically with k, is also a notable advantage over concurrent work [18].

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is convincing. The authors compare their method against strong baselines, including SIEVE-STREAMING. Critically, the baseline is given the significant advantage of knowing the set of removed elements E in advance, allowing it to optimize over V \ E. The fact that STAR-T-GREEDY, operating only on its pre-computed summary, performs comparably and sometimes even outperforms this baseline is a powerful demonstration of the proposed method's practical utility and the quality of the robust summary it creates. The interactive movie recommendation experiment further highlights its applicability in dynamic scenarios.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of the Analysis:** While the high-level ideas are clear, the presentation of the theoretical analysis could be substantially improved. The proof sketch in Section 4 is difficult to parse, making it hard to grasp the core insights that lead to the approximation guarantee. The paper claims an "innovative analysis," but the key innovative steps are not clearly explained in the main text. A more intuitive explanation of how Lemma 4.2, 4.3, and 4.4 are derived and balanced against each other would greatly improve the paper's clarity and impact.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Context (Lower Bounds):** The paper establishes an approximation constant of 0.149 but provides no discussion on lower bounds for the problem. Without this context, it is difficult to assess how close the achieved guarantee is to the best possible one. Including a discussion on the hardness of the problem, even if it involves adapting existing hardness results, would strengthen the theoretical contribution by situating the result within the landscape of what is achievable.

3.  **Limited Scope of Influence Maximization Experiments:** In the dominating set experiments, the objective function is the size of the neighborhood `f(Z) = |N(Z) U Z|`. While this function is monotone and submodular, its marginal gains are cheap to compute. This contrasts with standard influence maximization models like Independent Cascade (IC) or Linear Threshold (LT), where evaluating the marginal gain is #P-hard and typically requires expensive simulations. The feasibility of the proposed streaming approach is questionable for such functions, as the cost of evaluating an incoming element against every bucket could be prohibitive. The authors should discuss this limitation and the potential applicability of their algorithm to settings with expensive function oracles.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and important problem and provides a well-designed algorithm with a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The strengths—particularly the novelty of the problem formulation and the strong empirical results—outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the clarity of the presentation and the scope of the evaluation. The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on large-scale submodular optimization. I recommend acceptance.
