

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, where the algorithm must select k elements from a small retained set that remains robust to the removal of up to m items. The authors propose STAR-T, the first algorithm to jointly handle streaming and robustness requirements. Previous work has considered either streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) or robust submodular maximization (e.g., PRO), but not both simultaneously. The key innovation is a partitioned thresholding approach that arranges elements into exponentially increasing buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds, enabling robustness against any m removals while maintaining a summary of size O((m log k + k) log² k).

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with a well-defined algorithm and rigorous proof of a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149) under the stated assumptions. The authors carefully address the streaming and robustness requirements simultaneously, which is a significant theoretical advance. The experimental results on influence maximization and movie recommendation demonstrate practical effectiveness, showing that STAR-T matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows the removed elements in advance.

However, the paper has notable quality limitations. The claimed "innovative analysis" is not adequately explained - the proof sketch is difficult to follow, and key steps are omitted in the main text (referenced to supplementary material without sufficient clarity). The lack of non-trivial lower bounds is a significant gap, as it's unclear whether the 0.149 approximation factor is near-optimal or if a better guarantee is possible. The influence maximization experiments use a simplified neighborhood-size objective (f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|), which doesn't capture traditional network-dependent influence objectives (e.g., independent cascade or linear threshold models), raising questions about applicability to real-world influence maximization problems.

## Clarity
The paper is reasonably well-organized, with clear problem definition and algorithm presentation. The main algorithm (STAR-T) and its query stage (STAR-T-GREEDY) are well-described. However, the theoretical analysis suffers from poor clarity. The proof sketch of Theorem 4.1 is confusing and lacks sufficient explanation of the key insights. For instance, the argument about balancing the two bounds from Lemmas 4.3 and 4.4 is presented as a mere equation without intuitive explanation. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the partitioning structure and why the exponentially decreasing thresholds work for robustness.

## Originality
This paper makes a strong original contribution by being the first to jointly consider streaming and robust submodular maximization. While prior work has addressed either streaming (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) or robustness (e.g., PRO), the combination is novel. The partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds represents a significant departure from prior robust submodular work, which typically uses greedy bucket filling. The paper's key innovation is the clever use of partitioning with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and exponentially decreasing thresholds to achieve robustness with minimal memory overhead.

## Significance
The problem addressed is highly relevant to large-scale machine learning applications where data is too large to store and where robustness to element removal is critical (e.g., social networks, recommendation systems). The ability to precompute a robust summary that works for any set of m removals is particularly valuable in practice. The experimental results demonstrate strong practical performance, especially since STAR-T outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows the removed elements in advance. This is a compelling practical contribution that advances the state of the art in streaming submodular optimization.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to a challenging problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization. The theoretical contribution is significant, and the experimental results are promising. However, the lack of clear explanation for the innovative analysis and the absence of lower bounds weaken the theoretical contribution. The experimental validation is somewhat limited by the simplified influence maximization objective, which doesn't fully address the traditional network-dependent influence problems.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept**

The paper's core contribution is strong and original, addressing a meaningful gap in the literature. The theoretical guarantee is constant-factor and the practical results are compelling. The weaknesses (clarity issues and lack of lower bounds) are serious but not fatal, and the paper could be improved with better explanation of the analysis and more comprehensive experiments. The significance of the work for both theory and practice justifies acceptance, though the quality concerns prevent a stronger recommendation.
