- Introduces robust streaming submodular maximization: select streaming elements whose subset remains valuable after up to m removals.
- Proposes STAR-T, which maintains a compact summary and guarantees a constant-factor approximation even after m removals.
- Novel partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds distinguishes the method from prior robust submodular techniques.
- Empirical results on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks show competitive performance versus existing streaming algorithms (including ones with removal foreknowledge).
- Strength: tackles a non-trivial combined streaming and robustness problem with a provable constant-factor approximation.
- Strength: empirical validation on relevant applications strengthens the practical contribution.
- Strength: robustness focus addresses practical reliability of summaries in dynamic environments.
- Weakness: no lower bounds provided, so the tightness of the approximation guarantee is unclear.
- Weakness: analysis and writing lack clarity; the “innovative analysis” and proof sketch are hard to follow.
- Weakness: connection between the partitioning/decreasing thresholds and the approximation guarantee is not explicitly laid out.
- Weakness: experiments use a simplified dominating-set objective (neighborhood size), raising doubts about performance on realistic influence propagation models.
- Weakness: parameter sensitivity (especially choice of w) is not systematically analyzed; experiments hint w=1 works but lack broader study.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel/challenging problem, theoretical guarantee, competitive empirical performance, clear practical motivation.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing lower bounds, unclear technical exposition, limited experimental breadth, insufficient parameter analysis.
- Overall: valuable contribution meriting acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity, expand technical explanation, and discuss experimental limitations.
