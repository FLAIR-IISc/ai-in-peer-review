Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies robust streaming submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint. The goal is to make one pass over a stream of elements and construct a small summary T such that, after the removal of any m elements from T, one can still select a set S of size at most k from the remaining elements with value f(S) within a constant factor of OPT(k, V \ E). The authors propose STAR-T, which organizes memory into exponentially sized partitions of buckets and uses an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule to decide where to place arriving elements. In the query stage, STAR-T-GREEDY runs standard greedy on T \ E to produce the final solution.

The main theoretical claim is a constant-factor approximation: with a known near-optimal value, STAR-T stores O((k + m log k) log k) elements and guarantees f(S) ≥ c · f(OPT(k, V \ E)) with c ≈ 0.149 (approaching 0.149 as k grows). When OPT is unknown, running multiple instances at geometrically spaced thresholds adds an extra O(log k) factor, yielding memory O((k + m log k) log^2 k) and the same approximation up to a (1 + ε) factor. Empirically, STAR-T matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING [8] on social-network dominating set and MovieLens summarization, even when SIEVE-STREAMING is told the deletions in advance.

The work is well-situated relative to: (i) classical greedy for submodular maximization [7], (ii) streaming maximization (SIEVE-STREAMING) [8], (iii) robust but non-streaming formulations [9, 10, 11], and (iv) concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [18], which achieves a 1/2 − ε approximation using O(mk log k/ε) memory. Compared to [18], the present approach uses only O((m log k + k) polylog k) memory, which depends only logarithmically on k, at the price of a smaller constant factor.

Quality
- The theory appears technically sound. The analysis carefully splits into cases (many full buckets vs. mostly non-full) and leverages properties of greedy and submodularity. The constant 0.149 is derived transparently from these lemmas.
- The streaming construction and the robustness proof are nontrivial and original; the partitioned thresholds are a thoughtful extension of SIEVE-STREAMING.
- Experiments are clean and support the robustness claim, showing that a single summary can serve multiple post-hoc deletions.
- Minor concerns: There is an apparent discrepancy across sections between O((k + m log k) log k) and O((k + m log k) log^2 k). As I understand it, the former holds when a good τ is known, while the latter arises from running multiple instances to guess τ; please unify and state both cases clearly in the abstract and conclusion. Also, while [18] is concurrent and memory-hungrier, an empirical comparison (even approximate) would strengthen the experimental section.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The high-level intuition for the partitioned-threshold design is helpful.
- The formal development is dense; consider moving more intuition (e.g., why exponentially decreasing thresholds balance robustness vs. coverage) into the main text and consolidating the case analyses.
- Spell out the practical procedure for estimating τ (Section 4.1) in the main text, including the cost of running multiple parallel instances and how instances are pruned.
- Clarify how to choose w in practice and its effect on memory and accuracy; an ablation on w would help.

Originality
- The robust streaming setting, with a single-pass summary resilient to deletions and a query-time greedy, is novel. The partitioned thresholding idea is an elegant synthesis of non-uniform partitioning from robust non-streaming work [11] and streaming thresholding [8].
- The analysis technique (balancing two bounds across cases and tuning τ) seems new.

Significance
- The problem is timely: streaming summarization with “right to be forgotten” constraints is increasingly relevant. The memory bound that is only logarithmic in k (and linear in m up to log factors) is attractive.
- The approximation constant is modest (0.149), and the formulation is restricted to monotone submodular functions with a cardinality constraint, a fixed robustness parameter m, and one-pass insertion-only streams. This limits immediate applicability to broader ML settings (e.g., matroid/knapsack constraints, non-monotone objectives, dynamic streams).
- The empirical section focuses on two applications with relatively weak problem-specific motivation. More compelling end-to-end use cases or larger-scale evaluations would enhance impact.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Technically solid, nontrivial algorithm and analysis; clear advance over prior robust non-streaming work and competitive with concurrent streaming approaches in memory.
- Simple query-time procedure (greedy) with provable robustness after arbitrary deletions.
- One-pass streaming and small memory footprint; practical for large-scale settings.
- Empirical results indicate robustness and competitive performance even against informed baselines.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Scope is narrow: monotone, cardinality only; no extensions to common constraints or non-monotone objectives; robustness limited to a fixed m.
- Motivation and application stories are underdeveloped; experiments are limited in breadth and do not compare to concurrent deletion-robust streaming [18].
- Approximation constant is low; the practical benefit vs. simpler heuristics is not fully quantified.
- Relevance to NIPS is borderline: contribution is primarily algorithmic/theoretical with relatively modest ML application depth.

Suggestions for improvement
- Unify and foreground the two memory regimes (known-OPT vs. guessed τ), and report practical overhead.
- Add comparisons or discussion with [18] beyond asymptotics; include larger graphs/datasets and adversarial deletion patterns with m comparable to k and larger.
- Explore generalizations (e.g., matroid/knapsack constraints, sliding-window streams) or articulate why they are difficult.
- Provide an ablation on w and thresholds; report wall-clock time and oracle query counts.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is technically strong and proposes a clever and well-analyzed algorithm, but the application motivation is weak, the scope is narrow, and the fit to NIPS appears questionable. Strengthening the empirical section, clarifying the space bounds, and broadening the formulation would move this to a clear accept.
