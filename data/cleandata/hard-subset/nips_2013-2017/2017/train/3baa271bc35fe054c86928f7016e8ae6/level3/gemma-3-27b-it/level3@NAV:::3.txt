## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function in a streaming setting while simultaneously ensuring robustness to the removal of elements. This is a significant contribution, as it combines two important aspects – efficient processing of large datasets and resilience to data loss or adversarial attacks – that haven’t been jointly addressed before. The authors propose STAR-T, an algorithm based on partitioned thresholding, and provide a theoretical guarantee of a 0.149-approximation under the removal of *m* elements. 

The work builds upon existing research in both streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular maximization, acknowledging related work in these areas. The proposed partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule represent a novel approach, and the experiments on influence maximization and movie recommendation demonstrate competitive performance, even against algorithms that *know* which elements will be removed. This practical validation strengthens the contribution.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. A key limitation is the lack of lower bounds. While an upper bound on the approximation ratio is provided, establishing a lower bound would significantly strengthen the result and offer a more complete understanding of the problem’s inherent difficulty. 

More concerningly, the clarity of the analysis and writing is subpar. The authors claim an “innovative analysis,” but the description is vague and the proof sketch is difficult to follow.  A more detailed and well-explained presentation of the analysis is crucial for reproducibility and acceptance. The choice of a simplified neighborhood-size objective for influence maximization also raises questions about the applicability of the experimental results to more complex, network-dependent influence objectives commonly used in practice.  

**Arguments for acceptance:** Addresses a novel and important problem, achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee in a challenging combined setting, and demonstrates promising empirical performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**  Lacks non-trivial lower bounds, analysis and writing are unclear, and the influence maximization experiments use a simplified objective function.

**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, but the weaknesses in clarity and the absence of lower bounds prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection with request for revision**, contingent upon significant improvements in the clarity of the analysis and the inclusion of a discussion, even if preliminary, regarding potential lower bounds. The authors should also address the limitations of their experimental setup for influence maximization.
