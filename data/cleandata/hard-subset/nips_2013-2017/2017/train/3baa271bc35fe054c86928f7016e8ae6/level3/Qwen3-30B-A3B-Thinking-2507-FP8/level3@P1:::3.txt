

This paper addresses the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting, which combines two important practical constraints: (1) elements arrive in a streaming fashion, and (2) the solution must remain robust to the removal of up to m elements from the selected set. While robust submodular maximization (without streaming) and streaming submodular maximization (without robustness) have been studied separately, this paper is the first to consider both requirements simultaneously, making it a significant contribution to the field.

The authors propose STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that constructs a robust summary of size O((m log k + k) log²k) using a novel partitioned thresholding approach. The key innovation lies in the partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds, where elements are assigned to buckets based on their marginal gains relative to partition-specific thresholds. The query stage then applies a simple greedy algorithm on the remaining elements after any m removals, achieving a 0.149(1-1/log k) approximation guarantee. This is a substantial theoretical result, as previous robust algorithms (like PRO) required more memory or had weaker guarantees, while streaming algorithms (like SIEVE-STREAMING) did not address robustness.

The paper's strengths are clear: (1) the problem setting is highly relevant to practical applications where data is large and solutions must withstand element removals (e.g., social network influence maximization, personalized recommendations); (2) the theoretical guarantee is non-trivial and represents a significant advancement over prior work; (3) the experimental results demonstrate strong practical performance, matching or exceeding SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows the removed elements in advance.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. First, the theoretical analysis is poorly explained. The proof sketch is difficult to follow, and the paper fails to adequately justify its "innovative analysis" claim. Key steps in the analysis, particularly how the partitioning structure and thresholding rule combine to achieve the approximation guarantee, are not clearly articulated. This makes it challenging to verify the theoretical claims. Second, the paper lacks non-trivial lower bounds to complement its upper-bound guarantee, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. Third, the influence maximization experiments use a simplified neighborhood-size objective (f(Z)=|N(Z)∪Z|) rather than the traditional network-dependent influence spread model, which raises questions about the applicability of the results to standard influence maximization problems.

The paper's clarity could also be improved. The description of the algorithm and its analysis is dense and at times confusing. For example, the relationship between the threshold parameter τ and the unknown f(OPT(k, V\E)) is not sufficiently explained, and the proof sketch omits critical details. The supplementary material appears to contain more detailed proofs, but the main paper should stand on its own.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses a practically important problem that is increasingly relevant as data grows and systems must handle failures. The two-stage approach (precomputation followed by quick query) is particularly valuable for applications where re-optimization after removals is infeasible. The experimental results on movie recommendation and dominating set problems demonstrate practical utility, though the simplified objective in the influence maximization experiments somewhat limits their impact.

The paper makes a notable contribution to NIPS 2017 by introducing a new problem setting that combines streaming and robustness, which is not covered in previous NIPS proceedings. The authors correctly cite relevant prior work, including the recent PRO algorithm (Bogunovic et al., ICML 2017) and SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., KDD 2014).

In terms of quality, the theoretical results are sound (assuming the proofs hold), and the experiments are well-designed and convincing for the problem setting. The paper demonstrates good technical depth in the algorithm design and evaluation, though the analysis presentation needs improvement.

For the conference, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance. The problem is novel, the theoretical contribution is significant, and the practical results are compelling. The weaknesses noted (particularly the unclear analysis) are important but not fatal, as they could be addressed in revision. The lack of lower bounds is a minor issue given the novelty of the problem setting.

Pro: Novel problem setting combining streaming and robustness; strong theoretical guarantee; compelling experimental results matching or exceeding baselines even when baselines have prior knowledge of removals.

Con: Analysis is unclear and difficult to follow; lacks lower bounds to complement upper-bound guarantee; simplified influence maximization objective in experiments.

Given the novelty of the problem, the strength of the theoretical result, and the practical significance, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization, addressing a realistic and important challenge in large-scale data processing. While the analysis could be clarified, the core algorithmic insight is compelling and the results are convincing. The authors should revise the paper to provide a more detailed explanation of their analysis, which would significantly strengthen the contribution.
