Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting: given a monotone submodular function and a cardinality constraint k, the algorithm must make one pass over the stream, store a compact summary S, and later, after arbitrary removal of up to m elements, return a size-k subset Z ⊆ S\E whose value approximates OPT(k, V\E). The main contribution is STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that organizes its retained elements into partitions of exponentially growing bucket sizes with exponentially decreasing thresholds; in the query stage, STAR-T-GREEDY runs greedy on S\E. The authors prove a constant-factor approximation guarantee: with memory O((k + m log k) log k) when f(OPT) is known (and O((k + m log k) log2 k) with the standard parallel-instance trick to estimate f(OPT) within 1+ε), STAR-T-GREEDY achieves f(Z) ≥ c f(OPT(k, V\E)) with c ≈ 0.149(1 − 1/log k). Experiments on dominating set and movie recommendation show performance comparable to or better than SIEVE-STREAMING [8], even when the latter is allowed to know E in advance.

This work connects to robust submodular maximization (non-streaming) in [9,10] and to the partitioned design in PRO [11], but adapts these ideas to the streaming setting with a thresholded placement rule instead of sequential greedy bucket-filling. It extends streaming submodular maximization [5,8,12,13] to robustness against deletions. It is contemporaneous with deletion-robust streaming [18], which attains a 1/2 − ε approximation but with O(mk log k/ε) memory; by contrast, STAR-T trades a smaller constant factor for much milder dependence on k (logarithmic), which is attractive for large k.

Strengths
- Novel algorithmic design: the partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is elegant and well-justified, combining ideas from [8] and [11].
- Provable robustness: constant-factor guarantee for arbitrary E with one pass and sublinear space, with clear memory–approximation trade-offs and an analysis that separates cases (many full buckets vs. many non-full buckets).
- Practical relevance: the two-stage workflow aligns with applications where the full stream is not retained and deletions occur post hoc (e.g., right-to-be-forgotten, personalization).
- Empirical validation: on two tasks, STAR-T-GREEDY matches or exceeds SIEVE-STREAMING (even when handicapped), and the summary is reusable for multiple E.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Motivation and scope: The paper focuses on monotone cardinality constraints. In many summarization problems, matroid or knapsack constraints arise; a discussion (or preliminary results) on extending the structure/analysis to these broader constraints would increase generality.
- Constant factor: The theoretical constant (≈0.149) is conservative compared to 1/2 − ε in [18]. The paper rightly emphasizes the better memory scaling, but it would help to (i) plot the empirical memory–quality trade-off vs. [18] (if code is available), and/or (ii) show that STAR-T achieves higher empirical constants.
- Clarity on τ and w: The text first describes threshold τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i,k}. Please reconcile and state the precise rule, and provide intuitive guidance for setting w in practice beyond w=1 (e.g., sensitivity plots showing robustness vs. m for w ∈ {1,2,4}).
- Space bound discrepancy: The abstract/conclusion state O((k + m log k) log2 k), while Theorem 4.1 shows O((k + m log k) log k) given τ; make explicit that the extra log k comes from maintaining multiple instances to estimate f(OPT) (Theorem 4.5).
- Experiments: 
  - Include sensitivity to m and to the adversarial vs. random removal model; show performance as a function of |E| up to and beyond the assumed bound m, and include worst-case removals selected adaptively against STAR-T.
  - Add ablations (e.g., removing the partitioning or using uniform thresholds) to demonstrate the benefit of the proposed structure.
  - Where feasible, compare to the concurrent [18] on at least one dataset or, if not, provide a careful resource-adjusted comparison using their reported bounds.
  - Report wall-clock time and oracle call counts for the streaming and query stages.
- Relevance: While the applications are compelling, the introductory motivation could better emphasize ML use cases at NIPS (e.g., continual learning, privacy-related deletions, interactive recommendation) and why streaming robustness is essential there.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The technical development is sound and nontrivial. The case analysis is clear and leverages submodularity and greedy properties appropriately. Assumptions (monotonicity, normalization, oracle access) are standard. Some proofs are deferred; ensuring key lemmas are sufficiently sketched in the main text is appreciated.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized. Please fix minor inconsistencies (threshold definition; space bound) and typos (“peformance”; “st Conference…”). A small figure illustrating the bucket/partition structure with thresholds would aid intuition (Figure 1 already helps if present in the final).
- Originality: The combination of partitioned summaries with thresholded streaming assignment and the robust analysis appears novel. The work is differentiated from [11] (non-streaming) and [8] (non-robust), and draws a clear contrast to [18] in the memory regime.
- Significance: Advances the state of the art in robust streaming submodular maximization with a new space–approximation trade-off. Likely to influence subsequent work on deletion-robust ML systems and streaming summarization.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide tighter constants (empirical or theoretical) for practical regimes (e.g., random order streams or random deletions)?
2. How sensitive is performance to τ mis-specification when using the parallel-instance scheme? What ε do you use in experiments?
3. Can your analysis extend to knapsack or matroid constraints, or to non-monotone objectives with post-processing?
4. In movie recommendation, how is α chosen per user, and does STAR-T remain competitive across a range of α?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, novel algorithm with provable guarantees in an important streaming-robust setting.
- Strong space efficiency vs. prior robust streaming work.
- Clear, reusable two-stage design; promising empirical results on meaningful tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Conservative approximation constant; limited empirical exploration of parameter sensitivity and competing robust baselines.
- Narrow constraint class; motivation/exposition could better align with NIPS audiences.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a technically sound and practically relevant advance with good space–quality trade-offs. Addressing clarity issues and strengthening experiments (especially sensitivity and comparisons) would further solidify the contribution.
