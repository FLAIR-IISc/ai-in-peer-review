This paper addresses the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, tackling the scenario where a representative subset of data is selected in a streaming fashion and must remain useful even after a certain number of elements are removed. The authors present STAR-T, an algorithm designed to construct a robust summary of the data, and demonstrate a constant-factor approximation guarantee when combined with a simple greedy algorithm on the remaining elements after removal.

The motivation for this work stems from the need for data summarization in large-scale machine learning, particularly in scenarios requiring robustness to element loss. The authors cite applications like influence maximization and movie recommendation, suggesting scenarios where elements might be removed due to network failures or user preferences. However, the application examples feel somewhat contrived and lack a strong compelling narrative. The connection to real-world problems beyond these examples isn't fully explored, weakening the perceived impact.

The core contribution of this paper lies in the design of STAR-T and its associated analysis. The algorithm utilizes a partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds, allowing it to efficiently maintain a robust summary. The theoretical guarantee—a constant-factor approximation—is a significant result, particularly given the constraints of the streaming and robustness requirements. The authors provide a detailed proof sketch, and the rigorous analysis is a strength of the paper.

However, a critical weakness is the paper’s relevance to NIPS. While the techniques employed (submodular maximization, streaming algorithms) are well-represented at NIPS, the specific problem formulation feels somewhat narrow. The assumption of knowing the robustness parameter *m* upfront, and the focus on a constant-factor approximation, limit the generalizability of the approach. The related work section acknowledges existing research in robust submodular maximization, but doesn’t adequately position this work within the broader landscape. Existing work, such as [10, 11], achieves similar or better approximation ratios with different tradeoffs, and the specific benefits of the proposed approach aren’t clearly articulated. 

The experimental evaluation provides some validation of the algorithm's performance, demonstrating competitive results against SIEVE-STREAMING in dominating set and movie recommendation tasks. The ability to reuse the robust summary for different removal sets is a practical advantage. However, the experiments primarily focus on synthetic scenarios or small datasets, and a more comprehensive evaluation on larger, real-world datasets would strengthen the conclusions. The experimental setup also doesn't fully justify the claim of outperforming or matching existing methods, as SIEVE-STREAMING is given an unfair advantage by knowing the removed elements.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The algorithm and its analysis appear to be rigorously developed and mathematically sound.
*   **Novelty:** The partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding rule represent a novel approach to robust streaming submodular maximization.
*   **Theoretical Guarantee:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a valuable theoretical result.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Applicability:** The problem formulation is relatively specific, potentially limiting the broad impact of the work.
*   **Relevance to NIPS:** The paper’s contributions may not be sufficiently novel or impactful for a highly competitive conference like NIPS.
*   **Weak Motivation:** The application examples lack a strong compelling narrative and don’t fully justify the problem formulation.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are limited in scope and don’t provide a conclusive demonstration of superiority over existing methods.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel algorithm for a challenging problem.
*   The theoretical results are significant and provide a strong foundation for future research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The problem formulation is relatively narrow, and the relevance to NIPS is questionable.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited and doesn’t fully support the claims of superiority.
*   The motivation and application examples are weak.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid piece of technical work, but its relevance to NIPS is a significant concern. While the theoretical contributions are valuable, the limited applicability and lack of a compelling narrative weaken its overall impact. I would lean towards **reject** with the recommendation that the authors consider submitting to a more specialized conference focused on streaming algorithms or submodular optimization. To improve the paper for resubmission, the authors should broaden the applicability of the problem formulation, strengthen the motivation with more compelling examples, and perform a more comprehensive experimental evaluation on larger, real-world datasets.
