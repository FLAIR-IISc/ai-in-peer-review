Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint k in a streaming setting, where up to m items may be removed from memory after the stream. It proposes a two-stage method. Stage 1 is STAR-T, a one-pass streaming algorithm that maintains a partitioned summary: each partition contains exponentially many buckets with exponentially increasing capacities and exponentially decreasing acceptance thresholds. Incoming elements are placed into the first non-full bucket whose marginal gain exceeds that partition’s threshold. Stage 2 applies a standard greedy algorithm restricted to the remaining summary after an arbitrary removal of up to m elements. The main theoretical result (Theorem 4.1) shows that, for an appropriate memory parameter w and threshold τ, STAR-T builds a summary of size O((k + m log k) log k) and, after any deletion set E of size ≤ m, STAR-T-GREEDY returns a k-set with constant-factor approximation; the constant tends to 0.149 as k → ∞. Lemmas 4.2–4.4 underpin a two-case analysis: either a partition has many full buckets (giving a direct value guarantee despite deletions), or the last partition contains a not-full bucket, and combining upper/lower bounds around it yields a constant approximation. Theorem 4.5 removes the need to know f(OPT) by running parallel instances across a geometric grid of τ values, incurring an extra log factor and a (1+ε) loss.

Compared to non-streaming robust work (e.g., Krause et al. 2008; Orlin et al. 2016; PRO, Bogunovic et al. 2017), this is the first streaming algorithm designed specifically for robustness, extending greedy thresholding ideas from Sieve-Streaming (Badanidiyuru et al. 2014) to a partitioned, decreasing-threshold structure. The paper acknowledges concurrent work on deletion-robust streaming (Mirzasoleiman et al. 2017) achieving a 1/2−ε guarantee but with memory scaling as O(mk log k/ε); STAR-T’s memory is logarithmic in k (and linear in m up to log factors), which is attractive in large-scale settings.

Quality
The theory is careful and technically sound. The constant-factor guarantee of Theorem 4.1 holds uniformly in k (approaching 0.149 as k grows), with a clean memory–robustness tradeoff controlled by w ≥ 4 m log k / k. Lemmas 4.2–4.3 convincingly establish tolerance to arbitrary removals by combining the partitioning structure with greedy’s standard approximation properties. The τ-unknown issue is handled via a standard parallel-instances construction (Theorem 4.5), bringing the stated space up to O((k + m log k) log^2 k) for practical use. While the constant is small, this is common in robust/sublinear settings; empirical results partly mitigate concerns.

The experimental section demonstrates competitive performance on two tasks (influence-like dominating set and personalized movie recommendation). However, the evaluation is not very expressive: (i) STAR-T-GREEDY does not consistently outperform Sieve-Streaming (sometimes it only matches it), and (ii) the influence objective f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z| differs substantially from the classical Kempe et al. diffusion spread; hence, performance under the true diffusion models (IC/LT) remains unclear. Also, more detail about τ selection in practice, sensitivity to w (beyond noting w=1 works well), wall-clock/memory, and stream/adversarial orders would improve reproducibility and practical guidance.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The STAR-T data structure and decreasing-threshold rule are clearly described with helpful pseudocode. The two-case proof strategy is explained at a high level, with details deferred appropriately. The relationship between the O((k + m log k) log k) bound (base algorithm) and the O((k + m log k) log^2 k) bound (with τ-approximation) could be emphasized earlier to avoid confusion. Experimental setup should be clarified: (a) whether deletions are drawn from S or V, (b) how τ was instantiated, and (c) how many function evaluations/time per method.

Originality
The partitioned thresholding design for robust streaming is novel and well distinguished from both PRO (non-streaming, non-thresholded construction) and Sieve-Streaming (non-robust). Acknowledgment of concurrent work is appropriate, and the different memory scaling is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
Robust streaming summarization is an important and timely problem with clear applications. The proposed approach delivers a one-pass algorithm with strong robustness guarantees and modest memory, plus a practical second-stage greedy that can answer multiple queries (different removal sets) from a single summary. If supported by broader empirical evidence (especially under standard diffusion models), this could be a method practitioners adopt.

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate influence maximization under IC/LT using standard estimators (e.g., reverse influence sampling), or justify the dominating-set surrogate with correlations to true spread.
- Include ablations on w and ε, guidance for choosing τ in practice, and sensitivity to stream order and adversarial deletions.
- Report memory footprint, function evaluation counts, and wall-clock time.
- Compare against concurrent deletion-robust streaming methods and offline robust baselines when feasible (e.g., PRO on smaller instances).
- Clarify fairness of the deletion protocol (when E is sampled from S) and consider also deletions sampled from V.

Pros
- First streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization; principled partitioned thresholding design.
- Strong theory: one pass, logarithmic-in-k memory for the base algorithm, constant-factor guarantee as k → ∞.
- Practical convenience: τ can be approximated (Thm 4.5); w=1 often suffices empirically.
- Simple, reusable query stage (standard greedy), enabling multiple queries from a single summary.
- Empirical results competitive with strong streaming baselines.

Cons
- Experiments are limited and sometimes use a nonstandard influence objective; unclear performance under IC/LT.
- STAR-T-GREEDY does not consistently outperform Sieve-Streaming; advantage over baselines is not decisive.
- Practical guidance on τ, ε, and w selection and runtime/memory costs is sparse.
- Small theoretical constant; empirical validation is therefore crucial and should be broader.
