- Problem: Robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting where up to m elements may be removed.  
- Proposed method: STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm that builds a one-pass robust summary (using O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements) and yields a constant-factor approximation after removals.  
- Novel technical idea: Partitioned thresholding with partitions of exponentially decreasing thresholds, differing from prior greedy bucket-filling approaches.  
- Theoretical guarantee: Constant-factor approximation ~0.149 (approaches this as k grows), independent of m under reasonable scaling constraints.  
- Experiments: STAR-T matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING (even when that baseline knows removals) on network dominating-set summarization and movie recommendation tasks.  
- Strength: Novel joint formulation addressing a real-world gap (streaming + robustness).  
- Strength: Achieves a nontrivial constant-factor approximation in the combined setting.  
- Strength: Empirical results show a single robust summary can handle multiple removal scenarios.  
- Strength: Clear connection between theoretical results and experiments.  
- Weakness: No non-trivial lower bounds provided, so tightness of approximation is unclear.  
- Weakness: Analysis and writing are unclear in places; proof sketch and "innovative analysis" lack sufficient explanation and motivation.  
- Weakness: Partitioning structure and threshold rules are not adequately motivated in the text.  
- Experimental concern: Influence-maximization tests use a simplified neighborhood-size objective rather than standard diffusion models, limiting applicability claims.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on SIEVE-STREAMING and robust submodular literature and clearly states differences.  
- NIPS criteria — Quality: Solid algorithm and proofs but clarity of analysis needs improvement; experiments support claims.  
- NIPS criteria — Clarity: Generally organized but theoretical exposition is hard to follow.  
- NIPS criteria — Originality: Novel combination of streaming and robustness.  
- NIPS criteria — Significance: Important for large-scale applications; practical and theoretically backed solution.  
- Pro acceptance: Novel problem, meaningful theoretical advance (0.149 guarantee), strong empirical performance.  
- Con acceptance: Missing lower bounds, unclear/insufficiently motivated analysis, and nonstandard experimental objective for influence tasks.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong paper recommended for acceptance provided authors clarify analysis and better justify experimental choices.
