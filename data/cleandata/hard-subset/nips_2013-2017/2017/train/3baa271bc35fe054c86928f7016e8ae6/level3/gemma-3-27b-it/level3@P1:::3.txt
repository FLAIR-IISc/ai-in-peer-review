The paper introduces the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, a challenging combination of two well-studied areas. The authors tackle the scenario where elements arrive sequentially (streaming) and the selected subset must remain valuable even after removing a certain number of elements (robustness). They propose STAR-T, an algorithm that maintains a compact summary of the data and guarantees a constant-factor approximation even after *m* elements are removed from the selected set. This is a significant contribution as prior work typically focused on either streaming or robustness in isolation, and achieving both simultaneously is non-trivial. The algorithm leverages a novel partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds, differentiating it from existing robust submodular maximization techniques like those in [9, 10, 11].  Experimental results on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks demonstrate competitive performance against existing streaming algorithms, even those with foreknowledge of element removals, as shown in [8].

The strengths of this work lie in the non-triviality of the problem addressed and the successful development of an algorithm with a theoretical guarantee. Achieving a constant-factor approximation in this combined streaming and robustness setting is a substantial technical accomplishment. The empirical validation on relevant applications further strengthens the contribution. The robustness aspect is particularly appealing, as it addresses practical concerns about the reliability of selected summaries in dynamic environments.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, the lack of lower bounds is a significant omission. While the authors provide an upper bound on the approximation ratio, establishing corresponding lower bounds would provide a more complete understanding of the problem's inherent complexity.  It's unclear how tightly the algorithm's performance matches the optimal possible solution. Second, the clarity of the analysis and writing could be improved. The paper frequently refers to an "innovative analysis," but this analysis isn't clearly articulated, and the provided proof sketch is difficult to follow.  More detailed explanations and intermediate steps would significantly enhance understanding. Specifically, the connection between the partitioning structure, the decreasing thresholds, and the approximation guarantee needs to be more explicitly laid out.  

Third, the experimental evaluation, while positive, has limitations. In the dominating set experiments, the use of a simplified objective function (neighborhood size) raises concerns about the applicability of the results to more complex, realistic influence maximization scenarios where influence propagation dynamics are considered.  Existing work in influence maximization often employs more sophisticated models that account for network structure and individual node characteristics [3, 14], and it's unclear whether the proposed algorithm would perform as well in those settings. Finally, the parameter sensitivity of the algorithm (specifically the choice of *w*) is not thoroughly investigated. The experiments suggest *w*=1 performs well, but a more systematic analysis of how performance varies with *w* would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and challenging problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms and robust optimization.
*   Provides a theoretical constant-factor approximation guarantee.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on relevant data summarization tasks.
*   The problem is clearly motivated by practical applications such as influence maximization and recommendation systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of lower bounds to assess the tightness of the approximation guarantee.
*   The analysis and writing are unclear, making it difficult to fully grasp the technical details.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks breadth, particularly regarding more realistic influence maximization objectives.
*   Insufficient discussion of parameter sensitivity and its impact on performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The problem is important, the proposed algorithm is technically sound, and the experimental results are promising.  However, the lack of clarity in the analysis and the absence of lower bounds are significant concerns.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the analysis, providing a more detailed explanation of the key technical ideas, and including a discussion of the limitations of the experimental evaluation.  Addressing these points would significantly enhance the paper's impact and make it more accessible to the broader research community.
