### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a setting that is simultaneously streaming and robust. This is the first work, to my knowledge, to jointly consider these two challenging constraints. The problem is formulated as a two-stage process: first, a single-pass streaming algorithm processes a data stream to create a small, robust summary `S`. Second, after an adversary removes an arbitrary set of `m` items from `S`, a query algorithm must select a `k`-element solution from the remaining items in the summary.

The authors propose the STAR-T algorithm, which uses a novel partitioning and thresholding scheme to build the summary. The summary `S` is organized into partitions with exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds, and within each partition, there are multiple buckets of exponentially increasing size. An incoming stream element is placed into the first available bucket where its marginal gain exceeds the bucket's partition-level threshold. The paper proves that this method constructs a summary of size O((m log k + k) log^2 k). For the query stage, the authors propose running a standard greedy algorithm on the remaining summary, which achieves a constant-factor approximation of 0.149.

This work builds upon two distinct lines of research: streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING by Badanidiyuru et al., 2014) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., Orlin et al., 2016; Bogunovic et al., 2017). The key contribution is in elegantly merging the thresholding idea from streaming algorithms with a partitioning structure inspired by recent robust (non-streaming) algorithms to provide the first constant-factor guarantee for the combined problem. The efficacy of the approach is demonstrated on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation tasks.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a novel and highly relevant problem. The combination of streaming data and the need for robustness against removals or failures is a practical scenario in many large-scale applications (e.g., sensor placement, online advertising, recommendation systems). Formulating and providing the first constant-factor approximation for this problem is a significant contribution that advances the state of the art.
*   **Technical Quality:** The main theoretical result—achieving a constant-factor approximation guarantee in this challenging setting—is a strong and impressive technical achievement. The analysis appears sound, and the memory complexity of O((m log k + k) log^2 k) is reasonable, particularly its logarithmic (rather than linear) dependence on `k`, which compares favorably to concurrent work.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the algorithm's practical utility. STAR-T performs competitively against strong baselines, including SIEVE-STREAMING. The fact that it performs well even when the baseline is given the unrealistic advantage of knowing the removed set in advance highlights the effectiveness of the robust summary structure created by STAR-T. The movie recommendation experiment effectively illustrates the practical benefit of a pre-computed summary that can handle diverse, interactive queries/removals.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The primary weakness of the paper is its lack of clarity, particularly in the presentation of the theoretical analysis. The proof sketch in Section 4 is very difficult to follow and offers little intuition. For instance, the roles of the three key lemmas and the logic behind balancing their respective bounds are not explained clearly. The paper claims an "innovative analysis" but does not articulate what specific new techniques or insights constitute this innovation relative to prior work on submodular analysis. This makes the technical contribution hard to appreciate and verify without a deep dive into the appendix, hindering the paper's accessibility and impact.
*   **Completeness of Theoretical Results:** The paper provides an upper bound on the approximation ratio but lacks any discussion of non-trivial lower bounds for the problem. Without such bounds, it is difficult to contextualize the 0.149-approximation guarantee. Is this constant close to the best possible for this problem, or is there substantial room for improvement? A discussion on the hardness of the problem would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.
*   **Experimental Design for Influence Maximization:** The influence maximization experiments use a simplified "dominating set" objective function (`f(Z) = |N(Z) U Z|`). While this function is submodular, it is computationally much simpler than standard influence objectives (e.g., based on IC or LT models), which typically require costly Monte Carlo simulations to evaluate. The streaming setting poses a major challenge for these simulation-based objectives, as marginal gains cannot be computed efficiently on the fly. By using a neighborhood-based proxy, the paper sidesteps this key difficulty. This raises questions about whether the algorithm's excellent performance would transfer to more traditional and complex influence maximization problems in a true streaming scenario.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel, important, and practical problem at the intersection of streaming and robust optimization.
*   Provides the first constant-factor approximation guarantee for this problem, which is a strong theoretical contribution.
*   The proposed algorithm, STAR-T, is elegant and demonstrates strong empirical performance, even against baselines with oracle access to future removals.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The technical presentation is unclear, particularly the proof sketch, which obscures the core analytical insights.
*   The theoretical results are incomplete without a discussion of hardness or lower bounds.
*   The choice of objective function in one of the key experiments may oversimplify the problem and limit the generalizability of the findings for that application.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the significant issues with clarity and the limitations in one of the experiments, the novelty of the problem and the strength of the main theoretical result are substantial. This paper opens up a new and important direction of research. I lean towards acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to revise the paper to significantly improve the clarity of the technical sections. A more intuitive explanation of the proof strategy in the main body is essential for the paper to have its deserved impact.
