### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of monotone submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint `k` in a setting that is both streaming and robust. The goal is to find a solution that remains effective even after an adversary removes up to `m` elements. The authors propose a two-stage approach. In the first stage, a novel single-pass streaming algorithm called STAR-T processes the data stream to create a compact and robust summary `S`. In the second, query stage, after any `m` elements are removed from `S`, a standard greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining summary to produce the final solution.

The STAR-T algorithm is the core technical contribution. It organizes the summary `S` into a series of partitions, where each partition contains an increasing number of buckets. Elements from the stream are added to buckets based on an exponentially decreasing threshold rule, which is a key mechanism for ensuring robustness.

This work builds upon the foundations of both streaming submodular maximization (e.g., Sieve-Streaming by Badanidiyuru et al., 2014) and robust submodular maximization (e.g., the partitioning structure used in the non-streaming algorithm by Bogunovic et al., 2017). The main theoretical result is Theorem 4.1, which establishes a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the two-stage procedure, with a memory complexity that scales favorably (logarithmically) with the cardinality constraint `k`. The paper appears to be, along with concurrent work (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2017), the first to provide a provably effective streaming algorithm for this robust optimization problem.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a novel and practically relevant problem at the intersection of streaming algorithms, submodular optimization, and robustness. The ability to generate a robust summary from a data stream, which can then be queried efficiently after failures or adversarial removals, is a significant contribution with potential applications in areas like resilient sensor placement, robust influence maximization, and interactive recommender systems. This work pioneers a solution for a challenging problem and is likely to inspire follow-up research.

*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and provides a strong constant-factor approximation guarantee. The design of the STAR-T algorithm, with its partitioned structure and decreasing thresholds, is clever and well-motivated for achieving robustness. The proof strategy, which considers different cases based on the state of the buckets, is rigorous. The memory complexity of `O((k + m log k) log k)` is a notable strength, especially its logarithmic dependence on `k`, which contrasts favorably with the linear dependence in concurrent work.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem formulation is precise, and the proposed algorithms (STAR-T and STAR-T-GREEDY) are described in a way that is easy to follow. The intuition behind the partitioned bucket structure is effectively communicated.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

The primary weakness of this paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which does not fully support the practical advantages of the proposed method.

*   **Underwhelming Empirical Performance:** In the experiments, the proposed STAR-T-GREEDY does not consistently outperform the Sieve-Streaming baseline, which is given the strong advantage of knowing the removed set `E` in advance. For instance, in Figures 2(b) and 2(d), Sieve-Streaming achieves a slightly higher objective value for some `k`. While being competitive is a good result, a compelling case for the proposed algorithm would require scenarios where it demonstrates a clearer advantage, justifying its more complex structure.

*   **Questionable Choice of Objective for Influence Maximization:** A major concern is the use of the dominating set objective (`f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|`) for the influence maximization experiment. While this function is monotone and submodular, it is a significant simplification of the standard influence spread model introduced by Kempe et al. (2003), which is based on stochastic diffusion processes (e.g., IC, LT models) and is the common benchmark for this problem. The performance of STAR-T on the true influence maximization task remains unclear, and this choice of proxy function weakens the experimental claims. The authors should, at a minimum, justify this choice and discuss the potential limitations.

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to two applications and two datasets. A more comprehensive evaluation across a wider range of submodular functions and larger-scale datasets would strengthen the paper's claims about the algorithm's general applicability and performance.

### Detailed Comments

*   The practical utility of the algorithm is enhanced by the authors addressing the unknown `f(OPT)` value via parallelization (Theorem 4.5) and the finding that `w=1` works well in practice, simplifying hyperparameter tuning.

*   The theoretical approximation constant of `c ≈ 0.149` is somewhat low. While achieving any constant factor for this difficult problem is a strong theoretical achievement, a brief discussion on whether this bound is tight or if there are clear paths to improving it would be beneficial.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. The problem is important, and the proposed approach is original and well-analyzed. The main weakness is the experimental section, which is not fully convincing due to the choice of a proxy objective function for influence maximization and the lack of a clear empirical advantage over a strong baseline. Despite these experimental shortcomings, the paper's significant theoretical contributions to a new and challenging problem make it a valuable addition to the field. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the experimental concerns in the final version by either providing justification for their choices or including additional, more compelling results.
