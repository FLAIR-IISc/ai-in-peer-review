## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper tackles the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization – finding a representative subset of elements arriving in a stream that remains valuable even after the removal of a certain number of elements. The authors propose STAR-T, an algorithm employing a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding, providing a constant-factor approximation guarantee with a summary size of O((m log k + k) log^2 k).

The work builds on a substantial body of research in submodular optimization, particularly extending streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING to address robustness. It relates to prior work on robust submodular maximization (e.g., Krause et al., 2008; Orlin et al., 2016) and interactive/adaptive submodularity (Golovin & Krause, 2011). The proposed algorithm is a logical step in adapting techniques for large-scale, dynamic, and potentially unreliable data environments.

**Strengths:** The technical quality of the paper is high. The algorithm and its analysis appear sound and represent a non-trivial contribution. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the relevant literature and position their work effectively within it. The inclusion of experimental results, demonstrating comparable or superior performance to existing methods on the dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, strengthens the claims. The analysis of the space complexity is also clear and concise.

**Weaknesses:** While technically strong, the motivation and application examples feel somewhat weak. The introduction mentions robustness being useful in scenarios like movie recommendations, but doesn’t fully articulate *why* this robustness is critical or how the problem formulation specifically addresses real-world challenges beyond the theoretical.  Furthermore, the conference relevance is questionable.  The problem formulation, while well-defined, feels restrictive. The assumption of knowing *k* and *m* upfront, and the focus on removal *after* summarization feels somewhat contrived.  A more general formulation considering continuous removals or incorporating robustness directly into the summarization objective might be more broadly applicable.

**Originality:** The partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding are novel and contribute to the originality of the work. However, it largely builds on existing submodular streaming techniques.

**Significance:** The results are important within the niche of robust submodular optimization. The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a valuable theoretical result. However, the limited exploration of practical applications and the relatively specific problem formulation limit the potential broader impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Strong theoretical foundation and analysis.
*   Novel algorithmic approach.
*   Demonstrated performance on real-world datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Weak motivation and limited application scope.
*   Questionable relevance to NIPS (may be more appropriate for a more specialized optimization conference).
*   Restrictive problem formulation.



**Overall:** This is a technically sound and original paper. However, its limited scope and relatively weak motivation raise concerns about its suitability for NIPS. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to broaden the problem formulation and more thoroughly justify its practical relevance.
