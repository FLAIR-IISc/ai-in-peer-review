This paper addresses the important and novel problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting. It is the first work, to my knowledge, to consider both of these challenging constraints simultaneously. The problem formulation is well-motivated: in a single pass over a data stream, the algorithm must create a small summary set that is robust to the adversarial removal of `m` elements. From this reduced summary, a `k`-element solution can then be selected. The proposed STAR-T algorithm extends ideas from streaming literature (e.g., Sieve-Streaming) and robust optimization by introducing a novel partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds. Theoretically, the authors prove that STAR-T maintains a summary of size `O((m log k + k) log^2 k)` and achieves a constant-factor approximation of 0.149.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its originality and theoretical significance. Tackling the intersection of streaming and robust submodular maximization is a valuable contribution, and proving a constant-factor approximation in this setting is a strong and non-trivial result. The experimental results are also promising, showing that STAR-T performs competitively against methods that have oracle access to the removed items, which effectively demonstrates the utility of the robust summary.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its impact. A major concern is the clarity of the writing and analysis. The authors claim an “innovative analysis,” but the intuition behind this innovation is not clearly communicated, and the proof sketch in the main text is very difficult to parse. This lack of clarity significantly hinders the reader’s ability to verify the paper's core theoretical claims and detracts from its overall quality.

Furthermore, the theoretical results could be better contextualized. The paper provides an upper-bound guarantee but lacks any discussion of non-trivial lower bounds for the problem, making it difficult to assess how close the 0.149 approximation factor is to optimal. Finally, while the experiments are generally positive, the influence maximization task uses a simplified neighborhood-size objective. This function is convenient for a streaming setting but avoids the complexities of standard network-dependent influence objectives, raising questions about the algorithm's applicability and performance on more traditional, computationally intensive influence maximization problems.

In summary, while the paper introduces a novel problem and presents a strong theoretical result, its significant clarity issues in the analysis and methodological questions in the experiments are major drawbacks.
