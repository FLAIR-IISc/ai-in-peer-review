

This paper introduces a novel two-stage algorithm (STAR-T) for robust submodular maximization under cardinality constraints in streaming settings. The authors address the problem of finding a small representative subset that remains effective after removal of up to m elements, which is particularly important for large-scale applications where reoptimization after removals is infeasible. The work extends the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm to the robust setting by introducing a partitioned thresholding structure where buckets in different partitions have exponentially decreasing thresholds.

The main contribution is a theoretical guarantee showing that STAR-T (the streaming algorithm) outputs a summary of size O((k + m log k) log²k) that, when processed by a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY), achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee even after any m elements are removed from the summary. The authors prove this in Theorem 4.1, showing a constant approximation ratio approaching 0.149 as k → ∞. The proof relies on careful analysis of the partitioned thresholding structure, with Lemmas 4.2-4.4 showing how the algorithm handles both the case where many buckets are full and where most buckets remain partially filled.

The paper demonstrates competitive performance on two applications: dominating set (using Twitter and Amazon networks) and personalized movie recommendation (using MovieLens data). In these experiments, STAR-T-GREEDY matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, even though SIEVE-STREAMING is allowed to know in advance which elements will be removed. The authors also show that the same robust summary can be used for multiple different removal scenarios, which is a practical advantage.

The work builds on previous NIPS contributions in submodular streaming (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014; Norouzi-Fard et al., 2016) and robust submodular maximization (Bogunovic et al., 2017; Orlin et al., 2016). The key novelty lies in the partitioned thresholding approach that enables robustness against any m removals while maintaining a compact summary, which is not addressed by prior work. The authors also provide a practical extension (Theorem 4.5) to handle the case where f(OPT(k, V\E)) is unknown, using multiple parallel instances to approximate this value.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical contribution is significant - the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization with constant-factor approximation guarantee.
2. The partitioned thresholding structure is elegant and novel, providing a clean way to handle robustness in streaming settings.
3. The practical experiments demonstrate the algorithm's competitiveness against state-of-the-art methods, even when those methods have the advantage of knowing which elements will be removed.
4. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with detailed proofs in the supplementary material.
5. The authors provide a practical implementation note that w=1 works well in practice, which is helpful for practitioners.

Weaknesses:
1. The experimental results are somewhat limited - the dominating set objective (f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z|) differs from the standard influence maximization model (Kempe et al., 2003), so it's unclear how well the algorithm would perform on the more common diffusion models.
2. The performance gains over SIEVE-STREAMING are marginal in the experiments, and in some cases SIEVE-STREAMING slightly outperforms STAR-T-GREEDY.
3. The paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of the algorithm to the choice of parameters (τ and w), beyond noting that w=1 works well in practice.
4. The theoretical bound of 0.149 is relatively low compared to the (1-1/e)≈0.632 guarantee of the standard greedy algorithm.

Originality: The paper's approach to robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting is novel. While there has been work on robust submodular maximization (Bogunovic et al., 2017) and streaming submodular maximization (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014), this is the first to combine these two aspects in a streaming setting with a constant-factor guarantee.

Significance: The problem addressed is important for large-scale applications where data is produced rapidly and robustness against element removal is required. The two-stage approach (compute once, query multiple times) has practical value for applications like personalized recommendations where user preferences change over time.

Quality: The theoretical analysis is sound and well-motivated, with careful attention to the partitioned thresholding structure. The experimental design is reasonable, though the results don't strongly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a good balance between theoretical and experimental results. The algorithm descriptions are precise, and the supplementary material provides detailed proofs.

Pro: Novel approach to robust submodular maximization in streaming settings; solid theoretical guarantee; practical implementation with w=1 working well; two-stage structure enables multiple queries on the same summary.

Con: Marginal experimental improvements over existing methods; the dominating set objective differs from standard influence maximization models; the theoretical approximation ratio (0.149) is relatively low; limited exploration of parameter sensitivity.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution that addresses an important problem in submodular optimization. While the experimental results don't strongly demonstrate superiority over existing methods, the theoretical contribution is significant and the two-stage approach has practical value. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the literature. I recommend acceptance, as the theoretical contribution is novel and the problem addressed is important for large-scale machine learning applications.
