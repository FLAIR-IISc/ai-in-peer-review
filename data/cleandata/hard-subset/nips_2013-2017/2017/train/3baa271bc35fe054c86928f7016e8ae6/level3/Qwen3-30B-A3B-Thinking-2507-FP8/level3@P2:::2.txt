

# Review for "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper proposes a novel two-stage algorithm for robust submodular maximization under cardinality constraints in the streaming setting. The authors introduce STAR-T (Streaming Robust submodular algorithm with Partitioned Thresholding), which constructs a robust summary of size O((k + m log k) log k) in a single pass over the data. The second stage applies a simple greedy algorithm to the remaining elements after any m removals, achieving a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The work builds upon the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8] but extends it to handle robustness against element removals.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the growing literature on streaming submodular maximization [8, 12, 13]. It extends the partitioning structure from recent robust centralized algorithms [11] to the streaming setting, using an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with careful handling of the partition structure and thresholding scheme to achieve robustness against any m removals. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms or matches SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter knows the removed elements in advance, which is a significant practical advantage.

The main strengths of the paper include:
1. Clear theoretical contribution with a novel algorithmic approach for the robust streaming setting.
2. Theoretical guarantees that hold for any number of removals m (within the memory constraints).
3. Practical relevance demonstrated in two data summarization tasks: dominating set and personalized movie recommendation.
4. Theoretical analysis that carefully balances the trade-offs between memory usage and approximation guarantee.
5. Practical implementation details (e.g., w=1 working well in experiments) that enhance the paper's applicability.

The main weaknesses include:
1. The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope and does not thoroughly validate the claimed robustness. The influence maximization experiments use a non-standard objective f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z| rather than the classical diffusion model from Kempe et al. [3], making it unclear how the method would perform on the true influence maximization problem.
2. STAR-T-GREEDY does not consistently outperform SIEVE-STREAMING in the experiments, which weakens the practical significance of the claimed advantage.
3. The paper does not sufficiently compare against other robust submodular maximization approaches [9, 10, 11] in the streaming setting.
4. The theoretical analysis assumes knowledge of f(OPT(k, V\E)), which is later addressed through a parallel algorithm (Theorem 4.5), but the practical implementation details of this extension are not thoroughly discussed.

Regarding the NIPS criteria:
- **Quality**: The theoretical results are sound and well-motivated, with a clear proof structure. The experimental validation, while somewhat limited, is sufficient to support the main claims. The paper acknowledges the limitations of the experimental setup in the supplementary material.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The algorithm descriptions and theoretical results are presented in a logical sequence. The figures help illustrate the partitioning structure. Minor improvements could be made to the presentation of the experimental results to better highlight the key findings.
- **Originality**: The work represents a clear advance over existing streaming submodular maximization approaches by adding robustness guarantees against element removals. The partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is novel for this setting, and the paper properly situates itself against related work [9, 10, 11].
- **Significance**: The problem addressed has clear practical importance in large-scale data summarization where robustness to element removal is required. The constant-factor guarantee for the robust setting is significant, though the practical advantage over SIEVE-STREAMING needs stronger experimental validation.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a solid theoretical contribution to an important problem in large-scale machine learning, with clear connections to applications in data summarization. The work advances the state of the art in streaming submodular maximization by addressing a meaningful extension to the robust setting.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel algorithmic approach for robust streaming submodular maximization
- Strong theoretical guarantees with a clear analysis
- Practical relevance to important data summarization problems
- Demonstrated performance in two application domains
- Clear connection to related work in NIPS proceedings

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited experimental validation of the robustness claim
- Inconsistent performance advantage over SIEVE-STREAMING
- Non-standard objective function in influence maximization experiments
- Limited comparison with other robust submodular maximization approaches

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to a meaningful problem in streaming submodular maximization. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened to better demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method, the theoretical contribution is solid and the paper makes a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the experimental evaluation and discussion of the robustness claims.
