This paper studies the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a setting that combines two challenges: the data arrives in a stream, and the solution must be robust to the removal of up to `m` elements. The authors propose a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a single-pass streaming algorithm called STAR-T processes the data stream to create a robust summary `S`. This algorithm uses a novel structure of partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing greedy thresholds. In the second stage, after an adversary removes an arbitrary set `E` of at most `m` elements from the ground set, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining elements of the summary (`S \ E`) to produce a solution `Z` of size at most `k`.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The size of the summary `S` is shown to be `O((k + m log k) log k)`, which is notably sublinear in the size of the ground set. This work builds upon ideas from streaming submodular maximization, particularly the thresholding approach of SIEVE-STREAMING [8], and extends them to the robust setting. It also relates to the growing literature on robust submodular maximization [9, 11], but tackles the problem in a streaming context with a practical two-stage model. The authors also compare their work to a concurrent paper [18], highlighting a more favorable memory dependency on `k`. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a practical and challenging problem at the intersection of three important areas: submodular optimization, streaming algorithms, and robust optimization. The proposed two-stage model, where a robust summary is built once and can then be queried multiple times after different potential removals, is well-motivated for interactive applications and scenarios where re-processing the entire dataset is infeasible. This is a significant problem, and the proposed approach is novel and well-suited for it.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper's technical contribution is strong. The STAR-T algorithm, with its partitioned structure and exponentially decreasing thresholds, is an elegant extension of existing streaming techniques to handle the robustness requirement. The analysis is non-trivial, combining arguments about the properties of the greedy algorithm with a careful case analysis based on the state of the buckets. The result is a constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is a strong theoretical outcome for this challenging setting. The claims appear to be technically sound.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The algorithm and the two-stage process are explained clearly, and the proof sketch provides good intuition for the main theoretical result. The use of figures to illustrate the algorithm's structure and experimental results is helpful.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the practical effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison against SIEVE-STREAMING, a strong streaming baseline, is particularly insightful because the baseline is given the advantage of knowing the removed set `E` in advance. The fact that the proposed STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably or even better demonstrates the quality of the robust summary it constructs. The interactive movie recommendation scenario is a good showcase for the practicality of the two-stage model.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Problem Formulation and Motivation:** While the problem is interesting, the paper could do a better job of motivating the specific formulation and discussing its relationship to other models of robustness. For example, the classic robust formulation of Krause et al. [9] optimizes `max_Z min_{E ⊂ Z} f(Z \ E)`. The proposed model is different and arguably more practical for streaming, but a more explicit discussion of why this model was chosen over alternatives would strengthen the paper. The motivation in the application sections could also be sharpened. For instance, in the movie recommendation example, it should be made clearer why re-running a non-streaming algorithm on the (presumably much smaller) set of non-removed movies is not a viable option, thus necessitating the pre-computed robust summary.

2.  **Generalizability of the Adversarial Model:** The current formulation assumes an adversary removes up to `m` elements after the stream has been processed and before the query is made. It would be beneficial to discuss the limitations of this model. For instance, how would the approach fare if removals could happen *during* the stream? Or if the adversary was adaptive? A discussion of these aspects would help to better position the contribution and highlight interesting avenues for future work. This relates to the feeling that the problem formulation, while practical, might not be as general as it could be.

**Summary of pros and cons for acceptance:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a significant and practical problem by combining streaming, submodular maximization, and robustness.
    *   Presents a novel algorithm (STAR-T) with a strong, constant-factor approximation guarantee.
    *   The technical analysis is non-trivial and appears correct.
    *   The memory complexity has a better dependency on `k` than concurrent work.
    *   Empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness against a powerful baseline.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The motivation for the specific problem formulation and its advantages over other robustness models could be better articulated.
    *   The connection to machine learning applications could be strengthened to make the paper's relevance to the NIPS audience more explicit.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its strong technical contribution to an important problem, outweigh its weaknesses, which are primarily related to presentation and framing. This is a solid piece of work that advances the state of the art in large-scale submodular optimization.
