

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

The paper studies a variant of the classic submodular maximization problem where elements arrive in a streaming fashion, and the solution must remain robust to the removal of up to m elements after the stream is processed. The authors propose STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that constructs a summary set T of size O((m log k + k) log^2 k) such that after any m elements are removed from T, a greedy algorithm applied to the remaining elements achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee. This builds on SIEVE-STREAMING [8] for submodular maximization and extends it to handle robustness requirements.

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by introducing a partitioned thresholding approach with exponentially decreasing thresholds. The analysis is non-trivial, combining properties of the partitioned structure with the decreasing thresholding rule to achieve a constant-factor approximation. The authors prove that their method achieves a 0.149(1-1/log k)-approximation guarantee, which approaches 0.149 as k grows. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that STAR-T-GREEDY matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING even when the latter is allowed to know the removed elements in advance, which is a strong empirical result.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical results appear correct, with a well-structured proof that addresses multiple cases. The algorithm design is innovative for the specific problem setting, and the analysis carefully handles the robustness requirement. The paper also correctly relates to recent work on robust submodular maximization [9, 10, 11] and streaming submodular optimization [5, 8, 12, 13], noting the differences with concurrent work [18].

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. The motivation for the robustness requirement is weak and underdeveloped. The applications presented (movie recommendations, dominating set) are not compellingly argued as cases where robustness is essential rather than simply a theoretical curiosity. The problem formulation is also too narrow, focusing only on cardinality constraints rather than more general constraints that would make the results more broadly applicable. The paper does not address why robustness is needed in practice—why can't we simply recompute the summary after removals? This is a critical gap in the motivation.

The significance of the work is questionable for NIPS. While the technical contribution is sound, the problem studied is a specialized variant of streaming submodular maximization that doesn't significantly advance the field beyond incremental improvements. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this work differs from or improves upon existing streaming submodular maximization methods in ways that would be broadly useful to the machine learning community. The applications section fails to convince that robustness is a critical requirement in real-world scenarios.

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear algorithm descriptions and helpful figures. However, the theoretical contribution would be stronger if the problem formulation were more general, and the empirical section could better demonstrate the practical utility of robustness.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- The algorithm and analysis are technically sound and non-trivial
- The constant-factor approximation guarantee is theoretically significant
- The empirical results are strong, showing the method outperforms or matches baselines even when baselines have prior knowledge of removed elements
- The memory complexity is better than concurrent work [18] (logarithmic in k vs linear)

Con arguments against acceptance:
- The problem formulation is too narrow (only cardinality constraints)
- The motivation for robustness is weak and applications are not compelling
- The relevance to NIPS is questionable as it's a specialized variant of a well-studied problem
- The paper doesn't clearly explain why robustness is needed in practice (why can't we just recompute the summary after removals?)

Overall, while the technical contribution is solid, the paper's narrow problem formulation and weak motivation significantly limit its significance for NIPS. The work is more suitable for a specialized optimization conference than NIPS, which focuses on broader machine learning impact. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the motivation, broaden the problem formulation, and better articulate the practical need for robustness to make this a compelling NIPS contribution.
