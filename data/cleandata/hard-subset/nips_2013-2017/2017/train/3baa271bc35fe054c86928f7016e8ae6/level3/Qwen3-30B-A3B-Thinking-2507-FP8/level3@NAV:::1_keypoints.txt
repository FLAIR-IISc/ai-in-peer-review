- Proposes streaming robust submodular maximization via a two-stage algorithm (STAR-T and STAR-T-GREEDY) that builds a summary T of size O((k + m log k) log^2 k) enabling constant-factor approximation after any m removals.
- Builds on prior robust and streaming submodular work (Krause et al. 2008; Bogunovic et al. 2017; Badanidiyuru et al. 2014).
- Key innovation is partitioned thresholding with exponentially decreasing thresholds to tolerate arbitrary element removals while maintaining theoretical guarantees.
- Technical quality is strong: non-trivial algorithm design and a complex, apparently correct analysis.
- Theoretical results are well-supported by the paper’s proofs.
- Experiments show competitive performance against SIEVE-STREAMING on dominating set and movie recommendation tasks, even when the baseline knows removed elements.
- Motivation for robustness is superficial, relying on generic examples like “movie recommendations” without concrete critical applications.
- Problem formulation is overly narrow, limited to cardinality constraints rather than richer constraints common in ML.
- Practical significance is questionable because the robustness requirement’s real-world necessity is not convincingly demonstrated.
- Relevance to NIPS is doubtful due to weak connections to core machine learning problems and applications.
- Pro: strong technical contribution and promising empirical results.
- Con: weak motivation/applications, narrow problem scope, and limited perceived impact.
- Overall: technically sound but narrow and weakly motivated, making it hard to justify as a significant NIPS contribution.
