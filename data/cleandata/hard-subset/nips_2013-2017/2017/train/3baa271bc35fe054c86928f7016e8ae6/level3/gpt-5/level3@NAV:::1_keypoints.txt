- Problem: Studies streaming deletion-robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint with one-pass summary T that remains good after any m deletions.  
- Algorithm: Proposes STAR-T using partitioned summary with exponentially increasing bucket sizes and decreasing thresholds; STAR-T-GREEDY runs greedy on T \ E at query time.  
- Theoretical result: Proves a constant-factor (~0.149) approximation in one pass while storing O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements, improving dependence on k over concurrent work.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on SIEVE-STREAMING and robust non-streaming formulations and relates to recent NeurIPS submodular summarization literature.  
- Technical quality: Analysis appears sound; partitioned thresholding and case analysis are non-trivial and well handled.  
- Handling unknown OPT: Resolves dependence on OPT via standard parallel guesses.  
- Experiments: Evaluated on dominating set and MovieLens, compared to strong baseline (SIEVE-STREAMING) that knows deletions; STAR-T remains competitive.  
- Empirical limitations: Limited scope, no sensitivity analysis over parameters (w, m, τ), no oracle-query/runtime reporting, and no ablation isolating partitioning vs thresholds.  
- Clarity: Overall clear and organized, but constants/asymptotics are inconsistently stated and τ/multi-instance exposition and pseudocode need tightening.  
- Originality: Novel combination of partitioned summaries with exponentially decreasing thresholds in the streaming-robust setting and a meaningful memory-vs-k improvement.  
- Significance: Theoretical contribution is meaningful for scalable summarization with deletions, but the robustness model is narrow (deletions from summary, monotone f, cardinality only).  
- Pros for acceptance: Non-trivial correct-looking theory with improved memory dependence; simple one-pass two-stage design with constant-factor guarantee; competitive empirical results vs strong baselines.  
- Cons against acceptance: Weak motivation/application stories and limited evaluation; narrow problem formulation, small constant and extra log^2 factors; borderline fit for NeurIPS without stronger ML-facing evidence.  
- Suggestions: Strengthen application scenarios (e.g., right-to-be-forgotten), report time/query complexity, add sensitivity and ablation studies, and discuss extensions beyond monotone cardinality constraints.
