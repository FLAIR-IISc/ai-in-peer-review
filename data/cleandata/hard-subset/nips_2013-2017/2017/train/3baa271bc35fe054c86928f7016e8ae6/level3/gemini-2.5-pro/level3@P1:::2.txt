This paper addresses the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a streaming and robust setting. The goal is to select a summary set `S` in a single pass over the data, such that even after an adversary removes an arbitrary subset of `m` elements from the *entire ground set*, one can still extract a near-optimal solution of size `k` from the remaining elements of `S`.

The authors propose a two-stage procedure. The first stage is a streaming algorithm called STAR-T, which constructs the robust summary `S`. The core idea of STAR-T is a novel partitioned data structure, where elements are assigned to buckets based on their marginal gain relative to an exponentially decreasing threshold. This structure is inspired by recent non-streaming robust algorithms (e.g., Bogunovic et al., 2017) and extends the thresholding concept from non-robust streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014). In the second stage, after `m` elements are removed, a standard GREEDY algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining summary to find the final solution. The main theoretical result (Theorem 4.1) provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee for this procedure, requiring memory of size O((k + m log k) log k). This is a significant result, as it provides the first theoretical guarantee for the robust submodular maximization problem in the streaming setting. The paper also discusses a concurrent work (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2017), noting that the proposed algorithm achieves a more favorable memory complexity with logarithmic rather than linear dependence on `k`.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The main theoretical contribution, Theorem 4.1, establishes a constant-factor approximation guarantee, which is a strong result for this challenging problem setting. The proof sketch, based on balancing two cases via Lemmas 4.2-4.4, appears solid and combines ideas from robust and streaming analysis in a non-trivial way. The algorithm is also made practical by extending it to handle the unknown optimal value `f(OPT(k, V \ E))` using parallel instances, a standard but crucial technique.

However, the quality of the experimental evaluation is a point of concern. While the experiments demonstrate that the method works, they are not entirely convincing in establishing its superiority.
1.  In the dominating set experiments (Figure 2), STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably to or only marginally better than the SIEVE-STREAMING baseline. In some cases (e.g., Fig 2d for larger k), SIEVE-STREAMING appears to perform slightly better, even though it's a non-robust algorithm given the advantage of knowing the removed set in advance. The performance gap does not seem significant enough to strongly support the practical advantage of the proposed robust structure.
2.  The objective function used for the dominating set problem, `f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|`, is a simplification of the influence maximization problem. The standard influence maximization model, as introduced by Kempe et al. (2003), relies on simulating information diffusion (e.g., via IC or LT models), which is computationally more expensive but more realistic. The performance on this simplified proxy function may not directly translate to the more complex, true influence spread objective.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear, and the proposed two-stage algorithm is easy to understand, aided by the illustrative Figure 1. The main ideas behind the theoretical analysis are conveyed effectively in the proof sketches.

**Originality:** The paper's primary strength lies in its originality. It is the first work to formally define and provide a solution with theoretical guarantees for robust submodular maximization in the single-pass streaming model. The combination of a partitioned structure with an exponentially decreasing thresholding scheme is a novel algorithmic contribution that effectively marries ideas from two distinct lines of prior work (robust optimization and streaming algorithms).

**Significance:** The problem itself is highly significant. Many real-world applications of data summarization, such as sensor placement, influence maximization, and recommendation systems, operate on massive data streams and require solutions that are resilient to failures or adversarial attacks. This paper provides a principled and theoretically-grounded approach to this important problem. The logarithmic dependence of the memory on `k` is particularly appealing, making the algorithm potentially scalable. If the method proves effective in practice, it is likely that researchers and practitioners will build upon these ideas.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Problem Formulation and Solution:** The paper introduces and provides the first solution with theoretical guarantees for the important and challenging problem of robust streaming submodular maximization.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** It provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee with a memory complexity that is only logarithmically dependent on `k`, which is a significant theoretical achievement.
3.  **High Potential Impact:** The problem is well-motivated and relevant to many large-scale machine learning applications, giving the work high potential for future impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Underwhelming Experimental Results:** The empirical evidence does not compellingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over a strong non-robust baseline. The performance gains are marginal, and in some cases, the baseline performs better.
2.  **Questionable Experimental Proxy:** The use of a simplified objective for the influence maximization task raises questions about the generalizability of the empirical findings to real-world scenarios.

### Detailed Comments

- The experiments show that setting the memory parameter `w=1` works well, while the theory requires `w` to scale with `m` and `log k`. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on this gap between theory and practice. Is this because real-world removal patterns are less adversarial than the worst-case analysis assumes?
- In the movie recommendation experiment, the removals are quite drastic (e.g., 59% of the universe for the genre constraint). It is impressive that the algorithm remains robust. This is a strong point and could be emphasized more, as it shows the practical utility beyond the `|E| <= m` constraint for which the theory is developed.
- Could the authors comment on why SIEVE-STREAMING sometimes outperforms STAR-T-GREEDY in Figure 2? Is it because the greedy selection in the second stage is suboptimal compared to the thresholding of SIEVE-STREAMING, even on a smaller set? This might suggest that STAR-T-SIEVE is a more natural combination than STAR-T-GREEDY.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to a new and important problem. While the experimental validation could be strengthened, the novelty and significance of the theoretical results warrant its acceptance.
