This paper presents a novel algorithm, STAR-T, for the problem of streaming robust submodular maximization. The algorithm makes one pass over the data and outputs a set of elements that is robust against the removal of up to m elements. The authors show that after the removal of any m elements, a simple greedy algorithm that runs on the obtained set achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee for robust submodular function maximization.

The problem of streaming robust submodular maximization is well-motivated, as it arises in many large-scale machine learning applications where the extracted representative set is required to be robust against the removal of certain elements. However, the authors could have provided more concrete application examples and motivation to make the problem more compelling.

The main result of the paper is a constant-factor approximation algorithm that outputs a set of size (m log k + k) log^2 k. The algorithm and analysis are non-trivial and technically challenging. The authors use a novel partitioning structure and an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule to achieve the desired approximation guarantee.

The technical quality of the paper is high, and the results appear to be correct. The authors provide a detailed proof of their main result, which is well-organized and easy to follow. The paper also includes experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm in practice.

However, the relevance of the paper to the NIPS conference is questionable. While the paper does present a novel algorithm for a well-motivated problem, the problem itself may not be directly related to the core topics of NIPS.

One critique of the paper is that the problem formulation is not general enough. The authors assume that the submodular function is monotone and normalized, which may not be the case in all applications. Additionally, the authors assume that the removal of elements is arbitrary, which may not be realistic in all scenarios.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and original contribution to the field of submodular optimization. However, the relevance of the paper to NIPS and the generality of the problem formulation are concerns that need to be addressed.
