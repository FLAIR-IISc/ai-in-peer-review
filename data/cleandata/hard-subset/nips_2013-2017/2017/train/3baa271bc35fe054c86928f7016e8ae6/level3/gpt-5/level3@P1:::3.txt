This paper studies robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting: the goal is to make a single pass over a stream of elements, retain a succinct summary S, and later, after the adversarial removal of up to m elements, extract a size-k solution Z ⊆ S\E that approximates OPT(k, V\E). The proposed two-stage method, STAR-T + STAR-T-GREEDY, organizes S into partitions of exponentially sized buckets, with exponentially decreasing marginal-gain thresholds. Each arriving element is placed into the first non-full bucket whose threshold it meets; at query time a standard greedy algorithm is run on S\E. The authors prove that with S of size O((k + m log k) log k)–O((k + m log k) log2 k) elements (see comment on this discrepancy below), the method attains a constant-factor approximation: as k grows, f(Z) ≥ 0.149 · f(OPT(k, V\E)). Empirically, on (i) a dominating-set-style influence objective on large graphs and (ii) a personalized movie recommendation objective, STAR-T matches or outperforms strong streaming and greedy baselines, even when baselines are allowed to know the deletions beforehand.

Relation to prior work: The paper sits at the intersection of streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING, Badanidiyuru et al. 2014) and robustness to deletions (e.g., Orlin et al. 2016; PRO, Bogunovic et al. 2017). Unlike prior robust methods that are centralized and fill buckets greedily, STAR-T uses thresholded placement into a non-uniform partition structure akin to PRO but tailored for streaming. The concurrent deletion-robust streaming work [18] guarantees a 1/2 − ε approximation but requires O(mk log k/ε) memory; STAR-T trades a smaller constant (≈0.149) for memory only logarithmic in k and linear in m up to log factors.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important and previously unaddressed combination: streaming + deletion robustness with a single-pass algorithm and post hoc queries.
- Novel partitioned, thresholded summary construction that is simple to implement and analyze in streaming.
- Theoretical constant-factor guarantee after arbitrary m deletions with summary size sublinear in n and only logarithmic dependence on k (vs. linear in [18]).
- Experiments are thoughtfully designed: STAR-T is compared against strong baselines that even know the deletions; performance is competitive on two distinct tasks.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Inconsistency in space bounds: the abstract/conclusion state O((k + m log k) log2 k), while Theorem 4.1 and Eq. (3) imply O((k + m log k) log k). Please reconcile and state the tightest bound clearly (including constants when possible).
- Threshold rule mismatch: the text describes thresholds τ/2^i, while Algorithm 1 uses τ/min{2^i, k}. Clarify which is used in the analysis and unify notation.
- Constant 0.149 is low compared to 1/2 − ε of [18]; a more explicit discussion of the approximation–memory trade-off would strengthen the contribution (e.g., regimes where STAR-T is preferable).
- Lack of lower bounds or impossibility results. Even simple space-approximation trade-off lower bounds (or discussion of known barriers) would contextualize the constants and memory scaling.
- Proof sketch is hard to follow in places; the “innovative analysis” claims would benefit from a clearer high-level roadmap and intuition for the key lemmas, especially the balancing argument leading to the 0.149 constant.
- Experimental scope: the “influence” objective is neighborhood coverage, which is easier and less realistic than IC/LT models commonly used in influence maximization. Including at least an approximate influence estimator (e.g., RR-sets) or a discussion of applicability would improve significance claims.
- Evaluation omissions: missing wall-clock time, number of oracle evaluations, final summary size, and sensitivity to w, m, and stream order. Since STAR-T relies on thresholds, robustness to adversarial order is important to demonstrate.
- The algorithm requires τ close to f(OPT); the parallel variant increases memory by a log factor. More guidance on choosing ε, the number of parallel instances, and an empirical study of sensitivity to τ would be useful.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The structure and lemmas are plausible and align with standard greedy/submodularity analyses; however, the above inconsistencies and sketchy exposition impede full verification. Please check the constants, threshold definitions, and space accounting carefully.
- Completeness: The parallel-τ construction follows SIEVE-STREAMING; proofs appear largely deferred to the supplement. Adding more proof intuition in the main paper would help.
- Empirics: Positive but would benefit from broader objectives, resource usage reporting, and sensitivity analyses.

Clarity
- The algorithmic description is mostly clear, but reconcile threshold and space expressions, and define all parameters (w, τ) consistently. A small illustrative example of bucket filling across partitions would aid comprehension.
- Streamline the proof sketch: state the two cases, the role of full vs. non-full partitions, and how the balance yields 0.149.

Originality
- The partitioned, thresholded streaming design for robust deletions is original relative to prior robust (centralized) and streaming (non-robust) work. The memory–approximation trade-off compared to [18] is an interesting alternative.

Significance
- If tightened and clarified, this offers a practical approach for deletion-robust summarization with strong theoretical guarantees and modest memory. The ability to answer multiple post hoc queries from one summary is valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Resolve the space and threshold discrepancies; provide clear, consolidated statements of theorems with dependencies on (k, m, log k, ε).
- Discuss the trade-off with [18] explicitly and, if possible, add an empirical comparison in regimes where mk memory is affordable.
- Report runtime, memory, summary size, and number of function evaluations; plot quality vs. memory budget and vs. m.
- Add experiments under IC/LT or with RR-set approximations, or clearly scope the results to coverage-style objectives.
- Include sensitivity to w and stream order (random vs. adversarial).
- Provide guidance for selecting τ (or ε in the parallel variant) in practice.

Arguments for acceptance
- First constant-factor method for streaming robust submodular maximization with memory polylog in k.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with competitive empirical performance on two tasks.
- Clear contribution at the intersection of two active lines of work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Exposition and theorem statements need cleanup; key discrepancies and a low constant (0.149) reduce confidence/impact.
- Limited experimental scope on influence; missing resource and sensitivity analyses.
- No lower bounds to contextualize the achieved constant and memory.
