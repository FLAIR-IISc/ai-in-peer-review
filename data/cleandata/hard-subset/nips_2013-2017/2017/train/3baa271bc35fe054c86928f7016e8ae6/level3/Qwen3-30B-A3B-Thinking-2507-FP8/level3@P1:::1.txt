

This paper studies the problem of robust streaming submodular maximization, where the goal is to construct a summary set T from a stream of elements such that after removing any m elements from T, one can still select a k-sized set with high objective value. The authors propose STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that uses a partitioned thresholding approach to construct T with size O((k + m log k) log^2 k), and show that running a greedy algorithm on the remaining elements achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee.

The work builds on prior streaming submodular maximization (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014) and robust submodular maximization (Krause et al., 2008; Bogunovic et al., 2017). While previous robust submodular maximization work focused on offline settings, this paper extends it to the streaming setting with a novel partitioned thresholding structure. The key innovation is the exponentially decreasing thresholding rule across partitions, which allows the algorithm to maintain robustness against any m removals with sublinear memory.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The algorithm design is non-trivial and the theoretical analysis is comprehensive. The main result (Theorem 4.1) shows a constant-factor approximation guarantee (0.149(1-1/log k)) with a reasonable summary size bound. The proof sketches provided are sufficient to follow the main ideas, and the supplementary material appears to contain the complete proofs. The authors also address the practical challenge of not knowing f(OPT(k, V\E)) by providing a parallel variant (Theorem 4.5) that maintains the approximation guarantee with slightly increased memory.

The paper's strengths include:
- A technically sound algorithm with a solid theoretical foundation
- A clean and well-structured analysis
- Empirical validation showing competitive performance against SIEVE-STREAMING, even when SIEVE-STREAMING knows the removed elements in advance

The weaknesses are significant:
- The motivation and applications are weak. The paper mentions movie recommendations and dominating set problems, but doesn't convincingly demonstrate why robustness is crucial in these contexts or how the problem formulation addresses practical challenges. The "motivation" section reads more like a description of applications than a compelling justification for the robustness requirement.
- The problem formulation is too specific. The paper only considers removal of any m elements from the summary, but doesn't address more general robustness requirements (e.g., adversarial removal of m elements from the entire dataset, or probabilistic removal models). This limits the applicability of the work.
- The relevance to NIPS is questionable. While submodular optimization is a well-studied topic at NIPS, this work is a specialized variant that doesn't clearly advance the field in a way that would be of broad interest to the NIPS community. The problem is highly constrained, and the theoretical results don't appear to significantly advance the state of the art beyond prior work on robust submodular maximization (Bogunovic et al., 2017).

The paper's significance is limited by its narrow focus. While the technical contribution is solid, the problem is too specific to have broad impact. The authors claim their method "matches or outperforms existing greedy and streaming methods, even if they are allowed the benefit of knowing the removed subset in advance," but this is a narrow comparison that doesn't address whether the robustness requirement itself is meaningful in practice.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written with clear algorithm descriptions and a logical flow. The proofs are presented in a way that's accessible to experts in the field. However, the lack of compelling motivation and the narrow problem formulation reduce the paper's overall impact.

The main pro arguments for acceptance are:
- The technical contribution is sound and non-trivial
- The algorithm and analysis are well-executed
- The empirical results are promising for the specific problem setting

The main con arguments against acceptance are:
- Weak motivation and application examples
- Problem formulation is too narrow and not sufficiently justified
- Limited significance for the NIPS community

Given the narrow focus and weak motivation, this paper is likely too specialized for NIPS. While the technical quality is high, the problem is a highly constrained variant of a well-studied topic that doesn't clearly advance the field in a way that would be of broad interest to the NIPS community. The authors could strengthen the paper by providing more compelling motivation for the robustness requirement and demonstrating how their approach generalizes to other robustness models. I recommend rejection for lack of significance and relevance to NIPS.
