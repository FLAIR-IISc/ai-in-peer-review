Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles submodular maximization with two simultaneous constraints that have each been studied extensively on their own but not together: single-pass streaming with limited memory, and robustness to the deletion of up to m elements. The goal is to build a compact summary S in one pass such that, after any adversarial removal E of up to m items, one can extract a size-k set Z ⊆ S \ E with value comparable to OPT(k, V \ E).

The proposed STAR-T algorithm maintains a partitioned structure of buckets whose capacities grow exponentially across partitions, and uses exponentially decreasing thresholds to decide whether an arriving element is placed in the first non-full bucket whose threshold it satisfies. At query time, STAR-T-GREEDY runs standard greedy on S \ E to produce the final k-set. The analysis shows that storing O((k + m log k) log k) elements suffices if an approximation to f(OPT) is known; when τ is unknown, running multiple parallel instances yields total memory O((k + m log k) log^2 k) and a constant-factor approximation of 0.149 (as k → ∞), robust to any m removals.

The design draws on SIEVE-STREAMING (threshold-based streaming for non-robust submodular maximization) and on the partitioned bucket structure used in centralized robust algorithms such as PRO, but differs crucially in how buckets are populated (first bucket above threshold rather than fully greedy per bucket) and in the exponentially decreasing thresholds across partitions. The paper also situates itself against concurrent “deletion-robust” streaming work (Mirzasoleiman et al., ICML’17) offering a 1/2−ε approximation but with O(mk log k/ε) memory, highlighting STAR-T’s logarithmic dependence on k in the robustness overhead.

Technical quality:
- Theory: The main guarantee—that STAR-T stores O((k + m log k) log k) elements (or O((k + m log k) log^2 k) without f(OPT)) and achieves a 0.149-approximation after m deletions via a final greedy pass—is novel and nontrivial. The bucket/threshold design and case analysis (many full buckets vs. many non-full buckets) are interesting and plausibly correct. That said, the proof sketch is terse and hard to follow, and the claimed “innovative analysis” is not unpacked sufficiently to provide intuition. There are no non-trivial lower bounds for the streaming-robust trade-off, so the tightness of the 0.149 factor and memory dependences remains unclear.
- Algorithms/complexity: The per-element processing cost could be discussed more carefully (worst-case loops over O(wk) buckets per element), and guidance for choosing w beyond the asymptotic bound would help. The τ-approximation scheme via parallel instances is standard and appropriate.
- Experiments: Two applications are considered. For influence maximization, the objective is the neighborhood-size (dominating set) function, which is submodular but considerably simpler than IC/LT cascade models used in the influence literature; this raises questions about applicability to classic network-dependent influence objectives in streaming. The movie recommendation setup is sensible and demonstrates an interactive “right to be forgotten” use case. Empirically, STAR-T performs on par with or better than strong baselines, including methods that know the removals E beforehand.

Clarity:
The paper is generally understandable at a high level, but the analysis and proof organization need work. The differences from prior robust work (e.g., PRO) should be articulated more clearly, especially how thresholded placement changes the robustness argument. The role and selection of parameters w and τ deserve a more intuitive exposition, and the transition from the base memory O((k + m log k) log k) to O((k + m log k) log^2 k) when τ is unknown should be made explicit earlier to avoid confusion. The proof sketch would benefit from a figure illustrating the two main cases and from intuitive bounds before diving into constants.

Originality:
High. To my knowledge, this is the first to jointly address streaming and robustness for monotone submodular maximization with a constant-factor approximation and sublinear memory. The partitioned thresholding scheme and the way the analysis leverages “full vs. non-full” partitions are fresh combinations of ideas.

Significance:
High. Many practical summarization systems need to precompute small summaries under data volume constraints and then be resilient to deletions/preferences/failures. Achieving any constant-factor approximation with a one-pass, near-linear-in-k memory footprint in this joint setting is a strong result. The constant 0.149 is modest, but acceptable for a first result; memory scaling that is only logarithmic in k for the robustness overhead is attractive relative to concurrent work.

Strengths:
- First constant-factor algorithm for robust submodular maximization in the streaming model.
- Clear algorithmic idea (partitioned buckets with exponentially decreasing thresholds), distinct from prior robust methods.
- Tight memory–approximation trade-off in practice: O((k + m log k) log^2 k) elements without f(OPT); O((k + m log k) log k) if τ is known.
- Empirical performance competitive with baselines that even know the deletions in advance.

Weaknesses:
- No non-trivial lower bounds to contextualize the approximation factor and memory dependence in the joint streaming+robust setting.
- Analysis and writing are often unclear; the “innovative analysis” is not well explained and the proof sketch is hard to follow without the appendix.
- Influence maximization experiments use the simplified neighborhood objective rather than IC/LT, limiting claims about applicability to traditional influence models.
- Limited discussion of per-element time; practical guidance for w and τ is sparse.

Suggestions and questions for the authors:
- Provide intuition for the two main cases in the analysis and why the constants arise; a schematic figure would help.
- Clarify parameterization: when exactly does memory become O((k + m log k) log^2 k)? Offer practical recipes for choosing w and τ and report sensitivity to w in experiments.
- Discuss per-element processing time and any data structures to avoid scanning many buckets.
- For influence maximization, justify the neighborhood objective and, if possible, add experiments on RR-set based objectives or discuss barriers.
- Include any partial lower bounds (even for special cases) or at least compare to known streaming/non-robust and centralized/robust lower bounds.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and significant problem setting; first constant-factor guarantee.
- Sound algorithmic contribution with a clean, implementable design.
- Strong empirical results across two applications, including robustness against unknown deletions.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Clarity issues in analysis and missing lower bounds make it hard to assess tightness.
- Experimental influence objective is simplified, raising external validity concerns.
- Limited discussion of computational overhead and parameter sensitivity.

Overall: Weak accept. The core contribution—achieving a constant-factor approximation for robust submodular maximization in the streaming model with near-optimal-looking space—is important and timely. With improved exposition and a clearer empirical story for influence, this would be a solid accept.
