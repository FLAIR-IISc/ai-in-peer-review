## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function in a streaming setting while also ensuring robustness to the removal of a fixed number of elements. This is a novel and important combination, as prior work largely addressed these concerns separately. The authors propose STAR-T, an algorithm based on partitioned thresholding, and provide a theoretical guarantee of a 0.149-approximation under m removals, retaining only O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements. 

The work builds upon existing streaming submodular maximization techniques (like SIEVE-STREAMING) and robust submodular maximization approaches, but cleverly adapts and combines them to address the combined problem. The partitioning and thresholding scheme with exponentially decreasing thresholds appears to be a key innovation, and the attempt to provide a theoretical analysis is commendable. The experimental results on influence maximization and movie recommendation are encouraging, demonstrating performance comparable to, and sometimes exceeding, algorithms with full knowledge of removals.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the authors claim an “innovative analysis,” the explanation is unfortunately unclear. The proof sketch provided is difficult to follow, and a more detailed and accessible presentation of the analysis is crucial. A significant drawback is the lack of lower bounds. Establishing lower bounds would strengthen the theoretical contribution and provide a clearer understanding of the inherent difficulty of the problem. 

Furthermore, the influence maximization experiments utilize a simplified neighborhood-size objective, which may not fully represent the complexities of traditional network-dependent influence objectives. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results to more realistic scenarios. The experimental setup could be strengthened by including comparisons on more standard influence maximization benchmarks.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and important problem – combining streaming and robustness in submodular maximization.
*   Achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee, a strong result in this challenging setting.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance in practical applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Analysis is unclear and difficult to follow.
*   Lacks lower bounds to contextualize the upper bound guarantee.
*   Influence maximization experiments use a simplified objective function.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, but its impact is limited by the clarity of the analysis and the lack of complementary lower bounds. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors significantly improving the clarity of the analysis and addressing the concerns regarding the experimental evaluation. Specifically, a more detailed explanation of the analysis and a discussion of potential lower bounds would greatly enhance the paper's quality.
