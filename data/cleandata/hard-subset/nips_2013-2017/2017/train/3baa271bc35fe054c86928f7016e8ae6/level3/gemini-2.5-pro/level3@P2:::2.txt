This paper studies the problem of maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint `k` in a streaming setting, with the additional requirement of robustness against the removal of up to `m` elements. The authors propose a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, a single-pass streaming algorithm called STAR-T constructs a robust summary `S` of the data stream. The algorithm's design is novel, based on a partitioned structure where elements are assigned to buckets based on an exponentially decreasing threshold rule. In the second stage, after an arbitrary set of `m` elements is removed from the summary `S`, a standard greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining elements to produce the final solution.

The paper's approach is related to prior work in both streaming and robust submodular maximization. It extends the thresholding idea from the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8] to the robust setting. The partitioned structure of the summary bears some resemblance to that used in non-streaming robust algorithms like PRO [11], but is adapted here for the streaming context with a novel assignment rule. The authors also appropriately position their work relative to the concurrent work of Mirzasoleiman et al. [18], highlighting that their algorithm achieves a better memory dependence on `k` (logarithmic vs. linear).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a novel and highly relevant problem. Combining the challenges of streaming data, submodular optimization, and robustness is a natural next step for large-scale data summarization tasks where the underlying data or user preferences can change. The problem is well-motivated, and a practical, provable algorithm for this setting would be a significant contribution with wide potential applications. To my knowledge, this is one of the first works to provide a streaming algorithm for this robust formulation.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical contribution is strong. The main result (Theorem 4.1) provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee for the proposed two-stage procedure, which is a non-trivial achievement. The proof sketch clearly outlines the core ideas, cleverly balancing two cases: when a partition has many full buckets (Lemma 4.2) and when it does not (Lemmas 4.3 and 4.4). The analysis appears technically sound. The paper also correctly addresses the practical issue of the unknown optimal value `f(OPT)` needed for setting the threshold `τ`, providing a standard parallelization approach to remove this assumption (Theorem 4.5).

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the proposed algorithms (STAR-T and STAR-T-GREEDY) are explained well, and the high-level structure of the theoretical analysis is provided in the main text. The use of a figure to illustrate the partitioned structure of the summary is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments demonstrate the viability of the proposed method, they are not as convincing as they could be.
    *   In the dominating set experiments (Fig. 2a-d), the proposed STAR-T-GREEDY performs comparably to, but does not consistently outperform, the SIEVE-STREAMING baseline. While it is impressive to match a baseline that knows the removed set `E` in advance, the paper's narrative could frame this result more accurately. Being competitive against a clairvoyant baseline is a strong positive result in itself.
    *   The use of the dominating set objective (`f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|`) as a proxy for influence maximization is a limitation. This objective is computationally simpler but does not capture the stochastic diffusion dynamics of the canonical influence maximization problem defined by Kempe et al. [3]. It is therefore unclear how the algorithm would perform on the standard, more challenging influence maximization task.

2.  **Hyperparameter `w`:** The theory requires the memory parameter `w` to scale with `m` and `log k`, but the experiments show that `w=1` works well in practice. While this is a positive practical finding, the paper would be stronger if it provided some intuition or discussion for this discrepancy between theory and practice.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Could the authors provide more discussion on the comparison with the SIEVE-STREAMING baseline? Emphasizing that STAR-T operates without knowledge of `E` while the baseline does, and still achieves comparable performance, would strengthen the paper's claims.
*   The authors should acknowledge the limitation of using the dominating set objective function and perhaps suggest that evaluation on standard influence models is an avenue for future work.
*   Given the concurrent work by Mirzasoleiman et al. [18], a brief discussion of the potential practical trade-offs between their method (linear memory in `k`, better constant factor) and STAR-T (logarithmic memory in `k`, worse constant factor) would be valuable for practitioners. An empirical comparison, while perhaps beyond the scope of a revision, would be a very interesting follow-up.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel and important problem, provides the first streaming algorithm for it, and supports the algorithm with a solid constant-factor approximation guarantee. The technical contributions are significant.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The experimental results, while positive, do not show a decisive performance win over a strong baseline and use a simplified proxy for one of the key application tasks.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and strong theoretical foundation, outweigh its weaknesses. It makes a valuable contribution to the literature on large-scale submodular optimization. I recommend acceptance.
