## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper proposes a novel two-stage algorithm, STAR-T, for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint in a streaming setting. The key innovation lies in a partitioned thresholding approach used in the streaming stage, followed by a simple greedy stage to produce the final solution. The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing a constant approximation factor even as the cardinality constraint *k* grows large, and demonstrate competitive performance on influence maximization and movie recommendation tasks. 

This work addresses an important and increasingly relevant problem. The combination of streaming data, robustness requirements (due to potential data loss or user preferences), and the power of submodular optimization makes this a valuable contribution. The problem setting is well-motivated and clearly defined, and the algorithm is presented in a logical manner.  The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, with lemmas supporting the main theorem and providing insight into the algorithm’s behavior. The inclusion of Theorem 4.5, addressing the practical challenge of estimating the threshold parameter, is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling experimental results. While the experiments demonstrate competitive performance, STAR-T-Greedy does *not* consistently outperform the Sieve-Streaming baseline, and the improvements, where observed, are not substantial. This weakens the claim of a significant advance.  Furthermore, the experimental setup for influence maximization utilizes an objective function (|N(Z) ∪ Z|) that deviates from the standard Kempe et al. spread model. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results; performance on this modified objective doesn't necessarily translate to performance on the widely-used diffusion model.  The comparisons to random selection are useful, but don’t offer sufficient contrast to justify the algorithmic complexity. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization is a significant step forward.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant approximation guarantee, even for large *k*, is a strong theoretical result.
* **Practical Considerations:** Addressing the parameter estimation (Theorem 4.5) enhances the usability of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Validation:**  The experiments lack clear, consistent improvements over strong baselines.
* **Questionable Objective Function:** The modified influence maximization objective limits the applicability of those results.
* **Clarity:** Some sections, especially regarding the proofs, could benefit from more detailed explanations.



**Arguments for Acceptance:** The novelty of the approach and the theoretical guarantees justify publication, despite the relatively weak empirical evidence. This work opens up a new research direction and provides a strong foundation for future investigations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The lack of a significant empirical advantage over existing methods and the use of a non-standard objective function raise concerns about the practical impact of the work.

**Overall Assessment:** I lean towards **weak accept**. The theoretical contribution is strong, but the authors should be encouraged to conduct more comprehensive experiments using standard evaluation metrics and datasets, and to provide a more convincing demonstration of the practical benefits of their algorithm.
