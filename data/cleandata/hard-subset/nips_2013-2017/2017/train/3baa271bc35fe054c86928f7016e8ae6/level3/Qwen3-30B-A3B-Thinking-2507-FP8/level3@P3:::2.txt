

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper proposes a two-stage algorithm for robust submodular maximization under cardinality constraints in the streaming setting. The authors introduce STAR-T, a streaming algorithm that constructs a robust summary of size O((k + m log k) log² k), followed by a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) that operates on the remaining elements after any m elements are removed from the summary. The key innovation is a partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds that enables the algorithm to tolerate element removals while maintaining a constant-factor approximation guarantee.

The paper builds upon existing work in submodular maximization, particularly the SIEVE-STREAMING algorithm [8], and extends it to the robust setting. The authors relate their work to prior robust submodular maximization results [9, 10, 11], noting that their approach differs in its use of partitioning with exponentially decreasing thresholds rather than greedy bucket construction. The paper also acknowledges concurrent work [18] but highlights that their memory complexity (O((k + m log k) log k) vs. O(mk log k/ε)) is superior for large k.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that STAR-T-GREEDY achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee (c ≥ 0.149) as k → ∞. Theorem 4.1 and supporting lemmas (4.2-4.4) demonstrate that the algorithm can tolerate any m element removals from the summary while maintaining this guarantee. The proof techniques are novel, particularly the partitioning structure and the analysis of the decreasing threshold rule.

2. **Novelty:** The paper presents the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, addressing a gap in the literature. The partitioned thresholding approach is a significant contribution to the field of streaming submodular optimization.

3. **Practical Implementation:** The authors provide a practical implementation of the algorithm, with Theorem 4.5 showing how to approximate the unknown parameter τ without sacrificing the approximation guarantee. The experiments suggest that the hyperparameter w = 1 works well in practice, which is convenient for users.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Experimental Limitations:** The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks sufficient depth to convincingly demonstrate the advantages of STAR-T-GREEDY over SIEVE-STREAMING. While the paper claims STAR-T-GREEDY matches or outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, the results are not consistent across different scenarios (Figures 2(b,d) show SIEVE-STREAMING slightly outperforming in some cases). The experiments are limited to two applications (dominating set and movie recommendation) without sufficient statistical analysis or comparison to other robust algorithms.

2. **Questionable Experiment Setup:** The influence maximization experiments use an objective function f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|, which differs from the classical Kempe et al. spread model [3]. This makes it unclear whether the algorithm would perform well on the standard influence maximization problem, which is a major application area for submodular maximization.

3. **Limited Practical Validation:** The paper does not provide sufficient evidence of the algorithm's superiority in real-world scenarios. The experiments show that STAR-T-GREEDY performs similarly to SIEVE-STREAMING (which knows in advance which elements will be removed), but does not demonstrate cases where STAR-T-GREEDY clearly outperforms existing methods.

**Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance:**

- **Quality:** The theoretical results are sound and well-proven, though the experimental validation is weaker than the theoretical contribution. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of the approach.

- **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

- **Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization. The partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is novel and well-motivated.

- **Significance:** The problem addressed is important for real-world applications requiring robust data summarization. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of clear advantages over existing methods in experiments, and the questionable experiment setup for influence maximization.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical contribution to robust submodular maximization
- First streaming algorithm for this problem with a constant-factor approximation guarantee
- Well-structured theoretical analysis with proper proofs
- Practical implementation with useful hyperparameter suggestions

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Experiments do not consistently demonstrate superiority over existing methods
- Questionable experiment setup for influence maximization (uses non-standard objective)
- Limited scope of experimental validation
- Lack of clear demonstration of practical advantages in real-world scenarios

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances the field of streaming submodular optimization. While the experimental results do not fully demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed method, the theoretical results are compelling. Given the importance of the problem and the novelty of the approach, I recommend acceptance, with the understanding that the experimental section should be strengthened to better support the claims in future revisions.
