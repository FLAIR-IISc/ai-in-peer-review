This paper introduces a novel algorithm, STAR-T, for robust streaming submodular maximization. The problem considers maximizing a monotone submodular function subject to a cardinality constraint, with the added challenge of robustness to the removal of *m* elements from the selected set. The motivation stems from applications where selected items might become unavailable or undesirable, such as movie recommendations or influence maximization. The core contribution lies in a two-stage procedure: a streaming phase to construct a robust summary, and a query phase to select a near-optimal subset after potential removals.

The work builds upon existing research in submodular maximization, notably the greedy algorithm [Nemhauser et al., 1978], streaming algorithms like SIEVE-STREAMING [Badanidiyuru et al., 2014], and robust submodular maximization [Krause et al., 2008; Orlin et al., 2016; Bogunovic et al., 2017]. The authors position their work as extending SIEVE-STREAMING to the robust setting, introducing a partitioned thresholding scheme to achieve robustness.  Related work on interactive and adaptive submodular optimization [Golovin & Krause, 2011; Guillory & Bilmes, 2010] is also acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The algorithm and its analysis appear technically correct. The use of partitioning and exponentially decreasing thresholds is a clever approach to address the robustness requirement. The proofs, while detailed (and relegated to the supplementary material), seem rigorous.
* **Non-trivial Problem & Solution:** The problem of robust streaming submodular maximization is indeed challenging, and the proposed solution is not straightforward. The constant-factor approximation guarantee is a significant achievement.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper is well-organized, and the algorithm is clearly presented. The notation is consistent.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both the dominating set problem and personalized movie recommendations provide evidence that the algorithm performs well in practice, often matching or exceeding the performance of SIEVE-STREAMING, even with the latter having foreknowledge of removals.
* **Parallel Algorithm Extension:** The inclusion of Theorem 4.5, offering a parallel variant to approximate the unknown optimal value, demonstrates thoughtfulness and expands the applicability of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Weak Motivation & Application Examples:** While the idea of robustness is valuable, the provided applications (movie recommendations, influence maximization) feel somewhat contrived. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate *why* robustness is crucial in these specific scenarios beyond a general statement.  A more compelling motivation would strengthen the contribution.
* **Problem Formulation Generality:** The problem formulation, while well-defined, is somewhat restrictive.  The focus on removing elements from the *selected set* (S) rather than from the entire universe (V) feels limiting. Many real-world scenarios involve removals from the entire ground set.
* **Conference Relevance (Questionable):** NIPS has become increasingly focused on deep learning and related areas. While this paper presents a solid theoretical result, its connection to the core interests of the NIPS audience is not immediately apparent. The problem setting doesn't naturally align with current trends in the conference.
* **Space Complexity:** The space complexity of O((k + m log k) log k) is reasonable, but the dependence on both *k* and *m* could be a limitation for very large datasets. Although the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of potential strategies to mitigate the space requirement would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel algorithm with a theoretical guarantee.
* Technically sound and well-analyzed.
* Empirical validation supports the claims.
* Introduces a new approach to robust submodular maximization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Weak motivation and limited applicability of the problem formulation.
* Questionable relevance to the core interests of the NIPS audience.
* The space complexity might be a concern for very large datasets.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid technical contribution to the area of submodular optimization. The algorithm is well-designed, and the analysis is rigorous. However, the relatively weak motivation, restrictive problem formulation, and questionable relevance to NIPS raise concerns.  While the paper is not *incorrect* or *poorly executed*, its impact on the broader machine learning community is uncertain.

**Recommendation:**

I lean towards **reject**. While the technical quality is high, the limited motivation and questionable fit with the NIPS scope outweigh the strengths. The authors could potentially strengthen the paper by providing more compelling applications, broadening the problem formulation, or demonstrating a stronger connection to current research trends in the field.
