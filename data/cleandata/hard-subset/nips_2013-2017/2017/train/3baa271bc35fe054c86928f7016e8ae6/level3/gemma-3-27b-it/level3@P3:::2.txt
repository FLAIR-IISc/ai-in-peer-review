This paper proposes a two-stage streaming algorithm, STAR-T, for robust submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint. The core idea is to construct a robust summary using a partitioned thresholding approach, followed by a simple greedy stage on the remaining elements after potential removals. This tackles a challenging problem – maximizing a submodular function with guarantees even when elements are lost from the chosen set – relevant to numerous applications like influence maximization and recommendation systems. 

The work builds upon a line of research on submodular maximization, notably extending streaming algorithms like Sieve-Streaming to the robust setting. It addresses a gap by being the first streaming algorithm explicitly designed for *robust* submodular maximization, and the authors correctly position it within the context of related work on robustness (e.g., [9, 10, 11]) and streaming algorithms ([5, 8, 12, 13]). The theoretical contribution, Theorem 4.1, providing a constant approximation guarantee even as *k* approaches infinity, is noteworthy. The analysis, leveraging Lemmas 4.2-4.3 to demonstrate tolerance to element loss, appears sound, and the provision of a method to approximate the threshold parameter *τ* (Thm 4.5) is practical. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The primary strength is tackling a previously unaddressed problem: streaming robust submodular maximization.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The constant-factor approximation guarantee (Theorem 4.1) provides a strong theoretical foundation.
* **Practicality:** The algorithm uses a relatively simple greedy stage and claims w=1 works well, simplifying implementation.  The ability to reuse the summary for multiple removal scenarios is valuable.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The two-stage setting is well-defined and motivates the approach.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature on submodular maximization, streaming algorithms, and robustness.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Expressiveness:** The experimental results, while demonstrating reasonable performance, are not very convincing.  The performance of STAR-T-Greedy is often comparable to, but not consistently better than, Sieve-Streaming. This is a significant concern, as it doesn’t definitively showcase the benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Questionable Experimental Setup (Influence Maximization):** The use of *f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|* as the influence maximization objective is a significant limitation.  This differs from the standard Kempe et al. spread model and casts doubt on the generalizability of the results to the more widely studied influence maximization setting. The authors acknowledge this, but it weakens the practical relevance of the findings.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the paper states w=1 works well, a more thorough sensitivity analysis of both *w* and *τ* would strengthen the results.
* **Space Complexity Discussion:** Although the theoretical space complexity is mentioned, a clearer comparison of the memory footprint of STAR-T and Sieve-Streaming in practical scenarios would be helpful.



**Quality:**  The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined problem statement and a clear theoretical analysis. However, the lack of substantial experimental improvement over existing methods raises concerns. The authors are honest about the limitations regarding the influence maximization setup.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm descriptions are clear, and the theoretical analysis is presented in a logical manner. However, some sections (e.g., the proof sketches) could benefit from more detail.

**Originality:** The core idea of a streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization is novel. The partitioned thresholding approach is a specific contribution. 

**Significance:** The problem addressed is important, and a scalable solution for robust submodular maximization has the potential to be widely applicable.  However, the limited experimental validation weakens the claim of significant advancement.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a previously unstudied and important problem.
* Provides a strong theoretical guarantee.
* Represents a novel algorithmic approach.
* Demonstrates reasonable performance in experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Experimental results are not significantly better than existing methods.
* The influence maximization experiment uses a non-standard objective function.
* Lack of comprehensive parameter sensitivity analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the field of submodular maximization. However, the experimental results do not fully justify the claims of a significant advancement. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the lack of compelling empirical evidence hinders the overall impact of the work. I recommend **weak accept** with a request for more extensive and rigorous experimentation, particularly using a standard influence maximization model and a thorough sensitivity analysis of the parameters.  Demonstrating clear advantages over existing methods is crucial to solidify the value of this contribution.
