This paper addresses the problem of monotone submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint k, uniquely combining two challenging settings: the streaming model, where elements are processed in a single pass, and a robustness requirement, where the solution quality must be preserved after an adversary removes up to m elements. This appears to be the first work to formally consider and provide guarantees for this combined streaming and robust setting.

The proposed solution is a two-stage procedure. In the first stage, the STAR-T algorithm makes a single pass over the data to create a summary set S. This algorithm is inspired by prior work on both robust submodular maximization and streaming algorithms. It uses a partitioned structure of buckets, similar to recent centralized robust algorithms, but fills these buckets using an exponentially decreasing thresholding rule, which is a hallmark of streaming methods like SIEVE-STREAMING. In the second stage, after an arbitrary set E of at most m elements is removed from the summary S, a simple greedy algorithm (STAR-T-GREEDY) is run on the remaining set S \ E to produce the final solution Z.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this procedure achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee. Specifically, the authors show that for a summary S of size O((m log k + k) log k), the solution Z satisfies f(Z) >= 0.149 * f(OPT(k, V \ E)). The paper's empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the method on a dominating set problem (as a proxy for influence maximization) and a personalized movie recommendation task. The experiments show that the proposed method performs comparably to or better than SIEVE-STREAMING, even when the latter is given the significant advantage of knowing the removed set E in advance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a novel and highly relevant problem. As datasets grow, both streaming processing and the need for robust solutions are paramount. Combining these two requirements is a natural and important next step for the field, and this paper provides the first formal algorithm and analysis for it. The problem formulation is well-motivated by practical applications like interactive recommendation systems.

2.  **Quality of Theoretical Results:** The primary strength of the paper is its main theoretical result: a constant-factor approximation guarantee in this very challenging setting. While the constant (0.149) is not large, achieving any constant factor is a significant technical accomplishment that requires a novel combination of analytical techniques from both streaming and robust optimization literature. The memory bound is also reasonable, with a logarithmic dependence on k.

3.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and compelling. Comparing STAR-T-GREEDY against SIEVE-STREAMING that knows the removed set E beforehand is a very strong baseline. The fact that the proposed method is competitive in this setting provides powerful evidence for the quality of the robust summary S it constructs. The movie recommendation experiment effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the two-stage approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity of the Analysis:** The paper claims its result requires "novelty in the algorithm design as well as the analysis," but the intuition behind the analysis is not clearly conveyed. The proof sketch in Section 4 is difficult to follow. It presents a series of lemmas that partition the analysis into cases ("at least half of its buckets are full" vs. not) but fails to provide a high-level narrative connecting them. It is not obvious how these cases cover all possibilities or why this specific case distinction is the key to the proof. A more intuitive explanation of why the partitioned thresholding structure guarantees robustness would greatly improve the paper's clarity and impact.

2.  **Scope of Experimental Claims:** The experiment on the dominating set problem uses the objective f(Z) = |N(Z) U Z|, which is the size of the union of Z and its neighborhood. While this is a standard and valid submodular function, its use as a proxy for influence maximization is a simplification. Many real-world influence maximization objectives require expensive Monte Carlo simulations to evaluate the function, which is a major bottleneck in streaming settings. The paper does not discuss how the proposed algorithm would perform when function evaluations are computationally expensive, which limits the claimed applicability to the broader influence maximization problem.

3.  **Lack of Lower Bounds:** The paper provides an upper bound on the approximation ratio but does not provide any theoretical justification for why this particular guarantee might be close to optimal. A discussion of hardness results or non-trivial lower bounds for the robust streaming problem would help contextualize the achieved 0.149-approximation and clarify how much room for improvement remains.

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces and provides the first solution for the important problem of robust streaming submodular maximization. The theoretical result is significant, and the experimental validation is convincing. The main weaknesses are the lack of clarity in the presentation of the analysis and a slight overstatement of applicability in the influence maximization experiment. These are correctable, and the core contributions are substantial. The paper is a valuable addition to the literature on large-scale submodular optimization.
