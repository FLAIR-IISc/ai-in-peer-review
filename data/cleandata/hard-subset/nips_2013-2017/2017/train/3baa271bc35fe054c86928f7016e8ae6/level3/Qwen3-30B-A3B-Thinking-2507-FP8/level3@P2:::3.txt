

# Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting, where elements arrive sequentially, and the solution must remain effective even after up to m elements are removed. The authors propose STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm that first constructs a robust summary in one pass over the data (using O((m log k + k) log^2 k) elements) and then provides a constant-factor approximation guarantee after any m elements are removed from this summary. 

The problem is well-motivated by real-world applications such as data summarization, influence maximization, and personalized recommendation systems, where robustness to element removal is crucial. Previous work has considered streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING) or robust submodular maximization (e.g., PRO), but this paper is the first to address both aspects simultaneously. The authors' key innovation is a partitioned thresholding approach where the summary is organized into partitions with exponentially decreasing thresholds, differing significantly from prior robust submodular work that fills buckets greedily one after another.

The theoretical contribution is strong: the authors prove a 0.149 approximation guarantee (approaching this value as k increases), which is constant regardless of m (as long as m doesn't grow too fast with k). This represents a significant theoretical advance for this combined setting. The experimental results are promising, showing that STAR-T performs comparably to or better than SIEVE-STREAMING (which is allowed to know the removed elements in advance) in two data summarization tasks: dominating set problems on social networks and personalized movie recommendation.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. The problem formulation is novel and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature.
2. The algorithm achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee for a challenging combined setting.
3. The experimental results demonstrate practical utility, especially in showing that a single robust summary can handle multiple different removal scenarios.
4. The paper provides a clear connection between theory and practice with well-designed experiments.

Weaknesses include:
1. The paper lacks non-trivial lower bounds to complement the upper-bound guarantee, making it difficult to assess the tightness of the approximation ratio.
2. The analysis and writing are unclear in several places. The claimed "innovative analysis" is not sufficiently explained, and the proof sketch is difficult to follow. Key concepts like the partitioning structure and thresholding rule are not adequately motivated.
3. The influence maximization experiments use a simplified neighborhood-size objective (f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|) rather than traditional network-dependent influence objectives. This raises questions about the applicability of the results to standard influence maximization problems.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS, building on SIEVE-STREAMING (Badanidiyuru et al., 2014) and robust submodular maximization (Krause et al., 2008; Bogunovic et al., 2017), but makes a significant step by combining these two aspects. The authors correctly reference these prior works and clarify how their approach differs.

Regarding the four NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The paper presents a technically sound algorithm with a rigorous theoretical guarantee, but the analysis could be clearer. The experiments are well-designed and support the theoretical claims.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, but the theoretical explanation of the innovative analysis is unclear and difficult to follow.
- Originality: The joint consideration of streaming and robustness is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field.
- Significance: The problem is important for large-scale applications, and the algorithm provides a practical solution to a challenging problem, with demonstrated performance in real-world applications.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Novel problem formulation addressing a meaningful gap in the literature.
- Theoretical guarantee of 0.149 approximation is a significant contribution to the field.
- Strong experimental results showing practical utility, even outperforming baselines that know removals in advance.

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of lower bounds makes it difficult to assess the tightness of the approximation ratio.
- The analysis is not clearly explained, hindering verification of the theoretical claims.
- The influence maximization experiments use a simplified objective that may not reflect standard influence spread problems.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution to the field of submodular optimization. While the theoretical analysis could be clearer and the paper could benefit from lower bounds, the core contribution is significant and well-supported by experiments. The authors have made an important step toward solving the challenging problem of robust submodular maximization in the streaming setting. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors should clarify their analysis and provide more thorough justification for their experimental choices.
