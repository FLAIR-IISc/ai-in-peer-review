- Addresses robust streaming submodular maximization: selecting a representative subset that remains useful after removals.
- Proposes STAR-T algorithm that constructs a robust summary and, with greedy on remaining elements, achieves a constant-factor approximation.
- STAR-T uses a partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds to maintain summaries efficiently.
- Provides a rigorous analysis and proof sketch establishing the constant-factor approximation — technically sound.
- Novelty lies in the partitioning structure and thresholding rule applied to robust streaming settings.
- Theoretical guarantee under streaming and robustness constraints is a significant contribution.
- Problem formulation is narrow and assumes the robustness parameter m is known upfront, limiting generalizability.
- Related work is not well positioned; prior methods (e.g., [10,11]) achieve similar or better approximations with different tradeoffs.
- Application examples (influence maximization, movie recommendation) feel contrived and the practical motivation is weak.
- Experimental evaluation is limited, focusing on small or synthetic datasets rather than large real-world data.
- Experimental comparisons may be unfair (SIEVE-STREAMING was given knowledge of removed elements).
- Relevance to NIPS is questionable given the topic’s prevalence and perceived limited impact.
- Reviewer leans toward reject, recommending submission to a more specialized streaming/submodular venue.
- Suggested improvements: broaden problem applicability, strengthen motivation with compelling examples, and run comprehensive real-world experiments.
- Arguments for acceptance: technically sound, novel algorithm, and meaningful theoretical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow formulation, limited experiments, weak motivation, and questionable fit for NIPS.
