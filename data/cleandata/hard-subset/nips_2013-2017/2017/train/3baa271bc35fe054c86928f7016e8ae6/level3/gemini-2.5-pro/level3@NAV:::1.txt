This paper addresses the problem of streaming robust submodular maximization. The goal is to design a single-pass streaming algorithm that constructs a small summary set `T` from a large ground set `E`, such that even after an adversary removes any `m` elements from `T`, one can still select a `k`-element subset with a provably high objective value. This work builds upon the foundations of streaming submodular maximization (e.g., Badanidiyuru et al., 2014) and non-streaming robust optimization (e.g., Orlin et al., 2016), introducing a novel two-stage approach. The proposed STAR-T algorithm creates the summary using a partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds, and a subsequent greedy selection (STAR-T-GREEDY) finds the solution from the robust summary.

The primary strength of this work lies in its technical quality and originality. The problem is technically challenging, and the authors provide a non-trivial constant-factor approximation algorithm, a significant theoretical achievement. The algorithm's design and the corresponding analysis are novel, successfully merging ideas from streaming and robust optimization to achieve a new result. The size of the summary, `O((m log k + k) log^2 k)`, while large, has a favorable logarithmic dependence on `k`, which compares well with concurrent work.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The motivation and significance are not convincingly established. While robustness is an important practical consideration, the application examples given, such as movie recommendations, feel contrived and do not adequately justify the need for this specific and complex formulation. A more critical weakness is the problem formulation itself, which considers `m` deletions from the algorithm’s summary `T` rather than the ground set `E`. A more general and practical setting would involve resilience to deletions from the underlying data, which could then be reflected in the summary. This choice limits the direct applicability and impact of the results.

Furthermore, the paper's relevance to the NeurIPS community is questionable. The contribution is almost entirely theoretical and algorithmic, with limited connection to mainstream machine learning applications or methodologies. The work might be a stronger fit for a dedicated theoretical computer science conference.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality, technically sound, and original theoretical result for a challenging optimization problem. However, its significance is hampered by a narrowly defined problem formulation and weak motivation, which may limit its influence on practitioners and researchers in the broader machine learning community.
