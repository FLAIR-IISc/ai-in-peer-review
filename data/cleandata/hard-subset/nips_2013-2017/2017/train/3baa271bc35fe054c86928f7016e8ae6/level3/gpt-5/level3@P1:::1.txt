This paper addresses robust submodular maximization in the streaming model: given a monotone submodular function with a cardinality constraint k, elements arrive once in a stream, and after the stream, up to m elements may be removed before we must return a size-k solution. The authors propose a two-stage approach: (i) a one-pass streaming algorithm, STAR-T, that builds a compact, deletion-robust summary S using a partition of buckets whose sizes grow exponentially and whose acceptance thresholds decrease exponentially; and (ii) a query-stage procedure, STAR-T-GREEDY, that runs GREEDY on S \ E for any removal set E of size at most m. The main theoretical result is a constant-factor approximation: with summary size O((k + m log k) log^2 k), the returned solution achieves at least 0.149(1 − 1/log k) of OPT(k, V \ E) asymptotically. As in SIEVE-STREAMING [8], the method requires a threshold τ approximating OPT; the authors remove this assumption via parallel instances over a geometric grid, increasing memory by a log1+ k factor. Empirically, on dominating set and personalized movie recommendation, STAR-T-GREEDY matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING—even when SIEVE-STREAMING is allowed to know E in advance.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on classical GREEDY guarantees for monotone submodular maximization [7], the streaming SIEVE-STREAMING framework [8], and recent robust (non-streaming) approaches such as Orlin et al. [10] and PRO [11] that use non-uniform/bucketed constructions. It also relates to recent robust streaming work concurrent/independent to this submission [18], which attains a 1/2 − ε approximation but uses O(mk log k/ε) memory; the present work trades a smaller constant-factor guarantee for dramatically improved memory, with only logarithmic dependence on k in the m term. The empirical studies align with common submodular summarization tasks (dominating set, facility location based recommendations) seen previously at NIPS/NeurIPS (e.g., [1,4,8,13]).

Strengths:
- Technical novelty: The partitioned structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds is a clever synthesis of robust bucketed constructions (e.g., [11]) and streaming thresholding ideas (e.g., [8]). The two-case analysis (many full buckets vs. many non-full buckets) is nontrivial and carefully executed.
- Provable robustness: After any removal of up to m items, GREEDY on the residual summary retains a constant-factor approximation. The memory bound O((k + m log k) log^2 k) and one-pass processing are appealing for large-scale settings.
- Practical relevance: The query stage is fast and supports multiple distinct removals, making the approach suitable for interactive settings (e.g., “right-to-be-forgotten” or user-specific exclusions).
- Empirical validation: On two tasks, the algorithm is competitive with strong streaming and offline baselines and appears robust even when |E| > k in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Significance and scope: The contribution is focused on monotone submodular maximization under a single cardinality constraint. It does not address more general constraints (matroids, knapsacks) or non-monotone objectives, limiting generality. Robustness is defined only for a single post-stream deletion event up to m; streaming deletions during the pass or sliding-window models are not considered.
- Approximation factor: The proven constant 0.149 (as k → ∞) is modest. While the memory/one-pass advantages are real, the gap to 1/2 (e.g., [18]) may matter for some applications. A discussion of whether the factor is likely improvable under the same memory model would help.
- Dependence on τ and extra space: As in [8], the need to run parallel instances to approximate OPT adds a log1+ k factor in space. Together with the log^2 k factor from the partitioning, this can be substantial. A more explicit accounting of per-element processing time (scanning O(w log^2 k) buckets) and total space in practical regimes would improve clarity.
- Empirical evaluation could be stronger: Baselines do not include robust (non-streaming) algorithms such as PRO [11] or the concurrent streaming deletion-robust method [18]. Ablations on the memory parameter w, varying m (including adversarial vs. random removals), and sensitivity to τ-misspecification would strengthen the case. The motivating applications are discussed but could be deepened; currently, the narrative motivation feels thin relative to the technical content.
- Clarity: While overall clear, some parts would benefit from additional exposition: (i) intuition for the threshold schedule and why balancing Lemmas 4.3/4.4 yields the final constant; (ii) a more precise pseudo-code complexity analysis; (iii) guidance on choosing w beyond the asymptotic bound, and practical heuristics for τ.

Quality: The algorithm and analysis appear sound, with proofs for the main claims and careful handling of the two structural cases. The experimental methodology is reasonable and intentionally adversarial to the proposed method (removals sampled from S), which is commendable.

Clarity: Generally well-organized and readable. Minor suggestions above could further improve accessibility.

Originality: The partitioned thresholding design and its robust streaming analysis are novel, clearly differentiating from SIEVE-STREAMING [8] and from offline robust methods [10,11].

Significance: Within the niche of streaming robust submodular maximization, this is a meaningful advance, especially in memory efficiency versus [18]. However, limited generality and a modest constant may narrow impact.

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- Report per-element processing time and wall-clock comparisons versus SIEVE-STREAMING, and ablations on w and τ. How sensitive is performance to under/over-estimating τ?
- Can the analysis extend to other constraints (e.g., partition matroids) or to non-monotone functions?
- Can a two-pass variant improve the approximation constant or reduce space?
- Compare empirically to PRO [11] (offline robust) and to [18] if code is available, at least on smaller instances.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and technically solid algorithm with provable robust guarantees in the streaming setting.
- Strong memory-approximation trade-off; compact, reusable summaries for multiple deletion queries.
- Competitive empirical performance on representative tasks.

Cons for acceptance:
- Narrow problem formulation (monotone, cardinality) and modest approximation constant.
- Additional space/time overhead from parallel thresholds and many buckets; limited practical guidance.
- Empirical section could include stronger baselines and ablations; motivation/applications could be more compelling.
- Relevance to the conference may feel borderline given the theory/algorithms emphasis and limited ML modeling novelty, though prior related work has appeared at NIPS.

Overall: A solid theoretical contribution to streaming robust submodular maximization with a distinctive design and useful empirical indications. If the program prioritizes principled, scalable optimization methods for data summarization, this is within scope; otherwise the limited generality and modest constant may be viewed as constraints.
