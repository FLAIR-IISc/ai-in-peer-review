## Review of "Streaming Robust Submodular Maximization: A Partitioned Thresholding Approach"

This paper introduces a novel two-stage algorithm, STAR-T, for robust submodular maximization in a streaming setting with a cardinality constraint. The core idea is a partitioned thresholding approach, where the stream is processed to create a robust summary, followed by a greedy stage to select the final subset after potential element removal. The authors provide theoretical guarantees, demonstrating a constant-factor approximation even as k approaches infinity, and validate their approach through experiments on influence maximization and personalized movie recommendation.

The paper addresses a significant problem – scaling submodular maximization to large, dynamic datasets while ensuring robustness to data loss. The proposed approach is a valuable contribution as it’s the first streaming algorithm tackling this specific robust maximization problem, opening avenues for applications in areas like dynamic network analysis and real-time recommendation systems. The theoretical analysis appears sound, with clear lemmas supporting the main theorem. The choice of hyperparameters (τ and w) is also practical, with a method provided for approximating τ and empirical evidence suggesting w=1 performs well.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling experimental results. While the experiments demonstrate competitive performance, STAR-T-Greedy doesn’t consistently outperform Sieve-Streaming, diminishing the practical impact of the proposed method. More importantly, the experimental setup for influence maximization deviates from the standard Kempe et al. spread model, using f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z|. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results to more realistic influence maximization scenarios. The experiments feel somewhat limited in scope and fail to convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods. 

Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between the parameters τ and w, and their impact on performance and memory usage. While w=1 works well empirically, a more thorough analysis would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: First streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization.
*   Theoretical Soundness: Constant-factor approximation guarantee.
*   Potential Significance: Broad applicability to dynamic data scenarios.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited Experimental Validation: Performance not consistently superior to baselines.
*   Questionable Experimental Setup: Influence maximization objective differs from standard model.
*   Lack of Detailed Parameter Analysis: Limited discussion of τ and w trade-offs.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to a challenging problem. However, the limited and somewhat questionable experimental results weaken its impact. I recommend **weak accept** with a strong encouragement to the authors to address the experimental concerns and provide a more comprehensive analysis of the algorithm’s performance in realistic settings. A clarification of the influence maximization setup and additional experiments using the standard Kempe et al. model are crucial.
