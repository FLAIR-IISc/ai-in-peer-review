

This paper proposes a novel two-stage streaming algorithm, STAR-T, for robust submodular maximization under cardinality constraints. The authors address the problem where elements arrive in a stream and up to m elements may be removed from the final summary. STAR-T uses a partitioning structure with exponentially decreasing thresholds to create a robust summary, followed by a simple greedy stage that achieves a constant-factor approximation guarantee. The theoretical contributions are strong, with Theorem 4.1 establishing a constant approximation ratio as k→∞, and Lemmas 4.2-4.3 demonstrating robustness to element removals.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by being the first streaming algorithm for robust submodular maximization, which is an important problem with applications in influence maximization, recommender systems, and data summarization. The partitioning structure and decreasing thresholding rule represent a novel approach that extends Sieve-Streaming to the robust setting. The algorithm's practical implementation is well-justified, with Theorem 4.5 providing a method to approximate the required parameter τ, and experiments suggesting w=1 works well in practice.

However, the experimental validation is a major weakness. The influence maximization experiments use a non-standard objective function (f(Z)=|N(Z)∪Z|) rather than the classical Kempe diffusion model, making it unclear how the algorithm performs on the actual diffusion problem. The results show STAR-T-Greedy does not consistently outperform Sieve-Streaming, which undermines the claimed practical advantages. The experiments lack depth and fail to demonstrate clear superiority over existing methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with comprehensive theoretical proofs. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical significance is weakened by the limited experimental evidence. The work would benefit from additional experiments using standard influence maximization models and a more thorough comparison with existing methods. Overall, this is a technically sound paper with novel theoretical contributions, but the experimental results fail to convincingly demonstrate its practical advantages, which is critical for a conference like NIPS where both theoretical and empirical contributions are valued. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the experimental limitations and providing a clearer demonstration of the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
