This paper proposes a two-stage algorithm, STAR-T, for robust streaming submodular maximization with a cardinality constraint. The first stage efficiently constructs a summary of the data stream using a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholding. The second stage employs a simple greedy algorithm on this summary, guaranteeing a constant-factor approximation even after removing a specified number of elements. This work addresses a challenging problem with potential applications in diverse areas like data summarization, influence maximization, and recommender systems.

The problem setting is well-motivated, building upon the existing literature on submodular maximization and extending it to the robust streaming scenario. The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional greedy methods for large datasets and the need for robustness against element removal. The paper clearly relates its contribution to prior work, citing relevant papers on submodular optimization ([7]), streaming algorithms ([8, 12, 13]), and robust submodular maximization ([9, 10, 11]). The connection to SIEVE-STREAMING ([8]) is particularly relevant, as the experiments directly compare the performance of the proposed algorithm against it.

The core idea of partitioning the summary space and using decreasing thresholds is novel and intuitively appealing. The theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 4.1, is a significant contribution, providing a formal guarantee on the approximation ratio. The inclusion of Theorem 4.5, addressing the challenge of estimating the threshold parameter without prior knowledge of the optimal solution, further strengthens the practical applicability of the algorithm.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the theoretical guarantees are appealing, the constant factor achieved (0.149 as k approaches infinity) is relatively small, raising questions about the practical performance compared to other methods, especially for moderate values of k. The experiments, while demonstrating competitive performance, do not consistently show a clear advantage for STAR-T-GREEDY over SIEVE-STREAMING. In some cases, SIEVE-STREAMING slightly outperforms the proposed algorithm. The experiments are also somewhat limited in scope; exploring different datasets and application scenarios would enhance the robustness of the conclusions.

Furthermore, the choice of the objective function for the dominating set problem (f(Z) = |N(Z) ∪ Z|) deviates from the standard influence maximization model proposed by Kempe et al. ([3]). While this choice is acknowledged, it limits the direct applicability of the results to the classic influence maximization setting. It is unclear how well STAR-T would perform on more realistic diffusion models.

The clarity of the presentation is generally good, but some sections, particularly the theoretical analysis, could benefit from more detailed explanations and intuitive justifications. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm descriptions are clear. However, a more thorough discussion of the hyperparameters (τ and w) and their impact on performance would be helpful.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel approach to robust streaming submodular maximization.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees with a constant-factor approximation.
*   Clear problem statement and motivation.
*   Well-defined algorithm with a practical second stage (greedy algorithm).
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Small constant factor in the approximation guarantee.
*   Experiments do not consistently demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Deviations from the standard influence maximization model in the dominating set experiment.
*   Theoretical analysis could benefit from more intuitive explanations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound algorithm for a challenging problem.
*   The approach has the potential to be applicable to a wide range of applications.
*   The inclusion of Theorem 4.5 enhances the practicality of the algorithm.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The small constant factor in the approximation guarantee and the lack of consistent performance improvements in the experiments raise concerns about the practical utility of the algorithm.
*   The limited scope of the experimental evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of submodular optimization. While the practical performance may not be dramatically better than existing methods in all scenarios, the theoretical novelty and the potential for future improvements make it worthy of acceptance. I recommend **weak accept** with a request for the authors to address the limitations of the experimental evaluation and provide further insights into the impact of the hyperparameters on performance. Specifically, I suggest including additional experiments on standard influence maximization datasets and exploring a wider range of parameter settings. A more detailed discussion of the tradeoffs between the theoretical guarantees and the practical performance would also be beneficial.
