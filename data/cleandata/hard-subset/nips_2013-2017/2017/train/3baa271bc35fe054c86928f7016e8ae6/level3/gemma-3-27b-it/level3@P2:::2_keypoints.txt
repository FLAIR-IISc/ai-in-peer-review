- Proposes STAR-T, a two-stage algorithm for robust streaming submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint.  
- Stage 1 builds a compact summary using a novel partitioning structure and exponentially decreasing thresholds.  
- Stage 2 runs a simple greedy algorithm on the summary, guaranteeing a constant-factor approximation even after removing specified elements.  
- Problem setting is well-motivated and extends prior work on submodular maximization to the robust streaming scenario.  
- Properly situates contribution relative to prior work (including SIEVE-STREAMING) and compares experimentally.  
- The partitioning + decreasing-threshold idea is novel and intuitively appealing.  
- Theoretical analysis (Theorem 4.1) provides a formal constant-factor approximation guarantee.  
- Theorem 4.5 addresses practical threshold estimation without prior knowledge of OPT.  
- The asymptotic constant factor (≈0.149 as k → ∞) is small, raising practical-performance concerns.  
- Experimental results are competitive but do not consistently outperform SIEVE-STREAMING; sometimes SIEVE-STREAMING performs slightly better.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope; more datasets and application scenarios are needed.  
- Dominating set experiment uses f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z|, deviating from Kempe et al.’s influence model, limiting applicability to classical diffusion settings.  
- Unclear how STAR-T performs on realistic diffusion/influence models.  
- Presentation is generally clear, with consistent notation and clear algorithm descriptions.  
- Theoretical sections lack some intuitive explanations and could be more accessible.  
- The role and impact of hyperparameters (τ and w) are insufficiently discussed and need empirical analysis.  
- Strengths: novel approach, solid theoretical guarantees, clear motivation, practical greedy second stage, tackles a challenging problem.  
- Weaknesses: small approximation constant, inconsistent empirical advantage, limited experiments, deviation from standard influence model, and less-intuitive theoretical exposition.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretically sound algorithm with practical potential and improved practicality via Theorem 4.5.  
- Arguments against acceptance: modest constant factor and limited/uneven experimental improvements reduce perceived practical utility.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution deserving weak accept, with requests for broader experiments, hyperparameter analysis, and deeper discussion of theory-vs-practice tradeoffs.
