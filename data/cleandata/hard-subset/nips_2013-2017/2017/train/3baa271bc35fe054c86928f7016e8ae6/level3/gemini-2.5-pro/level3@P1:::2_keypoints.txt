- Problem: maximize a monotone submodular function under cardinality k in a single-pass streaming setting, robust to adversarial removal of m elements from the ground set.  
- Goal: produce a summary S so a near-optimal size-k solution can be extracted after up to m removals.  
- Algorithm: two-stage procedure—STAR-T (streaming summary built with a partitioned bucket structure and exponentially decreasing thresholds) then STAR-T-GREEDY (GREEDY on remaining summary after removals).  
- Key idea: novel partitioned data structure assigning elements to buckets by marginal gain relative to exponentially decreasing thresholds, combining robust and streaming ideas.  
- Theoretical result: Theorem 4.1 gives a constant-factor approximation with memory O((k + m log k) log k).  
- Significance: first theoretical guarantee for robust submodular maximization in the single-pass streaming model with logarithmic dependence on k.  
- Relation to prior work: extends thresholding from SIEVE-STREAMING and is inspired by non-streaming robust algorithms (e.g., Bogunovic et al. 2017); claims better memory dependence than concurrent Mirzasoleiman et al. (2017).  
- Quality (strength): technically sound proofs (Lemmas 4.2–4.4) and a solid proof sketch balancing two cases.  
- Practicality: handles unknown OPT via parallel instances, a standard and important practical extension.  
- Experimental weakness: empirical results are not fully convincing—STAR-T-GREEDY is only comparable to, or marginally better than, the SIEVE-STREAMING baseline.  
- Specific experimental concern: in some cases (e.g., Fig 2d for larger k) SIEVE-STREAMING performs slightly better despite being a non-robust baseline with extra information.  
- Modeling concern: the dominating set objective f(Z)=|N(Z) ∪ Z| is a simplified proxy for influence maximization and may not reflect performance on realistic diffusion models (IC/LT).  
- Theory/practice gap: experiments use w=1 while theory requires w to scale with m and log k; this discrepancy should be discussed (real-world removals may be less adversarial).  
- Positive empirical note: movie recommendation experiment shows strong robustness even under drastic removals (e.g., 59% removed), which is a practical strength worth emphasizing.  
- Algorithmic question: why does SIEVE-STREAMING sometimes outperform STAR-T-GREEDY—might suggest STAR-T-SIEVE as a more natural second-stage than GREEDY.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel problem formulation and first provable streaming-robust solution, strong theoretical contribution, and high potential impact for large-scale applications.  
- Arguments against acceptance: underwhelming empirical validation and questionable experimental proxy for influence maximization.
