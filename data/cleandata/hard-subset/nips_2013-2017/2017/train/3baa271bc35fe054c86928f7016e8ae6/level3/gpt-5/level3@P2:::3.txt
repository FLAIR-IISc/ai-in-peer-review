Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies a robust variant of streaming submodular maximization under a cardinality constraint. The two-stage setting is: (i) a single-pass streaming stage that builds a compact “robust summary” S, and (ii) a query stage that, after removal of any m elements from S, returns a size-k subset with high value using only S\E. The proposed algorithm, STAR-T, maintains a partitioned collection of buckets with exponentially increasing capacities and exponentially decreasing thresholds; arriving elements are placed into the first non-full bucket whose marginal gain exceeds its partition’s threshold. In the query stage, STAR-T-GREEDY runs standard GREEDY on S\E.

Theoretical results show that with memory parameter w ≳ (m log k)/k, STAR-T stores O((k + m log k) log k) elements when given an estimate τ of OPT, and achieves a constant-factor approximation after any m deletions; using a standard multi-instance technique to guess τ inflates memory by an extra O(log k) factor, giving O((k + m log k) log2 k) storage. The proved approximation factor tends to 0.149 as k grows.

This work builds on streaming submodular maximization (e.g., SIEVE-STREAMING [8]) and robust submodular maximization in centralized settings (e.g., Orlin et al. [10] and PRO [11]). It is concurrent with [18], which gives a 1/2 − ε approximation with O(mk log k/ε) memory. Compared to [18], the present paper trades a smaller approximation factor for substantially better memory dependence in k (logarithmic vs linear).

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and practically motivated problem: deletion-robust summarization in a single pass.
- Conceptually simple algorithmic template (partitioned buckets with decreasing thresholds) with a clean two-stage interface.
- Provable constant-factor robustness guarantee with memory sublinear in mk; favorable tradeoff relative to [18] for large k.
- Clear applicability to summarization/recommendation tasks; empirical results are competitive with strong baselines that even know the deletions a priori.

Weaknesses
- The analysis is hard to follow in places, and some inconsistencies appear (e.g., threshold formula and storage bounds), making it challenging to verify details.
- No nontrivial lower bounds on the memory–approximation tradeoff are provided to contextualize the achieved constants.
- Experimental evaluation for “influence maximization” uses simple neighborhood coverage (dominating set), not standard IC/LT models; this limits claims about applicability to classical influence settings.
- Runtime/throughput considerations are underdeveloped; per-element processing can be high in worst case due to scanning many buckets.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level proof strategy (two-case analysis with balancing lemmas) is sensible and consistent with submodular techniques. However, the presentation leaves gaps: the stated threshold in Algorithm 1 (τ/min{2^i, k}) differs from the earlier τ/2^i description, and the storage bound alternates between O((k + m log k) log k) and O((k + m log k) log2 k) across sections. Please reconcile these and specify which bound applies with/without unknown-OPT guessing. A brief, unified intuition for the critical lemmas (Lemmas 4.2–4.4) would help.
- Experiments: The tasks (dominating set coverage and hybrid recommendation objective) are reasonable proxies for submodular summarization. Results show that STAR-T-GREEDY matches or beats SIEVE-STREAMING and random under both random and adversarial deletions, even when baselines know E. That said, adding results on IC/LT influence (even smaller graphs or synthetic) and reporting wall-clock time and memory usage would strengthen the empirical case.
- Algorithmic practicality: Please discuss per-element update complexity. The number of buckets across partitions is Θ(wk), so naïvely checking all could be expensive. Are there data structures or early-stopping heuristics that keep average per-element cost near O(w log k)?

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally well structured, but several points need clarification:
  - Threshold rule: reconcile τ/2^i vs τ/min{2^i, k} and explain the role of k in the denominator.
  - Storage bounds: clearly state that Theorem 4.1’s O((k + m log k) log k) applies when τ is known/guessed within constant factor, and Theorem 4.5’s parallelization adds another O(log k) factor, yielding the abstract’s O((k + m log k) log2 k).
  - Define the base of logarithms and consistently use ⌈log2 k⌉ if that is intended.
  - Provide an explicit pseudo-code or description for the parallel-τ instantiation (number of instances, τ grid, stopping/merging policy).

Originality
- Combining streaming with deletion-robustness and obtaining a constant-factor guarantee with sublinear (in mk) memory is novel and valuable. The partitioned thresholding structure is a principled adaptation of non-uniform partitioning [11] to the streaming setting. Concurrency with [18] reduces novelty somewhat, but the memory–approximation tradeoff is a distinct contribution.

Significance
- The results advance the state of the art in deletion-robust streaming summarization and are likely to influence future work on robust sketches and “right-to-be-forgotten” scenarios. While the constant 0.149 is modest, it is nontrivial in a challenging setting, and the practical results are strong.

Questions for the authors
- Can you quantify/update per-element processing time, and propose implementation techniques to avoid scanning Θ(wk) buckets?
- How sensitive is performance to w in practice? Please include an ablation varying w vs robustness m and summary size.
- Could you include results under IC/LT influence models (even small-scale) to clarify applicability beyond dominating set?
- How is τ discretized in the parallel instances? What is the resulting multiplicative overhead (in practice) vs ε?
- Can you compare against [18] under matched memory budgets and report the tradeoff empirically?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important and understudied combination (streaming + deletion-robustness).
- Solid theoretical guarantees with favorable memory scaling.
- Simple and practical two-stage design; competitive empirical results.

Cons:
- Analysis/writing clarity issues and minor inconsistencies.
- Limited experimental coverage of classical influence models; no runtime/memory plots.
- No lower bounds or deeper discussion of optimality of the memory–approximation tradeoff.

Minor comments and nits
- Unify the threshold expression in text and Algorithm 1.
- Clarify when O((k + m log k) log k) vs O((k + m log k) log2 k) applies.
- Specify logarithm base and consistently use ⌈log k⌉ notation.
- A brief discussion of constants in the guarantee and behavior for small k would be useful.
- Proof sketch: expand the intuition for “balancing” Lemmas 4.3 and 4.4 and the choice of w ensuring γ ≤ 1/⌈log k⌉.
