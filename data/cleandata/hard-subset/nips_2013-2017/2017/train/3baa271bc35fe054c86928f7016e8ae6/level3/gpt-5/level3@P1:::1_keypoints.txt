- Problem: Robust monotone submodular maximization in one-pass streaming with up to m post-stream deletions under cardinality k.
- Approach: Two-stage method — STAR-T builds a compact deletion-robust summary via exponentially growing buckets and decreasing thresholds; STAR-T-GREEDY runs GREEDY on the summary after deletions.
- Theoretical guarantee: Constant-factor approximation of ≈0.149(1 − 1/log k) to OPT with summary size O((k + m log k) log^2 k) asymptotically.
- Handling unknown OPT: Uses parallel instances over a geometric grid (as in SIEVE-STREAMING), adding a log1+ k space factor.
- Empirical results: On dominating set and recommendation tasks, STAR-T-GREEDY matches or slightly outperforms SIEVE-STREAMING, even when SIEVE-STREAMING knows the removal set.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on GREEDY, SIEVE-STREAMING, and offline robust methods (e.g., PRO), and trades approximation for much lower memory than concurrent work achieving 1/2−ε.
- Technical novelty: The partitioned, exponentially-thresholded bucket structure and two-case analysis are a novel synthesis of prior robust and streaming ideas.
- Provable robustness: After any removal of ≤ m items, GREEDY on the residual summary retains the stated constant-factor guarantee.
- Memory/time appeal: One-pass processing and compact summary size with only logarithmic dependence on k in the m term are attractive for large-scale settings.
- Practical relevance: Fast query-stage, supports multiple removals, and suits interactive scenarios like right-to-be-forgotten or user exclusions.
- Empirical robustness: Appears robust in practice, even when |E| > k, despite theoretical focus on |E| ≤ m.
- Limited scope: Focuses only on monotone objectives under a single cardinality constraint; does not address matroids, knapsacks, non-monotone, or streaming deletions/sliding windows.
- Modest approximation: Proven constant ≈0.149 is weak compared to 1/2 achievable by some other methods, which may limit applicability.
- Extra space/time costs: Parallel thresholds and many buckets add log factors; per-element processing and practical space costs need clearer accounting.
- Empirical gaps: Missing comparisons to offline robust baselines (e.g., PRO) and concurrent streaming deletion-robust methods, plus needed ablations on w, m, and τ sensitivity.
- Clarity issues: Would benefit from more intuition on threshold scheduling, detailed pseudo-code complexity, and practical guidance for choosing w and τ.
- Quality: Analysis and proofs appear sound; experimental methodology is reasonable and intentionally adversarial about removals.
- Originality: The design and robust streaming analysis are novel and distinct from SIEVE-STREAMING and offline robust approaches.
- Significance: Meaningful within the niche of streaming robust submodular maximization due to memory-approximation trade-offs, but limited generality and modest constant may narrow impact.
- Recommendations/questions: Report per-element processing time, wall-clock comparisons, ablations on w and τ, consider extensions to other constraints/non-monotone functions, and compare to PRO and concurrent work if possible.
- Pros for acceptance: Novel, technically solid, provable streaming robustness with compact reusable summaries and competitive empirical performance.
- Cons for acceptance: Narrow problem scope, modest approximation, extra space/time overheads, weaker empirical baselines and motivation, and potentially borderline ML relevance.
