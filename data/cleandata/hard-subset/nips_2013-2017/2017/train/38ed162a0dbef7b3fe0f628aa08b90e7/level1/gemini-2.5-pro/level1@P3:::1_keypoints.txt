- The paper provides a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations, focusing on stability to small deformations and invariance to group transformations via a multilayer convolutional kernel in RKHSs.  
- It constructs a sequence of feature maps by repeated patch extraction, kernel mapping, and linear pooling.  
- The work generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks to continuous signal domains and adopts Mallat’s scattering-transform formalism.  
- Main contribution: extends Mallat’s stability analysis from fixed wavelet filters to learnable architectures; Theorem 6 gives a Lipschitz-like bound w.r.t. C1-diffeomorphisms.  
- Near-translation invariance is controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer.  
- Shows that a class of CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activations can be embedded in the RKHS and thus inherit the stability/invariance properties.  
- Strength: high-quality, significant theoretical contribution that formalizes why deep convolutional architectures build robustness to small deformations.  
- Strength: original unification bridging fixed-filter (scattering) and learnable models, linking RKHS norm, stability, and generalization (margin bounds).  
- Strength: clear, mathematically precise exposition with a step-by-step stability proof and an elegant extension to general group actions.  
- Weakness: gap to practice—requires smooth activations and a non-standard normalization term nk(u)=||Pk xk−1(u)||, whose practical role and relation to BatchNorm are not discussed.  
- Weakness: entirely theoretical with no empirical validation to illustrate the bounds or practical magnitude of stability constants.  
- Weakness: representation does not preserve the L2 norm due to pooling; potential cumulative information loss is under-discussed and depends on an external preprint.  
- Arguments for acceptance: provides a rigorous, novel framework extending scattering analysis to learnable models and deepens understanding of complexity, stability, and generalization.  
- Arguments for rejection: the analyzed CNN class deviates from standard practice (smoothed activations, normalization), and the paper lacks experiments.  
- Conclusion: reviewer highly recommends acceptance (clear accept for NIPS) despite the noted gaps between theory and practical implementations.
