Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for d-dimensional signals that generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) to continuous domains and analyzes its invariance to groups and stability to diffeomorphisms. Each layer performs patch extraction, a norm-preserving, non-expansive kernel map induced by homogeneous dot-product kernels, and linear Gaussian pooling. The representation preserves information layerwise (via recoverability from samples) while progressively increasing translation invariance. The core theoretical result (Theorem 6) gives a Lipschitz-type stability bound to C1-diffeomorphisms τ, scaling as C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn, thus translation invariance improves with the final pooling scale σn. The analysis carries over to discretized architectures with subsampling (Lemma 2) and to finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs) via non-expansive projections. Section 4 extends the construction to general locally compact groups, yielding equivariant layers and global invariance through final pooling on the group. Finally, the authors show that a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (and a local norm normalization) lie in the RKHS associated with their multilayer kernel (Proposition 7), implying stability and a link between RKHS norms, robustness, and generalization.

This work stands on the shoulders of Mallat’s scattering transform (Bruna and Mallat, 2013; Mallat, 2012), extending its deformation stability analysis beyond fixed wavelet filters to kernel-defined, learnable representations and a class of CNNs. It connects to CKNs (Mairal et al., 2014; 2016) by providing a continuous-domain, theoretically grounded construction and to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling, 2016) by defining equivariant patch and pooling operators on groups. It also relates to invariant kernels (Haasdonk & Burkhardt, 2008), orbit-based methods, and the “deep networks as kernel machines” perspective (Anselmi et al., 2015; Montavon et al., 2011).

Quality
The paper is technically solid. The stability result is carefully derived via commutator bounds, Schur’s test, and properties of Gaussian pooling. The structure reflects the scattering analysis but with nontrivial extensions: handling shifted integral operators [LcAσ, Lτ], compatibility with kernel maps that are non-expansive/isometric, and propagation through multilayers. The discretization and recoverability lemmas are reasonable, highlighting assumptions (e.g., ek ≥ sk and stable deconvolution). The CNN-in-RKHS inclusion leverages homogeneous activations and provides explicit norm recursion. The claims are appropriately scoped; the authors are clear about norm non-preservation due to pooling and how to mitigate it.

A few technical caveats remain: (i) the dependence on depth is linear via a telescoping sum; it is unclear whether this is tight or an artifact of bounding commutators layerwise. (ii) Stability assumes an initial low-pass A0 (though σ0 can be small), and ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2; (iii) constants C1, C2 are left abstract; dimension and κ dependencies could be more explicit for Gaussian h; (iv) the recoverability by deconvolution is stable only when filters are sufficiently low-pass.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written but dense. The operator-theoretic presentation is precise; assumptions (A1–A3) and their satisfaction by concrete kernels are helpful. The discretization section efficiently connects continuous and discrete parameters. Some clarity suggestions:
- Make constants explicit for Gaussian pooling (e.g., C2 in terms of σ, dimension, and ‖∇h‖1) and indicate the growth of C1 with κ and d in the main text.
- Summarize Appendix B’s kernel and activation choices in a small table with examples and whether (A1–A2) hold.
- In Section 5, emphasize more clearly that the CNN inclusion uses a normalization nk(u)=‖Pk zk−1(u)‖ and smooth homogeneous activations (smoothed ReLU, inverse polynomial), which differs from standard ReLU pipelines.
- Briefly discuss boundary effects introduced by finite support and standard padding/stride choices.

Originality
The main contributions are novel: a general RKHS-based multilayer convolutional representation with explicit stability to diffeomorphisms, a group-equivariant construction yielding global invariance, and a rigorous inclusion of a class of CNNs into the RKHS with explicit norm control. While inspired by scattering, the move from fixed wavelets to kernelized, learnable layers and the link to CNNs via RKHS norms are substantive advances. The discretization and approximation compatibility are also useful.

Significance
The results provide a unifying theoretical framework that connects invariance, stability, and generalization for convolutional architectures. They justify CKN-like design choices and suggest regularizing via RKHS norms to improve robustness. Although primarily theoretical, the implications are relevant to practitioners interested in stability/robustness and to ongoing efforts in group-equivariant and kernelized deep models. The practical impact would be strengthened by empirical validation, but the conceptual contribution is significant.

Pros
- Clear and general stability bound for multilayer convolutional kernel maps; translation invariance increases with pooling scale.
- Extension to group equivariance and global invariance via group pooling.
- Compatibility with discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs).
- Inclusion of a nontrivial class of CNNs into the RKHS with explicit norm bounds, linking stability to generalization.
- Careful, rigorous analysis; meaningful synthesis of scattering, kernels, and CNNs.

Cons
- Assumes initial low-pass and small ‖∇τ‖∞; constants and depth dependence may be pessimistic and are not quantified tightly.
- The CNN class covered requires smooth homogeneous activations and local norm normalization, diverging from standard ReLU CNNs; practical breadth unclear.
- Recoverability relies on stable deconvolution and ek ≥ sk; stability to discretization/padding/stride not fully quantified.
- No empirical experiments demonstrating invariance/stability gains or validating constants.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide explicit constants for Gaussian pooling and their dependence on d, σ, and κ; discuss tightness of the linear-in-n bound.
- Empirically validate Theorem 6 on synthetic deformations for CKNs/CNNs, including the dependence on σn and n.
- Clarify how close standard ReLU CNNs are to the smooth homogeneous activations considered (e.g., smoothed ReLU) and whether the normalization nk can be avoided or approximated in practice.
- Quantify the effect of kernel approximation (projection) error on stability and information loss.
- Connect RKHS norm control to standard generalization bounds (e.g., Rademacher complexity) to substantiate claims about sample complexity.

Overall evaluation
A strong theoretical paper that advances our understanding of invariance and stability in convolutional representations, bridging scattering, kernels, and CNNs. Despite limited empirical validation and some restrictive assumptions for the CNN inclusion, the analysis is solid and impactful. I recommend acceptance.
