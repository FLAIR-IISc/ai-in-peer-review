Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework for deep representations of signals defined on continuous domains. Each layer consists of (i) patch extraction, (ii) a pointwise RKHS mapping via a homogeneous dot-product kernel, and (iii) linear Gaussian pooling. The resulting representation, Φn, generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains and admits a multilayer, convolutional structure. The authors prove: (a) information preservation at each layer via suitable sampling (continuous and discrete settings), (b) stability to diffeomorphisms with a Lipschitz bound linear in the deformation gradient and inversely proportional to the final pooling scale for translations, (c) a construction for global invariance to general transformation groups via group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling on locally compact groups, and (d) inclusion of a broad class of CNNs (with smooth homogeneous activations and linear pooling) in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, together with an explicit norm bound. They argue that the RKHS norm simultaneously controls stability and generalization.

Relation to prior work
The analysis extends the scattering transform framework of Mallat, Bruna (e.g., PAMI 2013; CPAM 2012) from wavelet-based, non-learned filters to a kernelized, learnable multilayer construction encompassing CKNs. Invariance and stability proofs adapt harmonic analysis techniques (commutators, Schur’s test) to the present kernel setting, including group actions (cf. Cohen & Welling, 2016; Haasdonk & Burkhardt, 2007). The RKHS view relates to work on deep kernels and dual/convex views of neural networks (Daniely et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2016) and Parseval/Lipschitz networks (Cisse et al., 2017). Compared to prior CKN work (Mairal et al., NIPS 2014–2016), this paper focuses on geometry and stability, and provides a CNN embedding result under smooth homogeneous activations.

Strengths
- Clear, principled kernel construction with explicit continuous-to-discrete links and conditions under which each layer preserves information via sampling/deconvolution.
- Stability theorem to diffeomorphisms with explicit dependence on deformation gradient and translation magnitude; translation invariance increases with pooling scale.
- General, modular treatment of group equivariance/invariance using Haar integration; applicable beyond translations.
- Inclusion theorem showing a class of CNNs lies in the RKHS, with a recursive norm bound. This yields a unified control of stability and generalization through RKHS norms.
- Compatibility with practical approximations (finite-dimensional projections as in CKNs) thanks to non-expansiveness, preserving stability properties.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions limit direct applicability to standard CNNs: smooth homogeneous activations and linear Gaussian pooling; common choices (ReLU, max-pooling, strided convolutions) are outside the proved class, aside from smoothed ReLU approximations.
- Stability bound degrades linearly with depth (1 + n), and constants C1, C2 depend on kernel and patch parameters; their magnitude and practical tightness are not quantified.
- Information recovery in the discrete case relies on deconvolution stability and small pooling scales; robustness to discretization noise and boundary effects is not analyzed.
- No empirical validation illustrating the sharpness of bounds, the effect of kernel approximation error, or the stability–generalization link on real data.
- The need for an initial low-pass A0 and Gaussian pooling at all layers may deviate from typical learned subsampling strategies; the impact of replacing with learned filters is not addressed.

Quality
Technically solid within its scope. Assumptions are clearly stated (A1–A3). Proof strategies (operator commutators, Schur’s test, covariance/equivariance arguments) are standard and appropriately adapted. The CNN-in-RKHS embedding is precise, with a constructive norm bound. Some results (e.g., constants, stability of deconvolution in discrete settings) are qualitative; quantitative guidance for practice is limited. The lack of experiments is acceptable for a theory paper but weakens claims about practical implications.

Clarity
The paper is well organized, with precise definitions and consistent notation. Continuous- and discrete-domain constructions are clearly related. The discussion of norm preservation vs. stability and the role of the final pooling scale is helpful. Suggestions:
- Provide concrete kernel examples in the main text (moved from Appendix B) and instantiate Cσ for a couple of activations to illustrate magnitudes.
- Summarize constant dependencies (C1, C2) and κ’s effect on stability in a brief table/figure.
- Add a short discussion on boundary conditions and finite supports.

Originality
The combination of: (i) a multilayer convolutional kernel on continuous domains, (ii) a general stability analysis to diffeomorphisms beyond scattering, and (iii) an RKHS containment result for CNNs with explicit norm bounds, appears novel. The group-invariance construction is conceptually known but its kernelization and integration with the multilayer representation is useful.

Significance
Provides a principled theoretical framework connecting invariance, stability, and generalization for deep convolutional representations via kernels. It advances understanding beyond scattering by covering learned filters through kernel approximations and by relating to a class of CNNs. Potential to inform architectural/regularization choices (e.g., controlling RKHS norms), though practical impact would benefit from empirical corroboration.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contributions extending influential stability results to a broader, learnable setting.
- Clear linkage between representation stability and model generalization via RKHS norms.
- General treatment of group equivariance/invariance.
- Bridges kernel methods and deep CNNs in a nontrivial way.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited applicability to standard CNN components (ReLU, max-pooling) without smoothing/approximations.
- Bounds may be loose; constants and depth dependence not empirically examined.
- No experimental validation or case studies demonstrating the theory’s practical relevance.
- Discrete recovery and deconvolution assumptions may be fragile in realistic settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide small-scale experiments: measure stability of CKN/CNN variants vs. pooling scale and RKHS norm regularization; compare with scattering.
- Quantify constants for specific kernels/activations and report typical values.
- Discuss extensions to nonsmooth activations (ReLU) or max-pooling via approximation bounds, and the effect on stability.
- Analyze projection (Nyström) error impact on final stability bounds.
- Clarify implications for adversarial robustness and compare with spectral norm constraints.

Overall evaluation
A well-executed theoretical paper with meaningful extensions of stability and invariance analysis to multilayer kernel representations and a class of CNNs. While empirical evidence and coverage of common CNN components are limited, the work is likely of interest to NIPS due to its conceptual advances and careful analysis.
