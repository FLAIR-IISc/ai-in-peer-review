- Paper presents a theoretical framework analyzing invariance and stability of deep convolutional representations to small input deformations.  
- Constructs a multilayer convolutional kernel (generalizing CKNs) that builds feature maps via patch extraction, RKHS non-linear mapping, and linear (Gaussian) pooling at each layer.  
- Proves Lipschitz stability to C1-diffeomorphisms with a bound controlled by the deformation gradient norm (||∇τ||∞) and deformation magnitude (||τ||∞).  
- Shows translation invariance emerges from the stability bound and is controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer.  
- Extends the framework to obtain equivariance and invariance to general transformation groups (e.g., rotations) by defining operators on the group manifold.  
- Demonstrates the RKHS of the multilayer kernel contains a large class of standard CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activations, linking CNN stability to the RKHS norm.  
- Strong technical quality: rigorous mathematical development relying on harmonic analysis and kernel methods with clear assumptions (A1–A3).  
- Key strength is bridging scattering-transform theory and learned CNNs, grounding abstract analysis in practical models.  
- Main weaknesses: theory developed for continuous signals with only informal discretization discussion, and no formal discrete stability bounds.  
- Analysis restricted to smooth, homogeneous activations (approximate ReLU) and omits components like exact ReLU and batch normalization used in practice.  
- Paper is purely theoretical with no experiments to validate bound tightness or practical implications.  
- Clear and well-structured exposition but highly technical, assuming strong background in functional analysis and scattering transforms.  
- Original and significant: novel synthesis of scattering, kernel methods, and deep learning; links robustness, RKHS norm, and generalization with potential long-term impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, important bridge to CNNs, fundamental insights, and high-quality presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of empirical validation and reliance on idealized continuous setting and restricted network class.  
- Overall recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance despite limitations due to the paper’s foundational theoretical contributions.
