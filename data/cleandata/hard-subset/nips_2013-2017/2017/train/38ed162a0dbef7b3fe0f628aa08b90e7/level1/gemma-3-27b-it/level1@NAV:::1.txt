## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on invariance to transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel introduced in convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and utilize reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) geometry to analyze these properties. They aim to bridge the gap between the empirical success of CNNs and a more rigorous theoretical understanding of their functionality. This work builds upon previous efforts in characterizing convolutional architectures, notably the scattering transform [17], but extends the analysis to a broader class of CNNs beyond those with pre-defined filters. The authors demonstrate that their kernel framework provides similar guarantees of stability and invariance as the scattering transform, while allowing for learned filters.

The strength of this paper lies in its rigorous mathematical treatment of deep convolutional representations. The authors effectively leverage the kernel framework to provide a theoretical foundation for understanding invariance and stability, extending results from the scattering transform to more general CNN architectures. The link established between the RKHS norm and stability/generalization is a particularly valuable contribution. The thoroughness of the analysis, supported by detailed proofs in the appendix, is commendable. Furthermore, the clear connection drawn between the theoretical results and practical considerations, such as discretization and kernel approximation, adds to the paper’s relevance. 

However, the paper's complexity can also be a weakness. The heavy mathematical notation might pose a barrier to readers unfamiliar with RKHS theory. While the authors attempt to connect their findings to standard CNNs, the precise implications for typical CNN architectures used in practice are not fully elaborated. The assumption of smoothness and homogeneity for activation functions, while justified, limits the direct applicability to ReLU-based networks, requiring consideration of smoothed variants. The discussion of the practical implications of the results – how these theoretical guarantees translate to improved network design or training strategies – could be further developed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a rigorous theoretical framework for understanding invariance and stability in CNNs.
*   Extends existing theory (scattering transforms) to a broader class of models.
*   Connects RKHS norm to generalization and stability, offering potential for regularization strategies.
*   Thorough mathematical analysis with detailed proofs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   High mathematical complexity may limit accessibility.
*   Practical implications for standard CNNs (ReLU networks) are not fully explored.
*   Limited discussion on how to leverage these theoretical results in practice.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep convolutional networks. While the mathematical complexity might be a barrier for some, the rigor and novelty of the analysis are compelling. I recommend acceptance, with encouragement for the authors to consider clarifying the practical implications of their work in future iterations.
