This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional architectures, specifically focusing on invariance and stability. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel construction from Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) and relate it to the geometry of the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). They aim to provide theoretical guarantees for invariance to transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms, extending results previously established for scattering transforms to a broader class of CNNs.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the underlying geometry of deep networks. The connection to kernel methods offers a principled framework for analyzing these properties. The extension of scattering transform-like results to a more general class of CNNs is a significant contribution. The authors carefully build upon existing literature, referencing relevant work in kernel methods, scattering transforms, and CNN theory. The inclusion of a discussion regarding discretization and its effect on the theoretical results is practical and strengthens the paper.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, building on well-established concepts from kernel methods and harmonic analysis. The use of RKHS provides a solid mathematical foundation.
*   **Novelty:** Extending the stability and invariance analysis, previously limited to scattering transforms, to a broader class of CNNs through a kernel framework is a novel and valuable contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, with clear notation and a logical flow of ideas. The structure, with sections dedicated to kernel construction, stability, and connections to CNNs, aids understanding.
*   **Significance:** Understanding the stability and invariance properties of CNNs has significant implications for regularization, generalization, and robustness. This work advances the state-of-the-art by providing theoretical insights into these crucial aspects.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The mathematical formalism is quite dense, potentially hindering accessibility for readers not deeply familiar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis. While thorough, it demands significant effort from the reader.
*   **Practical Implications:** While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implications are not fully explored. It would be beneficial to discuss how these insights could be directly translated into improved CNN architectures or training procedures. The connection to specific architectural choices beyond the general class of CNNs considered is somewhat limited.
*   **Assumptions:** Several assumptions are made throughout the paper, particularly regarding smoothness and homogeneity of activation functions. While justified, a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper lacks empirical validation to support the theoretical claims. While not strictly necessary for a theoretical paper, demonstrating the practical relevance of the results through simulations or experiments would strengthen the contribution.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the properties of deep convolutional networks.
*   The results extend existing knowledge from scattering transforms to a broader class of CNNs.
*   The use of kernel methods provides a principled and rigorous framework for analysis.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the theoretical arguments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The high level of mathematical complexity may limit accessibility.
*   The lack of empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of the findings.
*   The discussion of practical implications and specific architectural connections could be more detailed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with significant contributions to the understanding of deep convolutional networks. The authors successfully extend existing theoretical results and provide a principled framework for analyzing invariance and stability. While the mathematical complexity and lack of empirical validation are minor weaknesses, the overall quality and novelty of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve accessibility and potentially expand the discussion of practical implications. Specifically, adding a section dedicated to outlining potential avenues for empirical validation and clarifying the types of CNN architectures that directly benefit from these theoretical results would strengthen the paper.
