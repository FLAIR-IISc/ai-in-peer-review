

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper studies the theoretical properties of deep signal representations that are near-invariant to groups of transformations and stable to the action of diffeomorphisms. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16] and analyze the geometry of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). Their main contribution is showing that the functional space they define contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, allowing them to establish theoretical stability results for these architectures.

The paper builds upon two key lines of previous work: the scattering transform [17], which provides invariance and stability guarantees for wavelet-based representations, and convolutional kernel networks [15, 16], which offer a kernel-based interpretation of CNNs. While the scattering transform provides elegant theoretical guarantees, it doesn't involve learning and differs from standard CNNs. CKNs, on the other hand, provide a kernel framework for CNNs but lack comprehensive theoretical analysis of stability properties. The authors bridge these gaps by showing how stability results from the scattering transform can be extended to more standard CNN architectures through the kernel framework.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical development. The authors establish a clear connection between the functional space of their kernel construction and the stability properties of CNNs. They prove key results (Lemma 1, Proposition 3, Theorem 6) with careful attention to mathematical details. The paper carefully distinguishes between theoretical guarantees and practical implementations (e.g., in Section 2.1 on discretization and Section 2.2 on kernel approximation), acknowledging limitations while maintaining theoretical rigor.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the authors provide helpful explanations of technical concepts. The connection between the theoretical framework and practical CNNs is well-explained, with the paper effectively bridging abstract mathematics and practical deep learning. The organization is logical, moving from theoretical foundations to practical implementation considerations.

*Originality:* This work significantly extends previous research by providing a unifying theoretical framework that connects scattering transforms, CKNs, and standard CNNs. The key insight that a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions reside in the RKHS defined by their kernel construction is novel. While related work exists on group invariance [6, 9, 22] and kernel methods for deep learning [4, 10, 15, 16], the paper's specific focus on stability to diffeomorphisms and the connection to the RKHS norm is distinctive.

*Significance:* The results have important theoretical implications for understanding why CNNs work well. The paper demonstrates that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization, suggesting that stable representations (low RKHS norm) may require fewer samples to achieve good generalization. This provides a principled theoretical basis for understanding the success of CNNs and could inform future regularization strategies. The connection between stability and generalization is particularly significant, as it bridges a gap between theoretical understanding and practical performance.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper would benefit from addressing two important limitations. First, while the authors acknowledge that their theoretical results are for continuous signals, their practical implementation (CKNs) deals with discrete signals. Although Section 2.1 discusses discretization, the paper could more thoroughly address how the continuous stability guarantees translate to discrete implementations, which is crucial for practical applications.

Second, the connection to standard CNNs is limited to networks with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The paper acknowledges that ReLU is not smooth, but the smoothed variant they propose (to satisfy their assumptions) might not be as practically useful as the standard ReLU. The authors could better address how their framework might extend to more common activation functions or provide empirical evidence demonstrating the practical relevance of their stability results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending stability results from the scattering transform to a broader class of CNNs.
2. It provides a unifying framework that connects several lines of research (scattering, CKNs, group invariance) in a novel way.
3. The results have clear theoretical implications for understanding the success of CNNs and could inform future model design.
4. The mathematical treatment is rigorous and well-executed.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. The practical relevance of the theoretical results could be strengthened with empirical validation on real datasets.
2. The limitation to smooth homogeneous activation functions (excluding ReLU) reduces the immediate applicability to standard CNNs.
3. The paper could more thoroughly address the translation of continuous stability guarantees to discrete implementations.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental question about deep learning: why do CNNs work well? The paper provides rigorous mathematical analysis that connects the stability properties of CNNs to their performance, demonstrating that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization. While the paper has some limitations in terms of practical applicability (due to the smooth activation function requirement and continuous signal focus), these are common challenges in theoretical deep learning papers.

The paper is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on similar topics (e.g., scattering transform [17], CKNs [15, 16]) and makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of CNNs. The results have the potential to inform future work on regularization and model design, and the paper is well-written and well-structured.

I recommend acceptance. This paper makes an important theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of deep convolutional representations, with clear implications for future research. While it would be strengthened by empirical validation, the theoretical depth and significance of the work justify acceptance at NIPS.
