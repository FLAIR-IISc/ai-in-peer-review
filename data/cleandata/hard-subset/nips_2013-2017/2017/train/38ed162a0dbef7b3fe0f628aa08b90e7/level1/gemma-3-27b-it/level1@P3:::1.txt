This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on invariance and stability. The authors leverage a kernel framework to generalize the multilayer kernel introduced in convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and analyze the geometry of the resulting reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). They demonstrate stability to diffeomorphisms and near-invariance to group transformations, connecting their results to the scattering transform while extending them to more general CNN architectures.

The paper is well-motivated. Understanding the theoretical underpinnings of CNNs is crucial for developing more robust and generalizable models, and the concepts of invariance and stability are central to this understanding. The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature – a lack of geometric characterization of the functional spaces inhabited by CNNs – and propose a kernel-based approach to address this. The connections to both CKNs and the scattering transform are clearly articulated, positioning the work within existing research.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous mathematical analysis, building upon well-established concepts from kernel methods and harmonic analysis. The proofs appear sound (though detailed verification would require significant effort).
* **Generalization of Existing Work:** The authors successfully extend the theory developed for scattering transforms to a broader class of CNNs, addressing a key limitation of the original scattering transform approach (its fixed architecture).
* **Clear Connections to CNNs:** The paper explicitly links the kernel framework to standard CNN architectures, demonstrating that their theoretical results have practical relevance. Proposition 7 is a significant result in this regard.
* **Insightful Discussion of Invariance and Stability:** The authors provide a nuanced discussion of the relationship between invariance, stability, and generalization, highlighting the role of the RKHS norm in controlling these properties.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The related work section is very thorough, covering relevant literature from kernel methods, signal processing, and deep learning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Accessibility:** The paper is mathematically dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis to fully grasp. The notation is heavy and could be simplified in some places. While thoroughness is appreciated, the barrier to entry is high.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the paper provides strong theoretical results, it lacks empirical validation. It would be valuable to demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed framework through experiments, for instance, by showing improved generalization performance on benchmark datasets or increased robustness to adversarial attacks. The discussion of the practical implications is largely theoretical.
* **Assumptions:** The stability results rely on certain assumptions (e.g., smoothness of activation functions, regularity of diffeomorphisms).  The authors acknowledge these, but a more thorough discussion of the impact of violating these assumptions would be helpful.  The assumption of homogenous activations feels restrictive.
* **Discretization Details:** The discussion on discretization (Section 2.1) feels somewhat rushed. While the core ideas are presented, more detail on how to handle boundary effects and the choice of sampling rates would be beneficial.
* **Overly Ambitious Claims:** The statement that the framework "contains" a large class of CNNs is a bit vague. It would be more precise to define the specific constraints on CNN architectures that fall within the framework.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents novel and significant theoretical contributions to the understanding of deep convolutional representations.
* The work is technically sound and rigorously analyzed.
* The paper builds upon and extends existing research in a meaningful way.
* The potential impact of the work is substantial, as it could lead to the development of more robust and generalizable CNN architectures.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of empirical validation weakens the paper's overall impact.
* The mathematical complexity may limit the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.
* The assumptions made in the analysis may limit the generality of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field, despite its weaknesses. The theoretical results are impressive, and the paper offers valuable insights into the geometry of deep convolutional representations. While the lack of empirical validation is a concern, the novelty and rigor of the work outweigh this limitation.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should consider:

* Adding a brief discussion of potential empirical validation strategies. Even a preliminary experiment would be helpful.
* Simplifying the notation and providing more intuitive explanations of key concepts.
* Clarifying the precise constraints on CNN architectures that fall within the proposed framework.
* Providing a more detailed discussion of the impact of violating the assumptions made in the analysis.
* Expanding on the discussion of discretization techniques.
