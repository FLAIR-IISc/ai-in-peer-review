Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals on continuous domains and studies its invariance to group actions and stability to diffeomorphic deformations. The construction generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) by defining, at each layer, patch extraction, an RKHS kernel map with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. The representation preserves signal information (recoverability from samples under coverage assumptions), and its discrete counterpart includes subsampling and kernel approximation steps.

The main theoretical result is a Lipschitz-type stability bound with respect to diffeomorphisms τ: for representation Φn(A0x), the deviation scales as C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn, where σn is the final pooling scale, yielding near-translation invariance as σn grows. The analysis follows and extends techniques from Mallat’s scattering framework (e.g., commutator bounds, Schur’s test), but applies to a broader class of multi-layer convolutional RKHS representations not restricted to wavelets. The paper also shows how to obtain global invariance to general locally compact groups G via group-covariant patching and pooling. Finally, it proves that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (smoothed ReLU-like) and a patch-norm normalization fall into the RKHS associated with the multilayer kernel; the RKHS norm then controls both stability and generalization of such CNNs.

This builds on and connects to: scattering networks (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat), CKNs (Mairal et al.), group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling), invariant kernels (Haasdonk & Burkhardt), and recent kernel perspectives on deep nets (e.g., Daniely et al.). The novelty is the unified RKHS framework providing stability/invariance guarantees for convolutional architectures that include CKNs and a subclass of CNNs.

Quality:
- The framework and assumptions are clearly stated. The stability theorem (Theorem 6) is technically sound given (A1–A3), the initial anti-aliasing A0, and ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2. The commutator analysis is carefully adapted to shifted pooling operators, extending prior scattering proofs.
- The discretization lemmas (signal recovery with subsampling) are plausible but rely on stable deconvolution of Gaussian pooling and coverage of Ω by extracted patches; while standard in signal processing, the stability of deconvolution depends on the scale and noise and is briefly discussed. It would help to spell out explicit conditions ensuring numerical stability.
- The kernel approximation step (finite-dimensional projections) preserves non-expansiveness, so stability is retained up to approximation error—this is appropriate and practically relevant for CKNs.
- The inclusion of CNNs into the RKHS (Proposition 7) is interesting, but it hinges on smooth homogeneous activations and a normalization nk(u)=‖Pkxk−1(u)‖, which deviates from standard CNN practice. While the paper argues smoothed ReLU variants can be accommodated (e.g., inverse polynomial kernel), the constants Cσ may be large; concrete examples and bounds for common σ/kernels would clarify when the norm bound is meaningful.
- No empirical validation is provided. Although the focus is theoretical, even small experiments measuring stability of CKN/CNN embeddings to synthetic deformations would strengthen the claims.

Clarity:
- The paper is dense but generally well organized. The three-operator layer (P, M, A) is clearly defined, and the role of each assumption is explicit. The connection between the continuous and discrete settings and the relation of parameters (patch size, pooling scale, subsampling) are helpful.
- Suggestions: provide a short table (or paragraph) giving explicit examples of admissible kernels κk and the corresponding Cσ for a few activations; clarify the scope of CNNs covered (how critical is the patch-norm normalization? can batch/layer norm mimic it?); and add a brief discussion on the magnitude of constants C1, C2 and their dependence on κ and dimension.
- Minor notation: state explicitly early that ‖·‖ is overloaded for different norms; some references to appendices could include brief proof sketches in the main text.

Originality:
- The stability analysis extends scattering results to a broader, kernel-defined convolutional hierarchy and carefully treats non-wavelet layers and pooling with shifts. The group-invariance construction is standard in principle but integrated coherently into the multilayer RKHS setting. The inclusion result for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and the resulting norm-based stability/generalization control are novel and timely.

Significance:
- The work advances theoretical understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations beyond scattering, potentially informing architecture design and regularization. The link between RKHS norm, stability to deformations, and generalization is conceptually useful. The practical impact would be enhanced by empirical corroboration and by clarifying how closely common CNNs fit the theoretical class.

Pros:
- Clean, rigorous stability theorem with interpretable dependence on deformation Jacobian and pooling scale.
- Unifying RKHS framework encompassing CKNs and a subclass of CNNs; explicit norm bounds for CNNs.
- Extension to general group actions via equivariant patching/pooling.

Cons:
- Requires smooth homogeneous activations and patch-norm normalization; standard ReLU CNNs are only covered via smoothing/approximation.
- Discretization/recovery results rely on deconvolution stability; practical conditions and numerical issues are not fully addressed.
- No experiments; constants and tightness of bounds are not quantified.
- Translation invariance/stability bounds scale linearly with depth (1+n); discussion on tightness and implications for very deep nets is limited.

Questions for authors:
- Can you provide explicit Cσ for the inverse polynomial and Gaussian kernels with smoothed ReLU, and discuss their magnitude for realistic filter norms?
- How sensitive are the stability bounds to replacing nk(u)=‖Pkxk−1(u)‖ by common normalizations (batch/layer norm) or by omitting normalization?
- Do your stability results extend (qualitatively) to ReLU via approximation arguments, and what is lost?
- Can you report simple experiments comparing stability of CKNs/CNNs under controlled deformations and varying σn?

Recommendation: Leaning toward accept for its solid and timely theoretical contribution that generalizes scattering stability to a broader convolutional RKHS framework and connects to CNNs; adding empirical illustrations and clarifying constants would further strengthen the paper.
