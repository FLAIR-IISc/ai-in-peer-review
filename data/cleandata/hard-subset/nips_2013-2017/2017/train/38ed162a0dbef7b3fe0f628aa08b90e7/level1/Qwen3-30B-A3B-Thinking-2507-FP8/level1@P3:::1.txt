

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations through the lens of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), generalizing the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and connecting it to the scattering transform theory developed by Mallat. The authors establish stability guarantees under diffeomorphisms for a functional space containing both CKNs and a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The work builds upon Mallat's scattering transform [17] but extends it to more standard convolutional architectures, while also addressing the connection to practical CNN implementations.

**Strengths:**

The paper's primary strength is its rigorous theoretical contribution. The authors successfully extend Mallat's stability analysis to a more general class of convolutional architectures, providing a clear mathematical framework for understanding invariance and stability properties. The proof techniques are well-executed, building logically on existing harmonic analysis methods from Mallat's work. The connection between the RKHS norm and stability/generalization is particularly insightful, establishing a principled way to control model complexity through the RKHS norm.

The paper makes an important contribution by bridging the gap between theoretical representation learning and practical CNNs. The demonstration that CNNs with homogeneous activations (including smoothed versions of ReLU) are contained within the RKHS of their kernel construction is novel and significant. This provides a theoretical foundation for why certain CNN architectures might generalize well, linking stability to generalization in a concrete way.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution by formalizing how global invariance to group actions can be achieved through appropriate design of patch extraction and pooling operators, extending previous work on group equivariance [9, 22].

**Weaknesses:**

The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper doesn't provide any experiments demonstrating how these stability properties translate to improved performance on real-world tasks. This is a significant omission for a NIPS paper, as the field increasingly values both theoretical and empirical contributions.

The assumption of homogeneous activation functions is restrictive. The authors acknowledge that ReLU itself doesn't satisfy their conditions, requiring smoothed variants. While they provide justification for this, the paper would be stronger with a more thorough discussion of how these theoretical requirements map to practical CNN implementations, especially considering ReLU's dominance in practice.

The paper's connection to practical CNNs remains somewhat abstract. While Proposition 7 shows that certain CNNs are contained within the RKHS, it doesn't demonstrate how this theoretical containment translates to improved stability or generalization in actual CNN training. The paper would benefit from a discussion of how practitioners might leverage these theoretical insights for better regularization or architecture design.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs that build on existing work in a logical manner. The theoretical claims are well-supported, though the lack of empirical validation is a notable gap.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical notation. The connection between the kernel framework and CNNs is explained thoroughly, though some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the mathematical concepts.

**Originality:** The paper extends Mallat's scattering transform theory to a broader class of CNNs, which is a meaningful contribution. While the multilayer kernel construction is inspired by CKNs [15, 16], the theoretical analysis of stability for this general class of models is novel.

**Significance:** The work provides important theoretical insights into why CNNs work well, connecting stability to generalization. This has potential implications for regularization and model design. However, the lack of empirical validation limits the immediate practical significance of the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides rigorous theoretical foundation for stability and invariance in convolutional representations
- Successfully extends Mallat's scattering transform theory to a broader class of architectures
- Establishes a principled connection between RKHS norm, stability, and generalization
- Makes a valuable contribution to understanding the theoretical underpinnings of CNNs

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Lacks empirical validation to demonstrate practical relevance
- Restrictive assumptions about activation functions (requiring homogeneous and smooth variants)
- The connection to practical CNN implementations remains somewhat abstract without concrete examples or experiments
- Doesn't address how these theoretical insights translate to improved performance on specific tasks

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes important progress in understanding the mathematical foundations of convolutional networks. While the lack of empirical validation is a notable weakness, the paper's theoretical rigor and novel connections to existing work in representation learning are significant contributions to the field. The work provides a valuable framework for future research on stability and generalization in deep learning.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the lack of empirical validation (perhaps by adding a brief discussion of how these theoretical properties could be tested empirically) and to more thoroughly discuss the practical implications of the homogeneous activation function requirement. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of convolutional networks, and its insights will likely inform future research on stability and generalization in deep learning.
