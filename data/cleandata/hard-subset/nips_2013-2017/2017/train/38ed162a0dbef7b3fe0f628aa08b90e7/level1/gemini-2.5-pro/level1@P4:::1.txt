This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations, focusing on their invariance to transformations and stability to small deformations. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel construction from Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) to signals on continuous domains. This construction involves a sequence of patch extraction, kernel mapping, and linear pooling operations. The primary contribution is a theoretical proof that this representation is stable to the action of C1-diffeomorphisms, building upon the framework and techniques introduced for the scattering transform by Mallat [17]. The derived stability bound demonstrates near-invariance to translations, which improves with the depth and pooling scale of the network. Furthermore, the authors show how the framework can be extended to achieve global invariance to arbitrary compact groups. Crucially, the paper establishes a formal link between this kernel representation and a class of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) that use smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This connection allows the stability results derived for the kernel representation to be applied to these CNNs, with the RKHS norm of the network function controlling its stability.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The mathematical framework is rigorously developed, building upon established concepts from functional analysis, harmonic analysis, and kernel methods. The proofs, detailed in the appendix, appear sound and follow a logical progression inspired by the analysis of scattering networks. The assumptions required for the main stability theorem (norm preservation and non-expansiveness of the kernel map, and a relation between patch size and pooling scale) are clearly stated. The paper carefully discusses the connection between the continuous-domain theory and its practical, discrete implementation, including aspects of signal recovery and subsampling. The work is a complete theoretical piece, providing a formal foundation for understanding stability in certain convolutional architectures.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It begins by introducing the multilayer convolutional kernel, then analyzes its stability and invariance properties, and finally connects the theoretical framework to CNNs. The notation, while dense, is consistent and well-defined. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to the key inspirations—CKNs and scattering transforms—which helps the reader understand the context and contributions. The step-by-step breakdown of the stability proof in Section 3, separating the bounds on different operator commutators, makes the argument easier to follow. The inclusion of sections discussing practical considerations like discretization and kernel approximation further enhances clarity by bridging theory and practice.

**Originality:** The work's originality lies in successfully extending the stability analysis of Mallat [17] from fixed wavelet transforms to a more general, learnable kernel-based framework. While the analytical tools (e.g., bounding operator commutators via Schur's test) are heavily borrowed from prior work, their application to a multilayer RKHS construction that encompasses CKNs and a class of CNNs is novel. The most original contribution is arguably the formal embedding of a family of CNNs within the constructed RKHS (Proposition 7). This provides a new theoretical lens through which to view CNNs, different from other kernel-based analyses that have focused on, for instance, infinite-width limits or two-layer networks. The generalization to arbitrary group invariances in Section 4 is also a valuable, though less central, contribution that connects the framework to a broader line of research on equivariant networks.

**Significance:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to our understanding of deep learning. By formally proving stability guarantees for a class of deep convolutional models, it helps move the field away from a purely empirical understanding of their success. The link between the RKHS norm, stability to deformations, and generalization is particularly important. It suggests that regularization schemes that control the RKHS norm of a network (e.g., weight decay, which is related to the bound in Proposition 7) not only improve generalization from a statistical learning perspective but also enforce robustness to input deformations. This provides a principled explanation for why such regularization is effective and suggests potential avenues for designing more robust architectures. While the direct applicability is limited to CNNs with specific activation functions, the conceptual framework is powerful and likely to inspire further theoretical work on more common architectures.

**Strengths:**
*   Provides a rigorous theoretical proof of stability to diffeomorphisms and near-invariance to translation for a broad class of convolutional representations.
*   Successfully bridges the gap between fixed-filter models like the scattering transform and learnable models like CNNs, showing that similar stability properties can hold.
*   Establishes a formal connection between the RKHS norm of a CNN function and its stability, linking regularization, generalization, and robustness.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and technically sound.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The class of CNNs shown to be in the RKHS is somewhat restrictive. It requires smooth, homogeneous activation functions and includes a normalization term (`nk(u)`) that is not standard in typical CNNs. While the authors propose a smoothed variant of ReLU, the results do not directly apply to the non-smooth ReLU activation used in the vast majority of modern networks.
*   The paper is entirely theoretical, with no empirical validation. Experiments measuring the stability of CKNs or the special class of CNNs, and correlating it with their RKHS norm or generalization performance, would have significantly strengthened the practical impact of the claims.
*   Unlike the scattering transform, the proposed representation does not preserve the signal's norm due to pooling, which may lead to information loss as depth increases. While the authors argue this is a minor concern, its implications could be further explored.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that provides valuable insights into the fundamental properties of deep convolutional models. Its main contribution is the generalization of stability analysis to a kernel framework that includes learnable architectures, providing a formal link between stability, regularization, and the geometry of the function space. Despite the limitations regarding the specific class of CNNs covered and the lack of experiments, the work is a significant step forward in the theoretical understanding of deep learning.
