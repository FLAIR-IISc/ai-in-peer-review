- Introduces a multilayer convolutional kernel representation generalizing CKNs to continuous domains and analyzes invariance to groups and stability to diffeomorphisms.  
- Each layer uses patch extraction, a norm-preserving/non-expansive kernel map from homogeneous dot-product kernels, and linear Gaussian pooling.  
- Shows layerwise information preservation via recoverability from samples while increasing translation invariance with pooling scale.  
- Main theoretical result (Theorem 6) gives a Lipschitz-type stability bound to C1-diffeomorphisms scaling as C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn, so larger final pooling σn improves translation invariance.  
- Analysis extends to discretized architectures with subsampling and to finite-dimensional CKN approximations via non-expansive projections.  
- Section 4 generalizes the construction to locally compact groups, producing equivariant layers and global invariance through group pooling.  
- Proves a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and local norm normalization lie in the RKHS of the multilayer kernel (Proposition 7), linking RKHS norms to stability and generalization.  
- Builds on Mallat’s scattering transform and extends deformation stability beyond fixed wavelets to kernel-defined, learnable representations and some CNNs.  
- Connects to prior work on CKNs, group-equivariant CNNs, invariant kernels, and the “deep networks as kernel machines” perspective.  
- Technical derivations are solid, using commutator bounds, Schur’s test, and Gaussian pooling properties, with nontrivial extensions over scattering analysis.  
- Discretization and recoverability lemmas are reasonable but rely on assumptions like ek ≥ sk and stable deconvolution.  
- Caveats: depth dependence is linear (unclear if tight), requires initial low-pass and ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2, constants C1/C2 left abstract, and recoverability needs sufficiently low-pass filters.  
- Writing is clear but dense; operator-theoretic presentation is precise and assumptions are well stated.  
- Clarity suggestions: make pooling constants explicit (esp. Gaussian), summarize kernel/activation choices in a table, emphasize normalization and activation differences from standard ReLU, and discuss boundary/padding effects.  
- Original contributions: novel RKHS-based multilayer construction with explicit deformation stability, group-equivariant extension, and rigorous inclusion of certain CNNs into the RKHS with norm control.  
- Significance: unifying theoretical framework linking invariance, stability, and generalization; justifies CKN design and suggests RKHS regularization for robustness, though empirical validation is lacking.  
- Pros: clear/general stability bound, group-equivariant extension, discretization/approximation compatibility, CNN-in-RKHS link, and rigorous analysis synthesizing scattering, kernels, and CNNs.  
- Cons: restrictive assumptions (low-pass, small ‖∇τ‖∞), CNN class requires smooth homogeneous activations and local normalization, recoverability/discretization stability not fully quantified, and no experiments.  
- Suggested improvements: give explicit Gaussian constants and depth-tightness discussion, empirical validation on synthetic deformations, clarify relation to standard ReLU and normalization practicality, quantify kernel approximation effects, and connect RKHS norms to generalization bounds.  
- Overall evaluation: strong theoretical paper advancing understanding of invariance and stability in convolutional representations; solid and impactful despite limited empirical validation and some restrictive assumptions — recommendation to accept.
