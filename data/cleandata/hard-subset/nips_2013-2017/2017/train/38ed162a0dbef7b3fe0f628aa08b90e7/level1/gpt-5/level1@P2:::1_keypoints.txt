- Proposes a multilayer convolutional kernel representation that preserves information, increases translation invariance via pooling, and is Lipschitz-stable to diffeomorphisms.  
- Derives a stability bound ‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1 + n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖, generalizing scattering results to broader convolutional architectures.  
- Generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains and studies the RKHS geometry of induced feature maps.  
- Shows how to build invariance to general locally compact groups through group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling.  
- Proves many CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lie in the RKHS of the multilayer kernel, with explicit norm bounds (Proposition 7).  
- Links function stability/generalization to RKHS norm control, suggesting principled regularization strategies.  
- Technical analysis is careful, using harmonic analysis tools (Schur’s test, commutator bounds) and extending lemmas to shifted pooling.  
- Assumptions (A1–A3) are reasonable for Gaussian pooling and homogeneous kernels and cover kernels used in CKNs.  
- Theory covers continuous signals, discretization with subsampling, finite-dimensional kernel approximations, and CNN connections; proofs are in the appendix.  
- Stability requires ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2 (as in scattering), which limits deformation magnitude.  
- Constants C1 can grow with patch/pooling alignment κ and with data dimension, potentially weakening bounds in high dimensions.  
- Translation invariance depends on σn and thus on pooling design choices, trading invariance vs. resolution.  
- Discretization/recovery relies on deconvolution, which may be ill-conditioned unless pooling scales are small; stability conditions are not fully quantified.  
- CNN-in-RKHS result requires smooth homogeneous activations with normalization nk(u), differing from standard ReLU practice (though smoothed ReLU may qualify).  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written for a theoretical audience; architecture and assumptions are precisely defined.  
- Suggested clarifications: quantify/tighten constants C1, C2 for typical settings and dimensional dependence.  
- Suggested clarifications: conditions for stable deconvolution in Lemma 2 and effects of border/finite-support discretization.  
- Suggested clarifications: explicit gap between standard ReLU CNNs and smooth/homogeneous activations, and interactions with batch norm.  
- Novelty lies in extending scattering-style deformation stability to general multilayer convolutional kernels, preserving information and embedding CNNs into the same RKHS.  
- Significance: offers conceptual clarity on deformation stability, invariance-resolution trade-offs, and a path to regularization/sample-complexity benefits, though practical impact is tempered by assumptions and lack of experiments.  
- Strengths: rigorous deformation stability with depth/pooling dependence; generalization beyond scattering; group-equivariant formulation; recursive CNN-in-RKHS norm bounds.  
- Weaknesses: no empirical evaluation of constants/tightness; reliance on Gaussian pooling and specific kernels limits applicability to standard pipelines; discretization recovery conditions under-specified; bounds degrade linearly with depth.  
- Questions for authors: quantify C1, C2 for d=2 and σk schedules and provide synthetic validation; sensitivity to non-Gaussian pooling or strided subsampling; extension to nonsmooth ReLU via limits or inclusion of batch-norm; derive deformation bounds on group manifolds beyond equivariance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong, novel theoretical unification of scattering and kernel perspectives with practical implications for regularization and stability-aware architectures.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of experiments and concrete constants limits practical uptake; some assumptions diverge from standard CNN practice; stability restricted to small diffeomorphisms and depth dependence may be pessimistic.  
- Overall recommendation: solid and insightful theory paper leaning toward acceptance, with requested additions of empirical illustrations and discussion of constants to strengthen impact.
