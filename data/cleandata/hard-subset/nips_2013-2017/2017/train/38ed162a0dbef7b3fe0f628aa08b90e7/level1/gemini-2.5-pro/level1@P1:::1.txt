This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations, focusing on their invariance to group transformations and stability to small deformations. The authors build a rigorous framework based on a multilayer convolutional kernel, which generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) to signals defined on a continuous domain. This construction allows for the analysis of the geometric properties of the associated Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS).

The core of the proposed representation consists of a sequence of layers, each applying three operators: 1) a patch extraction operator, 2) a non-linear kernel mapping into a new RKHS, and 3) a linear pooling operator (convolution with a Gaussian). The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this representation is Lipschitz stable to the action of C1-diffeomorphisms, extending the analysis of Mallat (2012) for scattering transforms to this new, more general setting. The stability bound `‖Φ(Lτx)− Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞)‖x‖` shows that stability to local deformations is controlled by the Jacobian of the diffeomorphism (`∇τ`), while near-invariance to global translations is controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer. The authors also show how the framework can be extended to achieve global invariance to arbitrary locally compact groups (e.g., rotations) by using group-equivariant operators. A key result of the paper is establishing a formal link between this kernel framework and standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). It is shown that the RKHS associated with the multilayer kernel contains a class of CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This implies that the stability and invariance guarantees derived for the kernel representation apply directly to these CNNs, where the stability of the prediction function is controlled by its RKHS norm.

This work sits at the intersection of several important lines of research. It directly builds upon the stability analysis of scattering networks [17] and the architecture of CKNs [15, 16]. It also contributes to the broader effort of understanding deep learning through the lens of kernel methods [10, 36] and analyzing group invariances in neural networks [9].

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and challenging problem: providing a theoretical justification for the stability and invariance properties of deep convolutional architectures. By connecting stability, the RKHS norm, and generalization, the work offers valuable insights into why these models are successful and how regularization might be principled. The extension of these theoretical guarantees from specialized models like scattering transforms to a class of more standard CNNs is a major step forward.

2.  **Originality:** While the paper leverages concepts and proof techniques from prior work (notably [17]), the synthesis is highly original. The generalization of CKNs into a continuous framework for theoretical analysis, the derivation of stability bounds in this new setting, and, most importantly, the explicit construction showing that certain CNNs are elements of the resulting RKHS, are novel and significant contributions.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs (provided in the appendix). The assumptions are clearly stated, and the mathematical development is clear and logical. The authors handle the transition from the continuous theoretical model to its discrete, practical counterparts (Section 2.1) thoughtfully.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The construction of the multilayer kernel is introduced systematically, and the motivation for each component is clear. The progression from the kernel construction to the stability analysis and finally to the connection with CNNs is logical and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Gap with Practical CNNs:** The connection to CNNs in Section 5, while being a main strength, has some limitations. The analysis requires a specific "homogenized" form of the activation function: `z̃ik(u) = nk(u)σ ( 〈wik, Pkzk–1(u)〉/nk(u) )`, where `nk(u) = ‖Pkzk–1(u)‖`. Standard CNNs do not include this explicit input-dependent normalization `nk(u)`. While the authors correctly note that ReLU is homogeneous, the presence of this normalization term is a non-trivial difference. A more detailed discussion on the role of this term and its potential relationship to practical normalization schemes like Batch Normalization would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about its applicability to standard CNNs.

2.  **Smoothness Assumption on Activations:** The analysis is restricted to CNNs with *smooth* activation functions to ensure they belong to the RKHS. The most widely used activation function, ReLU, is non-smooth. The paper suggests using smoothed variants as a workaround, which is a common practice in theoretical analyses. However, this does create a disconnect with what is most commonly used in practice and warrants acknowledgment and discussion.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While this is perfectly acceptable, the contribution could have been more impactful with some simple, illustrative experiments. For example, empirically measuring the stability of a trained CNN to small deformations and relating it to an estimate of its RKHS norm (as bounded by Proposition 7) could provide concrete validation for the theoretical findings.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   The paper provides a strong, novel theoretical framework for understanding stability and invariance in deep convolutional models, a central topic in the field.
*   It successfully bridges the gap between theoretical constructs (scattering transforms, kernel methods) and practical architectures (CNNs), which is a significant achievement.
*   The work is technically solid, well-written, and provides important insights that could influence future research on regularization and network architecture design.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The connection to standard, practical CNNs relies on assumptions (homogenized and smooth activations) that create a gap between the theory and practice.
*   The contribution is entirely theoretical, without any empirical evidence to support the derived bounds or illustrate the concepts.

**Overall Recommendation**

I recommend accepting this paper. The theoretical contributions are significant, original, and address a fundamental question in deep learning. The connection established between a principled kernel construction and standard CNNs provides a powerful new tool for analysis. While there is a gap between the theoretical model and some practical details of CNNs, this is a common and often necessary step in building a theoretical understanding. The paper's strengths in providing a rigorous and insightful framework for a critical problem far outweigh its weaknesses.
