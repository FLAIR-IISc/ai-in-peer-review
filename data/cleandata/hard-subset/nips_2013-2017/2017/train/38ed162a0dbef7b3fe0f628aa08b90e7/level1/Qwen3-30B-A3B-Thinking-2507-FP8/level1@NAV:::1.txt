

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically their invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15,16] to continuous domains, studying the geometry of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). They establish that the signal representation preserves information while being stable to small deformations, with stability guarantees similar to the scattering transform [5,17] but applicable to a broader class of architectures including standard CNNs. The key contribution is showing that a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions live within this functional space, allowing the stability results to directly apply to these models.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The authors provide rigorous theoretical analysis with careful attention to mathematical details, including proofs in the appendix. They appropriately compare their work to related approaches like the scattering transform [17] and CKNs [15,16], clarifying how their framework generalizes previous results while addressing limitations of prior work (e.g., the scattering transform's lack of learning capacity). The connection between RKHS norms and stability/generalization is particularly insightful.

Strengths: The theoretical framework is novel and comprehensive, bridging kernel methods and CNN theory. The stability proofs are elegant and well-motivated, with clear analysis of how pooling layers affect translation invariance. The paper successfully demonstrates how their framework applies to practical CNN architectures through Proposition 7.

Weaknesses: The connection to practical CNN implementations could be strengthened with more empirical validation. While the paper discusses discretization, the transition from continuous theory to discrete practice is somewhat abstract. Some readers might find the mathematical notation challenging without prior exposure to RKHS theory.

The work significantly advances our understanding of why CNNs work well, providing theoretical grounding for their invariance properties. The results have clear implications for regularization and sample complexity, making them highly relevant to the field. The paper meets all four quality criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), offers a novel theoretical perspective (Originality), and addresses a fundamental problem with broad significance (Significance).

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution connecting kernel methods and CNNs; rigorous stability proofs; clear exposition of how CNNs fit within the framework.
Con: Limited empirical validation; some mathematical abstraction may hinder accessibility for practitioners.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep learning architectures and should be accepted.
