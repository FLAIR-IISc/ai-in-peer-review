This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation whose associated RKHS yields feature maps that are near-invariant to group actions and Lipschitz-stable to C1 diffeomorphisms while preserving signal information through the hierarchy. The construction generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKN) to continuous domains via patch extraction, homogeneous kernel mappings, and Gaussian pooling, and then analyzes stability through commutator bounds and Schur’s test. The main result shows a deformation bound of the form ||Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)|| ≤ (C1(1+n)||∇τ||∞ + (C2/σn)||τ||∞)||x||, capturing depth and last-layer pooling scale. The framework extends to global invariance on compact groups through group-equivariant patching and pooling, and connects to CNNs: a class of convolutional architectures with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS, giving norm-based control of stability and generalization. The work relates closely to scattering transforms (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat), but covers a broader family than scattering and links to CKNs (Mairal et al.) and group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling).

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound, with clear assumptions (A1–A3), precise operator-norm bounds, and a thoughtful treatment of discretization and kernel approximation. The dependence on depth and pooling scale is explicit. One limitation is the reliance on smooth homogeneous activations and Gaussian pooling, which deviates from common ReLU/max-pooling practice; constants are not quantified, and empirical validation of stability is deferred.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and generally clear. Suggestions: consolidate continuous/discrete notation earlier; give an intuition box for the commutator argument; provide a small running example illustrating stability constants; clarify the scope of CNNs covered (e.g., smoothed ReLU variants).

Originality: The extension of scattering-style stability to a kernel framework encompassing CKNs and a nontrivial subset of CNNs is novel. The group-invariance layer construction in the kernel setting is a clean synthesis of prior ideas.

Significance: The results provide principled stability/invariance guarantees for architectures closer to practice than scattering and offer a norm-based lens on generalization in CNNs. This can influence theory-driven design of architectures and regularizers.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, general stability/invariance theory beyond scattering; explicit link to CNNs and RKHS norms.
- Careful treatment of discretization and finite-dimensional approximations (CKN).
- Group-equivariant formulation enabling global invariance.

Cons against acceptance:
- Assumptions (smooth activations, Gaussian pooling, ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2) limit direct applicability to standard CNNs.
- No experimental validation of bounds or constants; practical guidance (σk, patch sizes) is limited.
- Norm non-preservation and depth dependence may weaken guarantees for very deep nets.
