

# Peer Review: "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, focusing on invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16] to continuous domains and analyze the geometry of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). They establish that the signal representation is stable to diffeomorphisms and that models from this functional space (including a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations) inherit these stability properties. The work builds on Mallat's scattering transform [17] but extends it to more standard CNN architectures by using a kernel framework, which allows for a clear separation between data representation and predictive models. The paper also connects to related work on group-invariance [6, 9, 17, 22] and kernel-based deep learning [2, 10, 15, 16, 25].

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide complete proofs for their main results (in the appendix) and carefully distinguish between theoretical guarantees and practical implementations. The stability bound in Theorem 6 is well-motivated and extends previous work on scattering networks [17] by showing similar guarantees for more standard CNN architectures. The connection between RKHS norm and stability/generalization (Proposition 7) is particularly valuable for understanding the theoretical underpinnings of CNN performance.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout, and the relationship between the theoretical framework and practical implementations (discretization, kernel approximation) is clearly explained. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

*Originality:* The work makes a significant contribution by bridging the gap between the scattering transform framework and standard CNN architectures. The key novelty lies in showing how the kernel framework can accommodate a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (Section 5), which was previously not established. The approach to achieving global invariance to group actions (Section 4) through appropriately designed patch extraction and pooling operators is also novel.

*Significance:* The results have important theoretical and practical implications. By establishing that CNNs inherit stability properties from the RKHS framework, the paper provides a principled explanation for why CNNs work well in practice. The connection between RKHS norm and generalization (Proposition 7) offers a concrete way to control model complexity, potentially leading to improved sample efficiency. This work could inform future research on designing more robust and sample-efficient deep learning models.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper could benefit from more concrete examples of how the theoretical framework translates to practical CNN architectures. While Section 5 establishes a connection between the kernel framework and CNNs, the paper would be strengthened by providing specific examples of popular CNN architectures (e.g., ResNet, VGG) that fall within the framework, or by discussing how the stability results might guide architectural choices.

The discussion of the "norm preservation" issue in Section 3.1 is somewhat brief. While the authors acknowledge that the norm is not preserved (unlike the scattering transform), they could provide more insight into how this limitation affects practical performance and whether the proposed solutions (e.g., using an additional nonlinear prediction layer) are commonly adopted in practice.

The paper mentions that "the most successful activation function, namely rectified linear units, is homogeneous" but then notes that ReLU is not smooth and thus not in their RKHSs. It would be helpful to discuss more concretely how smoothed variants of ReLU (which satisfy the conditions of Lemma B.2) might impact practical performance compared to standard ReLU.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding CNN invariance and stability properties
- Successfully bridges the gap between scattering transform and standard CNN architectures
- Offers principled connections between stability, generalization, and RKHS norm
- Well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field
- The results have clear implications for designing better deep learning models

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The connection to practical CNN architectures could be strengthened with more concrete examples
- The discussion of norm preservation limitations and practical workarounds is somewhat brief
- The paper doesn't provide empirical validation of the theoretical claims, though this may be beyond the scope of a theoretical paper

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in understanding the geometric properties of deep convolutional representations. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field by extending the scattering transform framework to more standard CNN architectures. The results have important theoretical implications for understanding why CNNs work well and could inform future research on designing more robust and sample-efficient deep learning models. While the connection to specific practical CNN architectures could be strengthened, the theoretical contributions are substantial and well-supported. I strongly recommend acceptance.
