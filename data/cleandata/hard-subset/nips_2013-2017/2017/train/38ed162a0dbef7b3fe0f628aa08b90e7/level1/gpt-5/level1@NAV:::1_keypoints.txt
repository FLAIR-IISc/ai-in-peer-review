- Introduces a multilayer convolutional kernel representation whose RKHS feature maps are near-invariant to group actions and Lipschitz-stable to C1 diffeomorphisms while retaining signal information.  
- Generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKN) to continuous domains using patch extraction, homogeneous kernel mappings, and Gaussian pooling.  
- Proves a deformation bound ||Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)|| ≤ (C1(1+n)||∇τ||∞ + (C2/σn)||τ||∞)||x|| that captures dependence on network depth n and last-layer pooling scale σn.  
- Analyzes stability using commutator bounds and Schur’s test, with precise operator-norm bounds under assumptions A1–A3.  
- Extends framework to global invariance on compact groups via group-equivariant patching and pooling.  
- Shows a class of convolutional architectures with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS, enabling norm-based control of stability and generalization.  
- Relates closely to scattering transforms but covers a broader family and connects to CKNs and group-equivariant CNNs.  
- Theoretical development is careful and technically sound, with explicit depth and pooling-scale dependence and thoughtful discretization/approximation treatment.  
- Main limitation: reliance on smooth homogeneous activations and Gaussian pooling deviates from common ReLU/max-pooling practice.  
- Constants in bounds are not quantified and empirical validation of stability is absent.  
- Assumptions like ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2 restrict applicability to some deformations.  
- Clarity is generally good; recommended improvements include consolidating continuous/discrete notation, an intuition box for the commutator argument, and a small running example for stability constants.  
- Originality: novel extension of scattering-style stability to a kernel framework that unifies CKNs and a nontrivial subset of CNNs.  
- Significance: provides principled stability/invariance guarantees closer to practical architectures and offers a norm-based perspective on CNN generalization.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong general theory beyond scattering, explicit link to CNNs and RKHS norms, careful discretization treatment, and group-equivariant formulation.  
- Cons against acceptance: restrictive assumptions (smooth activations, Gaussian pooling), lack of experiments/quantified constants/practical guidance, and potential weakening of guarantees for very deep networks due to norm non-preservation and depth dependence.
