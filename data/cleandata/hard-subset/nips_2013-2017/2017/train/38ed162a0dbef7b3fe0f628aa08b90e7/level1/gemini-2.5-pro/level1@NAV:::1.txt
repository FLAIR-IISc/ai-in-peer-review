This paper provides a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors generalize the framework of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) to construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and study the geometry of its corresponding Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). By adapting analytical tools from the scattering transform literature (Mallat, 2012), the work proves that the resulting representation is Lipschitz stable with respect to small diffeomorphisms and that its degree of translation invariance is controlled by the final pooling layer's scale. Crucially, the paper establishes a formal link between this kernel framework and standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), showing that CNNs with certain smooth, homogeneous activation functions are contained within the RKHS. This connection allows the stability guarantees to be extended from the kernel representation to a class of learnable deep models, contributing to a line of work at NIPS that seeks to build theoretical foundations for deep learning.

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality and Significance:** The paper is technically sound and offers significant theoretical insights. It provides a rigorous and principled explanation for the stability of CNNs, linking a model's RKHS norm to both its robustness to deformations and its generalization ability. This is a highly valuable contribution that could inspire new approaches to regularization and a deeper understanding of network architecture.
*   **Originality:** The synthesis of concepts from scattering theory, CKNs, and RKHS analysis to derive stability guarantees for a class of learnable CNNs is novel and insightful. It successfully extends prior work on fixed-filter models to more practical, learnable architectures.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The theoretical construction is presented logically, and the connections between the continuous theory, its discretization, and practical CNN models are well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary limitation is that the connection to standard CNNs relies on a specific "homogenized" activation function that includes a patch-norm normalization term. While the authors motivate this by noting ReLU's homogeneity, this form is not standard in practice. A more detailed discussion on the implications of this deviation would strengthen the paper.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes an important contribution to our understanding of deep convolutional networks. The strengths in quality, originality, and significance far outweigh the minor weakness.
