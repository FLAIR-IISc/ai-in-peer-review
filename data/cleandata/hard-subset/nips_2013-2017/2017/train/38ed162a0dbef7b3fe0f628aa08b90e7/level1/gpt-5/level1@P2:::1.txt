Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals that (i) preserves information across layers, (ii) achieves increasing translation invariance via pooling, and (iii) is provably stable (Lipschitz) to diffeomorphisms. The construction generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains and studies the RKHS geometry of the induced feature maps. Under mild assumptions on homogeneous dot-product kernels and pooling, the authors derive a stability bound of the form
‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1 + n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖,
paralleling Mallat’s scattering results but for a broader class of convolutional architectures. They further show how to build invariance to general (locally compact) groups via group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling. Importantly, they prove that a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations belong to the RKHS of their multilayer kernel, with explicit norm bounds (Proposition 7). This links function stability/generalization to RKHS norm control, suggesting principled regularization. The work complements scattering [Bruna & Mallat; Mallat], CNN kernel perspectives [Montavon et al.; Daniely et al.; Anselmi et al.], group equivariant CNNs [Cohen & Welling], and prior CKNs [Mairal et al.], by providing a unified RKHS framework with deformation stability guarantees that transfer to CNN-like models.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The analysis is careful and builds on established harmonic analysis tools (Schur’s test; commutator bounds), extending key lemmas (e.g., to shifted pooling operators) and providing clear operator-norm control of deformation effects through commutators and the final pooling. Assumptions (A1–A3) are reasonable in the intended setting (Gaussian pooling; homogeneous kernels) and cover kernels used in CKNs.
- Completeness: The theory spans continuous signals, discretization with subsampling (Lemma 2), finite-dimensional kernel approximations (projections), and connections to CNNs. Proofs are deferred to the appendix but the main steps and dependencies are transparent in the body.
- Limitations: (i) Stability requires ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2, as in scattering; (ii) constants C1 can grow with the patch/pooling alignment parameter κ and with dimension; (iii) translation invariance is governed by σn and thus depends on design choices; (iv) the discretization/recovery result invokes deconvolution, potentially ill-conditioned unless pooling scales are small; (v) the CNN-in-RKHS result calls for smooth homogeneous activations with a normalization factor nk(u), which differs from standard ReLU practice, though smoothed ReLU variants can qualify. No empirical validation of bounds or constants is provided.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written for a theoretical audience. The architecture, operators, and assumptions are defined precisely, and the role of each layer (patch extraction, kernel map, pooling) is explicit. The relation to prior work is fair and thorough. Some suggestions:
- Discuss the magnitude/tightness of constants C1, C2 with typical parameter choices (σk growth, κ) and dimensional dependence.
- Clarify practical conditions under which the deconvolution in Lemma 2 is stable, and how border effects (discrete, finite supports) may affect stability.
- Make explicit the gap between standard ReLU CNNs and the smooth/homogeneous activations used in Proposition 7, and whether batch-norm or typical normalizations interact with the RKHS assumptions.

Originality
The core novelty lies in (i) extending scattering-style deformation stability to a general multilayer convolutional kernel representation, (ii) showing information preservation and principled discretization, and (iii) embedding a broad class of CNNs into the same RKHS with explicit norm control, thereby transferring stability guarantees to CNN-like models. While elements echo scattering theory and group-equivariant constructions, the RKHS-based unification and the norm-bounded CNN link are original and timely relative to prior CKN and kernel perspectives.

Significance
The results offer conceptual clarity on why deep convolutional representations can be deformation-stable and how invariance is traded against resolution via pooling. The RKHS norm connection gives a path to principled regularization and potential sample complexity benefits, relevant for theory and algorithm design. While immediate practical impact is tempered by assumptions (smooth activations, specific pooling) and lack of experiments, the theoretical insights are likely to influence subsequent analyses and designs (e.g., stability-aware architectures, kernel approximations).

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous derivation of deformation stability with explicit depth and pooling dependence.
- Generalization beyond scattering to a large kernelized convolutional class; compatibility with CKN approximations.
- Group-equivariant formulation yielding global invariance via final pooling.
- CNN-in-RKHS embedding with recursive norm bounds, linking stability and generalization.

Weaknesses
- No empirical evaluation to probe constants, tightness, or practical relevance (e.g., with CKNs or CNNs).
- Stability relies on Gaussian pooling and specific kernel assumptions; applicability to standard pipelines (ReLU, strided convs, BN) is indirect.
- Discretization recovery hinges on deconvolution stability; conditions not fully quantified.
- Bounds degrade linearly with depth via (1 + n); discussion of depth–norm trade-offs could be expanded.

Questions/Suggestions for authors
- Can you quantify C1, C2 for typical image settings (d = 2) and realistic σk schedules, and provide synthetic experiments validating the scaling with ‖∇τ‖∞ and σn?
- How sensitive are the results to non-Gaussian pooling or strided subsampling without prior low-pass filtering?
- Can the CNN-in-RKHS result be extended to nonsmooth ReLU via limiting arguments or to activations with batch normalization?
- For group invariance beyond translations, can analogous deformation stability bounds be derived on the group manifold (beyond equivariance/invariance statements)?

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, novel theoretical contribution that unifies and extends scattering and kernel perspectives to contemporary convolutional architectures.
- Clear path connecting representation stability to predictor stability via RKHS norms.
- Broad potential impact on understanding and regularizing deep convolutional models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of empirical evidence and limited guidance on constants/tightness may hinder practical uptake.
- Some assumptions depart from standard CNN practice (smooth activations; precise pooling), limiting immediate applicability.
- Stability restricted to small diffeomorphisms; linear dependence on depth may be pessimistic.

Overall evaluation
A solid and insightful theory paper with clear advances over prior work in invariance and stability of deep convolutional representations. Despite the lack of experiments, the rigor and conceptual contribution justify acceptance. I lean toward acceptance, with suggestions to add empirical illustrations and a discussion of constants to strengthen the final version.
