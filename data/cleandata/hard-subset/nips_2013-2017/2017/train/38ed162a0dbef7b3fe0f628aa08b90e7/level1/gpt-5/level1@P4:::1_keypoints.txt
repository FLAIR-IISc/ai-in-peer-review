- Proposes a multilayer convolutional kernel framework for continuous-domain signals with patch extraction, pointwise RKHS mapping (homogeneous dot-product kernel), and Gaussian pooling.  
- Shows information preservation per layer under suitable continuous and discrete sampling/deconvolution conditions.  
- Proves stability to diffeomorphisms with a Lipschitz bound linear in deformation gradient and inversely proportional to final pooling scale for translations.  
- Constructs global invariance to general transformation groups using group-equivariant patch extraction and Haar pooling on locally compact groups.  
- Demonstrates a broad class of CNNs (smooth homogeneous activations, linear pooling) lies in the induced RKHS with an explicit recursive norm bound.  
- Argues RKHS norm concurrently controls stability and generalization.  
- Extends scattering-transform analyses to a learnable, kernelized multilayer setting covering CKNs and group actions.  
- Relates to prior work on deep kernels and dual/convex neural network views, and to Parseval/Lipschitz network analyses.  
- Strength: clear continuous-to-discrete construction with explicit information-preservation conditions.  
- Strength: explicit stability theorem detailing dependence on deformation and pooling scale.  
- Strength: modular, general group-equivariance/invariance treatment applicable beyond translations.  
- Strength: inclusion theorem linking CNNs to the RKHS and unifying stability/generalization via RKHS norms.  
- Strength: compatibility with finite-dimensional CKN approximations that preserve stability due to non-expansiveness.  
- Weakness: assumptions exclude common CNN components (ReLU, max-pooling, strided convolutions) except via smoothing approximations.  
- Weakness: stability bounds degrade linearly with depth and involve constants (C1, C2) whose magnitudes and tightness are unquantified.  
- Weakness: discrete information recovery depends on deconvolution stability and small pooling scales; robustness to discretization noise and boundaries not analyzed.  
- Weakness: no empirical validation of bound sharpness, kernel approximation effects, or stability–generalization link.  
- Weakness: reliance on initial low-pass and Gaussian pooling may diverge from typical learned subsampling; impact of learned filters not addressed.  
- Quality: technically solid with clear assumptions and standard adapted proof techniques; some results remain qualitative.  
- Clarity: well organized with precise definitions; suggests moving kernel examples to main text, summarizing constant dependencies, and discussing boundaries.  
- Originality: novel combination of multilayer continuous kernels, diffeomorphism stability, and CNN-in-RKHS containment with explicit norms.  
- Significance: advances theoretical links among invariance, stability, and generalization for deep convolutional representations, though practical impact needs experiments.  
- Pros for acceptance: solid theoretical extension of stability results, RKHS–generalization link, general group treatment, and kernel–CNN bridge.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited applicability to common CNN components, potentially loose bounds and depth dependence, lack of experiments, and fragile discrete-recovery assumptions.  
- Suggested improvements: add small-scale experiments, quantify constants for specific kernels/activations, extend/approximate nonsmooth activations and max-pooling, analyze Nyström projection errors, and discuss adversarial robustness implications.  
- Overall: a well-executed theoretical contribution with meaningful extensions but limited empirical coverage and applicability to standard CNN components.
