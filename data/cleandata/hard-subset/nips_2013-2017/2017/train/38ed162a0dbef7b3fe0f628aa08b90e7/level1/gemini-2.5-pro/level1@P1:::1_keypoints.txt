- Presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations via a multilayer convolutional kernel generalized to continuous domains, enabling geometric analysis of the associated RKHS.  
- Representation built from three per-layer operators: patch extraction, nonlinear kernel mapping into an RKHS, and linear Gaussian pooling.  
- Main result: Lipschitz stability to C1-diffeomorphisms with bound ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞)‖x‖ — local deformation stability controlled by the Jacobian, translation invariance by pooling scale.  
- Framework can be extended to achieve global invariance to locally compact groups (e.g., rotations) using group-equivariant operators.  
- Establishes a formal link showing the RKHS contains a class of CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activations, so the kernel’s stability/invariance guarantees apply to those CNNs and are controlled by the RKHS norm.  
- Builds on scattering transforms and CKNs and contributes to kernel-based and group-invariance analyses of deep learning.  
- Strength — Significance: Provides theoretical justification for stability/invariance and connects RKHS norm, stability, and generalization/regularization.  
- Strength — Originality: Novel synthesis (continuous CKN generalization, new stability bounds, explicit CNN-in-RKHS construction).  
- Strength — Technical quality: Rigorous, well-supported proofs, clear assumptions, and considered continuous-to-discrete treatment.  
- Strength — Clarity: Well-written, logically organized presentation from construction to stability to CNN connection.  
- Weakness — Practical gap: Connection to CNNs requires an input-dependent normalization (nk(u)) not present in standard architectures and not fully related to practical normalization schemes.  
- Weakness — Activation smoothness: Analysis requires smooth activations (ReLU is non-smooth); relying on smoothed variants weakens direct applicability to common practice.  
- Weakness — No experiments: Purely theoretical work lacks empirical validation that could illustrate or support the derived bounds.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Strong, novel theoretical framework bridging kernels and CNNs with technical rigor and potential impact on regularization/architecture design.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Applicability limited by homogenized/smooth activation assumptions and absence of empirical evidence.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — theoretical contributions are significant and outweigh the practical gaps.
