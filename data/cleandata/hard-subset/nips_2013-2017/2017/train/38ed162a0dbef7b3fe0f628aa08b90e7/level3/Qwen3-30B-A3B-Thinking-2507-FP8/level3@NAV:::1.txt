

This paper presents a compelling theoretical framework for understanding the invariance and stability properties of convolutional representations. The authors generalize convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to study how signal representations become nearly invariant to transformation groups and stable to diffeomorphisms when patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling are properly designed. They prove that these properties extend to convolutional neural networks (CNNs) under certain assumptions, providing a rigorous mathematical foundation for understanding why CNNs work well in practice. The work builds elegantly on Mallat's scattering transform [17] and Mairal's convolutional kernel networks [15], extending their theoretical insights to a broader class of architectures.

The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and technically sound, with clear mathematical exposition and careful treatment of assumptions. The authors make a strong case for how the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization, offering valuable theoretical insights for practitioners. The connection between kernel methods and CNNs is particularly novel, showing that a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations can be embedded within the functional space defined by their kernel construction.

However, the major weakness is the absence of empirical validation. The paper makes strong theoretical claims about invariance and stability properties but provides no experiments to demonstrate these effects under the proposed settings. Without empirical evidence, it's difficult to assess how well these theoretical properties translate to real-world CNNs and whether the assumptions hold in practice. The reviewer requests experimental results demonstrating CKNs' invariance to transformations and diffeomorphisms under the proposed design.

The open question about whether random projection or explicit kernel mapping via Fourier features could serve as effective kernel approximations is particularly relevant to practical implementation. Addressing this could significantly enhance the paper's practical impact.

In summary, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of convolutional representations. While the theoretical framework is compelling, the lack of empirical validation represents a significant limitation for acceptance. The paper scores highly on clarity and originality but would benefit from empirical validation to strengthen its significance. I recommend acceptance with major revision to include experimental results addressing the theoretical claims.
