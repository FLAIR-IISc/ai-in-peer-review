This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel, which generalizes the Convolutional Kernel Network (CKN) framework to signals on continuous domains. The core theoretical contribution is proving that this representation is stable to the action of small C1-diffeomorphisms and nearly invariant to translations. This is achieved by carefully designing the patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators to be norm-preserving or non-expansive. The work builds upon and extends the analysis of the scattering transform by Mallat, but applies it to a framework that encompasses learned representations. A key result is that, under certain assumptions on the activation functions (smooth and homogeneous), a large class of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) can be shown to reside within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by the proposed kernel. This elegantly transfers the stability and invariance guarantees from the kernel representation to these CNNs, providing a principled theoretical underpinning for their observed robustness to small input variations.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Technical Quality and Clarity:** The manuscript is of high technical quality and is exceptionally well-written. The authors clearly lay out the construction of the multilayer kernel and build their theoretical arguments in a logical, step-by-step manner. The mathematical formalism is rigorous and leverages established tools from harmonic analysis, adapting them effectively to the CKN and CNN setting. The connection between the continuous domain analysis and its discrete, practical counterpart is also well-articulated.

2.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant and original contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep learning. While the scattering transform provided similar stability guarantees, it is limited to predefined wavelet filters. This work extends such an analysis to architectures with learned filters, which is a major step forward. The connection established between the RKHS of the deep kernel and the functions representable by CNNs (Proposition 7) is a particularly novel and powerful result. It provides a direct link between the geometry of the representation space and the properties of the models themselves, suggesting that the stability of a CNN can be controlled by its RKHS norm, which in turn relates to generalization. This work provides valuable theoretical insight into *why* convolutional architectures are so effective and is likely to inspire further theoretical research in this area.

3.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by the provided proofs. The analysis of how each component operator (patching, mapping, pooling) affects stability is thorough. The extension to general group actions in Section 4 is also a valuable generalization that broadens the applicability of the framework.

**Weaknesses / Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1.  **Complete Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the absence of any experimental results. While the theoretical contributions are strong, they are presented in a vacuum without empirical support. The analysis relies on idealized assumptions (e.g., continuous signals, infinite-dimensional feature maps before approximation, smooth activation functions) whose impact in practical, discrete settings is not assessed. This makes the work feel incomplete. Providing even simple experiments on synthetic data to validate the main stability result (Theorem 6) would have tremendously strengthened the paper's claims and demonstrated the practical relevance of the derived bounds.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

The theoretical framework you have developed is elegant and provides important insights. However, to bridge the gap between theory and practice, the following points should be addressed:

1.  **Request for Experiments:** I strongly encourage the authors to include an experimental section to validate the theoretical findings. This does not need to be a state-of-the-art result on a large-scale benchmark, but rather a targeted experiment to verify the core claims. For instance, one could:
    *   Implement a CKN following the paper's design principles.
    *   Generate a synthetic dataset of signals and apply controlled diffeomorphisms (e.g., small translations, rotations, or elastic deformations).
    *   Empirically measure the representational distance `||Φ(Lτx) - Φ(x)||` as a function of the deformation parameters (`||∇τ||∞` and `||τ||∞`).
    *   Verify if the observed distance scales as predicted by Theorem 6, particularly demonstrating the improved translation invariance with increasing pooling scale `σn`.
    This would provide concrete evidence that the theoretical analysis captures the real behavior of these models.

2.  **Impact of Kernel Approximations:** Section 3.2 mentions that stability is preserved when using projections onto finite-dimensional subspaces, as projections are non-expansive. This is a crucial step for making CKNs practical. However, many popular kernel approximation techniques, such as random Fourier features or the Nyström method, do not correspond to exact projections onto a fixed subspace but rather introduce an approximation error. Could the authors comment on how these practical approximation schemes might affect the stability guarantees? It seems plausible that the stability bound would need to be augmented with a term related to the kernel approximation error. A discussion on this point would be a valuable addition.

3.  **On Homogeneous Activations:** The connection to CNNs relies on using smooth, homogeneous activation functions to embed the network in the RKHS. The most common activation in modern CNNs is the ReLU, which is homogeneous but not smooth. While the paper notes this and references smoothed variants, it would be helpful to discuss the practical implications. How sensitive are the stability results to the degree of smoothness? Does the theory provide any insight into why the non-smooth ReLU works so well in practice, despite not strictly fitting the assumptions?

In summary, this is a well-written and technically sound paper presenting a significant theoretical contribution to our understanding of deep convolutional networks. Its primary weakness is the lack of empirical validation, which prevents it from being a complete piece of work. Nevertheless, the theoretical insights are novel and important enough to be of great interest to the NIPS community. If the authors can add experiments to substantiate their claims, this would be an excellent paper.
