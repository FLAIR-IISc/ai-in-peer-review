- Proposes a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for continuous-domain signals combining patch extraction, homogeneous dot-product kernel mappings, and Gaussian pooling.  
- Constructs a non-expansive feature map Φn with natural discretization and practical approximations via finite-dimensional projections, yielding convolutional kernel networks (CKNs).  
- Main theoretical result: Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms with bound C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn, showing linear-in-depth stability and increasing translation invariance with final pooling scale.  
- Generalizes the framework to arbitrary locally compact groups G, enabling group-covariant patching and near-invariance to group actions via last-layer pooling.  
- Shows the induced RKHS contains a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and provides recursive norm bounds linking RKHS norm to stability and generalization.  
- Situates the work between scattering transforms, CKNs, and group-equivariant CNNs, extending prior analyses with stronger invariance/stability guarantees.  
- Technical development is solid; stability proof uses commutator control and Schur-type operator bounds under standard assumptions (A1–A3).  
- Discretization and finite-dimensional approximation arguments are well-argued but rely on stable deconvolution.  
- The CNN embedding into the RKHS with homogeneous activations and layerwise norm control is technically interesting.  
- Practical impact is unclear because constants (growth in κ and depth n) and pooling-induced energy loss are not empirically explored.  
- Paper is well written with consistent notation and clear progression from continuous model to groups and CNN links.  
- Recommend adding a summary box of assumptions, main theorem constants, and mapping between discrete and continuous scales for clarity.  
- The combination of kernel methods, stability analysis, and group equivariance is novel and nontrivial.  
- Results are significant for theoretically grounding stability/invariance in CKNs and some CNNs, and for connecting RKHS norm to generalization.  
- Lack of experiments limits immediate empirical impact and leaves practical tightness of constants and approximations unresolved.  
- Pros: unified operator-theoretic framework; explicit stability bound depending on deformation, depth, and pooling; constructive RKHS–CNN link; addresses discretization and finite approximations.  
- Cons: no empirical validation; potentially pessimistic linear-in-depth and κ dependence; limited treatment of nonsmooth activations like ReLU and mechanisms like batchnorm.  
- Suggested experiments: synthetic diffeomorphism tests, group invariance (rotated datasets), discretization/ deconvolution ablations, and comparisons of projection-based, Nyström, and RFF approximations.  
- Request clearer quantification of constants (C1 dependence on κ and d) and energy loss across pooling with guidance for σk selection.  
- Suggest extending CNN linkage by constructing mollified ReLU approximations, quantifying RKHS approximation error, and commenting on batchnorm/residuals’ effects.  
- Open questions: whether random projections/RFF preserve non-expansiveness and commutator bounds; whether linear-in-depth dependence can be improved by contractive architectures; and how to handle nonsmooth activations directly.  
- Recommendation: strong theoretical contribution but weakened by lack of empirical validation; adding small-scale experiments would substantially strengthen the paper.
