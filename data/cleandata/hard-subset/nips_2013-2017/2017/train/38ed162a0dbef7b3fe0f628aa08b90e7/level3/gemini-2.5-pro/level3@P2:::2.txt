This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations, focusing on their invariance and stability properties. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel, generalizing the framework of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), which defines a corresponding Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The central contribution is a formal proof that representations within this framework are stable to the action of C^1 diffeomorphisms, provided the kernels satisfy certain properties (norm-preservation and non-expansiveness). This stability result is analogous to guarantees previously established for scattering networks [17], with stability to deformations controlled by the Jacobian of the diffeomorphism and near-invariance to translations controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer. The paper further shows how this construction can be extended to achieve equivariance to general locally compact groups and, importantly, demonstrates that the defined RKHS contains a class of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with smooth homogeneous activation functions. This establishes a connection that allows the stability guarantees to be applied directly to these CNNs.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is of high technical quality. The mathematical framework is carefully constructed, building upon established work in kernel methods [15, 16] and signal processing [17]. The main stability result, Theorem 6, is well-supported by a series of propositions and lemmas whose proofs appear to be sound. The authors are careful to state their assumptions (A1-A3) clearly. The work is a complete theoretical contribution, moving from the construction of the kernel representation to the stability analysis and its implications for both CKNs and standard CNNs.

### Clarity

The manuscript is well-organized and clearly written. The authors do an excellent job of guiding the reader through complex material, starting with the construction of the multilayer kernel, discussing practical discretization, and then proceeding to the theoretical analysis. The connection to CNNs in Section 5 is a highlight and is explained effectively.

However, there are several minor points that could be addressed to improve clarity:

*   **Motivation:** While the introduction mentions that stability to small deformations can lead to more robust models and improved sample complexity, the motivation could be stronger. The connection to adversarial robustness is mentioned only briefly in the related work section. A more detailed discussion on why stability to diffeomorphisms is a crucial property to study in the context of modern deep learning would strengthen the paper's impact.
*   **Notation:** The symbol `\kappa` is used both for the constant in assumption (A3) and as the function defining the homogeneous dot-product kernel in Equation (2). This overloading could confuse the reader.
*   The function `\phi_k` in Equation (2) defines the kernel mapping, but the notation `\phi` is used later in the text without being explicitly defined in relation to the overall kernel `K_n` (e.g., in the discussion of Equation (1)).
*   **Minor Corrections:** In Appendix B, the second constraint in the displayed equation between lines 384–385 seems redundant, as `K(z, z') = K(z', z)` by definition. There also appears to be a typo in the inequality around lines 427–428, which may hold with equality, and a variable mismatch (`||z|| - ||z||` should likely be `||z|| - ||z'||`).

### Originality and Significance

The paper's originality lies in extending the stability analysis of scattering networks to a more general class of learnable, deep convolutional architectures via the kernel framework. While the proof techniques are inspired by Mallat [17], their adaptation to the CKN framework and subsequent connection to CNNs is a novel and significant contribution.

The work is significant for several reasons:
1.  It provides a principled theoretical understanding of the geometric properties (invariance, stability) of a class of deep neural networks.
2.  By embedding CNNs within an RKHS, it connects the network's stability to its RKHS norm, linking generalization theory (via margin bounds) to robustness against input deformations. This provides a theoretical grounding for the intuition that smoother functions should generalize better.
3.  The analysis could inspire the design of new regularization techniques or architectures with provable stability guarantees.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** Provides a rigorous proof of stability to diffeomorphisms for a broad class of deep convolutional models, including CKNs and certain CNNs.
*   **Novel Connection:** Skillfully bridges kernel methods and deep learning, extending the theoretical understanding of CNNs.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making complex concepts accessible.
*   **High Significance:** The results are important for researchers interested in the theoretical foundations of deep learning, regularization, and model robustness.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Motivation could be stronger:** The practical importance of stability to diffeomorphisms could be more explicitly articulated and linked to current challenges in deep learning.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** Several minor issues with notation and typos, as detailed above, should be corrected.
*   **Incomplete Bibliography:** Many references are missing page numbers or publication details (e.g., [3, 6, 8, 9, 13, 14, 17, etc.]). This should be fixed for the final version.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to our understanding of deep convolutional networks. The weaknesses are minor and can be easily addressed in a revision. The work is well-suited for the NIPS audience and is likely to be influential.
