

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations" (NIPS 2017)

This paper studies the stability properties of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) with respect to diffeomorphisms, extending the work of Mallat on scattering networks [17] to a broader class of architectures. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel construction that generalizes CKNs and shows that representations from this functional space are stable to C^1 diffeomorphisms under appropriate conditions. The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical framework demonstrating that signal representations are stable when kernels satisfy norm-preserving (A1) and non-expansive (A2) properties with appropriately sized patches (A3). The paper also connects these stability results to predictive models by showing that RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization.

The work builds upon Mallat's scattering transform [17], which established Lipschitz stability for wavelet-based representations, but extends it to convolutional architectures that involve learning (unlike scattering networks). It also relates to the convolutional kernel networks framework [15,16], which previously provided an unsupervised learning interpretation for CNN-like architectures. The authors make a significant contribution by showing that the functional space of their kernel construction contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, thereby extending stability results to standard CNNs.

**Strengths:**

The paper is technically sound and well-organized, with clear mathematical development. The stability analysis for C^1 diffeomorphisms is thorough and provides meaningful theoretical guarantees that connect to practical concerns like robustness to adversarial examples. The extension to locally compact groups in Section 4, while sketchy, is a valuable theoretical direction. The manuscript effectively combines kernel methods with deep learning, providing a bridge between these two areas of machine learning. The results are of significant interest to the ML community, as they offer a principled approach to understanding and improving stability in CNNs.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary weakness is the insufficient motivation for studying stability. While the paper mentions Lipschitz stability's connection to adversarial robustness in a single sentence, it fails to elaborate on why stability is important beyond this brief reference. The authors should expand on how stability translates to improved performance on real-world tasks, perhaps through concrete examples or connections to specific applications where stability matters.

Several technical issues require correction:
1. Notation conflict: The symbol κ is overloaded in (A3) and used for the kernel function in Eq. (10), which will confuse readers.
2. Redundant constraint: The second constraint in the displayed equation between lines 384-385 is redundant due to kernel symmetry.
3. Missing definition: The symbol φ is not defined at line 416 (should be introduced via Eq. 10 as the feature map for K).
4. Typo: The inequality around lines 427-428 appears to hold with equality, and ||z|| - ||z|| should be ||z|| - ||z'||.
5. Bibliography issues: Several references lack page information ([3,6,8,13,14,18,25–27,30,31]), and references ([9],[17],[19],[32]) need updated publication details.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical results. The mathematical development is rigorous, though the minor technical issues noted above need to be addressed.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with good organization of concepts. The technical issues noted above would significantly improve clarity if addressed.

**Originality:** The paper extends Mallat's work on stability to a broader class of networks (CKNs and CNNs), providing a novel kernel-based perspective on CNNs. The extension to locally compact groups is also a meaningful theoretical contribution.

**Significance:** The results provide important theoretical insights into why CNNs work well, connecting stability to generalization properties. The framework could guide the development of more robust deep learning architectures. The connection between RKHS norm and stability has implications for regularization strategies and sample complexity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides rigorous stability guarantees for a broad class of CNNs
- Connects kernel methods and deep learning in a meaningful way
- Offers theoretical insights into why CNNs are robust
- Well-written and well-organized manuscript
- Significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of deep learning

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Insufficient motivation for studying stability beyond a single sentence
- Several technical issues that need correction before publication
- Some theoretical results (e.g., extension to locally compact groups) are only sketched
- Bibliography contains incomplete information

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of convolutional architectures. The minor technical issues noted above can be easily addressed in a revision. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, providing important theoretical insights that connect kernel methods with deep learning. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the technical issues and strengthen the motivation for stability analysis.
