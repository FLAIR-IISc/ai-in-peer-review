This paper studies deep convolutional representations through the lens of positive definite kernels and reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). It defines a multilayer convolutional kernel that mirrors the layerwise structure of CNNs (patch extraction → kernel map → pooling) on continuous domains, and shows: (i) stability to diffeomorphisms in the precise Lipschitz sense of Mallat’s scattering framework, with translation invariance governed by the final pooling scale; (ii) near-invariance to general group actions via suitably designed group-covariant patch extraction and pooling; and (iii) that a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations are contained in the resulting RKHS, so that stability properties transfer to these CNNs through norm control. Discretization, kernel approximation (CKN-style projections), and conditions ensuring information preservation under subsampling are treated, and the paper provides bounds that separate the roles of local pooling (stability) and the final pooling layer (global invariance). The work connects rigorously to scattering (Bruna & Mallat) while extending it to broader convolutional architectures and to CKNs (Mairal et al.).

Relation to prior work:
- Scattering: Adopts the Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms and group-theoretic setup of Mallat/Bruna, but replaces fixed wavelets with general kernel-induced layers and includes non-wavelet CNNs via RKHS containment.
- Convolutional Kernel Networks: Generalizes CKNs to continuous domains, clarifies signal preservation and discretization, and proves stability bounds that survive kernel approximations by non-expansive projections.
- Group-equivariant CNNs: Aligns with the equivariance logic of Cohen & Welling by building patch/pooling operators that commute with group actions, and provides a functional-analytic stability treatment.
- Invariant kernels and orbit methods: Connects in spirit to Haasdonk & Burkhardt and subsequent invariant-kernel constructions, but operates within a multilayer, convolutional RKHS with explicit diffeomorphism stability.

Quality:
- Technical soundness is high: assumptions are explicit (norm-preserving/non-expansive kernel maps; patch sizes proportional to pooling scale; small Jacobian norm of diffeomorphisms), proofs are provided in the appendix, and the key stability inequality cleanly separates deformation (∥∇τ∥∞) from translation (∥τ∥∞/σn).
- The continuous–discrete bridge (sampling, deconvolution-based recovery under ek ≥ sk) and kernel approximation via projections are carefully justified to preserve non-expansiveness and thus stability.
- One limitation is the 1+n dependence in constants and potential norm shrinkage through pooling, though the authors discuss mitigation via a normalized top kernel map.

Clarity:
- The manuscript is well organized and readable, with precise definitions and assumptions. Suggestions: consolidate the continuous/discrete notation earlier; highlight in the main text where the homogeneous kernel assumptions (A1–A2) are satisfied; and provide an executive summary of constants’ dependence (dimension d, κ, depth n).

Originality:
- The main novelty is a unified multilayer kernel construction whose RKHS contains a broad class of CNNs, enabling direct transfer of scattering-style stability to CNNs and CKNs; a general group-equivariant, pooling-based invariance mechanism; and signal-preservation/discretization guarantees in the multilayer setting. This goes beyond a mere restatement of CKNs by establishing stability to diffeomorphisms and connecting RKHS norm to predictive stability for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations.

Significance:
- Provides principled control of stability and invariance for widely used architectures, clarifies the role of pooling scales, and links RKHS norms to generalization/stability, which may inform regularization and architecture design. Even without experiments, the theoretical contribution is valuable to the community.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Scope of novelty vs. CKNs: Please make explicit what is new beyond Mairal et al. (2014–2016): is it (i) the continuous-domain construction, (ii) the full stability-to-diffeomorphisms bounds, (iii) the inclusion of CNNs via Prop. 7, or all three? A short comparison table would help.
- “Not losing signal information”: The paper proves recoverability under deconvolution and ek ≥ sk, but practical pipelines do lose information (finite support, noise, boundary effects). Why is full information preservation desirable given that many tasks benefit from discard of nuisance variability? A discussion of the bias–variance trade-off and sample complexity implications would strengthen the motivation.
- Practical implications: Can the RKHS norm bounds in Prop. 7 be turned into explicit generalization bounds (e.g., Rademacher complexity or margin-based bounds) that improve on standard analyses for CNNs? Even a qualitative comparison would clarify the value of the RKHS reformulation.
- Activations: The class of “smooth homogeneous” activations excludes ReLU; the paper suggests smoothed ReLUs. How sensitive are the bounds to the smoothing, and do they meaningfully predict behavior for true ReLUs?
- Constants and regimes: Please clarify how C1 depends on κ and d, and discuss the tightness of the (1+n) factor. Are there architectural choices (e.g., pooling schedules) that improve the depth dependence?
- Group invariance: The paper constructs group-covariant layers via operators commuting with Lg. Is there a characterization of all linear operators that commute with the group action in this setting (e.g., convolution with class functions on G)? Would any such operator ensure equivariance in your framework?
- Relation to invariant kernels: Please discuss connections and differences to Haasdonk & Burkhardt (2007) and related orbit/invariant kernel methods (e.g., Mroueh et al. 2015; Raj et al. 2017), particularly regarding global vs. local stability and computational treatment.

Suggestions:
- Add a small empirical illustration (CKN or smoothed-activation CNN) showing numerically the deformation Lipschitz behavior as σn varies.
- Provide guidance on choosing ek, sk, and σk to balance stability and information retention; discuss robustness of the deconvolution recovery to noise.
- Expand the discussion of how the RKHS norm can be controlled in practice (weight decay, Parseval regularization) and its observed effect on robustness.

Pros for acceptance:
- Rigorous, well-presented theory extending scattering-style stability to general convolutional kernel representations and a class of CNNs.
- Clear, modular separation of representation, invariance, and prediction, with actionable insights about pooling scales and norm control.
- Bridges multiple literatures (kernels, scattering, CNNs, group equivariance) with nontrivial new results.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited empirical validation and unclear practical tightness of constants.
- Some claims (signal preservation) may be over-emphasized relative to practical needs.
- Novelty relative to prior CKN work could be articulated more crisply.

Recommendation: accept.
