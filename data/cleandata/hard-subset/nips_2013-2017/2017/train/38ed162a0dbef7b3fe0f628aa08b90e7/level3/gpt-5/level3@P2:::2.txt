Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation that yields deep signal features which (i) are stable to C1-diffeomorphisms and (ii) can be made nearly invariant to translations and, more generally, to locally compact group actions via appropriate pooling. The construction generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains and studies the induced RKHS geometry. Theoretical contributions include: (a) signal-preservation lemmas in both continuous and discrete settings (Lemmas 1–2), (b) a stability bound to diffeomorphisms with translation invariance improving with depth via the final pooling scale (Theorem 6), (c) compatibility of stability with kernel approximation/projection steps used by practical CKNs, and (d) an embedding result showing that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS of the proposed kernel (Proposition 7), which transfers stability guarantees to these CNNs via norm control.

The work connects to and extends the scattering transform literature [5,17], which provides norm-preserving, translation-invariant and deformation-stable representations but uses fixed wavelet filters. It also builds directly on CKNs [15,16], offering a principled analysis of their invariance/stability, and relates to group-equivariant convolution (e.g., [9,22]) by providing a kernel/RKHS view on equivariance and last-layer global invariance through group pooling. The link to CNNs complements kernel perspectives on deep models [2,10,18,35,36].

Quality
The paper is technically sound. The assumptions (A1–A3) are clear and satisfied by a large class of homogeneous dot-product kernels; the nonexpansiveness and norm-preservation conditions enable tight operator-norm bounds via commutators and Schur’s test. The stability bound ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖ is well-motivated: it recovers translation equivariance at intermediate layers and makes invariance controlled by the final pooling scale; the dependence on ‖∇τ‖∞ matches the scattering analysis. The discretization and kernel-approximation sections carefully argue that practical CKNs inherit nonexpansiveness and thus stability (up to approximation error). Proposition 7 gives a constructive inclusion of CNNs in the RKHS under smooth homogeneous activations, with an explicit norm bound that composes across layers.

That said, two aspects could be strengthened:
- Motivation and empirical relevance: the manuscript briefly mentions adversarial robustness; a richer motivation (e.g., links to sample complexity, data augmentation, and robustness beyond ℓp perturbations) and even small empirical illustrations would help demonstrate practical impact.
- Constants and scope: clarify how C1 scales with κ, d, and h, and discuss the practical meaning of the ‖∇τ‖∞ ≤ 1/2 condition. The linear-in-depth factor (1+n) can be discussed in light of modern deep nets and whether larger σn mitigates this in practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with careful definitions and proofs (as per appendices). The continuous-to-discrete discussion is helpful, and the group generalization is clearly sketched. A few clarity/notation issues should be fixed:
- Notation overloading: κ in assumption (A3) conflicts with κk used as the scalar dot-product kernel in Eq. (2). Please rename one of them to avoid confusion (e.g., use cκ for (A3) or rename κk to ρk).
- Mathematical simplification: the second constraint in the displayed equation between lines 384–385 is redundant due to kernel symmetry; it can be removed.
- Missing definition: φ at line 416 should be explicitly introduced as the feature map associated with K (cf. Eq. (2)/(10)), before first use.
- Typo/corrections: around lines 427–428 the inequality appears to hold with equality; also replace “‖z‖ − ‖z‖” by “‖z‖ − ‖z′‖”.
- Bibliography formatting: page information is missing for [3,6,8,13,14,18,25–27,30,31]; and some references ([9],[17],[19],[32]) need updated publication/acceptance details.

Originality
The paper brings together the scattering-style stability framework with the CKN/kernel viewpoint to produce new stability and invariance guarantees for a broader class of deep representations, including a nontrivial link to CNNs via RKHS inclusion and norm control. The group-invariance extension is in line with prior equivariant CNNs but contributes a clean kernel/RKHS formulation. The discretization and signal-preservation statements are also of independent interest.

Significance
The results advance theoretical understanding of invariance/stability in deep convolutional representations, beyond fixed wavelet constructions, and show how these properties can be enforced/controlled via RKHS norms. This has implications for generalization, sample complexity, and robustness, and it informs the design of architectures and regularizers. While primarily theoretical, the compatibility with practical CKN approximations and the inclusion of CNNs suggest that the insights may transfer to real systems.

Pros for acceptance
- Rigorous stability analysis for multilayer convolutional kernels, matching and extending scattering guarantees.
- Clear path from continuous theory to discrete, implementable CKNs; stability is preserved under standard approximations.
- Inclusion of a wide class of CNNs in the RKHS, providing a principled link between stability and generalization via norm control.
- Group-equivariant formulation with last-layer global invariance is elegant and broadly useful.
- Well-written and likely of interest to the NeurIPS community.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical motivation and empirical illustration are limited; stronger evidence of impact would help.
- Assumptions exclude nonsmooth activations like ReLU (though smooth approximations are discussed); clarify breadth of admissible σ and provide concrete choices.
- The (1+n) dependence and the need for initial pooling A0 and σn choices warrant a more detailed discussion of trade-offs (information loss vs invariance).
- Some minor notation/typo/bibliography issues.

Questions and suggestions to authors
- Please expand the motivation beyond adversarial robustness: discuss sample complexity gains, connections to data augmentation and margin theory, and when deformation stability is expected to help in practice.
- Provide explicit examples of kernels and activations satisfying (A1–A2) and Cσ(a) < ∞, with guidance on parameter choices; a small synthetic experiment illustrating Theorem 6’s trends (varying σn, n) would be valuable.
- Clarify stability of the deconvolution-based recovery in Lemma 2: state conditions on the blur scale and boundary handling to ensure well-posedness.
- Discuss the scaling of constants C1, C2 with dimension and patch size, and whether tighter (sublinear-in-n) bounds are possible under additional architectural constraints.
- Consider adding a brief note on how the RKHS norm bound in Proposition 7 correlates with standard weight-norm regularization used in CNN training.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept
The work is technically solid, well-presented, and makes a substantive theoretical contribution by unifying and extending stability/invariance results to a kernel framework that encompasses CKNs and a class of CNNs. Addressing the minor issues and strengthening the motivation would further improve the impact. Confidence: high.
