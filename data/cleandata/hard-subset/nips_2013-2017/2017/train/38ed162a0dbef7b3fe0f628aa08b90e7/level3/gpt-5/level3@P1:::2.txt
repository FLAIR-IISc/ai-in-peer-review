This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation whose feature maps are equivariant to translations at intermediate layers, become increasingly invariant via Gaussian pooling, and are provably stable to C1 diffeomorphisms. The authors define a sequence of operators—patch extraction Pk, kernel mapping Mk into an RKHS with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling Ak—to build Φn(x) = AnMnPn ··· A1M1P1x. Under norm-preserving and non-expansive kernel maps (A1–A2) and patch sizes matched to pooling scales (A3), they establish a Lipschitz bound of the form ‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1 + n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖, yielding stability to deformations and near-translation invariance that improves with the last pooling scale σn. They also show signal preservation across layers under appropriate sampling, discuss discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs), and sketch an extension to locally compact groups via group-covariant patch extraction and pooling. Finally, they connect the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel to a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, so that RKHS norms control prediction Lipschitzness (and thus generalization) for these CNNs.

Relation to prior work: The analysis builds on and significantly generalizes scattering transforms (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat), which provide norm-preserving, stable representations but with fixed wavelet filters. Here, the kernel framework encompasses learned representations (CKNs) and a broad class of CNNs, preserving scattering-like stability guarantees. The group-equivariant construction relates to Cohen & Welling’s group convolution networks, and the RKHS perspective connects to Poggio and collaborators’ work on invariance/selectivity and to kernel analyses of deep nets. The discretization/approximation pipeline links to prior CKNs and classical kernel approximation (Nyström, low-rank). The discussion of Lipschitzness and robustness touches on Parseval networks and adversarial robustness.

Quality: The theory appears technically sound, with careful operator-norm bounds via commutators, Schur’s test, and clear control of constants (e.g., C1 depending on pooling kernel h and κ; C2 ∝ ‖∇h‖1/σn). The assumptions are transparent and standard for this line of work. The connection to CNNs via inclusion in the RKHS and norm bounds is well-motivated and valuable. Two limitations are worth noting: (i) stability requires ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2 and scales with depth as (1 + n); (ii) pooling is not norm preserving, so stability is traded against energy loss unless an additional final kernel layer is used. The discretization and deconvolution-based recovery arguments hinge on practical filter invertibility and noise sensitivity.

Clarity: The manuscript is well organized and readable. A few concrete edits would improve precision and reduce friction:
- Motivation: Provide stronger motivation for stability beyond a brief note on adversarial robustness. For example, connect the Lipschitz control via RKHS norm to sample complexity, regularization (margin-based generalization), data augmentation invariance, and domain shift robustness.
- Notation: κ is overloaded—used in (A3) for patch-size control and also as the kernel function in Eq. (10). Please rename one of them to avoid confusion.
- Minor math edits:
  - Around lines 384–385: the second constraint in the displayed equation is redundant by kernel symmetry; it can be dropped without loss.
  - Line 416: φ is not defined; introduce it explicitly as the feature map associated with kernel K in Eq. (10).
  - Lines 427–428: the inequality seems to hold with equality; also replace ||z|| − ||z|| with ||z|| − ||z′||.
- Bibliography: Please add page information for [3,6,8,13,14,18,25–27,30,31], and update publication/acceptance details for [9,17,19,32] to reflect final venues/years where applicable.

Originality: The main contributions—deriving scattering-like deformation stability for multilayer convolutional kernels that encompass CKNs and a class of CNNs; stability preserved under kernel approximation; and a general group-equivariant construction within the kernel framework—are novel and timely. The CNN-RKHS inclusion with explicit norm bounds for smooth homogeneous activations extends recent dual views on expressivity and is of independent interest.

Significance: The work advances theoretical understanding of invariance/stability in deep convolutional representations and offers a principled way to relate architectural choices (patch sizes, pooling scales) and function norms to robustness and generalization. It is likely to influence both theory and the design/regularization of practical models. The significance would be further strengthened by empirical illustrations validating the derived bounds (e.g., measuring Lipschitz constants and deformation sensitivity vs. σn and depth for CKNs/CNNs) and by guidance on choosing σk and Sk in practice.

Additional suggestions:
- Quantify constants for typical settings (e.g., d = 2 with Gaussian h) to give practitioners a sense of magnitude.
- Clarify the scope of “smooth homogeneous” activations and the practical impact of approximating ReLU by smoothed variants; a brief discussion of approximation error vs. stability would help.
- In Section 4, turn the sketch into a theorem mirroring Theorem 6 for compact or unimodular groups, stating stability to local group deformations and invariance via final pooling on G (or a subgroup), with explicit assumptions and constants.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, general theoretical results extending scattering-type stability to broader, learnable convolutional representations.
- Clear linkage between RKHS norms, stability, and generalization, including for a class of CNNs.
- Careful treatment of discretization and kernel approximation, making the theory relevant to practice.
- Well written and organized; topic squarely within NIPS scope.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical validation; no experiments corroborating bounds or offering design guidelines.
- Stability bound degrades linearly with depth and depends on initial low-pass A0; practical implications could be discussed more concretely.
- Group-invariance section remains a sketch; formal results would strengthen the contribution.
- Minor clarity and bibliographic issues to be fixed.

Overall: Strong theoretical contribution with clear relevance to the community. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarity/motivation edits and, if possible, adding a brief empirical illustration to substantiate the practical import of the stability bounds.
