### Review

**Summary**

This paper provides a theoretical analysis of the stability of deep convolutional representations, focusing on Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs). The authors prove that CKNs are Lipschitz stable to small C^1 diffeomorphisms, provided the underlying kernels are norm-preserving and non-expansive, and the patch sizes are appropriately scaled with the pooling layers. This result builds upon the stability analysis developed for scattering networks but extends it to a framework that more closely resembles modern CNNs. The paper also sketches an extension of this analysis to actions of locally compact groups and establishes a formal connection showing that the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) of the CKN contains a class of CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions, thereby transferring the stability guarantees to these networks.

**Strengths**

The paper has several significant strengths.

*   **Quality & Significance:** The main contribution—a formal proof of stability to diffeomorphisms for a CNN-like architecture—is technically sound and highly relevant. It provides a valuable theoretical lens through which to understand the geometry of deep representations and their robustness to small input variations. The connection between kernel methods and deep networks is a fruitful area of research, and this work makes a solid contribution by linking the RKHS norm to both stability and generalization. The results are an important step toward a principled understanding of why deep convolutional models succeed.

*   **Originality:** While inspired by prior work on scattering transforms, the paper's originality lies in adapting and extending this challenging analysis to the CKN framework, which is more directly related to practical CNN architectures. The result that certain CNNs are contained within the CKN's RKHS is an important step in unifying these two perspectives and extending theoretical guarantees from kernel machines to neural networks.

*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is well-organized and clearly written. The authors do an excellent job of building up the multilayer kernel construction before diving into the stability proof, making a complex topic accessible. The progression from the continuous-domain theoretical model to its practical discrete implementation is logical and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

Despite the strengths, there are several areas where the paper could be improved.

*   **Motivation:** The motivation for studying stability to diffeomorphisms could be strengthened significantly. The introduction frames stability as a desirable property for robust models, but the connection to practical issues like adversarial robustness is only mentioned in passing in the related work section. The authors should more explicitly articulate in the introduction why Lipschitz stability to this particular class of deformations is a critical property for deep networks, perhaps by discussing its implications for generalization, invariance learning, or defense against adversarial attacks in more detail. A stronger framing would increase the paper's impact.

*   **Minor Corrections and Clarifications:** I have identified several minor issues that, if addressed, would improve the paper's clarity and precision:
    *   **Notation:** The symbol `\kappa` is used to denote both a constant in assumption (A3) and the kernel function `\kappa_k` in Eq. (2). This overloading is confusing and should be resolved by using a different symbol for one of these quantities.
    *   **Redundant Condition:** The second constraint in the displayed equation between lines 384 and 385 appears to be redundant. Given that K is a symmetric positive definite kernel, this property is inherent and does not need to be stated as a separate constraint.
    *   **Undefined Term:** The feature map `\phi` is used at line 416 but is not explicitly defined in the surrounding text. For clarity, the authors should formally introduce it as the canonical feature map associated with the kernel K, referencing a definition such as Eq. (10).
    *   **Mathematical Correction:** In the proof around lines 427-428, the inequality appears to hold with equality. More importantly, the expression `||z|| - ||z||` should be corrected to `||z|| - ||z'||`.

*   **Bibliography:** The bibliography requires careful revision. Many entries are incomplete or out of date, which detracts from the paper's polish.
    *   Page information is missing for references [3, 6, 8, 13, 14, 18, 25–27, 30, 31].
    *   Several references that were preprints at the time of writing have since been published and should be updated ([9], [17], [19], [32]).

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong paper presenting a novel and significant theoretical result. The technical contributions are solid, and the work successfully combines ideas from kernel methods and deep learning to provide important insights into the stability of convolutional architectures. The weaknesses are minor and primarily relate to the presentation and motivation, all of which can be addressed in a revised version. I recommend acceptance.
