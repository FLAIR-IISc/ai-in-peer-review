

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper investigates the theoretical foundations of deep convolutional representations, focusing on their invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16] and establish a connection between this kernel-based approach and standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs). Their work builds upon the scattering transform [5, 17] but extends it to more general CNN architectures by studying the geometry of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The paper demonstrates that the signal representation preserves information while achieving stability, and shows how these properties transfer to predictive models through RKHS norm control.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound theoretical framework with rigorous mathematical proofs. The authors carefully establish stability guarantees under reasonable assumptions (A1-A3) and extend these results to CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The proofs in Appendix C are thorough and well-structured. However, the paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical claims, which is a significant limitation for a NIPS submission. While theoretical work is valuable, the absence of experiments to demonstrate the invariance and stability properties in practice weakens the contribution's impact. The paper would benefit from empirical results showing how these theoretical properties manifest in real-world tasks.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear mathematical notation and logical organization. The authors provide precise definitions of key concepts (e.g., patch extraction, kernel mapping, pooling operators) and carefully explain their connections to existing work. The transition from theoretical framework to practical implications is smooth and accessible to readers familiar with kernel methods and CNNs. The paper could be even more accessible with a brief example illustrating the multilayer construction, but this is a minor point.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of CNNs by providing a unified framework that bridges kernel methods and CNNs. While connections between kernel methods and CNNs have been explored previously (e.g., [2, 3, 15, 16]), this paper offers a more comprehensive theoretical analysis that specifically addresses stability and invariance properties. The extension of stability guarantees from the scattering transform to a broader class of CNN architectures represents a meaningful advancement. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by focusing on the RKHS geometry of representations rather than just the network architecture.

**Significance:** The theoretical insights have potential significance for understanding why CNNs work well in practice and for developing better regularization techniques. The connection between RKHS norm and stability/generalization is particularly valuable, as it suggests principled ways to control model complexity. However, without empirical validation, it's difficult to assess the practical impact of these theoretical results. The paper would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating how these theoretical properties translate to improved performance on standard vision tasks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides a novel theoretical framework connecting kernel methods and CNNs
- Rigorous mathematical analysis of stability and invariance properties
- Extends the scattering transform's stability guarantees to a broader class of CNN architectures
- Well-written and technically sound with clear connections to prior work
- Establishes a principled link between stability and generalization through RKHS norms

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Lacks empirical validation of theoretical claims (critical for a NIPS submission)
- Does not provide experimental results demonstrating the invariance and stability properties in practice
- The connection between the theoretical framework and practical CNN implementations could be strengthened with empirical evidence
- The open question about kernel approximations (random projection, Fourier features) is not addressed, which is a significant practical consideration for implementation

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, but with a strong caveat that the authors must provide empirical validation of their theoretical claims. The theoretical contribution is strong and significant, but the absence of experiments is a serious weakness for a NIPS paper. I would encourage the authors to include at least a small set of experiments demonstrating the invariance properties under controlled transformations (e.g., rotations, translations, small deformations) for both CKNs and CNNs.

**Specific Suggestions:**
1. Add a small empirical section showing stability to controlled deformations using standard datasets.
2. Compare the invariance properties of CKNs and standard CNNs under the same transformation conditions.
3. Address the open question about kernel approximations (random projection, Fourier features) and their impact on stability.
4. Consider adding a brief discussion of how these theoretical insights might translate to improved regularization techniques in practice.

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding deep convolutional representations. With empirical validation, it would be a strong candidate for NIPS. As it stands, the theoretical framework is compelling but lacks the practical grounding expected for a NIPS submission. I believe the authors can easily address this limitation with additional experiments.
