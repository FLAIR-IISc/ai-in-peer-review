- Defines a multilayer convolutional RKHS representation that generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks via patch extraction, kernel mappings, and pooling.
- Proves recoverability of signals from sampled feature maps on continuous domains.
- Shows Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms and increased translation invariance with larger pooling scale.
- Extends results to global invariance for general transformation groups using group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling.
- Proves the induced RKHS contains a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, allowing stability guarantees to transfer to these CNNs via RKHS-norm control.
- Discusses discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations, arguing stability is preserved by non-expansive projections.
- Positions work between scattering networks and conventional/group-equivariant CNNs, building on CKNs while adding RKHS-based stability analysis.
- Technical development is careful and largely sound; stability bound follows from operator-theoretic decomposition and commutator bounds.
- Group invariance treatment is clean and general; inclusion of CNNs in RKHS is useful and makes prediction stability explicit.
- Main limitation: no empirical validation of invariance/stability or discretization trade-offs.
- Paper is well written and organized with explicit assumptions and helpful continuous/discrete links.
- Suggestion: state constants and their dependence (e.g., κ^(d+1)) more prominently and discuss practical regimes.
- Suggestion: make accumulation of approximation error across layers explicit in Section 3.2.
- Suggestion: clarify the range of activations in Proposition 7 and give concrete kernels/activation examples with Cσ values.
- Original contributions: extends scattering-style stability to learnable multilayer kernels, links group equivariance to RKHS geometry, and provides novel discretization/recoverability and shifted-pooling commutator analysis.
- Potential significance: could inform why conv architectures generalize, guide architecture/regularizer design, and offer theoretically grounded alternatives to CNNs if empirically validated.
- Strengths: rigorous multilayer kernel analysis, transfer of stability to CNNs, general group-equivariant framework, and thoughtful discretization treatment.
- Weaknesses: lack of experiments, exclusion of standard ReLU (requires smoothed variants), possibly pessimistic dependence of constants on κ/dimension/depth, and unquantified effects of non-projection kernel approximations.
- Suggested experiments: demonstrate translation invariance vs pooling scale, stability to small diffeomorphisms on synthetic deformations, and comparisons to scattering and CNN baselines; correlate prediction stability with RKHS norm.
- Suggested theory work: quantify Cσ for smoothed-ReLU under common kernels, give practical bounds on ‖fσ‖, and provide numerical estimates for constants in 2D with typical κ/σ schedules.
- Suggestion on approximations: clarify how random features or RFF/Nyström can be incorporated—non-expansive projections preserve bounds; RFF needs rescaling or tracking Lipschitz growth across layers.
- Suggestion on discretization: discuss boundary effects, stability of deconvolution as σk varies, and guidance on sampling/grid choices in modern CNN regimes.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong, well-executed theoretical advance linking stability, invariance, and generalization in a learnable kernel framework with CNN connections.
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of empirical evidence, restrictive activation assumptions, and potentially loose/opaque constants without quantitative guidance.
- Overall recommendation: accept if concise experiments validating main claims and clarification on kernel approximations are added.
