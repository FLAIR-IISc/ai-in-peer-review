- Introduces multilayer convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) with patch extraction, homogeneous dot-product kernel mapping, and Gaussian pooling.  
- Proves near-invariance to transformation groups and Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms when patch sizes and pooling scales are properly coupled.  
- Stability bound scales linearly with deformation magnitude and Jacobian; translation invariance improves with larger final pooling scale.  
- Extends scattering transform theory by replacing wavelet modulus with norm-preserving, non-expansive kernel maps and analyzing the RKHS geometry.  
- Shows a large class of CNNs (with smooth homogeneous activations and normalization) lie in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel (Proposition 7).  
- Transfers invariance/stability properties from the kernel representation to these CNNs via RKHS norm control.  
- Provides discretization results, signal preservation lemmas, and analysis of group-equivariant layers for general locally compact groups.  
- Connects to prior work on group-equivariant CNNs, CKNs, scattering transforms, and kernel/representation theories.  
- Theoretical development is careful, technically sound, and assumptions are explicit and aligned with signal-processing practice.  
- Commutator bounds and depth dependence are well motivated and transparently analyzed.  
- Lack of empirical validation: no experiments to quantify invariance, stability, or practical relevance of constants and approximations.  
- Manuscript is well written, organized, self-contained, and uses consistent notation.  
- Original contribution: generalizes scattering-style stability/invariance to kernelized multilayer settings and transfers results to CNNs via RKHS embeddings.  
- Group-equivariant kernel construction and explicit patch/pooling stability analysis are novel.  
- Results provide principled architectural guidance (patch sizes, pooling scales, group pooling) and RKHS-based regularization insights.  
- Strength: rigorous Lipschitz stability proofs and interpretable constants.  
- Strength: explicit RKHS norm control linking stability and generalization for CKNs and some CNNs.  
- Strength: clear separation of intermediate stability and final global invariance via group-equivariant construction.  
- Strength: careful treatment of discretization and compatibility with finite-dimensional kernel approximations via non-expansive projections.  
- Weakness: no experiments to validate theoretical claims or measure how constants scale with depth/pooling.  
- Weakness: mapping CNNs into RKHS requires smooth homogeneous activations and normalization, which deviate from common practice (e.g., raw ReLU).  
- Weakness: limited discussion on how kernel approximation errors (subspace projection, Nyström, RFF) affect stability bounds.  
- Empirical requests: measure representation stability under controlled diffeomorphisms, test invariance to rotations with group pooling, compare CKNs/scattering/CNNs under deformations, and study kernel approximation effects.  
- Open question: can random projections or finite kernel mappings preserve stability, and how do approximation errors enter bounds (e.g., additive εapprox terms)?  
- Additional questions: is the (1 + n) depth dependence tight, robustness of stable deconvolution to noise/boundary effects, concrete smoothed-ReLU example, sensitivity to normalization, and relation between RKHS norm proxies and empirical robustness.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong general theoretical contributions, clear architectural guidance, and principled bridge between kernel methods and deep learning.  
- Cons for acceptance: absence of experiments, practical gaps for standard activations, and missing analysis of common kernel approximations.  
- Overall: technically solid and timely theory with clear impact potential but major shortcoming due to lack of empirical evidence; acceptance favored if experiments and approximation studies are added for camera-ready.
