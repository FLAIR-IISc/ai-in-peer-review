This paper investigates the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and their connection to more standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating that CKNs, and by extension a broad class of CNNs, can be nearly invariant to groups of transformations and stable to diffeomorphisms under appropriate design choices for patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operations. The work builds upon existing theoretical frameworks for analyzing CNNs, such as the scattering transform, and extends them to a more general kernel-based setting.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the behavior of deep networks – namely, understanding the geometry of the functional spaces they inhabit. The authors clearly articulate the motivation for their work, relating it to regularization and improved sample complexity. The technical presentation is strong, with clear definitions and careful mathematical derivations. The connection to related work is comprehensive, referencing relevant publications in kernel methods, scattering transforms, and group-invariant representations. The authors adeptly leverage tools from harmonic analysis and kernel theory, demonstrating a strong grasp of the underlying mathematical principles. The extension of the analysis from CKNs to a broader class of CNNs is a significant contribution, suggesting that the theoretical insights gained from the kernel framework can be applied to more practical network architectures.

A key strength of this paper lies in its theoretical rigor. The authors establish clear assumptions and derive precise bounds on stability and invariance. The use of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs) provides a powerful framework for analyzing the properties of the representations learned by these networks. The treatment of diffeomorphic deformations and group invariance is particularly well-done, drawing on existing results from the literature while extending them to the kernel setting. The discussion of how kernel approximations, such as those used in CKNs, impact the stability properties is also insightful.

However, the most significant weakness of this paper is the complete absence of empirical validation. While the theoretical results are compelling, they remain untested on real-world data. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not offer any discussion of potential experiments. It would be highly beneficial to see experimental results demonstrating the invariance and stability properties of CKNs, or CNNs, under transformations and diffeomorphisms, even in a limited setting. For example, visualizing the feature maps learned by a CKN under different deformations or measuring the network's performance on a dataset with augmented transformations would provide valuable support for the theoretical claims. Without such validation, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of the results.

Another potential area for future work, touched upon briefly in the discussion of kernel approximations, is exploring alternative approximation schemes. The authors mention the use of finite-dimensional subspaces. Exploring techniques like random projections or explicit kernel mapping via a finite number of Fourier features could provide practical alternatives, and it would be interesting to investigate how these approximations affect the stability guarantees.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations.
*   **Novelty:** The kernel-based approach offers a novel perspective on understanding the behavior of CNNs.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical derivations are clear and well-justified.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper builds upon and extends existing theoretical frameworks in the field.
*   **Potential Impact:** The insights gained from this work could lead to improved regularization techniques and a better understanding of generalization in deep learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of experimental results significantly weakens the paper's impact.
*   **Limited Practical Guidance:** While theoretically sound, it's unclear how the results translate into practical improvements for CNN training or architecture design without experimental verification.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of empirical validation, this paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of deep convolutional representations. The rigorous analysis and novel kernel-based approach are commendable. However, the absence of experiments is a significant drawback. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept** with the strong encouragement that the authors address the lack of empirical validation in a future version of the work. Adding even a limited set of experiments would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field. The theoretical contributions are solid enough to warrant acceptance, but the paper would be much more compelling with supporting experimental evidence.
