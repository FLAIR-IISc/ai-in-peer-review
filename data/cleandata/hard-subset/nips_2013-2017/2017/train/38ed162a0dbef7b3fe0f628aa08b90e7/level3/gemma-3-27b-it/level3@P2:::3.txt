This paper presents a compelling theoretical analysis of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by framing them within the rich mathematical context of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors demonstrate that a broad class of CNNs can be understood as operating on a functional space derived from a multilayer convolutional kernel, inheriting properties like stability to diffeomorphisms and approximate group invariance, mirroring results previously established for scattering transforms. The work is technically sound, clearly written, and builds logically upon prior work in convolutional kernel networks, harmonic analysis, and kernel methods. Overall, I believe this is a strong contribution to NIPS and recommend acceptance.

The core idea is to move beyond treating CNNs as “black boxes” and instead provide a principled framework for understanding their behavior through the lens of kernel methods. By carefully constructing a multilayer convolutional kernel, the authors establish a connection between CNNs and a well-understood mathematical structure, allowing them to leverage the existing theory of RKHS to analyze properties like stability and invariance. This is particularly valuable as these properties are increasingly recognized as crucial for generalization and robustness in deep learning. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Mathematical Analysis:** The paper’s strength lies in its detailed and rigorous mathematical treatment. Proofs are well-structured, and the assumptions are clearly stated.
*   **Novel Perspective:** Reinterpreting CNNs within the RKHS framework provides a fresh perspective and opens up possibilities for applying kernel methods techniques to analyze and improve CNNs.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors carefully position their work within the existing literature, drawing connections to scattering transforms, convolutional kernel networks, and related approaches.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with all the underlying mathematical concepts.
*   **Important Implications:** The demonstrated stability properties have direct implications for understanding generalization bounds and sample complexity.

**Weaknesses and Questions:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified to further enhance its impact. My primary concern revolves around the motivation for this particular reformulation. While technically elegant, the paper doesn’t fully articulate *why* representing CNNs as multilayer kernels is inherently useful. What specific advantages does this perspective offer that weren't available before? Does it lead to novel algorithms, improved generalization bounds, or more efficient approximation schemes? Addressing this would significantly strengthen the paper.

The relationship to prior work on "Convolutional Kernel Networks" (Mairal et al.) warrants further clarification. The authors acknowledge this work but don't clearly delineate the novelty of their contributions beyond a more general treatment and extension to standard CNN architectures. A more explicit comparison outlining the key differences and advancements would be valuable.

The emphasis on preserving "all signal information" seems potentially misplaced. Many real-world tasks require only task-relevant information, and discarding irrelevant details can actually improve performance and robustness. It would be helpful to discuss this trade-off and explain why preserving the full signal representation is crucial in the context of their theoretical analysis.

The paper would benefit from a discussion of connections to existing invariant kernel approaches, particularly the work of Hans Burkhardt and others on invariant kernel functions. This would help position the work within the broader landscape of kernel-based methods for achieving invariance.

Finally, the authors briefly mention achieving equivariance to group actions. It would be interesting to explore whether any general condition on an operator (beyond simply commuting with all group elements) guarantees equivariance for DCNNs, potentially providing a characterization of operators suitable for building equivariant networks.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for understanding CNNs.
*   Establishes a clear connection between CNNs and the well-developed theory of RKHS.
*   Demonstrates important stability properties with implications for generalization.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Potentially opens up new avenues for research in kernel methods for deep learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Motivation for the reformulation could be stronger.
*   Novelty relative to convolutional kernel networks needs further clarification.
*   The focus on preserving all signal information could be refined.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite these minor weaknesses, this is a valuable and well-executed piece of research. The authors have made a significant contribution to our theoretical understanding of CNNs. I believe the potential benefits of this work outweigh the remaining concerns and recommend acceptance to NIPS 2017. I look forward to seeing how this work inspires future research in this area.
