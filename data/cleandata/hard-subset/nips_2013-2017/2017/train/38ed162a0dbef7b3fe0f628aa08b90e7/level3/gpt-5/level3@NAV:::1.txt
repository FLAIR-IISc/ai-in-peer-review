Summary and relation to prior work: This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation whose patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling yield near-invariance to transformation groups and Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors prove bounds of the form C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn and show how equivariance/invariance to general groups emerges via group-covariant layers and a final pooling stage. Importantly, they embed a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations into the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, so the stability and invariance properties transfer to these CNNs when the RKHS norm is controlled. The work generalizes scattering theory (Bruna & Mallat) from fixed wavelets to learned kernel mappings/CKNs (Mairal et al.) and connects to group-equivariant architectures (Cohen & Welling) and kernel approximations used in large-scale learning.

Quality: Technically solid and careful. Assumptions (A1–A3) are explicit; the operator commutator analysis is rigorous; discretization and finite-dimensional CKN projections preserve non-expansiveness. The main limitation is the absence of empirical validation to corroborate the theoretical constants, trade-offs, and practical impact on modern CNNs.

Clarity: The manuscript is well written and well organized. The links between continuous-domain analysis, discretization, and practical CKNs/CNNs are clearly explained. A brief discussion quantifying how constants depend on kernel choices and patch/pooling schedules would further aid practitioners.

Originality: The RKHS-based pathway to transfer scattering-style stability to more general convolutional architectures is novel and timely. The inclusion of CNNs within the RKHS with norm-based control is an elegant bridge between kernel theory and deep learning.

Significance: The results could influence how invariance and stability are designed and regularized in deep models and motivate principled choices of pooling/patch geometry. With empirical support, the impact would be substantial.

Requests for experiments: Please add experiments that (i) measure representation distance under controlled translations, rotations, and small diffeomorphisms on synthetic data and datasets such as MNIST/CIFAR with elastic deformations; (ii) ablate patch size and pooling scale σk to verify the dependence in Theorem 6; (iii) compare CKNs vs CNN baselines under identical training; (iv) evaluate classification robustness and sample complexity.

Open question: Could random projections or explicit kernel approximations (e.g., Nyström, random Maclaurin for dot-product kernels, or Fourier/spherical features where applicable) replace exact mappings while preserving stability? Can the theory bound the additional approximation error and its effect on constants C1, C2?

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, general theoretical framework unifying kernels and CNNs
- Clear extension of scattering ideas with broader applicability
- Useful guidance on architecture design and regularization

Cons for acceptance:
- No empirical validation
- Practical implications of constants and approximations not quantified
- Assumptions on smooth activations may limit immediate applicability to standard ReLU CNNs without smoothing adjustments
