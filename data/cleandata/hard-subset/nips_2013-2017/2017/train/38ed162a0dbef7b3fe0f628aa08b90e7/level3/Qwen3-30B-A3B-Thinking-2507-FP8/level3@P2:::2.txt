

# Review for "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper studies theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on stability to diffeomorphisms and invariance to group transformations. The authors build upon the convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) framework [15,16] and extend Mallat's scattering transform analysis [17] to a broader class of convolutional architectures. The paper demonstrates that representations constructed from multilayer convolutional kernels with norm-preserving, non-expansive kernels (satisfying assumptions A1-A3) enjoy Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms, with translation invariance controlled by the final pooling layer scale. The authors further connect their kernel framework to standard CNNs with homogeneous activation functions, showing that stability properties apply to this broader class.

**Quality**: The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with rigorous proofs. The stability analysis generalizes Mallat's work [17] to more standard convolutional architectures, providing a theoretical foundation for understanding why convolutional networks exhibit desirable stability properties. The connection between the kernel framework and CNNs (Section 5) is particularly strong, showing that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization. The paper carefully addresses the trade-offs between stability and information preservation, acknowledging that pooling layers reduce signal energy but that natural signals often have significant energy in low-frequency components. The analysis is comprehensive, covering both continuous and discrete representations, and the discretization approach in Section 2.1 is well-motivated.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical presentation is precise, with sufficient detail to allow reproduction of the theoretical results. The authors effectively use figures (e.g., Figure 1) to illustrate the multilayer construction. The main contributions are clearly articulated in the introduction, and the notation is consistently applied throughout. However, there are a few minor issues: the kernel function κ is overloaded in assumption (A3) and in the definition of the kernel function in Equation (10), which could confuse readers. Additionally, the feature map φ is not defined at line 416 (where it's used in the proof of Lemma 4), and should be introduced via Equation (10) as the feature map for kernel K.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending stability analysis from the scattering transform [17] to a broader class of convolutional architectures, including standard CNNs with homogeneous activation functions. While the CKN framework [15,16] and scattering transform [5,17] are known, the authors provide a unified theoretical treatment that bridges these approaches. The extension to group invariance in Section 4 is a valuable addition, though it's somewhat sketchy compared to the main stability results. The work is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers on invariance, such as [6,9,17,22], by providing a more general theoretical framework applicable to standard CNNs.

**Significance**: The results have clear significance for the ML community. Understanding the stability properties of deep representations provides theoretical justification for the empirical success of convolutional architectures, and the connection to generalization (via RKHS norm control) offers practical insights for regularization. The paper's main contribution—proving stability for a broad class of convolutional architectures—addresses a fundamental question about why these models work well. However, the motivation for the stability analysis could be strengthened; the current connection to adversarial robustness (line 110) is only mentioned in a single sentence, while the paper's technical contribution is much broader.

**Minor issues**: 
1. The inequality around lines 427-428 appears to hold with equality, and the expression ||z|| - ||z|| should be ||z|| - ||z'||.
2. The second constraint in the displayed equation between lines 384-385 is redundant due to kernel symmetry.
3. The bibliography has several issues: page information is missing for references [3,6,8,13,14,18,25-27,30,31]; references [9,17,19,32] need updated publication details.

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- Strong theoretical contribution that extends previous work on stability
- Clear connection between kernel methods and standard CNNs
- Well-written, technically sound, and comprehensive analysis
- Addresses a fundamental question about convolutional architectures

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- Weak motivation for the stability analysis beyond a single sentence
- Minor but noticeable notation issues that could confuse readers
- Some minor technical errors in the proofs

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in understanding the properties of convolutional representations. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, with the main weakness being the insufficient motivation for the stability analysis. The authors should strengthen the connection between stability and practical ML challenges, particularly in the introduction and discussion. The minor technical issues noted above are easily fixable and don't undermine the core contribution.

I recommend acceptance, as the theoretical contribution is substantial, well-executed, and addresses a fundamental question in deep learning theory. The paper provides a valuable theoretical foundation that will benefit both researchers and practitioners working with convolutional architectures.
