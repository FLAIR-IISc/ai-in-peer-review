This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations through the lens of multilayer convolutional kernels and their associated Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). Building upon the framework of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), the authors construct a deep representation that is shown to be stable to the action of small diffeomorphisms and nearly invariant to translations. This work extends the stability analysis pioneered for scattering transforms to a broader class of models. The framework is also generalized to achieve equivariance and invariance to the actions of arbitrary compact groups. A key contribution is establishing a formal link between this kernel construction and a class of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) that use smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This connection allows the stability and invariance properties derived for the kernel representation to be directly applied to these CNNs, linking their stability to the RKHS norm of the learned function.

**Strengths**

*   **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The paper is technically sound, providing a rigorous mathematical treatment of convolutional representations. The theoretical claims regarding stability and invariance are well-supported by detailed proofs that build upon established techniques from harmonic analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. It carefully builds the multilayer kernel construction step-by-step, defines the core concepts of stability and invariance, and logically connects the kernel framework to practical CNNs. This clarity makes a complex theoretical subject accessible.
*   **Significance:** This work provides a valuable theoretical framework for understanding fundamental properties of CNNs. The analysis of stability with respect to diffeomorphisms formalizes the intuition that these models are robust to small deformations of the input. The link established between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization (Section 5) is particularly insightful, suggesting that controlling the function's norm is key to achieving both robustness and good generalization performance.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

The paper is strong, but its impact could be enhanced by addressing the following points:

1.  **Motivation and Utility of the RKHS Reformulation:** The main contribution can be viewed as an elegant reformulation of CNN properties within an RKHS context. To strengthen the paper's significance, it would be beneficial to more explicitly articulate the practical or theoretical advantages of this perspective. For instance, does this framework suggest new regularization strategies based on the RKHS norm that could outperform existing ones? Does it offer insights into architecture design or lead to tighter, more meaningful generalization bounds for CNNs? The discussion at the end of Section 5 is a good start but could be expanded upon.

2.  **Novelty with Respect to Prior Work:**
    *   **Convolutional Kernel Networks:** The paper is a direct and important extension of the CKN framework [15, 16]. However, the paper could be clearer in delineating the novel contributions here versus what was already present in the original CKN papers. Is the primary novelty the stability/invariance analysis, or are there also novel aspects to the kernel construction itself? A more direct comparison would help readers understand the specific advances made in this work.
    *   **Invariant Kernels:** The paper cites prior work on invariant kernels (e.g., [13]). It would be helpful to include a more detailed discussion on how the proposed multilayer, learned-feature approach relates to, and differs from, these classic methods that typically design a single, fixed invariant kernel.

3.  **Emphasis on Signal Preservation:** Lemma 1 and 2 establish that the representation can preserve all signal information. This is presented as a desirable property. However, for many supervised learning tasks like classification, the objective is often to build a representation that is selective and discards nuisance information irrelevant to the task. Could the authors comment on the motivation for preserving the full signal? Is the goal to learn a general-purpose representation that can then be used for multiple tasks via a simple linear classifier?

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Provides a rigorous and elegant theoretical framework for understanding the crucial properties of stability and invariance in CNNs.
    *   The paper is technically sound and very clearly written.
    *   The extension of stability analysis from scattering transforms to a class of CKNs and CNNs is a significant theoretical contribution.
    *   The work forges an important conceptual link between the RKHS norm of a network, its stability to deformations, and its generalization capacity.

*   **Con:**
    *   The practical implications and novel insights derived from the RKHS reformulation could be made more explicit.
    *   The novelty with respect to prior CKN literature could be more sharply defined.
    *   The motivation for preserving all signal information, a key feature of the representation, is not fully justified in the context of discriminative tasks.

**Recommendation**

I recommend accepting this paper. It is a high-quality, rigorous, and well-written theoretical contribution that deepens our understanding of deep convolutional architectures. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to framing and motivation, and addressing them in the final version would further strengthen an already excellent paper.
