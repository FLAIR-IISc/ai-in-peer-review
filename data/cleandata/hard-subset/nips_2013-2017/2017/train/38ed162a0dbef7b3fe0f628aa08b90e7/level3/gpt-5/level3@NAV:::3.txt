Summary and relation to prior work: This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation whose RKHS functional space contains a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations. The authors prove near-invariance to groups via pooling on the group and Lipschitz stability to C1-diffeomorphisms with constants that separate the roles of local deformation (∥∇τ∥∞) and global translation (∥τ∥∞/σn). The construction generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs; Mairal et al.) from discrete to continuous domains, gives a principled discretization/approximation path, and connects to the scattering transform (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) by extending stability arguments to more general kernels/CNNs. Links to group-equivariant architectures (Cohen & Welling) and invariant kernels (Haasdonk & Burkhardt) are touched upon.

Quality: The paper is technically strong and careful. Assumptions (A1–A3) are clear; proofs build on harmonic analysis tools (commutator bounds, Schur’s test) and are consistent with the scattering literature. The RKHS embedding of CNNs (Prop. 7) is well motivated and ties norm control to stability/generalization.

Clarity: The exposition is clean and self-contained, with a useful continuous-to-discrete bridge. I encourage the authors to more explicitly summarize assumptions and constants affecting stability, and to foreground what parts of the analysis extend beyond scattering/CKNs.

Originality: The main contribution is a rigorous reformulation of DCNNs as multilayer kernels with stability guarantees, unifying CNNs and CKNs in an RKHS perspective. Please clarify novelty beyond a pure RKHS reinterpretation: What stability or invariance statements were not available for CKNs/scattering or group-equivariant CNNs?

Significance: The work advances theoretical understanding of invariance/stability in learned conv architectures and suggests avenues for norm-based generalization bounds. It is likely to be influential for theory-oriented researchers and method designers.

Requests for clarification:
- Usefulness of the RKHS reformulation: Can it yield concrete generalization bounds (e.g., margin/Rademacher via ∥f∥H) or computational/approximation advantages over standard CNNs?
- Relation to Convolutional Kernel Networks: What new guarantees are obtained versus Mairal et al. (2014, 2016), especially under kernel approximation and finite-dimensional projections?
- Relation to invariant-kernel work (Haasdonk & Burkhardt) and orbit embeddings: Can your group pooling be seen as constructing invariant kernels, and how do your stability constants compare?
- “No loss of signal information”: Why is preserving full information preferable when many tasks require only task-relevant sufficiency? Can you discuss trade-offs with sufficiency/compression?
- Equivariance characterization: Beyond convolution/pooling, can you characterize all (bounded linear) operators that commute with the group action (intertwiners)? Does commuting with all group elements suffice for DCNN equivariance in your framework?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Rigorous, well-written theory; meaningful unification of kernels and CNNs.
- Clear stability and group-invariance results with practical discretization guidance.
- Insightful RKHS link suggesting norm–generalization connections.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Novelty relative to CKNs and invariant kernels could be sharpened.
- Limited discussion of practical implications and empirical validation.

Recommendation: Accept.
