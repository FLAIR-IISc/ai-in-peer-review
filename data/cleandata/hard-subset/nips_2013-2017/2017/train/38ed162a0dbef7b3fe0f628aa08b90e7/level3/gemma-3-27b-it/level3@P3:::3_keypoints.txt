- Reformulates DCNNs in an RKHS framework, proving approximate group invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms; reviewer recommends acceptance.  
- Core contribution is the RKHS reformulation, which is elegant and mathematically solid but needs clearer articulation of its practical value.  
- Paper should specify implications for generalization bounds or computational/approximation advantages (e.g., RKHS norm control, kernel-based optimization).  
- Need a clearer delineation of novelty relative to Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs): extension to broader architectures vs. new theoretical insights.  
- Questions the emphasis on “not losing signal information”; preserving all signal may be unnecessary or even harmful for generalization without justification.  
- Recommends deeper discussion of connections to invariant kernel approaches, notably Haasdonk & Burkhardt (2006), and how multilayer structure changes achievable invariances.  
- Asks whether any operator commuting with group elements guarantees DCNN equivariance and whether the work can characterize such operators for arbitrary groups.  
- Strength: high technical rigor with clear definitions and proofs.  
- Strength: RKHS viewpoint provides a novel theoretical perspective on DCNNs.  
- Strength: Strong results on stability to diffeomorphisms and approximate group invariance.  
- Strength: Builds on and connects to existing work in kernel methods, scattering transforms, and CNNs.  
- Strength: Clear and well-organized presentation.  
- Weakness: Lack of explicit motivation for RKHS reformulation beyond gaining intuition.  
- Weakness: Insufficiently clear novelty compared to prior CKN work.  
- Weakness: Unjustified claim about preserving all signal information and its practical relevance.  
- Weakness: Missing detailed comparisons to Burkhardt’s invariant kernel literature.  
- Weakness: Does not explore theoretical extension to characterize equivariance-ensuring operators.  
- Arguments for acceptance: rigorous, novel theoretical analysis addressing foundational questions; significant stability/invariance results; well-presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak explicit motivation for RKHS approach and unclear novelty relative to CKNs.
