

The paper "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations" makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding deep convolutional networks by establishing a formal connection between convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), group invariance, and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel framework from CKNs [15, 16] to continuous domains and demonstrate how this construction yields representations that are nearly invariant to transformation groups while maintaining stability to diffeomorphisms. This work builds upon Mallat's scattering transform [5, 17] by providing a kernel-based framework that encompasses standard CNN architectures rather than being limited to fixed filters.

The main technical contribution is the formal proof of stability properties under diffeomorphisms (Theorem 6), showing that the representation Φ(Lτx) - Φ(x) is bounded by (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2/σn‖τ‖∞)‖x‖, where σn is the final pooling scale. This formalizes the intuition that deeper networks with larger pooling scales achieve greater invariance to small deformations. Crucially, the paper establishes that these stability properties extend to standard CNNs with homogeneous activation functions (Section 5), demonstrating that CNNs with appropriate smooth activations can be embedded in the RKHS space defined by their kernel construction. This bridges a significant gap between theoretical frameworks and practical architectures.

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The mathematical development is rigorous, with well-motivated assumptions, precise definitions, and careful proofs (all relegated to appendices as appropriate for theoretical work). The writing is exceptionally clear, with excellent connections to related work throughout, including proper contextualization relative to Mallat's scattering transform [17], CKNs [15, 16], and group invariance literature [9, 22]. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches and position their work as a meaningful extension.

The originality is substantial. While scattering networks [5, 17] and CKNs [15, 16] have been studied separately, this paper provides a unified theoretical framework that explains how both achieve stability properties. The key insight—that stability properties of CKNs extend to standard CNNs with homogeneous activation functions—represents a significant step toward understanding the theoretical foundations of deep learning. The paper also properly references all relevant prior work, including the recent arXiv preprint by Bietti and Mairal [22] on group invariance.

The significance is high. The work provides a theoretical foundation for why convolutional networks perform well, particularly regarding their invariance properties. The connection between stability and generalization (via RKHS norm control) has direct implications for model design and regularization strategies. The paper demonstrates how stability may lead to improved sample complexity, which is particularly valuable for practitioners seeking to build more robust models.

However, the paper has a major weakness: it lacks empirical validation of the theoretical claims. While the theoretical results are compelling, the absence of experiments demonstrating CKNs' invariance to transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms under the proposed settings is a significant gap. The reviewer requests experimental results on standard datasets showing that CKNs (or CNNs with appropriate activations) indeed exhibit the claimed properties under various transformations and deformations.

Another minor concern is the practical implementation requirement for homogeneous activation functions. While the authors provide a smoothed version of ReLU that satisfies their conditions, it's unclear how this would compare to standard ReLU in practice. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of this requirement.

The paper raises an interesting open question about kernel approximations: "Could random projection or explicit kernel mapping via a finite number of Fourier features serve as kernel approximations, and how would they affect stability?" This is a valuable direction for future work that could bridge the gap between theory and practice.

In summary, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with excellent technical quality, clarity, and originality. The significance is substantial, but the lack of empirical validation represents a notable weakness. The theoretical results are compelling, but without experimental demonstration of the practical implications, the contribution is less complete than it could be.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution, rigorous proofs, clear writing, significant connection between CKNs and CNNs, important implications for stability and generalization.

Con: No empirical validation of theoretical claims, unclear practical implementation details for standard CNNs, limited discussion of computational aspects.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by adding empirical experiments that demonstrate the claimed stability properties. With such experiments, this would be a strong candidate for acceptance. Without them, the theoretical contribution is valuable but lacks the practical validation that would make it a more complete contribution to the field.
