- Presents a multilayer convolutional kernel construction (patch extraction → kernel mapping → pooling) and analyzes the associated functional space in RKHS.  
- Proves stability to small diffeomorphisms and near-invariance to translations and general transformation groups.  
- Shows the framework encompasses Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) and certain CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activations, extending stability/invariance to these models.  
- Builds on Mallat’s scattering formalism and extends it to a broader, learnable kernel-based setting.  
- Strength: technically rigorous construction and proofs, with detailed derivations in the appendix.  
- Strength: clearly written and well-organized presentation that makes complex material accessible.  
- Strength: significant unifying theoretical framework linking kernel methods, CKNs, and CNNs, with insights on RKHS norm, stability, and generalization.  
- Weakness: unclear practical impact of the RKHS reformulation—needs articulation of downstream benefits (architecture design, regularization, bounds, computational gains).  
- Weakness: novelty relative to prior CKN work is not fully delineated—clarify what is fundamentally new beyond applying stability analysis.  
- Weakness: relation to earlier invariant-kernel literature (e.g., Haasdonk & Burkhardt) requires deeper comparison to highlight originality.  
- Weakness: motivation for proving signal-preservation is unclear given that discriminative tasks often require discarding nuisance variability; discuss the trade-off.  
- Weakness: equivariant operator construction is constructive but lacks discussion on whether the provided conditions are necessary and sufficient for equivariance.  
- Conclusion: overall strong, recommend acceptance provided authors clarify the broader significance and novelty and address the listed questions.
