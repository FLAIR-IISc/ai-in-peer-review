- Paper studies theoretical foundations of deep convolutional representations, focusing on invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms.  
- Generalizes the multilayer kernel framework from CKNs and connects this kernel-based view to standard CNNs.  
- Builds on the scattering transform and extends stability results to more general CNN architectures via RKHS geometry.  
- Shows the representation preserves information while achieving stability and transfers these properties to predictors through RKHS norm control.  

- Technical quality is high with rigorous proofs and well-established stability guarantees under assumptions A1–A3.  
- Major limitation: no empirical validation of the theoretical claims, weakening practical impact for a NIPS submission.  

- Clarity is excellent with precise definitions and clear notation; one brief multilayer example would improve accessibility.  

- Original and significant theoretical contribution: unifies kernel methods and CNNs, extends scattering stability to broader CNN classes, and focuses on RKHS geometry rather than architecture.  

- Theoretical insights could inform why CNNs work and suggest principled regularization via RKHS norms, but practical impact is unclear without experiments.  

- Arguments for acceptance: novel kernel–CNN framework, rigorous analysis of stability/invariance, extension of scattering results, clear writing, and link between stability and generalization.  

- Arguments against acceptance: absence of experiments demonstrating invariance/stability, weak connection to practical CNN implementations, and unaddressed questions on kernel approximations (e.g., random projection, Fourier features).  

- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on adding empirical validation showing invariance/stability in practice.  

- Specific suggestion 1: add experiments demonstrating stability to controlled deformations on standard datasets.  
- Specific suggestion 2: compare invariance properties of CKNs and standard CNNs under the same transformations.  
- Specific suggestion 3: address kernel approximation methods and their impact on stability.  
- Specific suggestion 4: discuss how theoretical insights might translate into practical regularization techniques.
