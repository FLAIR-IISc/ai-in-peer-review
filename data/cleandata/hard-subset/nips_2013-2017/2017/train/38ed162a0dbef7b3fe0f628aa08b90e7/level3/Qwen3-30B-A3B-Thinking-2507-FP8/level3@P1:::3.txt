

This paper studies deep convolutional representations through the lens of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), generalizing the multilayer kernel framework of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15,16] to establish theoretical guarantees for stability to diffeomorphisms and group invariance. Building on Mallat's scattering transform [17], the authors prove that their functional space contains standard CNNs with homogeneous activations (e.g., smoothed ReLU variants), demonstrating that stability properties—controlled by the RKHS norm—directly impact generalization. The work extends CKNs to continuous domains while addressing discretization and kernel approximation, bridging theoretical analysis with practical CNN implementations. The paper's core contribution lies in formalizing how convolutional architectures achieve invariance through layered operators (patch extraction, kernel mapping, pooling), with stability bounds (Theorem 6) showing decay in translation-invariance error proportional to the last pooling scale.

**Strengths:** The paper excels in technical rigor and clarity. The proofs are meticulously structured (e.g., Lemma 4, Theorem 6), with precise control over commutator terms and stability constants. The discretization analysis (Section 2.1) effectively connects continuous theory to practice, while the CNN-RKHS correspondence (Proposition 7) provides a concrete bridge between theory and mainstream architectures. The writing is exceptionally clear, with well-motivated notation and a logical flow from abstract setup to practical implications. The work properly contextualizes its relationship to key prior art: Mallat's scattering [17] (for stability), Mairal's CKNs [15,16] (for the kernel framework), and group-invariant approaches [9,17,22] (for invariance). The stability-generalization link (Section 5) is particularly compelling, showing how low RKHS norm implies better generalization—aligning with margin theory for SVMs [27,28].

**Weaknesses:** The primary concern is clarifying novelty relative to existing work. While the paper correctly cites Mairal's CKNs [15], it does not sufficiently distinguish its theoretical advance. The RKHS reformulation of CNNs (Section 5) appears as a natural extension rather than a novel contribution; the authors should explicitly state *why* this perspective yields new insights beyond CKNs [15,16] or the scattering transform [17]. For instance, does the RKHS framework provide tighter generalization bounds, or enable new regularization techniques not possible in prior work? Additionally, the emphasis on "preserving full signal information" (Lemma 1) lacks justification. For classification tasks, retaining non-informative signal components is counterproductive—why not prioritize task-relevant compression? The paper should address when signal preservation is beneficial versus when it hinders efficiency. The connection to invariant kernel methods (e.g., Burkhardt's group-invariant kernels [13]) is superficial; a brief comparison would strengthen the novelty claim. Finally, the characterization of operators ensuring equivariance (e.g., "any operator commuting with group elements") is implied but not rigorously established, leaving a gap in the theoretical foundation.

**Significance and Impact:** The work’s significance lies in providing a unified theoretical foundation for CNN properties long observed empirically. By linking stability to RKHS norm, it offers a principled basis for regularization—e.g., controlling norm to improve sample complexity—which could inform future architectures. While the paper does not yet demonstrate concrete computational advantages over prior methods, the framework is broadly applicable to both CKNs and standard CNNs (Section 5), making it relevant to the broader NIPS community. The stability guarantees also resonate with recent work on adversarial robustness [8], though this connection is not explored.

**Recommendation:** The paper is technically strong, well-written, and addresses a fundamental question about CNNs. The minor weaknesses—primarily around novelty clarification and signal preservation justification—can be resolved with minor revisions. The work advances the field by rigorously formalizing properties that underpin CNN success, moving beyond "black box" intuition. It directly addresses NIPS criteria: **Quality** (sound proofs, clear evaluation), **Clarity** (excellent exposition), **Originality** (novel RKHS perspective on CNNs, though novelty depth requires clarification), and **Significance** (theoretical foundation for stability/generalization). The authors should explicitly: (1) contrast their RKHS framework with Mairal's CKNs [15] to highlight new insights; (2) justify signal preservation in the context of task-specific learning; and (3) briefly engage with Burkhardt's invariant kernels [13]. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution. **Accept**.
