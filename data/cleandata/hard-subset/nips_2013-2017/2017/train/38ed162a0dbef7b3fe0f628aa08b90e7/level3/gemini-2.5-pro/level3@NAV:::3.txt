This paper provides a theoretical framework for analyzing deep convolutional representations by describing their functional spaces as multilayer kernels. Building upon concepts from Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) and the scattering transform, the authors construct a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) that contains a large class of CNNs. Within this framework, they formally prove that these representations are stable to the action of small diffeomorphisms and can achieve near-invariance to groups of transformations, providing a principled understanding of these empirically successful architectures.

The paper is technically sound, rigorous, and very well-written. Its primary strength lies in providing a unified, elegant mathematical lens through which to view both CKNs and a class of conventional CNNs. The theoretical guarantees on stability and invariance are significant, as they move the understanding of these deep learning models beyond pure engineering intuition. By connecting the RKHS norm to the stability of the learned function, the work provides a valuable link between generalization theory and the geometric properties of deep representations, advancing the state of the art in deep learning theory.

While the technical contribution is strong, the authors could further clarify the practical and theoretical implications of their work. The main contribution appears to be a reformulation of DCNNs in an RKHS context. It would be beneficial to explicitly state why this reformulation is useful—does it lead to new generalization bounds, computational advantages, or novel network architectures? The novelty could also be sharpened, particularly regarding what this framework offers beyond a pure RKHS reformulation and how it extends the CKN literature (Mairal et al.).

Furthermore, a few points warrant discussion:
- The paper emphasizes "not losing the signal information," but for many discriminative tasks, discarding task-irrelevant information is precisely the goal. Why is complete signal preservation a desirable property?
- Is there a connection to earlier work on invariant kernels, such as that by Hans Burkhardt and his group?
- On a more theoretical note, could the authors characterize the full class of operators that ensure equivariance? For instance, would any operator that commutes with all group elements suffice?

Despite these requests for clarification, this is an excellent and insightful paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to our understanding of deep convolutional networks. The proposed framework is powerful and the results are important. I strongly recommend acceptance and believe the paper would be even stronger if the authors addressed the points above in their final version.
