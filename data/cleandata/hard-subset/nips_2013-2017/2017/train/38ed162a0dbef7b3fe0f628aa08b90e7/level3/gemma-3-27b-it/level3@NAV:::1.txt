## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper investigates the theoretical foundations of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on deep convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and their relationship to standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors demonstrate that, under appropriate design choices for patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling, CKNs exhibit near-invariance to transformation groups and stability to diffeomorphisms. Crucially, they prove that these properties extend to a broader class of CNNs. This work builds upon previous research in scattering transforms [17] and kernel methods for deep learning [15, 16], seeking to provide a more general framework for understanding these critical properties of deep networks. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides a compelling theoretical analysis. The mathematical formulations are clear and the connections to existing work are well-established.

A significant strength of this work lies in its rigorous mathematical treatment of invariance and stability. By framing the analysis through the lens of positive definite kernels and reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces, the authors are able to provide a nuanced understanding of the conditions under which these properties emerge in convolutional architectures. The extension of these results from CKNs to a wider class of CNNs is also a valuable contribution, potentially offering insights into the generalization capabilities of these networks. The work clearly articulates how choices in kernel design and pooling strategies impact stability and invariance. 

However, the primary weakness of this paper is the complete lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical results are compelling, it remains unclear how these properties manifest in practice. Demonstrating the invariance and stability of CKNs (and, by extension, CNNs) to transformations and diffeomorphisms through experimental results would significantly strengthen the paper. Specifically, showing examples of robustness to image distortions or demonstrating improved performance on datasets with varying transformations would be beneficial.

A pertinent open question raised by this work is the potential for approximating the kernels used in CKNs with more computationally efficient methods. Could random projection or explicit kernel mapping via a finite number of Fourier features serve as viable alternatives, and how would such approximations affect the stability guarantees? Exploring this trade-off between theoretical rigor and practical efficiency would be a valuable avenue for future research. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and rigorous theoretical analysis of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations, filling a gap in our understanding of these fundamental properties. The work is technically sound and well-written, and the connection to existing research is clearly established.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The lack of empirical validation is a significant drawback. Without experimental results, it is difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.



**Overall Score:** 6/10. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors including experimental validation of their theoretical results.
