- Presents a theoretical analysis of invariance and stability for deep convolutional representations by generalizing multilayer CKNs to continuous signals.  
- Constructs representations via patch extraction, RKHS kernel mapping, and linear pooling, proving stability to small diffeomorphisms and near-translation invariance.  
- Extends scattering network formalism and provides Lipschitz stability guarantees similar to scattering transforms.  
- Key contribution: links the kernel-based multilayer framework to conventional CNNs, showing the RKHS contains many CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activations.  
- Shows stability/invariance properties transfer to those CNNs, with the network's RKHS norm controlling stability and suggesting a principled regularizer.  
- Strength: addresses a fundamental and timely theoretical question, unifying CKNs, scattering transforms, and CNNs.  
- Strength: rigorous and technically sound mathematical analysis with well-constructed proofs grounded in harmonic analysis and kernel methods.  
- Strength: exceptionally clear and well-organized manuscript that explains assumptions and connects continuous theory to discrete implementations.  
- Weakness: complete absence of empirical validation—results are developed in an idealized continuous setting without experiments on discrete, modern architectures.  
- Suggested experiments: empirically measure representation stability under controlled deformations and verify Theorem 6 bounds.  
- Suggested experiments: test whether RKHS-norm regularization improves robustness to geometric transformations or adversarial perturbations versus standard regularizers.  
- Question: reliance on smooth homogeneous activations—can the analysis extend to non-smooth ReLU, and how restrictive is this assumption for practical CNNs?  
- Question: could large-scale kernel approximations (random Fourier features, Nyström) be integrated, and how would they affect the stability analysis and bounds?  
- Recommendation: include even simple experiments (e.g., MNIST affine perturbations) to validate theoretical predictions.  
- Overall: high-quality, original theoretical contribution with clear writing; main drawback is lack of experiments, but still a valuable contribution to understanding convolutional models.
