This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations, focusing on their invariance to transformations and stability with respect to the action of diffeomorphisms. The authors build upon the framework of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), extending the construction to signals on continuous domains and deriving a multilayer convolutional kernel. The main contribution is a proof that the resulting signal representation is stable under C1-diffeomorphisms, meaning the L2-norm of the change in the representation is bounded by a function of the diffeomorphism's norm and its Jacobian's norm. This result is analogous to the stability guarantees established for scattering transforms by Mallat (2012), but it is extended here to a broader class of architectures that includes CKNs and, as shown in Section 5, certain types of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with smooth, homogeneous activation functions. The analysis also briefly sketches how this framework can be generalized to achieve equivariance and invariance for actions of locally compact groups beyond simple translations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Technical Quality and Significance:** The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of an important and timely topic. Understanding the theoretical properties of deep networks, such as stability to input deformations, is a fundamental challenge in the field. This work makes a significant contribution by establishing explicit stability bounds for a class of deep convolutional models, linking the level of translation-invariance to the pooling scale of the final layer. The connection drawn in Section 5, which shows that a class of CNNs can be embedded within the constructed Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), is particularly strong, as it extends the applicability of the theoretical results beyond kernel methods to more conventional deep learning models.

2.  **Originality:** The work provides a novel synthesis of ideas from several areas: the CKN framework of Mairal et al. (2014, 2016), the stability analysis of scattering transforms by Mallat (2012), and classical RKHS theory. While inspired by prior work, the extension of these stability proofs to the CKN/CNN setting is non-trivial and original. It provides a new perspective on why these architectures are successful and offers a principled way to think about their geometric properties.

3.  **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building up their framework, starting from the construction of the multilayer kernel, discussing practical discretization, and then proceeding to the core stability analysis. The arguments are easy to follow, and the notation, while dense, is used consistently. The paper provides enough detail to demonstrate the validity of its claims, with full proofs appropriately relegated to the appendix.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Motivation for Stability Analysis:** While the technical contribution is strong, the motivation for *why* this specific form of stability is important could be strengthened. The introduction links stability to generalization and robustness, and the related work section briefly mentions adversarial examples. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion connecting the derived stability bound (Eq. 4) to practical benefits. For instance, how does this theoretical understanding help in designing better architectures, choosing regularization strategies, or explaining generalization performance? A stronger motivating narrative in the introduction would increase the paper's impact and make the significance of the results clearer to a broader audience.

2.  **Minor Clarity Issues and Corrections:**
    *   **Notation:** The symbol κ is overloaded. It is used to define the kernel function in Equation (2) (`κ_k`) and also as a constant bounding the patch size relative to the pooling scale in assumption (A3). Using a different symbol for one of these would improve clarity.
    *   **Redundancy in Appendix:** In the appendix (between lines 384-385 in the proof of Lemma B.2), the second constraint in the displayed equation appears to be redundant, as `K(z, z') = K(z', z)` by the symmetry property of positive definite kernels. This could be simplified.
    *   **Missing Definition in Appendix:** At line 416, the symbol `φ` is used without a formal definition. It should be explicitly introduced as the feature map corresponding to the kernel K, i.e., `K(z, z') = <φ(z), φ(z')>`.
    *   **Typo in Appendix:** Around lines 427-428, the inequality appears to be a strict equality due to the definition of the kernel. Furthermore, `||z|| - ||z||` should presumably be `||z|| - ||z'||`.
    *   **Bibliography:** The bibliography requires some attention. Page numbers are missing for several references (e.g., [14], [26], [27]). Several other entries are preprints that have since been published in conference proceedings (e.g., [9] at ICML 2016) or journals ([17] in Comm. Pure Appl. Math.). These should be updated for the final version to ensure proper citation.

Despite these minor points, the weaknesses do not detract significantly from the core contribution of the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant theoretical result concerning the stability of deep convolutional representations.
*   The work successfully extends stability guarantees, previously known for scattering transforms, to a broader and highly relevant class of models, including CKNs and certain CNNs.
*   The manuscript is very clearly written and well-organized, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The motivation for the importance of stability to diffeomorphisms could be more strongly articulated to broaden the paper's impact.
*   The paper contains several minor notational and bibliographic issues that, while easily correctable, should be addressed.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a high-quality piece of theoretical work that advances our understanding of deep learning models. I recommend its acceptance.
