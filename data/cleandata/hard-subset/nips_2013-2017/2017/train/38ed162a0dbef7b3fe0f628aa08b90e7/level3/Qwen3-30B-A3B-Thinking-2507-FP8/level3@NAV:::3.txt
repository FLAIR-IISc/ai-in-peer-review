

This paper presents a rigorous theoretical framework for understanding deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs) through the lens of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), demonstrating that DCNN functional spaces can be described as multilayer kernels with approximate group invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors build upon the convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) framework and extend it to continuous domains, showing that the resulting functional space contains a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The theoretical results on stability to deformations and translation invariance are compelling and well-supported, drawing connections to Mallat's scattering transform while providing novel insights into DCNNs.

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The mathematical arguments are sound, the notation is well-defined, and the proofs are thorough. The authors successfully demonstrate that stability properties can be translated to predictive models through RKHS norm control, offering potential implications for generalization bounds. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the complex mathematics accessible to the intended audience.

However, the paper would benefit from greater clarification on the novelty and utility of the RKHS reformulation. While the connection to CKNs is mentioned, the paper should explicitly state how this work advances beyond Mairal et al.'s Convolutional Kernel Networks, particularly in terms of theoretical contributions and practical implications. The authors should also address why preserving full signal information is advantageous when many tasks only require task-relevant information, and clarify the relationship to invariant-kernel approaches like Burkhardt's work.

The significance of this work lies in providing a unified theoretical framework that bridges CNNs and kernel methods, potentially enabling better regularization strategies. The paper's main contribution appears to be a reformulation rather than a novel technique, though it is technically sound and rigorously presented.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding DCNNs. The main weakness is insufficient justification of the reformulation's utility and novelty relative to existing work. With minor clarifications addressing these points, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
