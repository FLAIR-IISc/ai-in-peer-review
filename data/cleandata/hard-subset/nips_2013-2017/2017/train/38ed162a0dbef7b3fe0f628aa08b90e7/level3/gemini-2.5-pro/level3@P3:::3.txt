This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations by framing them within the theory of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors generalize the construction of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) to define a multilayer convolutional kernel that operates on signals in continuous domains. The core of the work is to analyze the geometric properties of the functional space induced by this kernel. The main theoretical contributions are proofs that this representation is stable to the action of small C1-diffeomorphisms and approximately invariant to translations. This framework is then extended to achieve near-invariance to more general transformation groups. A key result is showing that this RKHS contains a large class of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) that use smooth, homogeneous activation functions, which allows the stability and invariance guarantees to be extended to these widely used models. This work connects to and builds upon the scattering transform (Mallat, 2012) for its analysis of stability and the CKN framework (Mairal et al., 2014, 2016) for its architectural construction.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Quality and Rigor:** The paper is technically sound, well-written, and mathematically rigorous. It successfully uses tools from harmonic analysis and kernel methods to formalize properties of deep convolutional architectures that are often discussed only intuitively.
*   **Significant Theoretical Results:** The primary strength is the formal characterization of stability and invariance. Proving that these representations are stable to diffeomorphisms (with an explicit bound depending on the deformation) and that translation invariance increases with network depth provides a valuable theoretical justification for the empirical robustness of CNNs.
*   **Unifying Framework:** The connection established between the multilayer kernel space and a class of standard CNNs is significant. It provides a principled way to analyze CNNs through the well-understood lens of kernel theory, linking properties like stability to the RKHS norm of the learned function. This elegantly connects the capacity control of a model (its norm) to its robustness against input deformations.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper could be improved by clarifying several points regarding its implications and novelty.

*   **Utility of the RKHS Reformulation:** The main contribution is a sophisticated reformulation of DCNNs within an RKHS framework. While this is an elegant theoretical exercise, the paper would be more impactful if it elaborated on the utility of this perspective. What new insights, practical advantages (e.g., in algorithm design or regularization strategies), or theoretical results (e.g., tighter generalization bounds) does this framework enable that were not accessible through prior analyses? The connection between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization is mentioned, but this implication could be discussed in more depth.
*   **Novelty Beyond Reformulation and CKNs:** Related to the previous point, it would be helpful to clarify if the novelty extends beyond a pure RKHS reformulation. Furthermore, the work builds heavily on "Convolutional Kernel Networks" (Mairal et al.). The authors should more explicitly delineate the novel theoretical contributions of this paper compared to the original CKN papers, which introduced the core architecture. A clearer statement on what stability and invariance results are new would be beneficial.
*   **Emphasis on Signal Preservation:** The paper highlights that the representation "preserves all information about the signal" (Lemma 1). However, for many supervised learning tasks like classification, the goal is precisely to discard intra-class variability and irrelevant information to achieve selectivity for task-relevant features. Could the authors comment on this apparent tension? Is full information preservation always a desirable property, or is there a trade-off between stability/invariance and task-specific selectivity that this framework could also help to characterize?
*   **Connection to Invariant Kernel Literature:** The discussion on group invariance is well-posed but could be enriched by connecting it to the extensive literature on invariant kernels (e.g., the work of Haasdonk and Burkhardt). Placing the proposed group-equivariant operators within this broader context would strengthen the paper's positioning and acknowledge related prior art.
*   **Characterization of Equivariant Operators:** The construction in Section 4 demonstrates how to build G-equivariant layers. A point of curiosity is whether this construction is exhaustive. Is it possible to provide a more general characterization of the set of all linear operators that would ensure G-equivariance in this layered architecture? For instance, does any operator that commutes with all group actions Lg suffice, and how does that relate to the proposed convolution-based pooling?

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical paper that provides a rigorous and valuable analysis of deep convolutional representations. The establishment of stability and invariance properties within a unifying kernel framework is a significant contribution that deepens our understanding of why these models work so well. Despite some points that could be clarified regarding practical implications and relation to prior work, the paper is technically strong and addresses fundamental questions of interest to the NIPS community.

**Recommendation: Accept.**
