Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies deep convolutional representations through the lens of positive definite kernels. It defines a multilayer, convolutional RKHS representation that generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), built by composing patch extraction, a kernel mapping into layer-wise RKHSs, and pooling. On continuous domains, the authors prove: (i) the representation preserves signal information (recoverability from sampled feature maps), (ii) stability to C1 diffeomorphisms in the Lipschitz sense, with improved translation invariance as pooling scale increases, and (iii) extensions to global invariance for general transformation groups via group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling. Importantly, they show that the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel contains a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, and that the same stability guarantees transfer to these CNNs via control of the RKHS norm. They also discuss discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations (as in CKNs), arguing stability is preserved by non-expansive projections.

This work is positioned between scattering networks (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat), which provide tight invariance/stability guarantees with fixed wavelets, and conventional CNNs/groupeq CNNs (Cohen & Welling), which emphasize architectural equivariance but lack RKHS-based stability analysis. It builds on CKNs (Mairal et al.) and advances their theoretical characterization, while connecting to stability notions relevant to adversarial robustness.

Quality
The technical development is careful and largely sound. The stability bound (Theorem 6) follows from a clear operator-theoretic decomposition (Proposition 3) and commutator bounds (Lemmas 4–5), mirroring and extending scattering proofs to the kernel/CKN setting, including shifted kernels and continuous-to-discrete links. The group invariance section is clean and general. The inclusion of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations in the RKHS (Proposition 7) is useful and, together with inequality (1), makes the transfer of stability to predictive models explicit.

The main limitation is empirical: no experiments corroborate invariance/stability in practice, nor the trade-offs from discretization and finite-dimensional approximations. Given the paper’s practical claims (e.g., benefits for generalization/sample complexity), some validation is warranted.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. Assumptions (A1–A3) are explicit; the roles of patch size and pooling scale are explained; continuous/discrete links (Lemmas 1–2) are helpful. Suggestions to improve clarity:
- State the constants and their dependence (e.g., C1’s κ^(d+1) growth) more prominently, and discuss practical regimes where they remain moderate.
- Make explicit the accumulation of approximation error across layers in Section 3.2.
- Clarify the range of activations covered by Proposition 7 and provide concrete kernels/σ examples with explicit Cσ.

Originality
The work extends scattering-style stability analysis to a broader, learnable, multilayer kernel framework, and shows that a class of CNNs lies inside the resulting RKHS, thus inheriting stability via norm control. The group-invariance construction is conceptually related to prior equivariant architectures, but here linked to RKHS geometry and stability bounds. The discretization/recoverability results and the operator-commutator extension to shifted pooling are also novel within this context.

Significance
If validated empirically, the results could impact our understanding of why deep convolutional architectures generalize and how to design architectures/regularizers that trade invariance and stability. Showing that the same RKHS norm controls both generalization and stability is conceptually appealing. The general group setting broadens applicability beyond translations. The link to CKNs supports theoretically grounded alternatives to standard CNNs.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous analysis of invariance and stability in a multilayer kernel framework.
- Transfer of stability guarantees to a class of CNNs via RKHS inclusion and norm control.
- General treatment of group equivariance/invariance.
- Thoughtful treatment of discretization and finite-dimensional approximations.

Weaknesses
- No experiments to validate theoretical claims or quantify constants in realistic settings.
- Practical scope of activations (smooth/homogeneous) excludes standard ReLU; reliance on smoothed variants may limit direct applicability.
- The dependence of constants on κ, dimensionality, and depth may be pessimistic; limited guidance on parameter choices.
- The effect of approximate kernel mappings beyond projections (e.g., random features) is not quantified.

Questions and suggestions
- Empirical validation: Please include experiments demonstrating (i) translation invariance improving with σn, (ii) stability to small diffeomorphisms on synthetic deformations, and (iii) comparison to scattering and standard CNN baselines. Simple 1D signals and MNIST/CIFAR with controlled deformations would suffice. Show how prediction stability correlates with RKHS norm.
- CNN inclusion: Can you quantify Cσ for commonly used smoothed-ReLU surrogates under specific kernels (e.g., inverse polynomial or arc-cosine), and give practical bounds on ‖fσ‖?
- Constants and depth: Provide numerical estimates for C1, C2 in 2D with typical κ and σ schedules; discuss whether the (1+n) factor is tight or improvable.
- Kernel approximations beyond projections: Could random projections or explicit random Fourier feature (RFF) maps be used? If the per-layer finite map T has operator norm ≤1 (e.g., an orthonormal projection or a map rescaled by its spectral norm), the non-expansiveness needed in Section 3.2 holds, and stability bounds extend with an additive term accounting for kernel approximation error (accumulating across layers). For RFF, one could either (a) post-scale to make T 1-Lipschitz, or (b) accept a Lipschitz constant L>1 and track L^n in the bound; the latter risks degrading stability. A Nyström approximation with orthogonalized features naturally yields a projection and thus preserves non-expansiveness. It would be useful to formalize a bound of the form: stability ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖ + Σk εk, where εk reflects kernel approximation error at layer k.
- Scope of discretization results: Please discuss boundary effects and the stability of deconvolution in Lemma 2 as σk varies, and provide guidance on ek ≥ sk choices in modern CNN regimes.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, well-executed theoretical contribution that generalizes scattering insights to learnable convolutional kernels and connects to CNNs in a principled RKHS framework.
- Clear path from representation stability to prediction stability via norm control, tying together invariance, robustness, and generalization.
- Broad group-equivariant formulation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of empirical evidence leaves practical relevance uncertain.
- Restrictions on activations and assumptions (A1–A3) may limit immediate applicability to standard CNN practice.
- Constants in bounds may be loose; no quantitative guidance on their magnitude in realistic settings.

Overall
A solid and timely theoretical paper that advances our understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations and their link to generalization. I recommend acceptance provided the authors add concise experiments validating the main claims and clarify the impact of kernel approximations (including random features) on stability.
