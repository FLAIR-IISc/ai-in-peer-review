

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations" (NIPS 2017)

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, focusing on their invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15,16] and study the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) to characterize the functional space of deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs). The work demonstrates that this functional space contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, allowing the authors to establish theoretical stability guarantees for these architectures.

The paper builds on Mallat's scattering transform [5,17], which provides a theoretical framework for analyzing invariance and stability properties of wavelet-based representations. While the scattering transform is non-learning (filters are predefined), this work extends similar theoretical analysis to more standard CNN architectures by introducing a kernel-based perspective. The authors also connect to Mairal et al.'s Convolutional Kernel Networks [15,16], showing how their framework generalizes CKNs while providing additional theoretical guarantees.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is exceptionally well-written and rigorous, with clear mathematical formulation and thorough proofs.
- The key theoretical contribution is the demonstration that DCNN functional spaces can be described as multilayer kernels, with the authors establishing (approximate) group invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms.
- The connection between RKHS norm and generalization is insightful, suggesting that stability may lead to improved sample complexity.
- The discretization analysis and connection to practical implementations (CKNs) is well-executed and addresses a key gap between theory and practice.

**Weaknesses and Clarifications Needed:**
The main contribution is a reformulation of CNNs in the RKHS context, but the paper does not sufficiently articulate why this reformulation is useful. The authors should explicitly discuss:
1. How this perspective enables new theoretical insights beyond previous work
2. Concrete implications for generalization bounds (beyond the general RKHS connection)
3. Any computational or approximation advantages gained from this reformulation

The novelty relative to Mairal et al.'s Convolutional Kernel Networks [15,16] requires clarification. The paper states that their framework "generalizes the construction of convolutional kernel networks," but it's unclear how their multilayer kernel construction differs from the CKN framework. The authors should explicitly state what new theoretical insights their framework provides beyond the CKN work.

The emphasis on "not losing the signal information" warrants discussion. The paper acknowledges that pooling operators may remove high-frequency information, yet argues this is acceptable for natural signals. However, for many practical tasks, only task-relevant information matters, not the full signal. The authors should address why preserving full signal information is beneficial for classification tasks compared to more selective representations.

The connection to invariant-kernel approaches (e.g., Burkhardt's work [13]) is not explored. Given the focus on invariance, the authors should discuss how their framework relates to prior invariant-kernel methods.

Finally, the characterization of operators ensuring equivariance would be valuable. The paper discusses how specific operators (patch extraction and pooling) commute with group actions, but a more general characterization of operators that ensure DCNN equivariance would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of DCNNs through the lens of RKHS and multilayer kernels, making a significant contribution to understanding the functional spaces of deep convolutional representations. The theoretical results on stability to diffeomorphisms and group invariance are well-established and extend previous work on scattering networks.

While the core contribution is a reformulation in RKHS terms, the paper demonstrates how this perspective leads to new theoretical insights about stability and generalization. The authors have made a compelling case for why this framework is useful for analyzing DCNNs, though they could strengthen this by more explicitly stating the advantages of their reformulation over previous approaches.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria:
- **Quality:** Technically sound, with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough proofs.
- **Clarity:** Excellent writing, well-organized, and accessible to the intended audience.
- **Originality:** Extends prior work on scattering networks and CKNs with new theoretical insights about stability and invariance.
- **Significance:** Provides a deeper theoretical understanding of why DCNNs work well, with implications for generalization and regularization.

Given the paper's high technical quality, clear presentation, and significant theoretical contribution, I recommend acceptance. The authors should address the clarifications requested above in the camera-ready version to further strengthen their contribution.
