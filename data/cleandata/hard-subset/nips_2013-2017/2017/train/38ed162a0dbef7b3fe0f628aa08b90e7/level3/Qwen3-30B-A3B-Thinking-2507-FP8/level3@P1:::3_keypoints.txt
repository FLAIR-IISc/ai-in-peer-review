- Extends multilayer convolutional kernel framework to RKHS, proving stability to diffeomorphisms and group invariance for deep convolutional representations.
- Shows the RKHS contains standard CNNs with homogeneous activations and links stability (controlled by RKHS norm) to generalization.
- Generalizes CKNs to continuous domains and addresses discretization and kernel approximation to connect theory with practical CNNs.
- Formalizes how layered operators (patch extraction, kernel mapping, pooling) produce invariance, with stability bounds showing translation-invariance error decays with pooling scale (Theorem 6).
- Proofs are technically rigorous and clearly presented, with careful control of commutator terms and stability constants (e.g., Lemma 4, Theorem 6).
- Discretization analysis effectively bridges continuous theory and practice; the CNN–RKHS correspondence (Proposition 7) links theory to mainstream architectures.
- Stability–generalization link (Section 5) compellingly argues low RKHS norm implies better generalization, aligning with margin/SVM theory.
- Novelty relative to prior work (Mairal’s CKNs and Mallat’s scattering) is insufficiently clarified; the RKHS reformulation seems a natural extension rather than a clearly distinct advance.
- Unclear whether the RKHS perspective yields tighter generalization bounds or enables new regularization methods beyond prior work.
- The claim of "preserving full signal information" (Lemma 1) lacks justification—paper should address when preservation helps versus when task-specific compression is preferable.
- Connection to invariant kernel methods (e.g., Burkhardt) is superficial and needs a brief comparative discussion to strengthen novelty.
- Characterization of operators ensuring equivariance is implied but not rigorously established, leaving a theoretical gap.
- Significance: provides a unified theoretical foundation for CNN properties and a principled basis for RKHS-norm regularization, with potential relevance to adversarial robustness.
- Does not demonstrate concrete computational advantages over prior methods but is broadly applicable to CKNs and standard CNNs.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—specifically clarify novelty versus CKNs, justify signal-preservation claims, and engage briefly with Burkhardt’s invariant kernels.
