

This paper studies the stability properties of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) under diffeomorphisms, extending the theoretical framework of the scattering transform [17] to more general convolutional architectures. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that generalizes CKNs [15, 16] and establishes theoretical guarantees for stability to small deformations while preserving signal information. The paper demonstrates that this functional space contains both CKNs and a large class of convolutional neural networks with smooth homogeneous activation functions, allowing the stability results to apply broadly to standard CNN architectures. This work builds on Mallat's scattering transform theory [17] but extends it to kernel-based representations and more general CNN models, while also connecting to related work on group invariance [9, 22] and kernel methods [15, 27].

The paper's primary strength is its technically sound theoretical contribution that provides rigorous mathematical foundations for understanding stability properties of convolutional representations. The authors establish clear conditions (A1-A3) under which the representation is stable to C^1 diffeomorphisms, with a bound that decays with pooling scale σ_n (Theorem 6). This is a significant advancement over previous work, as it applies to standard CNN architectures rather than just pre-defined filter networks like scattering. The connection between RKHS norm and generalization ability (Proposition 7) provides a principled way to understand why stable representations generalize better, which is particularly valuable for understanding the success of deep networks.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from theoretical framework to practical implementation. The mathematical development is thorough, with careful attention to technical details (e.g., discretization in Section 2.1 and the signal preservation lemmas). The manuscript effectively bridges kernel methods and deep learning, offering a fresh perspective on convolutional architectures that should be valuable to both communities. The extension to group invariance in Section 4 is a thoughtful addition that demonstrates the framework's versatility.

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. The motivation for the stability analysis is underdeveloped; the paper briefly mentions that Lipschitz stability may help with adversarial robustness [8], but this connection needs deeper exploration to justify the significant theoretical effort. A more compelling argument for why stability to diffeomorphisms (beyond translations) is crucial for practical applications would strengthen the paper's significance.

The manuscript contains several minor technical issues that need correction. The notation κ is overloaded in (A3) (where it bounds patch sizes) and also used to denote the kernel function in Equation (10), which could confuse readers. The second constraint in the displayed equation between lines 384-385 is redundant due to kernel symmetry. At line 416, the feature map φ is not defined, though it should be introduced via Equation (10) as the kernel feature map. The inequality around lines 427-428 appears to hold with equality, and the term ||z|| - ||z|| should be ||z|| - ||z'||.

The bibliography requires attention; several references ([3,6,8,13,14,18,25-27,30,31]) lack page information, and references [9,17,19,32] need updated publication details (e.g., [17] should cite the full PAMI paper rather than the preprint). These are necessary for proper academic attribution.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a clear contribution by extending the scattering transform theory to kernel-based representations and establishing stability for standard CNN architectures. The connection between RKHS norm and stability generalizes the understanding of how convolutional architectures operate, providing a theoretical foundation for the success of deep networks that goes beyond the engineering perspective. While the scattering transform [17] established similar stability properties for pre-defined networks, this work shows these properties hold for a much broader class of models.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it provides a theoretical basis for understanding why convolutional architectures work well for structured data. The stability guarantees directly address the need for robustness to small deformations, which is critical for real-world applications. The connection between RKHS norm and generalization ability (Proposition 7) provides a principled way to regularize CNNs, potentially leading to improved sample complexity—a key challenge in deep learning.

Quality is excellent: the theoretical results are well-supported with careful proofs, and the paper demonstrates a deep understanding of both kernel methods and convolutional architectures. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their framework (e.g., the trade-off between norm preservation and pooling effects).

In conclusion, this paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of convolutional representations. While the motivation for stability analysis could be strengthened, and minor technical issues need correction, the core results are significant and well-founded. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the stability results, particularly regarding how they might inform regularization strategies in CNNs. Given its technical soundness, clarity, and theoretical importance, I recommend acceptance.
