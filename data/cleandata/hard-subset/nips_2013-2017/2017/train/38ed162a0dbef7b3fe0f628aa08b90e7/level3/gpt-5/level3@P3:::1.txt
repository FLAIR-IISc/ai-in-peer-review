Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation in which each layer performs patch extraction, kernel mapping via a homogeneous dot-product kernel, and Gaussian pooling. The authors prove that when patch sizes and pooling scales are properly coupled, the resulting deep convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) are nearly invariant to transformation groups (via group-equivariant layers and a final group pooling) and are Lipschitz-stable to diffeomorphisms. The stability bound scales linearly with deformation magnitude and Jacobian, and translation invariance improves with the final pooling scale. The work extends the scattering transform framework of Mallat/Bruna to a broader class of architectures by replacing wavelet modulus with kernel maps that preserve norms and are non-expansive, and by analyzing the geometry of the corresponding RKHS.

A key contribution is to show that, under smoothness and homogeneity assumptions on the activation and appropriate normalization, a large class of CNNs lies in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel. Hence, the invariance/stability properties established for the kernel representation carry over to those CNNs via RKHS norm control. The paper also provides discretization results with patch extraction and subsampling, signal preservation lemmas, and an analysis of group-equivariant layers for general locally compact groups, connecting to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling) and prior kernel/representation theories (Anselmi et al., Montavon et al., Daniely et al.). It relates directly to prior NIPS work on CKNs and supervised CKNs, and to scattering-based invariance/stability.

Quality
The theoretical development is careful and technically sound. Assumptions (norm-preserving, non-expansive mappings; patch size vs pooling scale; initial anti-aliasing) are explicit and align with standard signal processing practice and scattering analyses. The commutator bounds are well motivated, and the depth dependence is transparent. The link to CNNs (Proposition 7) is nontrivial and clarifies how stability translates to learned networks with smooth homogeneous activations. However, the work lacks empirical validation: no experiments are presented to quantify invariance, stability, or the practical relevance of constants and approximations (projection to finite-dimensional subspaces, discretization).

Clarity
The manuscript is well written, well organized, and self-contained. Notation is consistent, and connections to related work are clear. The discretization and kernel-approximation sections are helpful for bridging theory and practice.

Originality
The paper generalizes scattering-style stability/invariance to a broader kernelized, multilayer, convolutional setting and shows a principled transfer of these properties to a class of CNNs via RKHS embeddings. The group-equivariant construction within the kernel framework and the explicit stability analysis with patch/pooling operators are original contributions that extend prior theory.

Significance
Understanding when and how deep convolutional representations yield invariance and stability is important for both theory and practice. The results offer principled guidance for architecture design (patch sizes, pooling scales, group pooling) and regularization via RKHS norms. If supported by experiments, this could influence practice in building stable and invariant networks and in designing kernel approximations for deep models.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous analysis establishing Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms and near-invariance to transformations with interpretable constants.
- Generalization of scattering-style results to CKNs and a meaningful class of CNNs; explicit RKHS norm control connects stability and generalization.
- Group-equivariant construction that cleanly separates intermediate stability and final global invariance.
- Thoughtful treatment of discretization and signal preservation; compatibility with finite-dimensional kernel approximations via non-expansive projections.

Weaknesses
- No empirical validation. The paper does not quantify stability/invariance in practice, nor verify how constants scale with depth or pooling.
- Assumptions needed to map CNNs into the RKHS (smooth homogeneous activations, normalization by patch norms) deviate from standard practice (e.g., ReLU without smoothing), raising questions about practical coverage.
- Limited discussion on how kernel approximation errors (subspace projection, Nyström, RFF) propagate into stability bounds.

Requests for empirical validation
- Quantify representation stability: measure ||Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)||/||x|| as a function of ||τ||∞ and ||∇τ||∞ on benchmarks (MNIST/CIFAR) under controlled synthetic diffeomorphisms (translations, elastic deformations, small rotations). Plot empirical slopes vs. theoretical predictions and depth/pooling scales.
- Invariance to group actions: evaluate group pooling on rotations (SO(2)) using rotated MNIST or texture datasets; show invariance improves with final pooling scale without excessive information loss.
- Compare CKNs, scattering, and standard CNNs: report representation distances and classification robustness under deformations; ablate patch sizes and σk.
- Kernel approximation effects: compare exact RKHS maps (as feasible), learned subspace projections (CKN), Nyström, and random Fourier features on stability metrics and accuracy.

Open question and suggestion
- Can random projections or explicit kernel mappings with a finite number of Fourier features serve as kernel approximations while preserving stability? Please analyze how approximation error and projection distortion affect non-expansiveness and the constants in Theorem 6. Is there a bound of the form ||Φapprox(Lτx) − Φapprox(x)|| ≤ (C1 + εapprox)(||∇τ||∞) + (C2/σn)||τ||∞? Are Johnson–Lindenstrauss-type embeddings or RFF concentration sufficient to keep εapprox small with high probability, and how does it scale with dimensionality and depth?

Additional comments/questions
- Depth dependence: Theorem 6 yields (1 + n) dependence. Is this tight? Can Parseval/contractive layers (cf. Cissé et al.) or smaller κ reduce the constant growth?
- Boundary and deconvolution stability: Lemma 2 relies on stable deconvolution; discuss robustness to noise and finite-support boundary effects.
- Activation smoothness: Provide a concrete smoothed ReLU instance satisfying Lemma B.2, and discuss empirical sensitivity to smoothing.
- Practical normalization: The nk(u) factors homogenize activations; how sensitive are results and practice to this normalization if omitted or approximated?
- Generalization: Any empirical correlation between RKHS norm proxies (e.g., product of spectral norms or weight decay) and stability/robustness?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, general theoretical contributions extending scattering-style guarantees to CKNs and a class of CNNs.
- Clear architectural guidance connecting patch sizes, pooling scales, and group pooling to invariance and stability.
- Bridges kernel methods and deep learning in a principled way.

Cons for acceptance
- No experiments; current submission is purely theoretical without empirical evidence.
- Practical gap regarding standard activations and implementation choices.
- Missing analysis of kernel approximation effects commonly used in scalable CKNs.

Overall assessment
Technically solid and timely theoretical work with clear potential impact, but the absence of experiments is a major shortcoming. I would be inclined toward acceptance if the camera-ready includes empirical demonstrations of the stated invariance/stability and an initial study of kernel approximation effects (e.g., subspace projection vs. RFF/Nyström).
