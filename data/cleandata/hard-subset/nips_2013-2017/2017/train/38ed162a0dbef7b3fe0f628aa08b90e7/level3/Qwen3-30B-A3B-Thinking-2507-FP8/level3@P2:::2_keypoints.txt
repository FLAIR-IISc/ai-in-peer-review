- Studies stability to diffeomorphisms and invariance to group transformations for deep convolutional representations.  
- Builds on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and extends Mallat’s scattering transform analysis to broader convolutional architectures.  
- Shows representations from multilayer convolutional kernels with norm-preserving, non-expansive kernels (A1–A3) enjoy Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms.  
- Demonstrates translation invariance is controlled by the final pooling layer scale.  
- Connects the kernel framework to standard CNNs with homogeneous activations, extending stability results to these networks.  
- Proves RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization, linking kernel viewpoint to practical regularization.  
- Carefully addresses the stability vs. information-preservation trade-off, noting pooling reduces energy but natural signals often have low-frequency energy.  
- Analysis covers both continuous and discrete representations; the discretization approach in Section 2.1 is well-motivated.  
- Technical results are rigorous with detailed proofs and generalize Mallat’s work to more standard architectures.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; notation is mostly consistent and figures effectively illustrate constructions.  
- Minor clarity issues: kernel function κ is overloaded (assumption A3 vs Eq. 10) and feature map φ is not defined where used (Lemma 4).  
- Extension to group invariance (Section 4) is valuable but somewhat sketchy compared to the main stability results.  
- Motivation could be stronger—connection to adversarial robustness is only briefly mentioned and underdeveloped.  
- Minor technical errors: a likely typo in an inequality (||z|| - ||z|| should be ||z|| - ||z'||) and a redundant constraint in one equation.  
- Bibliography needs fixes: missing page info for several refs and updated publication details for others.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, clear kernel–CNN connection, comprehensive and rigorous analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak practical motivation, minor notation confusions, and a few technical errors to correct.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — substantial, well-executed theoretical advance with easily fixable minor issues.
