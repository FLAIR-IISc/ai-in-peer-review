This paper investigates the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and their connection to more standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors build upon the framework of the scattering transform, extending its theoretical guarantees to a broader class of models. They demonstrate that, with appropriately designed patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operations, CKNs exhibit near-invariance to transformation groups and stability to diffeomorphisms. Crucially, they show that these properties extend to CNNs containing CKNs as a subset, offering a theoretical underpinning for the robustness often observed in deep learning.

The work is deeply rooted in the existing literature. The authors thoroughly contextualize their contribution with respect to prior work on scattering transforms [Bruna & Mallat, 2013], convolutional kernel networks [Mairal et al., 2014, 2016], group invariance [Cohen & Welling, 2016], and kernel methods for deep learning [Cho & Saul, 2009]. They appropriately acknowledge and build upon concepts such as reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) [Saitoh, 1988] and Lipschitz stability. The theoretical analysis is presented with mathematical rigor, and the connection between the continuous and discrete representations is carefully considered.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations, going beyond empirical observations.
* **Generalization to CNNs:** The extension of results from CKNs to a wider class of CNNs is a significant contribution, potentially offering new insights into the behavior of these models.
* **Clear and Rigorous Presentation:** The mathematical notation is well-defined, and the arguments are presented in a clear and logical manner. The organization of the paper is excellent.
* **Novel Combination of Techniques:**  The authors effectively combine concepts from kernel methods, harmonic analysis, and convolutional networks, offering a novel perspective on understanding deep learning.
* **Addresses a Fundamental Question:**  The paper tackles a crucial question in deep learning – understanding the geometric properties of the functional spaces where these networks operate.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness is the complete absence of empirical results. While the theoretical analysis is compelling, it lacks validation on real-world datasets. It's unclear how well these theoretical properties translate into practical performance gains.  Specifically, it would be beneficial to demonstrate the invariance to transformations and diffeomorphisms in practice.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the framework is theoretically sound, the practical implementation of the described CKNs might be challenging and computationally expensive. The paper doesn't address these potential practical limitations.
* **Assumptions on Activation Functions:** The requirement for “smooth homogeneous” activation functions limits the applicability of the results to widely used activation functions like ReLU. The authors acknowledge this and suggest smoothed variants, but the impact on performance isn't explored.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:**  The manuscript does not sufficiently address the computational trade-offs associated with the proposed kernel constructions, particularly compared to standard CNNs.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound analysis of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations.
* The extension of these properties to CNNs is a significant contribution with potential implications for understanding and improving deep learning models.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented.
* The paper tackles a fundamental problem in deep learning and offers a unique perspective.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The complete lack of empirical validation weakens the impact of the theoretical results.
* The practical limitations and computational cost associated with implementing the proposed framework are not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of deep convolutional representations. However, the absence of empirical validation is a major concern.  While the theoretical results are compelling, it’s difficult to assess their practical relevance without experimental evidence. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend a **weak reject**.  I believe the paper has merit and could be significantly strengthened by including empirical results.  Specifically, the authors should demonstrate the invariance to transformations and diffeomorphisms predicted by their theory. Even limited experiments would greatly enhance the paper’s credibility and impact. Further discussion of the practical computational challenges and potential avenues for approximation (e.g., using random Fourier features [Rahimi & Recht, 2007]) would also be beneficial. Addressing these points would dramatically increase the value of this work.
