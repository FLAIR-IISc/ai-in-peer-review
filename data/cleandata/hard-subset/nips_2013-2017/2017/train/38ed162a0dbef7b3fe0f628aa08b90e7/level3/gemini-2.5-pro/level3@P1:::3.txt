This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations through the lens of multilayer kernels and reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to construct a deep representation by composing operators for patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling. The authors then analyze the properties of the functional space associated with this construction. The primary contributions are formal proofs demonstrating that this representation (1) is stable with respect to the action of small diffeomorphisms, and (2) can achieve near-invariance to translations and, more generally, to the action of transformation groups. The framework is shown to be general enough to encompass both Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) and a class of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This allows the stability and invariance results to be extended to these widely-used models. The work draws heavily on the formalism introduced by Mallat for the analysis of scattering networks and extends it to a broader, learnable kernel-based setting.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Quality and Rigor:** The paper is of high technical quality. The construction of the multilayer convolutional kernel is well-defined, and the subsequent stability analysis is mathematically rigorous, building upon established tools from harmonic analysis. The claims are well-supported by theoretical derivations, and the proofs are provided in the appendix.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It systematically introduces the multilayer kernel construction before moving to the analysis of its properties, such as stability, invariance, and its connection to CNNs. This logical flow makes the complex material accessible.

3.  **Significance:** The primary significance of this work is in providing a unifying theoretical framework that connects kernel methods, CKNs, and certain types of CNNs. Proving that these architectures can be embedded in an RKHS with demonstrable stability properties is a valuable contribution. It provides a principled way to understand why these models are robust to small deformations of the input, a key property for tasks in computer vision and signal processing. The link between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization is an important insight.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors (Arguments against acceptance, or for improvement):**

The paper is strong overall, but its contribution could be clarified and strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Significance of the RKHS Reformulation:** The core contribution appears to be the reformulation of DCNN-like architectures within an RKHS framework, followed by a stability analysis. While this is a valuable theoretical result, the paper could do more to articulate *why* this specific reformulation is useful beyond the analysis itself. Does this perspective offer new avenues for designing architectures, developing novel regularization techniques based on the RKHS norm, deriving tighter generalization bounds, or providing computational or approximation advantages? Clarifying the downstream implications would significantly elevate the impact of the work.

2.  **Novelty Beyond Existing Work:**
    *   **Versus Convolutional Kernel Networks (Mairal et al.):** The paper states that it generalizes the CKN construction. It would be helpful if the authors could more explicitly delineate their novel contributions compared to the original CKN papers. Is the primary novelty the application of the stability analysis (inspired by scattering networks) to this generalized CKN framework, or are there fundamental additions to the kernel construction itself that enable new capabilities?
    *   **Versus Invariant Kernels:** The goal of achieving group invariance with kernels has a long history (e.g., the work of Haasdonk and Burkhardt, cited as [13]). A more in-depth discussion comparing the proposed multilayer construction to these earlier methods for building invariant kernels would help to better contextualize the work and highlight its originality.

3.  **Motivation for Signal Preservation:** The authors show that their construction can preserve signal information (Lemmas 1 and 2). However, for many supervised learning tasks (e.g., classification), the objective is precisely to discard nuisance variability and retain only task-relevant information. Why is preserving the full signal information a desirable property in this context? A discussion on the trade-off between information preservation and the creation of a selective, discriminative representation would be insightful.

4.  **Characterization of Equivariant Operators:** Section 4 provides a constructive method for achieving group equivariance by designing pooling and patch extraction operators that commute with the group action. This is well-explained. A natural theoretical question that arises is whether this is a complete characterization. That is, would *any* operator that commutes with all elements of the group G suffice to ensure equivariance in a layer? A brief discussion on the necessary and sufficient conditions for operators within this framework to be equivariant could further strengthen the theoretical contribution.

**Conclusion:**

This is a well-written and rigorous paper that makes a solid theoretical contribution to our understanding of deep convolutional networks. It successfully establishes a connection to RKHS theory and proves important stability and invariance properties. The weaknesses identified are primarily requests for clarification regarding the broader significance and novelty of the work. I believe the strengths significantly outweigh these points, and I recommend acceptance. The paper would be even stronger if the authors could address the questions raised, particularly regarding the practical or theoretical implications of their RKHS reformulation.
