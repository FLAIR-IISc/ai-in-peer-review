This paper studies the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) from the perspective of positive definite kernels. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel introduced in the context of convolutional kernel networks and study the geometry of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). They show that the signal representation is stable and that models from this functional space, such as a large class of CNNs, may enjoy the same stability.

The paper is well-written, rigorous, and demonstrates that DCNN functional spaces can be described as multilayer kernels, showing approximate group invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, and their notation and mathematical tools are well-defined. The construction of the multilayer convolutional kernel is presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner.

The paper's main contribution is a reformulation of CNNs in the RKHS context. However, it would be beneficial for the authors to explicitly state why this reformulation is useful, e.g., what implications it has for generalization bounds or computational/approximation advantages. The authors should also clarify whether the novelty of their work goes beyond a pure RKHS reformulation of DCNNs.

The authors draw connections to previous work, including convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and the scattering transform. However, they should clarify the novelty of their work relative to "Convolutional Kernel Networks" (Mairal et al.). Additionally, the authors may want to consider discussing the connection to invariant-kernel approaches, such as Hans Burkhardt's work.

The paper's emphasis on "not losing the signal information" is interesting, but the authors should explain why preserving full signal information is necessary when many tasks only require task-relevant information. Furthermore, the authors should investigate whether any operator that commutes with all group elements would ensure DCNN equivariance, i.e., characterization of operators ensuring equivariance.

The paper's strengths include its rigorous mathematical treatment of CNNs and its ability to provide stability and invariance guarantees for a large class of CNNs. However, the authors should address the limitations of their work, such as the assumption of smooth homogeneous activation functions and the potential loss of signal information due to pooling layers.

In terms of originality, the paper's contribution is significant, as it provides a new perspective on CNNs and their theoretical properties. However, the authors should ensure that their work is clearly differentiated from previous contributions in the field.

The paper's significance is substantial, as it has the potential to impact the field of deep learning and computer vision. The authors' results could lead to improved understanding and design of CNNs, which could have far-reaching consequences for various applications.

In conclusion, the paper is well-written, rigorous, and makes significant contributions to the field of deep learning. However, the authors should address the limitations and potential extensions of their work to further strengthen their contribution.

Recommendation: Accept.
