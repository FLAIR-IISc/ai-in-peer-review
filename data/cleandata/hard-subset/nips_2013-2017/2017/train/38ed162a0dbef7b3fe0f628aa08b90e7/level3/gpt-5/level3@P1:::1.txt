Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals on continuous domains, built by composing (i) patch extraction, (ii) kernel mappings into RKHSs with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and (iii) Gaussian pooling. The resulting feature map Φn is non-expansive at each step (except for potential energy loss due to pooling), admits a natural discretization, and can be implemented approximately via projections to finite-dimensional subspaces—yielding convolutional kernel networks (CKNs). The main theoretical results establish Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms τ under mild regularity (‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2), with a bound of the form C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn, showing increasing translation invariance with the final pooling scale and stability that scales linearly with depth. The framework is then generalized to arbitrary locally compact groups G by defining group-covariant patch extraction and pooling, enabling near-invariance to group actions (e.g., rotations) via last-layer pooling on G and stability to a subgroup via intermediate pooling. Finally, the authors show that the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel contains a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations; they provide norm bounds that propagate layerwise, linking the RKHS norm to stability and generalization. This situates the work between scattering transforms (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) and modern CNN theory, while extending CKNs (Mairal et al.) with stronger invariance/stability guarantees and group-theoretic equivariance akin to group CNNs (Cohen & Welling). It also connects to kernel methods for invariance (Haasdonk & Burkhardt; Mroueh et al.) and to robustness via Lipschitz control (Parseval networks).

Quality:
The technical development is solid and carefully reasoned. The stability bound follows from commutator control of patch/pooling with deformation operators and Schur-type operator norm bounds; assumptions (A1–A3) are standard in this context and are satisfied by the proposed homogeneous kernels. The discretization lemmas and compatibility with finite-dimensional kernel approximations are well argued, though dependent on stable deconvolution. The link to CNNs via embedding into the RKHS with homogeneous activations is technically interesting and the recursive norm control is clear. However, the constants in the bounds (growth in κ and depth n) and the practical impact of pooling-induced energy loss are not empirically explored. Proofs are deferred to the appendix/extended version, which is acceptable, but adding intuition about constant magnitudes would help.

Clarity:
The paper is well written, with consistent notation and clear operator-theoretic structure. The progression (continuous model → discretization → approximations → stability → groups → CNNs) is coherent. Minor suggestions: provide a summary box with assumptions (A1–A3), main theorem constants, and a mapping between discrete parameters (ek, sk) and continuous scales (σk). Give concrete examples of admissible σ (smoothed ReLU variants) and kernels, and a short table comparing the obtained bound to the scattering transform bounds.

Originality:
The work meaningfully generalizes scattering-style stability analysis to a broader (kernelized) class of convolutional architectures and shows that a nontrivial class of CNNs belong to the induced RKHS, with norm-based stability implications. The group invariance construction is conceptually aligned with prior equivariant CNNs and scattering on groups but is integrated here into the RKHS/kernels framework. Overall, the combination is novel and nontrivial.

Significance:
The results provide theoretical grounding for stability/invariance in CKNs and a subset of CNNs, clarifying how pooling scale and depth trade off invariance and sensitivity. The RKHS norm perspective links stability and generalization, which can inform regularization. However, without experiments demonstrating the practical relevance of constants and approximations, the immediate empirical impact is limited.

Pros:
- Clear, general operator-theoretic framework unifying CKNs, group equivariance, and stability to diffeomorphisms.
- Stability bound with explicit dependence on deformation magnitude, depth, and pooling scale; near-translation invariance grows with σn.
- Constructive link showing certain CNNs reside in the RKHS with controllable norms, tying stability to generalization.
- Discretization and finite-dimensional approximations addressed; compatibility with CKN practice.

Cons:
- No empirical validation; unclear practical tightness of constants, sensitivity to discretization/deconvolution, and effect of kernel approximations.
- Dependence linear in depth (1+n) and in κ may be pessimistic; no discussion on whether architectural choices can mitigate this.
- Assumes homogeneous smooth activations; relation to widely used nonsmooth ReLU and batchnorm not fully explored.

Requests and suggestions for improvement:
- Add controlled experiments to validate the stability/invariance claims:
  - Synthetic 2D signals with parametrized diffeomorphisms (elastic deformations, translations) to measure ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ versus ‖∇τ‖∞ and ‖τ‖∞ for varying σn and depth, comparing CKN, scattering, and standard CNNs.
  - Group invariance experiments (e.g., rotations on rotated MNIST or CIFAR-10 with rotations), illustrating the effect of group pooling on invariance and accuracy.
  - Ablations on discretization parameters (ek, sk) and deconvolution stability/noise sensitivity.
  - Study how projection-based kernel approximations affect stability: compare learned subspace projections (CKN), Nyström, and random Fourier features (RFF) with matched dimensionality; report the trade-off between approximation error and empirical Lipschitz constants.

- Clarify the magnitude of constants:
  - Provide explicit bounds/estimates for C1 and its dependence on κ and dimension d; compare with scattering constants.
  - Quantify energy loss across pooling layers for typical natural image spectra; provide guidance for selecting σk schedules.

- Extend the CNN linkage:
  - Discuss mollified ReLU and leaky-ReLU; give explicit constructions of σ satisfying Lemma B.2 approximating ReLU and quantify the induced approximation error in the RKHS norm.
  - Comment on the impact of batch normalization and residual connections on the RKHS embedding and stability.

Open questions:
- Can random projections or explicit kernel maps via a finite number of random Fourier features preserve the non-expansiveness and stability bounds? Under what conditions (e.g., Johnson–Lindenstrauss guarantees or Bochner-based RFF with appropriate scaling) do the commutator bounds remain valid up to small additive distortion that depends on feature dimension?
- Is the linear-in-depth dependence improvable with contractive intermediate operators or architectural constraints (e.g., Parseval-like normalization)?
- Can the theory accommodate nonsmooth activations directly (beyond smoothing), perhaps via generalized RKHS or Lipschitz network analysis?

Recommendation:
This is a strong theoretical paper with clear contributions and good potential impact, but the lack of empirical validation weakens the case. Adding even small-scale experiments as suggested would substantially strengthen the submission.
