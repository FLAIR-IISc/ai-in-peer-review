Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a rigorous kernel-theoretic view of deep convolutional representations. The authors define a multilayer convolutional kernel for signals on continuous domains, yielding RKHS feature maps constructed by patch extraction, homogeneous dot-product kernel mappings, and Gaussian pooling. They prove (i) stability to C1-diffeomorphisms with a Lipschitz-type bound analogous to scattering, where translation invariance scales with the last pooling scale, and (ii) near-invariance to general groups by designing G-equivariant patch extraction and pooling on the group. They then show that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations belongs to the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, with explicit RKHS norm bounds, so the same stability applies to these CNNs. They also discuss discretization and CKN-style finite-dimensional approximations, preserving non-expansiveness.

This work connects and extends prior lines: scattering transforms (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat) for diffeomorphism stability, convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.) for multilayer kernel maps and practical approximations, and group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling) and invariant kernels (e.g., Haasdonk & Burkhardt) for invariance via group actions. The main novelty is a unified RKHS framework covering CKNs and a large class of CNNs with provable stability/invariance, and an explicit link between RKHS norm, stability, and potential generalization.

Strengths
- Technically sound and careful. Proofs leverage harmonic analysis and operator bounds; assumptions are stated clearly (e.g., ∥∇τ∥∞ < 1/2, homogeneous kernels satisfying A1–A2).
- Clear separation between representation and predictor; crisp operator-level stability decomposition (commutators).
- Generalizes scattering-style stability guarantees beyond pre-specified wavelets to kernelized, learnable multilayer maps and to CNNs in the induced RKHS.
- Group-equivariant construction is simple and general (patch extraction and pooling on G), covering near-invariance via final pooling.
- Practical bridge: discretization and CKN approximations remain non-expansive; insights plausibly inform network design.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Usefulness of the RKHS reformulation: Please make explicit what new leverage the RKHS view brings beyond a conceptual unification. For example:
  - Can you translate your norm bounds (Prop. 7) into explicit Rademacher complexity or margin bounds for CNNs in HKn, thereby yielding sample-complexity statements that depend on depth/filters/pooling scales?
  - Are there computational or approximation benefits (e.g., convex surrogates, Nyström/random features trade-offs) suggested by your construction relative to standard CNN training?
- Novelty relative to CKNs (Mairal et al. 2014; 2016): The construction closely follows CKNs. Please sharpen the contribution boundary: which stability/invariance theorems are new, which assumptions are relaxed/strengthened, and what was not possible in prior CKN analyses? A concise table or paragraph contrasting (i) multilayer kernel definition, (ii) discretization/recovery, (iii) diffeomorphism stability, (iv) group pooling, (v) inclusion of CNNs with homogeneous activations, would help.
- Beyond a pure RKHS reformulation: The paper claims “models from this functional space, such as a large class of CNNs, may enjoy the same stability.” Are there concrete classes of standard CNNs (architectures/activations) that are excluded or included? How critical is the homogeneity assumption and the nk(u) normalization in Eq. (8)? A discussion of how close common ReLU CNNs are (e.g., via smoothed ReLU limits) would strengthen impact.
- “Not losing signal information”: The paper emphasizes near-invertibility of intermediate layers (Lemmas 1–2). For supervised tasks, preserving all information may be unnecessary or even undesirable (information bottleneck perspective). Please motivate when invertibility is beneficial and how your theory trades off invariance/stability with sufficiency for the task. Can you relate pooling scales and subspace approximations to an explicit bias–variance or compression–invariance trade-off?
- Relation to invariant-kernel approaches: There seems to be a close connection between your last-layer group pooling and invariant kernels obtained by group integration/orbit averaging (e.g., Haasdonk & Burkhardt 2007; Mroueh et al. 2015; Raj et al. 2017). Please discuss equivalences/differences: is your Kn after final pooling equivalent to a group-averaged kernel? What advantages does the multilayer/local-stability structure add over a single invariant kernel?
- Relation to group-equivariant CNNs: Your G-equivariant patch/pooling mirrors group convolutions (Cohen & Welling 2016). Please clarify how your RKHS construction recovers group convolutions and whether your stability-to-diffeomorphisms extends to these architectures beyond exact group actions.
- Characterization of equivariance-inducing operators: For signals on a group with left action Lg, any bounded linear operator that commutes with all Lg is (up to technicalities) a right-convolution by a measure/kernel (via the commutant of the left regular representation). Could you add a formal statement referencing this result and situate your pooling/convolution within that class? For nonlinearities, is “pointwise channelwise nonlinearity” the maximal class commuting with Lg?
- Constants and depth dependence: The stability bound grows as O(n) in depth. Is this tight? Are there architectures (e.g., Parseval/orthogonal filters, normalized pooling) that mitigate constant growth? Any guidance for setting σk and Sk to optimize the C1, C2 trade-off?

Quality
- High. The analysis is rigorous, assumptions are reasonable, and proofs build on solid tools. Some constants and discretization details could be discussed more fully, but this does not undermine correctness.

Clarity
- Very clear and well organized. Figures and operator notation help. I suggest adding the requested positioning w.r.t. CKNs/invariant kernels and a short “practical implications” subsection.

Originality
- Moderate-to-high. Reformulating DCNNs as multilayer kernels is known from CKNs; scattering provides stability templates; group equivariance is well-studied. The novel contribution is the unified RKHS geometry tying together CKNs and a sizable class of CNNs, with stability/invariance guarantees and norm-based control.

Significance
- High. The results inform our theoretical understanding of CNN stability, equivariance, and generalization, and may influence principled architecture design and regularization.

Pros for acceptance
- Rigorous, unifying theory; clear stability/invariance results.
- Bridges CNNs and kernels with concrete norm bounds.
- General group handling and connections to practice via CKN.

Cons against acceptance
- Some contributions overlap conceptually with prior CKN/scattering/invariant-kernel work; need sharper positioning.
- Limited empirical validation of constants or practical guidance.

Recommendation
- Accept.

Additional minor comments
- Please gather kernel examples satisfying A1–A2 (e.g., inverse polynomial, arc-cosine) in the main text.
- A small empirical illustration (e.g., measuring stability vs σn on images) would be valuable.
- Typos were minimal; overall presentation is polished.

Confidence
- High.
