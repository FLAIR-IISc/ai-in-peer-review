This paper presents a theoretical framework for analyzing the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional architectures. Building upon prior work on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and the stability analysis of scattering networks (Mallat, 2012), the authors demonstrate that deep CKNs can achieve near-invariance to transformation groups and stability to diffeomorphisms. This is accomplished through a careful design of the patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operations. A key contribution is extending these theoretical guarantees from the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) of the CKN to a specific class of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) that employ smooth, homogeneous activation functions.

The paper's primary strength lies in its technical quality and clarity. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the geometric properties of deep representations, a topic of great importance to the community. The connection established between the kernel framework and standard CNNs is particularly significant, offering a principled way to understand why these models are robust to small input variations. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized, making the complex mathematical arguments easy to follow.

However, the major weakness of this work is the complete lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical results are elegant, their practical implications remain speculative without experimental support. The paper makes strong claims about the invariance and stability that can be achieved through specific design choices, but it provides no experiments on real or synthetic data to demonstrate these effects. This omission makes the contribution feel incomplete for a conference like NIPS, where theoretical advancements are often expected to be grounded by empirical evidence.

To significantly strengthen the paper, the authors should include experimental results that validate their theoretical analysis. For instance, they could demonstrate the CKN representation's stability under controlled diffeomorphisms or its invariance to a given transformation group on a standard dataset, measuring the norm of the difference in the feature space. Furthermore, an interesting point for discussion would be the impact of practical kernel approximations on the derived stability bounds. For example, could random projection or explicit kernel mapping via a finite number of Fourier features serve as valid approximations, and how would this choice quantitatively affect the stability guarantees?
