## Review of “Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations”

This paper presents a compelling theoretical analysis of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by framing them within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) context. The authors demonstrate that DCNN functional spaces can be described as multilayer kernels, exhibiting approximate group invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms – properties crucial for robustness and generalization. The work builds upon and generalizes previous efforts in convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and scattering transforms, offering a unified perspective on invariance in deep learning. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically rigorous. The mathematical derivations are clear, and the connection to existing literature (e.g., Mallat’s scattering transforms, kernel methods) is thoroughly established. The authors successfully extend the kernel framework to more general CNN architectures, providing theoretical guarantees for both CKNs and a broader class of networks with smooth activations. The emphasis on stability, particularly in relation to diffeomorphisms, is a strong point, offering insights into why CNNs perform well even with noisy or deformed inputs.

However, while the technical achievement is significant, the paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of *why* this RKHS reformulation is useful beyond a theoretical exercise. The authors touch upon implications for regularization and sample complexity, but a more detailed discussion of potential benefits – such as tighter generalization bounds or computational advantages stemming from the kernel structure – would strengthen the contribution. 

Furthermore, the novelty relative to existing work, particularly the “Convolutional Kernel Networks” (Mairal et al.), could be more explicitly delineated. While the authors generalize the CKN framework, a more focused comparison highlighting the specific advancements and broader applicability of their approach would be valuable. The statement about “not losing signal information” feels somewhat unmotivated; preserving all signal information isn’t always necessary or desirable, and the authors should address why it’s a priority in their framework. 

Finally, exploring connections to invariant-kernel approaches, such as the work of Hans Burkhardt, and investigating whether any operator commuting with all group elements guarantees DCNN equivariance, would further enrich the analysis and potentially open new avenues for research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and rigorous.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Demonstrates important theoretical properties of DCNNs.
*   Builds upon and generalizes existing work in a meaningful way.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of a compelling justification for the RKHS reformulation beyond theoretical insight.
*   Need for a more detailed comparison to CKNs.
*   Unclear motivation for preserving full signal information.



**Overall Recommendation:** Accept. Despite the minor points for improvement, the paper represents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of CNNs and warrants acceptance to NIPS.
