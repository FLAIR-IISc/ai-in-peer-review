This paper studies the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and their connection to more standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors generalize the multilayer kernel construction from CKNs and analyze the geometry of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). A central result demonstrates stability to the action of diffeomorphisms (smooth, invertible deformations) and near-invariance to groups of transformations, mirroring properties found in scattering transforms but extending them to a broader class of CNN architectures. The work is mathematically rigorous, well-organized, and provides a valuable contribution to understanding the theoretical foundations of deep learning.

The paper effectively builds on prior work in several areas. It acknowledges the successes of CNNs [14] while recognizing the lack of complete theoretical understanding of their invariance properties. The authors connect their work to the scattering transform [5, 17], leveraging techniques from harmonic analysis but aiming for broader applicability to CNNs. They also relate their approach to previous investigations into kernel methods for deep learning [2, 7, 10, 15, 16, 18, 25-27, 31, 34, 36] and group-invariant representations [6, 9, 22]. The introduction and related work sections adequately position the paper within the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper is mathematically rigorous, with clear definitions and proofs. The stability results are derived using a solid foundation in kernel theory and harmonic analysis.
* **Clarity:** The writing is generally clear and well-structured, making the complex mathematical concepts accessible. The organization of the paper, progressing from kernel construction to stability analysis and connections to CNNs, is logical.
* **Originality:** The work offers a novel perspective on analyzing CNNs through the lens of kernel methods and RKHS geometry. Extending the stability analysis beyond scattering transforms to a wider class of networks is a significant contribution.
* **Significance:** Understanding the stability and invariance properties of deep networks is crucial for improving their robustness, generalization ability, and sample complexity. The results presented here have the potential to inform the design and training of more reliable and efficient deep learning models. The connection to adversarial robustness, while briefly mentioned, highlights a potentially impactful application.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Motivation:** While the paper states the importance of understanding invariance and stability, the motivation for focusing specifically on stability and its broader implications (beyond adversarial robustness) could be strengthened. A more detailed discussion of why stability is a desirable property in various applications would be beneficial.
* **Notation:** The symbol *κ* is overloaded. It is used in assumption (A3) and also appears within the kernel function definition in Eq. (10), potentially causing confusion. A different symbol should be used for one of these instances.
* **Mathematical Simplification:** The second constraint in the equation between lines 384-385 appears redundant due to the symmetry property of kernel functions.
* **Missing Definition:** The symbol *φ* is not explicitly defined at line 416; the authors should refer to its definition as the feature map associated with kernel K via Eq. (10).
* **Minor Errors:** The inequality around lines 427–428 appears to hold with equality, and a typographical error seems to exist; ||z|| - ||z|| should be ||z|| - ||z'||.
* **Bibliography:** Several references are missing page information ([3,6,8,13,14,18,25–27,30,31]). Additionally, some references require updated publication/acceptance details, such as [9, 17, 19, 32].

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound analysis of the stability and invariance properties of deep convolutional representations.
* The results extend existing theoretical frameworks (like scattering transforms) to a broader class of CNN architectures.
* The clear writing and logical organization make the complex material accessible to a wider audience.
* The potential implications for improving the robustness and generalization ability of deep learning models are significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The motivation for the stability analysis could be stronger and more detailed.
* Minor notational and typographical issues exist that could be easily addressed.
* The bibliography requires some minor corrections.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and deserves acceptance. The technical rigor, clarity, and originality of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses. Addressing the suggested improvements (strengthening motivation, clarifying notation, correcting errors, and completing the bibliography) would further enhance the quality of the paper. The results presented offer valuable insights into the theoretical foundations of deep learning and have the potential to impact the development of more robust and reliable models.
