This paper studies deep convolutional representations from the perspective of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), demonstrating that a large class of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) can be understood within a kernel framework. Specifically, the authors generalize convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to develop a multilayer convolutional kernel, proving invariance to groups of transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms—properties reminiscent of the scattering transform, but applicable to a broader class of models. The work is technically sound, clearly written, and builds upon a strong foundation of related work in kernel methods, signal processing, and deep learning.  I recommend acceptance.

The core contribution lies in establishing a connection between standard CNN architectures and RKHS theory. By framing CNNs as operating within a specific RKHS, the authors leverage tools from functional analysis to rigorously analyze their properties, namely invariance and stability. This provides a theoretical underpinning for the empirical successes of CNNs and opens avenues for further analysis, particularly regarding generalization.  However, the authors would benefit from more explicitly articulating *why* this reformulation is useful. While the technical achievement is significant, a stronger discussion of its implications—for example, connections to generalization bounds, potential for improved regularization techniques, or computational advantages—would strengthen the paper considerably. 

The paper builds upon previous work on CKNs (Mairal et al.). The authors acknowledge this connection but should further clarify the novelty of their approach *beyond* simply extending CKNs. Does the RKHS perspective afforded by their generalization provide new insights or capabilities not present in the original CKN framework? A more detailed comparison highlighting the specific advances offered by this work would be valuable.

The authors emphasize the preservation of “signal information” as a key property. While mathematically sound, the practical relevance of preserving *all* signal information is questionable.  Many downstream tasks only require task-relevant features, and discarding irrelevant information can improve performance and robustness. The authors should address this point, perhaps discussing the trade-offs between signal preservation and task-specific performance.

The related work section references a broad range of papers, which is commendable.  However, it would be beneficial to explicitly discuss connections to invariant kernel approaches, such as the work of Hans Burkhardt on invariant kernel functions. Exploring this connection could provide additional context and highlight potential synergies.

Finally, the discussion of group invariance raises an interesting theoretical question.  The authors demonstrate how specific constructions can achieve equivariance. Is there a broader characterization?  Specifically, does *any* operator that commutes with all group elements guarantee DCNN equivariance? Addressing this question, even briefly, would further solidify the theoretical contributions of the paper. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a mathematically sound analysis of CNNs within the RKHS framework.
*   **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, making the complex concepts accessible.
*   **Strong Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the relevant literature.
*   **Novel Contribution:**  The generalization of CKNs and the resulting theoretical insights are a significant contribution.
*   **Potential for Future Work:** The framework established in this paper provides a solid foundation for future research in areas such as generalization bounds and regularization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical benefits of the RKHS reformulation.
*   **Clarification of Novelty Needed:** The novelty compared to existing CKN work could be more clearly articulated.
*   **Questionable Emphasis on Full Signal Preservation:** The necessity of preserving all signal information is not fully justified.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the understanding of CNNs.
*   The results are significant and have the potential to influence future research in the field.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical implications of the work are not fully explored.
*   The novelty compared to existing work could be more clearly articulated.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions. The technical rigor, clarity of presentation, and potential for future work outweigh the concerns. I strongly recommend acceptance to NIPS 2017.
