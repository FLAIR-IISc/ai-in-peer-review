This paper presents a rigorous analysis of deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs) through the lens of multilayer kernels, demonstrating approximate group invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms. The work is well-written, technically sound, and represents a significant step towards understanding the theoretical foundations of DCNNs. I recommend acceptance.

The core contribution lies in reformulating DCNNs within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. While elegant and mathematically solid, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of *why* this reformulation is valuable. Currently, the motivation is primarily about gaining intuition. Specifying potential implications for generalization bounds (e.g., tighter bounds via RKHS norm control) or computational/approximation advantages (e.g., kernel-based optimization techniques) would strengthen the paper’s significance.  

The authors state the work generalizes the construction of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) [Mairal et al.]. It’s crucial to clearly delineate the novelty beyond this generalization. Is the novelty primarily in extending the theory to a broader class of CNN architectures, or are there novel theoretical insights gained *through* this broader application? A more explicit comparison, highlighting the specific advantages of this approach over CKNs, is needed.

I question the emphasis on “not losing signal information.” While technically interesting, preserving full signal information might be unnecessary for many practical tasks. Many applications only require task-relevant information. Justifying the importance of this preservation – perhaps by demonstrating its benefit in specific scenarios or highlighting its connection to robustness – would be helpful. Does retaining all information hinder generalization by preventing the network from focusing on relevant features?

The authors allude to connections with invariant kernel approaches, but a more detailed discussion relating to the work of Hans Burkhardt on invariant kernel functions [Haasdonk & Burkhardt, 2006] would be valuable. Are the approaches fundamentally similar, or does this work offer a novel perspective?  Specifically, how does the multilayer structure affect the types of invariances that can be achieved compared to Burkhardt’s approach?

I am curious about a related theoretical question. Would *any* operator that commutes with all group elements guarantee DCNN equivariance? Could this work be extended to provide a characterization of operators that ensure equivariance with respect to arbitrary groups? This characterization could offer a powerful tool for designing equivariant architectures.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Rigor:** The paper is mathematically rigorous, with clear definitions and proofs.
*   **Novel Perspective:** The RKHS formulation provides a fresh perspective on DCNNs.
*   **Stability and Invariance Results:** The demonstrated stability to diffeomorphisms and approximate group invariance are significant theoretical results.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper builds upon and connects to a strong body of existing research in kernel methods, scattering transforms, and CNNs.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Explicit Motivation:** The benefits of the RKHS reformulation beyond intuition are not fully articulated.
*   **Limited Novelty Discussion:** The novelty relative to CKNs needs to be more clearly defined.
*   **Unjustified Signal Preservation:** The importance of preserving all signal information is not fully justified.
*   **Missing Connections:** A deeper discussion of connections to Burkhardt's work on invariant kernels is lacking.
*   **Unexplored Theoretical Extension:** The potential for characterizing equivariance-ensuring operators is not explored.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a rigorous and novel theoretical analysis of DCNNs.
*   The results on stability and invariance are significant contributions to the field.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The paper addresses an important open problem in understanding the foundations of deep learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of explicit motivation for the RKHS reformulation weakens the overall impact of the work.
*   The novelty relative to existing work (CKNs) could be better articulated.
