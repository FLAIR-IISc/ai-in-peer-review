- Studies deep convolutional representations via positive-definite kernels and RKHS, defining a multilayer convolutional kernel that mirrors CNN layer structure on continuous domains.  
- Proves stability to diffeomorphisms in Mallat’s Lipschitz sense, with translation invariance controlled by the final pooling scale.  
- Shows near-invariance to general group actions using group-covariant patch extraction and pooling.  
- Proves that many CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lie in the constructed RKHS, transferring stability via norm control.  
- Treats discretization and kernel approximation (CKN-style projections) and provides bounds that preserve non-expansiveness and stability.  
- Gives conditions ensuring information preservation under subsampling and deconvolution-based recovery (ek ≥ sk).  
- Provides bounds separating roles of local pooling (stability) and final pooling (global invariance).  
- Connects rigorously to scattering (Bruna & Mallat) while extending to broader convolutional architectures and CKNs.  
- Relation to prior work: replaces fixed wavelets with general kernel-induced layers and includes non-wavelet CNNs via RKHS containment.  
- Relation to CKNs: generalizes CKNs to continuous domains, clarifies signal preservation/discretization, and proves stability under kernel approximations.  
- Relation to group-equivariant CNNs: aligns with equivariance logic by building operators that commute with group actions and provides functional-analytic stability.  
- Relation to invariant-kernel/orbit methods: operates within a multilayer convolutional RKHS emphasizing diffeomorphism stability, distinct from prior global invariant-kernel constructions.  
- Technical quality: high—explicit assumptions, proofs in appendix, clean separation of deformation and translation in key stability inequality.  
- Continuous–discrete bridge and kernel-projection arguments are carefully justified to preserve non-expansiveness.  
- Limitation: constants include a 1+n depth dependence and potential norm shrinkage through pooling (mitigated by normalized top kernel map).  
- Clarity: manuscript is well organized and readable; suggests consolidating notation, highlighting homogeneous-kernel assumption satisfaction, and summarizing constants’ dependence.  
- Originality: unified multilayer kernel whose RKHS contains CNNs, enabling scattering-style stability transfer, group-equivariant pooling mechanism, and multilayer signal-preservation guarantees.  
- Significance: offers principled control of stability/invariance, clarifies pooling scale roles, and links RKHS norms to generalization/stability with potential design implications.  
- Weakness: novelty vs. prior CKN work needs explicit clarification (continuous-domain, diffeomorphism bounds, CNN inclusion—specify which are new).  
- Question: practical relevance of “full information preservation” given common task-driven nuisance filtering and finite-support/noisy pipelines.  
- Question: can RKHS norm bounds yield explicit generalization bounds (Rademacher/margin) compared to standard CNN analyses?  
- Question: exclusion of ReLU—how sensitive are bounds to smoothing and do they predict true ReLU behavior?  
- Question: clarify dependence of constants (C1) on κ and d and tightness of the (1+n) factor; can pooling schedules improve depth dependence?  
- Question: characterize all linear operators commuting with group action (e.g., convolutions with class functions) and their equivariance in this framework.  
- Suggestion: add a small empirical illustration (CKN or smoothed CNN) showing deformation Lipschitz behavior vs σn.  
- Suggestion: provide practical guidance for choosing ek, sk, σk and discuss robustness of deconvolution recovery to noise.  
- Suggestion: expand discussion of controlling RKHS norm in practice (weight decay, Parseval regularization) and observed effects on robustness.  
- Pros for acceptance: rigorous theory extending scattering-style stability to convolutional kernel representations and CNNs, modular separation of concepts, bridges multiple literatures.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical validation, potential over-emphasis on signal preservation, and novelty relative to prior CKNs needs crisper articulation.  
- Recommendation: accept.
