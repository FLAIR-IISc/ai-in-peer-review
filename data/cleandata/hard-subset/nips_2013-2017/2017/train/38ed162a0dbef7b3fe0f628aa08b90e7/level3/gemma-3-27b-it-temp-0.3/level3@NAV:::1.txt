## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper investigates the theoretical foundations of invariance and stability in deep convolutional networks (CNNs), framing the analysis through the lens of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The authors demonstrate that properly designed CKNs exhibit near-invariance to transformation groups and stability to diffeomorphisms, and importantly, extend these properties to a broader class of CNNs under certain assumptions. This work builds upon previous research in scattering transforms [17], kernel methods [15, 16], and group-invariant representations [6, 9], offering a kernel-based perspective to understand the geometric properties of these networks. The manuscript is well-written, technically sound, and presents a clear and logical progression of ideas. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the connections to existing literature are well-established. 

A key strength of this paper lies in its theoretical contribution. By leveraging kernel theory, the authors provide a formal framework for analyzing invariance and stability, offering insights into why CNNs might generalize well despite their large number of parameters. The extension of these properties from CKNs to CNNs is a significant step towards a more general understanding of deep learning. The paper also clearly articulates the link between stability and generalization, suggesting that controlling the RKHS norm can lead to improved sample complexity. 

However, the most significant weakness is the complete lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical results are compelling, they remain untested. The authors claim that their findings apply to CNNs, but without experimental results demonstrating the invariance and stability properties of CKNs or CNNs under the proposed settings, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of their work. Demonstrating these properties, even on simple datasets, would significantly strengthen the paper.

An open question raised by the authors concerns the use of random projections or explicit kernel mapping via Fourier features as kernel approximations. Exploring this avenue and analyzing its impact on stability would be a valuable extension of this work. It’s unclear whether these approximations would maintain the desirable stability properties, and investigating this trade-off between computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees would be insightful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous mathematical analysis.
*   Novel application of kernel theory to analyze CNNs.
*   Clear and well-written manuscript.
*   Potential to advance our understanding of generalization in deep learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Theoretical results need to be grounded in experimental evidence.



**Overall:** While the paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution, the absence of experimental results is a major drawback. I recommend **rejection** pending the inclusion of empirical validation. The authors should prioritize demonstrating the invariance and stability properties of their proposed framework through experiments, even if limited in scope.
