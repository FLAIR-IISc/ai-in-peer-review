Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals on continuous domains and studies its invariance to groups and stability to diffeomorphisms. The construction generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to a continuous setting, interleaving patch extraction, a homogeneous dot-product kernel map, and Gaussian pooling at increasing scales. The authors prove (i) information preservation across layers under appropriate sampling/deconvolution assumptions; (ii) Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms with a bound of the form C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞, paralleling scattering results but for a broader family of architectures; (iii) a general recipe for global invariance to locally compact groups via group-covariant patching and pooling; and (iv) that a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, so their predictions inherit the same stability when their RKHS norm is controlled.

The work sits between scattering transforms (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) and CKNs (Mairal et al.). Compared to scattering, it relaxes architectural constraints beyond wavelets and connects stability to RKHS geometry; compared to CKNs, it provides continuous-domain analysis, deformation stability, and group invariance guarantees. It also relates to invariant kernel methods (e.g., Haasdonk & Burkhardt) and group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling), framing invariance via poolings on groups.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous analysis with well-motivated assumptions (homogeneous kernels; non-expansive maps; patch-size/scale coupling).
- Stability bound to diffeomorphisms (Theorem 6) that mirrors scattering while covering a broader class of architectures and allowing kernel approximations.
- Clean group-theoretic treatment of equivariance/invariance via Haar integration for pooling; flexible enough to mix stability to subgroups with global invariance at the last layer.
- Useful link between RKHS norm, Lipschitz stability, and generalization for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (Proposition 7).
- Thoughtful discussion of discretization and information preservation across layers.

Weaknesses / questions
- Novelty vs reformulation: Several results reinterpret known ideas (equivariance via commuting operators; stability bounds akin to scattering) in RKHS terms. Please sharpen what is genuinely new beyond the RKHS formalism and beyond prior CKN work: e.g., which stability statements cannot be derived from scattering or CKN papers?
- Scope of CNNs covered: Proposition 7 requires smooth homogeneous activations; ReLU is handled only via smoothing. How representative is the covered class for practical CNNs? Can you quantify the approximation gap for ReLU networks?
- Signal preservation: Lemmas 1–2 rely on deconvolution and coverage assumptions; practically, deconvolution can be ill-conditioned and boundary effects matter. Please clarify the stability of recovery, the role of σk relative to sk, and whether “not losing information” is necessary or beneficial for downstream tasks that only require task-relevant invariances.
- Depth dependence: The bound grows with (1 + n)‖∇τ‖∞ and translation invariance scales as 1/σn. Are there choices of σk, Sk that mitigate the linear dependence on depth? Any lower bounds indicating inevitability?
- Constants and assumptions: C1 grows with κd+1; A3 ties patch radii to pooling scales. Please comment on realistic ranges for these constants in typical architectures and their numerical impact.
- Empirical/practical implications: The paper argues that RKHS norm control yields stability and potentially better sample complexity, but no explicit generalization bounds are provided for the multilayer kernel/RKHS class. Can you offer margin/Rademacher-style bounds specialized to your construction, or at least discuss capacity control vs architecture depth/width?
- Relation to invariant kernels and G-CNNs: The link to invariant kernels (e.g., Haasdonk & Burkhardt) could be elaborated—your local pooling/composition vs global orbit/integration trade-offs, and when one is preferable. Similarly, how does your group pooling framework relate to group convolutions (Cohen & Welling) and their completeness theorems?
- Characterizing equivariant operators: Beyond the specific pooling defined via Haar convolution, is equivariance here equivalent to commuting with all Lg? Can you provide a characterization (e.g., via representation theory) of the class of linear operators on L2(G) that are G-equivariant in your setting?

Quality
- Theoretical results appear technically sound; the stability bound is carefully decomposed via commutators and Schur’s test, with transparent use of assumptions (A1–A3). The discretization section is honest about border effects and the role of deconvolution. The RKHS inclusion of CNNs is well constructed for smooth homogeneous activations, with a norm bound that propagates layerwise.
- Caveats: stability of signal recovery under deconvolution and the sensitivity of constants deserve further discussion. The continuous-discrete link is plausible but relies on idealizations (e.g., exact Gaussian smoothing).

Clarity
- The paper is well written and well organized. The separation between continuous theory, discretization, kernel approximations, and links to CNNs is clear. It would help to:
  - Make the novelty vs prior CKN/scattering work explicit in the introduction.
  - Summarize the class of admissible kernels/activations in the main text (now in Appendix B).
  - Provide intuition for Proposition 7 and a compact example instantiating the construction.

Originality
- Moderate to high. The synthesis—extending scattering-style stability to a general multilayer kernel/CNN framework and tying it to RKHS geometry—is valuable. However, several building blocks (equivariance via commuting, group pooling, invariant kernels) are known; the paper’s originality lies in unifying and extending them to this kernelized multilayer setting with clear stability guarantees.

Significance
- The work advances theoretical understanding of deep convolutional representations, providing tools to reason about invariance/stability and their link to generalization via RKHS norms. This is likely to influence further theory on deep architectures and kernel approximations. Practical impact would be stronger with explicit bounds and/or empirical demonstrations, but the theoretical contribution is substantial.

Pros for acceptance
- Rigorous, clear theory generalizing scattering stability to broader convolutional architectures.
- Conceptual unification of CNNs and CKNs within an RKHS where norms control stability/generalization.
- Flexible and principled treatment of group equivariance/invariance.

Cons against acceptance
- Some contributions are reformulations of known ideas; the incremental novelty vs CKNs and scattering could be clearer.
- Practical implications (generalization bounds, constants, coverage of ReLU) are not fully developed.
- Information preservation claims hinge on idealized deconvolution.

Questions to authors
1) Beyond the RKHS reformulation, what new insights or capabilities does your framework provide relative to scattering and CKNs (e.g., sharper stability, broader architectures, learning/regularization benefits)?
2) Can you quantify how well smoothed activations approximate ReLU in your RKHS and the effect on stability/generalization?
3) Please elaborate on the connection to invariant-kernel approaches (Haasdonk & Burkhardt) and contrast global orbit integration with your layered local pooling.
4) Can you characterize all linear operators that are G-equivariant in your setting (i.e., commute with Lg for all g), and situate your pooling within that class?
5) Could you provide or sketch margin/Rademacher bounds specialized to your multilayer RKHS to substantiate the sample-complexity claims?

Recommendation: Accept. This is a solid and timely theoretical contribution that clarifies the invariance/stability properties of deep convolutional representations and links them to RKHS geometry, with potential impact on both theory and practice.
