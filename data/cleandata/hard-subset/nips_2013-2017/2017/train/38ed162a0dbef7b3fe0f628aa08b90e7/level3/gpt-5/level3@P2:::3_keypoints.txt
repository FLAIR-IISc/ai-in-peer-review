- Paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel for continuous-domain signals, generalizing CKNs with patch extraction, homogeneous dot-product kernel maps, and Gaussian pooling.  
- Main theoretical results: information preservation under sampling/deconvolution; Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms with bound C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞; recipe for group invariance via covariant patching/pooling; many smooth homogeneous CNNs lie in the induced RKHS.  
- Positions work between scattering transforms and CKNs, relaxing wavelet constraints and providing continuous-domain deformation stability and group-invariance guarantees.  
- Strength: rigorous, well-motivated analysis with clear assumptions (homogeneous kernels, non-expansive maps, patch/scale coupling).  
- Strength: stability bound mirrors scattering but applies to a broader class of architectures and allows kernel approximations.  
- Strength: clean group-theoretic treatment using Haar integration enabling flexible subgroup stability and final-layer global invariance.  
- Strength: links RKHS norm to Lipschitz stability and generalization for smooth homogeneous-activation CNNs.  
- Strength: thoughtful discussion of discretization and layerwise information preservation.  
- Weakness: novelty concern—many results reinterpret known ideas in RKHS language; need clearer statements of what is genuinely new beyond prior CKN/scattering work.  
- Weakness: Proposition 7 requires smooth homogeneous activations; ReLU only via smoothing—unclear how representative this is for practical CNNs or the approximation gap for ReLU.  
- Weakness: signal preservation relies on deconvolution/coverage assumptions—practical ill-conditioning, boundary effects, and stability of recovery need clarification.  
- Weakness: depth dependence—stability bound grows with (1+n) and translation invariance scales as 1/σn; unclear if choices of σk, sk can mitigate linear depth dependence or if lower bounds exist.  
- Weakness: constants/assumptions (C1 growth, A3 linking patch radii to pooling) need comment on realistic ranges and numerical impact in typical architectures.  
- Weakness: empirical/practical implications underdeveloped—no explicit generalization bounds for multilayer kernel/RKHS; request for margin/Rademacher-style bounds or capacity discussion.  
- Weakness: relation to invariant kernels and G-CNNs could be elaborated—trade-offs between local layered pooling and global orbit integration, and relation to group-convolution completeness.  
- Weakness: characterization of equivariant operators beyond Haar pooling is requested—are equivariant operators exactly those commuting with Lg and can representation theory characterize them?  
- Quality: theoretical results appear technically sound, with careful decomposition and transparent assumptions; discretization discussion honest but idealized.  
- Clarity: paper is well written and organized; suggested improvements include explicit novelty statement, main-text summary of admissible kernels/activations, and intuitive example for Proposition 7.  
- Originality: moderate–high—valuable synthesis extending scattering-style stability to a general multilayer kernel/CNN framework, though many components are known.  
- Significance: advances theoretical understanding of invariance/stability and connects RKHS norms to generalization; practical impact would benefit from explicit bounds or experiments.  
- Pros for acceptance: rigorous generalization of scattering stability, unification of CNNs and CKNs in RKHS, and principled group equivariance treatment.  
- Cons against acceptance: some contributions are reformulations of known ideas, practical implications and constants are underdeveloped, and information-preservation relies on idealized deconvolution.  
- Key questions for authors: clarify novel insights beyond RKHS reformulation; quantify ReLU approximation via smoothing; contrast local layered pooling vs global invariant kernels; characterize all G-equivariant linear operators; provide/sketch margin or Rademacher bounds.  
- Recommendation: Accept—solid and timely theoretical contribution with potential impact on theory and practice.
