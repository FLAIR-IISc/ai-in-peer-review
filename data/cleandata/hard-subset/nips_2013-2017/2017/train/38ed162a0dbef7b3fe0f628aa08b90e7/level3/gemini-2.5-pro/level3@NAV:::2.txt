This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the stability and invariance properties of deep convolutional representations, framed within the context of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs). Drawing inspiration from the analysis of scattering transforms, the authors study the stability of these representations with respect to C^1 diffeomorphisms. The main theoretical result is a proof that stability is guaranteed for CKNs constructed with norm-preserving and non-expansive kernels (A1-A2) and appropriately constrained patch sizes (A3). The work connects the rich theory of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces to modern deep learning architectures and sketches a generalization of the stability analysis from translations to the action of locally compact groups.

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength is its rigorous and technically sound theoretical contribution. By grounding the analysis of deep convolutional architectures in kernel theory, it provides a principled way to understand their stability properties. The manuscript is well-organized and clearly written, successfully bridging concepts from kernel methods, signal processing, and deep learning. The connection established between the CKN framework and conventional CNNs with specific homogeneous activations is a significant step toward a more unified theory. The stability result is novel and of clear interest to the machine learning community, particularly for researchers studying geometric deep learning, invariance, and the theoretical underpinnings of adversarial robustness.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**
While the paper is strong, several areas could be improved:
1.  **Motivation:** The motivation for studying stability to diffeomorphisms is underdeveloped. The link to adversarial robustness is mentioned in a single sentence but deserves more elaboration to fully convey the work's significance and impact.
2.  **Clarity and Notation:** There are minor notational issues that could confuse readers. The symbol `\kappa` is overloaded, representing a constant related to patch size in assumption (A3) and also the kernel function in Equation (2). Additionally, the feature map `\phi` should be explicitly reintroduced in the context of `K_k` to ensure clarity.
3.  **Bibliography:** The bibliography requires significant revision. Page information is missing for many references (e.g., [3, 6, 8, 13, 14]), and several others need to be updated with their final publication details (e.g., [9, 17, 19]).

**Conclusion:**
This is a high-quality paper presenting an important theoretical result on the stability of deep convolutional representations. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to presentation and can be addressed in a revision. The work is a valuable contribution to understanding the principles behind deep networks and is well-suited for the conference.
