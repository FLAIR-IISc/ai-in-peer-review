Summary
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation that (i) is near-invariant to translations via pooling, (ii) is provably stable to C^1 diffeomorphisms under Lipschitz-type bounds, and (iii) preserves signal information across layers (up to pooling) with a principled discretization and RKHS-compatible approximations. The central stability theorem shows that, under assumptions A1–A3—norm-preserving, non-expansive kernel maps and patch sizes tied to pooling scales—the representation Φ satisfies a deformation stability bound of the form C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞. A sketch is provided to extend equivariance/invariance from translations on (R^d, +) to general locally compact groups via Haar integration, enabling global invariance through last-layer pooling. The RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel is shown to contain a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations; hence the stability transfers to these CNNs and their predictions can be controlled by the RKHS norm. The work generalizes scattering transform stability (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) from fixed wavelet networks to learned kernel/CNN-type architectures (CKNs), connecting deep networks and kernel methods.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on CKNs (Mairal et al.), extending to continuous domains and providing deformation stability guarantees.
- Adapts harmonic analysis tools from scattering transforms to a more general kernel/CNN framework.
- Complements group-equivariant CNNs by providing a kernel/RKHS route to equivariance and invariance on locally compact groups.

Strengths
- Quality: The analysis is technically careful and sound. Operator-commutator bounds with Schur’s test are used cleanly; assumptions (A1–A3) are explicit and reasonable for homogeneous dot-product kernels. The link to CNNs inside the RKHS is non-trivial and well motivated.
- Clarity: The manuscript is well organized and clearly written; discretization and approximation steps are discussed, and the impact on stability is explained.
- Originality: Extends scattering-style stability to a significantly broader class (CKNs and a subset of CNNs) and sketches a natural generalization to locally compact groups.
- Significance: Provides theoretical foundations for stability/invariance in architectures close to practical CNNs and kernel networks, offering a unifying view where RKHS norms control both generalization and deformation robustness.

Weaknesses
- Motivation: The practical motivation for deformation-stability is too brief (a single sentence linking Lipschitzness to adversarial robustness). The paper would benefit from a broader perspective on why diffeomorphism stability matters empirically and statistically.
- Constants and assumptions: The dependence on κ and dimension in Lemma 4 (scaling like κ^(d+1)) and the linear dependence on depth (1+n) in Theorem 6 could be discussed more, including tightness and practical implications. Assumption (A3) may constrain patch/pooling design.
- Approximation/discretization: While non-expansive projections preserve stability qualitatively, a more explicit accounting of approximation error in the constants would help practitioners.
- Experiments: The work is primarily theoretical; even brief empirical illustrations (toy deformations, constants vs. depth/σn) would strengthen the case for impact.

Detailed comments and requested fixes
- Stronger motivation: Please expand beyond adversarial robustness. Suggested angles:
  - Sample complexity: stability-implies-margin control via RKHS norms and data augmentation equivalences for small diffeomorphisms.
  - Distribution shift: robustness to geometric nuisance variability (e.g., small pose/elastic deformations).
  - Certified robustness: the analysis bounds can yield certificates against bounded C^1 deformations.
  - Links to generalization: covering-number/Rademacher bounds for Lipschitz predictors in RKHSs.
- Notation: κ is overloaded—used in (A3) as the patch-to-pooling ratio and also as the scalar profile of the dot-product kernel in Eq. (10). Consider renaming one (e.g., use ρ for the patch-size factor, or ψ for the scalar kernel profile) to avoid confusion.
- Math simplification: The second constraint in the displayed equation between lines 384–385 is redundant by kernel symmetry; it can be dropped without changing the argument.
- Missing definition: ϕ is not defined at line 416; introduce it via Eq. (10) as the feature map for K before first use.
- Typo/correction:
  - The inequality around lines 427–428 appears to hold with equality.
  - Replace ||z|| − ||z|| with ||z|| − ||z′||.
- Bibliography:
  - Please add page information (and volume/issue where applicable) for [3,6,8,13,14,18,25–27,30,31].
  - Update publication/acceptance details for [9] (ICML; include pages), [17] (Communications on Pure and Applied Mathematics; full citation), [19] (NIPS; final details), [32] (Network: Computation in Neural Systems 14(3):391–412, 2003).
- Group extension (Section 4): The construction is clear and elegant. It would help to:
  - State a formal proposition clarifying conditions under which the last-layer pooling yields global invariance on G (and how intermediate pooling on subgroups yields stability).
  - Comment on deformation stability on groups (beyond equivariance), and whether analogues of Lemma 4 carry over with Haar integration.
- Discretization and CKN approximation (Sections 2.1–2.2): Clarify how the constants in Theorem 6 are affected by subsampling, boundary effects, and projection error; a small bound or discussion would guide implementation choices (ek, sk, σk).

Evaluation
- Quality: High; proofs are rigorous with clear assumptions. Some constants and practical ramifications merit more discussion. 8/10.
- Clarity: High, with minor notation/definition issues noted. 8/10.
- Originality: Good extension of known ideas to a broader, more practical class. 7.5/10.
- Significance: Theoretical insights likely to inform future architectures and analyses; immediate practical impact could be bolstered by experiments. 7.5/10.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, general, and useful theoretical contribution bridging kernels and CNNs.
- Clear statement of assumptions and elegant use of operator methods.
- Extension to locally compact groups broadens applicability.

Cons for acceptance
- Motivation for deformation stability is underdeveloped.
- Limited empirical illustration.
- Some notational and bibliographic rough edges.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a valuable and technically sound advancement on invariance and stability for deep convolutional representations, with clear relevance to both the kernel and deep learning communities. Addressing the motivation and minor issues above would further strengthen the final version.
