

# Review for NIPS 2017

This paper investigates deep convolutional representations through the lens of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), generalizing the multilayer kernel framework of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to study invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors demonstrate that the functional space defined by their kernel construction contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, allowing them to establish theoretical stability guarantees similar to those of the scattering transform [17], but for more standard CNN architectures.

The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging the gap between the theoretical understanding of signal representations (as in Mallat's work) and practical CNN models. The authors rigorously prove that the proposed kernel representation preserves signal information while achieving stability to diffeomorphisms, with the stability bounds depending on the pooling scale of the final layer. They further show how these stability properties translate to predictive models through the RKHS norm, which controls both stability and generalization. This connection between stability and generalization is particularly valuable, as it provides theoretical justification for why stable representations may lead to improved sample complexity.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically sound, with clear mathematical exposition and thorough proofs. The organization is logical, moving from theoretical construction to practical discretization, stability analysis, and finally to connections with CNNs. The authors carefully distinguish their work from related approaches, particularly CKNs [15,16] and the scattering transform [5,17], and demonstrate how their framework generalizes and extends these previous results.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a compelling case for extending the theoretical analysis of signal representations to standard CNN architectures, rather than the pre-defined filter approaches of the scattering transform. However, the novelty relative to Mairal's CKN work requires further clarification. The authors should more explicitly state how their theoretical framework differs from and extends the CKN framework, particularly regarding the benefits of the RKHS perspective for CNNs.

The significance of this work is strong, as it provides a theoretical foundation for understanding why CNNs work well in practice. The connection between stability and generalization through RKHS norms offers a principled way to control model complexity and may inform future regularization strategies. However, the paper could better articulate the practical implications of these theoretical results for model design and training.

I have a few specific concerns that need addressing. First, the emphasis on "not losing signal information" requires justification—why preserve full signal information when many tasks only require task-relevant information? The authors should discuss the trade-offs between information preservation and task-specific representation efficiency.

Second, the utility of the RKHS reformulation needs clearer articulation. The paper should explicitly state how this perspective provides new insights or advantages over direct analysis of CNNs, such as improved generalization bounds or computational advantages. The connection to invariant-kernel approaches (e.g., Burkhardt's work [13]) would also strengthen the theoretical context.

Third, the characterization of operators ensuring equivariance to group actions needs further elaboration. The paper mentions that certain operators commute with group actions to achieve equivariance, but a more complete characterization would be valuable.

The paper's main strength is its rigorous theoretical contribution to understanding CNN representations through the RKHS framework, which could inform future work on stability and generalization. The clear exposition and strong technical foundation make it a valuable addition to the NIPS literature.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Rigorous theoretical contribution to understanding CNNs
- Clear connection between kernel theory and CNNs
- Demonstrates stability properties with meaningful implications for generalization
- Well-written and technically sound with thorough proofs

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Needs stronger justification for why the RKHS reformulation is useful beyond theoretical interest
- Novelty relative to CKNs requires clearer articulation
- Questionable emphasis on preserving full signal information
- Connection to invariant-kernel approaches (Burkhardt) could be strengthened

I recommend acceptance with the following revisions: (1) clarify the utility of the RKHS reformulation for practical model design and generalization; (2) more explicitly differentiate the contribution from CKNs and other kernel-based approaches; (3) address the signal preservation question by discussing trade-offs with task-relevant information; and (4) strengthen the connection to invariant-kernel literature. These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and impact.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of deep convolutional representations. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS 2017.
