

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper proposes a kernel framework for studying theoretical properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs), extending the scattering transform approach to more standard CNN architectures. The authors define a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and establishes stability properties under diffeomorphisms. They connect these representations to CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, showing how stability results translate to predictive models.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by bridging the gap between kernel methods and CNNs, extending the scattering transform's stability analysis to more standard architectures. The framework provides a principled way to control stability and generalization through RKHS norms, which is valuable for understanding CNNs' behavior.

**Weaknesses:** Despite its theoretical promise, the paper contains numerous technical and formatting inconsistencies that undermine its credibility as a scientific contribution:

1. **Formatting errors:** The paper contains a clear typo in the conference reference ("st Conference" instead of "31st Conference") and inconsistent notation (e.g., z' vs. z' in Equation 1).

2. **Critical missing content:** The paper repeatedly refers to "Appendix B" for concrete kernel examples but provides no such appendix. The proof of Lemma 1 is relegated to "Appendix C" which is not included. The paper cites "the extended version of the paper [3]" but fails to include this reference in the bibliography.

3. **Mathematical inconsistencies:** Equation (2) uses inconsistent notation (Kk(z, z ′) vs. Kk(z, z')), and Theorem 6's bound (C2 σn ‖τ‖∞) doesn't match the form in Equation (4) (C2‖τ‖∞), suggesting a missing normalization factor.

4. **Technical gaps:** The paper claims "for appropriate σ" in Section 5, but never defines what "appropriate" means for the activation function. The connection to CNNs is vague - the authors state "the most successful activation function... is homogeneous" but don't explain how ReLU (which is nonsmooth) fits their framework.

5. **Reference issues:** The paper cites "the extended version of the paper [3]" multiple times without including this reference in the bibliography, making it impossible to verify claims about norm preservation with an additional prediction layer.

6. **Discretization claims:** In Section 2.1, the authors claim "we choose s0 = 1 in the following, without loss of generality," but this sampling interval assumption is not generally valid and significantly impacts the theoretical results.

These issues collectively represent a failure to properly prepare a scientific paper. The missing appendices, inconsistent notation, and unverified claims about the extended work undermine the paper's credibility. While the theoretical approach is promising, the paper as submitted lacks the completeness and precision expected at NIPS. The authors have not demonstrated sufficient care in preparing their manuscript, which is particularly concerning for a theoretical paper where precision is paramount.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**
- Pro: Promising theoretical framework connecting kernel methods to CNN stability
- Pro: Establishes meaningful connections between stability and generalization
- Con: Critical missing content (appendices, extended work reference)
- Con: Multiple mathematical inconsistencies in equations and notation
- Con: Unverified claims about activation function compatibility
- Con: Formatting errors that undermine professionalism

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper presents interesting ideas but fails to deliver a complete, precise scientific contribution due to multiple technical inconsistencies and missing content. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmission. While the core idea has merit, the current submission does not meet NIPS's standards for technical rigor and completeness.
