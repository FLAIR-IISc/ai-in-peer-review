Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for multidimensional signals that mirrors the structure of CNNs while living in RKHSs. Each layer applies patch extraction, a pointwise kernel map with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling, yielding feature maps on a continuous domain. The authors establish: (i) signal preservation properties and a principled discretization scheme; (ii) Lipschitz stability of the representation to C1-diffeomorphisms with a bound that depends linearly on the deformation magnitude and inversely on the final pooling scale, paralleling the scattering transform (Mallat, Bruna & Mallat), but for more general architectures; (iii) compatibility of these stability guarantees with finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs) due to non-expansiveness of projections; (iv) a group-theoretic construction for equivariance and global invariance to general transformation groups via group pooling; (v) an inclusion result showing that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and Gaussian pooling belongs to the RKHS induced by the multilayer convolutional kernel, with explicit norm bounds depending on network weights. They connect the RKHS norm to prediction smoothness and generalization through standard RKHS margin arguments.

This work builds on and generalizes the scattering framework [Bruna & Mallat; Mallat] by moving from fixed wavelet filters to learned-feature-friendly RKHS constructions (CKNs), and complements group-equivariant CNNs [Cohen & Welling] and invariant kernel methods [Haasdonk & Burkhardt] by providing stability bounds to diffeomorphisms. It relates to analyses of deep nets via kernels [Daniely et al., Montavon et al.] and to convexified/dual views of CNNs [Zhang et al.], but focuses on convolutional, pooled, multilayer kernel maps and their geometry. The link to adversarial robustness is noted through Lipschitz stability.

Strengths
- Clear, modular operator-based construction (patch extraction, kernel mapping, pooling) that aligns with CNN intuitions while enabling RKHS analysis.
- Rigorous stability bound (Theorem 6) to diffeomorphisms: dependence C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn mirrors scattering-style guarantees while extending beyond wavelets; proof tools (commutator bounds via Schur’s test) are appropriate and well-situated in prior harmonic analysis.
- Discretization and signal recovery lemmas articulate when information is preserved under subsampling and pooling; practical relevance to standard subsampling choices (ek ≥ sk) is discussed.
- Compatibility with finite-dimensional CKN approximations: non-expansive projections preserve stability inequalities.
- Group-equivariant/invariant extension on general locally compact groups via Haar integration provides a unified lens on pooling for invariances beyond translation.
- Inclusion of CNNs into the induced RKHS with smooth homogeneous activations and explicit norm bounds (Proposition 7) offers a concrete bridge between CNN parameters and stability/generalization in RKHS terms.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Assumptions restrict generality: smooth homogeneous activations are required; ReLU is handled only via smoothed variants; max-pooling is not covered (Gaussian averaging only); standard discrete practices (strides, padding) are not fully analyzed.
- Stability bound grows linearly with depth (1+n); no discussion of tightness or whether normalization can reduce this dependence as in scattering’s path-length analysis.
- The framework relies on an initial low-pass A0 and Gaussian poolings; the trade-off between invariance (large σn) and information loss/norm shrinkage is acknowledged but not quantified.
- Signal recovery via deconvolution assumes invertibility and small pooling scales; stability to noise and discretization errors is not analyzed; border effects are deferred.
- Group invariance section is largely conceptual; no stability bounds on groups beyond translation; practical implementation details for non-commutative groups are minimal.
- The CNN-to-RKHS mapping uses normalization nk(u)=‖Pkzk−1(u)‖, which is nonstandard; implications for typical architectures (batch norm, unnormalized ReLU) are not detailed. Constants Cσ are left abstract; practical magnitude of the resulting norm bounds is unclear.
- No empirical validation: no experiments demonstrating measured stability to deformations, comparative invariance, or the utility of the RKHS norm as a regularizer.

Quality
The technical development is sound and appropriately cites and extends established results. Assumptions are stated, and the use of operator norms and Schur’s test is standard. Proofs are in the appendix. The absence of experiments limits validation but does not detract from the theoretical contribution.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized. Definitions and assumptions (A1–A3) are explicit, and the operator composition is clear. Some parts rely on appendices (e.g., kernel examples and constants), and additional intuition regarding constants and depth dependence would improve readability. The discretization section is careful for 1D; extending explicit statements to 2D would help.

Originality
The combination of a multilayer convolutional RKHS with stability to diffeomorphisms, compatibility with learned approximations (CKNs), and inclusion of CNNs via RKHS norms is novel relative to scattering and prior kernel analyses. The commutator analysis for shifted integral operators appears to extend known lemmas.

Significance
The work provides a principled theoretical framework to reason about invariance/stability in modern convolutional representations, potentially informing architecture and regularization design. Immediate empirical impact may be limited without experiments, but the conceptual advance is substantive.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide explicit examples of kernels satisfying (A1–A2) with closed-form Cσ (e.g., inverse polynomial) and plot their smoothed ReLU counterparts; quantify constants C1, C2 in 2D.
- Discuss how to adapt the analysis to max-pooling and strided convolutions; what breaks and what can be recovered?
- Clarify practicality of the nk(u) normalization: can standard ReLU networks without this factor be encompassed?
- Quantify the depth dependence: can normalization or architectural constraints avoid linear blow-up in n?
- Add empirical demonstrations of stability to controlled diffeomorphisms for CKNs and typical CNNs, and explore correlation between estimated RKHS norms and generalization.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, nontrivial theoretical contribution generalizing scattering-style stability to broader convolutional architectures.
- Clear bridge between CNN parameters and RKHS norms with implications for regularization and generalization.
- Group-invariance formulation within the same framework.

Arguments for rejection
- No empirical validation; practical impact and tightness of bounds not demonstrated.
- Assumptions exclude common components (ReLU without smoothing, max pooling).
- Depth dependence and constants left abstract; applicability to large-scale discrete CNNs remains somewhat idealized.

Overall recommendation
A technically sound and original theoretical paper that advances understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations within an RKHS framework. Acceptance is justified on theoretical grounds, but the paper would be strengthened by clarifying constants, broadening to common CNN components, and adding empirical validation.
