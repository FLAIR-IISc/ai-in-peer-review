This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors build upon the framework of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), extending it to signals defined on continuous domains. Drawing inspiration and analytical tools from Mallat's work on scattering transforms, the paper demonstrates that this CKN representation is stable to the action of small diffeomorphisms and nearly invariant to translations. The degree of translation invariance is shown to be controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer. The framework is also generalized to achieve invariance to the action of arbitrary locally compact groups. A key contribution is the connection of this kernel framework to a class of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) that use smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This link allows the stability guarantees derived for the kernel representation to be applied to these CNNs, connecting the model's RKHS norm to both its stability and its generalization properties.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper tackles a fundamental and highly relevant problem: developing a rigorous theoretical understanding of the stability and invariance properties that are crucial to the success of CNNs.
*   The primary theoretical contribution—extending the stability analysis of scattering networks to a more general CKN framework that incorporates learning—is novel and technically interesting. It provides a valuable bridge between the well-understood theory of scattering transforms and the less-understood, but more practical, domain of learned convolutional architectures.
*   The connection established in Section 5 between the RKHS norm of a CNN function, its stability to deformations, and its generalization capacity is insightful. It provides a principled perspective on why learning stable functions (i.e., with small RKHS norm) can lead to better sample complexity.
*   The generalization to global invariance for arbitrary group actions in Section 4 is elegant and well-formulated.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, despite its promising direction, is undermined by a significant number of minor flaws in its technical details, presentation, and scope, which collectively detract from its overall quality.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   **Notation:** The decision to "drop norm subscripts" and use `‖·‖` for Hilbert space norms, L2 norms, and L2→L2 operator norms is a significant impediment to clarity. This forces the reader to constantly disambiguate from context, which is particularly taxing in a dense, theory-heavy paper. This is not a standard or advisable notational practice.
    *   **Formatting:** The manuscript contains formatting artifacts, such as the author affiliation and conference header (`∗Univ. Grenoble Alpes...`, `st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...`) embedded in the main text. This suggests a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   **Self-Containment:** The paper relies on an external preprint [3] for certain details (e.g., the Gaussian kernel mapping for the prediction layer). For a conference submission, all essential components of the argument should be contained within the paper or its appendix. Similarly, relegating all proofs to the appendix makes it difficult to follow and verify the logical flow of the arguments in the main text.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Assumptions:**
    *   **Connection to CNNs:** The link to practical CNNs established in Section 5 is tenuous and potentially misleading. The analyzed CNNs require a non-standard activation function `z̃ik(u) = nk(u)σ ( 〈wik, Pkzk–1(u)〉/nk(u) )`, where `nk(u) = ‖Pkxk−1(u)‖`. This explicit normalization by the patch norm is absent from standard CNN architectures (e.g., VGG, ResNet). While ReLU is indeed homogeneous, its implementation does not involve this `nk(u)` factor. Therefore, the stability results apply to a specific, and arguably non-standard, class of networks, and the claim of providing results for "standard convolutional architectures" is overstated.
    *   **Signal Recovery:** The claims of signal preservation in Lemma 1 and Lemma 2 rely on recoverability from the pooled feature maps. As acknowledged briefly, this requires a deconvolution step, which is a notoriously ill-posed inverse problem, highly sensitive to noise and numerical precision. The paper does not sufficiently discuss the practical implications or instability of this recovery process, which weakens the strength of the "no loss of information" claim.
    *   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While theory papers are welcome at NIPS, the claims made here beg for some form of empirical validation. For instance, how tight are the derived stability bounds? Do the "RKHS-friendly" smoothed ReLUs proposed as an alternative to the non-smooth ReLU perform comparably in practice? Without any experiments, the practical significance of the theoretical results is difficult to assess.

**Summary of pros and cons:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper provides a novel and technically sophisticated framework for analyzing the stability of learned convolutional features.
*   It addresses a fundamental question in deep learning theory.
*   The theoretical connection between RKHS norm, stability, and generalization is an important insight.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The presentation suffers from unclear notation and formatting errors, indicating a lack of polish.
*   The crucial connection to practical CNNs is weak, as it relies on a non-standard architecture with explicit patch-norm normalization.
*   The paper completely lacks empirical validation, making it difficult to judge the real-world relevance or tightness of its theoretical findings.
*   Claims regarding signal preservation via deconvolution are presented without adequate discussion of the associated numerical stability issues.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the research direction is valuable and the theoretical ideas are interesting, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. The combination of unclear presentation, a tenuous link to the models practitioners actually use, and a complete absence of empirical grounding makes the contribution feel incomplete and preliminary. The authors should be encouraged to refine their presentation, strengthen the connection to standard CNNs (or be more precise about the limitations), and provide at least some empirical results to support their theoretical analysis before resubmission.
