Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework on continuous domains and studies its invariance and stability properties to translations and small diffeomorphisms. Each layer consists of patch extraction, a kernel mapping into an RKHS using homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. The authors prove that (i) the representation is stable to C1-diffeomorphisms with a Lipschitz-type bound proportional to the deformation Jacobian (for local stability) and inversely proportional to the last-layer pooling scale (for global translation invariance); (ii) intermediate layers are equivariant and the final pooling controls invariance; (iii) the discrete, subsampled construction can preserve the signal under mild conditions (patch size at least subsampling factor), and the kernel approximation used in convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) preserves stability due to non-expansive projections; (iv) the construction extends to general locally compact groups, yielding group equivariance/invariance via group pooling; and (v) the RKHS associated with the multilayer kernel contains a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, together with explicit RKHS norm bounds, thereby linking stability to generalization via the RKHS norm.

Relation to prior work: This builds on scattering transforms (Mallat, Bruna) by deriving analogous stability bounds but for a larger class of architectures (homogeneous dot-product kernels; CKNs; certain CNNs). It connects to CKNs (Mairal et al.) by showing their approximation steps are compatible with the stability analysis, and to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling) through a kernel/RKHS lens. It also relates to recent theory on deep networks viewed as kernels or convexified architectures (Anselmi et al., Daniely et al., Zhang et al.). The work distinguishes itself from scattering by allowing learned filters via kernel approximation and by embedding certain CNNs in the RKHS with norm control.

Strengths
- Clear, principled kernel-based construction that mirrors deep convolutional architectures on continuous domains and addresses discretization.
- Stability to diffeomorphisms with explicit bounds: linear in depth for local deformation (via commutator control) and translation invariance improving with final pooling scale; careful reuse and extension of harmonic analysis tools.
- Compatibility with practical approximations: the stability survives finite-dimensional projections used by CKNs due to non-expansiveness.
- Group-theoretic extension presented in a unified way, clarifying how to obtain equivariance/invariance to general groups via pooling on groups or subgroups.
- Inclusion of a class of CNNs in the RKHS with explicit norm bounds for smooth homogeneous activations, providing a concrete link between network norms, stability, and generalization.

Weaknesses
- Novelty vs. scattering: many proof techniques and stability statements closely follow Mallat (e.g., Lipschitz under diffeomorphisms, dependence on last pooling scale), with key differences being the kernelized view, an extended commutator bound with shifted kernels, and the CNN-in-RKHS inclusion. The incremental nature should be acknowledged more explicitly.
- Assumptions and practicality: stability requires norm-preserving, non-expansive homogeneous kernel maps and Gaussian pooling at prescribed scales, plus an initial pooling A0 that models acquisition. The CNN inclusion requires smooth homogeneous activations and per-patch normalization factors n_k(u)=||P_k x_{k-1}(u)||, which deviates from standard CNN practice (ReLU without explicit normalization). The extent to which commonly used CNNs are covered is limited (smoothed ReLU variants, not ReLU).
- Information preservation statements depend on deconvolution and conditions like e_k ≥ s_k; in practice, deconvolution with Gaussian filters can be ill-conditioned, border effects are ignored, and finite support is assumed; these caveats should be discussed more prominently.
- No empirical validation. Given the claims about robustness and sample complexity implications, simple experiments (e.g., measuring Lipschitz stability under synthetic diffeomorphisms for CKNs/CNNs) would strengthen the paper.

Quality
- The mathematics are careful and well grounded. The extension of commutator bounds to operators with shifted kernels is technically nontrivial and useful. The discretization and kernel approximation discussions are reasonable, though proofs are deferred to the appendix.
- Claims are appropriately scoped, but the practical impact (e.g., improved sample complexity) remains suggestive rather than demonstrated.

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable for an expert audience. The continuous-to-discrete mapping and link to CKNs are clearly explained. It would help to bring key constants (C1, C2) and their dependence (e.g., on κ and dimension) into the main text, and to add a small figure/table mapping discrete hyperparameters (e_k, s_k) to continuous ones (|S_k|, σ_k).
- The CNN-in-RKHS section would benefit from a concrete example of σ and kernel yielding finite C_σ, and a brief discussion of how the normalization factors n_k could be implemented or approximated in practice.

Originality
- Moderate. The conceptual unification of scattering-style stability, convolutional kernels, and a subclass of CNNs in an RKHS is valuable. The commutator extension and explicit norm bounds for CNNs in this RKHS are new (to my knowledge). However, several components (stability framework, group-equivariant pooling) are adaptations of known ideas.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is solid: provides a clean framework to study invariance/stability and generalization via norms for kernelized conv representations and some CNNs. This can inform the design of architectures and regularizers (e.g., norm control for stability).
- Practical significance is limited by assumptions and lack of experiments, but the results may spur follow-up work on stable CKNs and norm-based regularization.

Questions for authors
1) Provide explicit σ and kernel pairs with computed C_σ (e.g., inverse polynomial kernel + smoothed ReLU): what smooth approximation to ReLU do you use, and how tight are the induced norm bounds?
2) How sensitive are constants C1, C2 to dimension and κ; are the resulting bounds meaningful at realistic depths?
3) Can you quantify the effect of projection error in CKNs on stability (e.g., additive term in the bound)?
4) Do any common CNN practices (batch normalization, learned pooling, strided conv) violate key assumptions? How would this affect stability?
5) Could you include a small empirical study validating stability under synthetic deformations?

Pros
- Solid theoretical analysis with clear bounds; extends scattering theory to broader architectures.
- Unifies CKNs and a class of CNNs within an RKHS, enabling norm-based stability/generalization reasoning.
- Group-theoretic extension is clean and useful.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions not fully aligned with standard CNNs.
- Limited novelty compared to scattering literature; no experiments.
- Information preservation via deconvolution may be unstable in practice.

Recommendation: 7 (accept)
A well-executed theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of invariance and stability in convolutional representations and connects kernels and CNNs. Adding minimal empirical validation and clarifying practical relevance would further strengthen the paper. Confidence: high.
