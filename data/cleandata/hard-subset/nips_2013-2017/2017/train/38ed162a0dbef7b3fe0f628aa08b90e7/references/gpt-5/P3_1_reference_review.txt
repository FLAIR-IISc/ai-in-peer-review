Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework on continuous domains that mirrors CNN architectures (patch extraction, pointwise nonlinearity via a kernel map, and pooling), generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), and yields a representation Φn(x) with provable stability to diffeomorphisms and increasing translation invariance with depth. The authors prove a Lipschitz-type stability bound similar in spirit to the scattering transform, with dependence on the Jacobian of the deformation and on the last-layer pooling scale σn. They further show how to achieve equivariance/invariance to general locally compact groups by defining group-covariant patch extraction and pooling. Finally, they prove that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations is contained in the RKHS induced by their multilayer kernel, and bound the RKHS norm of such CNNs, thereby linking stability and generalization via RKHS norm control. Discretization and finite-dimensional approximations (CKN-style projections) are discussed, with arguments that stability is preserved by non-expansive projections.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on Mallat’s theory of scattering transforms (invariance and deformation stability) and on convolutional kernel networks. It positions itself relative to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling), hierarchical kernel machines (Anselmi et al.), and convexified/two-layer kernelized neural nets (Zhang et al., Daniely et al.). The main novelty is to (i) extend scattering-type stability proofs to a broader class of multilayer convolutional kernel representations with learned projections and (ii) connect this RKHS to a class of CNNs via smooth homogeneous activations, providing explicit norm bounds.

Quality and correctness
- Stability: Theorem 6 provides the central result: ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖, under assumptions (A1–A3) and initial low-pass A0. The decomposition via commutators and bounds via Schur’s test is standard and credible, and Lemma 5 aligns with Mallat’s results. The linear growth in n and 1/σn dependence are reasonable; constants depending on κ and dimension are acknowledged.
- Discretization/approximation: The discretized construction and Lemma 2 argue recoverability under subsampling when ek ≥ sk and deconvolution is possible. This is mathematically plausible but practically ill-conditioned; the paper notes stability requires small pooling scales. The claim of “signal preservation” (Lemma 1) in the continuous case hinges on invertibility of smoothing and the presence of linear functionals in Hk; while correct in an idealized sense, its stability to noise and discretization is not quantified.
- CNN–RKHS link: Proposition 7 is technically interesting. The containment requires smooth homogeneous activations and a normalization factor nk(u)=‖patch‖, which deviates from standard CNN practice; ReLU is not directly covered, though smoothed variants are discussed. The recursive norm bound via Cσ is clear, but constants are opaque; it is unclear how tight/practical these bounds are for realistic networks.

Overall, the theoretical development is careful, assumptions are spelled out, and the claims are consistent with prior harmonic analysis proofs. Some steps (signal recovery, approximation errors) would benefit from quantitative stability/error bounds.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The mapping between continuous and discrete settings is clear, and the role of each operator is explicit. Overloaded norm notation could be lightened, and a concise list of kernel choices satisfying (A1–A2), with explicit κk, Cσ, would help. More intuition about constants and their dependence on depth and patch/pooling scales would improve readability. Proofs are in the appendix; a brief high-level sketch in the main text for the main theorem would help non-experts.

Originality
- New: A general multilayer convolutional kernel on continuous domains with scattering-like stability guarantees; extension of commutator bounds to shifted integral operators [LcAσ, Lτ]; the RKHS containment and norm bounds for a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations.
- Less new: The group equivariance construction mirrors standard group-convolution arguments; the idea that last-layer pooling controls global invariance is well known.

Significance
This work provides a unifying theoretical lens that covers CKNs and a subset of CNNs, linking stability, invariance, and generalization via RKHS norms. It could influence principled regularization and architectural design, and it clarifies when CKN-style approximations preserve desirable properties. However, practical impact is tempered by: (i) dependence on normalized, smooth activations not commonly used; (ii) lack of quantitative, finite-sample/approximation error bounds; (iii) no empirical corroboration of improved sample complexity or robustness in practice.

Strengths
- Clear, modular operator framework tying together patching, kernel maps, and pooling.
- Stability bound to diffeomorphisms with explicit dependence on depth and pooling scale.
- Generalization to groups with clean equivariance argument.
- Conceptual bridge between CNNs and RKHSs, with norm-based control of stability.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Signal “recovery” after smoothing/pooling is ill-posed; stability/noise sensitivity not analyzed.
- Constants in stability bound may be loose (linear in depth; dependence on κ and dimension via κ^(d+1)); no discussion of optimal scaling of σk, Sk to mitigate this.
- CNN containment requires nonstandard normalization and smooth activations; standard ReLU CNNs are not directly covered.
- No explicit quantitative bounds for the effect of finite-dimensional projections (Nyström/subspace learning) on stability or expressivity.
- Group invariance section does not provide new stability/invariance theorems beyond construction.

Questions for authors
1) Can you quantify approximation error introduced by projections Πk in terms of kernel approximation quality and propagate it into the stability bound?
2) Is the linear-in-depth factor (1+n) tight? Could scale scheduling (σk, Sk) yield depth-independent constants?
3) How sensitive is Lemma 2’s recovery to noise? Can you provide a stability constant for the deconvolution step?
4) Can you extend the RKHS-containment to (smoothed) leaky-ReLU without the explicit nk normalization, or argue that standard batchnorm approximates this?
5) For specific kernels (e.g., inverse polynomial, arc-cosine), can you provide concrete values/estimates of Cσ and the resulting norm bounds?

Minor comments
- Clarify the role of σ0 on the final constants; making A0 ≈ I seems to worsen high-frequency control.
- Provide a table of example κk and whether (A1–A2) hold.
- Some references lack years/page ranges.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a solid and timely theoretical contribution that generalizes scattering-style stability to a broader, CNN-like kernel setting and provides a useful RKHS perspective on CNNs. While practical implications and constants could be sharpened and the connection to standard ReLU CNNs is indirect, the conceptual advances and careful analysis merit publication. My confidence is high.
