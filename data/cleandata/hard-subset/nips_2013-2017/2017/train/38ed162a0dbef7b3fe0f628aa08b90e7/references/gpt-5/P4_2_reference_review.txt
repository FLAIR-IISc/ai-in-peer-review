Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that models convolutional architectures on continuous domains and yields feature maps analogous to CNN layers: patch extraction, kernel mapping into an RKHS via homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. It proves (i) signal preservation/recoverability across layers under suitable sampling, (ii) Lipschitz stability to C1-diffeomorphisms with constants that separate local deformation and global translation terms, where translation invariance increases with the last pooling scale, (iii) compatibility of these guarantees with practical discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations (as in Convolutional Kernel Networks, CKNs), (iv) group-equivariant constructions and global invariance via final pooling over a group, and (v) that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and a specific normalization lies in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, with an explicit recursive norm bound; hence, stability and generalization links can be controlled via the RKHS norm.

This work generalizes Mallat’s scattering transform theory [Bruna & Mallat 2013; Mallat 2012] from predefined wavelet filters to broader convolutional architectures encoded by positive definite kernels, including CKNs [Mairal et al. 2014, 2016], and connects to analyses of invariance in kernel methods [Haasdonk & Burkhardt 2007; Mroueh et al. 2012] and hierarchical/kernel views of CNNs [Anselmi et al. 2015; Montavon et al. 2011; Daniely et al. 2016]. The group-equivariant layer design resonates with group-equivariant CNNs [Cohen & Welling 2016] and group-invariant scattering [Sifre & Mallat 2013].

Quality (technical soundness)
- The stability bound (Theorem 6) follows from a commutator decomposition (Proposition 3), bounding inner-layer commutators via Lemma 4 (an extension of Mallat’s commutator control to shifted kernels) and the last-layer translation term via Lemma 5 (from Mallat). Assumptions (A1–A3) ensure non-expansiveness and scale growth aligned with pooling. The resulting bound is of the desired form: O((1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + ‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖, matching the scattering paradigm while allowing general kernels.
- The discretization scheme and Lemma 2 establish recoverability with subsampling (ek ≥ sk) using deconvolution with a Gaussian, which is invertible but may be ill-conditioned at large scales; the text acknowledges stability considerations.
- The kernel approximation step (finite-dimensional projections) preserves non-expansiveness, so stability carries over to CKN implementations, at the cost of approximation error.
- The CNN-in-RKHS result (Proposition 7) constructs CNNs using smooth homogeneous activations and an explicit normalization nk(u)=‖Pkzk−1(u)‖, yielding a recursive RKHS norm bound. This provides a principled route to extend stability/generalization arguments to a nontrivial class of CNNs; the reliance on smooth activations and homogenization is carefully discussed, with relu-like smooth surrogates covered by the referenced kernel class.

Overall, the analysis is careful, with proofs deferred to the appendix. Some steps (e.g., Lemma 1 signal recovery through sampling after a nonlinear kernel map and pooling) hinge on the RKHS containing linear functionals and the invertibility of pooling; additional explicit proof sketches in the main text would aid verification.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear and well-organized, with a clean separation between the continuous theory, discretization, kernel approximation, group extensions, and CNN connections. Notation is heavy but consistent. Recommendations:
- Move a concise catalog of admissible kernels (with their κk, Cσ) from Appendix B to the main text.
- Provide a short proof sketch of Lemma 1 to clarify how linear functionals in the RKHS enable patch recovery post-Mk.
- Emphasize early that an initial anti-aliasing/pooling A0 is assumed (and why this does not limit generality).

Originality
The core novelty lies in:
- Extending diffeomorphism stability and translation-invariance guarantees from scattering to a broad multilayer kernel class and to CKNs.
- Establishing that certain CNNs fall within the multilayer kernel RKHS with explicit norm bounds, thereby transferring stability and connecting to generalization control.
- A group-equivariant construction integrated into this kernel framework.

While elements draw on known tools (commutators, group convolution/pooling), the integration and extension to general kernels and CNNs provide a substantive original contribution.

Significance
Theoretical significance is solid: the work provides a unifying lens on invariance/stability for deep convolutional representations beyond scattering, with implications for regularization via RKHS norms and for designing architectures with controllable invariance. Practical impact is indirect; empirical validation is minimal, and some modeling choices (smooth homogeneous activations with normalization) diverge from standard CNN practice. Nonetheless, the results are likely to be used as a foundation for further theoretical and methodological developments.

Pros
- Clear, principled extension of scattering stability to general convolutional kernel representations.
- Compatibility with discretization and kernel approximation (CKNs), preserving non-expansiveness.
- Group-equivariant layer design enabling targeted stability and global invariance.
- Inclusion of a class of CNNs in the RKHS with explicit norm bounds, linking stability and generalization.

Cons
- Lack of empirical validation demonstrating the predicted stability/invariance in practical CKNs/CNNs.
- Assumptions may limit direct applicability: smooth homogeneous activations with normalization; stability of deconvolution in Lemma 2; linear growth (1+n) in the deformation term may be loose.
- Norm non-preservation relative to scattering; the proposed fix via an extra RKHS layer is discussed but not demonstrated empirically.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide concrete kernel choices and their constants (κ, C1, C2, Cσ) with typical parameter values, and discuss the trade-off between pooling scale, patch size, and stability constants.
- Clarify the stability of the deconvolution step in Lemma 2 (conditioning of Gaussian deconvolution) and how it affects robustness under noise.
- Discuss the gap between the homogenized smooth activations and standard ReLU networks; quantify approximation error and whether Proposition 7 can tolerate nonsmooth activations via smoothing/regularization.
- Comment on whether the (1+n) dependence in Theorem 6 is tight; can chaining arguments or contractive layers improve it?
- Include small experiments (e.g., on images with synthetic deformations) comparing CKNs/CNNs trained with the proposed principles versus baselines, to corroborate theoretical stability and invariance.

Overall assessment
The paper is a technically sound and original theoretical contribution that extends invariance and stability analysis to a broader class of deep convolutional representations and connects to CNNs via RKHS embeddings. Its significance is primarily theoretical; empirical support would strengthen the work but is not strictly necessary for acceptance.
