- Develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework yielding feature maps like CNNs/CKNs and analyzes invariance/stability to translations and small diffeomorphisms.  
- Iterates patch extraction, norm-preserving/non-expansive kernel maps with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling; derives Lipschitz stability bounds of form C1(1+n)∥∇τ∥∞ + C2∥τ∥∞/σn.  
- Unifies scattering-style stability analysis with a kernel construction that encompasses CKNs and contains a class of CNNs, while addressing discretization and group-equivariance.  
- Technical analysis is careful, leveraging commutator bounds, Schur’s test, and Mallat’s lemmas; Theorem 6 follows under stated assumptions.  
- Discretization section gives reasonable conditions (ek ≥ sk, Gaussian bandwidth) for deconvolution/recovery and honestly notes conditioning depends on scale.  
- Proposition 7 showing a class of CNNs lies in the RKHS is nontrivial, requires smooth homogeneous activations and normalization, and gives explicit recursive norm bounds.  
- Caveat: pooling breaks norm preservation; authors propose remedies but do not provide quantitative constants for energy loss.  
- Caveat: deconvolution-based signal preservation in discrete settings is exact only in noiseless case; practical conditioning with noise is not analyzed.  
- Caveat: stability bound scales linearly with depth n and tightness or lower bounds are not provided.  
- Paper is clearly written and well organized; assumptions are explicit and proofs sketched with detailed appendices.  
- Clarity could improve by giving concrete kernel choices/Cσ, quantifying dependence of constants on κ/dimension, and discussing deconvolution conditioning with noise.  
- The unification and commutator analysis for shifted integral operators are novel and nontrivial.  
- The work meaningfully extends scattering, kernel views of deep nets, and group-equivariant CNN analyses.  
- Significance: provides theoretical grounding for deformation stability and links RKHS norm control to generalization, informing architectural choices (pooling scales, patch sizes, activation smoothness).  
- Practical impact is indirect—no empirical validation or new algorithms—but strengthens theoretical foundations of CKNs and CNN robustness.  
- Pros: rigorous theoretical contribution bridging scattering, kernels, and CNNs; explicit deformation stability bounds with architectural dependence.  
- Pros: inclusion of certain CNNs in the RKHS with norm bounds and principled group-equivariant pooling; careful discretization/CKN compatibility discussion.  
- Cons: no empirical validation to illustrate constants, tightness, or practical benefits.  
- Cons: restrictive assumptions on CNN class (smooth homogeneous activations, normalization, initial low-pass A0).  
- Cons: several constants and conditioning issues (deconvolution) are qualitative rather than quantified; depth dependence might be loose.  
- Suggestions: add experiments quantifying deformation stability vs depth/σn and comparisons to standard CNNs/CKNs.  
- Suggestions: provide concrete kernel/activation examples with computed Cσ and resulting norm bounds.  
- Suggestions: analyze noise/conditioning in Lemma 2 and robustness of recovery.  
- Suggestions: clarify practical guidance for choosing σk, Sk and trade-offs between invariance and information loss.  
- Overall recommendation: technically strong, clearly written, and significant theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance despite limited empirical validation and some restrictive assumptions.
