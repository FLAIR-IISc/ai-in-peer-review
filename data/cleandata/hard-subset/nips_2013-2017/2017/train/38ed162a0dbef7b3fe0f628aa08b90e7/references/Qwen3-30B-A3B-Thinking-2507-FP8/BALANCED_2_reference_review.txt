

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through a kernel-based framework, extending the scattering transform approach to more general CNN architectures. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and demonstrates how this framework provides theoretical guarantees for translation invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms. They establish connections between this kernel framework and standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, showing that their stability results apply to these networks as well.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it extends Mallat's scattering transform theory to more general CNN architectures through a kernel framework, providing rigorous stability bounds for deformations. Second, it establishes a theoretical connection between kernel-based representations and standard CNNs with smooth activations, showing that CNNs with homogeneous activation functions (like smoothed ReLU) can be embedded in the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces. Third, it provides a theoretical foundation for understanding why CNNs generalize well, linking stability to generalization through the RKHS norm.

The paper is technically sound with well-constructed proofs (referenced to the appendix) and carefully distinguishes its contributions from related work on scattering transforms [17] and convolutional kernel networks [15]. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their framework, particularly regarding non-smooth activations like ReLU, and suggest smoothed variants as a potential solution. The mathematical framework is clear and well-motivated, with good notation and organization.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical contribution is substantial, extending the scattering transform framework to more general CNN architectures
- The connection between kernel representations and actual CNNs with smooth activations is novel and well-executed
- The stability analysis provides a theoretical basis for understanding why CNNs generalize well
- The paper carefully addresses limitations and relates its work to previous NIPS contributions
- The mathematical framework is rigorous and well-constructed

**Weaknesses:**
- The connection to practical CNNs is limited by the focus on smooth activations, as ReLU (the most common activation) is non-smooth. While the authors suggest smoothed variants, they don't provide empirical evidence that these variants work well in practice.
- The paper lacks experimental validation of the theoretical claims, making it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the stability bounds.
- The discussion of kernel approximation in CKNs could be strengthened by addressing how information loss affects performance in practice.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently explore how their framework could guide the design of better CNN architectures with improved stability properties.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**
1. Include experiments demonstrating the stability properties on real datasets, comparing CNNs with different activation functions.
2. Provide more concrete guidance on how the theoretical insights could be applied to improve CNN design.
3. Expand the discussion on the practical implications of the RKHS norm control for generalization, possibly with examples of how this could inform regularization strategies.
4. Address the ReLU limitation more thoroughly, either by including smoothed ReLU experiments or by more clearly delineating the gap between their framework and standard CNN practice.
5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how the framework might apply to other types of neural networks beyond CNNs.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of CNNs
- The framework provides a unifying perspective on invariance and stability properties
- The connection to practical CNNs with smooth activations is novel and valuable
- The paper is well-written and technically sound

*Con:*
- The limited connection to standard CNN practice (ReLU) reduces practical impact
- Lack of experimental validation makes it difficult to assess real-world relevance
- The practical implications for network design are not sufficiently explored

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to understanding the invariance and stability properties of CNNs. The kernel framework provides a valuable unifying perspective that extends previous work on scattering transforms and connects to practical CNN architectures. While the theoretical results are significant, the paper would be considerably strengthened by experimental validation of the theoretical claims and more concrete guidance on how these insights could be applied to improve CNN design.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important theoretical question. The main limitation is the gap between the theoretical framework and practical CNN implementations (due to the smoothness requirement). However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest potential pathways forward.

Given the high quality of the theoretical contribution and the clear significance to understanding why CNNs work well, I recommend acceptance. The paper represents a meaningful advance in the theoretical understanding of CNNs that would be valuable to the NeurIPS community. The minor weaknesses are readily addressable in a revised version, and the paper's theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance even without experimental validation.

The paper is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on similar topics (e.g., scattering transforms [17], CKNs [15]) and makes a clear contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in understanding the theoretical properties of CNNs, which is a fundamental question in deep learning research.
