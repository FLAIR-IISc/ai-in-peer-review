This paper investigates the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel, generalizing convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), and demonstrate theoretical guarantees for translation invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms, mirroring results for scattering transforms but extending them to more standard CNN architectures. The central idea is to provide a theoretical framework for understanding the geometry of the functional spaces induced by these networks, potentially aiding regularization and improving sample complexity.

While the paper presents a compelling theoretical framework and establishes connections between kernel methods and CNNs, several aspects require significant refinement. My overall assessment is leaning towards rejection due to a combination of technical limitations, lack of concrete empirical validation, and a concerning level of detail-oriented issues, despite the potentially valuable core ideas.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The attempt to formalize the study of CNN representations using kernel methods is valuable. The connections drawn to scattering transforms and the extension to broader CNN architectures are insightful.
*   **Mathematical Rigor:** The paper demonstrates a strong command of mathematical tools, including reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces, harmonic analysis, and functional analysis. The proofs, while relegated to the appendix, appear generally sound (though detailed scrutiny is needed – see weaknesses).
*   **Potential Impact:** A deeper understanding of the geometry of CNN representations could indeed inform regularization techniques and improve generalization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** This is a major flaw. The paper is entirely theoretical. There is *no* empirical validation of the derived stability guarantees, nor any comparison of the theoretical predictions with the observed behavior of actual CNNs. The authors state they provide a theoretical framework *for understanding* CNNs, but without demonstrating a connection to real-world performance, the work remains largely abstract. Demonstrating the tightness of the bounds or showing how they relate to generalization performance would be crucial.
*   **Discretization Details & Assumptions:** The treatment of discretization in Section 2.1 feels rushed and introduces potentially problematic assumptions. The requirement of *ek ≥ sk* seems arbitrary and unnecessarily restrictive. The discussion about recovering xk–1 from xk relies heavily on the assumption of sufficient sampling, without providing clear guidance on how to determine appropriate sampling rates in practice. Lemma 2's conditions require an additional constraint that the sampling be able to cover the entire domain, and is not explicitly stated in the original definition of the operators.
*   **Homogeneity Assumption & Activation Functions:** The reliance on “homogeneous” activation functions and the need for smoothness (Cσ(a) < ∞) in Proposition 7 significantly limits the applicability of the results to standard CNNs. While the authors acknowledge ReLU's non-smoothness, the discussion of smoothed ReLU variants feels inadequate. This is a critical limitation, as ReLU is a foundational component of modern CNN architectures. The discussion on which kernels satisfy assumptions in Appendix B is sparse, with no justification.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Clarity Issues:** There are several instances of imprecise notation and unclear explanations. For instance, the notation for Hilbert space norms is inconsistent (using “‖ · ‖” for L2, Hilbert, and operator norms without clear distinction). The introduction of notation in Section 2 (e.g., *νk*) is not always consistent with its subsequent use. The use of the term "stability" is overloaded: stability to diffeomorphisms vs. stability during learning.
*   **Missing Connections to Existing Literature:** While related work is mentioned, the connections are often superficial. The authors do not adequately address how their theoretical results relate to empirical observations about CNN robustness and generalization in the deep learning literature (e.g., adversarial examples, double descent). They don't discuss how their results compare to other recent attempts to theoretically understand CNNs.
*   **Lemma 1 & Signal Preservation:** Lemma 1 asserts recovery from sampling, but the proof isn't sufficiently detailed to convince. The condition about containing linear functions seems very strong and not generally true for all kernels.
*   **Appendix Organization:** The appendix feels disorganized. Mathematical details are crucial, but their presentation needs to be more structured and self-contained.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a novel theoretical framework for analyzing CNN representations.
*   The mathematical treatment is rigorous.
*   The work could potentially lead to a better understanding of CNN generalization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation renders the theoretical results largely abstract.
*   The restrictive assumptions on activation functions and discretization limit the applicability of the framework.
*   Notational inconsistencies and clarity issues hinder understanding.
*   The paper lacks a thorough engagement with existing empirical and theoretical literature.
*   Several technical points require further clarification and justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially valuable core ideas, the significant limitations outlined above lead me to recommend **rejection**. The lack of empirical validation, combined with the restrictive assumptions and clarity issues, undermines the practical relevance of the work. While the paper demonstrates mathematical sophistication, it fails to convincingly bridge the gap between theory and practice. Addressing the empirical validation, clarifying the assumptions, and improving the clarity of the presentation are essential for future work in this direction. A stronger focus on connecting the theoretical results to observed phenomena in CNNs would also significantly enhance the impact of this research.
