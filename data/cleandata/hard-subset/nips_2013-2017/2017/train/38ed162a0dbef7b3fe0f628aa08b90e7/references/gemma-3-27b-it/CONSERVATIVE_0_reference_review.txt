This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks through the lens of positive definite kernels, aiming to provide a more rigorous understanding of invariance and stability. The authors extend prior work on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and scattering transforms to a broader class of CNNs, demonstrating theoretical guarantees for stability to diffeomorphisms and translation invariance. The core idea is appealing – leveraging kernel methods to analyze and potentially improve the understanding of deep learning models.

However, despite a solid mathematical foundation and clear presentation, I find the paper lacking in substantial novelty and practical impact. The theoretical results, while correct, largely *reaffirm* properties already known from the scattering transform literature, extending them to a slightly more general setting. The contribution feels incremental rather than transformative. The connection to practical CNN architectures is asserted, but not convincingly demonstrated through compelling empirical evaluation. The authors state the results "apply to this class of CNNs," but the specific characteristics of this class, and how common it is in practice, are not sufficiently clarified.  Furthermore, the extension to CKNs feels like retracing steps, as CKNs themselves are not a dominant paradigm in the field.

**Strengths:**

*   **Mathematical Rigor:** The paper is mathematically sound, employing well-established tools from kernel methods and harmonic analysis. The proofs appear correct and are relegated to a well-structured appendix.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The writing is clear and organized, making the technical details accessible. The motivation is well-explained, and the paper builds logically from related work.
*   **Connection to Existing Theory:** The paper successfully connects the analysis of CNNs to existing theoretical frameworks, particularly the scattering transform and kernel methods.
*   **Potential for Regularization Insights:** The observation that stability (controlled by the RKHS norm) may relate to generalization is a potentially valuable direction for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core results—stability to diffeomorphisms and translation invariance—are already established in the scattering transform literature. The extension to a broader class of CNNs, while technically correct, does not represent a major breakthrough.
*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:**  The paper is entirely theoretical. There is *no* empirical evaluation to support the claims or demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical findings. It’s unclear whether the derived properties hold in practice, or if they translate to improved performance on real-world tasks.
*   **Limited Scope of Applicable CNNs:** The requirement of "smooth homogeneous activation functions" significantly limits the applicability of the results to standard CNNs which often use ReLU or other non-smooth activations. While they attempt to bridge this gap, the discussion feels insufficient.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** The paper feels more like a formalization and extension of existing ideas rather than a truly novel contribution. The connection to CKNs, while interesting, does not address a major open problem in the field.
*   **RKHS Norm as Practical Regularizer:** The paper suggests that controlling the RKHS norm could be a good regularizer, but it doesn’t provide any guidance on *how* to do so effectively in practice.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper is theoretically sound and provides a rigorous analysis of CNNs using kernel methods.
*   It contributes to a better understanding of the theoretical foundations of deep learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper lacks significant novelty and practical impact.
*   The absence of empirical validation is a major weakness.
*   The results are limited by the restrictive assumptions on activation functions.
*   The connection to CKNs does not justify the added complexity.


**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While technically sound and clearly written, its lack of novelty, empirical validation, and limited applicability to practical CNN architectures outweigh its strengths. The paper feels like a theoretical exercise that does not significantly advance the field.  I would encourage the authors to consider including extensive empirical evaluation, relaxing the assumptions on activation functions, or focusing on a more significant open problem. A stronger connection to real-world applications and a demonstration of practical benefits are essential for acceptance at NIPS. The paper currently reads as an interesting mathematical exploration, but not a compelling scientific contribution.
