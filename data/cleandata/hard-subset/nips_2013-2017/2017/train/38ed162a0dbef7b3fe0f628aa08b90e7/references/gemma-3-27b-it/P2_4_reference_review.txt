## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate that it contains a large class of CNNs, allowing them to study the invariance and stability of these models. They show that this kernel framework provides guarantees similar to the scattering transform regarding stability to diffeomorphisms, while offering a more general setting applicable to standard CNN architectures.  A key finding is that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization, suggesting a link between these properties and improved sample complexity.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds on Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) and the theory of scattering transforms. It distinguishes itself by extending the analysis to a broader class of CNNs beyond those constrained by the CKN framework. The authors also connect their work to the growing body of research combining deep learning with kernel methods, referencing relevant papers in both areas. They appropriately position their contribution as an attempt to move beyond the “black box” view of CNNs by providing theoretical insights into their properties.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a mathematically sound analysis of CNNs, leveraging tools from kernel theory and harmonic analysis.  The proofs (relegated to the appendix) appear thorough and well-structured.
* **Bridging Theory and Practice:**  The authors successfully connect abstract theoretical concepts (RKHS, kernels) to practical CNN architectures, outlining how to apply their framework to existing models. The discussion of discretization and kernel approximation is particularly valuable.
* **Stability and Generalization Link:** The established connection between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization is a significant result. This provides a theoretical justification for the regularization effects observed in deep learning.
* **Generalization of Scattering Transform Results:**  Extending the stability guarantees of the scattering transform to more general CNN architectures is a notable contribution.
* **Clear Presentation (Generally):** While dense, the paper is well-organized and logically progresses from kernel construction to stability analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Accessibility:** The heavy mathematical notation and abstract concepts (RKHS, kernel theory) make the paper challenging to follow for readers not already familiar with these areas.  The introductory sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:**  The paper is primarily theoretical. While the authors suggest implications for practical CNNs, there is no empirical validation to support their claims. Demonstrating the stability properties or improved sample complexity on real datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Homogeneous Activations Constraint:** The restriction to CNNs with homogeneous activations is a limitation. While the authors acknowledge that ReLU is not directly covered, the necessity of this constraint for their theoretical results should be more thoroughly discussed, along with potential avenues for relaxing it.
* **Norm Preservation Concern:** While acknowledging the lack of norm preservation in their framework, the paper doesn’t fully address the potential consequences. The claim that this is less relevant for signals with a low-frequency bias needs further justification.
* **Potential Overlap with Existing Kernel Methods:** The connection to existing kernel-based learning methods, while acknowledged, could be explored more deeply. It's not entirely clear what novel advantages their approach offers compared to established kernel techniques.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous analysis.
* Important connection between stability and generalization.
* Extension of scattering transform results to a wider class of CNNs.
* Potential to provide new insights into the behavior of deep learning models.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* High level of mathematical complexity may limit accessibility.
* Lack of empirical validation to support theoretical claims.
* Restriction to homogeneous activations.
* Limited discussion of the practical implications and potential drawbacks of their framework.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of deep convolutional networks. The use of kernel theory provides a powerful framework for analyzing invariance and stability, and the link between these properties and generalization is particularly interesting. However, the paper’s reliance on theoretical results without empirical validation and the high level of mathematical complexity are significant weaknesses.  

**Rating:**  **Weak Accept**. 

I believe the paper is of sufficient quality to warrant acceptance, but with the caveat that future work should focus on empirical validation and addressing the limitations mentioned above.  Addressing the accessibility issue with more intuitive explanations would also broaden the impact of this research. I recommend the authors be encouraged to explore empirical studies and potentially relax the homogeneous activation constraint in future iterations.
