

# Peer Review: "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

## Summary and Context

This paper presents a theoretical framework for understanding invariance and stability properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) using positive definite kernels. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and demonstrates how this framework can characterize the stability of CNN representations to diffeomorphisms (smooth deformations). The work builds upon Mallat's scattering transform [17] and CKNs [15], extending their theoretical guarantees to a broader class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions.

The paper's key contribution is showing that standard CNNs can be embedded within the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) defined by their proposed kernel, allowing the authors to translate stability results for the kernel representation to CNNs. The main theoretical result (Theorem 6) establishes that the representation is stable to deformations with a bound proportional to the Jacobian norm of the deformation, with translation-invariance improving as the last pooling layer scale increases.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor and Novelty**: The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing a theoretical connection between kernel methods and CNNs. The authors successfully extend Mallat's scattering framework [17] to a broader class of networks, demonstrating that stability properties apply to standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations. This is a substantial theoretical advance over previous work.

2. **Clear Mathematical Framework**: The construction of the multilayer convolutional kernel is well-defined, with careful attention to the mathematical details of the operators (patch extraction, kernel mapping, pooling). The paper's notation is consistent, and the mathematical arguments are sound.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The connection to CNNs (Proposition 7) is particularly valuable. The authors show how the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization, providing a principled theoretical basis for regularization in CNNs. This bridges an important gap between theoretical analysis and practical model design.

4. **Comprehensive Related Work**: The paper situates itself well within the literature, discussing relevant connections to scattering transforms [17], CKNs [15], group-invariance [9, 22], and other kernel-based approaches [2, 10, 18].

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical without any experimental results to validate the proposed framework. While theoretical work is valuable, a brief empirical demonstration showing how the stability properties manifest in practice would have strengthened the paper significantly. For instance, showing that CNNs with activations satisfying the paper's conditions exhibit better stability to deformations would have been compelling.

2. **Limited Scope of CNNs Covered**: The connection to standard CNNs (Proposition 7) is limited to networks with smooth homogeneous activation functions. While the authors acknowledge that ReLU is homogeneous but not smooth, they don't provide a clear path to extend their framework to handle non-smooth activations that dominate modern CNNs. This is a significant limitation, as most practical CNNs use ReLU or variants.

3. **Discretization Considerations**: While the paper discusses discretization (Section 2.1), it doesn't adequately address how the theoretical guarantees translate to real-world discrete images. The assumption of continuous signals is common in theoretical work, but the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the results apply to typical discrete image data.

4. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the proposed kernel framework compared to standard CNNs. For the framework to be practically useful, it would need to be computationally feasible, which isn't discussed.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to our understanding of CNNs, successfully bridging kernel methods and deep learning theory. The stability results (Theorem 6) are rigorous and extend Mallat's work to a broader class of networks. The connection between RKHS norms and generalization is particularly insightful, providing a theoretical foundation for why certain regularization strategies work.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its lack of empirical validation. While theoretical work is important, the field of deep learning particularly values results that can be connected to practical applications. The absence of any experiments makes it difficult to assess how the theoretical guarantees translate to real-world performance.

The limited scope regarding activation functions is also problematic. ReLU, the most common activation in CNNs, is homogeneous but not smooth. While the authors mention a smoothed variant, they don't demonstrate how their framework would apply to standard ReLU networks, which are the backbone of most modern computer vision systems.

The paper would have been significantly strengthened by:
1. Including empirical results showing stability to deformations in practice
2. Extending the connection to standard CNNs with ReLU activations
3. Discussing computational efficiency compared to standard CNNs
4. Providing more intuition about the practical implications of the RKHS norm control

## Recommendation

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution to our understanding of CNNs. The mathematical framework is sound, and the connection between kernel methods and CNNs is novel and valuable. However, the lack of empirical validation and the limited scope regarding practical CNN architectures are significant drawbacks.

The paper is of high quality and originality within the theoretical deep learning space. While it's purely theoretical, this is acceptable for NIPS as long as the theoretical contribution is substantial. The significance is moderate - the work provides valuable theoretical insights but doesn't directly lead to new algorithms or significant performance improvements.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should consider adding a brief empirical section demonstrating how their stability results manifest in practice, and more thoroughly address the connection to standard CNNs with ReLU activations. Given the strength of the theoretical work, these additions would significantly enhance the paper's impact.

## Final Thoughts

This paper represents a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of CNNs, extending Mallat's work on scattering transforms to a broader class of networks. The connection between RKHS norms and stability/generalization provides a principled theoretical foundation for regularization in CNNs. While the lack of empirical validation is a drawback, the theoretical rigor and novelty make this a strong candidate for NIPS. With minor revisions addressing the limitations noted above, this paper would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
