

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper introduces a novel kernel framework to study the invariance and stability properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs), bridging the gap between the scattering transform [17] and standard CNN architectures. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16] and demonstrates that this framework contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The key contribution is showing that stability results for the kernel framework can be translated to predictive models, with the RKHS norm controlling both stability and generalization.

The paper makes several important theoretical contributions. First, it provides a rigorous analysis of translation invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms (small deformations) for the kernel representation, establishing a bound similar to the scattering transform but applicable to standard CNNs. Second, it connects group invariance to the kernel framework, showing how different transformations can be encoded through adapted patch extraction and pooling operators. Third, and most significantly, it establishes that CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations can be embedded within the RKHS of the proposed kernel framework, allowing the stability results to apply to standard CNNs.

The paper's main strength is its theoretical novelty. While scattering transforms [17] and CKNs [15, 16] have been studied previously, this work provides the first unifying framework that connects these approaches to standard CNNs. The authors successfully demonstrate how the stability properties of the kernel framework apply to CNNs, which is a significant theoretical advance. The mathematical development is rigorous, with careful attention to assumptions and proofs (relegated to appendices as is standard for theoretical work). The paper also makes a valuable connection between stability and generalization, showing that functions with low RKHS norm (large margin) generalize better, which provides theoretical justification for why CNNs generalize well.

The paper addresses a fundamental question in deep learning: why do CNNs work so well? By characterizing the geometry of the functional spaces where CNNs live, the authors provide new theoretical insights that could inform regularization techniques and help design more robust models. The connection between stability to deformations and sample complexity is particularly significant for practical applications.

However, the paper has some limitations. The framework applies to CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, which excludes the most commonly used ReLU activation function (though the authors do discuss a smoothed variant). This limits the immediate applicability to state-of-the-art CNN architectures. The paper also lacks empirical validation of the theoretical claims, which would have strengthened the connection between theory and practice. While theoretical papers don't always require experiments, the absence of any empirical demonstration of the stability properties in real CNNs is notable for a paper making such strong theoretical claims.

The paper is well-written and well-structured, with clear notation and logical flow. The mathematical content is dense but presented coherently. The related work section properly contextualizes the contributions relative to scattering transforms, CKNs, and group invariance literature.

**Strengths for Acceptance**:
- Significant theoretical novelty in unifying scattering transforms, CKNs, and standard CNNs
- Rigorous analysis of stability properties with clear connections to generalization
- Addresses a fundamental question about why CNNs work well
- Provides new theoretical insights that could guide future work on regularization and robustness
- Well-executed mathematical development with proper proofs

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited applicability to standard CNNs due to smooth activation requirement (excludes ReLU)
- No empirical validation of theoretical claims
- Connection to state-of-the-art CNN architectures not fully developed
- Some practical implementation details for using the framework are not sufficiently elaborated

Despite these limitations, I believe this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of convolutional representations. The novelty of the kernel framework and the theoretical connection to standard CNNs outweighs the limitations, especially given the paper's focus on theoretical insights rather than empirical results. The work opens up new theoretical avenues for understanding CNNs and could inspire future research on stability-based regularization techniques.

The paper is clearly within NIPS scope, addressing a fundamental theoretical question about deep learning architectures. It builds on but significantly extends previous work on scattering transforms and convolutional kernel networks, providing a novel perspective that distinguishes it from prior NIPS papers.

I recommend acceptance. While the paper could be strengthened with empirical validation and a more direct connection to standard CNN architectures, the theoretical novelty and significance of the contribution are substantial. This work provides a valuable new lens through which to understand convolutional representations, which aligns with NIPS's emphasis on novel theoretical advances. The framework could inspire new approaches to regularization and robustness in deep learning that build on these stability guarantees.
