Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals on continuous domains, generalizing convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), and analyzes its invariance and stability properties to translations and small diffeomorphisms. The construction composes patch extraction, RKHS kernel mappings with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. The main theoretical result (Theorem 6) establishes a Lipschitz-type stability bound to diffeomorphisms: the representation difference scales linearly with the deformation magnitude and with the last pooling scale governing translation invariance, paralleling scattering transform results. The authors discuss discretization with subsampling and show (under assumptions) invertibility across layers (Lemmas 1–2). They extend equivariance/invariance to general groups via group-convolution-like pooling. Finally, they show a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (and a normalization factor) lies in the RKHS of their multilayer kernel (Proposition 7), linking RKHS norm control to stability and generalization.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Elegant RKHS formulation that cleanly separates representation and prediction; connects scattering ideas, CKNs, and a subset of CNNs.
- Stability analysis: The diffeomorphism stability with explicit dependence on depth and pooling scale mirrors classical harmonic analysis arguments, adapted to convolutional kernels. The commutator-based proof outline is clear.
- Group-equivariant layer design: The group-theoretic pooling construction is sound and aligns with prior work on group equivariance.
- Practical pathway: Discussion of discretization and kernel approximation (projections) explains how to implement a tractable representation (CKN), while preserving non-expansiveness.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/assumption gaps
- Signal “preservation” claims (Lemmas 1–2) are strong and insufficiently qualified. Recovery requires: (i) kernels whose RKHS contains all linear functionals, (ii) deconvolution of Gaussians, and (iii) zero-noise, well-conditioned filters. In practice, deconvolution after subsampling is ill-conditioned; the stability of recovery is not quantified. The statement risks overstating invertibility.
- Theorem 6 constants: C1 and C2 are left abstract; the (1+n) dependence is likely loose. No attempt is made to optimize constants or characterize their dependence on patch sizes, dimension d, and κ. This limits interpretability.
- Assumptions (A1–A2) are central but their practicality is underexplored. Not all homogeneous kernels are non-expansive; the paper defers to Appendix B without main-text guidance on concrete, widely used kernels and their induced constants Cσ.
- CNN inclusion (Proposition 7) covers networks with smooth homogeneous activations and a nonstandard normalization nk(u)=||Pk zk−1(u)|| inside the activation. This deviates from common CNN practice (e.g., ReLU without such normalization). Relu is excluded; “smoothed” relu variants are suggested but not specified or evaluated. The resulting class may be too restrictive to claim broad relevance to standard CNNs.
- Zero-norm patches: The definition z̃ik(u)=nk(u)σ(⟨wik,Pkzk−1(u)⟩/nk(u)) does not address nk(u)=0 (0/0). A limit/continuity definition or ε-regularization is needed.
- Discretization: Border effects, aliasing vs invariance trade-offs, and the choice ek≥sk are treated informally. The recommendation “small ek, sk” has no quantitative support, and contradicts common anti-aliasing practice if the Gaussian is too narrow relative to sampling.

Clarity and organization
- Notation overloading: The choice to drop norm subscripts and use ‖·‖ for Hilbert/L2/operator norms can obscure arguments; at key steps (Proposition 3, Lemmas 4–5) exact norms matter.
- Input naming: Theorem 6 uses x with x0=A0x but elsewhere x0 is the input. This can confuse the reader. Keep one convention.
- Reliance on appendices: Many key proofs and kernel/activation conditions (Appendices B–D) are essential to assess correctness but are not sketched in the main text. The paper is not sufficiently self-contained.

Originality and relation to prior work
- The stability argument closely parallels Mallat’s scattering framework; the main novelty is applying it to a more general convolutional kernel pipeline and showing RKHS containment of a class of CNNs. However, related perspectives (Anselmi et al., Montavon et al., Daniely et al.) and group equivariance (Cohen & Welling) already connect deep nets and kernels/groups. The paper should sharpen what is genuinely new: e.g., the exact mapping conditions for CNNs into the multilayer convolutional RKHS and the specific commutator extension [LcAσ,Lτ].
- The “extended version” [3] is cited for key statements (norm preservation with an extra Gaussian layer), which weakens the originality of the submission as a standalone NeurIPS paper.

Significance
- Theoretical insights are valuable, but the impact is blunted by the restrictive activation class, unquantified constants, and lack of empirical illustrations. There are no experiments validating stability trends (depth, σn) or demonstrating benefits of RKHS norm control for generalization. Claims about sample complexity improvements remain speculative.

Specific suggestions/questions
- Quantify C1, C2 for canonical choices (Gaussian pooling, inverse polynomial kernel) and report practical magnitudes.
- Address nk(u)=0 rigorously; provide the exact σ smoothing and its Cσ.
- Provide a toy experiment comparing stability vs σn and n for CKNs and CNNs with smoothed ReLU, and show how RKHS norm correlates with robustness.
- Clarify the stability of deconvolution-based recovery under noise and subsampling; otherwise tone down “signal preservation.”
- Improve clarity by keeping distinct notations for norms; clean up the x/x0 conventions.

Formatting/typos (illustrative, not exhaustive)
- “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)” stray “st”.
- Many inconsistencies: L 2 vs L2; H 0/H0; missing spaces (R d), extra commas in references, missing years/venues for many citations.
- “It is then be possible” → “It is then possible”.
- Define all symbols on first use (e.g., κ in (A3), σ0 relation to s0).

Pros
- Solid, general stability framework for convolutional kernels; clean link to group equivariance.
- Conceptual bridge from RKHSs to a class of CNNs with norm-based stability/generalization control.

Cons
- Over-reliance on restrictive assumptions; key “preservation” claims not practically credible.
- Constants and practical implications opaque; no empirical validation.
- Deviations from standard CNNs limit applicability.
- Numerous clarity and formatting issues.

Recommendation: Reject
While the direction is interesting and the theory is largely sound, the submission contains too many technical and presentation shortcomings, and lacks empirical grounding. Tightening assumptions/claims, quantifying constants, clarifying the CNN class covered, and adding minimal experiments would substantially strengthen the paper.
