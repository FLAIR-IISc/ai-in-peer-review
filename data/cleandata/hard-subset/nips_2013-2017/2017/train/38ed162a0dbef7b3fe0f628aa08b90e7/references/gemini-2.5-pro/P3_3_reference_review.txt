This paper provides a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations, extending concepts from the scattering transform to a broader class of models including Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) and standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). The central idea is to construct a multilayer convolutional kernel on continuous signals, which defines a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The representation is built by composing three operators layer-wise: patch extraction, a kernel mapping, and a linear pooling operator.

The authors' main theoretical contribution is a proof of Lipschitz stability of this representation to the action of small C1-diffeomorphisms. Following the methodology of Mallat (2012) for scattering networks, they show that the representation's variation is bounded by a term proportional to the deformation's gradient (local stability) and another term proportional to the deformation's magnitude (global invariance). Crucially, the global invariance term is shown to be inversely proportional to the scale of the final pooling layer, formally connecting network depth and pooling size to increased translation invariance. The framework is also elegantly extended to achieve equivariance and invariance to general group actions.

A key strength of the paper is its final section, which establishes a formal connection between the proposed kernel framework and standard CNNs. By showing that CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions (such as smoothed ReLUs) can be embedded within the constructed RKHS, the stability results derived for the kernel representation are directly transferred to these CNNs. This provides a principled explanation for the robustness of CNNs and links their stability to the RKHS norm of the learned function, which in turn relates to generalization.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper successfully bridges the gap between the well-understood but non-learned scattering transform and practical, learned CNN architectures. By using the flexible language of RKHSs, it generalizes the stability analysis of Mallat (2012) to a much wider class of representations, including those with learnable components (as in CKNs). This is a valuable step forward in building a cohesive theory for deep learning.

2.  **Clarity and Rigor:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and technically sound. The construction of the multilayer kernel is presented clearly and logically. The mathematical assumptions are stated explicitly (A1-A3), and the derivation of the main stability result (Theorem 6) is well-decomposed into a series of understandable lemmas. The proofs, while deferred to the appendix, appear to be rigorous adaptations of techniques from harmonic analysis.

3.  **Important Implications:** The work provides a powerful insight: the RKHS norm of a CNN-based predictor simultaneously controls its stability to deformations and its generalization capacity (via margin bounds). This formalizes the intuition that models robust to small input variations should generalize better. This connection provides a theoretical underpinning for regularization techniques that penalize model complexity (e.g., weight decay), framing them as controllers of the model's Lipschitz constant with respect to input deformations.

4.  **Generality:** The extension to general group invariances in Section 4 is elegant and demonstrates the power of the proposed framework. By defining patch extraction and pooling operators on a group G, the authors show how to construct G-equivariant representations, connecting their work to the growing literature on geometric deep learning (e.g., Cohen & Welling, 2016).

**Weaknesses and Areas for Clarification:**

1.  **Gap between Theory and Practice in CNN Connection:** The connection to CNNs in Section 5, while being a major strength, relies on a "homogenized" activation of the form `‖z‖σ(〈g, z〉/‖z‖)`, where `z` is an input patch. While ReLU is indeed homogeneous, standard CNNs implement `σ(〈g, z〉)`. The paper's formulation requires an explicit normalization by the patch norm `‖Pkxk-1(u)‖` at each location `u`. This is a non-standard operation and represents a subtle but important departure from common practice. The authors should discuss the significance of this difference. Does this normalization have a practical analog, perhaps related to local response normalization or batch normalization? Without this clarification, the claim that the results apply to "standard" CNNs is slightly overstated.

2.  **Smooth Activation Requirement:** The theoretical framework requires the activation function `σ` to be smooth enough to be represented in the RKHS. Consequently, the analysis does not directly apply to the non-smooth ReLU, which is the most widely used activation in practice. While the authors correctly propose using a smoothed variant, the implications of this discrepancy deserve more discussion. It is a common limitation in theoretical works, but its impact on the tightness or applicability of the derived bounds should be acknowledged.

3.  **Signal Preservation Claim:** Lemma 1 claims that the signal `xk-1` can be recovered from `xk`, implying information preservation. However, the pooling operator `Ak` is a convolution with a low-pass filter (a Gaussian), which will inevitably attenuate or remove high-frequency components of the signal. While the convolution may be theoretically invertible, this is often an ill-posed problem, and from a signal-processing perspective, information is lost. The statement should be qualified, perhaps by specifying that recovery is possible up to a certain frequency cutoff determined by the pooling scale `σk`.

4.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** While this is primarily a theoretical paper, its claims have direct empirical implications. For instance, the theory predicts that CNNs with smaller RKHS norms (as bounded in Proposition 7) should be more stable to small deformations. A simple experiment verifying this correlation on a toy problem, or comparing the robustness of a standard CNN to one with the proposed explicit patch normalization, would have significantly strengthened the paper's impact and grounded the theory more firmly in practice.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong and elegant theoretical contribution to our understanding of deep convolutional networks. It provides a unifying framework that connects stability, invariance, and generalization in a principled way. The technical quality is high, and the writing is clear. The main weaknesses relate to small but important gaps between the theoretical model and the exact form of widely used CNNs. Despite these points, the paper's strengths are substantial, and it represents a significant advance in the theoretical foundations of deep learning. It is very likely to inspire follow-up work, both theoretical and empirical.
