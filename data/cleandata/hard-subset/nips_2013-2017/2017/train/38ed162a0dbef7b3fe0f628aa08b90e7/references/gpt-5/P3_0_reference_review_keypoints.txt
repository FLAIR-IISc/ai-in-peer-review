- Proposes a multilayer convolutional kernel framework on continuous domains mirroring CNNs with patch extraction, homogeneous dot-product kernel maps, and Gaussian pooling, and proves non-expansiveness/contractivity properties.  
- Shows signal recoverability from sampled feature maps and a discretized subsampling construction that preserves information when ek ≥ sk via stable deconvolution.  
- Derives Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms with a scattering-style bound that decays with last pooling scale σn and provides a group-equivariant layer design for global invariance.  
- Demonstrates a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations can be embedded in the induced RKHS and gives a norm upper bound linking Lipschitz stability to generalization.  
- Strength: extends scattering-style stability to broad convolutional kernel representations and connects these kernels to a subclass of CNNs.  
- Strength: clear operator-level decomposition using harmonic analysis (Schur’s test, commutators) yielding interpretable stability constants.  
- Strength: thoughtful discretization showing continuous arguments justify common design choices (small ek, sk; Gaussian anti-aliasing) and preserve information under conditions.  
- Strength: clean, general group-equivariant construction compatible with prior group-equivariant CNN work.  
- Strength: norm-based view ties prediction Lipschitzness to RKHS norm and hence to sample complexity via margin arguments.  
- Weakness: depth dependence is loose—the bound scales linearly with depth (factor 1+n) and could be tightened by exploiting increasing smoothing across layers.  
- Weakness: pooling breaks norm preservation; needs explicit quantification of energy retention for natural images to support the lower bound on ‖Φ(x)‖.  
- Weakness: CNN inclusion requires nonstandard input-dependent normalization (nk(u)), deviating from typical CNNs; need clarification on practical approximations (batch/instance norm) and sensitivity.  
- Weakness: ReLU excluded; requires smooth homogeneous activations—authors should provide concrete σ/kernel pairs, constants Cσ, and plots for Bn,i growth to assess potential blow-up.  
- Weakness: CKN projection approximations lack explicit additive error terms in stability bounds and need analysis of error accumulation with layer depth and subspace size.  
- Weakness: discrete deconvolution recoverability is ill-posed with noise/boundary effects—stability constants and noise amplification should be clarified and claims phrased as approximate recovery with quantified error.  
- Weakness: pooling over non-compact groups (e.g., SE(2)) requires assumptions on filter localization and finite support integration; need conditions for well-posedness and computational feasibility and effect on stability constants.  
- Weakness: no empirical validation—small-scale experiments testing predicted trade-offs and constants would substantiate theoretical claims.  
- Quality: technically careful and plausible proofs, but some constants and depth dependence appear loose and discretization/projection error accumulation is under-quantified.  
- Clarity: generally well-organized; suggest adding a worked kernel/activation example with explicit constants, summarizing assumptions (A0–A3) in one place, and detailing reconstruction/deconvolution stability.  
- Originality: meaningful extension of scattering, CKNs, and group-equivariance—novel multilayer convolutional RKHS formalism and CNN embedding.  
- Significance: broadens theoretical grounding for invariance/stability beyond scattering and motivates RKHS-norm regularization, but practical impact needs quantitative bounds and empirical support.  
- Key questions for authors: can the (1+n) factor be reduced, provide explicit σ/kernel with Cσ and bounded Bn,i, quantify additive projection errors in CKNs, assess sensitivity to replacing nk(u) with fixed/batch-normalized scale, and specify conditions for non-compact group pooling.  
- Pros for acceptance: solid theoretical contribution, clear RKHS-CNN connection, rigorous operator framework and group-equivariant extension.  
- Cons for acceptance: missing empirical evidence, loose quantitative bounds for projection/discretization errors, reliance on nonstandard normalization and smooth activations limiting direct applicability.
