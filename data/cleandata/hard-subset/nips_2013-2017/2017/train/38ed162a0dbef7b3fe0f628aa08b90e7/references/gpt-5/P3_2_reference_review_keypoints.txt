- Summary: Proposes a multilayer convolutional kernel mirroring CNN architectures, proving Theorem 6 (Lipschitz-type stability to C1-diffeomorphisms) with depth- and pooling-dependent constants, plus discretization, projection approximations, group extensions, and RKHS inclusion of a class of CNNs (Prop.7).

- Relation to prior work: Adapts scattering-transform tools to convolutional kernels and CKNs, connects to group-equivariant CNNs and prior Lipschitz/kernel analyses.

- Strength: Conceptual unification of scattering theory, RKHS/kernel methods, and CNNs with clear separation of representation geometry and predictor complexity via RKHS norm.

- Strength: Provides diffeomorphic stability and translation-invariance guarantees (Theorem 6) with sound commutator-based analysis extending Mallat’s lemmas.

- Strength: Addresses practical issues—discretization, subsampling, and finite-dimensional kernel approximations—arguing stability is preserved under non-expansive projections.

- Strength: Offers a principled recipe for group equivariance/invariance via group-covariant patching and pooling.

- Strength: Shows a nontrivial class of smooth homogeneous-activation CNNs lies in the RKHS, giving recursive norm bounds linking stability and generalization.

- Weakness: Relies on Gaussian pooling, norm-preserving/non-expansive kernel maps, and patch-size assumptions (A1–A3) that diverge from common CNN practices (e.g., ReLU, striding).

- Weakness: Stability bounds scale linearly with depth and involve constants (C1) dependent on kernel and dimension, with unclear quantitative tightness and practical magnitude.

- Weakness: Signal-preservation lemmas assume ideal deconvolution and coverage; discrete/noisy settings and conditioning are not quantified.

- Weakness: Group invariance section is qualitative and lacks quantitative stability/invariance bounds analogous to Theorem 6 for non-abelian groups.

- Weakness: No empirical validation of predicted trade-offs, RKHS-norm effects on generalization, or comparisons to scattering/CNNs.

- Weakness: Proposition 7 depends on kernel-specific constants (Cσ) that may be large, making norm bounds potentially vacuous and offering little guidance for training/regularization.

- Quality: Proof strategy and technical tools are solid and consistent with scattering literature; assumptions are explicit and finite-dimensional approximations are handled reasonably.

- Quality concern: Claims about signal recovery would benefit from quantified stability to noise and finite support.

- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized; continuous/discrete notation separation is careful.

- Clarity suggestions: Clarify reconstruction procedures and conditioning, make A3 concrete relative to CNN hyperparameters, give explicit kernel examples and Cσ values (e.g., 2D), and discuss boundary effects.

- Originality: Extends scattering stability to broader convolutional kernels and rigorously relates them to CNNs; commutator extension and recursive RKHS norm bounds are novel.

- Significance: Advances theoretical understanding of invariance/stability in deep convolutional representations and could inform robustness and kernelized model design; practical impact needs empirical/quantitative support.

- Detailed questions/suggestions: Sensitivity to non-Gaussian pooling; quantify constants for d=2 and σ schedules; provide Riesz-type deconvolution stability under noise and cases with ek < sk; analyze strided/dilated convolutions; instantiate Cσ for specific kernels and smoothed ReLU; derive a group-analog of Theorem 6 (e.g., SO(2)); relate RKHS norm bounds to layerwise spectral/Lipschitz control and adversarial robustness.

- Pros summary: Solid theoretical unification, clear stability/invariance guarantees, addresses discretization and kernel approximations, extends to group actions and includes a CNN class in RKHS.

- Cons summary: Relies on assumptions atypical in practice, potentially loose/large constants, lacks empirical validation, and group section lacks quantitative stability results.

- Recommendation: Weak accept.
