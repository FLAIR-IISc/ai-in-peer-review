

# Peer Review: "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of convolutional neural networks by bridging the gap between the scattering transform framework and standard CNN architectures through a kernel-based approach. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and demonstrate that this functional space contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. Their key theoretical contribution is establishing stability guarantees to diffeomorphisms and translation invariance for this general class of models, showing that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Rigor and Novel Connection:** The paper establishes a mathematically sound connection between kernel methods and CNNs, which is both novel and important. The authors successfully extend Mallat's scattering transform framework to standard CNN architectures through the kernel lens, providing theoretical guarantees that were previously only available for non-learnable architectures.

2. **Clear Theoretical Framework:** The construction of the multilayer convolutional kernel is well-motivated and carefully developed. The authors provide precise mathematical definitions of the operators (patch extraction, kernel mapping, pooling) and their properties, with appropriate assumptions (A1-A3) that are well-justified.

3. **Significant Implications for Generalization:** The demonstration that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization is particularly valuable. This provides a principled theoretical basis for understanding why stable representations generalize better, connecting to established concepts like margin bounds in SVMs.

4. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper properly situates its contribution relative to the scattering transform, CKNs, and other relevant work on group invariance. The authors clearly articulate how their work extends previous approaches.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Discretization Gap:** While the paper discusses discretization in Section 2.1, the theoretical results are established for continuous signals, with only a brief connection to discrete practice. The authors claim their results "match the desired notion of stability" for discrete representations, but the gap between continuous theory and discrete implementation (where most CNNs operate) is not sufficiently bridged. This is a significant concern for practical relevance.

2. **Activation Function Limitation:** The connection to standard CNNs is restricted to smooth homogeneous activation functions. While the authors acknowledge that ReLU is not smooth and mention a smoothed variant, they don't fully resolve this gap. The most widely used activation function in CNNs (ReLU) is explicitly excluded from their theoretical framework, which weakens the claim that their results apply to "standard CNNs."

3. **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical with no empirical validation of the stability properties. While theoretical work doesn't always require experiments, for a paper making claims about the properties of standard CNNs, some empirical demonstration (e.g., measuring stability to deformations in practice) would significantly strengthen the contribution.

4. **Assumption Limitations:** The stability bound requires ‖∇τ‖∞ ≤ 1/2, which is a strong constraint on the deformation. While this is common in the scattering literature, it's worth noting that natural image deformations can sometimes violate this condition, potentially limiting the practical applicability of the bounds.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding CNNs through the lens of kernel methods and stability. The core results are technically sound and address an important gap between the scattering transform framework and standard CNN architectures. The connection between RKHS norm, stability, and generalization is particularly compelling.

However, the practical relevance is somewhat limited by the continuous-domain focus and the restriction to smooth activations. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these limitations—either by extending the analysis to discrete domains or providing empirical evidence that the continuous theory translates well to practice. The exclusion of ReLU, the dominant activation in CNNs, is a notable weakness that should be addressed.

**Recommendation:**

The paper has strong theoretical merit and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While it has some limitations regarding practical applicability, these are common in theoretical deep learning papers. I recommend acceptance, as the theoretical insights are valuable and could guide future work on stable representation learning.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Provides the first general theoretical stability guarantees for standard CNN architectures
- Establishes a principled connection between kernel methods and CNNs
- Demonstrates the theoretical link between stability and generalization
- Well-written with clear mathematical exposition

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Theoretical results are limited to continuous domains (not directly applicable to discrete images)
- Excludes the most common activation function (ReLU) from the theoretical framework
- Lacks empirical validation of the theoretical claims

This paper advances our theoretical understanding of why CNNs work well, and while it has some limitations, the core contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. The authors have opened up an important line of inquiry that could inform future work on designing more stable and generalizable deep learning architectures.
