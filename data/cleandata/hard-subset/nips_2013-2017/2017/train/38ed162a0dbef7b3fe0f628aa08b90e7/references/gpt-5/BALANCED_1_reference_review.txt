Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework for analyzing invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. Building on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), the authors define a sequence of RKHS feature maps via patch extraction, kernel mapping with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. For continuous-domain signals, they prove stability to C1 diffeomorphisms in the sense of Lipschitz continuity, with a bound of the form C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞, thus recovering the intuition that deeper hierarchies and larger final pooling scale increase translation invariance while controlling sensitivity to local deformations. They extend the construction to group-equivariant layers and show how to attain global invariance to general locally compact groups via final-layer pooling on the group. Finally, they relate the RKHS to a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, showing such CNNs lie in the RKHS and that their RKHS norm controls Lipschitz smoothness and hence stability and generalization.

This work is situated at the intersection of scattering transforms and kernel-based views of deep networks. It generalizes Mallat/Bruna’s scattering theory (e.g., Bruna & Mallat, PAMI’13; Mallat, CPAM’12) by replacing pre-defined wavelets with learned/learnable kernel maps while retaining provable stability to diffeomorphisms. It also connects to CKNs (Mairal et al., NIPS’14/16) by formalizing their continuous-domain counterpart and to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling, ICML’16), highlighting how equivariance/invariance arise from commuting operators. The RKHS inclusion of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations echoes dual/kernel views of deep nets (e.g., Montavon et al., JMLR’11; Daniely et al., NIPS’16), but with a convolutional, spatially local flavor and explicit stability bounds.

Strengths
- Clear, principled multilayer kernel construction that mirrors CNNs and CKNs, separating representation from prediction and enabling RKHS-based control of Lipschitz constants.
- Stability to diffeomorphisms with explicit dependence on depth and pooling scale (Theorem 6), matching scattering-style results but in a wider class of architectures.
- Group-theoretic extension showing how to build equivariant/invariant layers for general locally compact groups via patch extraction and group pooling.
- Connection showing that a nontrivial class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations are functions in the RKHS, with explicit norm bounds (Proposition 7), tying stability to generalization.
- Thoughtful treatment of discretization and kernel approximation, arguing that finite-dimensional projections (as in practical CKNs) preserve nonexpansiveness and thus stability.

Weaknesses and detailed comments (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance)
- Quality/technical: 
  - The discretization “signal preservation” (Lemmas 1–2) relies on deconvolution with Gaussian filters and “mild” assumptions (ek ≥ sk). In practice, deconvolution can be ill-conditioned and noise-sensitive. It would strengthen the result to quantify stability constants for deconvolution (e.g., conditioning of h̄k’s frequency response, boundary conditions), and to state robustness to noise/quantization.
  - The main bound’s constants: C1 depends on the pooling kernel and κ, growing like κ^{d+1}. For d=2 this may be manageable, but the paper could better discuss the magnitude of constants and their practical implications, especially as κ scales with patch size/pooling scale. The (1+n) factor may also be pessimistic; some discussion on whether tighter depth dependence is possible would help.
  - The assumption set (A1–A3) merits more explicit cataloging of kernels satisfying them. Appendix B is referenced, but having a succinct main-text list with examples (Gaussian, inverse polynomial, etc.) and their Cσ would improve reproducibility.
  - The CNN inclusion requires smooth homogeneous activations. Standard ReLU is excluded; a smoothed variant is suggested. It would be useful to specify a concrete smooth ReLU approximation, verify it satisfies Lemma B.2, and discuss the approximation error w.r.t. true ReLU networks.
  - The statement that the RKHS norm controls sample complexity is conceptually right, but no explicit generalization bound is provided. A Rademacher/margin bound in terms of ‖f‖HKn and data norms would substantiate the claim.

- Clarity:
  - The continuous-to-discrete mapping is helpful; still, a concise “recipe” table mapping (ek, sk, σk) in the discrete model to (Sk, σk) in the continuous one, with recommended scaling laws and anti-aliasing constraints, would aid practitioners.
  - Some results crucial to understanding (e.g., proof sketches of Lemma 4, details of Cσ) are deferred to appendices; short intuition in the main text would be appreciated.
  - The role of the “homogenization” factor nk(u)=‖Pkxk−1(u)‖ in Eq. (8) is nonstandard in CNNs. A clearer discussion about implementational implications (normalization vs. batch/layer norm analogs) would help.

- Originality:
  - The specific commutator bound [LcAσ, Lτ] and its use to control stability across layers seems novel within this kernelized framework. The group pooling approach is consistent with prior equivariance literature; the novelty is in unifying this with the RKHS analysis.
  - The inclusion of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations into the RKHS, with recursive norm bounds, meaningfully extends prior kernel views.

- Significance:
  - The theoretical insights are valuable for understanding and regularizing convolutional representations beyond scattering, especially for CKNs. However, the paper lacks empirical validation demonstrating, e.g., that increasing σn improves invariance without undue loss of discriminative power, or that RKHS norm regularization correlates with robustness/generalization in CKNs/CNNs. Even small-scale experiments (e.g., stability under controlled deformations on MNIST/CIFAR) would increase impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, careful theory extending scattering-style stability to a broader, learnable convolutional kernel framework, with clear operator-theoretic arguments.
- Unifies equivariance/invariance, RKHS control of Lipschitzness, and a constructive link to a class of CNNs.
- Relevant and timely for principled analysis of deep representations; likely to influence future theoretical and algorithmic work (e.g., norm-based regularization of CKNs).

Arguments against acceptance
- No empirical evaluation to support the practical relevance of constants, discretization robustness, or the CNN inclusion.
- Assumptions (smooth activations, homogenization) limit direct applicability to standard CNNs with ReLU.
- Some key constants and conditions (deconvolution stability, size of Cσ) are not quantified in the main text.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a small experimental section verifying the stability/invariance trends and illustrating the trade-off with σn and depth on CKNs.
- Quantify constants (C1, C2, κ dependence) and provide practical guidance for parameter choices.
- Specify concrete smooth ReLU approximations and compute Cσ for common kernels; discuss approximation to ReLU networks empirically/theoretically.
- Add a generalization bound (e.g., via Rademacher complexity) to support sample complexity claims.
- Clarify discretization robustness: assumptions on boundary conditions, frequency support, and noise.
- Discuss whether the (1+n) factor can be tightened and how pooling schedules affect the bound.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper offers a meaningful theoretical contribution that extends and unifies important threads (scattering, kernels, equivariance) with clear potential impact, despite missing empirical validation and some restrictive assumptions.
