Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework for signals on continuous domains and studies invariance and stability properties of the resulting representations. The construction mirrors CNNs/CKNs: each layer performs patch extraction, a norm-preserving and non-expansive kernel mapping to an RKHS, and Gaussian pooling. Main theoretical results include: (i) a stability bound to C1-diffeomorphisms with a term linear in depth and a translation term decaying with the last pooling scale, analogous in spirit to scattering but for a broader class of homogeneous dot-product kernels; (ii) a discussion of discretization and a signal-recovery argument under subsampling via deconvolution; (iii) an equivariant layer construction over general groups via group-convolution-style pooling; and (iv) a link showing that the RKHS associated with the multilayer kernel contains a family of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, with a recursive norm bound, implying that the stability results carry over to this subclass of CNNs.

Relation to prior work
This builds directly on scattering networks (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) by adapting commutator-based proofs to a different architecture, and on convolutional kernel networks by placing them in a continuous-domain RKHS framework. It also connects to group-equivariant CNNs and kernel views of deep networks. The paper is careful to cite much of this literature.

Strengths
- Technically careful operator-theoretic analysis that generalizes the scattering stability framework to a broader family of kernels and provides a unified view encompassing CKNs and a subclass of CNNs.
- Clear decomposition of the role of patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling; the depth-dependent diffeomorphism bound and the explicit 1/σn translation term are intuitive and align with prior theory.
- The group-equivariant formulation is clean and aligns with established constructions on locally compact groups.
- The RKHS embedding of certain CNNs with homogeneous smooth activations and the induced norm control provide a useful conceptual link between stability and generalization.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to scattering/CKNs is limited. The main stability result adapts known techniques (commutators, Schur’s test) with similar assumptions and yields qualitatively similar bounds; constants are unspecified and depth dependence is linear as in prior work. The group section largely restates standard equivariant constructions without new quantitative results.
- Practical relevance to standard CNNs is tenuous. The required assumptions (norm-preserving, non-expansive homogeneous kernel maps; pre-smoothing A0; Gaussian pooling; homogenized activations with nk(u) factors; smoothed ReLU variants) deviate from common implementations (ReLU, learned/non-Gaussian pooling, striding, BatchNorm, padding). The link to CNNs covers a restricted class; ReLU is excluded, and the impact of smoothing/homogenization on performance is not explored.
- Discretization and “signal recovery with subsampling” rely on deconvolution of Gaussian filters. In practice this is ill-conditioned and noise-sensitive; the stability of the inverse is only heuristically addressed. Border effects are acknowledged but not analyzed. The claimed preservation of information thus seems fragile under realistic conditions.
- No empirical validation. Given the paper’s motivation (stability, invariance, and implications for generalization/robustness), there are no experiments quantifying the derived bounds, comparing to scattering or modern CNNs, or demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed representations or the RKHS-norm perspective.
- Claims about improved sample complexity are qualitative; no generalization bounds beyond the standard Lipschitz/RKHS norm control are provided.

Quality
The theoretical arguments appear sound under the stated assumptions, and the organization of operators is rigorous. However, some key statements (signal preservation via sampling and deconvolution; stability under kernel approximations) are asserted at a high level with proofs deferred and without quantitative constants or robustness analysis.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with appropriate background. Some dependencies of constants (e.g., on κ, dimension, kernel choices) could be made more explicit. The role of nk(u) and how it interacts with learning should be clarified. A brief discussion of how striding/padding/boundaries affect the theory would help.

Originality
Moderate. The paper extends established scattering results to a broader kernelized multilayer setting and formalizes a link to a restricted class of CNNs. The group-equivariant part is standard.

Significance
Conceptually useful for understanding invariance/stability in kernelized deep architectures and for framing a subset of CNNs within an RKHS. Practical impact is limited by restrictive assumptions and lack of empirical verification. The contributions are more incremental than transformative.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide explicit magnitudes for C1, C2 for typical h and κ, and a discussion of tightness and depth dependence. Are there architectures/settings where the (1+n) factor can be improved?
- Empirically verify stability: measure ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ versus ‖τ‖∞, ‖∇τ‖∞ for CKNs, compare to scattering and to standard CNNs.
- Clarify stability under discretization: conditions for stable deconvolution (SNR, σk relative to sk), and sensitivity to boundary handling.
- Discuss how BatchNorm, striding, and common nonlinearities could be incorporated or how the theory fails.
- For non-translation groups, can you extend Lemma 4-type bounds to compact groups with geodesic distances?

Pros
- Solid, careful theory extending a respected line of work.
- Unifying view connecting kernels, scattering-like stability, and a subclass of CNNs.
- Clear articulation of how pooling scale controls translation invariance.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond known scattering/CKN frameworks.
- Strong, nonstandard assumptions vis-à-vis practical CNNs.
- Discretization/invertibility assumptions are fragile.
- No experiments; practical significance not demonstrated.

Recommendation
Overall score: 5 (weak reject). I appreciate the rigor and the unifying perspective, but the incremental nature relative to scattering, the restrictive assumptions for CNNs, and the lack of empirical validation reduce the significance for NeurIPS. Strengthening the novelty claims and adding empirical support would move this toward acceptance.

Confidence: 4/5.
