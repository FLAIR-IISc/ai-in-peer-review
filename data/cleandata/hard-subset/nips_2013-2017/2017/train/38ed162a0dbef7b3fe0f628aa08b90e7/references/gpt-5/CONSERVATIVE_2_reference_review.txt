Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals on continuous domains, mirroring CNN architectures via three operators per layer: patch extraction, a kernel mapping to an RKHS using homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. The resulting representation Φn induces a kernel Kn whose RKHS contains linear predictors over the final feature map. The authors prove stability to C1-diffeomorphisms, with a bound of the form (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖, yielding increasing translation invariance with the last-layer pooling scale. They discuss discretization and signal recovery under subsampling, and argue that the stability results extend to practical CKNs via non-expansive projections onto finite-dimensional subspaces. They then generalize pooling and patch extraction to locally compact groups to obtain equivariance and, via the last pooling layer, global invariance to group actions. Finally, they establish that the RKHS associated with their multilayer kernel contains a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (via a homogenized activation scheme), providing a norm bound for such networks and linking stability/generalization to the RKHS norm. The work is positioned relative to scattering networks, convolutional kernel networks, and group equivariant CNNs.

Strengths
- Provides a clean operator-theoretic framework that parallels CNNs while enabling RKHS-based analysis.
- Stability result to diffeomorphisms closely aligned with the scattering literature, with clear dependence on depth and pooling scale.
- Thoughtful treatment of discretization and subsampling (including a recovery lemma), rather than staying purely in the continuum.
- Extension to group actions via equivariant patch extraction and pooling is natural and aligns with established group-CNN ideas.
- The inclusion result for a class of CNNs in the induced RKHS with an explicit norm bound is technically interesting and connects to recent “kernel views” of deep nets.

Weaknesses
- No empirical validation. Claims about “preserving signal information,” “near-invariance,” and implications for generalization/sample complexity are not substantiated experimentally, nor are the constants in the bounds illustrated numerically. Given the strong practical motivation (CNNs/CKNs), at least toy experiments comparing stability versus scattering or standard CNNs under controlled deformations would strengthen the case.
- Assumptions narrow the scope. Norm-preserving kernel maps (A1–A2), Gaussian pooling, and the initial low-pass A0 are convenient for analysis but diverge from many practical architectures. The stability bound depends linearly on depth (1+n), which may be pessimistic and is not contrasted empirically.
- The claim that the functional space “contains a large class of CNNs” hinges on homogenized activations with nk(u)=‖Pk xk−1(u)‖. This is nonstandard; ReLU is not directly covered (only smoothed variants). It is unclear how representative these CNNs are of high-performing practical models, and whether the induced normalization affects training/expressivity.
- Relation to prior work sometimes feels incremental. Stability bounds under diffeomorphisms, commutator-based proofs, and group equivariance are well-trodden in the scattering literature; the main novelty is to transport them to the proposed multilayer kernel/CKN setting. The paper would benefit from sharper contrasts with scattering bounds (e.g., constants, depth dependence, norm preservation) and with existing “deep kernel machine” analyses.
- Discretization section relies on deconvolution stability and conditions like ek ≥ sk; practical guidance on choosing ek, sk, σk and quantifying the stability of deconvolution is limited. Border effects and computational aspects are deferred.
- The RKHS-norm-based generalization discussion restates standard margin-based intuitions. No actual generalization bounds tailored to this architecture are provided.

Quality
The theoretical development appears careful and largely sound, with proofs deferred to the appendix. However, the lack of empirical tests and absence of quantitative constants leave several claims practically untested. Some claims (e.g., “preserve all information” after pooling and subsampling) rely on ideal deconvolution and coverage; stability in realistic discretized, noisy settings is not addressed.

Clarity
Generally well written, but dense. The operator view is clear and the relation to prior work is outlined, though several important technical details are in the appendices. The paper would benefit from:
- A concise table of kernel choices and the corresponding Cσ(·).
- Concrete examples illustrating the bounds numerically on simple signals.
- Clearer guidance on parameter scaling (ek, sk, σk) and the discretization/continuous link.

Originality
Moderate. The main originality is extending scattering-style stability analysis to a multilayer convolutional kernel that includes CKNs, and showing inclusion of certain CNNs in the induced RKHS with norm bounds. Group equivariance construction follows known patterns. The homogenized activation link to CNNs is interesting but constrained.

Significance
Potentially useful for theoretical understanding of CKNs and for bridging kernel and deep learning perspectives. Without empirical validation and with restrictive assumptions on activations, impact on mainstream CNN practice is uncertain.

Pros
- Solid theoretical analysis of stability/invariance for a multilayer convolutional kernel.
- Bridges CKNs and a class of CNNs via RKHS inclusion and norm control.
- Clear group-theoretic treatment of equivariance/invariance.

Cons
- No experiments; practical relevance not demonstrated.
- Assumptions and activation constraints limit applicability to standard CNNs.
- Incremental relative to scattering in terms of techniques and guarantees.
- Pessimistic depth dependence and unspecified constants.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide empirical evaluations of deformation stability for CKNs/CNNs consistent with your theory, including comparisons to scattering?
2. How sensitive are the bounds/constants to kernel choices? Can you tabulate Cσ(·) for the proposed kernels and give numerical ranges?
3. How realistic is the homogenized activation in practice? Does a smoothed ReLU with the proposed normalization retain performance on standard tasks?
4. Can you relax A1–A2 and still retain useful stability guarantees?
5. How do you select ek, sk, σk in practice to balance stability, invariance, and information loss?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contribution is neat and likely correct, but feels incremental relative to established scattering results, and the practical implications are untested. Adding empirical validation and clarifying the relevance to standard CNNs would notably strengthen the case.

Confidence
High.
