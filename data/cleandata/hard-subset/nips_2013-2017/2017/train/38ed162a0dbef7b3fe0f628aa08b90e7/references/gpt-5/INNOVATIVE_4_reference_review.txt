Summary
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that mirrors CNN architectures while living in an RKHS, enabling a principled analysis of translation invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms. The construction alternates patch extraction, kernel mapping via homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling, yielding a feature map on Ω and a kernel Kn whose RKHS contains functions acting on the representation. The authors prove: (i) signal preservation and recoverability (under sampling assumptions); (ii) Lipschitz stability to C1-diffeomorphisms with a bound scaling with (1 + n)‖∇τ‖∞ and translation-invariance improving with the last pooling scale σn; (iii) compatibility of these properties with practical discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs); (iv) an extension to global invariance for general locally compact groups via group-equivariant patching and pooling; and (v) an inclusion result showing that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations belongs to the RKHS induced by the proposed multilayer kernel, linking stability to RKHS norm control and generalization.

Relation to prior work
The work is inspired by scattering transforms (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat), which provide norm-preserving, deformation-stable representations using fixed wavelets, and by Convolutional Kernel Networks (Mairal et al.), which approximate layered RKHS maps. It extends scattering-style stability results to a broader class of multilayer convolutional operators beyond pre-specified wavelets, and formalizes group invariance similarly to scattering on groups and group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling). It also connects to the literature on kernels for deep networks and norm-based generalization. The bridge to CNNs via RKHS inclusion is reminiscent of dual/kernel views (e.g., Anselmi et al., Daniely et al.) but targets convolutional architectures with pooling and spatial structure.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a unified RKHS framework capturing both CKNs and a nontrivial subset of CNNs, with provable stability to diffeomorphisms and tunable translation invariance via pooling scale. This goes beyond scattering by allowing learned (or at least non-wavelet) feature maps through kernel choices.
- Technically sound derivations: clean decomposition of the stability bound via commutators, with careful handling of shifted integral operators; clear dependence on depth and pooling scale; compatibility with kernel projections due to non-expansiveness.
- Generality: the group-equivariant construction is principled and flexible, separating local stability (inner pooling) from global invariance (final pooling), applicable to roto-translation and other groups.
- Insightful link to CNNs: the RKHS-containment result for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations provides a path to carry stability and norm-based generalization to learned networks, clarifying how architectural choices (patch size, pooling scale) influence invariance/stability.
- Practical pathway: the discretization lemmas and discussion of CKN approximation indicate how the continuous analysis informs implementable models.

Weaknesses
- Empirical validation is missing. Even small-scale experiments (e.g., numerical stability under controlled diffeomorphisms for CKNs; effect of σn; group invariance cases) would strengthen the practical relevance and calibrate constants.
- Activation restrictions: the RKHS inclusion relies on smooth homogeneous σ and normalized pre-activations with nk(u)=‖patch‖. While smoothed ReLU variants exist, the deviation from standard CNN practice (biases, batch norm, ReLU, striding/max pooling) limits direct applicability. A clearer discussion of how to adapt/approximate standard ReLU CNNs would help.
- Bounds may be loose: the dependence on (1 + n)‖∇τ‖∞ and on κ in Lemma 4 is likely conservative; no discussion of empirical tightness or improved constants for specific kernels/pooling. Also, the norm of Φ can decay with depth, complicating direct comparisons with scattering’s isometry.
- Signal “recovery” via deconvolution (Lemma 2) is brittle in practice and sensitive to noise; the conditions ek ≥ sk and small pooling bandwidth are somewhat restrictive; more guidance on stable parameter regimes would be useful.
- Group invariance section, while clean, overlaps with prior equivariance literature; the novelty lies more in the RKHS framing than in the group mechanics.

Quality
- The theoretical results are sound and well-motivated, with appropriate use of harmonic analysis and operator bounds. Some proofs are deferred, but the main arguments are credible and trace known techniques.
- The lack of experiments and limited discussion of constants/robustness are the main quality gaps.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized; the operator view (Pk, Mk, Ak) is helpful. However, some assumptions (A1–A3) and their practical implications (e.g., choosing κ, growth of σk and patch sizes) could be emphasized earlier. A schematic relating discrete parameters (ek, sk) to continuous (Sk, σk) is appreciated.

Originality
- High. Extends scattering-style stability to a significantly broader, multilayer kernel class and ties it to CNNs via RKHS inclusion. The commutator analysis with shifted pooling and the explicit containment of CNNs are notable.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: provides a theoretical lens for understanding invariance/stability in learned conv representations; suggests norm-based control for stability and generalization. Likely to influence further analysis of CNNs and design of kernelized deep architectures.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide small-scale experiments quantifying the stability bound’s behavior with depth and σn for CKNs? Even synthetic 1D signals would help.
- How sensitive are the results to using average or max pooling instead of Gaussian? Any extension of Lemma 5 beyond Gaussian kernels?
- Can the RKHS inclusion be extended to standard ReLU (e.g., via mollification limits) and to include biases and batch normalization?
- What practical guidance emerges for choosing σk and patch sizes to balance information preservation vs. invariance?
- Do you have bounds quantifying the additional error from kernel projection (Πk) in the stability inequality?

Pros
- Novel, unifying theoretical framework; rigorous stability and invariance analysis; principled link to CNNs; clear guidance on the role of pooling.

Cons
- No empirical validation; activation/architecture restrictions; potentially loose bounds and reliance on continuous idealizations.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant and forward-looking conceptual framework with solid theory that broadens our understanding of convolutional representations beyond scattering. Despite limited empirical validation and some idealized assumptions, the novelty and potential impact justify inclusion.
