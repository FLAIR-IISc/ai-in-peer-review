Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that yields feature maps akin to CNNs and CKNs, and studies its invariance and stability properties to translations and small diffeomorphisms. The representation is built by iterating patch extraction, a norm-preserving/non-expansive kernel map with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. The main technical results show Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms with a bound of the form C1(1+n)∥∇τ∥∞ + C2∥τ∥∞/σn, paralleling scattering transform guarantees but for a broader class of architectures. The authors also describe discretization (subsampling) and kernel approximation steps, proving that information can be recovered under mild conditions and that stability is preserved by projections used in CKNs. They further show that a nontrivial class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (and a normalization) lie in the RKHS of the constructed kernel, with explicit recursive bounds on the RKHS norm, linking stability and generalization.

This work is well situated within and extends prior lines of research: scattering networks (Mallat, Bruna) for provable invariance/stability; CKNs (Mairal et al.) for practical kernelized conv nets; kernel views of deep nets (Anselmi/Poggio, Daniely et al.); and group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling). The novel contribution is to unify scattering-style stability analysis with a kernel construction that (i) encompasses CKNs and (ii) contains a class of CNNs, while also addressing discretization and group-equivariance in a principled way.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The analysis is careful and builds on solid harmonic analysis tools (commutators, Schur’s test) and known results (e.g., Mallat’s lemma for pooling and diffeomorphisms). Proposition 3 isolates stability as commutators of pooling/patching with diffeomorphic warps; Lemma 4 extends Mallat’s commutator control to shifted integral operators; Lemma 5 provides the expected 1/σ decay for translations. Theorem 6 then follows cleanly under assumptions A1–A3.
- The discretization section gives reasonable conditions (ek ≥ sk, appropriate Gaussian bandwidth) under which deconvolution and recovery are possible, acknowledging that stability depends on conditioning (scale not too large). This is honest and important for practice.
- The RKHS containment of a class of CNNs (Proposition 7) is technically nontrivial and useful: it requires smooth homogeneous activations and a normalization nk(u)=∥Pk xk−1(u)∥. The recursive norm bound is explicit and ties to kernel parameters via Cσ. This justifies transferring stability guarantees to certain CNNs via the basic RKHS Lipschitz inequality.
- Caveats: (i) Norm preservation is lost due to pooling; the authors discuss why this may be acceptable for natural signals and how adding a final Gaussian RKHS layer restores unit-norm stability, but quantitative constants governing energy loss are not provided. (ii) The deconvolution-based “signal preservation” in discrete settings is exact only noiselessly; the paper notes stability when σ is small enough, but the practical conditioning is not analyzed. (iii) The stability bound scales linearly with depth n, which may be loose for typical choices of σk, Sk; no lower bounds or tightness discussion is provided. Overall, the proofs appear sound given the stated assumptions.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized: the operator construction is precise; assumptions are stated explicitly; connections to discretization and practical CKNs are explained. Proofs are in the appendix but sketched intuitively in the main text. The group-equivariance section is concise and clear.
- Some parts could be clearer: (a) spell out concrete choices of kernels and corresponding Cσ to help readers instantiate Proposition 7; (b) quantify the dependence of constants C1 on κ and dimension, and discuss how to choose σk, Sk across layers; (c) explicitly discuss conditioning of the deconvolution in Lemma 2 with noise.

Originality
- The unification is novel: extending scattering-style deformation stability to a kernel framework that includes CKNs and a class of CNNs, with a general group pooling/equivariance construction. The commutator analysis for shifted integral operators and the CNN-in-RKHS construction with recursive norm control are new and nontrivial.
- Related work is well covered and the distinctions are clear: unlike scattering, filters are not fixed wavelets; unlike prior kernel analyses, the paper provides deformation stability and group invariance within a multilayer convolutional RKHS and relates it to practical CKN approximations and certain CNNs.

Significance
- The results provide theoretical grounding for why convolutional architectures with pooling enjoy deformation stability and translation/group invariance beyond the scattering setting. The RKHS perspective links stability to generalization via norm control, a message likely to influence both theory and design (e.g., choice of pooling scales, patch sizes, activation smoothness).
- Practical impact is indirect: the paper does not present empirical validation or new algorithms, but it strengthens the theoretical foundations of CKNs and suggests principled architectural choices. Given the community’s interest in robustness/invariance and theory of deep nets, this is significant.

Pros for acceptance
- Rigorous, well-motivated theoretical contribution bridging scattering, kernels, and CNNs.
- Clear stability bound to diffeomorphisms with explicit architectural dependence.
- Inclusion of CNNs in the RKHS with norm bounds; principled group-equivariant pooling.
- Careful discussion of discretization and compatibility with CKN approximations.

Cons against acceptance
- No empirical validation to illustrate constants, tightness, or practical benefits.
- Assumptions constrain the class of CNNs (smooth homogeneous activations, normalization nk) and require initial low-pass A0.
- Some constants and conditioning issues (deconvolution) left qualitative rather than quantified; depth dependence might be loose.

Suggestions
- Add small experiments quantifying deformation stability versus depth/σn and comparing to standard CNNs and CKNs.
- Provide concrete kernel/activation examples with computed Cσ and resulting norm bounds.
- Discuss noise/conditioning in Lemma 2 and robustness of recovery.
- Clarify how to choose σk, Sk in practice and the trade-off between invariance and information loss.

Overall recommendation
This is a strong theoretical paper: technically sound, clearly written, and meaningfully extends prior work. Despite limited empirical validation and some restrictive assumptions, the contribution is significant for NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance.
