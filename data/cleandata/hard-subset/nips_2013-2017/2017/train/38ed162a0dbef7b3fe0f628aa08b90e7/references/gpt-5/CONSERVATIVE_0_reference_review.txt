Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that mirrors CNN architectures: each layer applies patch extraction, a norm-preserving kernel map into an RKHS, and Gaussian pooling. Within this kernelized setting, the authors analyze stability to diffeomorphisms, deriving Lipschitz-type bounds analogous to scattering transform results. They extend the construction to group-equivariant layers with pooling on general groups to induce global invariance. They discuss discretization (including subsampling and deconvolution issues), finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs), and show that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations can be embedded in the resulting RKHS, yielding norm-based control of Lipschitz stability and a conceptual link to generalization.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical framing that separates representation (RKHS features) from prediction and leverages kernel machinery to control Lipschitz stability.
- Extension of scattering-style stability analysis to a broader family that includes CKNs and certain CNNs; the commutator-based decomposition is natural and technically sound under stated assumptions.
- Group-equivariant formulation of patching and pooling is clean and connects to established results on Haar measures and equivariance.
- The connection showing inclusion of some CNNs in the RKHS with explicit (recursive) norm bounds is interesting and may help bridge empirical CNNs and kernel theory.
- The paper is generally well written and well situated within relevant literature.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical validation is essentially absent. Claims about practical relevance (e.g., “stability may lead to improved sample complexity” or that norm preservation is less relevant) are not supported by experiments comparing to standard CNNs or scattering networks. This is a significant gap for NeurIPS.
- Key assumptions limit applicability to common practice: activation functions must be smooth and homogeneous and require normalization by local patch norms (nk(u)), which differs from standard ReLU-based CNNs. The paper mentions smoothed ReLU variants, but concrete instantiations and their empirical behavior are not demonstrated.
- The discretization and “signal preservation” claims rely on deconvolution of Gaussian pooling. Gaussian blur is not stably invertible on L2 (the inverse is unbounded); the statement that deconvolution is stable “when the scale is small enough” is not quantified. This undermines the practical message that information is preserved after pooling and subsampling.
- Constants and depth dependence: The stability bound grows linearly with depth via a (1+n) factor, but the constants C1, C2 are not instantiated for canonical settings, making it hard to assess tightness or practical impact. The analysis acknowledges that pooling reduces norms with depth, unlike scattering, but does not quantify the trade-off.
- The group-invariance construction assumes pooling over the entire group to obtain global invariance. For non-compact groups (e.g., translations), this is not directly realizable; the paper hints at last-layer pooling at large scale, but the precise conditions for “near-invariance” and error vs. scale are not made explicit.
- The link to generalization is qualitative (relying on RKHS norms) without sample complexity bounds tailored to this architecture and its approximations. Given the focus on stability/generalization, a concrete bound would strengthen the contribution.

Quality
The theoretical components build on established harmonic analysis and kernel methods and appear sound under the stated assumptions. However, several practically critical steps (discretization, deconvolution stability, finite-dimensional approximations) are treated at a high level without quantitative error bounds. The inclusion result for CNNs is interesting but constrained to a subset of activations and a nonstandard normalization.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with precise operator definitions and a helpful decomposition via commutators. Important proofs and kernel examples are deferred to appendices; some main-text statements (e.g., Lemma 1 and Lemma 2 claims about recovery) would benefit from more precise conditions in the main body.

Originality
The work extends scattering-style stability to a broader kernelized convolutional class and connects to CNNs via RKHS inclusion and norm control. The ingredients (Gaussian pooling, equivariance, kernel mappings, CKNs) are known, but their integration and some technical lemmas (e.g., commutator with shifted kernels, recursive norm bounds for CNNs) are novel.

Significance
Potentially valuable for theoretical understanding of deep convolutional representations. Without empirical validation or concrete sample complexity results, the immediate impact on practice is limited. The significance would increase with evidence that the framework predicts or improves performance/robustness in realistic settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide empirical evaluations: measure stability to diffeomorphisms and translations on standard datasets (e.g., MNIST, CIFAR), comparing CNNs, scattering, and CKNs; demonstrate how σn and depth affect invariance and accuracy.
- Quantify discretization/approximation: give explicit error bounds for finite-dimensional projections and for deconvolution under noise; clarify conditions under which recovery is stable.
- Instantiate constants for canonical settings (2D images, typical σk schedules, patch sizes) to assess the magnitude of bounds.
- Clarify the class of admissible activations with examples, and evaluate a smoothed-ReLU variant empirically.
- For group invariance, distinguish compact vs. non-compact groups and provide precise near-invariance statements for practical pooling windows.

Pros
- Solid theoretical analysis extending scattering ideas to kernelized convolutional representations.
- Clean group-equivariant formulation.
- Interesting bridge between CNNs and RKHS with explicit norm control.

Cons
- No experimental validation.
- Restrictive assumptions on activations and normalization.
- Questionable and under-quantified claims about information preservation after pooling/subsampling.
- Uninstantiated constants and limited guidance for practice.

Recommendation
Lean reject in current form. The theory is interesting but, given the emphasis on practical deep models, the lack of empirical validation and the reliance on nonstandard assumptions for CNNs make it premature for NeurIPS. Strengthening with experiments and quantitative approximation/discretization analyses could change this assessment.
