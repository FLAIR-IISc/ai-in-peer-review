## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs), specifically their invariance and stability, through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors construct a multi-layer convolutional kernel and demonstrate that it encompasses a broad class of CNNs, enabling them to derive theoretical guarantees for invariance to diffeomorphisms and stability to deformations, mirroring results from scattering transforms but for a wider range of architectures.  They establish a connection between stability (controlled by the RKHS norm) and generalization performance.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and the theory of scattering transforms. It extends the analysis of CKNs to more general CNN architectures and places these within a kernel framework. The paper appropriately acknowledges and cites relevant prior work in kernel methods, CNN invariance, and harmonic analysis. It positions itself as a bridge between theoretical kernel analysis and the empirical success of deep learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical treatment of invariance and stability, using tools from kernel theory and harmonic analysis. The proofs, while relegated to the appendix, appear sound.
* **Generality:** The framework encompasses both CKNs and a wide class of standard CNNs, broadening the applicability of the theoretical results.
* **Connection to Generalization:** Establishing a link between stability (RKHS norm) and generalization potential is a significant contribution.  This provides a theoretical justification for regularization techniques that promote stable representations.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of understanding the geometry of the functional spaces underlying deep networks.
* **Well-structured and Written:**  The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Results:** While the extension of scattering transform-like stability guarantees to more general CNNs is valuable, the core *results* – stability to diffeomorphisms and translation invariance – are conceptually similar to those already established for scattering transforms. The paper doesn’t drastically alter the landscape of theoretical understanding.
* **Practical Implications Not Fully Explored:** While the link to generalization is established, the paper doesn’t provide concrete guidance on *how* to leverage these theoretical insights to improve CNN training or architecture design. The discussion of how to apply the theory to guide network selection or regularization is somewhat superficial.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of smooth homogeneous activation functions, while allowing for theoretical tractability, restricts the applicability to a specific class of CNNs.  The discussion of how the results extend to ReLU-like activations is promising but could be more detailed.
* **Complexity:** The mathematical formalism is dense and may be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis. While the authors strive for clarity, the sheer amount of mathematical detail can be overwhelming.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** 8/10.  The paper is technically sound and the claims are generally well-supported. However, the lack of truly novel results somewhat lowers the score.
* **Clarity:** 7/10. The writing is generally clear, but the mathematical complexity makes it a challenging read. More intuitive explanations of the key concepts would be beneficial.
* **Originality:** 6/10. The work extends existing theory but doesn't introduce fundamentally new ideas. The main originality lies in the framework unifying CKNs and CNNs.
* **Significance:** 7/10.  The theoretical insights provided are valuable and could potentially influence future research on CNN regularization and generalization.  However, the lack of immediate practical implications limits the overall significance.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Rigorous theoretical analysis of CNN properties.
* Broad applicability to a large class of architectures.
* Connection between stability and generalization.
* Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Against:**

* Limited novelty compared to existing work on scattering transforms.
* Lack of concrete guidance for practical applications.
* Mathematical complexity might hinder accessibility.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

This is a solid theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of CNNs. While the results are not groundbreaking, the rigorous analysis, broad applicability, and connection to generalization are significant. I recommend acceptance, contingent on minor revisions to:

1.  **Expand the discussion of practical implications:** Provide more concrete examples of how the theoretical results can be used to guide CNN design and training.
2.  **Provide a more detailed discussion on the applicability of results to ReLU and other commonly used activations.**
3.  **Consider adding a short section or table summarizing the key assumptions and their impact on the generality of the results.**
