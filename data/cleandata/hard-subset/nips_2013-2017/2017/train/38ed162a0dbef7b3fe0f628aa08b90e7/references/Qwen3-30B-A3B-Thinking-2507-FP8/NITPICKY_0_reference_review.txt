

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper studies the theoretical properties of convolutional architectures using positive definite kernels, extending the scattering transform framework to more standard CNNs. The authors propose a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and provides stability guarantees to diffeomorphisms. The main contribution is showing how this kernel framework connects to CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, allowing theoretical results to apply to both CKNs and standard CNNs.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a theoretically interesting connection between kernel methods and CNNs, providing a formal framework for understanding invariance and stability properties. The stability results in Theorem 6 are well-motivated and extend previous work on scattering networks. The paper's mathematical development is generally rigorous, and the connection to practical CNN architectures is valuable.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the significant theoretical contribution, the paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies and minor flaws that undermine its credibility and clarity:

1. **Formatting and Notation Inconsistencies:** The paper states it "drops norm subscripts" but then inconsistently uses "‖ · ‖" for Hilbert space norms, L² norms, and L²→L² operator norms. In equation (1), it writes "‖f‖H‖ϕ(z)− ϕ(z ′)‖H" but later uses "‖ · ‖" without subscripts, contradicting the stated notation.

2. **Technical Errors in Formulation:** Proposition 3 contains a notational error: "n ∑ k=1" should be "∑_{k=1}^n". This is a basic mathematical error that would confuse readers. The paper also uses "Lτ" for general diffeomorphisms but "Lc" for translations without clear explanation of the distinction.

3. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper claims "the scattering transform [5, 17] is a recent attempt" but the scattering transform was introduced in 2013, making "recent" inaccurate for a 2017 paper.

4. **Critical Omissions:** The paper states "the resulting architecture differs significantly from the most used ones" but never specifies what "most used ones" are (e.g., ResNet, VGG, etc.) or how they differ. This lack of specificity weakens the paper's relevance to practical deep learning.

5. **Reference Issues:** The Related Work section cites "Group invariance and stability to deformations of deep convolutional representations, A. Bietti, J. Mairal, preprint arXiv:1706.03078" which appears to be the same paper under review, creating a serious reference error.

6. **Minor but Significant Typographical Errors:** 
   - "st Conference" should be "The Conference" or "NIPS"
   - ANR grant number "ANR14-CE23-0003-01" should be "ANR-14-CE23-0003-01"
   - The reference list contains a trailing comma in "Information and Inference, 5(2):134–158,,"

7. **Lack of Concrete Connection to Standard CNNs:** While the paper claims to connect to "a large class of CNNs," it fails to provide a specific example of a commonly used CNN architecture (e.g., ResNet, VGG) that falls within their framework, making the connection feel abstract.

**Significance Assessment:** The theoretical connection between kernel methods and CNNs is interesting, but the paper's failure to clearly articulate how this applies to practical, modern CNN architectures limits its significance. The minor technical errors and inconsistencies significantly undermine the paper's credibility.

**Quality Assessment:** The theoretical development is generally sound but marred by numerous technical and formatting errors that suggest insufficient proofreading. The paper's claims about the relationship to standard CNNs lack concrete examples, and the reference to "most used ones" is vague.

**Clarity Assessment:** The paper is generally well-organized but suffers from inconsistent notation and minor technical errors that would confuse readers. The connection to practical CNNs is not clearly articulated.

**Originality Assessment:** The paper extends previous work on scattering networks to a kernel framework, which is a reasonable contribution. However, the lack of specific examples of standard CNNs within their framework limits the novelty.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important theoretical question, the numerous minor technical errors and inconsistencies (particularly the reference error and notational inconsistencies) significantly undermine its quality. The paper would benefit from substantial revision to address these issues before being considered for publication. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot overlook these errors, which are particularly concerning in a theoretical paper where precision is paramount.

The paper's contribution is potentially valuable, but the current version's technical shortcomings make it difficult to evaluate the theoretical claims with confidence. The authors should address these issues before resubmission.
