This paper tackles a fundamental question regarding the geometry of functional spaces inhabited by deep convolutional neural networks – specifically, their invariance and stability properties. The authors leverage the kernel framework, extending convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to a more general setting and aiming to provide theoretical guarantees analogous to those established for scattering transforms, but for a broader class of CNN architectures. This is a bold and ambitious undertaking, venturing into the largely uncharted territory of formally characterizing the behavior of these complex models.

The core idea of framing CNNs through a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) and analyzing their stability to diffeomorphisms is compelling. Establishing connections to both CKNs and the scattering transform provides valuable context, and the effort to extend existing theoretical results to more standard CNN architectures is commendable. The key strength lies in the attempt to move beyond purely empirical observations and towards a more principled understanding of *why* CNNs work, potentially informing regularization strategies and improving sample complexity. The authors demonstrate a strong grasp of the relevant mathematical tools (kernel theory, harmonic analysis) and present a rigorous, albeit dense, analysis.

However, the paper suffers from a degree of technical complexity that hinders accessibility. While rigor is appreciated, the presentation can be opaque, making it difficult to grasp the key insights without significant effort. The link between the theoretical analysis and practical implications for CNN training and architecture design is somewhat tenuous. The discussion of how the derived stability results translate to improved sample complexity, while mentioned, remains largely conceptual and lacks concrete examples or empirical validation. 

A significant weakness is the lack of empirical evaluation. While the authors acknowledge the need for discretization and approximation in practical implementations, they do not present any experimental results demonstrating the benefits of their theoretical framework. Showing, for instance, that CNNs within their defined RKHS exhibit improved robustness to perturbations or require fewer training samples would substantially strengthen the paper. The connection between the derived bounds and observed performance in real-world tasks needs to be demonstrated. The analysis heavily relies on assumptions about smoothness and homogeneity of activations, which may not hold for all practical CNN architectures. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The kernel-based approach to analyzing CNN stability and invariance is a genuinely novel contribution. It offers a fresh perspective on understanding the underlying principles of deep learning.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a mathematically sound analysis, building upon established results from kernel theory and harmonic analysis.
*   **Potential Significance:** A deeper theoretical understanding of CNNs could lead to improved regularization techniques, better generalization, and more efficient training algorithms.
*   **Bold Approach:** The authors tackle a challenging and fundamental problem, attempting to bridge the gap between theory and practice in deep learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of experimental results weakens the impact of the theoretical findings.
*   **Technical Complexity:** The paper is dense and requires a strong mathematical background to fully comprehend.
*   **Limited Practical Guidance:** The connection to practical CNN design and training is not fully established. The theoretical results, while interesting, do not immediately translate into actionable insights for practitioners.
*   **Strong Assumptions:** The reliance on assumptions about activation functions and data distributions limits the generality of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its technical complexity and lack of empirical validation, I believe this paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field. The attempt to rigorously analyze the stability and invariance properties of CNNs using a kernel framework is commendable, and the potential implications for understanding and improving these models are substantial. I lean towards **acceptance**, recognizing that the paper's strengths (novelty, theoretical rigor) outweigh its weaknesses (lack of empirical validation, technical complexity). I encourage the authors to consider adding experimental results in future work to solidify their claims and demonstrate the practical relevance of their theoretical framework. A more accessible presentation of the core ideas would also broaden the impact of this important research.
