**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors' main contribution is to extend the stability analysis pioneered for scattering transforms [17] to a broader class of architectures, including Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) [15, 16] and certain types of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).

The core of the work is the construction of a multilayer convolutional kernel for signals defined on a continuous spatial domain. This construction iteratively builds a sequence of feature maps, where each layer is formed by three operations: (1) a patch extraction operator, (2) a non-linear mapping of patches into a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) using a homogeneous dot-product kernel, and (3) a linear pooling operator (convolution with a Gaussian kernel) to introduce local invariance.

Using this framework, the authors derive several key theoretical results. First, they prove that the resulting representation is stable with respect to the action of small C1-diffeomorphisms, providing a Lipschitz-like bound on the representation's change that depends on the norm of the diffeomorphism and its gradient. This result is analogous to that for scattering networks. Second, this stability bound implies that the representation is nearly invariant to translations, with the degree of invariance controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer, which typically grows with network depth. Third, the authors show how this framework can be generalized to achieve equivariance and invariance to the action of arbitrary compact groups by defining appropriate patch and pooling operators on the group manifold. Finally, and perhaps most significantly, they establish a formal link to standard CNNs by showing that the RKHS associated with their kernel construction contains CNNs that use specific smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This allows the stability guarantees derived for the kernel representation to be applied directly to this class of CNNs, connecting stability properties to the RKHS norm of the predictive function, which in turn relates to generalization.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development is rigorous, building upon established techniques from harmonic analysis and the framework of the scattering transform [17]. The authors carefully define their multilayer kernel construction on continuous domains, which is appropriate for theoretical analysis, and then connect it to practical, discrete implementations. The proofs outlined in the main text and detailed in the appendix appear correct. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis provided.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work within the context of prior art, particularly CKNs and scattering transforms. The step-by-step construction of the multilayer kernel in Section 2 is easy to follow. The authors do a good job of explaining the purpose and properties of each operator (patch extraction, kernel mapping, pooling). The main theoretical results in Section 3 are stated precisely, and the high-level proof strategy is well-communicated. The connection between the abstract kernel representation and practical CNNs in Section 5 is a crucial part of the paper and is explained clearly.

**Originality:** The work is original and makes a novel contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep learning. While the analytical tools and the form of the stability guarantee are heavily inspired by Mallat's work on scattering transforms [17], the key originality lies in successfully applying and extending this analysis to a different and broader class of models that includes learned filters (via the kernel/CKN framework) and standard CNNs. The demonstration that a class of CNNs is contained within the function space of the constructed RKHS (Proposition 7) is a significant and novel result. This bridges the gap between the analysis of handcrafted, fixed-filter networks (like scattering) and the more common learned-filter architectures.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant for the field. They provide a principled theoretical framework for understanding why deep convolutional architectures are robust to small deformations of their input, a crucial property for tasks like object recognition. By linking the stability of a CNN's predictions to its RKHS norm (via Eq. 1 and Proposition 7), the work offers a concrete connection between stability, regularization (controlling the norm), and generalization. This provides a theoretical justification for the intuition that controlling model complexity can lead to more robust predictors. Researchers working on the theory of deep learning, group equivariant models, and kernel methods are likely to build upon these ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Connection to Standard CNN Practice:** The link to CNNs in Section 5, while a key strength, relies on a specific model formulation (Eq. 8) that includes a normalization term `nk(u) = ||Pkzk-1(u)||`. This normalization by the patch norm is not a standard component of most widely used CNN architectures (e.g., ResNet, VGG). While the authors correctly point out that the ReLU activation is homogeneous, the explicit normalization factor is an important theoretical requirement that creates a gap with common practice. A more detailed discussion on the implications of this mismatch would be beneficial.
2.  **Smoothness of Activation Functions:** The theory requires smooth activation functions for the RKHS inclusion result to hold. However, the most popular activation function, ReLU, is non-smooth. The authors propose using a smoothed variant, but it remains unclear how the theoretical guarantees change or degrade when using the non-smooth activations that are dominant in practice.
3.  **Restrictiveness of Assumptions:** The stability analysis relies on the assumption that `||∇τ||∞ < 1/2`, which limits the results to very small deformations. This is a standard assumption inherited from related work [17], but it is a limitation nonetheless.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a rigorous and technically sound theoretical analysis of stability and invariance in deep convolutional models.
*   Successfully extends the powerful analytical framework of scattering transforms to architectures with learned components, including CKNs and a class of CNNs.
*   The established link between the kernel framework and CNNs is a novel and significant contribution, providing new insights into regularization and generalization.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical model of a CNN used in the analysis deviates from standard practice due to the inclusion of an explicit patch-norm normalization and the requirement of smooth activations. This may limit the direct applicability of the results to off-the-shelf deep learning models.
*   The core analytical techniques are heavily borrowed from prior work on scattering transforms, somewhat reducing the technical novelty of the proofs themselves, though their application is novel.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of deep convolutional networks. The strengths, particularly the novel connection between the kernel stability analysis and CNNs, substantially outweigh the weaknesses, which primarily concern the gap between the theoretical model and practical implementations—a common feature of foundational theoretical work.
