**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors introduce a multi-layer convolutional kernel framework, extending the Convolutional Kernel Network (CKN) model, to define a representation of signals in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The core contribution is a formal proof that this representation is Lipschitz stable to the action of small C1-diffeomorphisms, a result analogous to that established for the scattering transform by Mallat. The stability bound shows that sensitivity to local deformations is controlled by the Jacobian of the diffeomorphism, while sensitivity to global translations decays with the scale of the final pooling layer, thus achieving near translation-invariance. A key second contribution is establishing a formal link between this kernel construction and standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). The authors demonstrate that CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions reside within the RKHS defined by their kernel, allowing the stability guarantees to be transferred to this class of learnable models. The RKHS norm then provides a principled way to control the stability of the final prediction function, connecting stability to generalization.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** This is a strong theoretical paper that provides valuable insights into why deep convolutional architectures work. It successfully bridges the gap between the well-understood but non-adaptive scattering transform and learnable, practical CNNs. Extending the stability analysis from fixed-filter networks to a framework encompassing learnable models is a significant and non-trivial step.

2.  **Elegant and Powerful Framework:** The use of a multi-layer convolutional kernel and the associated RKHS is an elegant way to analyze the geometry of the function space. It allows the authors to decouple the properties of the data representation (stability of the feature map Φ(x)) from the properties of the predictive model (controlled by the norm of the weight vector `w`). The fundamental inequality `|f(z)− f(z′)| ≤ ‖f‖H‖ϕ(z)− ϕ(z ′)‖H` is leveraged effectively to connect representational stability to predictor stability.

3.  **Clear Connection to Related Work:** The paper is well-situated within the existing literature, clearly building upon the foundational work of Mallat on scattering transforms and Mairal et al. on CKNs. The authors do an excellent job of adapting the proof techniques from harmonic analysis used for scattering networks to their more general kernel setting.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. The construction of the multi-layer kernel is presented methodically, followed by the main stability analysis, and finally the crucial connection to CNNs. The theoretical assumptions are stated clearly, and the main results are presented in an understandable manner.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **The "Homogenization" Gap with Practical CNNs:** The most significant weakness lies in the assumptions required to embed CNNs into the RKHS. The formulation in Equation (8) requires a non-standard normalization `nk(u) = ‖Pkzk–1(u)‖` and a smooth, homogeneous activation function `σ`.
    *   The normalization `nk(u)` acts as a form of local response normalization, which is not a standard component of modern architectures like ResNets or VGG networks (the latter being cited). How critical is this term to the analysis? Does its absence break the connection to the RKHS, or does it merely complicate the norm bound?
    *   The most prevalent activation, ReLU, is homogeneous but not smooth. The authors propose smooth variants, but this creates a disconnect from what is used in practice. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the implications of using the non-smooth ReLU. Could the analysis be extended using tools for non-smooth functions, or is smoothness a fundamental requirement of the RKHS framework as presented?

2.  **Signal Preservation and Norm Decay:** The authors state in Section 3 that their representation does not preserve the norm, unlike the scattering transform, due to the pooling operators. While they argue this may be a "less relevant concern," it seems important. Pooling explicitly discards high-frequency information. This means that with increasing depth, `‖Φn(x0)‖` can decrease, potentially to zero. This could diminish the utility of the stability bound `‖Φ(Lτx)− Φ(x)‖ ≤ C‖x‖`, as `‖Φ(x)‖` itself could be vanishingly small. Could the authors comment on how the signal energy is preserved through the layers for signals that are not strictly low-pass? Does the analysis hold for architectures without pooling (e.g., using strided convolutions), which are also common?

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** While this is a theoretical work, a small, targeted experiment could have significantly bolstered its impact. For instance, training two identical CNNs—one with a standard ReLU and one with the proposed smooth homogeneous activation—and empirically measuring their stability to deformations would provide a concrete validation of the theory. Similarly, tracking the RKHS norm bound of a trained model (as derived in Proposition 7) and correlating it with its observed generalization or robustness would be highly compelling.

4.  **Scope of Deformations:** The analysis relies on the condition `‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2`, inherited from Mallat's work. This restricts the analysis to relatively small, non-compressive deformations. While this is a standard starting point, it's worth acknowledging this limitation more explicitly in the main text.

**Detailed Comments**

*   In Section 2.1, the discussion of discretization is helpful. However, Lemma 2 states that `x̄k–1` can be recovered from `x̄k` if `ek ≥ sk`. This recovery depends on deconvolution, which can be numerically unstable. The authors mention this briefly, but a more explicit statement about the conditioning of this recovery process would be useful.
*   The extension to general group actions in Section 4 is a nice addition. It elegantly shows the generality of the patch-extraction/pooling paradigm for building equivariant representations.

**Overall Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a substantial contribution to our theoretical understanding of deep convolutional networks. It provides a rigorous and insightful analysis of stability and invariance, successfully connecting the theory of scattering networks and kernel methods to learnable CNN models. Despite the gap between the theoretical model and some practical CNN architectures (particularly regarding the activation function and normalization), the framework and the results are highly significant. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a fundamental question in the field. I strongly recommend acceptance. The weaknesses identified are primarily avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws.
