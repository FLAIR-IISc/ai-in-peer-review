## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate that it encompasses a broad class of CNNs, including convolutional kernel networks (CKNs). They then analyze the translation invariance and stability of this kernel representation to diffeomorphic deformations, obtaining guarantees comparable to those of the scattering transform, while avoiding the limitations of pre-defined filters.  Crucially, they link these stability properties to potential improvements in generalization performance via RKHS norm control.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon the foundations of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and the scattering transform. It distinguishes itself from CKNs by providing a more general framework applicable to standard CNN architectures. Compared to the scattering transform, it offers theoretical results for *learned* filters rather than fixed wavelets.  The paper appropriately acknowledges related work in kernel methods, group invariance, and adversarial robustness. 

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis using tools from kernel methods and harmonic analysis. The use of RKHS and diffeomorphic stability provides a powerful framework for understanding CNN behavior.
* **Bridging the Gap:**  The work effectively bridges the gap between the theoretical guarantees of kernel methods (like stability and generalization bounds) and the practical success of deep learning.
* **Generality:** The framework is general enough to encompass a wide range of CNN architectures, making the results potentially applicable to many practical scenarios.
* **Clear Structure & Mathematical Rigor:** The paper is well-structured and the mathematical derivations appear sound (though a full verification is beyond the scope of this review). The authors provide a detailed notation section and promise proofs in the appendix.
* **Novelty:** Extending the theoretical analysis of stability and invariance, previously concentrated on scattering transforms and CKNs, to standard CNNs is a significant contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Implications:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the paper lacks a strong empirical validation. It would be beneficial to demonstrate these theoretical properties (e.g., improved stability or generalization) on real-world datasets, even with simple experiments.
* **Complexity:** The mathematical formalism is dense and may be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis.  More intuitive explanations or examples might improve accessibility.
* **Assumptions:**  The assumptions required for the stability results (e.g. smoothness of activations, regularity of deformations) are potentially restrictive. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of the impact of these assumptions would be helpful.
* **Limited Discussion of Computational Costs:**  While the paper addresses discretization and approximation for practicality, it doesn't deeply explore the computational costs associated with these approximations, or whether the resulting methods are scalable to large datasets and complex architectures.



**Addressing Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and provides well-supported claims. The analysis seems complete within the defined framework. The authors are careful to state assumptions and limitations. **Strong.**
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-organized, but the mathematical notation and derivations can be dense. A more pedagogical approach with illustrative examples would enhance clarity. **Good.**
* **Originality:**  The work presents a novel combination of techniques – applying kernel methods to analyze the stability and invariance of learned CNN representations – and extends existing theoretical results to a broader class of models. **Strong.**
* **Significance:** The results have the potential to advance our understanding of why CNNs work and to guide the development of more robust and generalizable models. However, the lack of empirical validation limits the immediate impact. **Good.**

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Provides a strong theoretical foundation for understanding CNNs.
* Extends existing theoretical work to a more general class of models.
* Offers potential insights into improving generalization performance.
* Well-written and technically sound.

**Con:**

* Lacks empirical validation to support the theoretical claims.
* The mathematical complexity might limit accessibility.
*  Assumptions could be restrictive in practice.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong** contribution to the field. The theoretical results are valuable and the framework is well-motivated. While the lack of empirical validation is a significant limitation, the novelty and potential impact of the work warrant acceptance. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with the suggestion that the authors consider including some empirical results (even preliminary ones) to demonstrate the practical relevance of their findings.  Addressing the clarity issues with more intuitive explanations would also be beneficial.
