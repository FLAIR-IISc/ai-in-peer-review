## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to understanding the properties of deep convolutional neural networks by framing them within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) framework. It successfully extends results from scattering transforms to a broader class of CNNs and convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), establishing connections between stability, invariance, and generalization. While the theoretical development is strong, the practical implications and discussion of existing CNN architectures feel somewhat limited. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Summary:**

The authors investigate the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional networks through the lens of positive definite kernels. They construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate that it encompasses a wide class of CNNs.  A key result is that this kernel representation achieves similar stability guarantees to the scattering transform under diffeomorphic deformations, while retaining more information and allowing for learning.  Furthermore, they show that stability, captured by the RKHS norm, relates to generalization performance.  The paper builds upon previous work in CKNs, scattering transforms, and kernel methods, offering a unified perspective on these areas.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper’s mathematical development is rigorous and well-motivated. The use of RKHS theory provides a powerful framework for analyzing the properties of CNNs. The proofs appear sound (though a detailed examination is beyond the scope of this review).
* **Extension of Existing Theory:** Successfully extending the stability results from scattering transforms to a more general class of CNNs is a significant contribution. Scattering transforms, while theoretically elegant, are not easily adaptable to standard CNN architectures. This work bridges that gap.
* **Connection Between Stability and Generalization:** The explicit link between the RKHS norm and generalization is a valuable insight. This provides a theoretical grounding for the empirical observation that flatter minima often lead to better generalization.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough overview of relevant literature, including CKNs, scattering transforms, and various kernel-based approaches. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the field.
* **Clear Presentation (Mostly):** The core ideas are clearly explained, and the paper is well-structured. The notation is generally consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how these findings translate into practical guidelines for designing and training CNNs.  For example, how can the RKHS norm be estimated or regularized in practice?
* **Focus on Specific CNN Architectures:** The connection to *typical* modern CNN architectures (e.g., ResNets, DenseNets) isn't fully fleshed out. While they demonstrate their framework *contains* a certain class of CNNs,  a more concrete discussion of how these popular architectures fit into their theoretical framework would be beneficial.
* **Homogeneous Activations:** The reliance on homogeneous activation functions is a limitation.  While the authors acknowledge the non-smoothness of ReLU and discuss smoothed variants, the requirement limits the direct applicability of their results to many commonly used architectures. 
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn’t address the computational challenges associated with working with RKHS norms and kernel matrices in practice. Although CKNs offer approximation schemes, the cost remains significant.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** The explanation around Lemma 1 and the discretization process in Section 2.1 could be made clearer.  The connection between the continuous and discrete settings, while technically sound, isn't immediately intuitive.



**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

* **Section 3.1:**  The constant factors (C1, C2) in Theorem 6 are mentioned but not explicitly defined.  Providing more details about these constants and their dependence on various parameters would enhance the practical understanding of the stability bounds.
* **Section 4:** The discussion of group invariance is well-presented, but it could be strengthened by providing concrete examples of applications where this theoretical framework could be particularly useful (e.g., medical image analysis, computer graphics).
* **Section 5:** The treatment of ReLU and homogeneous activation functions feels a bit hand-wavy. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between smoothness, homogeneity, and practical performance would be valuable.
* **Appendix:** The appendices contain a significant amount of technical detail, which is commendable.  However, improving the organization and readability of the appendices would further enhance the paper.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for understanding the properties of CNNs.
* It successfully extends existing results from scattering transforms to a broader class of models.
* It establishes a clear connection between stability and generalization, providing a theoretical justification for common regularization techniques.
* The paper is well-written and thoroughly researched.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The practical implications of the theoretical results are not fully explored.
* The reliance on homogeneous activation functions limits the direct applicability of the framework.
* The computational complexity of working with RKHS norms and kernel matrices remains a concern.




**Overall, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep convolutional networks.  Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance.**
