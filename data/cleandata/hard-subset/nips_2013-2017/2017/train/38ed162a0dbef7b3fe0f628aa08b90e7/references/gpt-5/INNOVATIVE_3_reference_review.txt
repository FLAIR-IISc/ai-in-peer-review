Summary
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework on continuous domains that mirrors the structure of CNNs (patch extraction → kernel map in an RKHS → linear pooling), and studies its invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors prove a Lipschitz-type stability bound that separates the effect of local deformations (controlled by ‖∇τ‖∞ and depth) from global translations (controlled by ‖τ‖∞/σn, with σn the final pooling scale). They show how to discretize the construction without losing information (under mild conditions) and how finite-dimensional kernel approximations yield tractable convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) while preserving non-expansiveness. They also generalize the pooling to locally compact groups to obtain group-equivariant layers and global invariance through the final pooling stage. Finally, they establish that a nontrivial class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations is contained in the RKHS induced by their multilayer kernel, yielding norm-based control of stability and generalization.

Relation to prior work
This work sits between scattering transforms (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) and CKNs (Mairal et al.). It borrows the deformation-stability formalism from scattering, extends it to a broader class of learned, kernel-based convolutional architectures, and provides a functional analytic treatment via RKHSs that includes certain CNNs. The group equivariance part connects to G-CNNs (Cohen & Welling) and invariant kernels (Haasdonk & Burkhardt), while the RKHS perspective relates to kernel analyses of deep nets (Montavon et al.; Daniely et al.) and Poggio’s hierarchical kernel view. Compared to scattering, the novelty is to (i) provide stability guarantees for architectures that learn filters via kernel approximation; (ii) connect these guarantees to CNNs through an explicit RKHS inclusion with norm bounds; and (iii) show how invariance to general groups emerges via pooling on groups in this kernelized setting.

Quality
The theory is carefully developed, with clear operator-level definitions and assumptions (A1–A3). The main stability result (Theorem 6) follows from a commutator decomposition (Proposition 3), a new bound for shifted pooling commutators (Lemma 4), and a translation-invariance bound (Lemma 5). The discretization and recovery lemmas are well-motivated and consistent with practical sampling and anti-aliasing. The RKHS inclusion for CNNs with homogeneous activations is nontrivial and comes with explicit norm recursions (Proposition 7). Proofs are delegated to the appendix, which is acceptable in NIPS format.

There are some limitations: (1) stability constants and their dependence on κ and dimension are not quantified tightly; the depth dependence is linear in n; (2) the setup requires Gaussian pooling and kernels that satisfy norm-preserving/non-expansive properties; (3) the inclusion result for CNNs hinges on homogenized, smooth activations and local normalization nk(u), which is not standard in deployed CNNs and excludes vanilla ReLU; (4) empirical validation is minimal—there is no numerical illustration of the stability bounds or their behavior under discretization/projection; and (5) the claim of signal recovery after pooling hinges on stable deconvolution conditions that are realistic but deserve more discussion.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and readable given the mathematical content. The operator notation is consistent, and the continuous-to-discrete story is clear. Some parts could be streamlined (e.g., keeping all key constants in the main text; giving a concise table of kernel choices and the corresponding Cσ). The discussion of norm preservation vs. pooling loss is reasonable, though a small empirical plot would help.

Originality
The conceptual synthesis is the key novelty: a multilayer convolutional RKHS that both generalizes CKNs and provably contains a family of CNNs, with stability-to-diffeomorphisms results that parallel scattering but apply beyond fixed wavelets and include learned filters via kernel approximation. The group-equivariant pooling in RKHS form is also elegant, though related ideas exist in the literature. Overall, this is more than an incremental rephrasing; it opens a coherent functional-analytic route to reason about invariance and generalization of learned convolutional representations.

Significance
The work provides a principled lens to study invariance and stability of modern conv architectures with a clear path from continuous analysis to discrete implementations. It can inform architecture design (role of pooling scales, patch sizes, group pooling) and regularization (norm control as stability/generalization proxy). The RKHS embedding of CNNs with homogeneous activations is a promising bridge between kernel theory and deep nets. Despite limited experiments, the theoretical contribution is substantial and likely to influence future research on provable properties of learned convolutions.

Pros
- Conceptual unification of scattering, CKNs, and a class of CNNs via a multilayer convolutional kernel and RKHS.
- Nontrivial deformation-stability bound with a clean separation between local deformation and translation invariance, and explicit depth/scale trade-offs.
- Practical path from continuous theory to discrete, subsampled architectures; compatibility with kernel approximations used in CKNs.
- Group-equivariant formulation with global invariance via final pooling; connects elegantly to G-CNN ideas.
- RKHS inclusion and norm bounds for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, tying stability to generalization.

Cons
- Requires specific kernels and Gaussian pooling; constants and depth dependence not tightly quantified.
- CNN inclusion relies on homogenized, smoothed activations and local normalization that diverge from standard practice (e.g., ReLU without normalization).
- Minimal empirical validation; no experiments demonstrating stability under controlled deformations or the effect of projections/discretization on constants.
- Some assumptions (stable deconvolution for recovery; σ choices) are idealized and could be discussed more thoroughly.

Suggestions
- Provide example values/estimates of C1, C2 for common settings and discuss their scaling with κ, d, and depth.
- Add a small empirical study (even synthetic) validating the stability bound trends and translation invariance vs. σn.
- Clarify the practicality of the homogenized activation and normalization in CNNs; relate more directly to smooth ReLU approximations and how close they are in practice.
- Discuss how the projection quality in CKNs affects stability constants; give guidance on subspace dimension vs. stability.
- If space permits, sketch a generalization bound (e.g., via Rademacher complexity) that makes the norm–stability–sample complexity link more explicit.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant and timely theoretical framework that unifies and extends prior lines of work on invariance and stability in convolutional representations. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the lack of extensive experiments and some idealized assumptions.
