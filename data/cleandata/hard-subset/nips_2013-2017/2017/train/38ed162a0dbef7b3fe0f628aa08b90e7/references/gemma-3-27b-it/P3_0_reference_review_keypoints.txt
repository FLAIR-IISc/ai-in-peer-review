- Summary: Paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel generalizing CKNs to analyze CNN translation invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms, linking stability to generalization via RKHS norms.

- Strength: Strong theoretical foundation using positive definite kernels and harmonic analysis to derive stability and invariance guarantees.

- Strength: Successfully generalizes scattering-transform theory to a broader class of CNN architectures beyond wavelet-based models.

- Strength: Identifies a compelling connection between stability (RKHS norm) and sample complexity, suggesting principled regularization.

- Strength: Provides detailed mathematical analysis with rigorous proofs and helpful appendix material.

- Strength: Addresses practical discretization and kernel approximation issues and relates framework to implementable CKNs.

- Weakness: Relies on simplifying assumptions (smooth activations, regular diffeomorphisms with ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2) that limit applicability to real CNNs.

- Weakness: Homogenization step to cover standard CNNs introduces an artificial element not typical in practical architectures.

- Weakness: Computational complexity and scalability concerns remain; no concrete efficient algorithms beyond CKN approximations.

- Weakness: Limited empirical validation; lacks experiments on benchmarks to demonstrate practical benefits.

- Weakness: Framework does not inherently preserve norms in intermediate layers, which may affect performance.

- Weakness: Substantial overlap with scattering transform literature; unique contributions need clearer delineation.

- Originality: Novel kernel-based perspective combining kernel methods and harmonic analysis to extend CNN theory and link to sample complexity.

- Significance: Potential to inform design and regularization of more robust CNNs by connecting stability and generalization.

- Arguments for acceptance: Solid theoretical foundation, generalization beyond scattering transforms, link between stability and sample complexity, rigorous proofs.

- Arguments against acceptance: Strong assumptions, lack of empirical validation, computational scalability issues, overlap with existing scattering work.

- Overall assessment: Technically strong and well-written theoretical contribution with meaningful insights, despite limitations.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

- Requested revisions: More thorough discussion of limitations and possible relaxations, add limited experiments to demonstrate practicality, and more clearly articulate novelty versus scattering transforms.
