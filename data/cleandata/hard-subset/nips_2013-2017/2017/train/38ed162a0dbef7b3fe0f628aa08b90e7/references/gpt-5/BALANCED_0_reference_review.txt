Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework for signals on continuous domains and studies its invariance and stability properties under translations and diffeomorphisms. The construction mirrors convolutional neural networks (CNNs): at each layer, patches are extracted, mapped via a homogeneous dot-product kernel into an RKHS, and pooled with a Gaussian. The resulting representation Φn induces an RKHS HKn. The paper proves Lipschitz stability to C1-diffeomorphisms with constants that (i) scale linearly with the number of layers via commutator bounds and (ii) improve translation invariance with the final pooling scale σn. It further shows how to obtain global invariance to general transformation groups by making patch extraction and pooling equivariant to the group action. Importantly, the authors connect this kernel RKHS to a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (smoothed ReLU-like), giving explicit RKHS norm bounds (via a recursive constant Cσ) for such CNNs, thereby transferring the stability results to these CNNs. The paper also discusses discretization, kernel approximation via projections (CKNs), and signal preservation under mild conditions.

The work is positioned between Mallat’s scattering transform (Bruna & Mallat, 2013; Mallat, 2012), which provides norm-preserving and provably stable representations with fixed wavelet filters, and convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) (Mairal et al., 2014; 2016), which approximate multilayer kernels with learned finite-dimensional subspaces. It leverages harmonic analysis techniques similar to scattering (commutators, Schur’s test) but generalizes to a broader class of homogeneous dot-product kernels and to CNNs with learned filters. It also relates to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling, 2016) by formalizing group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling, and to kernel-theoretic perspectives on deep nets (Daniely et al., 2016; Montavon et al., 2011; Anselmi et al., 2015).

Strengths:
- Conceptual unification: Provides a clear kernel-based formalism that subsumes scattering, CKNs, and a nontrivial class of CNNs with homogeneous activations.
- Rigorous stability analysis: Proves Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms with explicit dependence on ∥∇τ∥∞ and ∥τ∥∞, and shows translation invariance grows with σn.
- Group invariance: Clean construction of G-equivariant layers and characterization of when to pool over subgroups vs. the full group for desired stability/invariance.
- Link to generalization: The RKHS norm simultaneously controls stability and (via standard margin arguments) generalization, offering an interpretable regularization perspective for CNNs/CKNs.
- Practical alignment: Addresses discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations; shows stability is preserved under projection (non-expansive maps), aligning with implementable CKNs.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Assumptions on operators and activations: Key results rely on (A1–A3), in particular norm-preserving, non-expansive homogeneous kernel maps and Gaussian pooling. This excludes common choices like max-pooling or exact ReLU (nonsmooth), and the homogenization term nk(u) departs from standard CNN practice. While smoothed ReLU variants are discussed, practical implications of this departure could be elaborated.
- Depth dependence: The stability bound has an O(n) factor via a sum of commutators. It would be useful to clarify whether tighter depth dependence is possible under additional structural assumptions (e.g., scales/patch sizes increasing geometrically), or to quantify constants more explicitly (dependence on κ, dimension d, and kernel h).
- Discretization and recovery: Signal preservation in the discrete setting uses deconvolution of Gaussian pooling and ek ≥ sk. While mathematically valid, deconvolution can be ill-conditioned in practice; some quantitative stability/noise sensitivity discussion would strengthen the bridge to applications.
- Empirical validation: The paper is theory-focused. Small numerical illustrations (e.g., measuring empirical Lipschitz constants vs. σn and n for CKNs/CNNs under smooth deformations, or correlating RKHS norm bounds with generalization) would help substantiate the practical relevance of constants and assumptions.
- Scope of group invariance: The group-invariance section is elegant but high level. Concrete instantiations (e.g., roto-translation with specific choices of S and h, computational costs) would aid adoption and comparison to group-equivariant CNNs.

Assessment by criteria:
- Quality: Theorems are technically sound and build carefully on established harmonic analysis arguments. Assumptions are stated, and proofs are (reportedly) complete in the appendix. Some constants and robustness aspects could be made more explicit.
- Clarity: The manuscript is well-organized and readable, with helpful pointers to appendices. A few areas could benefit from added intuition: (i) the role and magnitude of κ in (A3), (ii) examples of kernels satisfying (A1–A2) with explicit Cσ, and (iii) a more transparent comparison to scattering (e.g., norm preservation vs. final prediction layer trick).
- Originality: Extends scattering-style stability to a broader, learnable kernelized multilayer setting and establishes a concrete RKHS that contains a family of CNNs with norm bounds. This synthesis appears novel and nontrivial.
- Significance: Provides theoretical tools that can inform architecture/regularization design and connects invariance/stability to generalization via RKHS norms. While immediate practical impact may be moderate, the conceptual contribution is valuable to NeurIPS.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical advance generalizing scattering guarantees to kernel/CNN settings.
- Clear, modular construction with practical implications for CKNs and group invariance.
- Bridges stability and generalization through explicit RKHS norms for CNNs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Relies on specific pooling and smooth homogeneous activations; limited coverage of common choices like max-pooling and ReLU.
- O(n) depth factor and constants are not tightly characterized; limited empirical support.
- Discretization/recovery arguments may be fragile under noise and finite support.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide explicit examples of kernels and activations with computed Cσ (e.g., inverse polynomial kernel), and discuss how close smoothed ReLU must be to ReLU for the bounds to remain meaningful.
- Clarify the dependence of C1 on κ and d, and discuss whether choosing σk and |Sk| to grow geometrically can reduce the effective depth factor or yield path-length style bounds akin to scattering.
- Discuss robustness of the discrete deconvolution step to noise and boundary effects; include a short empirical test showing approximate recovery and stability for finite support signals.
- Add a small experiment quantifying empirical Lipschitz stability of CKN/CNN features under synthetic diffeomorphisms as σn varies, and relating RKHS norm bounds to validation accuracy.
- Compare and connect to norm-based capacity control for neural networks (e.g., spectral/Path norms, margin bounds) to situate the proposed RKHS norm control within broader generalization theory.
- Comment on extending the analysis to other pooling (average, strided convolutions) and to practical ReLU via approximation or limiting arguments.

Recommendation: Accept
This is a well-executed theoretical paper that meaningfully generalizes scattering stability to a kernel/CNN framework and provides a principled link between invariance, stability, and generalization. Despite limited experiments and some restrictive assumptions, the contribution is sufficiently original and valuable for the NeurIPS community.
