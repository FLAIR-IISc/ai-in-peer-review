- Paper constructs a continuous-domain multilayer convolutional kernel representation from patch extraction, kernel mapping, and Gaussian pooling.  
- Proves stability to C1-diffeomorphisms (Lipschitz in ∥∇τ∥∞ and ∥τ∥∞) with translation invariance controlled by final pooling scale σn.  
- Discusses discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs) and shows stability preserved under non-expansive projections.  
- Extends construction to general transformation groups via group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling.  
- Shows CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and linear pooling lie in the RKHS of the multilayer kernel, giving norm-based control of stability and generalization.  
- Core stability result (Theorem 6) is carefully derived using commutator decomposition and integral-operator bounds (Schur’s test).  
- Assumptions A1–A3 (homogeneous dot-product kernels, patch diameter scaling) are reasonable and align with CNN design heuristics.  
- Authors acknowledge pooling is not norm-preserving and stability holds only for small deformations (∥∇τ∥∞ < 1/2).  
- Discretization analysis includes signal recoverability under mild conditions (ek ≥ sk) but notes practical stability constraints.  
- Proposition 7 bridges CNNs and RKHS, providing recursive norm bounds consistent with prior kernel-CNN theory.  
- Proofs are relegated to the appendix; the main text is precise but lacks empirical validation.  
- One technical caveat: stability bound accumulates linearly in depth (factor 1 + n), and tightness or mitigation strategies are not fully explored.  
- Manuscript is well-organized with clear notation; continuous-to-discrete discussion usefully connects to CKN practice.  
- Suggest adding concrete examples/tables of admissible activations and Cσ values, and discussing smoothed ReLU trade-offs.  
- Recommend providing intuition for growth of constants with κ and dimension and for the linear-in-depth factor.  
- Suggest including a short illustrative experiment or toy example to visualize invariance vs. σn and stability behavior.  
- Novelty: extends scattering-style stability to broader multilayer convolutional kernels and connects stability to RKHS norms for a class of CNNs.  
- Significance: advances theoretical understanding of invariance/stability and offers a principled route to control stability and generalization via RKHS norms.  
- Pros: clear theoretical framework, generalizes scattering, operator-theoretic analysis, RKHS inclusion for CNNs, and group-equivariant extension.  
- Cons: requires smooth homogeneous activations (indirect for ReLU), linear depth dependence, potentially large constants in high dimensions, discrete recovery assumptions, and no empirical validation.  
- Key questions: can the (1 + n) factor be improved or shown necessary; sensitivity to non-Gaussian pooling; degradation from finite-dimensional projections; explicit activations with finite Cσ; and empirical illustration of σn vs. invariance and margin–stability link.  
- Overall: solid, timely theoretical contribution that broadens scattering results to CKNs/CNNs and merits acceptance with suggested clarifications and small illustrative experiments.
