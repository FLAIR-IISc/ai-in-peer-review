Summary
The paper studies invariance and stability of deep convolutional representations via a multilayer convolutional kernel on continuous domains. It generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) by defining a sequence of patch extraction, kernel mappings with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling operators. The main theoretical results establish Lipschitz stability of the representation to diffeomorphisms with constants that scale linearly in depth and inversely with the final pooling scale, paralleling scattering transform guarantees while allowing a broader class of kernels. The framework is extended to group-equivariant layers and global invariance via group pooling. Finally, the authors show that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, yielding norm-based control of stability and a link to generalization.

Relation to prior work: The work stands on two lines: scattering transforms (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) for stability/invariance proofs and CKNs (Mairal et al.) for practical kernel approximations in deep convolutional architectures. It also connects to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling), invariant kernels (Haasdonk & Burkhardt), and the kernel analyses of deep networks (Montavon et al., Daniely et al.). The novelty is to provide a unified RKHS-based formalization that both recovers scattering-like stability results and contains a class of CNNs.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual bridge between scattering, kernels, and CNNs; proof techniques are adapted and extended (e.g., commutators with shifted pooling [LcAσ]).
- Stability bound to diffeomorphisms with explicit dependencies on ∥∇τ∥∞, ∥τ∥∞, depth n, and last pooling scale σn (Theorem 6), matching desired behavior (increasing invariance with increasing σn).
- Group-equivariant construction is clean and general, with a practical recipe (pool over subgroups in intermediate layers, whole group at the end).
- The RKHS containment result for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and a recursive norm bound (Proposition 7) is interesting and may inform regularization and margin-based generalization.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Lemma 1 (signal preservation) is informal/optimistic: “xk−1 can be recovered from a sampling of xk = AkMkPkxk−1” requires (i) invertibility of Mk on its image (which holds only in the abstract, infinite-dimensional RKHS setting and requires access to xk(u) as an element, not just inner products), and (ii) stable deconvolution of Ak. The “union of patches covers Ω” condition is insufficient by itself; please state precise conditions for the pooling kernel (e.g., non-vanishing Fourier transform and bandwidth relative to sampling density) and stability constants.
- Discretization (Sec. 2.1): The e−1/2_k factor in P̄k is introduced without justification; please prove isometry to the continuous Pk under the chosen measures and sampling rates. The s1/2_k factor in Āk also deserves a derivation to ensure norm scaling matches the continuous L2 setting. Boundary conditions (finite support images) are acknowledged but not analyzed; some stability constants depend on boundary handling.
- Assumptions (A1–A2): While Appendix B is referenced, the main text should give at least one explicit kernel verifying both norm preservation and non-expansiveness (e.g., inverse polynomial, exponential dot-product) and indicate the exact Lipschitz constants of ϕk. Non-expansiveness of ϕk does not follow from homogeneity alone.
- Proposition 3: The telescoping bound excludes Mk terms by appealing to pointwise commutation with Lτ, but the argument is only sketched. Please provide a precise derivation (including measurability and Fubini/Tonelli hypotheses) in the main text or clarify the conditions in the appendix.
- Theorem 6: The (1+n) scaling is potentially pessimistic; can the dependence be tightened (e.g., replacing n by the number of layers with increasing receptive field) or by summing geometric contributions when σk grows? Also, constants C1, C2 should be stated explicitly in terms of kernel smoothness and dimensionality; currently they are opaque.
- CKN approximation (Sec. 3.2): The claim that “conclusions of Theorem 6 remain valid” after projecting onto finite-dimensional subspaces needs an explicit error term that accumulates across layers; non-expansiveness preserves stability inequalities but approximation errors compound. A bound of the form ∥Φapprox(Lτx)−Φapprox(x)∥ ≤ (stability term)+Σk εk would make this rigorous.
- CNN containment (Sec. 5): The homogenization nk(u)=∥Pkzk−1(u)∥ is nonstandard and can be zero; how is division handled when the norm vanishes? The paper mentions “smoothed ReLU,” but concrete σ and corresponding Cσ(a) for a given kernel are not provided. An explicit example with computed bounds would strengthen the result.

Clarity
- Notational overload: Using ‖·‖ for Hilbert, L2, and operator norms is error-prone; several places (e.g., “xk ∈ L (Ω,Hk)”) appear to drop the 2. Please standardize notation and fix typos: “discusssed,” “It is then be possible,” “st Conference...” in the author block.
- Some equations miss measure qualifiers or hypotheses (e.g., Fubini applications require integrability; state them once).
- Many references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted (missing years/venues/pages; stray commas; “Foundations and Trends R ©”; duplicated commas). Please correct.

Originality
- The stability result closely mirrors scattering theory; the incremental novelty is the RKHS/kernels viewpoint for multilayer convolutional representations, the shifted commutator bound, and the inclusion of a class of CNNs. This is a meaningful synthesis, though much of the technical machinery is adapted rather than entirely new.

Significance
- The theoretical insights are potentially useful, particularly the norm-based perspective on stability and generalization for CNN-like models. However, the paper lacks any empirical validation (even simple synthetic experiments) to illustrate constants, tightness, or practical consequences for CKN/CNN training and robustness. This limits immediate impact.

Pros
- Solid and relevant theoretical problem; clear linkage to influential prior frameworks (scattering, CKNs).
- General group-equivariant/invariant construction.
- RKHS inclusion result for CNNs with norm bounds.

Cons
- Several technical gaps or underspecified conditions (recovery Lemma 1, approximation error accumulation, explicit kernel examples/constants).
- Numerous minor typos/formatting/reference inconsistencies that hinder readability.
- No empirical illustrations to calibrate bounds or demonstrate applicability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide explicit kernel examples satisfying (A1–A2) with computed Lipschitz constants and Cσ(a); include a worked smoothed-ReLU example.
- Tighten Theorem 6 with explicit constants and discuss depth dependence; add a short experiment (toy 1D signals or images) to visualize stability versus σn and τ.
- Make Lemma 1 precise with conditions for invertibility/stability of Ak and exact recovery steps; clarify the role of access to RKHS elements versus finite approximations.
- Add an explicit cumulative error bound for finite-dimensional CKN projections.
- Clean up notation and references thoroughly; fix typos and incomplete author block.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper addresses an important question and contains promising ideas, but the accumulation of technical gaps and numerous presentation/formatting issues, combined with the absence of empirical corroboration, prevents me from recommending acceptance in its current form. A revised version addressing the above points could be strong.
