This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors aim to characterize the invariance and stability of these networks, extending previous work on scattering transforms to more general CNN architectures. They construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate that it contains a broad class of CNNs, allowing them to apply theoretical results regarding stability to deformations and translation invariance.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question regarding the theoretical underpinnings of CNNs. The connection to kernel methods provides a powerful framework for analysis, and the extension of existing results from scattering transforms is a significant step. The authors carefully build up the theoretical framework, starting with the kernel construction and progressing to stability and invariance properties. The inclusion of a discussion on discretization and practical implementation via Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) strengthens the relevance of the work. The authors also establish a link between their results and the generalization capabilities of CNNs through the RKHS norm.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper demonstrates a strong theoretical foundation, employing concepts from kernel methods and harmonic analysis. The proofs, while relegated to the appendix, appear thorough and support the claims.
*   **Novelty:** Extending the theoretical framework developed for scattering transforms to more general CNN architectures represents a novel contribution. The kernel-based approach provides a fresh perspective on analyzing CNNs.
*   **Clarity (of technical sections):** Despite the mathematical complexity, the technical sections are generally well-organized and clearly presented. The notation is consistent, and the key steps are explained in detail.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature, building upon previous work on scattering transforms, CKNs, and kernel methods.  The extensive related work section demonstrates awareness of the field.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The discussion of discretization and the connection to CKNs demonstrate the potential for practical applications of the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity (of motivation):** While the motivation for understanding the geometry of functional spaces is stated, the concrete benefits of this understanding beyond regularization and sample complexity are not fully elaborated. A more compelling connection to practical challenges in deep learning would be beneficial.
*   **Assumptions:** The paper relies on several assumptions (e.g., smoothness of activations, kernel properties) that might be restrictive in practice. The implications of relaxing these assumptions are not discussed. Specifically, assumption (A3) relating patch size and pooling scales could be problematic when dealing with very deep architectures that do not follow that scaling relationship exactly.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is primarily theoretical and lacks empirical validation. While the authors demonstrate connections to CNNs, they do not present any experimental results to support their claims about the stability and invariance properties of real-world CNNs.
*   **Complexity:** The mathematical formalism is dense and could be a barrier to entry for readers unfamiliar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis.
*   **Homogeneous Activations:** The strong reliance on homogeneous activations limits the direct applicability of the results to widely-used non-homogeneous activations like ReLU. While smoothed variants are mentioned, their effect on the theoretical properties is not fully explored.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to analyzing CNNs using kernel methods.
*   The extension of scattering transform theory to general CNNs is a significant contribution.
*   The results offer valuable insights into the stability and invariance properties of deep networks.
*   The work has the potential to inform the design of more robust and generalizable CNN architectures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the theoretical results.
*   The reliance on restrictive assumptions may limit the applicability of the findings.
*   The mathematical complexity may make the paper inaccessible to a broad audience.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of deep convolutional networks. While the lack of empirical validation and the reliance on certain assumptions are weaknesses, the novelty and rigor of the work outweigh these concerns. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, and it has the potential to influence future research in this area. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity of the motivation and to briefly discuss the implications of relaxing some of the key assumptions.  Adding a brief discussion of empirical tests that *could* be performed to validate the theoretical findings would also be helpful.
