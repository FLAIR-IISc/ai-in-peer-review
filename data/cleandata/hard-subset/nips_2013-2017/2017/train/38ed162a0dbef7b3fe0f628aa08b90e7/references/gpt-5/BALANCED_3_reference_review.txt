Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals defined on Rd. Each layer applies (i) patch extraction, (ii) a pointwise kernel map using a homogeneous dot-product kernel, and (iii) linear Gaussian pooling, yielding a continuous-domain analogue of convolutional networks. The authors define Φn(x) as the composed representation and the associated RKHS HKn. They show: (1) signal information is preserved layerwise (up to sampling assumptions); (2) a principled discretization and subsampling scheme that still permits signal recovery; (3) Lipschitz stability of Φ to diffeomorphisms with a bound of the form C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞, paralleling Mallat’s scattering theory but for a much broader class of architectures; (4) the stability is retained under kernel approximations used in Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs); (5) a group-equivariant construction that yields global invariance via final-layer pooling on a transformation group; and (6) an embedding of a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations into HKn, with explicit RKHS norm bounds, thereby linking stability and generalization via the RKHS norm.

This work sits at the intersection of scattering transforms (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat), convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.), and group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling). It extends Mallat’s diffeomorphism stability analysis from pre-specified wavelet networks to kernel-induced multilayer constructions and to a nontrivial subset of learned CNNs. It also connects to earlier invariance kernels (Haasdonk & Burkhardt), kernel analyses of deep nets (Montavon et al.; Daniely et al.; Anselmi et al.), and robustness/Lipschitz control (Cissé et al.).

Strengths
- Technical depth and rigor: Careful operator-theoretic treatment on continuous domains with precise assumptions (A1–A3). The commutator analysis and the extension to shifted pooling kernels are nontrivial.
- Unifying perspective: Provides a kernel/RKHS framework encompassing CKNs and a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, linking representation stability to predictor smoothness via RKHS norms.
- Clear invariance mechanism: Translation invariance tied to final pooling scale σn; inner layers yield stability to local deformations—mirroring scattering but in a more general setting.
- Practically relevant bridges: Discretization and kernel-approximation sections explain how CKNs inherit the stability guarantees. Group-equivariant layer construction connects to modern equivariant CNNs.
- Potential implications: The RKHS norm bound for CNNs suggests a pathway from architectural/regularization choices to stability and generalization.

Weaknesses
- Scope of novelty vs. scattering: The main stability theorem closely mirrors Mallat’s bounds (including the ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2 regularity and a linear dependence on depth), with extensions largely arising from the kernelized architecture and shifted-pooling commutator. The conceptual leap, while valuable, may feel incremental to experts in scattering.
- Practical reach: The CNNs provably contained in HKn require smooth homogeneous activations and a normalization factor nk(u)=‖Pkxk−1(u)‖ in the nonlinearity, which deviates from standard ReLU/BN practice. The non-inclusion of vanilla ReLU networks weakens immediate applicability.
- Lack of empirical validation: No experiments assess the tightness of constants, sample-complexity benefits, or robustness to deformations/adversarial perturbations compared to CNNs/scattering/CKNs.
- Assumption sensitivity: Stability relies on Gaussian pooling and homogeneous dot-product kernels; max pooling, strides without prefiltering, padding/border effects, and discrete artifacts are not theoretically handled. The deconvolution-based recovery in discretization can be ill-conditioned in practice.
- Bound quality: The (1+n) factor may be pessimistic; constants C1, C2 are not instantiated for common kernels/parameters, limiting interpretability.

Quality
The theoretical development appears sound and is well-situated within harmonic analysis and RKHS theory. Proofs are deferred, but the main lemmas (commutators via Schur’s test; stability under projections) are plausible. Some assumptions (A1–A2) constrain kernel choices but are justified with examples.

Clarity
The paper is dense but generally well-organized. The continuous-to-discrete narrative, operator definitions, and assumptions are clearly laid out. Suggestions:
- Summarize the constants and their dependence on kernel/patch/scale in the main text.
- Include a simple running example (e.g., 1D signal with two layers) to instantiate Theorem 6 and the CNN-in-RKHS mapping.
- More explicit comparison table vs. scattering (norm preservation, scope of architectures, stability constants).

Originality
Extends scattering-style stability guarantees to a broader, kernel-induced multilayer class and connects them to a subset of CNNs via RKHS embeddings and norm bounds. The group-invariance layer design is consistent with prior work; the shifted-kernel commutator bound is technically new to my knowledge. Overall, moderate originality.

Significance
Conceptually important for understanding invariance/stability in learned convolutional representations beyond scattering, and for grounding generalization via RKHS norms. Practical impact would be strengthened by empirical support and broader activation/pooling coverage. Likely to influence theoretical work and CKN variants.

Pros for acceptance
- Rigorous, well-motivated theory bridging kernels, CNNs, and scattering
- New stability results applicable to CKNs and a class of CNNs
- Clear group-equivariant/invariant construction
- Advances understanding of how pooling/scale control invariance

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to scattering; bounds carry similar structure
- Applicability restricted by activation assumptions and Gaussian pooling
- No empirical validation or instantiated constants
- Pessimistic depth dependence and unclear practical tightness

Constructive suggestions
- Provide numerical constants for Gaussian and inverse polynomial kernels and typical σk, patch sizes; discuss bound tightness empirically on simple datasets with controlled deformations.
- Examine stability under common architectural choices: striding without anti-aliasing, max pooling, ReLU (via smooth approximations), and batch normalization; quantify approximation error when replacing σ with ReLU approximants.
- Derive sample complexity/margin bounds that explicitly combine the RKHS norm with Theorem 6’s constants to make the generalization–stability link concrete.
- Extend the group section with stability to small group perturbations and a comparison to G-CNNs, including when only a subgroup is pooled at intermediate layers.
- Discuss discretization/aliasing more practically: prefiltering before subsampling, conditioning of deconvolution, boundary handling.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper offers a solid, unifying theoretical contribution with careful analysis and meaningful extensions beyond scattering, despite limited empirical/practical validation and some restrictive assumptions.
