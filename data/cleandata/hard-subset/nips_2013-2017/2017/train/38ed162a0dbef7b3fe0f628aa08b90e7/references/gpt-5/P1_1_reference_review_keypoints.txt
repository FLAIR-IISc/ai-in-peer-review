- Paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework on continuous domains to study invariance and stability to translations and small diffeomorphisms.
- Layer architecture: patch extraction → homogeneous dot-product kernel mapping into an RKHS → Gaussian pooling (with discretization/subsampling discussed).
- Main theoretical results: representation is stable to C1-diffeomorphisms with Lipschitz-type local bounds and translation invariance improving with final pooling scale.
- Main theoretical results: intermediate layers are equivariant while final pooling controls invariance.
- Main theoretical results: discrete subsampled construction can preserve signals under mild conditions (patch size ≥ subsampling) and CKN finite-dimensional approximations preserve stability via non-expansive projections.
- Main theoretical results: extension to general locally compact groups gives group equivariance/invariance via group pooling.
- Main theoretical results: RKHS of the multilayer kernel contains a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, with explicit RKHS norm bounds linking stability to generalization.
- Relation to prior work: builds on scattering transforms, extends stability bounds to broader architectures (homogeneous dot-product kernels and CKNs) and connects to group-equivariant CNNs and kernelized views of deep nets.
- Strength: clear, principled continuous-to-discrete kernel construction mirroring CNNs and addressing discretization.
- Strength: explicit stability bounds (linear in depth for local deformations; dependence on last pooling scale) using extended harmonic analysis/commutator tools.
- Strength: compatibility with practical approximations—stability survives CKN finite-dimensional projections due to non-expansiveness.
- Strength: unified group-theoretic extension clarifying equivariance/invariance via pooling on groups/subgroups.
- Strength: inclusion of a class of CNNs in the RKHS with explicit norm bounds, enabling norm-based stability/generalization reasoning.
- Weakness: novelty is incremental relative to scattering literature; many techniques and statements closely follow Mallat with kernelized and commutator extensions as the main differences.
- Weakness: strong assumptions for stability (norm-preserving homogeneous kernel maps, Gaussian pooling, initial pooling A0) and CNN inclusion requires smooth activations and per-patch normalization not standard in practice.
- Weakness: information-preservation claims rely on deconvolution and conditions (e_k ≥ s_k); deconvolution with Gaussians can be ill-conditioned, ignores border effects and finite support issues.
- Weakness: no empirical validation; experiments on robustness/stability would strengthen claims about robustness and sample complexity.
- Quality: mathematics are careful; commutator extension to shifted kernels is nontrivial and useful; proofs largely in appendix.
- Clarity: generally well organized and readable for experts; suggest bringing key constants (C1,C2) and parameter mappings into main text and adding examples for CNN-in-RKHS normalization.
- Originality: moderate—useful conceptual unification and new commutator/explicit norm-bound contributions, but many components are adaptations of known ideas.
- Significance: solid theoretical contribution with potential to inform architecture/regularizer design; practical impact limited by assumptions and lack of experiments.
- Key questions for authors: provide explicit σ/kernel pairs with computed C_σ and example smoothed ReLU; quantify sensitivity of constants to dimension/κ and practical depths; quantify projection error effect in CKNs on stability; discuss common CNN practices (batch-norm, learned pooling, striding) w.r.t. assumptions; include a small empirical stability study.
- Recommendation: Accept (score 7) — well-executed theoretical advance, would benefit from minimal empirical validation and clearer discussion of practical relevance.
