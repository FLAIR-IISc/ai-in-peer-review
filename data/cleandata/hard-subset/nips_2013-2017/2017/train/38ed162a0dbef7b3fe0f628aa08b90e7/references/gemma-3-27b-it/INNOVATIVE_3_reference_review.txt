This paper investigates the geometry of functional spaces induced by deep convolutional neural networks, framing the analysis through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors aim to provide theoretical guarantees on invariance and stability, extending results from scattering transforms to more general CNN architectures. They construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate its connections to both convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and standard CNNs. The core contribution lies in establishing stability to diffeomorphisms and demonstrating how this stability relates to generalization performance.

This is a highly promising and ambitious work. The attempt to ground CNN analysis within a robust kernel framework is commendable and represents a significant departure from purely empirical investigations. The extension of stability results from the scattering transform to a broader class of CNNs, particularly those with smooth, homogeneous activations, is a valuable contribution. The paper tackles a fundamental question – understanding *why* CNNs work – and offers a potential pathway toward principled regularization and improved sample complexity. The connection drawn between stability (RKHS norm) and generalization is particularly insightful. The thoroughness of the related work section, acknowledging connections to diverse areas like kernel methods, wavelet analysis, and harmonic analysis, is also appreciated.

However, the paper is dense and theoretical. While the mathematical rigor is impressive, the practical implications remain somewhat abstract. The “smooth homogeneous activation functions” requirement feels limiting; while acknowledged as such, the implications for practical applications using ReLU or its variants aren’t fully explored beyond mentioning smoothed alternatives. While Proposition 7 shows CNNs can be *contained* within the RKHS, the degree to which standard CNN architectures fully leverage the theoretical benefits isn’t entirely clear. The link between the theoretical stability guarantees and *actual* observed generalization in large-scale image classification tasks needs further elaboration. The discussion of discretization, while present, could benefit from more concrete examples and a more detailed analysis of its impact on the stability bounds. The fact that the constant C1 in theorem 6 relies on κ, and thus could explode with increasingly large patches (and deeper networks) is a concern.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The kernel-based approach to analyzing CNNs is highly novel and provides a fresh perspective on understanding their behavior.
*   **Theoretical Significance:** The stability guarantees and their relation to generalization are theoretically significant contributions, potentially influencing regularization techniques.
*   **Generality:** Extending results from scattering transforms to a broader class of CNNs is a valuable step toward a more comprehensive theory of deep learning.
*   **Rigor:** The mathematical analysis is thorough and demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practicality:** The reliance on smooth, homogeneous activations and the abstract nature of the results limit immediate practical application.
*   **Density and Accessibility:** The paper is very dense and requires significant mathematical background to fully comprehend.
*   **Incomplete Validation:** While theoretically sound, the connection to real-world CNN performance and large-scale datasets isn’t fully established.
*   **Potential Scalability Issues:** The dependence of stability on parameters like patch size and depth, through constants like κ, needs further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, this paper presents a genuinely novel and significant contribution. The ambition and theoretical depth are commendable. The paper isn’t a complete, polished product ready for immediate application, but it outlines a promising new direction for research. The weaknesses are primarily related to practicality and accessibility, rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology. Given the novelty and potential impact, I lean towards **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the concerns regarding practicality and provide more concrete examples or simulations to bridge the gap between theory and practice in a future version. I’d suggest the authors focus on clarifying the implications of their results for more commonly used activation functions and providing a more detailed discussion of the scalability of their approach.
