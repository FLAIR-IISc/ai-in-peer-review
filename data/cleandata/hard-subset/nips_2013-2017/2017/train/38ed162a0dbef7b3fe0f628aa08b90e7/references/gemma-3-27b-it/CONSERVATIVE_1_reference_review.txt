This paper explores the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations through the lens of positive definite kernels, aiming to provide a more rigorous understanding of their invariance and stability. The authors extend existing kernel-based approaches (Convolutional Kernel Networks – CKNs) and connect them to the scattering transform, seeking to generalize results to more standard CNN architectures. The core idea is to construct a functional space derived from a kernel that encompasses many CNNs, enabling theoretical analysis of their properties.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors meticulously build their framework, starting with necessary mathematical notation and progressively developing the multilayer convolutional kernel. The connection to existing work, especially scattering transforms and CKNs, is well-established. The attempt to formalize stability and invariance within a kernel framework is commendable. However, despite the careful presentation, the paper suffers from a crucial weakness: a lack of empirical validation and novel insights beyond extending existing theory.

The main strength lies in the rigorous mathematical development. The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of kernel methods and harmonic analysis. The extension of stability results to a broader class of CNNs is a reasonable goal. The theoretical arguments regarding invariance and stability, particularly the connection to diffeomorphism transformations, are sound, *given the assumptions*. 

However, the paper's weaknesses are substantial. The central claim – that this kernel framework provides a deeper understanding of CNNs – feels largely unsupported. The theoretical results largely *reproduce* existing guarantees from the scattering transform literature, but at the cost of significantly increased complexity. The extension to standard CNNs is more a statement of inclusion within the framework than a demonstration of new properties.  Crucially, there’s no empirical evidence presented to suggest this theoretical framework actually *explains* the behavior of CNNs in practice, or enables improvements in CNN design or training. The paper relies heavily on abstract mathematical constructions without grounding them in experimental observation. The authors acknowledge that the norm preservation property doesn't necessarily hold in practice due to pooling layers, but don’t explore the implications of this deviation. Furthermore, the discussion on homogeneous activations feels somewhat disconnected; simply stating that ReLU can be approximated by a homogeneous function doesn't provide significant new insight. The link between the RKHS norm and generalization, while mentioned, isn’t developed beyond a general observation.  The authors state the results apply to CNNs, yet provide no CNN experiments.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous mathematical treatment of CNNs within a kernel framework.
*   Extension of existing stability results to a wider class of models.
*   Clear exposition of complex mathematical concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation to support theoretical claims.
*   Limited novelty beyond extending existing theory (scattering transforms, CKNs).
*   The theoretical framework feels abstract and disconnected from practical CNN behavior.
*   The paper doesn’t demonstrate how the developed framework can be used to improve CNNs.
*   The discussion regarding homogeneous activations feels incomplete.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a technically sound, but ultimately incremental, contribution. It provides a theoretical perspective on CNNs using kernel methods, but fails to deliver compelling evidence of its practical significance. While the mathematical rigor is admirable, the absence of empirical results and novel insights leads me to believe this paper is not suitable for NIPS at this time. The paper feels more like a theoretical exploration than a substantial advancement in the field. I recommend **rejection**. A revised version with significant empirical validation and a clear demonstration of the framework’s utility would be more competitive.
