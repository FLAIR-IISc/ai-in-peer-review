This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors construct a functional space based on a multilayer convolutional kernel, demonstrating that it encompasses a broad class of CNNs, including convolutional kernel networks (CKNs). They then analyze the invariance and stability of this space to diffeomorphic transformations, obtaining results comparable to those for scattering transforms, but applicable to a wider range of CNN architectures. A key contribution is demonstrating that the stability properties translate to predictive models, linking stability to generalization performance.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the theoretical underpinnings of CNNs. The connection to kernel methods provides a rigorous framework for analyzing invariance and stability. Extending the theory of scattering transforms to more general CNN architectures is a significant step. The results are presented with mathematical clarity and the authors carefully connect their work to related research, including CKNs, scattering transforms, and kernel methods for deep learning. The inclusion of lemmas and propositions with deferred proofs allows for a focused presentation of the main ideas. The discussion regarding discretization and its relation to the continuous model is also valuable.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The core idea of leveraging kernel theory to analyze CNNs isn’t entirely novel, as evidenced by several cited works (e.g., [2, 4, 7, 10, 18, 26]). While the authors convincingly extend the existing theoretical framework, the extent of novelty beyond the extension to more general CNNs and the careful stability analysis isn’t entirely clear. The link between stability and generalization, while intuitively appealing, is presented as a consequence of RKHS norm control, which isn’t a particularly novel insight in kernel methods. The assumptions (A1-A3) seem quite restrictive. While the authors mention kernels satisfying these assumptions, the practical relevance and impact of these restrictions on the applicability of the results aren’t fully discussed. For example, the assumption that Hk contains linear functions is strong. The treatment of activations is also somewhat limited. The authors acknowledge that ReLU is non-smooth and not directly contained in their RKHSs, but simply mention smoothed variants. A more thorough discussion of the implications of this approximation would be beneficial. The connection to adversarial robustness, mentioned briefly, feels somewhat tacked on and could be explored in more detail. Finally, the paper is dense and mathematically involved, making it potentially inaccessible to readers not already familiar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   Rigorous mathematical framework for analyzing CNNs based on kernel theory.
*   Extension of scattering transform-like stability results to a broader class of CNNs.
*   Clear connection between stability and potential for generalization.
*   Detailed analysis of discretization and its impact on the theoretical results.
*   Comprehensive related work section.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty beyond extending existing theory and stability analysis.
*   Restrictive assumptions (A1-A3) and insufficient discussion of their practical implications.
*   Limited discussion of activation functions, particularly ReLU.
*   Dense and mathematically challenging presentation.
*   The connection to adversarial robustness is superficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and mathematically rigorous analysis of CNNs.
*   The extension of scattering transform-like results to a wider class of CNNs is a valuable contribution.
*   The paper offers a new perspective on understanding the generalization properties of CNNs through the lens of stability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the work is incremental.
*   The restrictive assumptions limit the practical applicability of the results.
*   The density and mathematical complexity may hinder accessibility for a broad audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of CNNs, but its novelty is somewhat limited. The technical soundness is high, but the restrictive assumptions and dense presentation may hinder its impact. While not groundbreaking, it represents a valuable extension of existing work and provides further insights into the geometry of deep learning models. I would rate this as a **weak accept**. Further improvements in clarity and discussion of the limitations would strengthen the paper significantly.
