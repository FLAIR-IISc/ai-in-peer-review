Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional positive-definite kernel on continuous domains that (i) induces a sequence of feature maps analogous to CNN layers (patch extraction, pointwise kernel map, pooling), (ii) yields stability to small diffeomorphisms and increasing translation invariance with depth, (iii) extends to global invariance via group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling, and (iv) contains a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations as functions in the RKHS induced by the kernel, with explicit norm bounds. The authors also discuss discretization, finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs), and how stability properties survive these approximations. The theoretical development builds on and generalizes scattering transform results (Mallat/Bruna) and connects to convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.).

Relation to prior work
- Scattering transform: Adopts the Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms framework and translation invariance via pooling; extends beyond wavelets to general homogeneous dot-product kernels and to a class of CNNs via RKHS embeddings.
- Convolutional kernel networks: Generalizes the CKN construction to continuous domains and clarifies when approximations preserve stability.
- Group equivariant CNNs: Presents a kernel/RKHS perspective on equivariance and global invariance via group pooling, consistent with prior G-CNNs and group-scattering.
- Generalization/robustness: Connects RKHS norms to Lipschitz control of predictions, linking stability and sample complexity.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The results are mathematically careful and leverage standard tools (Schur’s test, commutator bounds, properties of Gaussian smoothing). The core stability theorem gives a bound of the form C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞, matching the desired Lipschitz characterization and explaining the role of depth and final pooling scale. The commutator bound with a shifted kernel (Lemma 4) is a nontrivial extension of scattering analyses. The CNN-in-RKHS result (Proposition 7) is technically substantial and provides explicit norm recursions.
- Assumptions and limitations: Stability requires norm-preserving, nonexpansive kernel maps and a relation between patch size and pooling scale; the analysis is for continuous signals with boundary effects ignored. The class of CNNs covered requires smooth homogeneous activations (ReLU is handled via smoothing/homogenization), and the “homogenization” term nk(u) is not standard in practice. Discretization relies on stable deconvolution and assumes filters and subsampling regimes that may be delicate numerically. The dependence of constants on kernel/patch parameters is mostly qualitative, and the linear dependence on depth could be pessimistic. Claims relating RKHS norm to generalization are conceptual; no explicit generalization bounds are derived beyond the standard margin intuition.
- Empirical support: The paper is theoretical; no experiments quantify constants, validate stability under deformations, or demonstrate practical benefits of design choices (e.g., σn growth). This is acceptable for a theory paper, but even small illustrative experiments would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- The paper is dense but generally well organized. The operator viewpoint (Pk, Mk, Ak) and the connection to CNN layers are clearly explained. Discretization and the link to CKNs help bridge theory and practice. Proofs are deferred to appendices; the main text states assumptions and key lemmas cleanly.
- Suggestions: (i) more explicit discussion of constant sizes and typical choices for σk, ek, sk; (ii) a brief concrete example of an activation/kernel pair satisfying Lemma B.2; (iii) a figure illustrating group pooling; (iv) an empirical or synthetic demonstration of stability vs. σn and depth.

Originality
- The work meaningfully extends scattering stability results to a more general convolutional kernel setting and shows that a nontrivial class of CNNs lies within the induced RKHS with explicit norm bounds. The commutator analysis for shifted integral operators and the unification of scattering/CKN/CNN under an RKHS lens are novel and insightful. The group-invariance construction is conceptually aligned with prior work but appropriately integrated.

Significance
- The paper advances theoretical understanding of deep convolutional representations by isolating architectural elements that confer stability and invariance and by tying prediction stability to RKHS norm control. This is likely to influence both theory (e.g., sample complexity analyses of conv nets) and practice (design of stable architectures, kernel approximations). Immediate practical impact is limited by the lack of experiments and by the gap between the analyzed activation class and widely used ReLU networks, though the smoothing/homogenization workaround partially closes that gap.

Pros
- Rigorous stability bound for a broad multilayer convolutional kernel, with clear architectural implications.
- Unifies scattering, CKNs, and a class of CNNs in one RKHS framework; explicit norm bounds for CNNs.
- Thoughtful treatment of discretization and kernel approximations; stability preserved under projection.
- Clean extension to group equivariance and global invariance via pooling on groups.

Cons
- No empirical validation; constants and tightness of bounds unquantified.
- Assumptions (smooth homogeneous activations, homogenization factors, deconvolution stability) may limit direct applicability to common CNNs.
- Linear dependence on depth and potential loss of norm due to pooling raise questions about scalability of the guarantees.
- The group-invariance section is conceptually known; incremental in isolation.

Recommendation
This is a strong theoretical paper that provides a unifying and general framework for understanding invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations, with technically solid and novel components. Despite the lack of experiments and some practical gaps, I lean toward acceptance for its conceptual clarity and potential impact on the theory of deep learning. Constructive additions (even small-scale experiments and explicit constant estimates) would further strengthen the submission.
