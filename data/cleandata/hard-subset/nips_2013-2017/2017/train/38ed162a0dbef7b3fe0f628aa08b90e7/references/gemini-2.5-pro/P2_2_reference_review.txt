**Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"**

**Summary**
This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The core of the work is the construction of a multilayer convolutional kernel, building upon the framework of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs). This kernel defines a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) of functions. The authors then leverage this kernel framework to prove that the resulting representations are Lipschitz stable to the action of small diffeomorphisms, a property previously established for scattering networks. A key result is that the degree of translation invariance is controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer, which increases with network depth. The framework is also shown to be generalizable to achieve invariance to arbitrary group actions. Crucially, the authors establish a formal link between their kernel representation and standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), demonstrating that CNNs with certain smooth, homogeneous activation functions are contained within the constructed RKHS. This allows the stability guarantees to be extended to this class of CNNs, thereby connecting stability properties to the RKHS norm, which is in turn related to generalization.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution (Quality, Originality, Significance):** This is a high-quality theoretical paper that provides a rigorous mathematical framework for understanding properties of CNNs that are often only discussed intuitively. By synthesizing concepts from kernel methods (CKNs), harmonic analysis (scattering networks), and deep learning, the paper offers a novel and powerful perspective. The formal proof of Lipschitz stability for a CNN-like architecture is a major strength.

2.  **Principled Link between Kernels and CNNs (Originality, Significance):** The paper's most impactful contribution is arguably establishing that a class of CNNs resides within the RKHS of a well-defined convolutional kernel (Proposition 7). This is a non-trivial result that bridges two traditionally separate domains. It allows the rich theoretical machinery of kernel methods to be applied to analyze deep networks, providing a principled way to reason about properties like stability and their connection to generalization via the RKHS norm (Eq. 1).

3.  **Clarity of the Stability Argument (Quality, Clarity):** The stability analysis is well-structured and builds on prior work by Mallat, adapting it to the kernel setting. The decomposition of the stability bound in Proposition 3 into commutator terms, which are then individually bounded in Lemmas 4 and 5, makes the argument easy to follow. The conclusion that stability to local deformations is handled by intermediate layers while global invariance is governed by the final pooling scale is an elegant and insightful result.

4.  **Generality of the Framework (Quality):** The proposed framework is quite general. It is defined for d-dimensional signals, its extension to arbitrary group invariances is discussed (Section 4), and the practical considerations of discretization and kernel approximation (leading to CKNs) are thoughtfully addressed. This demonstrates that the authors have considered the broader applicability of their ideas beyond a narrow, idealized setting.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Modern Practice (Significance):** The primary weakness is the disconnect between the class of CNNs analyzed and those used in state-of-the-art practice. The analysis requires activation functions to be smooth and homogeneous. The most prevalent activation function, the Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU), is homogeneous but crucially non-smooth, and thus falls outside the scope of the RKHS construction. While the authors mention smooth ReLU variants, this limitation means the direct applicability of the results to widely-used architectures (like ResNets) is unclear. Furthermore, the homogenization term `nk(u) = ||Pkxk-1(u)||` in the CNN formulation (Eq. 8) is non-standard and its practical implications are not discussed.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation (Quality):** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a theory-focused contribution, the arguments would have been substantially strengthened by even simple experiments. For instance, empirically verifying the stability of a CNN trained with a smooth activation versus a standard ReLU to small, generated diffeomorphisms could have provided valuable validation. Similarly, measuring an approximation of the RKHS norm for trained models and correlating it with their observed robustness would have made the link between the norm and stability more concrete.

3.  **Assumptions and Idealizations (Clarity):** The main theorems are derived for signals on a continuous domain `Ω = R^d`. While discretization is discussed in Section 2.1, the stability proof in Section 3 relies on the continuous formulation. It is not explicitly shown how the constants `C1` and `C2` in the final stability bound (Theorem 6) translate to the discrete setting, where aliasing and sampling artifacts could complicate the analysis. Similarly, border effects are acknowledged but not analyzed.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This paper is a strong theoretical contribution to our understanding of deep learning. Its main strengths are the rigorous formulation of a convolutional kernel framework and the elegant proof of its stability to deformations. The connection it establishes between this kernel space and a class of CNNs is a significant and original insight, providing a new formal language to discuss the relationship between stability, generalization, and network architecture.

The weaknesses, primarily the gap with common practices like ReLU activations and the absence of empirical support, do temper its immediate practical impact. However, they do not undermine the core theoretical value of the work. This paper provides foundational ideas and analytical tools that future research can build upon to tackle more complex and practical architectures. It clearly advances the state of the art in the theoretical understanding of deep networks.

Given the high quality of the theoretical results, the originality of the approach, and its potential significance for the research community, I recommend **acceptance**. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a fundamental problem in the field. It is an excellent fit for NIPS.
