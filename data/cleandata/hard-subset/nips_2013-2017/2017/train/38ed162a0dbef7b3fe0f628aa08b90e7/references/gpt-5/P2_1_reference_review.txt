Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework on continuous domains that mirrors CNN architectures (patch extraction, pointwise nonlinear mapping into an RKHS, and Gaussian pooling), then establishes stability to small diffeomorphisms and near-translation invariance. The main stability result (Eq. 4, Theorem 6) shows Lipschitz continuity of the representation with constants scaling linearly in depth and inversely with the last pooling scale. The analysis covers discretization (with recovery guarantees under subsampling), finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs), and extends to global invariance to general groups via equivariant patch extraction and pooling on locally compact groups. A key contribution is showing that a nontrivial class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, with explicit norm bounds (Proposition 7), thereby transferring the representation’s stability guarantees to such CNNs and linking stability and generalization via RKHS norms.

Relation to prior work is clearly positioned: it generalizes Mallat’s scattering stability theory (from fixed wavelets to learned homogeneous dot-product kernels), connects to convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.), and aligns with group-equivariant networks (Cohen & Welling). Compared to Poggio/Anselmi’s kernel viewpoints, this work provides rigorous, layerwise commutator bounds for diffeomorphisms and a concrete inclusion of a CNN class into an RKHS with computable norms.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The assumptions are explicit—norm-preserving, non-expansive kernel maps (A1–A2), bounded patch radii tied to pooling scales (A3), and a mild smoothness condition on the deformation (‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2). The commutator analysis is careful; Lemma 4 extends prior scattering results to shifted pooling operators, and Lemma 5 controls the non-commutation term with a dependence on 1/σn. The stability bound (Theorem 6) is well-motivated and consistent with intuition about pooling-driven invariance.
- Completeness: The theoretical path from continuous signals to discrete implementations is addressed via sampling and deconvolution (Lemma 2), and compatibility with finite-dimensional projections preserves non-expansiveness. The group-invariance treatment is principled.
- Limitations are acknowledged (loss of norm due to pooling, border effects, small-deformation regime). There are no experiments; while not fatal for a theory paper, empirical support illustrating constants/tightness would strengthen the work.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and self-contained, with clear separation between construction, stability, discretization, group extensions, and CNN connections. The notation load is high but consistent.
- Helpful suggestions: 
  - Early table summarizing operators (Pk, Mk, Ak), their norms, and covariance properties would aid readability.
  - Clarify which concrete activation functions satisfy Lemma B.2 and show typical constants Cσ for the kernels used.
  - Provide an explicit example of schedules for (Sk, σk) and how they translate under discretization.
  - Briefly quantify the impact of border effects or state precise conditions under which they are negligible.

Originality
- Novel aspects include: (i) a multilayer RKHS construction that subsumes CKNs and admits a class of CNNs, (ii) a general diffeomorphism stability bound for this construction with a new commutator control for shifted pooling, (iii) discrete recovery under subsampling in this setting, and (iv) group-invariant pooling formulated within the same kernel framework. 
- The conceptual bridge from CNNs to RKHS with explicit norm bounds that imply stability is insightful. While the high-level stability theme follows scattering, the generalization to homogeneous dot-product kernels and the connection to learned filters go beyond prior art.

Significance
- The results advance our theoretical understanding of invariance/stability in deep convolutional representations beyond scattering and offer a unified view that covers CKNs and certain CNNs. 
- Practically, the link between RKHS norms, stability, and generalization suggests principled regularization, potentially informing architecture and training choices (e.g., choosing pooling scales, kernel maps, or norm controls).
- Immediate empirical impact is limited by the gap between the analyzed CNN class and standard ReLU/BN architectures, and by the small-deformation regime, but the conceptual contribution is likely to influence future theoretical and methodological work.

Strengths
- Clear, principled construction tying together kernels, convolution, pooling, and group actions.
- Rigorous stability analysis with explicit dependence on depth and pooling scale.
- Compatibility of discretization and finite-dimensional projections with stability.
- Nontrivial inclusion of a CNN class in an RKHS with norm bounds, linking stability to generalization.
- Broad applicability to translations and general groups.

Weaknesses
- The covered CNN class requires smooth homogeneous activations and normalization by local patch norms (nk), deviating from standard ReLU pipelines; claims about “containing a large class of CNNs” should be calibrated.
- Stability constants grow linearly with depth; discussion on tightness and practical magnitudes is limited.
- Translation invariance hinges on large σn; guidance on balancing invariance with information loss is brief.
- Recovery via deconvolution hinges on small pooling scales; stability of deconvolution in realistic settings deserves more discussion.
- No experiments illustrating the theory’s practical relevance (e.g., stability vs. σn, deformation robustness) or comparing to scattering/CNN baselines.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, well-scoped theoretical contribution that extends and unifies important lines of work.
- New technical tools (commutator bound for shifted pooling) and explicit RKHS norms for CNNs.
- Clear path from continuous theory to discrete/approximated models.
- Relevance to core NeurIPS topics (representation learning, invariance, kernels, deep theory).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited immediate empirical validation and practical guidance.
- CNN coverage excludes standard nonsmooth activations unless smoothed; requires nonstandard normalization.
- Assumptions may limit applicability beyond small diffeomorphisms.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper delivers a careful and meaningful theoretical advance on invariance and stability for convolutional representations, with a unifying kernel perspective and a credible link to (a class of) CNNs. While empirical support and broader coverage of common activations would strengthen the case, the contribution is significant and well-aligned with NeurIPS.
