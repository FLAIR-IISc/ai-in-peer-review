Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals on continuous domains, closely mirroring CNN architectures via three operators per layer: patch extraction, a norm-preserving homogeneous kernel mapping, and Gaussian pooling. The authors analyze the induced RKHS and prove stability to diffeomorphisms: ||Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)|| ≤ (C1(1+n)||∇τ||∞ + C2/σn ||τ||∞) ||x||, where n is depth and σn the final pooling scale. They discuss discretization (patch size, subsampling, anti-aliasing), showing under mild conditions that feature maps retain enough information to reconstruct previous layers. They extend the construction to achieve equivariance/invariance for general groups via group-covariant patching and pooling. Finally, they prove that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the constructed RKHS, yielding norm-based Lipschitz control and a conceptual link between stability and generalization. The analysis relates to scattering transforms (Mallat/Bruna) and convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), with proofs deferred to the appendix.

Relation to prior work
The work is squarely in the lineage of scattering networks and group-equivariant CNNs, adopting Mallat’s diffeomorphism-stability framework and extending it from predefined wavelet filters to a broader kernel class (including CKNs). The group-invariance section echoes established formulations of group equivariance and pooling on groups. The RKHS view connecting CNNs to kernel machines aligns with Poggio/Rosasco/Daniely et al. The novelty appears to be: (i) a unified kernel-based multilayer formulation on continuous domains with discretization guidance; (ii) a commutator bound for shifted pooled operators; and (iii) an inclusion of certain CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations into the RKHS with recursive norm bounds.

Strengths
- Clear, modular construction aligning well with practical CNN intuitions (patching, pointwise nonlinearity, pooling).
- Stability theorem in Mallat’s sense for a class broader than scattering, with translation invariance scaling with σn and explicit depth dependence.
- Careful discussion of discretization and anti-aliasing; conditions like ek ≥ sk are transparent and match common practice.
- Extension to general groups through equivariant patching and pooling is coherent and connects to prior group-equivariant work.
- The RKHS containment result for CNNs provides a principled route to Lipschitz control via norms and may inform regularization.
- Compatibility with finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs) is addressed, preserving non-expansiveness.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to scattering and CKNs. The main stability bound is qualitatively similar to Mallat’s, with linear depth dependence and last-layer pooling governing invariance; the incremental generality (homogeneous kernels) may not justify a NeurIPS publication without empirical validation.
- No experiments. Claims about practical implications (stability vs. generalization, advantage for CKNs, near-invariance to groups) remain untested, especially compared to strong baselines (standard CNNs, scattering, G-CNNs).
- Heavy reliance on assumptions: norm-preserving, non-expansive kernel maps; Gaussian pooling; initial pooling A0 modeling acquisition; and deconvolution stability in discretization. The impact of violating these in real systems (e.g., padding, strided convolutions, non-Gaussian pooling) is not quantified.
- The inclusion of CNNs into the RKHS excludes the ubiquitous ReLU (nonsmooth); the suggested “smoothed ReLU” substitution is not rigorously analyzed for approximation error nor for practical relevance.
- Constants C1, C2 are not explicit, and the κ-dependence can grow quickly with dimension, limiting interpretability. The bound’s linear dependence on depth and potential norm shrinkage due to pooling may weaken utility for deep hierarchies.
- The projection/approximation error for CKNs is not quantified in the final stability bound; we only get a qualitative non-expansiveness statement.
- The claimed generalization implications are qualitative; no sample complexity or margin bounds specialized to this setting are provided.

Quality
Technically careful at a high level, but many proofs are deferred, constants are not explicit, and several crucial steps hinge on strong modeling choices (Gaussian pooling, homogeneous kernels). The discretization lemmas rely on stable deconvolution, which can be ill-conditioned. The RKHS-norm recursion is clear, but whether resulting norms are finite and non-vacuous for realistic network sizes is not demonstrated.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with clear operator-level descriptions and a reasonable notation strategy. The paper would benefit from:
- More explicit statements of main contributions versus prior work.
- A concise table mapping continuous parameters to discrete hyperparameters.
- Explicit constants or at least their scaling with dimension, κ, and σ.

Originality
Moderate. The framework synthesizes known components; the shifted commutator bound and the RKHS containment for smooth homogeneous CNNs appear to be the most original technical pieces.

Significance
Potentially useful for understanding CKNs and theoretically motivated CNN variants, but without empirical validation or sharper bounds, the near-term impact is uncertain. The community has multiple established approaches (scattering, G-CNNs) with proofs and experiments.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide explicit constants (or tight upper bounds) for C1 and its dependence on κ and dimension? How large do they become in 2D/3D?
- How sensitive are your results to non-Gaussian pooling and to common discrete operations (stride, padding, boundary effects)?
- Can you quantify the effect of kernel projection (approximation error) on the final stability bound for CKNs?
- Is there a rigorous approximation result showing that ReLU CNNs are close (in RKHS norm or functionally) to your smooth-homogeneous-activation class?
- Can you provide even small-scale experiments comparing deformation stability: scattering vs. CKN vs. standard CNNs?

Pros
- Solid and careful theoretical development.
- Unifies scattering-style stability with kernelized CNN-like architectures.
- Clear path to group-equivariant/invariant constructions.
- Potentially informative norm-based perspective on stability/generalization.

Cons
- Incremental w.r.t. established theory; novelty is limited.
- No empirical evidence; practical relevance not demonstrated.
- Strong assumptions; constants and scalability unclear.
- ReLU gap and discrete implementation issues remain.

Recommendation
Score: 5 (leaning reject). While the theory is careful and potentially useful, the contribution feels incremental relative to scattering and CKNs, and the absence of empirical validation or sharper, explicit bounds limits significance at NeurIPS. Strengthening with experiments and clarifying constants/assumptions would improve the case.

Confidence
High.
