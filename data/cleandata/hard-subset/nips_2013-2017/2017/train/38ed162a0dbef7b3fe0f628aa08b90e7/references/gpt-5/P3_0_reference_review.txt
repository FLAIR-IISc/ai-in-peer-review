Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework on continuous domains that mirrors CNN architectures: each layer applies patch extraction Pk, a pointwise kernel map Mk into an RKHS with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling Ak. The representation is shown to be non-expansive across Pk/Mk and contractive under Ak. The authors prove (i) signal preservation/recoverability from sampled feature maps under mild conditions, (ii) a discretized construction with subsampling that is information-preserving (via stable deconvolution) when ek ≥ sk, (iii) Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms τ with a bound of the scattering form ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ ≤ [C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn]‖x‖, and (iv) a group-equivariant layer design yielding global invariance by pooling over a group G. Finally, they show a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations can be embedded in the RKHS induced by their multilayer kernel, and derive an explicit norm upper bound that controls Lipschitz stability and suggests a link to generalization.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge: Extends scattering-style stability analysis to a broad class of convolutional kernel representations and, via RKHS containment, to (a subclass of) CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations.
- Clear operator-level decomposition and use of harmonic analysis tools (Schur’s test, commutators), yielding interpretable stability constants. The translation-invariance term decays with the last pooling scale σn, aligning with engineering intuition.
- Discretization section is thoughtful: shows how continuous arguments motivate standard design choices (small ek, sk; anti-aliasing via Gaussian low-pass) and preserves information under reasonable conditions.
- Group-equivariant construction is clean and general, compatible with prior work on group-equivariant CNNs and group pooling.
- The norm-based view—prediction Lipschitzness controlled by RKHS norm—connects invariance/stability with sample complexity via standard margin arguments.

Weaknesses and points to clarify
- Depth dependence: The bound scales linearly with depth, via ∑k‖[PkAk−1, Lτ]‖ ≤ C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞. Given σk and patch radii typically grow with k, a uniform C1 over k appears loose; a refined analysis that exploits the increasing smoothing (decaying commutators for larger σk−1) could remove or attenuate the linear factor.
- Norm non-preservation: Unlike scattering, norm is not preserved due to pooling. The discussion suggests adding a final Gaussian kernel map to re-normalize, but a more explicit quantification of energy retention for natural images (e.g., under 1/fα spectra) would strengthen the practical relevance of the lower bound ‖Φ(x)‖ ≥ ‖An⋯A0x‖.
- CNN inclusion requires nonstandard normalization: The constructed CNN class uses z̃ik(u) = nk(u) σ(⟨wik,Pkzk−1(u)⟩/nk(u)) with nk(u)=‖Pkxk−1(u)‖. This homogenization deviates from typical CNNs (which apply σ to affine responses). While relu is homogeneous, the need for input-dependent scaling is a gap between theory and practice. Please clarify whether practical architectures (e.g., with batch/instance norm) approximate this normalization and how sensitive the RKHS inclusion is to deviations.
- Activation smoothness: ReLU is excluded; results rely on smooth homogeneous σ (e.g., smoothed ReLU or inverse-polynomial kernels). Providing a concrete σ and kernel pair with explicit Cσ, and plotting Bn,i growth with depth/width, would make Proposition 7 more actionable. Is the recursion for Bn,i prone to exponential blow-up without explicit weight constraints?
- Approximation effects: The CKN projection steps are asserted to preserve non-expansiveness, but the stability bound does not include an explicit term for the projection residuals. A bound of the form of Theorem 6 plus additive approximation errors that accumulate across layers would be valuable, including how these errors scale with subspace size and data distribution.
- Discrete recoverability via deconvolution: Lemma 2 relies on invertibility/stability of deconvolution with Gaussian filters. In the presence of noise and boundary effects, this is ill-posed in practice. Clarify the stability constants and noise amplification, and whether the claim should be phrased as approximate recovery with quantified error.
- Group invariance: Pooling over non-compact groups (e.g., SE(2)) requires localized filters and finite support integration; please detail the assumptions ensuring well-posedness and computational feasibility and how this affects stability constants.
- Empirical validation is absent. Even small-scale synthetic experiments (e.g., measuring ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ vs ‖τ‖∞, σn, and depth; testing projection approximation effects) would substantiate the constants and the trade-offs predicted.

Quality
- Technically careful with appropriate use of harmonic analysis. Most proofs are deferred; the main steps are plausible and consistent with scattering literature. Some constants and depth dependence seem loose; approximation and discretization error accumulation are not fully quantified.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable. The continuous-to-discrete bridge is appreciated. Suggestions: give one worked example of a valid kernel/activation pair with explicit Cσ; summarize all assumptions (A0–A3) in one place; be more explicit about the reconstruction operator in Lemma 1 and stability of deconvolution in Lemma 2.

Originality
- Builds meaningfully on scattering, CKNs, and group-equivariance: the main novelty is the multilayer convolutional RKHS formalism with stability bounds applicable beyond wavelets, and the embedding of a CNN class in the induced RKHS. Related work is well-situated.

Significance
- Provides theoretical grounding for invariance/stability in a broader class than scattering, and a rationale for regularizing by RKHS norm in convolutional kernel models. Practical impact would be strengthened by quantitative approximation/stability bounds and empirical support.

Questions for the authors
- Can the (1+n) factor be reduced by leveraging increasing σk or by tightening Lemma 4 per layer?
- Provide an explicit example of σ and kernel with computed Cσ and demonstrate bounded Bn,i under reasonable weight norms.
- Can you quantify the additive stability error introduced by RKHS projections in CKNs?
- How sensitive is Proposition 7 to replacing nk(u) with a fixed or batch-normalized scale?
- For non-compact groups, what are the precise conditions on h and support to ensure stability and invariance?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution generalizing scattering stability to a wider, practically relevant class.
- Clear connection to CNNs via RKHS embedding and norm-based stability/generalization.
- Rigorous, well-motivated operator framework and group-equivariant extension.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of empirical evidence and missing quantitative bounds for projection/discretization errors.
- CNN inclusion relies on a nonstandard homogeneous normalization and smooth activations, limiting direct applicability.
- Some bounds appear loose with respect to depth and constants.

Overall, this is a strong theory paper. Addressing the quantitative gaps and adding minimal empirical validation would substantially strengthen it.
