Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that mirrors the architecture of CNNs and CKNs, and analyzes its invariance and stability properties. Each layer consists of patch extraction, a kernel map into an RKHS using homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling; the composition yields a representation Φn(x) with increasing receptive fields and controlled invariance. The main theoretical result (Theorem 6) establishes Lipschitz stability of Φn to C1 diffeomorphisms: ||Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)|| ≤ [C1(1+n)||∇τ||∞ + C2/σn ||τ||∞] ||x||, matching the classical scattering-style stability notion while allowing more general kernels and architectures. The analysis handles commutators of shifted pooling operators [Lc Aσ, Lτ], extending Mallat’s results. The paper also: (i) discusses discretization and conditions under which feature maps at each layer preserve information and can be recovered (via deconvolution) after subsampling; (ii) shows that kernel approximation via projections (CKN implementation) preserves non-expansiveness and thus stability; (iii) generalizes pooling to locally compact groups to obtain equivariance and global invariance; (iv) links the RKHS HKn induced by the multilayer kernel to a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, bounding the RKHS norm of the resulting predictors (Proposition 7). This connects stability to generalization via standard RKHS margin reasoning.

This work builds on scattering transforms [Bruna & Mallat; Mallat], CKNs [Mairal et al.], group-equivariant CNNs [Cohen & Welling], and group-invariant kernels [Haasdonk & Burkhardt; Mroueh et al.]. Compared to scattering, filters are not fixed wavelets, kernels are broader, and the class of covered CNNs is larger (smooth homogeneous activations). Relative to prior kernel analyses of deep networks [Daniely et al.; Anselmi et al.], the paper provides deformation stability bounds and a constructive multilayer kernel/operator view aligned with practical CNN/CKN components.

Strengths
- Clear and principled operator-theoretic framework that mirrors modern CNNs (convolution, nonlinearity, pooling) while enabling RKHS-based analysis.
- Stability to diffeomorphisms with explicit dependence on local Jacobian and translation magnitude; translation-invariance improving with pooling scale. The commutator bound for shifted pooling [Lc Aσ, Lτ] is a technically useful extension beyond prior scattering analyses.
- Integration of discretization and kernel approximation steps, with non-expansiveness preserving stability; recovery lemmas clarify information preservation across layers and subsampling conditions.
- Group-equivariant pooling design is clean and general; shows how to combine local stability to a subgroup with global invariance to a full group in the last layer.
- Inclusion result for a class of CNNs in the RKHS with a recursive norm bound connects architectural choices and weights to RKHS norms, suggesting a pathway from stability to generalization.
- Writing is generally clear with precise assumptions (A1–A3), and relation to prior work is largely adequate.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Practical gap: no empirical validation (even small-scale) to calibrate constants, confirm deformation-Lipschitz behavior, or illustrate trade-offs with σk, patch sizes, and depth.
- Assumptions may limit applicability to standard CNNs: smooth homogeneous activations exclude ReLU unless smoothed; the normalization nk(u)=||Pk xk−1(u)|| alters the usual forward pass semantics and could introduce implementation subtleties.
- The linear dependence on depth (1+n) in stability can be pessimistic; discussion on whether tighter depth dependence is possible (as in scattering path-length analysis) would help.
- The signal recovery with subsampling relies on deconvolution of Gaussian pooling. This is ill-conditioned in the presence of noise and finite support, and the stability of recovery is not quantified.
- Constants C1, C2 and their dependence on dimensionality, κ, and h are not explicit; guidance on realistic magnitudes is missing.
- The group-invariant construction, while correct, parallels existing treatments; novelty here is mostly the unification within the proposed kernel framework.
- The claim that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization is standard; providing a concrete generalization bound (e.g., via Rademacher complexity in terms of the recursive Bk,i) would strengthen the contribution.

Quality
Technically sound with careful operator bounds and reasonable assumptions (non-expansive kernel maps, Gaussian pooling, bounded patch sizes relative to pooling). Proof sketches align with known harmonic analysis tools (Schur’s test, commutators). Missing are empirical checks and more explicit constants, but the theoretical development is coherent and self-consistent.

Clarity
The paper is well-organized: construction, discretization/approximation, stability, group invariance, and CNN linkage. Some details are deferred to appendices; in the main text, assumptions and takeaways are clear. Minor typos remain (see below). A small figure summarizing the operator pipeline and the role of each assumption would further aid readability.

Originality
The blend of CKN-style RKHS layers with scattering-style deformation analysis and the inclusion of a nontrivial CNN class via RKHS embeddings is novel and timely. The shifted commutator bound and the recursive norm bound for CNNs under specific kernels/activations add value beyond related work.

Significance
Likely to influence theoretical understanding of invariance/stability in deep architectures beyond wavelets, and to inform design/regularization of CKNs and possibly CNNs. The lack of experiments reduces immediate practical impact but does not diminish the theoretical contribution.

Questions for the authors
- Can the inclusion result and stability extend to ReLU via smoothing limits, and do the constants remain controlled?
- How do C1 and C2 scale with dimension d and κ? Any empirical estimates?
- Is the linear-in-depth factor necessary, or can it be replaced by a function of receptive field growth or effective path length?
- In the discrete setting, how stable is the deconvolution-based recovery to noise and finite support? Can you provide a stability constant?
- For the CNN-in-RKHS construction, how sensitive is the norm bound to the choice of kernel (e.g., inverse polynomial vs Gaussian) via Cσ?

Minor comments and typos
- Several spacing issues with L2(Ω,H). “It is then be possible” → “It is then possible.” “discrete analogues of their discrete counterparts” → “discrete analogues of their continuous counterparts.”
- Clarify whether the normalization nk(u) is clipped to avoid division by zero when patches are empty or nearly zero.

Suggestions
- Add a small experimental section: synthetic 1D/2D deformations verifying the bound, and CKN vs CNN robustness under controlled diffeomorphisms as σn varies.
- Provide examples of admissible σ and explicit Cσ for the kernels you advocate, and a short recipe for selecting σk, patch sizes, and κ in practice.
- Discuss links to spectral norm regularization/Parseval networks as practical means to control Lipschitz constants of learned predictors.

Reproducibility
Theoretical results are reproducible given appendices. No code/experiments are needed, but including reference implementations for CKN layers with the proposed normalizations would help.

Overall score: 7/10 (accept)
Confidence: 0.8

Reasons to accept
- Solid, novel theoretical analysis extending scattering-style stability to a broader, practically relevant multilayer kernel/CNN setting.
- Clear unification of invariance, stability, discretization, and group actions within one operator framework.
- Useful inclusion and norm bounds for a class of CNNs.

Reasons to reject
- No empirical validation; constants and practical implications remain abstract.
- Applicability to standard ReLU CNNs is indirect (requires smoothing/normalization).
- Some parts (group invariance design) are incremental relative to prior work.
