Summary
The paper studies invariance and stability of deep convolutional representations through a multilayer convolutional kernel construction. A signal x0 ∈ L2(Ω,H0) is mapped through repeated patch extraction Pk, RKHS kernel maps Mk with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling Ak to produce feature maps xk ∈ L2(Ω,Hk). The representation Φn(x0) = (AnMnPn· · ·A1M1P1)x0 induces a kernel Kn whose RKHS contains linear functionals on Φn. The authors prove stability to C1-diffeomorphisms τ with ‖∇τ‖∞ ≤ 1/2: the deviation ‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ is bounded by a term linear in ‖∇τ‖∞ and a translation term proportional to ‖τ‖∞/σn, similar in spirit to scattering networks. They also discuss discretization (subsampling and deconvolution), kernel approximation via projections (CKNs), group-equivariant patching and pooling for invariance to general groups, and show that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS associated to Kn, with a recursive RKHS norm bound controlling stability and generalization.

Relation to prior work
This builds on scattering (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat), convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.), group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling), and theoretical analyses of deep networks via kernels (Daniely et al., Anselmi et al., Montavon et al.). The novelty is adapting Mallat’s harmonic-analysis techniques to a multilayer kernelized convnet with generic homogeneous kernels and connecting the induced RKHS to (a class of) CNNs.

Strengths
- Clear, modular operator view (Pk/Mk/Ak) that unifies scattering, CKNs, and a subclass of CNNs.
- Stability bound (Theorem 6) with explicit translation-invariance dependence 1/σn and linear dependence on depth, paralleling scattering but for a broader kernel class.
- Group-equivariant construction (Section 4) is clean and compatible with the stability picture.
- The RKHS embedding of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (Proposition 7) is interesting; the recursive norm bound provides a bridge between stability and generalization.
- Discussion of discretization and kernel approximation is thoughtful and connects the theory to CKN practice.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality/technical correctness
- Signal “preservation” (Lemma 1) is stated very strongly. Recovering xk−1 from a discrete sampling of xk after a nonlinear map Mk and smoothing Ak requires (i) inverting Ak (deconvolution), (ii) inverting a pointwise nonlinear embedding via availability of all linear functionals in Hk. The lemma’s condition (“Hk contains linear functions ⟨w,·⟩ with w ∈ Pk”) is not sufficient for stable inversion in the presence of smoothing, finite sampling, or noise, and the reconstruction procedure is not specified. This claim should be qualified as theoretical identifiability under idealized conditions; as written it is misleading.
- Discrete recovery with subsampling (Lemma 2) hinges on deconvolution of a Gaussian. Even with ek ≥ sk, Gaussian deconvolution is notoriously ill-conditioned; “stable when σ is small enough” is vague. A quantitative stability bound (conditioning vs. σ and frequency support) and explicit assumptions on noise are needed.
- Stability bound constants hide critical dependencies. Lemma 4 mentions C1 depends on h and κ, potentially scaling like κd+1. The paper advocates increasing patch and pooling scales with depth; this can inflate κ and thus C1, making the linear-in-n bound pessimistic or vacuous in high dimensions. Please make the κ and d dependence explicit in Theorem 6.
- Assumption (A3) ties patch radius to σk−1 via κ; the practical guidance “σk and supc∈Sk|c| of the same order” should be formalized. What regimes of σ growth preserve nontrivial stability while not annihilating signal energy?
- Norm non-preservation is downplayed. While “natural images” have 1/f2 spectra, the analysis does not quantify energy loss across layers, nor how the proposed “top Gaussian kernel” normalization interacts with preceding layers. The statement “norm preservation may be less relevant” should be supported by a precise result (the current inequality ‖Φ(x)‖ ≥ ‖An···A0x‖ is trivial).
- CNN-to-RKHS embedding (Proposition 7) depends on a homogenized activation z → ‖z‖σ(⟨g,z⟩/‖z‖). For ReLU, this reduces to the standard activation, but the paper ultimately uses a smoothed variant. The empirical gap between standard ReLU CNNs and the smoothed/homogenized model is not assessed. Moreover, the recursive constants Bk,i can blow up with width and filter norms; without concrete bounds this result provides little practical control.
- The treatment of kernel approximation via projections (Section 3.2) asserts that Theorem 6 “remains valid,” but only sketches that non-expansiveness is preserved. A formal statement (with an approximation error term per layer) is needed for the finite-dimensional CKN instantiation.

Originality
- The stability framework and many proof techniques are inherited from scattering; the group-equivariant pooling is in line with established work. The substantive novelty is (i) handling general homogeneous dot-product kernels and (ii) the RKHS containment result for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations. These are meaningful, but feel incremental without sharper constants or new qualitative phenomena.

Significance
- If sharpened, the results could inform regularization in CKNs and provide a unifying lens on invariance/stability in CNNs. As is, the lack of empirical validation and opaque constants limit practical impact. The group-invariance section is mainly a restatement in this framework.

Clarity and presentation
- Heavy reliance on appendices and an external “extended version” for key proofs undermines self-containment. The main text should at least sketch proofs of Lemma 1, Lemma 2, and Proposition 7 beyond high-level claims.
- Notational overload and inconsistencies: “L 2” vs “L2”; “R d”; mixing ‖·‖ for Hilbert, L2, and operator norms is confusing despite the note; define e−1/2_k normalization in discrete patch extraction more clearly; H̄ ek k–1 notation is awkward; use consistent subscripts/superscripts.
- Several typos/formatting glitches: “is then be possible,” “discusssed,” “st Conference…,” missing years/venues/page ranges in references, stray characters (“R R,” “H̄ ek k–1”), inconsistent equation punctuation, and occasional missing commas.
- The link between discrete and continuous parameters (Section 2.1) could use a concise table; currently it’s easy to lose track of ek, sk, σk scaling.

Missing/under-cited related work
- The paper should discuss contemporaneous mathematical analyses of CNN stability beyond scattering (e.g., early versions of Wiatowski & Bölcskei’s theory of deep CNNs were on arXiv in 2017), and spectral norm/Lipschitz control literature more broadly.

Pros
- Clean, modular operator framework.
- Extends scattering-style stability to a broader class of kernels and to a CNN subclass via RKHS.
- Conceptually unifies invariance/stability and margin-based generalization.

Cons
- Overstated “signal preservation” and recovery claims; deconvolution stability not quantified.
- Hidden and potentially unfavorable constants; dependence on κ, d, widths not explicit.
- No experiments or numerical illustrations; practical implications unclear.
- Incomplete proofs in the main text and reliance on external appendix.
- Numerous presentation and formatting issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The ideas are promising, but the accumulation of technical gaps, opaque constants, and lack of empirical support outweighs the conceptual contribution in its current form.

Requests for revision
- Provide explicit constants in Theorem 6 (dependence on κ, d, σk schedule).
- Qualify and precisely state the conditions for Lemma 1 and Lemma 2 with stability bounds.
- Formalize the projection/approximation stability with error accumulation across layers.
- Clarify the CNN class captured; quantify the gap to standard ReLU CNNs.
- Improve writing/notation and fix reference formatting; include at least small-scale experiments validating the stability bounds for CKNs/CNNs.
