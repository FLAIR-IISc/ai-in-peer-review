

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper proposes a kernel framework for studying invariance and stability properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and demonstrates stability to diffeomorphisms similar to the scattering transform [17], but for more standard CNN architectures. The paper connects this kernel framework to traditional CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, showing that stability properties translate to predictive models through RKHS norm control.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a theoretically rigorous framework for understanding invariance in CNNs, which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep learning. The connection between stability, generalization, and RKHS norms is well-motivated and potentially valuable for future research. The paper also carefully distinguishes its approach from the scattering transform by incorporating learning into the framework.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the theoretical interest, the paper contains several significant technical inconsistencies and presentation issues that undermine its credibility:

1. **Notational inconsistency and confusion:** The paper uses "‖ · ‖" to denote multiple different norms (Hilbert space, L2, operator norms) without clear distinction. For example, in equation (1), it writes "‖f‖H‖ϕ(z)− ϕ(z ′)‖H" with inconsistent subscripting. This is confusing for readers and suggests a lack of attention to mathematical precision.

2. **Contradiction in activation functions:** Section 5 claims that their framework applies to CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, but then states that ReLU (the most common activation) is homogeneous. However, ReLU is not smooth, and the paper explicitly requires smoothness for their RKHSs (Lemma B.2). The paper acknowledges this contradiction but fails to provide a concrete example of a smooth homogeneous activation actually used in practice that satisfies their conditions.

3. **Mathematical errors:** In the pooling operator definition (equation for xk(u)), the scaling is incorrect: hσk(u) = σ^(-d)_k h(u/σk) but the convolution is written as ∫ hσk(u-v)MkPkxk-1(v)dv, which would imply h((u-v)/σk) rather than h((u-v)/σk)/σk^d. This is a critical error in the formulation of the convolution operation.

4. **Missing technical details:** The paper claims that "the resulting architecture of CKNs resembles traditional CNNs" but fails to provide any comparison of computational complexity or practical implementation details. The connection to traditional CNNs is theoretical but lacks concrete examples of how this framework would be implemented.

5. **Formatting and presentation issues:** The paper has inconsistent spacing around mathematical symbols (e.g., "‖x‖ 2" vs "‖x‖^2"), and uses inconsistent notation for the same concepts (e.g., referring to input signals as both "x0" and "x"). The reference to "Appendix A" without providing the appendix is a formatting error that should have been corrected before submission.

6. **Unjustified assumptions:** The paper assumes "‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2" without explaining why this condition is necessary or how restrictive it is for practical deformations. This condition is critical for the stability results but is presented without justification.

7. **Lack of experimental validation:** While the paper is theoretical, it fails to provide even a simple synthetic example to illustrate the claimed stability properties. This is particularly problematic given the paper's focus on practical implications for CNNs.

**Conclusion:** While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework, the numerous technical inconsistencies, mathematical errors, and presentation issues significantly undermine its credibility. The contradiction regarding activation functions is particularly problematic, as it directly challenges the main claim that the framework applies to standard CNNs. The mathematical errors in the pooling operator definition are serious enough to make the entire theoretical analysis questionable.

The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues, but as submitted, it does not meet NIPS's standards for technical rigor. The minor flaws, when considered collectively, are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The paper's contribution is interesting, but its execution is flawed to the point that the results cannot be trusted.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The technical inconsistencies and errors are too numerous and fundamental to be considered minor flaws. The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before being suitable for publication at NIPS.
