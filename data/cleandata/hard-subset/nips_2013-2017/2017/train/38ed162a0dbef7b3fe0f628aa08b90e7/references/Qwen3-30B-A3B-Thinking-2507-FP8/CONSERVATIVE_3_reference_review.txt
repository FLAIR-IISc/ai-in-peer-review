

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper proposes a theoretical framework using positive definite kernels to study invariance and stability properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors extend Mallat's scattering transform framework to more standard CNN architectures by introducing a multilayer convolutional kernel construction. They claim to provide theoretical guarantees for translation-invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms, and connect these results to both convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions.

The paper builds on existing work in the area, particularly Mallat's scattering transform [17] and Mairal's convolutional kernel networks [15,16]. The mathematical derivation of the stability results appears technically sound, with proofs provided in the appendix. The authors establish a connection between the RKHS norm of the representation and the Lipschitz stability of the predictive model, which is a theoretically interesting contribution.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear theoretical framework for understanding invariance properties of CNNs.
- The mathematical derivations are rigorous and well-structured.
- The connection between the RKHS norm and stability/generalization is conceptually valuable.
- The paper carefully addresses the relationship between continuous and discrete representations, which is important for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**
- The most significant weakness is the lack of empirical validation. The authors make strong theoretical claims about the stability properties of standard CNNs, but provide no experiments to demonstrate that these theoretical properties translate to practical improvements in model performance or generalization. Without empirical evidence, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of the theoretical results.
- The connection to standard CNNs is tenuous. The authors acknowledge that standard activation functions like ReLU don't satisfy the smooth homogeneous conditions required by their framework, and they rely on smoothed variants that aren't commonly used in practice. The paper doesn't demonstrate how these theoretical insights would actually improve standard CNN implementations.
- The paper doesn't compare against established baselines or demonstrate how their framework would lead to better-performing models. The claim that "stability may lead to improved sample complexity" is purely theoretical without supporting evidence.
- The paper appears to be primarily theoretical with limited practical implications. While theoretical insights are valuable, NIPS typically prioritizes work that demonstrates both theoretical rigor and practical impact.

**Major Concerns:**
The paper's main contribution is theoretical, but it fails to address the critical gap between theory and practice. The authors claim their framework applies to "a large class of CNNs," but they don't provide any evidence that this framework actually leads to better models or that the theoretical guarantees translate to improved performance. This is a significant weakness for a NIPS submission, which typically values both theoretical and empirical rigor.

The paper also doesn't adequately address why their kernel framework provides advantages over existing approaches for studying CNN properties. The connection to CKNs is well-established in [15,16], and the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how their extension provides new insights beyond what's already known from Mallat's work on scattering transforms [17].

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution to understanding CNN invariance properties, but it falls short of the empirical validation expected for NIPS submissions. The theoretical framework is interesting, but without experimental evidence demonstrating its practical relevance, the contribution is limited in scope. The authors make claims about how their framework could improve CNN performance without providing any evidence to support these claims.

I recommend rejection. The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors provided empirical results demonstrating how their theoretical insights translate to improved CNN performance on standard benchmarks. Without such evidence, the paper remains a theoretical curiosity without clear practical significance for the NIPS community. The field would benefit more from work that bridges theory and practice, rather than theoretical frameworks that aren't validated against standard baselines or real-world applications.

**Pro/Con Summary:**
Pro: Rigorous theoretical framework, clear mathematical derivations, important theoretical connection between stability and generalization.
Con: No empirical validation, tenuous connection to standard CNNs, no comparison to established baselines, lacks practical relevance.

The paper's theoretical contribution is valuable, but the absence of empirical evidence to support the practical implications of the theory makes it difficult to assess its significance for the NIPS community. For a paper to be accepted at NIPS, it should demonstrate either significant theoretical novelty with strong connections to practice, or substantial empirical results that advance the state of the art. This paper lacks the latter, and the former is not sufficiently compelling without empirical validation.
