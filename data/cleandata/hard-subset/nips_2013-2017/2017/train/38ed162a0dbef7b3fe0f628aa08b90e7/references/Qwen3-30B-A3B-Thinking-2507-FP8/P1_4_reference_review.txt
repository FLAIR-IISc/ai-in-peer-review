

# Peer Review: "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

## Summary
This paper investigates the theoretical properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16] and demonstrates how this framework can represent a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The paper establishes translation-invariance and stability properties under diffeomorphisms, showing that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization. This work builds on the scattering transform [5, 17] but extends it to more standard CNN architectures within a kernel framework, providing theoretical guarantees that were previously limited to pre-defined filter networks.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with rigorous proofs. The authors carefully define their framework, establish necessary assumptions (A1-A3), and prove stability bounds that match the desired notion in Eq. (4). The connection between stability and generalization through RKHS norm control is particularly compelling. The paper also addresses practical considerations, including discretization and kernel approximation, showing how theoretical results translate to practical implementations like CKNs.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging the gap between kernel methods and CNNs. The key novelty lies in demonstrating that the RKHS defined by their kernel construction contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, extending beyond the specific CKN architecture. This provides a unified theoretical framework for understanding invariance properties across different convolutional architectures, which is novel compared to previous work on scattering networks [5, 17] or group-invariant CNNs [9, 29].

**Significance:** The theoretical results have clear implications for understanding why CNNs work well in practice and for developing better regularization strategies. By connecting stability to generalization through the RKHS norm, the paper provides a principled way to control model complexity, potentially leading to improved sample complexity. This is particularly relevant given the growing interest in understanding the theoretical properties of deep learning models.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity:** While the paper is well-organized, the mathematical notation is dense and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis. Some key concepts (like the precise relationship between the continuous and discrete representations) could be clarified with additional examples or a more detailed discussion. The connection between the theoretical framework and practical CNN implementations could be made more explicit.

**Scope:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical analysis and does not include empirical validation. While theoretical work is valuable for NIPS, the lack of experiments to demonstrate the practical implications of their stability results (e.g., comparing generalization performance of stable vs. unstable models) is a notable limitation. The connection to actual CNNs is established theoretically, but without empirical evidence, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of these theoretical insights.

**Assumptions:** The stability results depend on several strong assumptions (e.g., ‖∇τ‖∞ ≤ 1/2, specific kernel choices satisfying (A1-A3)). The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss how restrictive these assumptions are in practice or provide guidance on how to handle violations. The authors acknowledge that the norm is not preserved due to pooling layers, but the practical implications of this limitation aren't fully explored.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the invariance properties of convolutional representations. The paper makes a clear, novel connection between kernel methods and CNNs, providing a theoretical framework that unifies several previous approaches. The technical quality is high, with careful proofs and thoughtful discussion of limitations.

The main weakness is the lack of empirical validation to demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical results. While theoretical work is valuable at NIPS, the absence of experiments makes it difficult to fully assess the significance of the findings. The paper would be significantly strengthened by at least one empirical study demonstrating how the stability properties translate to improved generalization in practice.

## Recommendation

**Accept** (with minor revisions)

The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that addresses an important question in deep learning theory. The connection between kernel methods and CNNs is novel and well-executed. While the lack of empirical results is a limitation, the theoretical rigor and significance of the results justify acceptance. The authors should consider adding a brief empirical validation in the revision to strengthen the practical relevance of their findings.

## Pro/Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding invariance in convolutional architectures
- Successfully connects kernel methods with CNNs through a unified framework
- Demonstrates how stability relates to generalization via RKHS norm control
- Extends previous work on scattering networks to more general CNN architectures

**Con:**
- Lacks empirical validation to demonstrate practical significance
- Some technical assumptions may be restrictive in practice
- Discrete implementation details could be clarified for broader accessibility

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of CNNs, and with minor revisions (particularly adding a brief empirical validation), it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
