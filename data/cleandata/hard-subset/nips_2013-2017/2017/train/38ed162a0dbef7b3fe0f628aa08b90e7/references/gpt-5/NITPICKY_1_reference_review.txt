Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals defined on continuous domains and studies its invariance to translations and stability to small diffeomorphisms. The construction mirrors CNNs: each layer extracts patches, maps them into an RKHS via a homogeneous dot-product kernel, and pools with a Gaussian. The representation is shown to have (i) stability bounds analogous to scattering transforms (dependence on ‖∇τ‖∞ and 1/σn), (ii) a mechanism to encode invariance to general groups by using group-covariant patch extraction and pooling, and (iii) a functional connection to a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, with norm-based bounds for the resulting RKHS function. Practical aspects such as discretization and kernel approximation (CKNs) are discussed, with claims that the theoretical stability extends to these approximations.

Strengths
- Clear and principled operator-theoretic framework linking convolution, kernels, and pooling; the commutator-based proof structure is elegant and aligned with Mallat’s scattering analysis.
- The stability bound in Theorem 6 is intuitive, isolates the roles of ‖∇τ‖∞ and σn, and recovers translation invariance as σn grows.
- The group-equivariant construction is clean and consistent with established harmonic analysis on groups.
- The bridge to CNNs via RKHS embeddings and norm control is appealing for connecting stability and generalization, and aligns with the CKN literature.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Overclaiming “signal preservation.” Lemma 1 asserts recovery of xk−1 from samples of xk = AkMkPkxk−1 when patches cover Ω. With continuous Gaussian pooling (low-pass), exact recovery requires deconvolution. The Gaussian convolution is invertible on L2 but with an unbounded inverse; after sampling, invertibility further requires bandlimiting/Nyquist conditions. As stated, the lemma ignores ill-posedness and sampling constraints, and seems incorrect without strong stability and bandlimit assumptions.
- Discretization inconsistencies. Lemma 2 relies on deconvolution with a discrete Gaussian. The text claims stability “when its scale parameter … is small enough,” which is counterintuitive: deconvolution is typically better conditioned for stronger low-pass (larger σ relative to sampling), not smaller. Moreover, the condition ek ≥ sk is insufficient for stable inversion; spectral conditions on the discrete filter are needed.
- Assumptions on the kernel mapping’s non-expansiveness (A2) are only deferred to Appendix B. For dot-product kernels K(z,z′)=‖z‖‖z′‖κ(⟨z,z′⟩/(‖z‖‖z′‖)), ‖ϕ(z)‖=‖z‖ follows from κ(1)=1, but the metric contraction dK(z,z′) ≤ ‖z−z′‖ requires nontrivial constraints on κ (e.g., smoothness and second derivative bounds). These conditions should be made explicit in the main text; otherwise the stability result hinges on opaque assumptions.
- Scope of the “CNNs contained in the RKHS.” The CNNs considered include a nonstandard, input-dependent normalization nk(u)=‖Pkzk−1(u)‖ inside the activation (Eq. 8). This is a constrained class and materially different from standard CNNs with ReLU/bounded activations and no such scaling. While the text notes homogenization is “not standard,” the abstract and conclusions strongly suggest general CNNs are covered, which overstates the contribution.
- Constants and depth dependence. Theorem 6 has a (1+n) factor, but constants also depend on κ and dimensionality; κ can grow with patch radii relative to σk−1. Although (A3) keeps supc∈Sk|c| ≤ κσk−1, the discussion does not quantify how κ propagates through layers, nor how C1 scales with κ (mentioned qualitatively). This weakens the practical interpretability of depth dependence.
- Missing empirical validation. Given reliance on kernel approximations and discretization, even small-scale experiments to illustrate the stability bound (e.g., deformation robustness vs. σn and n) for CKNs/CNN surrogates would substantially strengthen the paper. As is, claims about “practical” discretization and approximation are largely untested.
- Presentation and rigor gaps:
  - Proposition 3: The telescoping/commutator bound is plausible, but the proof sketch omits norm control across nonexpansive Mk and Ak compositions; a clear derivation in the main text would help.
  - The passage from continuous to discrete models elides border effects and ignores the necessity of anti-aliasing conditions beyond A0.
  - The global group-invariance section is high-level; conditions on h (e.g., integrability, localization) and on G (unimodularity, use of left vs. right Haar) should be spelled out. For non-commutative G, clarify the role of left- vs. right-convolutions.

Quality
Technically promising, but several core claims (signal recovery under pooling and sampling; stability of deconvolution; non-expansiveness of kernel mappings) are under-specified or, as stated, incorrect. The central stability theorem is credible contingent on (A1–A3), but those assumptions are too vaguely justified in the main text.

Clarity
Generally readable, but marred by numerous typos and notational inconsistencies: “L 2” vs “L2”, “HKn” vs “HKn”, missing subscripts (e.g., L2(Ω,Hk)), grammatical errors (“is then be possible”), and an odd footnote (“st Conference…”). Equations could be better motivated, and key assumptions should be stated in the main text, not deferred.

Originality
The stability analysis closely follows scattering transforms, extending from wavelet filters to homogeneous kernel maps and pooling. The group section echoes known group-equivariant constructions. The RKHS view of certain CNNs is aligned with prior CKN work. The combination is useful but not radically new.

Significance
Potentially valuable for theoretical understanding of CKNs and a subset of CNNs, but the current form limits impact due to the restricted CNN class and unsettled discretization/approximation issues.

Specific suggestions
- Fix Lemma 1 and Lemma 2: add bandlimit assumptions, explicit sampling rates, and conditioning analyses for deconvolution; otherwise weaken to information preservation without sampling or state ill-posedness.
- Move explicit sufficient conditions on κ for (A2) to the main text, with examples (Gaussian/RBF on spheres, inverse polynomial kernel) and Lipschitz constants.
- Calibrate constants in Theorem 6, showing dependence on dimension, κ, and kernel smoothness.
- Clarify the relation to standard CNNs: state precisely the class covered, and avoid implying coverage of generic CNNs with ReLU.
- Provide simple experiments demonstrating deformation stability vs σn and n for CKNs.
- Thorough pass on notation, grammar, and references (years, venues, consistent formatting).

Pros
- Solid operator-theoretic framework; clear decomposition into patching, kernel map, and pooling.
- Stability result structurally mirrors known scattering bounds and extends to a broader class.
- Clean group-equivariant formulation.

Cons
- Questionable “signal preservation” claims post-pooling/sampling; discretization section has conceptual errors.
- Overclaims about inclusion of “CNNs”; actual class is restricted by homogenized activations.
- Heavy reliance on appendices for key assumptions; limited clarity and no empirical support.
- Numerous minor formatting and notation issues.

Recommendation
Reject. While the direction is interesting and parts of the analysis are sound, the accumulation of technical and presentation issues (especially around reconstruction, discretization, kernel assumptions, and overclaiming) undermines confidence. A revised version addressing the above concerns could be much stronger.
