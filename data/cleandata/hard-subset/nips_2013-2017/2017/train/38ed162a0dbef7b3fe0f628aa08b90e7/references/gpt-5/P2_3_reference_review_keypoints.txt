- Paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework mirroring CNNs and generalizing Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), yielding an RKHS containing CKNs and a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations.  
- Main theoretical results: stability to C1-diffeomorphisms with Mallat-style Lipschitz bounds; deformation term ∝ ‖∇τ‖∞ and translation-invariance improves with final pooling scale σn.  
- Stability bound grows linearly with depth (factor ≈ 1 + n); intermediate layers are equivariant and global invariance mainly induced by final pooling.  
- Discretization and subsampling can preserve signal information under mild conditions (ek ≥ sk), supporting practical CNN/CKN-like architectures.  
- Group-equivariant layers are constructed via patch extraction and pooling on locally compact groups; full invariance achieved by pooling on the group at the last layer.  
- Constructive inclusion: CNN predictors with smooth homogeneous activations and specific normalization belong to the RKHS, linking RKHS norm to Lipschitz stability and generalization.  
- Extends Mallat’s scattering stability analysis to a broader, learnable, kernelized multilayer convolutional framework connecting kernels, CKNs, and a class of CNNs.  
- Technical analysis is careful and uses standard tools (Schur’s test, commutators, harmonic analysis); assumptions are explicit and motivated.  
- Theoretical contributions (Theorem 6 and lemmas) are clearly articulated; proofs are in appendices and discretization arguments show stability survives projection steps.  
- Limitations: kernel maps must be homogeneous dot-product kernels and included CNN class requires smooth homogeneous activations plus a normalization factor differing from standard practice.  
- ReLU is only handled via a smoothed variant, limiting direct applicability to standard activations.  
- Linear growth with depth may be conservative; constants (e.g., dependence on κ^(d+1)) could be large in high dimensions.  
- The representation norm is not preserved (unlike scattering); authors use energy arguments and an optional final Gaussian map to mitigate this.  
- Signal recovery under subsampling depends on stable deconvolution and ek ≥ sk; practical robustness of this step is discussed qualitatively, not quantified.  
- Submission is purely theoretical; lack of empirical validation (even small-scale) weakens practical claims.  
- Paper is well organized and notation is standard though heavy; assumptions and parameter-constant dependencies are discussed but could benefit from summary tables.  
- Minor clarity suggestions: clarify gap between normalized activation form and conventional CNNs, role/necessity of A0, and mapping of discrete to continuous parameters for d > 1.  
- Originality: nontrivial extension of scattering results, commutator bounds for shifted integral operators, discretization/subsampling treatment, and RKHS inclusion for a CNN class.  
- Significance: offers principled guidance for designing invariant/stable convolutional representations and links RKHS norms to robustness and regularization.  
- Practical impact is promising but indirect because the included CNN class differs from standard nets; empirical validation would aid adoption.  
- Pros for acceptance: rigorous theoretical generalization of scattering, unifying framework linking kernels/CKNs/CNNs, group-equivariant construction, and continuous-to-discrete treatment.  
- Cons against acceptance: no experiments, restrictive activation and normalization assumptions, potentially loose stability constants, and reliance on unquantified deconvolution stability.  
- Suggested questions: can the (1 + n) depth factor be improved; which kernels best balance (A1–A2) and capacity; sensitivity of RKHS norms to kernel projection quality; provide simple controlled stability experiments.  
- Recommendation: leaning accept (theory track) — strong theoretical contribution but would benefit from empirical validation.  
- Reviewer confidence: high for theory/prior work, moderate for practical implications.
