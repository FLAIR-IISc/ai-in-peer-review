This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The core of the work is the construction of a multilayer convolutional kernel for signals defined on a continuous domain. This construction, which generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), involves a sequence of patch extraction, kernel mapping, and linear pooling operations. The authors leverage this framework to prove that the resulting representation is stable to small C1-diffeomorphisms and approximately invariant to translations. This analysis is heavily inspired by the theory of scattering transforms developed by Mallat, extending similar stability guarantees to a broader class of architectures that can incorporate learning. A key contribution is establishing a formal link between the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by this kernel and a class of standard CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This connection allows the stability results to be applied directly to these CNNs, showing that their stability is controlled by their RKHS norm, thereby linking stability to generalization.

**Strengths**

1.  **Rigorous Theoretical Framework:** The paper provides a solid and elegant mathematical framework for studying the geometric properties of convolutional architectures. By grounding the analysis in the theory of reproducing kernels and borrowing tools from harmonic analysis, the authors derive precise, non-asymptotic bounds on the stability of the representation to deformations. This moves beyond intuitive arguments and provides a formal basis for understanding these crucial properties.

2.  **Generality and Connection to CNNs:** A major strength is the successful bridging of theory and practice. The framework is more general than the scattering transform, as it is not tied to predefined filters. More importantly, the paper establishes a clear connection showing that a class of CNNs are functions within the constructed RKHS (Proposition 7). This is a significant result, as it makes the theoretical stability analysis directly relevant to a subset of widely used deep learning models, offering a principled explanation for their robustness.

3.  **Clear and Insightful Results:** The main result (Theorem 6) cleanly separates the effects of local deformations and global translations. It shows that stability to local deformations (`||∇τ||∞`) is an aggregate property of the layers, while invariance to global translations (`||τ||∞`) is primarily governed by the scale of the final pooling layer (`σn`). This provides clear, actionable insights into architectural design—for instance, that increasing the final pooling receptive field enhances translation invariance. The extension to general group invariances in Section 4 is also a valuable conceptual contribution.

4.  **High Clarity and Quality of Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The construction of the multilayer kernel is introduced logically and methodically. The assumptions are stated clearly, and the progression from the continuous theoretical model to its discrete, practical counterparts (CKNs) is well-handled. This makes a technically dense topic accessible.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Standard CNN Practice:** While the connection to CNNs is a major strength, it relies on assumptions that deviate from common practice. The analysis requires smooth, homogeneous activation functions, whereas the most common activation, ReLU, is non-smooth. The authors propose a smoothed variant, but the implications of this discrepancy are not fully explored. Furthermore, the CNN formulation in Eq. (8) includes an explicit normalization term `nk(u) = ||Pkxk-1(u)||`, which is not standard. While related to some normalization schemes, it would be beneficial to discuss this difference more thoroughly.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the theoretical contributions are strong on their own, the absence of any experiments leaves some questions open. For example, empirical validation could have demonstrated the trade-off between the final pooling scale `σn` and translation invariance on a simple task. An experiment measuring the stability of a trained CNN to small deformations and correlating it with the derived RKHS norm bound would have made the paper's claims even more compelling.

3.  **Nuance in Signal Preservation Claims:** Lemma 1 and 2 state that the signal can be recovered at each layer. However, this recovery relies on deconvolution, which is an ill-posed problem and can be numerically unstable if the pooling filter has zeros in its frequency response. While the authors briefly allude to this, the claim that information is "preserved" could be stated with more nuance. The pooling operators inherently act as low-pass filters and discard high-frequency information, so the invertibility is conditional and may not be robust.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   To strengthen the connection to practical CNNs, the authors could add a discussion on the potential for extending their analysis to non-smooth activations like ReLU, perhaps by leveraging different mathematical tools. It would also be helpful to elaborate on the relationship between the `nk(u)` normalization term and existing techniques like Layer Normalization or weight normalization to better situate the theoretical model.
*   Even a simple, illustrative experiment on a toy dataset would significantly enhance the paper's impact. For instance, visualizing the output change of a simple network under translations for different final pooling sizes (`σn`) could provide a powerful visual confirmation of Theorem 6.
*   The discussion on signal preservation could be clarified. Rephrasing from "signal preservation" to "invertibility under specific conditions" might be more accurate. A brief comment on the stability of the deconvolution process, particularly in relation to the properties of the pooling filter `h`, would be valuable.
*   The RKHS norm bound in Proposition 7 is given recursively. It would be insightful to provide a brief interpretation of this bound. For example, does it imply that networks with smaller filter norms (as encouraged by L2 regularization) are inherently more stable, thus connecting regularization, stability, and generalization through the lens of this framework?

**Summary of Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, and the theoretical development is thorough and rigorous.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is very well-written and easy to follow despite its technical nature.
*   **Originality:** High. The work originally synthesizes ideas from scattering transforms and convolutional kernel networks to provide a novel theoretical perspective on the stability and invariance of modern CNNs.
*   **Significance:** High. The paper addresses a fundamental and important question: why do convolutional networks work so well? It provides a compelling theoretical answer rooted in stability to deformations, linking architecture, robustness, and generalization. These ideas are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.

Despite the minor weaknesses regarding the gap with standard practice and the lack of experiments, the theoretical contributions are substantial, novel, and significant. This is a very strong paper that provides valuable insights into the functioning of deep convolutional networks. I recommend acceptance.
