- Paper constructs a multilayer convolutional kernel (CKN) that generalizes prior CKNs and many standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations.  
- Shows the kernel-derived RKHS has invariance and Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms, analogous to the scattering transform.  
- Demonstrates stability results translate to predictive models, linking RKHS norm–based stability to potential improvements in generalization and sample complexity.  
- Builds on scattering transform theory, convolutional kernel networks, group invariance, and kernel methods for deep learning, situating the work well in prior literature.  
- Theoretical rigor: provides a solid, formal analysis of CNN invariance and stability using kernel methods.  
- Generalization of frameworks: unifies and extends CKNs and a broad class of CNNs for analysis.  
- Connects stability to generalization, suggesting principled regularization approaches for CNNs.  
- Clear presentation and mostly consistent notation despite mathematical complexity.  
- Thorough and appropriate related-work discussion.  
- Practical implications unclear: implementing the full kernel framework may be computationally prohibitive and under-discussed.  
- Requirement of smooth homogeneous activations is limiting; ReLU’s non-smoothness raises applicability concerns.  
- Limited empirical validation: primarily theoretical with no small-scale experiments to demonstrate practical benefits.  
- Strong assumptions (A1–A3) on kernel operators (norm preservation, non-expansiveness) may be restrictive; sensitivity to violations is not analyzed.  
- Discretization treatment feels rushed with hand-wavy parameter choices and insufficient analysis of its effect on stability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel rigorous analysis, valuable stability–generalization insights, meaningful generalization of CKNs, and good exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of practical validation/cost discussion, restrictive activation assumptions, and potential difficulty translating theory into practical CNN improvements.  
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution on CNN invariance/stability but weakened by limited empirical support and restrictive assumptions.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; reviewer requests discussion of computational costs, RKHS vs practical regularization, a small empirical study, sensitivity to A1–A3, and more detailed discretization discussion.  
- Reviewer confidence: 7/10.
