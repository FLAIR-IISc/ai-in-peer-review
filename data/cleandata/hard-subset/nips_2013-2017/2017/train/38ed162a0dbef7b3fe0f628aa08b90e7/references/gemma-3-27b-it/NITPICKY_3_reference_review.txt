This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels, aiming to provide a more principled understanding of invariance and stability. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate stability guarantees similar to those of the scattering transform, while extending the analysis to a broader class of CNNs. 

The paper is meticulously written and demonstrates a strong technical foundation. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the connection to existing literature – scattering transforms, convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), and kernel methods – is clearly articulated. The effort to extend existing theoretical results to more general CNN architectures is commendable. However, despite the technical soundness, the presentation suffers from a degree of density and the practical implications remain somewhat obscured. My overall assessment is that while the paper represents a solid contribution, several minor yet persistent flaws prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed mathematical analysis of the proposed kernel construction and its properties.
*   **Extension of Existing Work:** The authors successfully extend theoretical tools from scattering transforms to a more general class of CNNs and the CKN framework.
*   **Clear Connection to Kernel Methods:** The framework provides a novel perspective on CNNs through the established theory of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS).
*   **Well-Referenced:** The related work section is comprehensive and demonstrates awareness of relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density and Readability:** The paper is densely written, making it difficult to follow the mathematical arguments without significant effort. The notation, while standard, is used extensively, leading to a cognitive load. A more accessible presentation, perhaps with more intermediate steps and intuitive explanations, would significantly improve clarity.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the theoretical results are compelling, the paper lacks any empirical validation. Demonstrating the practical relevance of these theoretical guarantees through experiments (even on synthetic data) would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Discretization Details:** While the authors address discretization (Section 2.1), the discussion feels rushed and lacks sufficient detail. For instance, the connection between the patch size in continuous vs. discrete domains isn’t fully elaborated, making it hard to assess the impact of discretization on the stability guarantees. 
*   **Homogeneous Activation Assumption:** The reliance on "smooth homogeneous activations" (Section 5) feels restrictive. While acknowledging ReLU and its smoothed variants, the limitation to this class of activations weakens the generality of the results. A more detailed discussion of the implications of relaxing this assumption is warranted.
*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:** There are several minor inconsistencies and imprecise statements. For example, in Section 3.1, the discussion of signal preservation mentions that it requires kernels containing linear functions, yet Appendix B doesn’t explicitly demonstrate this for *all* kernels listed. Also, Lemma 1 states signal recovery from *sampling*, while Lemma 2 focuses on *subsampling* – clarifying the interplay between these concepts would be beneficial.
*   **Formatting issues:** The first author affiliation is not standard NIPS format.

**Specific Points:**

*   **Equation (1):** The statement regarding “reasonably small norm” is vague. A more precise characterization of what constitutes a “reasonably small norm” would be useful.
*   **Lemma 4:** The constant C1 in Lemma 4 depends on κ, but the precise dependence is not stated.
*   **Section 4:** The discussion of group invariance feels underdeveloped. While the general idea is clear, a concrete example illustrating how to adapt the patch extraction and pooling operators for a specific group (e.g., rotations) would be beneficial.
*   **Appendix A:** Schur's test is mentioned but not fully explained. A brief reminder of the theorem itself would be helpful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of CNNs.
*   Extends existing theoretical frameworks in a meaningful way.
*   Potentially provides a foundation for developing more principled regularization techniques.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is dense and lacks accessibility.
*   Absence of empirical validation.
*   Restrictive assumptions (e.g., homogeneous activations).
*   Minor technical inconsistencies and imprecise statements.
*   Formatting issues

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its strengths, the paper's density, lack of empirical support, and minor technical flaws prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The contributions are valuable, but the presentation and the absence of validation limit their impact. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit a revised version. A focus on improving clarity, providing empirical evidence, and addressing the technical inconsistencies would significantly strengthen the paper. I also recommend adhering to the NIPS formatting guidelines.
