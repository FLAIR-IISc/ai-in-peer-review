This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate that it encompasses a broad class of CNNs, including convolutional kernel networks (CKNs). They then analyze the translation-invariance and stability of this kernel representation to diffeomorphisms, achieving guarantees comparable to the scattering transform, but for a wider range of architectures. A key finding is that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization, suggesting a link between these properties and improved sample complexity.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question regarding the geometry of functional spaces inhabited by CNNs. It builds upon prior work in kernel methods (CKNs), scattering transforms, and harmonic analysis, clearly positioning itself within the existing literature. The authors correctly identify the limitations of viewing CNNs as "black boxes" and strive for a more principled understanding of their behavior. The extension of results from scattering transforms to more general CNN architectures is a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a mathematically sound analysis, leveraging concepts from kernel theory, harmonic analysis, and functional spaces. The proofs appear well-structured and complete (though detailed examination requires careful scrutiny of the appendix).
*   **Generality:** The framework encompasses a wide class of CNNs beyond CKNs, making the results more broadly applicable.
*   **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper effectively relates its contributions to prior research, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.
*   **Insightful Link between Stability and Generalization:** The connection drawn between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization potential is a significant theoretical result.
*   **Addresses Discretization:** The discussion on discretization and ensuring signal preservation during the transition from continuous to discrete representations is practical and important.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity and Accessibility:** The mathematical notation and concepts used are quite advanced. While rigorous, this makes the paper less accessible to readers not deeply familiar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is primarily theoretical, lacking extensive empirical validation. Demonstrating the practical implications of the theoretical results on real-world datasets would strengthen the work. While acknowledging the work extends to CKNs, no empirical results on that architecture are shown.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumptions made regarding smoothness and homogeneity of activation functions and the regularity of diffeomorphisms might be restrictive. A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the theoretical framework is established, the practical computational cost of working with the kernel representation could be high, especially for large-scale problems. This is not directly addressed.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the extension to more general CNNs is valuable, the core ideas build heavily on existing work, particularly the scattering transform. The novelty, while present, is incremental rather than groundbreaking.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of CNNs using a novel kernel framework.
*   The results extend existing theory on scattering transforms to a broader class of architectures.
*   The established link between stability and generalization offers valuable insights into the behavior of CNNs.
*   The work is of high quality and relevant to the NIPS audience.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper’s theoretical nature and limited empirical validation might not meet the NIPS standards for impact.
*   The complexity and mathematical density could limit its accessibility to a broad audience.
*   The incremental nature of the contribution could be a concern.
*   The potential practical computational challenges are not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid theoretical paper with valuable insights into the properties of CNNs. The mathematical rigor and careful analysis are commendable. However, the lack of substantial empirical validation and the complexity of the presentation are potential drawbacks. I would rate this as a **weak accept**. Addressing the empirical validation and perhaps clarifying the key findings with more intuitive explanations would significantly strengthen the paper.
