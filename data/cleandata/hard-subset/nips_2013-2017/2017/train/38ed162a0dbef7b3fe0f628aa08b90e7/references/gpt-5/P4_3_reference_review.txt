Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework for signals on continuous domains that mirrors CNN architectures but lives in RKHSs. Each layer applies patch extraction, a homogeneous dot-product kernel map, and Gaussian pooling. The authors analyze two core properties:

- Stability to diffeomorphisms: Building on Mallat’s scattering theory, they prove Lipschitz stability of the resulting representation Φ to C1-diffeomorphisms τ with ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2, yielding a bound of the form ‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖. The last-layer pooling scale σn controls translation invariance.
- Group equivariance/invariance: They show how to construct layerwise operators (patch extraction, kernel map, pooling on a group with Haar measure) that commute with group actions, enabling local stability and global invariance to arbitrary groups by appropriate choice of intermediate vs last-layer pooling.

They also (i) address discretization and provide signal recovery lemmas under mild conditions on subsampling and pooling, (ii) show that finite-dimensional kernel projections as in CKN preserve non-expansiveness and thus stability, and (iii) prove that the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel contains a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and explicit normalization, yielding RKHS norm bounds for such CNNs and linking norm control to both stability and generalization.

This work generalizes theoretical guarantees from scattering transforms [Bruna & Mallat; Mallat] to broader convolutional representations, and connects to convolutional kernel networks [Mairal et al.], group-equivariant CNNs [Cohen & Welling], and kernel perspectives on deep nets [Anselmi et al.; Daniely et al.]. Unlike scattering, filters here are not wavelets and can be learned via RKHS subspace projections.

Strengths
- Clear, modular construction of a convolutional kernel representation that closely aligns with CNN practice (patching, nonlinearity, pooling), enabling separate analysis of representation and predictor via RKHS tools.
- Stability to diffeomorphisms proved for multilayer convolutional kernels with explicit dependence on deformation magnitude and last-layer pooling, paralleling scattering but in a more general setting.
- General group-equivariant layer design on locally compact groups with Haar measure, giving a clean recipe to combine local stability and global invariance.
- Inclusion of a class of CNNs in the RKHS with homogeneous smooth activations (smoothed ReLU variants), and an explicit recursive RKHS norm bound that ties stability/generalization to architectural/weight parameters.
- Thoughtful treatment of discretization and kernel approximations (CKN-style projections), showing stability properties are preserved by non-expansive projections.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Assumptions differ from standard CNN practice: homogeneous smooth activations with explicit normalization nk(u)=‖patch‖, Gaussian pooling, and continuous-domain operators. The included CNN class excludes standard non-smoothed ReLU and typical max pooling; the practical gap is acknowledged but reduces direct applicability.
- Stability bound degrades linearly with depth (1 + n) and requires ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2 and initial low-pass A0. The constants and their growth with patch size factor κ can be nontrivial; quantitative tightness is unclear.
- Signal preservation and discretization rely on deconvolution invertibility and ek ≥ sk. Stability of deconvolution under noise and boundary effects are only qualitatively discussed.
- Claims about improved sample complexity via RKHS norm control are qualitative; no explicit generalization bounds or empirical validation are provided.
- The translation invariance term scales as 1/σn, but there is no empirical or quantitative study of the invariance–information trade-off as σn increases.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The analysis uses standard tools (operator commutators, Schur’s test) and carefully tracks stability through layers. The decomposition in Proposition 3 and bounds in Lemmas 4–5 are consistent with prior scattering analyses, extended to shifted integral operators and general kernels.
- The inclusion of CNNs in the RKHS via homogeneous smooth activations and the recursive norm bound are technically plausible and well-motivated; relying on Schoenberg-type expansions and kernel properties is appropriate. However, the gap to non-smooth ReLU remains, and approximation error from smoothing is not quantified.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized. Assumptions are explicit (A1–A3). The construction is clear and parallels CNN terminology. Proofs are deferred to the appendix; key proof ideas are sketched. Notation is heavy but standard for this literature. The discretization section is helpful, though some practical choices (e.g., boundary handling, choice of ek, sk, σk) could be further clarified.

Originality
- Extends scattering stability guarantees to a broader, kernel-based convolutional architecture and links these to a nontrivial class of CNNs via RKHS embeddings and norm control. The group-equivariant kernel-layer construction is conceptually aligned with existing equivariant CNNs but framed in an RKHS kernel context. Relative to prior kernel views of deep nets, the diffeomorphism stability and explicit multilayer convolutional kernel construction appear novel.

Significance
- Conceptual significance is solid: it provides a principled lens to analyze invariance/stability of deep convolutional representations beyond scattering, with implications for regularization via RKHS norms. Practical significance depends on how well the assumptions map to deployed CNNs; nonetheless, the results can inform architecture design (pooling scales, activation smoothness) and motivate stable kernelized deep models (CKNs).

Questions and suggestions
- Can the authors quantify constants C1, C2 for specific kernels and pooling scales, and discuss empirical tightness?
- How sensitive are stability guarantees to replacing Gaussian pooling with common alternatives (e.g., average or max pooling)? Any extension to max pooling’s nonlinearity?
- Can the gap between smoothed and standard ReLU be bounded to transfer RKHS norm/stability results approximately to ReLU networks?
- In discretization, can stability to noise be quantified for the deconvolution step, and how do boundary effects impact Lemma 2?
- Is there a margin-based generalization bound stated explicitly in terms of the RKHS norm bound from Proposition 7 for classification losses?

Arguments for acceptance
- Provides a rigorous, general framework for invariance and stability of deep convolutional representations extending scattering theory.
- Bridges kernel methods (CKN) and CNNs with explicit inclusion and norm control.
- Clear theoretical contributions with potential to influence future work on stable deep architectures and regularization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical gap to standard CNNs (activation, pooling, normalization) and lack of empirical validation or quantitative constants.
- Stability bound dependence on depth and strong assumptions (‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2, initial low-pass) may limit applicability.
- Claims about improved sample complexity are not substantiated by new theoretical generalization bounds or experiments.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and conceptually meaningful theoretical advancement on stability/invariance for convolutional representations within an RKHS framework, with clear connections to existing kernel and CNN literature, albeit with limited practical validation and some restrictive assumptions.
