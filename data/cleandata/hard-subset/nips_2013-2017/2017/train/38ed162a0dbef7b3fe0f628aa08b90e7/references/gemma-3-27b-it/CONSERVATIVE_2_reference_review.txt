This paper explores the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations through the lens of positive definite kernels, aiming to bridge the gap between the empirical success of CNNs and a more principled understanding of their behavior. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate invariance and stability properties analogous to those found in the scattering transform, but for a broader class of CNN architectures. They connect these properties to generalization ability via RKHS norms.

The primary strength of this work lies in its attempt to provide a theoretical foundation for understanding CNNs using well-established mathematical tools from kernel methods and harmonic analysis. The extension of scattering transform-like stability results to more general CNN architectures is a positive step. The authors demonstrate a good grasp of relevant literature, particularly concerning convolutional kernel networks and the scattering transform. The attempt to link stability to generalization via RKHS norms is also a valuable direction.

However, several weaknesses significantly diminish the impact of this work. The theoretical framework, while mathematically sound, feels largely detached from practical considerations. The paper introduces a complex kernel construction and spends considerable effort establishing theoretical properties, but provides little discussion of *how* these insights could inform the design or training of actual CNNs.  The connection between the derived stability properties and empirically observed generalization performance remains speculative. The authors acknowledge this but do not offer concrete avenues for investigation.

The paper relies heavily on assumptions about smoothness and regularity (e.g., smooth activations, Lipschitz continuity of diffeomorphisms). While common in theoretical analysis, these assumptions are often violated in practice. The analysis also assumes access to the full kernel, which is computationally intractable for large datasets, necessitating the approximations used in CKNs.  The impact of these approximations on the derived stability guarantees is not fully explored. While the authors mention the link to CKNs, the discussion largely avoids the practical challenges associated with approximating kernel methods. 

The presentation is dense and at times difficult to follow. While mathematically rigorous, the paper could benefit from more intuitive explanations and concrete examples to illustrate the theoretical concepts. The inclusion of Lemma 1, showing signal preservation, appears critical for justifying discretization, yet the link to practical implications feels weak.  Similarly, the discussion of group invariance, while correct, lacks a compelling application beyond extending existing theoretical results.  The claim that this work applies to CNNs is made, but the space of CNNs that fall under their theoretical framework seems limited by the homogeneous activation requirement.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of CNNs using kernel methods.
*   It extends stability results from the scattering transform to a broader class of CNNs.
*   It connects stability properties to potential generalization benefits.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical framework is largely disconnected from practical considerations.
*   Strong assumptions are made regarding smoothness and regularity.
*   The paper lacks concrete examples or guidance for applying the theoretical results to improve CNN design.
*   The space of CNNs covered by the theory is limited.
*   The presentation is dense and difficult to follow.



Overall, this paper presents a mathematically sound but ultimately unsatisfying contribution. It offers theoretical insights but lacks practical relevance. The authors establish a connection between kernel methods and CNNs, but fail to demonstrate how this connection can be leveraged to advance the field. The work feels more like a preliminary exploration of a promising direction than a complete and impactful research contribution. I would rate this as a **weak reject**. More substantial empirical validation and a clearer articulation of the practical implications are needed to elevate this work to an acceptable standard for NIPS. The lack of discussion around the computational cost of the kernel approximations also hurts its appeal.
