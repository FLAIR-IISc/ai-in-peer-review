**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel framework, generalizing Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), that operates on continuous signals. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this representation is Lipschitz stable with respect to small C1-diffeomorphisms, mirroring results previously established for the scattering transform. The stability bound demonstrates that the representation is robust to local deformations and approximately invariant to global translations, with the degree of invariance controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer. The framework is also extended to achieve invariance to general group actions. Finally, the authors establish a connection to standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) by showing that a class of CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions resides within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) of the proposed kernel, thereby inheriting its stability properties. The RKHS norm of the CNN function is then shown to control its stability.

**Strengths**

1.  **Important Theoretical Contribution:** The paper tackles the fundamental and challenging problem of understanding the geometric properties of deep neural networks. Providing a unified theoretical framework to analyze the stability of both CKNs and certain CNNs is a significant step forward.
2.  **Rigorous Analysis:** The work successfully extends the sophisticated mathematical formalism and proof techniques from the analysis of scattering transforms to a more general and learnable class of models. This is a non-trivial technical achievement.
3.  **Insightful Connection:** The paper draws a clear and valuable link between the RKHS norm of a function, its stability to input deformations, and its generalization capacity. This provides a principled way to think about how architectural choices and regularization can promote robustness in deep models.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents a valuable theoretical direction, it is undermined by a significant number of minor flaws in clarity, methodological details, and the strength of its claims.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The notation is frequently ambiguous. The symbol `‖ · ‖` is overloaded to represent three distinct concepts (Hilbert space norms, L2 norms, and L2→L2 operator norms) as declared by the authors. While this is a choice of convenience, it hinders readability and requires the reader to constantly infer the correct meaning from context, which is particularly challenging in dense mathematical propositions like Proposition 3.
    *   There are several unsubstantiated claims. For example, in Section 2.2, the authors state that border effects are "negligible when dealing with large realistic images" without providing justification or citation. Similarly, the extension of the 1D discretization analysis to higher dimensions is claimed to be "easy" for box-shaped patches, but potential complexities are not discussed.
    *   The manuscript contains distracting formatting errors. The conference information in the footnote on the first page ("st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems") is malformed and misplaced. Such details detract from the overall professionalism of the work.

2.  **Gaps between Theory and Practice:**
    *   The connection to practical CNNs is tenuous. The analysis in Section 5 applies not to standard CNNs, but to a specific variant where the activation is "homogenized" by a patch-norm dependent factor `nk(u) = ‖Pkxk−1(u)‖`. This is a non-standard form of normalization that is architecturally distinct from conventional CNNs (e.g., those using batch normalization or no normalization). The paper does not discuss the performance or behavioral implications of this modification, weakening the claim that the results explain the properties of widely used CNNs.
    *   The treatment of discretization in Section 2.1 is cursory. The signal recovery in Lemma 2 depends on a deconvolution operation whose stability is asserted to be sufficient when the scale parameter is "small enough," a vague condition that is not formalized. This leaves a critical gap between the continuous-domain theory and its practical discrete implementation.
    *   The dismissal of the norm-preservation property, a key feature of the scattering transform, is not fully convincing. The argument rests on the low-pass nature of "natural signals" and the optional addition of a final Gaussian kernel mapping. This feels like an ad-hoc justification rather than an integral part of the theory, and the potential information loss from non-isometric pooling operators is not quantified.

3.  **Limitations of the Analysis:**
    *   The stability results are contingent on strong assumptions whose implications are not fully explored. The diffeomorphism condition `‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2`, inherited from prior work, restricts the analysis to very small deformations. More importantly, the kernel assumptions (A1-A2) of norm preservation and non-expansiveness are restrictive. The paper does not discuss the potential trade-off between satisfying these conditions for theoretical tractability and the expressive power of the resulting model.

**Recommendation**

Reject.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The connection to standard, practical CNNs is significantly overstated. The theoretical results apply to a modified, non-standard architecture, and this distinction is not sufficiently highlighted or justified.
*   The paper suffers from an accumulation of minor but numerous issues, including ambiguous notation, unsubstantiated claims, formatting errors, and a superficial treatment of the crucial theory-to-practice link (discretization).
*   Key departures from the scattering transform framework (e.g., the lack of norm preservation) are not addressed with sufficient rigor.

While the core theoretical direction is promising and the mathematical work is substantial, the current manuscript lacks the meticulousness and precision expected of a top-tier publication. The number of unaddressed minor issues and overstated claims collectively constitutes a major flaw. A significant revision would be required to clarify the notation, properly qualify the claims regarding CNNs, and more thoroughly bridge the gap between the continuous theory and its discrete application.
