Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that mirrors CNN architectures and encompasses both Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) and a nontrivial subset of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations. Each layer applies (i) patch extraction, (ii) a norm-preserving, non-expansive RKHS mapping induced by a homogeneous dot-product kernel, and (iii) Gaussian pooling. The authors analyze the geometry of the resulting representation and prove stability to C1 diffeomorphisms and increasing translation invariance with depth. A key stability bound shows Lipschitz continuity of the representation with respect to deformations, with a term linear in the deformation’s Jacobian and a translation term that decays as 1/σn (the last pooling scale). They also extend the construction to group-equivariant layers to obtain global invariance to compact transformation groups by using group-covariant patch extraction and pooling.

The paper bridges theory and practice via (a) discretization, proving signal recoverability under mild conditions and deconvolution, and (b) kernel approximation by finite-dimensional projections (CKN), which preserve non-expansiveness and thus stability. Finally, they show that the RKHS of the proposed multilayer kernel contains a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations; they provide an explicit RKHS norm bound (via a recursion on layers and filter norms) that links model stability/generalization to the RKHS norm, thereby offering a principled regularization perspective. The work is positioned with respect to scattering transforms (Mallat/Bruna), group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling), invariant kernels, and prior CKNs.

Strengths
- Clear, unified operator view (patch → kernel map → pooling) that subsumes CKNs and a meaningful subset of CNNs.
- Theoretical advances: a deformation stability result akin to scattering but for more general convolutional kernel representations, with a new commutator bound for [LcAσ, Lτ]; translation invariance increases with pooling scale.
- Group invariance extension: layerwise equivariance for locally compact groups via group patch extraction and pooling; last-layer pooling yields global invariance while intermediate layers yield stability to a subgroup.
- Practical considerations: discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximation are addressed; non-expansiveness ensures stability is retained after projection.
- Link to CNNs: the inclusion result (Proposition 7) with RKHS norm control provides a conceptual bridge between deep CNN regularization, stability, and generalization.
- Related work is broad and appropriate; the paper situates itself well between scattering, kernels, and modern CNNs.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions may be strong for standard CNNs: norm-preserving, non-expansive kernel maps; Gaussian pooling; patch size tied to pooling scale (A3). It is not fully clear how typical trained CNNs with ReLU, strided convolutions, and non-Gaussian pooling fit, beyond the smooth homogeneous surrogate.
- The inclusion of CNNs requires homogenization (nk(u)=‖patch‖) and smooth activations; ReLU is excluded (though smoothed variants are suggested). Practical implications for real networks are therefore indirect.
- The discretization “signal recovery by deconvolution” hinges on invertibility of Gaussian blurs and is not robust to noise; stability of deconvolution is only qualitatively discussed.
- Constants in stability bounds (C1, C2) and their dependence on κ and dimension can be large; linear dependence on depth n may be pessimistic. Tightness and empirical relevance of these constants are not evaluated.
- Limited empirical validation: the theoretical claims would benefit from experiments demonstrating the predicted stability in CKNs/CNNs, effects of σn, and the relation between RKHS norm and generalization.
- The generalization link is conceptual (via RKHS norm), without explicit sample-complexity bounds for the proposed class.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with careful operator-theoretic reasoning, appropriate use of Schur’s test, and clean decomposition via commutators. The proofs are deferred, but the statements are plausible and align with scattering literature. Some steps—especially discretization recovery and assumptions ensuring (A1–A2) for the chosen kernels—could be further qualified for robustness. The CNN inclusion is well constructed but somewhat idealized.

Clarity
Well organized and clearly written for a theoretically inclined audience. The roles of P, M, A are explicit, and the intuition for stability and invariance is conveyed. Suggestions:
- Move representative kernel examples from the appendix into the main text and explicitly verify (A1–A2).
- Discuss the magnitude and practical choice of σk and κ, and the effect on constants.
- Clarify the stability of deconvolution under noise and boundary effects.
- Provide a small experimental section illustrating the bounds qualitatively.

Originality
The work extends scattering stability results to a broader, kernel-based multilayer class and shows inclusion of a subset of CNNs in an RKHS with controlled norm. The commutator analysis for shifted pooling operators and the group-equivariant kernel-layer construction are valuable. While built on established ideas (scattering, CKNs, equivariance), the synthesis and generalization are novel.

Significance
The framework advances our theoretical understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations beyond fixed wavelet designs, and offers a path to principled regularization via RKHS norms. It is likely to influence further work on theory of deep architectures, kernel approximations of CNNs, and group-equivariant designs. Immediate practical impact is limited by the idealized assumptions and lack of experiments, but the conceptual contributions are significant.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- How sensitive are the bounds to the choice of kernel κk? Can you instantiate constants for a concrete kernel (e.g., inverse polynomial or arc-cosine) and typical σk schedules?
- Can you extend inclusion to non-smooth ReLU via alternative function spaces or smoothing approximations with quantitative approximation error?
- Provide numerical illustrations: deformation stability vs σn and n, and empirical correlation between RKHS norm bounds and generalization error.
- Discuss robustness of Lemma 2 under noise and discretization artifacts; can you give stable recovery conditions (e.g., bounds on σk vs sk)?
- Can the analysis accommodate strided convolutions and max/average pooling used in practice?

Pros
- Solid, general theoretical framework; extends scattering to broader architectures.
- Clear invariance/stability results; group-equivariant extension.
- Conceptual link between CNNs and RKHS norm-based regularization.

Cons
- Strong/idealized assumptions; limited coverage of standard CNN components.
- Constants and depth dependence may be loose; no empirical validation.
- Deconvolution-based recovery is fragile in practice.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is meaningful and well executed; adding even small empirical illustrations and clarifying practical implications would strengthen the paper. Confidence: high.
