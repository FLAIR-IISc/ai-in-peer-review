Summary and contributions
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that mirrors CNN architectures: each layer performs (i) patch extraction, (ii) a norm-preserving, non-expansive kernel map into an RKHS using homogeneous dot-product kernels, and (iii) Gaussian pooling. Working on continuous domains, you prove (a) stability to C1-diffeomorphisms in the sense of Mallat (Lipschitz in ∥∇τ∥∞ and ∥τ∥∞), with translation invariance controlled by the last pooling scale σn, (b) that discretized and sub-sampled versions still preserve the signal under mild conditions, and (c) that finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKN projections) preserve non-expansiveness and thus stability. You also show how to achieve global invariance to general groups via equivariant patch extraction and pooling on groups. Finally, you connect this RKHS to a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, proving such CNNs lie in the RKHS, with an explicit recursive bound on the RKHS norm in terms of filter norms; this links stability and generalization via standard margin arguments.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned between scattering networks (Mallat, Bruna) and convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.). It borrows harmonic analysis techniques used for scattering to obtain diffeomorphism stability, and it adopts the CKN perspective to design multilayer kernel maps. Compared with scattering, the architecture is more general (learned filters possible via kernel approximation; broader class of homogeneous activations) but loses strict energy conservation. Compared with group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling), the group section provides an RKHS-centric derivation of equivariance/invariance via pooling over groups. Relative to Poggio/Anselmi’s hierarchical kernels and Daniely et al.’s compositional kernels, the new elements are (i) a continuous-domain multilayer kernel with explicit patch/kernel/pooling operators, (ii) stability bounds that mirror scattering but for this broader architecture, and (iii) an embedding of a class of CNNs into the RKHS with norm control.

Strengths
- Clear, principled construction of a deep convolutional RKHS that subsumes CKNs and contains a nontrivial class of CNNs; the norm-preserving and non-expansive properties are used coherently.
- Diffeomorphism stability bound: ‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ [(1+n)C1‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞]‖x‖, matching scattering-style guarantees while allowing more flexible architectures.
- Careful treatment of discretization and kernel approximation; the observation that projection steps maintain non-expansiveness is important for practical CKNs.
- Conceptual link between invariance/stability and generalization via RKHS norms for CNN-like models, with a constructive norm bound (Proposition 7).
- Extension to general transformation groups via equivariant patch extraction and pooling on groups.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Class of CNNs covered: The inclusion result requires smooth homogeneous activations and a nonstandard normalization by nk(u)=‖patch‖. Typical CNNs use ReLU (non-smooth) without this explicit normalization. While you argue smoothed ReLUs suffice, the induced architecture deviates from standard practice, and the implications for actual networks (with ReLU, BatchNorm, biases, and strided/MaxPool operations) are not fully addressed. Handling nk(u)=0 needs a robust convention.
- Quantitative sharpness: Constants C1, C2 and their dependence on dimension and κ are abstract. The linear growth in n in the ∥∇τ∥∞ term is pessimistic; some discussion of whether this is tight for realistic filter sizes/scales would help. The statement that norm reduction by pooling is “less relevant” with an extra Gaussian top layer is plausible but shifts away from standard linear readouts.
- Discretization and recovery: Lemma 2 relies on stable deconvolution of Gaussian pooling and the condition ek ≥ sk. This is delicate in the presence of noise and boundary effects; the practical stability region and conditioning are not quantified. The assumption that A0 models physical acquisition is reasonable but deserves clearer guidance on σ0 vs sampling rate.
- Generalization: The connection to sample complexity remains qualitative. The recursive norm bound in Prop. 7 can blow up with depth depending on Cσ and filter norms; no explicit Rademacher/covering number bounds are provided. It would be valuable to state a concrete capacity bound for the induced CNN class under natural norm constraints.
- Group invariance: The group section gives a clean equivariance/invariance construction but stops short of providing diffeomorphism-type stability results for groups beyond translations, or of discussing discretization on noncommutative groups and computational costs of global pooling.
- Empirical validation: No experiments demonstrate the stability predictions, the role of σn, or the practical relevance of the RKHS norm bound for generalization. Even small-scale numerical illustrations (e.g., stability vs deformation amplitude, comparison to scattering/standard CNNs) would help.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, though dense. It would help to bring concrete kernel examples (Gaussian dot-product, inverse polynomial, arc-cosine) and their Cσ into the main text. Explicitly restate assumption (A3) in terms of common discrete hyperparameters (filter size, stride, pooling scale) and provide a typical schedule that satisfies it.
- Define how nk(u) is handled when it is zero (e.g., add ε). Briefly summarize the conditions ensuring deconvolution stability in Lemma 2. A figure contrasting the proposed architecture with scattering and standard CNNs would improve accessibility.
- Minor typos/formatting: there are a few glitches (e.g., “st Conference” in the header).

Questions for the authors
- Can you quantify C1, C2 numerically in 2D for Gaussian h and typical κ? What σn is needed for ε-invariance to translations of size t?
- Can you derive an explicit generalization bound (e.g., Rademacher complexity) for the CNN class in Prop. 7 under simple norm constraints?
- How do your results adapt to strided convolutions and max pooling, which are prevalent in practice?
- Do you have empirical evidence that smoothed ReLU versions with the nk(u) normalization behave comparably to ReLU CNNs and exhibit the predicted stability?

Recommendation
This is a solid theoretical paper that meaningfully extends scattering-style stability to a broader, CNN-like kernel framework and provides a novel RKHS perspective on CNNs with homogeneous activations. Despite limitations in scope (assumptions on activations/normalization, lack of empirical corroboration), the contribution is significant and timely. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the above points and better quantifying constants and practical regimes.
