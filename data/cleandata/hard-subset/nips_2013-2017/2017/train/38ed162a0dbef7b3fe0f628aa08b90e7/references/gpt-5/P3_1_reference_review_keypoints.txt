- Summary: Proposes a multilayer convolutional kernel on continuous domains mirroring CNNs, proving diffeomorphism stability and increased translation invariance with depth, plus group-equivariant extensions and an RKHS that contains a class of smooth homogeneous-activation CNNs.
- Main theoretical result: Theorem 6 gives stability ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖ under assumptions A1–A3, with dependence on depth n and final pooling scale σn.
- Discretization/approximation: Discusses CKN-style finite-dimensional projections and subsampling recoverability (Lemma 2), claiming stability is preserved by non-expansive projections but noting practical ill-conditioning.
- CNN–RKHS link: Shows a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the induced RKHS and provides recursive RKHS norm bounds, though requiring nonstandard normalization and excluding plain ReLU.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on Mallat’s scattering transforms and CKNs, extends commutator-based stability proofs to learned projections, and connects to group-equivariant CNN literature.
- Correctness assessment: Stability proofs use standard commutator decompositions and Schur tests and are credible; constants and some assumptions (κ, dimension) are acknowledged but opaque.
- Concerns on signal recovery: Continuous signal preservation arguments are idealized; deconvolution/subsampling recovery is mathematically plausible but likely ill-conditioned and sensitive to noise.
- Discretization gaps: No quantitative finite-sample/approximation-error bounds are provided for Nyström/subspace approximations and their effect on stability or expressivity.
- Activation limitations: RKHS containment requires smooth homogeneous activations and patch normalization nk(u)=‖patch‖, so standard ReLU CNNs aren’t directly covered.
- Constants and scaling: Stability constants may be loose (linear in depth, κ^(d+1) dependence); paper lacks guidance on optimal scheduling of σk and Sk to control growth.
- Clarity: Paper is generally well written and organized, but could reduce overloaded notation, list concrete kernel choices satisfying assumptions, and provide high-level proof sketches in the main text.
- Originality: New in generalizing scattering-style stability to learned multilayer kernel maps and in the RKHS containment result; group-equivariance and last-layer pooling observations are less novel.
- Significance: Provides a unifying theoretical framework linking CKNs and a subset of CNNs and suggests RKHS-based regularization implications, but practical impact limited by activation requirements, absent quantitative approximation bounds, and no empirical validation.
- Strengths: Modular operator framework, explicit diffeomorphism stability bound, clean group-equivariance argument, and a conceptual bridge between CNNs and RKHSs.
- Weaknesses: Ill-posed signal recovery after smoothing, potentially loose constants, restrictive activation/normalization requirements, and missing quantitative bounds for finite-dimensional projections.
- Questions for authors: (1) Can approximation errors from projections Πk be quantified and propagated into stability bounds? (2) Is the (1+n) linear depth term tight or avoidable via scale scheduling? (3) How stable is Lemma 2’s recovery to noise—any deconvolution stability constants? (4) Can RKHS-containment be extended to (smoothed) leaky-ReLU without nk normalization or related to batch-norm? (5) For specific kernels (e.g., inverse polynomial, arc-cosine), can concrete Cσ and norm-bound estimates be given?
- Minor comments: Clarify σ0’s role in final constants, provide a table of example κk satisfying (A1–A2), and fix incomplete reference details.
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — solid theoretical contribution with careful analysis but practical constants, activation restrictions, and quantitative approximation issues need sharpening.
