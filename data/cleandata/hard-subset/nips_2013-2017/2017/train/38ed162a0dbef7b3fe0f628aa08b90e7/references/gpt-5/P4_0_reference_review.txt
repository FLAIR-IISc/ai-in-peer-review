Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals on continuous domains and analyzes its invariance and stability properties. The construction mirrors convolutional networks: each layer performs patch extraction, a pointwise kernel map into an RKHS using homogeneous dot-product kernels, and linear Gaussian pooling. The authors (i) prove stability to C1-diffeomorphisms with a Lipschitz-type bound analogous to scattering transforms [17], with translation invariance controlled by the final pooling scale; (ii) show how to achieve global invariance to general transformation groups via group-covariant patch extraction and pooling; (iii) discuss discretization and kernel approximation, arguing that practical finite-dimensional CKNs [15,16] preserve the derived stability; and (iv) establish that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, with explicit norm bounds, thereby transferring stability guarantees to these CNNs via RKHS norm control.

This extends the stability and invariance analysis of scattering transform networks [5,17]—which use fixed wavelets—to a broader class that includes learned kernel representations (CKNs) and certain CNNs. It relates to group-equivariant CNNs [9] and invariance kernels [13,19,22] by deriving group-covariant layers in the kernel framework. It also connects to dual/kernel views of deep nets [2,10,18,35,36].

Quality
- Theory: The stability result (Theorem 6) follows from a commutator decomposition (Proposition 3) combined with bounds on [LcAσ, Lτ] (Lemma 4) and on LτAσ − Aσ (Lemma 5 from [17]). Assumptions (A1–A3) are clearly stated: norm-preserving, non-expansive kernel maps; patch size scaling with pooling; and an initial low-pass A0 to control high frequencies. The resulting bound matches the desired form, with a linear dependence on ‖∇τ‖∞ and a translation term decaying as 1/σn. The extension to group actions is mathematically consistent via Haar measure and group convolution pooling. The RKHS inclusion for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (Proposition 7) is constructive, with a recursive norm bound.

- Practicality: The discretization section introduces subsampling and shows recovery (Lemma 2) under ek ≥ sk and deconvolution stability; the discussion of kernel approximation via projections is careful about non-expansiveness. However, important constants and conditioning issues (e.g., deconvolution stability, dependence of C1 on κ and dimension) are not quantified, and proofs are deferred to appendices or the extended version.

- Empirics: The paper is purely theoretical; there are no numerical validations (e.g., empirical stability tests, comparisons to scattering or standard CNNs).

Clarity
The presentation is generally clear, with well-defined operators and careful notation. The high-level pipeline and its connection to CNNs/CKNs are explained. The stability argument is modular and traceable. Some claims would benefit from more intuition in the main text (e.g., the signal preservation Lemma 1 with pooling, and the role/conditioning of deconvolution in Lemma 2). Dependence of constants on depth, dimension, and patch/pooling scales could be highlighted earlier.

Originality
The paper contributes:
- A generalized convolutional kernel framework on continuous domains with multilayer structure and precise invariance/stability analysis, extending scattering theory beyond fixed wavelets to learned kernel maps and CKNs.
- A new commutator bound for shifted integral operators [LcAσ, Lτ], complementing [17].
- An RKHS inclusion result for multi-layer CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and an explicit norm bound that ties architectural parameters to stability/generalization.

While related ideas (hierarchical kernel views, group equivariance) exist [2,9,15,16,19,22,35,36], the specific combination and the stability analysis for this broader class appear novel.

Significance
Providing stability guarantees for representations that encompass learned filters is valuable for understanding CNNs and CKNs, and the RKHS norm connection offers a principled link between robustness and generalization. The group-invariance construction is conceptually useful and aligns with ongoing interest in equivariant architectures. However, the lack of empirical evaluation and the restrictions on activation smoothness/homogeneity and architectural normalization may limit near-term practical impact.

Pros
- Extends scattering-style stability analysis to a wider class including CKNs and certain CNNs.
- Clear, modular theoretical framework; commutator-based proof technique is sound and reusable.
- Group-equivariant formulation with principled path to global invariance.
- RKHS norm bounds provide a theoretical lens on robustness/generalization.

Cons
- No empirical validation; practical relevance of constants and depth dependence not quantified.
- Assumptions may be restrictive: norm-preserving homogeneous kernel maps; smooth homogeneous activations and per-patch normalization (nk(u)) not standard in CNNs; ReLU requires smoothing.
- Signal recovery claims with pooling (Lemma 1) and deconvolution-based recovery (Lemma 2) hinge on stability/conditioning that are not fully analyzed under noise and discretization.
- The linear dependence on depth in the ‖∇τ‖∞ term could be significant; mitigation strategies are not discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide numerical experiments illustrating the stability bounds (e.g., measuring ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ versus ‖∇τ‖∞ and σn) and comparing to scattering and standard CNNs.
- Quantify constants C1 and C2 for typical choices (Gaussian h, common kernel maps), and discuss their scaling with dimension and κ; compare to bounds in [17].
- Clarify and tighten Lemma 1 and Lemma 2: explicitly state conditions under which pooling/deconvolution are stably invertible, and analyze noise sensitivity.
- Discuss the impact of projections in kernel approximations on approximation error and stability; provide bounds on the loss introduced by finite-dimensional subspaces.
- Elaborate on activation assumptions: give concrete smooth approximations to ReLU used in practice, and discuss whether the per-patch normalization nk(u) can be avoided or approximated.
- Relate the RKHS norm bounds to generalization/sample complexity more concretely (e.g., margin-based bounds), even at a high level.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution that generalizes known invariance/stability results to learned convolutional kernel representations and connects them to CNNs under clear assumptions. The work would be strengthened by empirical validation and tighter discussion of constants and practical constraints.
