Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework whose associated RKHS contains a large class of (continuous-domain) CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations as well as Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs). Within this kernel/RKHS view, the authors establish stability to diffeomorphisms and increasing translation invariance with depth, extending Mallat’s scattering theory to more standard convolutional architectures. The multilayer construction alternates patch extraction, a norm-preserving/non-expansive kernel map, and Gaussian pooling; invariance arises from commutation with translations (or more general groups), and stability is controlled by commutator bounds. A key result (Theorem 6) shows that the representation is Lipschitz with respect to small diffeomorphisms, with a bound that scales linearly in depth and decays with the last pooling scale. The framework accommodates discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs) without breaking stability. The authors also constructively embed CNNs into the RKHS and bound their RKHS norm via the network weights and the chosen activation, thereby linking prediction Lipschitzness and generalization to stability. They further generalize pooling and patch extraction to arbitrary locally compact groups to obtain group-equivariant layers with global invariance via the last pooling.

Relation to prior work
This work builds directly on scattering transforms (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat 2012–2013) by adopting their diffeomorphism-stability formalism but moves beyond fixed wavelet filters to kernel-induced multilayer constructions that encompass learned CKNs and a class of CNNs. It connects with the kernel/CNN literature (e.g., Mairal et al. on CKNs; Montavon et al.; Anselmi/Rosasco/Poggio on invariance; Daniely et al. on kernel views of deep nets; convexified CNNs) and with group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling), offering a harmonic-analysis perspective that retains the compositional, convolutional structure. A notable technical extension is a commutator bound for shifted pooling kernels, and the explicit RKHS inclusion of CNNs with norm bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A clean RKHS-based construction that includes both CKNs and a meaningful subclass of CNNs, enabling transfer of scattering-style invariance/stability guarantees to architectures closer to practice.
- Stability result with explicit architecture dependence: Theorem 6 precisely decomposes deformation and translation terms; translation invariance improves with the last pooling scale, matching intuitive CNN design principles.
- Group-generalization: A clear recipe for equivariance/invariance to arbitrary locally compact groups by modifying patch extraction and pooling; aligns with but is more general than 2D roto-translation examples.
- Compatibility with discretization and kernel approximation: The paper carefully discusses sampling, subsampling, and finite-dimensional projections, arguing that stability is preserved while quantifying information loss to approximation quality.
- CNN-in-the-RKHS construction: The inclusion result (Proposition 7) and recursive norm bounds provide a rigorous route to relating network complexity, stability (via Lipschitzness), and generalization.
- Technical novelty: The commutator analysis for LcAσ with diffeomorphisms and the careful operator-norm control via Schur’s test extend scattering proofs to shifted, pooled convolutions.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Empirical validation is minimal: The claims would be strengthened by even small-scale experiments demonstrating deformation stability and its impact on sample complexity for CKNs/CNNs, or sanity checks of the constants’ practical regimes.
- Assumptions narrow the CNN class: The RKHS inclusion and norm bounds require smooth homogeneous activations and a homogenization term nk(u)=‖Pkxk−1(u)‖; standard ReLU is excluded (though a smoothed variant is possible). The practical implications for common CNNs are discussed but not empirically probed.
- Dependence on depth and constants: The linear dependence on n and constants that can scale with κd+1 may be pessimistic; discussion of tightness or lower bounds is limited.
- Initial low-pass assumption A0: While physically motivated, the need to insert an initial pooling to control high frequencies may complicate direct comparisons to standard pipelines; the effect of σ0→0 is argued but not quantified in discrete settings.
- Discretization and deconvolution: Signal recovery under subsampling relies on deconvolution stability and choices ek≥sk; the robustness of this claim under finite-support filters and boundary effects is not fully analyzed.
- Relation to group-equivariant CNNs: The group pooling construction is elegant but overlaps conceptually with G-CNNs; a more explicit comparison (e.g., when the proposed pooling matches group correlation layers) would help position contributions.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound and careful, extending nontrivial harmonic-analysis tools to a broader convolutional setting. Proof roadmaps are clear, and assumptions are stated. Some constants and practical regimes could be discussed more concretely. The absence of experiments is a drawback but not fatal for a theory paper of this scope.

Clarity
The paper is well organized, with a helpful exposition of operators and notation, and a clear separation between continuous theory, discretization, and kernel approximation. Some sections are dense; brief intuition for commutators and a summarized table of assumptions (A1–A3) and their implications would improve readability.

Originality
The blend of RKHS multilayer kernels with scattering-style stability, the commutator extension, and the CNN inclusion with norm bounds constitute a novel and bold direction. While related to several strands, the synthesis and generality go beyond incremental extensions.

Significance
High. Providing a rigorous path to deformation stability for architectures closer to practice than scattering, and a principled link between stability and generalization via RKHS norms, is valuable. This framework is likely to influence future analyses of deep models and motivate stability-regularized training.

Suggestions and questions
- Can the authors provide a small empirical illustration (even in the supplement) of deformation robustness vs. σn for CKNs or smoothed-ReLU CNNs?
- Clarify the magnitude of C1, C2 and how κ affects them in typical 2D settings; any empirical estimates?
- Discuss the gap between smoothed ReLU and standard ReLU in the inclusion result; does a limiting argument yield approximate inclusion?
- For group pooling, relate explicitly to group convolutions/correlations in G-CNNs and indicate when the proposed operators coincide.
- Expand on the discrete-to-continuous correspondence, especially boundary effects and finite-support pooling filters.

Pro/Con
Pros:
- Strong, general theoretical contribution; unifies kernels and CNNs.
- New stability guarantees beyond scattering.
- Path to group invariance and to controlling generalization via RKHS norms.

Cons:
- No experiments; constants and practicality not stress-tested.
- Activation and homogenization assumptions restrict direct applicability to standard CNNs.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant and original theoretical framework that advances our understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations. Despite limited empirical validation, the conceptual contributions are substantial and likely to spur further work.

Confidence
High.
