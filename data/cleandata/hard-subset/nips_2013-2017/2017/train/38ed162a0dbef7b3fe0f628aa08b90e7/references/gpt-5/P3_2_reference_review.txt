Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for d-dimensional signals that mirrors CNN architectures (patch extraction → kernel map → pooling), generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), and provides theoretical guarantees on invariance to translations and stability to small diffeomorphisms. The core result (Theorem 6) gives a Lipschitz-type bound of the representation distance under C1-diffeomorphisms with constants that (i) scale linearly with the number of layers and the deformation Jacobian norm, and (ii) yield increasing translation invariance with the last pooling scale. The analysis includes discretization (signal recovery with subsampling), stability under finite-dimensional kernel approximations (projections), and an extension to group actions via group-covariant patch extraction and pooling. Importantly, the RKHS associated to the constructed kernel contains a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, with explicit norm bounds (Proposition 7), so prediction stability follows from RKHS norm control, linking invariance/stability and generalization.

Relation to prior work: The framework and proofs adapt tools from the scattering transform literature (Mallat, Bruna) to more general convolutional kernel architectures and to a class of CNNs, while building on CKNs to maintain computational tractability. Group invariance connects to group equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling) and invariant kernels. The work complements Lipschitz-robustness analyses and kernel-based views of deep nets.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Bridges scattering theory, RKHS/kernel methods, and CNNs in a clean operator-theoretic framework. Clear separation of representation geometry from predictor complexity via RKHS norm.
- Stability guarantees: Theorem 6 provides diffeomorphic stability and translation invariance with intuitive dependence on pooling scale and depth; commutator-based analysis is technically sound and extends Mallat’s lemmas to shifted integral operators.
- Practical considerations: Addresses discretization, subsampling, and kernel approximation via non-expansive projections; argues stability is preserved in CKNs.
- Group invariance: Offers a principled recipe for equivariance/invariance on locally compact groups through group-covariant patching and pooling.
- CNN inclusion: Shows a nontrivial class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lie in the RKHS, yielding norm-based generalization/stability insights (recursive norm bounds reminiscent of path norms).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and realism: Stability hinges on Gaussian pooling, norm-preserving and non-expansive kernel maps (A1–A2), and patch sizes tied to pooling scales (A3). Many modern CNNs employ non-Gaussian pooling/striding and ReLU; the paper relies on smooth, homogeneous activations (smoothed ReLU) and homogenization factors nk(u), which are nonstandard in practice.
- Bounds and constants: The stability bound grows linearly with depth and includes constants C1 that depend on κ and dimension; quantitative tightness and practical magnitudes are unclear. Translation invariance term decays as 1/σn but at the expense of information loss due to low-pass pooling.
- “Signal preservation” claims: Lemma 1/2 rely on perfect deconvolution and sufficient coverage; in discrete/noisy settings deconvolution is ill-conditioned. The conditions (ēk ≥ sk, small pooling scale) are plausible but the robustness of reconstruction is not quantified.
- Group invariance section: Provides an equivariant construction but lacks quantitative stability/invariance bounds analogous to Theorem 6 on non-abelian groups or subgroups beyond qualitative discussion.
- Empirical validation: No experiments demonstrating the predicted stability/invariance trade-offs, the effect of RKHS norm control on generalization, or comparisons to scattering/CNNs.
- CNN RKHS inclusion: Proposition 7 depends on kernel-specific Cσ; for common choices, these constants may be large, potentially making the norm bounds vacuous. The connection to practical training/regularization (how to control this norm in CNN training) is not detailed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Proof strategy (operator norms, commutators, Schur’s test) is solid and consistent with scattering literature. Assumptions are explicit; the extension to projected finite-dimensional approximations is reasonable via non-expansiveness. Some claims (signal recovery) would benefit from quantified stability to noise/finite support.
- Completeness: The theoretical development is largely complete for the stated setting. The lack of empirical support limits practical impact assessment.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Continuous/discrete notations are carefully separated. Suggestions:
  - Clarify reconstruction procedures and stability (conditioning) in Lemma 1/2.
  - Make A3 more concrete and discuss how it maps to typical CNN hyperparameters (kernel sizes, strides).
  - Provide explicit examples/plots of admissible kernels and Cσ values; quantify constants in a 2D image setting.
  - Discuss boundary effects for finite supports and their impact on stability.

Originality
- Extends scattering stability theory to a broader convolutional kernel class and connects it rigorously to a subset of CNNs via RKHS inclusion. The commutator extension to shifted kernels and the recursive RKHS norm bound for CNNs are novel and nontrivial.

Significance
- The work advances theoretical understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations beyond scattering, with potential implications for regularization and sample complexity. The framework is likely to inform future analyses of CNN robustness and design of kernelized deep models. Practical significance would be strengthened by empirical validation.

Detailed questions/suggestions
- How sensitive is Theorem 6 to choosing non-Gaussian pooling (e.g., compactly supported low-pass)? Can the lemmas be adapted?
- Can you quantify constants C1, C2 for d=2 and typical σk schedules, to illustrate trade-offs?
- For discretization, can you provide Riesz-type stability bounds for deconvolution under noise? What happens when ek < sk, as in some strided convs?
- Does the stability bound persist with strided convolutions replacing explicit pooling, or with dilations?
- In Proposition 7, please instantiate Cσ for the inverse polynomial kernel and a specific smooth ReLU, to gauge norm sizes for small networks.
- For group invariance, can you provide a theorem analogous to Theorem 6 on a compact group (e.g., SO(2)), quantifying stability to small group perturbations and global invariance?
- Discuss the relation to spectral norm/Lipschitz control in CNNs and adversarial robustness; can your RKHS norm bounds be related to layerwise spectral norms?

Pros
- Solid theoretical contribution unifying kernels, CNNs, and scattering
- Clear stability/invariance guarantees with intuitive dependencies
- Addresses discretization and kernel approximation
- Extension to group actions and inclusion of a CNN class in RKHS

Cons
- Relies on assumptions (Gaussian pooling, smooth homogeneous activations) that diverge from common practice
- Bounds may be loose with large constants; limited quantitative guidance
- No empirical validation; reconstruction claims may be fragile in practice
- Group invariance section lacks quantitative stability results

Recommendation: Weak accept. High-quality theory with meaningful insights; empirical or more quantitative illustrations would further strengthen the paper.

Reviewer confidence: High.
