## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate that it contains a broad class of CNNs, including convolutional kernel networks (CKNs). They establish theoretical guarantees for translation invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms for representations within this kernel space, achieving results comparable to the scattering transform but for a wider range of CNN architectures. A key finding is that stability, quantified through the RKHS norm, connects to generalization performance.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds upon prior research in convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [Mairal et al.], scattering transforms [Bruna & Mallat], and kernel methods for deep learning [Cho & Saul, Schölkopf et al.]. It aims to bridge the gap between the theoretical understanding of scattering transforms and the more prevalent, learned architectures of modern CNNs. The paper also acknowledges and builds upon related work on group invariance [Cohen & Welling] and adversarial robustness [Cisse et al.].

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid mathematical foundation for analyzing CNN representations, leveraging the well-established theory of positive definite kernels and reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS).  The proofs appear detailed and well-structured (though I didn't verify all of them).
* **Generality:** The kernel framework allows the authors to analyze a broader class of CNNs than previous theoretical work focused on scattering transforms or CKNs. Demonstrating that common CNN architectures fall within their framework is a significant achievement.
* **Connection to Generalization:**  Establishing a link between stability (RKHS norm) and generalization is a crucial contribution. This provides a theoretical grounding for the observed benefits of regularization techniques in deep learning.
* **Discretization Analysis:** The thorough discussion of discretization, and how to preserve signal information during this process, is particularly valuable for practical applications.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making the complex theoretical concepts relatively accessible. The inclusion of a notation section is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Core Results:** While the extension of stability results from scattering transforms to a wider class of CNNs is valuable, the core ideas (stability to diffeomorphisms, connection to generalization) are not entirely novel.  The primary contribution is the *framework* for analysis, rather than a breakthrough new result.
* **Practical Implications Not Fully Explored:**  The paper doesn't delve deeply into the practical implications of the theoretical results. How can these insights be used to *design* better CNNs or regularization strategies? A more detailed discussion of this would be beneficial.  For example, how do different kernel choices impact stability and generalization in practice?
* **Assumptions:** Several assumptions are made (e.g., smoothness of activation functions, homogeneous kernels) that may limit the applicability of the results to certain CNN architectures. The discussion of how these assumptions affect the applicability of the findings could be strengthened.
* **Computational Cost:** The theoretical framework using RKHSs can be computationally expensive, which is acknowledged in relation to CKNs, but a further discussion around the scalability of this approach would be useful.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by mathematical analysis. However, the limited novelty of the core results prevents a higher score.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is generally very well-written and organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the explanations are generally accessible.
* **Originality (7/10):** The framework for analyzing CNNs using kernel methods is a valuable contribution, but the underlying ideas are rooted in existing work.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results are likely to be of interest to researchers working on the theoretical foundations of deep learning. The connection between stability and generalization is a potentially important insight.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Provides a rigorous theoretical framework for analyzing CNN representations.
* Extends existing stability results to a broader class of CNNs.
* Establishes a link between stability and generalization.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Core results are not entirely novel.
* Practical implications are not fully explored.
* Relies on several assumptions that may limit applicability.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of CNNs. While the core results are not revolutionary, the rigorous theoretical framework and the connection between stability and generalization are significant. I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions, specifically requesting that the authors:

1.  Expand the discussion of practical implications – how can these insights be used to design better CNNs or regularization strategies?
2.  Clarify the limitations imposed by the assumptions made in the analysis.
3. Briefly address the computational cost and scalability of the proposed framework.
