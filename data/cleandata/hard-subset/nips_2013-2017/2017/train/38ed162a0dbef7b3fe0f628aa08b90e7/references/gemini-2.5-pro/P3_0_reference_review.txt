### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors' main contribution is to develop a framework based on multilayer convolutional kernels, which generalize Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), to study these properties. By constructing a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) associated with this kernel, they derive formal guarantees on the representation's stability to small diffeomorphisms, akin to the results established for scattering transforms. Specifically, they show that the representation's variation is bounded by a term proportional to the deformation's gradient norm (stability) and another term that decays with the final pooling scale (translation invariance). A key result is demonstrating that a large class of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with smooth, homogeneous activation functions can be embedded within this RKHS. This embedding allows the stability guarantees derived for the kernel representation to be transferred to these CNNs, providing a principled connection between the RKHS norm of a network (related to generalization) and its stability to input deformations. The work also extends this framework to achieve global invariance to arbitrary group actions.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous and technically sound analysis, bridging the gap between the well-understood theory of scattering networks and the less-understood properties of modern, learned CNNs. It successfully extends the harmonic analysis techniques used by Mallat [17] to a more general class of architectures within a kernel framework. This is a significant theoretical achievement.

2.  **Elegant Synthesis of Concepts:** The work masterfully connects several important areas: deep learning (CNNs), kernel methods (RKHS, CKNs), and signal processing (scattering transforms, group theory). By framing CNNs within an RKHS, the authors provide a powerful lens through which to understand their geometric properties. The link between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization (via Eq. 1) is particularly insightful, offering a principled explanation for why models that generalize well are often robust to small input variations.

3.  **Generalization and Broader Applicability:** The framework is more general than that of the scattering transform. While scattering networks rely on fixed wavelet filters, this kernel-based approach can accommodate learned features (as implemented in CKNs). The demonstration that standard CNNs (under certain assumptions) are contained within the function space is a crucial step towards making the theory relevant to practical models. The extension to general group invariance in Section 4 is also a valuable and well-formulated contribution.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The authors clearly lay out their multilayer kernel construction, systematically prove the stability properties, and then carefully establish the connection to CNNs. The notation is consistent, and the theoretical development is easy to follow for a reader familiar with the prerequisite concepts.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The Gap Between Theory and Practice for CNNs:** The connection to CNNs in Section 5, while being a central contribution, relies on two important assumptions that distance the theoretical model from widely-used CNN architectures.
    *   **Activation Functions:** The theory requires smooth, homogeneous activation functions. The most common activation, ReLU, is homogeneous but not smooth. The authors propose smooth variants, but it is unclear how well the theoretical bounds would apply to the non-smooth, piecewise linear functions used in practice.
    *   **Normalization Term:** The CNN construction in Eq. (8) includes a normalization factor `nk(u) = ||Pkzk-1(u)||`. This is not a standard component in modern architectures like ResNets or VGGs (though it bears some resemblance to local response normalization). The necessity of this term for the embedding into the RKHS makes the class of covered CNNs somewhat specialized. A discussion on the possibility of relaxing this requirement would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theoretical contribution is strong on its own, the claims could have been substantially reinforced with even simple experiments. For example, one could empirically measure the stability of a CNN trained with a smooth activation function versus one with a standard ReLU to see if the theoretical predictions about stability hold. Alternatively, one could try to approximate the RKHS norm of a trained network and correlate it with its empirically measured robustness to deformations, providing evidence for the link between norm, stability, and generalization.

3.  **Signal Preservation:** The authors argue that the lack of norm preservation (unlike in scattering transforms) is not a major concern. The pooling operators `Ak` act as low-pass filters and can discard significant high-frequency information. While natural signals are often low-frequency dominated, this information loss can be critical for certain tasks. The argument that a final Gaussian kernel mapping can restore a unit norm feels like a post-hoc fix rather than an intrinsic property of the representation. A more detailed analysis of the trade-off between the stability gained through pooling and the information lost would be beneficial.

### Overall Evaluation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep learning. It provides a novel and powerful framework for analyzing the geometric properties of CNNs.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The mathematical derivations are sound and build upon a solid foundation of related work in harmonic analysis and kernel methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is excellently written and easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it draws heavily from scattering transforms and CKNs, the synthesis is novel, and the extension of stability results to a class of standard CNNs is a new and important contribution.
*   **Significance:** The results are very significant for the theoretical deep learning community. The paper provides new tools and insights for understanding why CNNs work, linking their stability to their generalization capacity in a principled manner. This work is likely to be influential and inspire further research into the geometry of deep representations.

Despite the weaknesses related to the gap between the theoretical model and practical CNNs, the paper's strengths are overwhelming. The theoretical framework itself is a major step forward. I strongly recommend acceptance.
