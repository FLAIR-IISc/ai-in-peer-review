Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals on continuous domains, yielding a CNN-like hierarchy: patch extraction, a kernel feature map into an RKHS, and linear Gaussian pooling at each layer. The authors analyze invariance to translations and Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms, deriving bounds of the form C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞, paralleling and extending Mallat’s scattering framework to a broader class of architectures. They further (i) bridge to practice via discretization with subsampling and show signal recoverability under mild conditions, (ii) show that kernel approximation via projections (CKNs) preserves stability due to non-expansiveness, (iii) generalize pooling to locally compact groups to obtain equivariance and global invariance to general group actions via Haar convolution, and (iv) prove that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, with explicit RKHS norm bounds that control Lipschitz stability and connect to generalization (via margin-type arguments).

The work synthesizes ideas from scattering networks [Bruna & Mallat; Mallat], CKNs [Mairal et al.], group-equivariant CNNs [Cohen & Welling], and invariant kernels [Haasdonk & Burkhardt; Anselmi et al.], but pushes beyond by providing a unified RKHS construction that contains both CKNs and a family of CNNs, and by porting scattering-style stability guarantees to this hybrid kernel/CNN setting.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: The multilayer convolutional kernel provides a clean functional-analytic framework where one can separate representation from prediction, control geometry via RKHS norms, and reason about invariance/stability. This bridges scattering, CKNs, and (a subset of) CNNs in one space.
- Novel stability analysis for more general architectures: The commutator-based proof (Proposition 3 + Lemmas 4–5) yields diffeomorphism stability for representations that are not rigid wavelet cascades. The bound’s dependence on pooling scale and depth is transparent and interpretable.
- Group invariance: The extension to general locally compact groups via equivariant patch extraction and pooling is elegant, and clearly delineates where stability (inner layers) and global invariance (final pooling) arise.
- Practical connections: The discretization and kernel approximation sections show how the continuous theory informs discrete models, including CKNs. The non-expansive projection argument preserving stability is useful for implementations.
- CNN containment and norm control: Showing that CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations live in the RKHS, together with recursive norm bounds, provides a principled link between architecture, stability, and generalization via the RKHS norm.
- Clarity of assumptions: The paper states the needed conditions (norm-preserving/non-expansive kernel maps, patch/pooling scale relation, small ‖∇τ‖∞), making the scope of the theorems explicit.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Practical gap to modern CNNs: The RKHS containment requires smooth homogeneous activations. ReLU is handled only via smoothing; batch norm, striding, max-pooling, and non-Gaussian filters are not covered explicitly. It would help to discuss how sensitive the results are to replacing Gaussian pooling by commonly used discrete pooling/stride operations.
- Constants and tightness: The bounds hide constants depending on h and κ and scale at least linearly with depth. A discussion (or empirical proxy) for their magnitudes would clarify practical relevance.
- Norm preservation and energy loss: Unlike scattering, the representation does not preserve norm due to pooling. The text suggests adding a top Gaussian RKHS layer to normalize, but a more concrete treatment of energy retention for natural images would strengthen the argument.
- Discretization and recovery: Signal recoverability with deconvolution assumes favorable filters and boundary conditions; stability of that inversion in realistic settings (noise, finite support) is only briefly mentioned.
- Empirical validation: The paper is theoretically focused. Even small experiments illustrating the predicted stability vs. σn and n for CKNs or smoothed-ReLU CNNs would substantiate the claims and help readers gauge constants.
- Clarity/notation density: The manuscript is quite notation-heavy. A running 1D example carried across sections and a summary figure mapping operators to standard CNN layers would improve accessibility.

Questions for the authors
- Can the framework admit exact ReLU via limiting kernels (e.g., inverse polynomial with a limit) or alternative RKHS constructions, and do the norm bounds remain meaningful?
- How do the stability results change with non-Gaussian pooling (e.g., box, learned low-pass) and with explicit subsampling/striding? Are analogous commutator bounds available?
- What are typical values for C1, C2 in practice for 2D images and common σk schedules? Any numerical stability plots?
- Could the group-equivariant pooling be combined with partial pooling along subgroups earlier and full pooling late to yield tighter constants or better depth scaling?

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, original theoretical contribution unifying kernels, CNNs, and scattering with new stability guarantees beyond wavelet-based constructions.
- Clear path from continuous theory to practical CKNs via non-expansive projections.
- General, elegant extension to invariance on arbitrary locally compact groups.
- Insightful link between RKHS norm, stability, and generalization that may influence future architecture design and regularization.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical corroboration; practical impact on mainstream CNNs (with ReLU, striding, batch norm) is indirect.
- Bounds may be loose; constants and depth dependence not quantified.
- Some assumptions (Gaussian pooling, deconvolution-based recovery) are idealized relative to modern pipelines.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a bold and timely theoretical paper that advances our understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations by unifying kernel methods, scattering theory, and a subset of CNNs. While empirical validation is light and some assumptions are idealized, the conceptual novelty and the generality of the analysis make it a valuable contribution likely to spur follow-up work in both theory and practice.
