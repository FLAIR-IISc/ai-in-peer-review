

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper presents a theoretical framework for understanding the invariance and stability properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) using positive definite kernels. The authors generalize convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16] to continuous domains, establishing a connection between kernel representations and standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The work builds on Mallat's scattering transform [17] but extends it to more standard CNN architectures, providing theoretical guarantees for translation-invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms.

The main contributions include: (1) a rigorous analysis of the stability properties of the kernel-based representation under diffeomorphisms, (2) a characterization of how the representation preserves signal information, (3) a connection showing that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization, and (4) a theoretical link between this framework and standard CNNs through homogeneous activation functions. The paper also extends the framework to handle group invariance under transformations like rotations.

**Strengths:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by bridging the gap between the scattering transform framework and standard CNNs. The mathematical analysis is thorough and well-supported by detailed proofs in the appendix. The authors successfully demonstrate that their kernel framework generalizes CKNs and provides theoretical guarantees similar to the scattering transform, while being applicable to more standard CNN architectures. The connection between stability and generalization via the RKHS norm is particularly insightful, providing a theoretical foundation for understanding why stable representations may lead to better sample complexity.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written for a technical audience, with consistent notation and careful exposition of mathematical concepts. The authors properly situate their work within the existing literature, citing relevant NIPS papers [5, 15, 16, 17, 22] and other foundational work on invariance in deep learning [6, 9, 17]. The discussion of the relationship between the kernel framework and standard CNNs (Section 5) is particularly strong, with Proposition 7 establishing a concrete connection between CNNs and the RKHS.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper's highly theoretical nature may limit its accessibility to a broader NIPS audience. While the mathematical rigor is commendable, the paper could better articulate the practical implications of the theoretical results. For instance, it would be valuable to discuss how the stability results might inform practical model design choices (e.g., layer depth, pooling size, or activation functions) or suggest concrete experiments to validate the theoretical claims.

The connection between the theoretical framework and specific, widely-used CNN architectures (like ResNet or EfficientNet) is somewhat abstract. While the paper shows that CNNs with homogeneous activations are contained in the RKHS, it doesn't provide concrete examples of how these theoretical insights might translate to improved model performance in practice. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the stability bounds might inform model selection or regularization strategies.

The paper also lacks empirical validation of the theoretical results. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the absence of experiments to demonstrate the practical relevance of the stability bounds weakens the significance claim. A small set of experiments showing how the stability properties correlate with generalization performance would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Include a brief discussion of practical implications: How might the theoretical stability bounds inform the design of CNNs? For example, how would the bounds suggest optimal pooling sizes or layer depths?

2. Add a concrete example: Show how the framework applies to a specific CNN architecture (e.g., ResNet-18) and discuss how the stability properties might explain its performance on a specific dataset.

3. Include minimal experiments: Even a simple experiment demonstrating that CNNs with better stability properties (as predicted by the theory) exhibit better generalization would greatly strengthen the paper's significance.

4. Clarify the relationship to practical implementations: The paper mentions CKNs as a practical implementation, but it would be helpful to provide more details on how the theoretical framework translates to actual training procedures.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding the invariance and stability properties of CNNs. The mathematical analysis is sound, the connection to existing work is well-executed, and the results have potential significance for both theory and practice. However, the lack of empirical validation and limited discussion of practical implications prevents the paper from fully demonstrating its significance for the broader NIPS community.

While the paper is technically strong and makes a genuine theoretical contribution, the absence of concrete connections to practical model design and limited empirical validation reduces its impact. The theoretical framework is elegant but somewhat abstract without clearer demonstrations of how it translates to real-world performance improvements.

**Recommendation:**

Weak Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of CNNs, but would benefit from additional discussion of practical implications and minimal empirical validation to better demonstrate its significance. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
