

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper proposes a theoretical framework using positive definite kernels to study invariance and stability properties of convolutional networks, claiming to generalize convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and provide theoretical guarantees similar to the scattering transform. The authors derive stability bounds under diffeomorphisms and explore connections to standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations.

**Strengths:** The paper presents technically sound mathematical analysis building on established frameworks (RKHS, harmonic analysis) and properly cites relevant prior work, including Mallat's scattering transform and Mairal's CKNs. The theoretical development is rigorous, with careful attention to mathematical details and assumptions.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the strong theoretical foundation, the paper suffers from significant limitations that undermine its contribution to the NIPS community:

1. **Lack of empirical validation:** The paper provides no experiments on real datasets, failing to demonstrate how the theoretical framework translates to practical improvements in model performance. NIPS papers are expected to include either theoretical results with empirical validation or strong empirical results with theoretical justification. This paper offers neither.

2. **Tenuous connection to practical CNNs:** The paper claims to connect to standard CNNs but restricts analysis to "smooth homogeneous activation functions" while acknowledging that ReLU (the most popular activation) doesn't satisfy their requirements. The authors propose a "smoothed variant" of ReLU, but provide no evidence that this variant would outperform standard ReLU in practice or that it's necessary for the theoretical results.

3. **Limited novelty:** The main results (stability bounds under diffeomorphisms) are essentially a rederivation of Mallat's results for the scattering transform, with minimal new insights. The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how their framework provides advantages over existing approaches for practical deep learning.

4. **No comparison to established baselines:** There's no discussion of how the theoretical guarantees translate to improved performance on standard benchmarks, nor any comparison to established methods like standard CNNs, scattering networks, or other invariant representations. The paper claims "improved sample complexity" but provides no empirical evidence.

5. **Computational impracticality:** The paper acknowledges that the continuous-domain formulation is abstract and requires discretization, but doesn't address how the approach would scale to large-scale image recognition tasks. The kernel approximation techniques described (CKNs) are known to be computationally expensive compared to standard CNN training.

6. **Misleading claims about applicability:** The paper states "the RKHS HKn contains a large class of CNNs" but the class is highly restricted to smooth homogeneous activations. The most successful CNN architectures (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet) use ReLU and other non-homogeneous activations, making the connection largely theoretical rather than practically relevant.

**Critical assessment:** As a reviewer who prioritizes established methods and rigorous validation, I find the paper's theoretical contribution insufficiently compelling. The framework is a natural extension of existing work but fails to demonstrate why it matters for practitioners. The absence of any empirical results on standard vision datasets is particularly concerning for a NIPS submission, as it prevents evaluation of the approach's practical relevance.

The paper would benefit significantly from:
- Experiments comparing the kernel-based approach to standard CNNs on benchmark datasets
- Analysis of how the theoretical guarantees translate to actual model performance
- Discussion of computational costs compared to standard CNN training
- Clearer explanation of why the smooth homogeneous activation requirement doesn't limit practical applicability

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the theoretical analysis is technically sound, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed framework offers meaningful advantages over established methods. The lack of empirical validation, limited connection to practical CNN architectures, and minimal novelty relative to existing work make it unsuitable for NIPS. The paper would be better suited for a more theoretical venue, but even then, it would need substantial empirical validation to be compelling.
