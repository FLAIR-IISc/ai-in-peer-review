- Proposes a multilayer convolutional kernel framework mirroring CNNs/CKNs with patch extraction, homogeneous dot-product RKHS maps, and Gaussian pooling to build Φn(x) with growing receptive fields and controlled invariance.  
- Main theoretical result (Theorem 6): Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms: ||Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)|| ≤ [C1(1+n)||∇τ||∞ + C2/σn ||τ||∞] ||x||, extending scattering-style stability to more general kernels/architectures.  
- Technical novelty: analysis of commutators [Lc Aσ, Lτ], extending Mallat’s scattering analysis.  
- Addresses discretization and conditions for information preservation and deconvolution-based recovery after subsampling.  
- Shows kernel approximation via projections (CKN implementation) preserves non-expansiveness and thus stability.  
- Generalizes pooling to locally compact groups to obtain equivariance and a final global invariance layer.  
- Connects the multilayer RKHS HKn to a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, giving recursive bounds on RKHS norms (Proposition 7) to link stability and generalization.  
- Strength: principled operator-theoretic framework that mirrors modern CNN components while enabling RKHS analysis.  
- Strength: explicit dependence of stability on local Jacobian and translation magnitude; pooling scale improves translation invariance.  
- Strength: integration of discretization and kernel approximation with recovery lemmas clarifying information flow across layers.  
- Strength: clean, general group-equivariant pooling construction combining local stability and global invariance.  
- Strength: inclusion of a CNN class in RKHS with recursive norm bounds ties architecture/weights to RKHS norms.  
- Writing generally clear with precise assumptions and adequate relation to prior work.  
- Weakness: no empirical validation to calibrate constants, verify deformation-Lipschitz behavior, or illustrate trade-offs among σk, patch sizes, and depth.  
- Weakness: assumptions exclude common ReLU activations unless smoothed; normalization nk(u)=||Pk xk−1(u)|| changes usual forward semantics and may complicate implementation.  
- Weakness: linear (1+n) depth dependence in stability may be pessimistic; tighter depth dependence is not shown.  
- Weakness: deconvolution-based recovery from Gaussian pooling is ill-conditioned with noise/finite support and lacks quantified stability.  
- Weakness: constants C1, C2 and their dependence on dimension, κ, and kernel parameters are not explicit or practically guided.  
- Weakness: group-invariant construction mainly parallels existing work; novelty is mainly its unification in the kernel framework.  
- Weakness: claim that RKHS norm controls stability/generalization is standard; lacks concrete generalization bounds in terms of the recursive coefficients Bk,i.  
- Quality: technically sound with coherent operator bounds and proofs, but missing empirical checks and explicit constants.  
- Clarity: well-organized with clear assumptions; some minor typos and suggested figure to illustrate the operator pipeline.  
- Originality: novel blend of CKN-style RKHS layers with scattering-style deformation analysis, shifted commutator bound, and recursive norm bound for CNNs.  
- Significance: likely to advance theoretical understanding of invariance/stability beyond wavelets, though the lack of experiments reduces immediate practical impact.  
- Questions for authors: can results extend to ReLU via smoothing with controlled constants; how do C1/C2 scale with d and κ; is linear depth factor necessary; how stable is deconvolution to noise; sensitivity of norm bounds to kernel choice?  
- Suggestions: add small synthetic experiments verifying bounds, give examples/recipes for admissible σ and Cσ and hyperparameter selection, and discuss connections to spectral-norm/Parseval regularization.  
- Reproducibility: theoretical derivations reproducible from appendices; including reference CKN layer implementations would help.  
- Overall recommendation: accept (7/10) — solid novel theoretical contribution with practical gaps (no experiments, ReLU applicability, explicit constants).
