Summary
This paper studies invariance and stability of deep convolutional representations through a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs). The authors (i) construct a continuous-domain, layered representation from patch extraction, kernel mapping, and Gaussian pooling; (ii) prove stability to C1-diffeomorphisms in the sense of Mallat (Lipschitz with respect to ∥∇τ∥∞ and ∥τ∥∞, with translation invariance controlled by the last pooling scale σn); (iii) discuss discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKN) and show stability is preserved by non-expansive projections; (iv) extend the construction to general transformation groups via group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling; and (v) show that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and linear pooling are contained in the RKHS of the proposed multilayer kernel, yielding norm-based control of stability and generalization for these CNNs. The work builds conceptually on scattering theory (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) but broadens it to more standard CNN-like architectures and kernel representations, and connects stability to margin-like RKHS norms.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The core stability result (Theorem 6) is carefully derived. The decomposition via commutators (Proposition 3) is standard and appropriate; bounds on [PkAk−1, Lτ] (Lemma 4) follow from integral operator techniques and Schur’s test; translation invariance is correctly tied to ∥LτAn − An∥ (Lemma 5), with decay in 1/σn. Assumptions (A1–A3) are reasonable within the chosen kernel class: homogeneous dot-product kernels ensure norm preservation and non-expansiveness, and patch diameter proportional to the prior pooling scale matches CNN design heuristics.
- Completeness and limitations acknowledged: The authors are explicit that pooling is not norm-preserving and that stability holds for small deformations (∥∇τ∥∞ < 1/2). They discuss discretization (Lemmas 1–2) and show signal recoverability under mild conditions (ek ≥ sk) via deconvolution, while noting practical stability constraints.
- CNN link: Proposition 7 is a useful bridge: with smooth homogeneous activations, CNN predictors are elements of the RKHS HKn, with a recursive norm bound Bk,i that depends on filter norms and the kernel-dependent constant Cσ. This is consistent with prior theory (e.g., Cho & Saul; Daniely et al.; Poggio et al.) but extends it to multilayer convolutional kernels and stability guarantees.
- Scope: Proofs are relegated to the appendix; within the main text, statements are precise and plausible. Empirical validations are not the focus; the paper is a theoretical contribution. One technical caveat is the depth dependence (1 + n) in the stability bound; discussion of tightness or possibilities for better accumulation control (e.g., via scale growth) would strengthen the analysis.

Clarity
The manuscript is well-organized and clearly written for a theory paper, with careful notation and explicit operator definitions. The continuous-to-discrete discussion is helpful and connects to practice (CKNs). Suggestions for clarity:
- Provide concrete examples of admissible activations with finite Cσ and a short table of kernels with their Cσ to guide practitioners; explicitly discuss how a smoothed ReLU fits and the trade-offs versus true ReLU.
- Offer brief intuition for the growth of constants with κ and dimension (κd+1) and for the linear-in-depth factor.
- Include a short illustrative experiment or toy example to visualize invariance vs. σn and the stability bound behavior.

Originality
The paper’s novelty lies in extending scattering-style stability guarantees to a broader class of multilayer convolutional kernel representations and, via Proposition 7, to a nontrivial subset of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations. The group-equivariant pooling construction is conceptually aligned with Cohen & Welling and scattering on groups, but the kernel/RKHS framing that controls stability and prediction via norms is a meaningful twist. The discretization and approximation discussion ties theory to the practical CKN architecture. Related work is appropriately covered.

Significance
The results advance theoretical understanding of invariance and stability in CNN-like architectures, and they give a principled pathway to control both stability and generalization through RKHS norms. This is valuable for theory and may inform architecture/regularizer design (e.g., choosing σn, patch scales, and norm control). The lack of empirical demonstration limits immediate practical impact, but the conceptual contribution is strong and likely to be cited by researchers working on theory of deep representations, kernels, and equivariance.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated theoretical framework connecting kernels, CNNs, and stability.
- Generalizes scattering stability to a richer class of architectures.
- Precise operator-theoretic analysis with intuitive consequences (σn controls translation invariance).
- Inclusion result for CNNs in the RKHS with norm bounds, linking to generalization.
- Extends to general groups with an explicit equivariant layer design.

Cons
- Requires smooth homogeneous activations; direct applicability to standard ReLU CNNs is indirect (via smoothing).
- Depth dependence is linear; tightness and implications for very deep nets are unclear.
- Stability constants depend on design parameters (κ, σk) in ways that may be large in high dimensions.
- Discrete recovery relies on deconvolution stability and assumes ek ≥ sk; practical robustness is not quantified.
- No empirical validation to complement the theory.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can the (1 + n) factor be improved (e.g., replaced by a sum weighted by decreasing operator norms as σk grows)? Any lower bounds indicating necessity?
- How sensitive are constants to non-Gaussian pooling filters? Are there optimal choices for h balancing stability and information retention?
- Can you quantify the degradation introduced by finite-dimensional projections (CKN) on the stability bound?
- Provide explicit activations with finite Cσ (e.g., softplus with parameter) and report typical Cσ values.
- Any empirical illustration (even synthetic) showing the predicted trade-off between σn and translation invariance, and the margin–stability link?

Overall evaluation
A solid, careful, and timely theoretical contribution that strengthens our understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations beyond scattering, with a meaningful bridge to practical architectures via CKNs and a class of CNNs. I recommend acceptance, while encouraging the authors to add small illustrative experiments or more concrete guidance on constants/activations.
