This paper tackles a fundamental, and often overlooked, question regarding the properties of deep convolutional neural networks: understanding the geometry of the functional spaces they inhabit, and specifically, their invariance and stability. The authors approach this problem through the lens of positive definite kernels, extending convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and drawing parallels to the scattering transform. This is a *bold* and conceptually appealing direction, aiming to bridge the gap between the empirical success of CNNs and a more rigorous theoretical understanding.

The core idea – representing CNNs within a kernel framework to analyze their stability and invariance – is highly novel. While kernel methods have been combined with neural networks before (as the related work section details), this paper goes beyond simply *using* kernels and attempts to *characterize* the functional space *of* CNNs via a kernel construction. The connection to the scattering transform is also insightful, leveraging existing theory to establish similar guarantees for more standard architectures. The theorem establishing stability under diffeomorphisms (Theorem 6) is a significant result, and the effort to tie it back to practical considerations like discretization and homogeneous activations is commendable.

However, the paper isn’t without its weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is well-developed, the empirical validation is completely absent. The authors mention the potential for regularization based on RKHS norms and improved sample complexity, but provide *no* experimental evidence to support these claims. This is a substantial omission, especially given the computational advances that make experimenting with these ideas feasible. Furthermore, the connection to “standard” CNNs, while stated in Proposition 7, feels somewhat hand-wavy. The requirement of “smooth homogeneous activations” significantly narrows the class of CNNs to which the results apply, and the implications of this restriction aren't fully explored. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it's a significant one. The notation is also dense and requires careful parsing, potentially hindering accessibility. Finally, the repeated references to an "extended version of the paper" for proofs and details are slightly frustrating – a complete treatment within the main body would be preferable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The kernel framework for analyzing CNNs is a genuinely novel approach.
*   **Strong theoretical foundation:** The stability and invariance results are mathematically rigorous and build upon established theory.
*   **Potential for significant impact:** A deeper understanding of the geometry of CNNs could lead to improved regularization techniques and more sample-efficient learning algorithms.
*   **Bridging different fields:** Successfully connects concepts from kernel methods, harmonic analysis, and deep learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of empirical validation:** The absence of experiments is a major drawback.
*   **Limited applicability:** The requirement of smooth homogeneous activations restricts the scope of the results.
*   **Notational complexity:** Dense notation can hinder readability.
*   **Incomplete presentation:** Reliance on an "extended version" for crucial details.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the lack of empirical validation and some limitations in scope, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach and the strength of the theoretical results outweigh the weaknesses. The paper tackles a fundamental question with a fresh perspective and lays the groundwork for future research. I am willing to overlook the missing experiments in favor of the innovative theoretical framework.  I recommend **acceptance**, but strongly encourage the authors to include experimental results in a follow-up work. The potential payoff of this research is substantial, and further investigation is warranted.
