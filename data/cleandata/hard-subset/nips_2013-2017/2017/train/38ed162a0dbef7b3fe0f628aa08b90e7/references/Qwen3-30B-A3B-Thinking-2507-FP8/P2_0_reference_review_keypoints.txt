- Summary: Introduces a multilayer convolutional kernel linking CKNs and standard CNNs, proving translation-invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms and showing RKHS norms control stability and generalization, generalizing the scattering transform.

- Strength — Quality: Strong, rigorous theoretical contributions with well-structured proofs and careful assumptions that connect kernel theory to deep convolutional architectures.

- Strength — Clarity: Exceptionally well-written and organized; mathematical notation and logical flow are clear, with good discretization and implementation discussions.

- Strength — Originality: Novel kernel framework embedding standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and a new link between diffeomorphism stability and generalization via RKHS norms.

- Strength — Significance: Addresses why CNNs generalize, offering a framework that could influence regularization, robust model design, and future theoretical work.

- Weakness — Limited Experimental Validation: Lacks empirical experiments demonstrating how theoretical claims translate to performance on real tasks.

- Weakness — Scope of CNNs: Results rely on smooth homogeneous activations, leaving applicability to common non-smooth activations like ReLU insufficiently established.

- Weakness — Practical Implementation: Does not analyze computational complexity or efficient implementation of the kernel-based approach compared to standard CNNs.

- Overall Assessment: A substantial, elegant theoretical advance that fills an important gap in deep learning theory despite limited empirical validation.

- Recommendation: Accept.
