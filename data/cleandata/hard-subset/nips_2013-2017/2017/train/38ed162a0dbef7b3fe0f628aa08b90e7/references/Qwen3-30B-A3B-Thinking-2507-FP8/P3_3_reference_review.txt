

# Peer Review: "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

## Summary

This paper presents a theoretical framework to study invariance and stability properties of convolutional representations using kernel methods. The authors generalize the scattering transform framework to standard convolutional architectures by introducing a multilayer convolutional kernel that encompasses convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The paper establishes rigorous stability guarantees to diffeomorphisms and translation invariance, showing that the RKHS norm of the representation controls both stability and generalization properties. The work builds upon Mallat's scattering transform [17] and CKNs [15,16], extending them to provide theoretical foundations for more standard CNN architectures.

## Strengths

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by bridging kernel methods with CNNs in a principled way. The authors' main technical achievement is Theorem 6, which establishes a stability bound to diffeomorphisms for their multilayer representation. This result is well-motivated and rigorously proven, with careful attention to the commutator bounds for each layer. The connection between the RKHS norm and generalization properties (Proposition 7) is particularly insightful, as it provides a theoretical foundation for the empirical observation that stable representations generalize better.

The paper's theoretical framework is comprehensive and well-structured, with clear definitions and careful analysis of the different components (patch extraction, kernel mapping, pooling). The authors properly acknowledge related work, including the scattering transform [17], CKNs [15,16], and group invariance literature [9,17,22]. The extension to group invariance (Section 4) is well-executed and adds significant value to the framework.

## Weaknesses

The most significant weakness is the lack of empirical validation. The paper makes strong theoretical claims about the stability and generalization properties of CNNs, but provides no experiments to demonstrate how these theoretical guarantees translate to practical performance on real datasets. Without empirical evidence, it's difficult to assess the practical relevance of these theoretical results. The authors mention "practical implementation" in Section 2.2, but this is purely a discussion of discretization and approximation, not an empirical evaluation.

The connection between the theoretical framework and standard CNNs is somewhat abstract. While Proposition 7 shows that certain CNNs are contained in the RKHS, it's not clear how this translates to practical network design or training. The paper doesn't address how to implement the theoretical framework in practice or how it compares to standard CNN training procedures.

The paper also doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of their kernel-based approach compared to standard CNNs. The authors reference the need for finite-dimensional approximations (Section 2.2), but don't discuss the trade-offs between computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees. This is a significant omission, as the practical adoption of any theoretical framework depends heavily on computational feasibility.

The paper could better distinguish its contribution from related work. While the authors cite several relevant papers, they don't sufficiently clarify how their approach differs from recent work on kernel-based CNNs [2,10,18] or from the CKN framework [15,16]. For instance, the paper claims to generalize CKNs, but doesn't explicitly state how their framework improves upon or differs from the CKN construction.

## Significance and Originality

The paper makes a genuinely original contribution by providing a unified theoretical framework for understanding invariance properties in convolutional architectures. The connection between stability to diffeomorphisms and generalization via the RKHS norm is novel and significant. This work addresses a fundamental question about why CNNs work well, contributing to the theoretical understanding of deep learning.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of empirical validation and the abstract nature of the connection to standard CNNs. The paper would be significantly stronger with experiments demonstrating how these theoretical guarantees translate to improved performance on standard vision tasks.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper with strong theoretical contributions that advance our understanding of convolutional representations. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with rigorous proofs for all main results. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the theoretical foundations of CNNs.

However, the lack of empirical validation significantly weakens the paper's impact. The theoretical results are compelling, but without evidence of their practical relevance, the paper remains primarily a theoretical contribution without clear pathways to practical application. The connection to standard CNNs is somewhat tenuous, and the computational considerations are underdeveloped.

For NIPS, which values both theoretical and empirical contributions, this paper would be stronger with additional experimental validation. The theoretical framework is excellent, but the paper would benefit from demonstrating how these theoretical insights can be leveraged in practice.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes an important theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of CNNs, but the lack of empirical validation and limited discussion of practical implementation significantly reduce its impact for a venue like NIPS. If the authors can provide additional experiments showing how their theoretical framework translates to practical improvements in CNN performance, this would be a strong paper for NIPS. As it stands, the theoretical contribution is significant but remains largely disconnected from practical deep learning practice.
