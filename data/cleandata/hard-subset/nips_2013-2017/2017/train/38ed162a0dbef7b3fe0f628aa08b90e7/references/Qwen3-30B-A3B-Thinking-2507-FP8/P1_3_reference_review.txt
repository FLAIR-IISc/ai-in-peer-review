

# Peer Review: "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental question in deep learning: understanding the geometry of functional spaces where convolutional neural networks (CNNs) operate. The authors develop a kernel framework that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16] to analyze invariance and stability properties of convolutional representations. Building on Mallat's scattering transform theory [17], they establish that their framework provides similar stability guarantees to diffeomorphisms while preserving signal information. Crucially, they demonstrate that their functional space contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, allowing their theoretical results to apply directly to standard CNN architectures. The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging the theoretical gap between scattering networks and practical CNNs, showing that RKHS norms control both stability and generalization properties.

This work extends Mallat's scattering transform framework to more standard CNN architectures, while also generalizing CKNs. It relates to recent theoretical work on kernel methods for deep learning [7, 10, 18, 36] and group-invariant representations [6, 9, 22], but provides a more unified theoretical perspective that connects these different approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Quality**: The paper presents a rigorous theoretical framework with careful mathematical definitions and proofs (provided in appendices). The authors establish precise stability bounds for their convolutional kernel representation under diffeomorphisms, demonstrating that stability improves with network depth due to pooling operations. The proofs are sound and well-constructed.

2. **Theoretical Insight**: The key insight that RKHS norms control both stability and generalization is particularly valuable. The paper provides a clear theoretical explanation for why stable representations may lead to better sample complexity, connecting stability to generalization through a principled framework.

3. **Novel Connection**: The paper successfully bridges the gap between theoretical constructions like the scattering transform and practical CNN architectures. By showing that their kernel framework encompasses CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (including smoothed variants of ReLU), they establish that the stability results apply to a broad class of networks.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly define all mathematical concepts before using them, provide a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results, and include helpful figures (Figure 1) to illustrate their multilayer construction.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Empirical Validation**: While the paper is theoretically sound, it lacks empirical validation. The theoretical results would be significantly strengthened by experiments demonstrating how the stability properties translate to improved performance on real-world tasks, especially since the connection to practical CNNs relies on smooth homogeneous activations (which exclude standard ReLU).

2. **Restrictive Activation Assumption**: The paper's connection to standard CNNs requires smooth homogeneous activation functions. While they discuss smoothed variants of ReLU, the paper doesn't fully address how the theory applies to networks using the standard ReLU activation, which dominates modern CNN architectures.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions discretization and kernel approximation (Section 2.1-2.2) but doesn't provide concrete guidance on how to implement their framework in practice. The relationship between the theoretical framework and actual training procedures could be clarified.

4. **Scope of "Stability"**: The paper focuses on stability to diffeomorphisms (small deformations), but doesn't address other important stability concerns like adversarial robustness, which has been a major focus in recent deep learning literature.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the geometric properties of convolutional representations. The framework provides valuable insights into why CNNs work well on natural images and establishes a principled connection between stability and generalization. While the lack of empirical validation is a notable weakness, the paper's strong theoretical foundation and clear exposition make it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Provides a unified theoretical framework connecting scattering transforms and standard CNNs
- Establishes a clear mathematical link between stability and generalization
- Rigorous proofs and careful mathematical treatment
- Addresses a fundamental question in deep learning with significant theoretical implications
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Limited empirical validation of theoretical claims
- Relies on smooth activation functions that don't directly apply to standard CNNs
- Doesn't address other important stability concerns like adversarial robustness
- Implementation details for practical application are sparse

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a substantial contribution to the understanding of deep convolutional representations. While the lack of empirical validation is a limitation, the paper's theoretical insights are significant and well-supported. The connection between stability, invariance, and generalization via RKHS norms provides a valuable framework for future work in designing more robust and sample-efficient deep learning models.

The paper addresses a fundamental question in deep learning that has been largely unexplored theoretically, and the authors present a rigorous framework that advances our understanding of why convolutional architectures work well on natural images. The work is clearly written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

Given the high theoretical quality and significance of the contribution, I recommend acceptance. The paper would be strengthened by future work that validates the theoretical insights experimentally, but the current theoretical contribution is substantial enough for acceptance at NIPS.

**Overall Rating: Strong Accept**
