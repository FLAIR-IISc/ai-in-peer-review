Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework for signals on continuous domains, yielding feature maps built by repeated application of three operators: patch extraction, a norm-preserving kernel map associated with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. The resulting representation induces a positive-definite kernel Kn whose RKHS contains functions of the form f(x) = ⟨w, Φn(x)⟩. The authors analyze translation invariance and Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms, obtaining a bound of the form ‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1 + n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖, with σn the last-layer pooling scale. They extend the construction to local and global invariance on general (locally compact) groups via equivariant patch extraction and pooling. They also link the RKHS to a class of CNNs: CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations can be embedded in HKn with a norm bound depending on layerwise weights, implying predictions inherit stability through the RKHS Lipschitz inequality. Discretization is addressed by defining discrete analogues with subsampling and proving signal recovery under mild conditions (invertible Gaussian blur and ek ≥ sk), and they argue that finite-dimensional kernel approximations (as in Convolutional Kernel Networks, CKNs) preserve stability due to non-expansive projections.

This work connects and generalizes: (i) scattering transforms (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat) by proving analogous stability to diffeomorphisms in a broader class of convolutional kernel representations; (ii) convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.) by providing a continuous-domain analysis and stability guarantees; (iii) group-equivariant CNN literature (Cohen & Welling; Sifre & Mallat) by formulating group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling; and (iv) kernel views of deep networks (Daniely et al.; Anselmi et al.; Montavon et al.) by explicitly characterizing an RKHS that contains a class of CNNs and linking RKHS norm to stability and generalization.

Strengths
- Quality/technical soundness:
  - Clear and rigorous operator-theoretic formulation on L2(Ω, H) with precise use of commutators, Schur’s test, and assumptions ensuring non-expansiveness.
  - The main stability theorem (Theorem 6) is well-motivated, parallels scattering theory, and makes the role of pooling scale and depth explicit.
  - The discretization lemmas provide a concrete bridge to practice and explain when recovery is possible.
  - The RKHS embedding of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, together with a norm bound (Proposition 7), is technically interesting and ties stability to generalization control via RKHS norms.
- Clarity:
  - The hierarchical construction (Pk, Mk, Ak) is clearly defined, and assumptions (A1–A3) are explicit.
  - The relation to prior work is thorough and properly credited.
- Originality:
  - Extends Mallat’s scattering stability analysis to a more general, learnable convolutional kernel setting and establishes inclusion of a class of CNNs in the induced RKHS.
  - Provides a unified kernel-based view that bridges scattering, CKNs, and certain CNNs, with group-equivariant extensions.
- Significance:
  - Offers theoretical grounding for invariance/stability in architectures closer to practical CNNs than scattering, potentially informing design/regularization (e.g., via norm control).
  - The group-invariance formulation is broadly applicable beyond translations.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Quality/technical:
  - Signal recovery via deconvolution of Gaussian pooling is theoretically possible (non-vanishing Fourier transform) but can be ill-conditioned; stability of recovery is only qualitatively discussed (“small enough scale”), without quantitative error bounds.
  - Stability constant grows linearly with depth (1 + n). While similar factors appear in scattering for bounded path length, the practical magnitude of constants C1, C2 (and their dependence on dimension and κ) is not quantified, limiting actionable guidance.
  - The improved sample complexity claim is qualitative; no explicit generalization bounds in terms of the derived RKHS norms are provided.
  - The CNN inclusion requires smooth homogeneous activations; ReLU, while homogeneous, is nonsmooth. The proposed smoothed variants are discussed but empirical or theoretical comparisons of the approximation gap are not provided.
  - Assumption (A2) (non-expansiveness of ϕk) is central; while Appendix B reportedly provides admissible kernels, the main text could better delineate which commonly used kernels satisfy it and the induced Lipschitz constants.
- Clarity:
  - Some steps rely on appendices (e.g., the non-expansiveness of specified kernels, constants in commutator bounds); limited intuition is provided for the role/magnitude of these constants.
  - Notational slips (e.g., missing L2 subscripts in a few places) and minor typos.
- Originality/scope:
  - The group-equivariant section largely recapitulates known constructions in a kernelized setting; novelty there is incremental relative to existing group convolution frameworks.
- Significance/practicality:
  - No empirical evaluation supporting the theoretical stability claims (e.g., measured invariance/stability of CKN features, or effect of σn on translation invariance).
  - The analysis depends on an initial anti-aliasing A0; while realistic, the effect on information/norm preservation is only briefly addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative bounds or empirical illustrations of:
  - The conditioning of deconvolution recovery as a function of pooling scale.
  - The effect of finite-dimensional kernel approximation (projection error) on overall stability constants.
  - Stability to translations/diffeomorphisms in discretized CKNs on simple datasets as σn and depth vary.
- Expand Appendix B or the main text with explicit examples of κ functions/kernels satisfying (A1–A2) and their Lipschitz constants; clarify whether popular arc-cosine kernels, inverse polynomial kernels, or Gaussian RBF on normalized patches are admissible.
- Discuss the gap between smoothed ReLU and ReLU in the RKHS embedding: approximation error, potential impact on norm bounds, and whether the inclusion extends to piecewise-linear activations via limiting arguments.
- If possible, provide a simple norm-based generalization statement (e.g., Rademacher complexity or margin bound) instantiated with the derived RKHS norms to substantiate the sample complexity claims.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid and careful theoretical contribution that generalizes scattering stability to a more flexible convolutional kernel class and links to CNNs via RKHS inclusion.
- Unifies several strands of theory (kernels, scattering, group equivariance) with clear architectural correspondences (patch extraction, kernel map, pooling).
- Potential impact on understanding and regularizing deep convolutional representations.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of empirical validation and limited quantitative guidance on constants may restrict practical influence.
- Key assumptions (smooth homogeneous activations; specific norm-preserving kernels; initial anti-aliasing) may limit direct applicability to standard CNNs.
- Some results (e.g., group equivariance) are conceptually known, with modest novelty in the kernel framing.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper with clear contributions to the understanding of invariance and stability in convolutional representations within a kernel framework and with a principled connection to CNNs.
