Summary
The paper studies invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations through a multilayer convolutional kernel constructed on continuous domains. Each layer applies: (i) patch extraction, (ii) a norm-preserving, non-expansive homogeneous kernel map into an RKHS, and (iii) Gaussian pooling. The overall representation Φn induces a kernel Kn whose RKHS is used to analyze stability to diffeomorphisms, translation invariance, and links to predictive models via norm control.

Main results:
- Stability to diffeomorphisms: Under assumptions (A1–A3) and an initial low-pass A0, Theorem 6 proves Lipschitz stability ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2/σn‖τ‖∞)‖x‖, matching the Mallat framework for scattering but for more general (kernelized) constructions. The proof uses commutator bounds, extending Mallat’s lemma to shifted integral operators.
- Translation invariance: It is governed by the last pooling scale σn, as pooling approximately commutes with translations.
- Discretization and kernel approximation: The paper discusses discrete analogues with subsampling, providing conditions (ēk ≥ s̄k) under which signals are recoverable (via deconvolution) and arguing stability is preserved when replacing exact kernel maps by orthogonal projections (CKNs).
- Group equivariance/invariance: Adapts patch extraction and pooling defined on locally compact groups G to commute with left actions, suggesting inner layers stabilize local deformations (e.g., translations) and the last layer achieves invariance to the whole group.
- Link to CNNs: Proposition 7 shows that CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and a specific normalization (nk(u)=‖Pkzk−1(u)‖) belong to the RKHS HKn, with explicit norm bounds propagating across layers. This connects Lipschitz stability and generalization via RKHS norms.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned between scattering transforms (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) and convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.), borrowing the notion of diffeomorphism stability and hierarchical invariance while replacing wavelets with kernel maps and Gaussian pooling. It references group equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling) and invariant kernels. The extension of commutator bounds to shifted operators and the RKHS inclusion of certain CNNs are the main differentiators.

Strengths
- A clear, principled framework that unifies scattering-style analysis with kernelized multilayer constructions and connects to CKNs.
- The stability theorem with explicit dependence on deformation Jacobian and pooling scale is technically sound and extends known results (notably the commutator bound to LcAσ).
- Group-equivariant construction is clean and general for locally compact groups with Haar measure.
- The RKHS inclusion of a class of CNNs provides a path to norm-based generalization/stability control.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions vs. practice:
  - Gaussian linear pooling at every layer and an initial low-pass A0 are central to the analysis, but modern CNNs typically use strided convolutions or average/max pooling; it is unclear whether the theorems extend to these cases. The paper should clarify which parts survive if Gaussian pooling is replaced by standard operators.
  - The activation and normalization for the CNN inclusion (smooth homogeneous σ; per-patch normalization nk(u)) are nonstandard. ReLU is non-smooth; the proposed smooth surrogate changes the model class. The practical relevance of Proposition 7 to widely used architectures (ReLU, BN, residuals) remains limited.
- Discrete theory:
  - Lemma 1/2 rely on deconvolution to achieve “signal preservation.” Deconvolution is notoriously ill-posed/noise-amplifying unless stringent conditions hold. The stability of this recovery in the presence of noise/approximation is not addressed.
  - The links between continuous and discrete parameters are stated but quantitative discretization error bounds are missing.
- Constants and depth dependence:
  - The constants C1, C2 and their dependence on κ and dimension can be large; the bound scales with (1+n)‖∇τ‖∞. No discussion is provided on whether these yield meaningful guarantees for realistic depths.
  - Pooling reduces energy; unlike scattering (norm preserved), the paper proposes adding a Gaussian kernel mapping on top to normalize. This diverges from standard practice and muddies comparability with prior bounds.
- Lack of empirical validation:
  - No experiments demonstrate the claimed stability, invariance, or generalization benefits (e.g., CKNs vs. CNNs under controlled deformations), nor are constants calibrated empirically. Given the practical motivations (regularization, sample complexity), some quantitative support would strengthen the claims.
- Originality/scope:
  - Several components (group equivariance, diffeomorphism stability framing) are adaptations of known ideas. The novelty lies mainly in the kernel-based multilayer construction and the RKHS-based inclusion for a restricted class of CNNs. The incremental nature should be acknowledged.

Questions for the authors
- Do any of the main stability results extend to average pooling or strided convolutions? If so, what changes in the constants?
- How sensitive are Lemma 1/2’s recovery guarantees to noise/model mismatch? Can you provide stability-to-noise bounds?
- Can you quantify C1’s dependence on κ and d, and provide practical ranges of σk, ek, sk where the bounds are non-vacuous?
- How tight is Proposition 7 for networks with smoothed ReLU? Does the induced normalization nk(u) materially change behavior relative to standard CNNs?
- Can you provide empirical demonstrations of the stability bound (e.g., measuring ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖) and a correlation between RKHS norm and generalization on a simple benchmark?

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, but it is notation-heavy. Clarifying the continuous-to-discrete transition with explicit error bounds and discussing the practicality of Gaussian pooling and the proposed activations would improve readability and relevance.

Pros
- Solid theoretical development; extends scattering-style stability to a broader kernelized setting.
- Clear group-equivariant formulation.
- Bridges to CKNs and offers a (limited) path to CNNs via RKHS.

Cons
- Strong assumptions not aligned with common CNN practice.
- Recovery and discretization results rely on deconvolution without robustness analysis.
- No empirical validation; practical impact remains speculative.
- CNN inclusion class excludes standard ReLU/BN/stride choices, limiting significance.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical contributions are careful and potentially valuable for understanding kernelized convolutional architectures, but the mismatch with standard CNN practice and the absence of empirical validation make the practical significance unclear. Strengthening the connection to widely used architectures (or demonstrating empirical relevance for CKNs) and clarifying robustness of discretization/recovery would significantly improve the paper.
