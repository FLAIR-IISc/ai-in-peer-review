This paper explores the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations through the lens of positive definite kernels, aiming to provide a more rigorous understanding of their invariance and stability. The authors generalize convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to a broader class of CNNs and demonstrate stability to diffeomorphisms, mirroring results from the scattering transform. The central idea of separating data representation and predictive models via a kernel framework is promising.

However, despite the potentially significant contribution, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor – yet cumulatively substantial – flaws in presentation, technical detail, and justification. The sheer number of these issues raises concerns about the thoroughness of the work and hinders a clear assessment of its core claims.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Framework:** The use of kernel methods to analyze CNNs offers a potentially powerful approach to understanding their properties.
*   **Stability Results:** Establishing stability guarantees akin to the scattering transform for a wider class of CNNs is a valuable contribution.
*   **Link to CNNs:** The connection drawn between the kernel framework and conventional CNNs is important.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the need for a deeper understanding of CNNs' internal workings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistency:** The notation is inconsistent throughout. For instance, the meaning of “||.||” shifts without clear indication between Hilbert space norms, L2 norms, L2->L2 operator norms, and Euclidean norms. This makes following the derivations extremely difficult.
*   **Lack of Concrete Examples:** While the authors mention various kernels in Appendix B, concrete examples illustrating the application of the theory to specific CNN architectures are lacking. It's unclear how readily the results translate to practical models.
*   **Overly Abstract:** The paper remains highly abstract for too long. The discussion of discretization in Section 2.1 feels somewhat tacked on and doesn't fully bridge the gap between the theory and real-world implementations.
*   **Weak Justification of Assumptions:** Assumptions (A1-A3) are stated without sufficient justification. Why are these assumptions reasonable for typical CNN architectures? A more detailed discussion is needed.
*   **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The paper contains several instances of redundant statements and overly verbose explanations. For example, the discussion on signal preservation feels repetitive.
*   **Minor Technical Errors:** Several minor technical inaccuracies were observed. For example, the statement about recovering xk from a sampling of xk (Lemma 1) requires more precise conditions regarding the sampling density, which are not adequately addressed.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical, lacking any empirical validation to support the claims. While theoretical results are valuable, some experimental evidence would strengthen the argument.
*   **Referencing Issues:** Several references are missing details (e.g., conference name, year) and there appears to be an inconsistency regarding the NIPS year stated on the title page (2017 vs. the actual submission year).
*   **Formatting Problems**: The formatting is generally inconsistent. For example, the definition of the operators Pk, Mk, and Ak does not follow a consistent style.

**Specific Concerns & Detailed Comments:**

*   **Section 2.1:** The connection between the continuous and discrete models lacks a rigorous justification. The assumption that x0 = A0x is not clearly motivated.
*   **Theorem 6:** The constants C1 and C2 are introduced without sufficient explanation of their dependence on various parameters.
*   **Section 4:** The discussion on group invariance feels underdeveloped. The authors state that the framework can be adapted to different groups but provide limited details on how to do so.
*   **Section 5:** The proof sketch in Proposition 7 is too brief and lacks crucial details. The condition Cσ(a) < ∞ needs more justification.
*   **Appendix A:** Schur's test is mentioned but not explicitly used in the derivations, creating confusion.
*   **Appendix B:** The discussion of kernels is too brief and lacks concrete examples.
*   **Overall:** The paper reads as a preliminary draft that needs significant refinement. The authors need to address the numerous minor flaws and provide more concrete examples and empirical validation.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The sheer number of minor flaws suggests a lack of meticulousness and attention to detail.
*   The highly abstract nature of the paper limits its practical impact.
*   The lack of empirical validation weakens the claims.
*   Notational inconsistencies and redundancies make the paper difficult to follow.

**For:**

*   The theoretical framework is novel and potentially valuable.
*   The stability results are significant.
*   The paper addresses an important problem in deep learning theory.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting ideas, the numerous flaws and lack of concrete validation lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a theoretical contribution, it falls short of the rigor and clarity expected of a NIPS publication. The authors need to address the issues raised in this review thoroughly and provide more convincing evidence to support their claims before resubmitting. The paper, in its current state, feels more like a work-in-progress than a finished contribution.
