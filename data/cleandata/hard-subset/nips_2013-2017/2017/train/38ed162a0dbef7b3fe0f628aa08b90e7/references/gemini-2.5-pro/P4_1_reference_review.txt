### Summary

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The core of the work is the construction of a multilayer convolutional kernel for signals defined on a continuous spatial domain. This construction, which generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), involves a sequence of layers, each comprising three operators: patch extraction, a nonlinear kernel mapping into a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), and a linear pooling (convolution with a Gaussian kernel).

Building upon the formalism and proof techniques developed for the scattering transform [17], the authors analyze the stability of this kernel representation to the action of small diffeomorphisms. The main theoretical result is a Lipschitz-style stability bound, showing that the norm of the difference between the representations of a signal and its deformed version is bounded by a linear combination of the sup-norms of the deformation field and its gradient. This result demonstrates that the representation is stable to small deformations. Furthermore, the bound reveals that near-invariance to translations is achieved, with the degree of invariance being controlled by the scale of the final pooling operator. The framework is also extended to describe how to build representations that are equivariant or invariant to more general transformation groups.

A key contribution of the paper is linking this theoretical kernel framework to practical Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). The authors show that the RKHS associated with their constructed kernel contains a large class of CNNs that use smooth, homogeneous activation functions (such as a smoothed version of the ReLU). This connection allows the stability and invariance guarantees derived for the kernel representation to be directly transferred to this class of CNNs, providing a principled explanation for these empirically observed properties. The analysis suggests a link between stability, generalization (controlled by the RKHS norm), and the architectural choices in CNNs.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed mathematical arguments, with proofs provided in the appendix. The analysis rigorously extends concepts from harmonic analysis, previously applied to scattering networks [17], to a more general, learnable kernel-based framework. The work is presented as a complete theoretical piece, carefully laying out assumptions (e.g., properties of the kernels, regularity of the deformation) and deriving the consequences. The authors are honest about the assumptions made, such as the need for an initial pooling/smoothing operator and the specific form of the activation functions required for the CNN connection.

**Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and situating their work within the relevant literature. The construction of the multilayer convolutional kernel is explained logically, starting from the continuous case and then discussing practical considerations like discretization and kernel approximation. The main stability theorem and its components are presented clearly, making the core theoretical result easy to understand. The connection to CNNs in Section 5 is a crucial part of the paper, and it is also explained with sufficient detail. The paper provides enough information for an expert in kernel methods and signal processing to follow the derivations.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it draws heavily from the analysis of scattering transforms [17] and the architecture of CKNs [15, 16], the synthesis of these ideas is novel and significant. The primary contribution lies in extending the stability analysis from a framework with fixed filters (wavelets in scattering) to a learnable kernel framework that is shown to encompass a class of standard CNNs. This provides a new and powerful theoretical lens through which to understand deep convolutional architectures. The connection established in Section 5 between the RKHS of a specifically constructed kernel and the functional space of certain CNNs is a non-trivial and insightful result.

**Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep learning. It addresses the fundamental question of why CNNs are robust to small variations in their input. By formalizing the concepts of stability and invariance within a principled kernel framework, the authors provide concrete theoretical guarantees that can help guide future architectural design and regularization strategies. The link between the RKHS norm, which controls both stability (via the Lipschitz property of functions in the RKHS) and generalization, is particularly insightful. This work is likely to be influential for researchers working on the theory of deep learning, kernel methods, and signal processing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

1.  **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous and novel theoretical analysis of stability and invariance in deep convolutional models, which is a central topic in deep learning theory.
2.  **Bridges Theory and Practice:** It successfully connects an abstract kernel-based analysis to a concrete class of CNNs, making the theoretical results directly relevant to models used in practice.
3.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The work is technically sound, clearly written, and builds upon a solid foundation of previous work in a logical and well-motivated manner.
4.  **New Insights:** It offers valuable insights into the geometry of the function spaces implemented by CNNs and clarifies the role of architectural components like pooling in achieving invariance.

**Arguments against acceptance (or points for improvement):**

1.  **Limited Scope of CNN Connection:** The established connection to CNNs holds for networks with smooth, homogeneous activations and a specific normalization term `nk(u)`. This does not cover the most common CNN architecture using standard, non-smooth ReLUs without this explicit normalization. A discussion on how this gap might be bridged or the potential implications would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Absence of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is not a flaw in itself, the inclusion of even simple experiments to empirically validate the stability bounds (e.g., by measuring the representation's response to controlled deformations) could have provided valuable intuition and further strengthened the paper's claims.
3.  **Dependence of Bound on Depth:** The stability bound grows linearly with the number of layers `n`. While the authors note this is similar to scattering transforms, a more detailed discussion of the implications for very deep networks would be welcome. Does this linear factor suggest a trade-off between depth and stability?
