This paper explores the theoretical properties of deep convolutional networks through the lens of positive definite kernels, aiming to provide a more rigorous understanding of their invariance and stability characteristics. The authors build upon existing work in convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and scattering transforms, generalizing them to more standard CNN architectures. The central idea is to represent CNNs within a kernel-induced functional space, enabling the analysis of their behavior under deformations and establishing links between stability and generalization.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex theoretical framework. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of relevant mathematical tools, including reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and harmonic analysis. The connections drawn between kernel methods, CKNs, and standard CNNs are logically structured. However, despite the technical soundness, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence to support its claims of broader significance. The presented analysis, while mathematically rigorous, feels largely theoretical and detached from practical considerations in modern deep learning. 

A key weakness lies in the limited empirical validation. The paper relies heavily on theoretical results and proof, but offers no concrete experiments demonstrating the benefits of the proposed framework. While acknowledging the need for discretization and approximation in CKNs, it doesn’t demonstrate the practical relevance of these theoretical findings in improved performance or efficiency. The discussion on CNNs contained within the RKHS is interesting, but the conditions required for CNNs to fit within the framework (smooth, homogeneous activations) are restrictive and don't encompass many currently popular architectures employing ReLU or other non-smooth activations. The authors mention smoothed variants, but lack any demonstration of the performance using those.

The paper also suffers from a somewhat conservative approach to originality. While building upon established concepts (kernels, CKNs, scattering transforms), it doesn’t present a truly novel breakthrough. The extension to general CNNs feels incremental, and the benefits of framing CNNs within a kernel framework remain unclear. The connection to generalization via RKHS norms is a standard result, and its application to CNNs, while correct, doesn’t offer new insights without concrete experimental evidence. A significant portion of the related work section is descriptive, rather than critical, failing to highlight how this work meaningfully advances the field. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis is rigorous and well-executed.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized.
*   **Connections to Established Fields:** Effectively links kernel methods, CKNs, and scattering transforms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The absence of experiments significantly weakens the impact of the theoretical results.
*   **Limited Originality:** The contribution feels incremental, lacking a substantial breakthrough.
*   **Restrictive Assumptions:** The requirement for smooth, homogeneous activations limits the applicability to many modern CNN architectures.
*   **Theoretical Detachment:** The analysis remains largely theoretical, with unclear practical implications.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a mathematically rigorous analysis of CNNs.
*   The framework provides a potential avenue for understanding the theoretical underpinnings of deep learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of empirical validation casts doubt on the practical relevance of the work.
*   The limited originality and incremental nature of the contribution may not justify acceptance at a competitive venue like NIPS.
*   The restrictive assumptions on activation functions limit the broader applicability of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the work is technically sound and well-written, the lack of empirical validation and limited originality prevent it from making a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical framework, while interesting, feels detached from practical considerations and does not offer compelling evidence of its usefulness. I believe the paper would benefit from substantial experimental validation and a more critical engagement with the limitations of its assumptions. It currently falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS.
