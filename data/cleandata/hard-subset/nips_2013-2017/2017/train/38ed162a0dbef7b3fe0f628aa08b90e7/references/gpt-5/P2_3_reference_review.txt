Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework for signals on continuous domains that mirrors CNN architectures (patch extraction → pointwise nonlinear kernel map → linear pooling), generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), and yields a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) in which both CKNs and a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations reside. The main theoretical results show:
- Stability of the representation to C1-diffeomorphisms with a Lipschitz-type bound of the Mallat form: the deformation term scales with ‖∇τ‖∞, while translation-invariance improves with the final pooling scale σn (term ∝ ‖τ‖∞/σn).
- The stability bound scales linearly with depth (1 + n). Intermediate layers are equivariant; global invariance is induced mainly by the last pooling.
- Discretization and subsampling can preserve signal information under mild conditions (ek ≥ sk), suggesting practical architectures akin to modern CNNs/CKNs.
- Group-equivariant layers are obtained by designing patch extraction and pooling on locally compact groups; full invariance can be achieved by pooling on the group at the last layer.
- A constructive inclusion: CNN predictors with smooth homogeneous activations and a specific normalization lie in the RKHS of the proposed kernel; their Lipschitz stability is then controlled by the RKHS norm, linking stability and generalization.

This extends Mallat’s scattering analysis (invariance and stability via diffeomorphism bounds) to a broader kernelized, multilayer, convolutional framework and bridges kernel methods, CKNs, and a nontrivial class of CNNs. It relates to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling) and invariant kernels, and to recent theoretical views of deep nets as kernel machines.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The analysis is careful and builds on standard tools (Schur’s test, commutators, harmonic analysis). The bounds are plausible and well motivated. Assumptions are stated (norm-preserving nonexpansive kernel maps, patch size tied to pooling scale, initial anti-aliasing A0).
- Scope and completeness: The core theoretical contributions (Theorem 6 and supporting lemmas) are clearly articulated; proofs are deferred to appendices. The paper also treats discretization and finite-dimensional approximations, arguing stability survives projection steps used in CKNs.
- Limitations/assumptions:
  - The kernel maps must be homogeneous dot-product kernels satisfying (A1–A2); the CNN class included requires smooth homogeneous activations and a normalization factor nk(u), which differs from standard practice. ReLU is handled only via a smoothed variant.
  - Linear growth with depth may be conservative; constants (e.g., C1’s dependence on κ^(d+1)) could be large in high dimensions.
  - Norm is not preserved (unlike scattering); authors mitigate via energy arguments and an optional final Gaussian map.
  - The signal recovery under subsampling relies on stable deconvolution and ek ≥ sk; the practical robustness of this step is only qualitatively discussed.
- Empirical verification: The submission as presented is purely theoretical; even small-scale experiments illustrating stability versus σn, the role of depth, or the impact of kernel projection quality would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized: continuous construction → discretization → stability → groups → CNN link. Notation is heavy but standard for the area; assumptions are explicit and motivated.
- Discussion of how constants depend on kernel and architectural parameters is helpful; a short table/summary (even in the appendix) of kernels satisfying (A1–A2) and the corresponding Cσ would aid readability.
- Minor suggestions: clarify the practical gap between the normalized activation form (eq. 8) and conventional CNN layers; expand on when the A0 assumption is essential versus benign; state explicitly how the discrete parameters map to continuous ones for d > 1.

Originality
- Extends scattering stability results to a broader, learnable RKHS-based multilayer convolutional representation; unifies CKNs and a class of CNNs under one functional framework with explicit stability guarantees.
- The commutator bound for shifted integral operators [LcAσ, Lτ] and the treatment of discretization/subsampling within this framework are technically nontrivial.
- The RKHS inclusion result for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and the norm-based stability/generalization link are valuable conceptual bridges. Prior related analyses (e.g., invariant kernels, G-CNNs, “deep nets as kernels”) don’t provide this specific combination.

Significance
- The work provides principled guidance for designing invariant/stable convolutional representations (e.g., how σn governs invariance; how intermediate pooling stabilizes deformations) beyond scattering networks.
- The RKHS view can inform regularization: controlling final-layer norms translates to robustness. This is timely given interest in Lipschitz/contraction properties and adversarial robustness.
- Practical impact is promising but indirect: CKNs are implementable, yet the exact CNN class in the RKHS differs from standard nets. Empirical validation would increase adoption and influence.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, rigorous theoretical contribution that generalizes influential scattering results.
- Unifying framework connecting kernels, CKNs, and (a class of) CNNs with interpretable stability controls.
- Group-equivariant layer construction and invariance–stability trade-offs are cleanly articulated.
- Addresses both continuous and discrete settings; discusses approximation effects.

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of empirical validation in the submission; no quantitative illustration of bounds or practical benefits.
- Assumptions restrict the class of activations and require normalization not standard in CNNs.
- Stability constants may be loose; depth dependence and constants’ growth could limit practical tightness.
- Recovery and discretization arguments rely on deconvolution stability not quantified in practice.

Questions/suggestions
- Can the (1 + n) factor be improved under architectural constraints (e.g., increasing σk faster, larger patches)? Any lower bounds?
- Which specific kernels in Appendix B best balance (A1–A2) and representational capacity in practice?
- How sensitive are the RKHS norms (and stability) to kernel projection quality in CKNs?
- Can you provide simple experiments comparing stability of CKN/CNN features under controlled diffeomorphisms as σn varies?

Recommendation
- Leaning accept (theory track). Strong, well-grounded theoretical contribution that extends a central line of work and clarifies invariance/stability in convolutional representations. The lack of experiments is the main weakness; adding even small-scale validations would solidify the impact.

Reviewer confidence: High on theory and prior work; moderate on practical implications.
