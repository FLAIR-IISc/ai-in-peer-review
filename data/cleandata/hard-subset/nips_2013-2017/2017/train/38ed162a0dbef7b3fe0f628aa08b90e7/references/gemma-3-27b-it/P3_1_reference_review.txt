## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel (MCK) and analyze its translation invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms, drawing parallels to the scattering transform but for a broader class of CNN architectures. They demonstrate that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization, suggesting a link between these properties and improved sample complexity. The work extends existing convolutional kernel network (CKN) formulations and provides theoretical justification for the observed robustness of CNNs.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon several lines of research: convolutional kernel networks [15, 16], the scattering transform [5, 17], and kernel methods for deep learning [2, 7, 8]. It effectively bridges the gap between these areas, leveraging the mathematical rigor of kernel theory to analyze the behavior of more standard CNN architectures. The authors correctly position their work as an extension of the scattering transform’s theoretical framework to models with learned parameters and a wider range of activation functions, and acknowledge the existing body of work connecting CNNs and kernel methods. The references provided are comprehensive and relevant.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of CNNs, utilizing powerful tools from kernel theory and harmonic analysis. The stability results, inspired by the scattering transform, are a significant contribution.
* **Generality:** The MCK framework encompasses a wide range of CNN architectures, including CKNs, and the theoretical results apply to these models. This generality enhances the impact of the work.
* **Connection to Generalization:** The demonstrated link between stability (controlled by the RKHS norm) and generalization is insightful and potentially valuable for regularization strategies. This highlights the importance of controlling the "smoothness" of learned functions.
* **Clear Exposition:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the research question, and the mathematical derivations are presented with reasonable clarity.
* **Discretization Discussion:** The careful treatment of discretization and its impact on the theoretical results is a crucial practical consideration that is often overlooked.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the paper focuses on theoretical analysis, a lack of empirical validation limits the impact. Demonstrating the theoretical results on real-world datasets or through targeted experiments would strengthen the claims. For instance, showing that networks with lower RKHS norms achieve better generalization performance would be compelling.
* **Computational Challenges:** The paper acknowledges the computational intractability of directly working with the MCK. While the CKN approximation is discussed, a more detailed analysis of the practical implications of this approximation – e.g., the impact on stability guarantees – would be beneficial.
* **Activation Function Restrictions:** The requirement for "smooth homogeneous" activation functions limits the applicability of the theoretical results to a specific class of CNNs. While the authors discuss ReLU and its smoothed variants, it’s unclear how well the theory extends to more complex or commonly used activations.
* **Norm Preservation Caveat:** The authors acknowledge that the representation doesn't preserve the norm and discuss potential remedies. However, the implications of this norm loss on the overall analysis could be further explored.
* **Complexity:** The theoretical framework is quite involved, potentially making it inaccessible to a wider audience. Simplifying some of the derivations or providing more intuitive explanations could improve clarity.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of CNNs.
* It provides a rigorous justification for the observed robustness of these models.
* The framework is general and encompasses a broad class of CNN architectures.
* The connection to generalization is a valuable insight.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation weakens the claims.
* The computational challenges of working with the MCK are not fully addressed.
* The restriction on activation functions may limit the applicability of the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that offers a valuable contribution to the field. The authors have successfully leveraged the tools of kernel theory to analyze the stability and invariance properties of CNNs. While the lack of empirical validation is a weakness, the theoretical insights and generality of the framework are significant. I believe this paper is a worthy addition to NIPS, and recommend **acceptance**.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Include some limited empirical validation to demonstrate the theoretical results.
* Elaborate on the practical implications of the CKN approximation.
* Discuss the limitations of the smoothness requirement on activation functions and potential avenues for extending the theory.
* Provide more intuitive explanations of the mathematical framework.
