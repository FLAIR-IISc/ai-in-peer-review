This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks, specifically focusing on invariance and stability, through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors build a functional space derived from a kernel that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and demonstrate that it contains a broad class of CNNs. They aim to provide theoretical guarantees for invariance to diffeomorphisms and relate stability to generalization performance, drawing connections to the scattering transform.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The work tackles a fundamental and important problem – understanding the theoretical underpinnings of CNNs’ success.  The kernel framework is a compelling approach, allowing for a more rigorous analysis of stability and invariance than is typically possible with direct analysis of CNNs. The connections drawn to the scattering transform are valuable, as it builds upon a well-established theoretical framework. The extension of previous results to a wider class of CNNs, including those with smooth homogeneous activations, is a significant contribution. The authors are careful to address the practical challenges of discretization and kernel approximation, offering concrete steps toward implementing these theoretical results. The paper is mathematically rigorous, and the inclusion of proofs in the appendix (although lengthy) demonstrates thoroughness. The exploration of group invariance beyond simple translations is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The mathematical complexity is high, making the paper difficult to follow for readers not already deeply familiar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis. While the authors attempt to bridge the gap with definitions and explanations, the density of notation and the abstract nature of some arguments may hinder accessibility. The practical implications of the theoretical results are not fully explored. Although the authors mention connections to CKNs and discuss discretization, a more concrete demonstration of how these results translate to improved performance on standard CNN architectures would strengthen the impact of the work. The link between stability and generalization, while theoretically appealing, is stated somewhat vaguely. A more detailed discussion of how the RKHS norm can be used as a regularizer and how it affects generalization bounds would be beneficial.  The assumption of smooth homogeneous activations is restrictive. While the authors acknowledge this and point to smoothed ReLU variants, it still limits the applicability of the results to a specific class of CNNs. The discussion regarding the differences between their results and the scattering transform could be more nuanced.  While they highlight the broader applicability to CNNs, they don’t fully address the norm-preservation property of scattering transforms, which is a notable difference. The paper's length and detailed mathematical derivations might make it challenging for a broad NIPS audience.  Some notation (e.g. using the same notation for different norms) can be confusing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of CNNs using a novel kernel framework.
*   Extends existing theoretical results (scattering transforms, CKNs) to a broader class of models.
*   Establishes connections between stability, invariance, and generalization.
*   Addresses practical considerations of discretization and kernel approximation.
*   Mathematically sound and thorough.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   High mathematical complexity and potentially limited accessibility.
*   Limited discussion of practical implications and empirical validation.
*   Restrictive assumption of smooth homogeneous activations.
*   The connection between stability and generalization could be elaborated.
*   The lengthy paper and intricate mathematical details may deter a broader NIPS audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound and theoretically significant paper. The authors make a compelling case for using a kernel framework to analyze the properties of CNNs, and their results offer valuable insights into the relationship between stability, invariance, and generalization. However, the high mathematical complexity and limited discussion of practical implications are significant drawbacks.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the field, but it needs to be made more accessible and its practical implications need to be better articulated. Specifically, the authors should consider:

1.  Providing a more intuitive explanation of the key concepts and mathematical derivations.
2.  Including preliminary experiments demonstrating the applicability of the results to standard CNN architectures.
3.  Elaborating on the connection between the RKHS norm and generalization performance, potentially by deriving or discussing relevant generalization bounds.
4.  Addressing the limitations of the smooth homogeneous activation assumption.
5.  Streamlining the presentation and focusing on the most impactful results.
