This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The core of the work is the construction of a multilayer convolutional kernel for signals on continuous domains, which generalizes the framework of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs). Using this kernel construction, the authors derive a functional space (an RKHS) and analyze the properties of the feature map Φ(x). The main theoretical results are Lipschitz stability bounds for the representation with respect to the action of small diffeomorphisms. These bounds are analogous to those derived for the scattering transform (Mallat, 2012), demonstrating that the representation is stable to small deformations and becomes progressively more invariant to translations with increasing depth. The framework is also extended to achieve global invariance to arbitrary transformation groups by defining group-equivariant operators. A key contribution is linking this kernel framework to standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), showing that CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions are contained within the derived RKHS. This allows the stability results to be applied to a class of CNNs, providing a connection between the function's RKHS norm, its stability, and its generalization properties.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution (Quality, Significance):** The paper provides a rigorous and elegant theoretical framework for understanding stability and invariance in deep convolutional models. It successfully extends the powerful analytical tools from the scattering transform literature to a more general setting that is not restricted to predefined wavelet filters. This is a valuable step towards building a deeper theoretical foundation for deep learning.

2.  **Generality and Connection to CNNs (Originality, Significance):** A major strength is the connection established between the proposed kernel representation and a class of CNNs (Section 5). By showing that certain CNN functions lie within the constructed RKHS, the paper provides a powerful tool for analyzing their geometric properties. This bridges the gap between the purely constructive approach of scattering networks and the learned representations of CNNs, offering a novel perspective that is highly significant for the field.

3.  **Principled Framework for Invariance (Quality, Clarity):** The paper provides a clear, constructive methodology for building representations with desired stability and invariance properties. The analysis clearly separates the effects of local deformations (controlled by the commutator bounds) and global translations (controlled by the final pooling layer). The extension to general group invariances in Section 4 is a compelling generalization that demonstrates the flexibility of the framework.

4.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. It begins with the construction of the kernel, analyzes its properties, discusses practical considerations like discretization, and finally establishes the link to CNNs. The notation is consistent, and the theoretical arguments are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Gap between Theory and Practice for CNNs (Quality, Significance):** The connection to CNNs in Section 5, while being a key contribution, rests on assumptions that deviate significantly from common practice.
    *   **Activation Function:** The analysis requires smooth activation functions to ensure the resulting CNN function lies in the RKHS. However, the most widely used activation, ReLU, is non-smooth. While the authors mention smooth approximations, the practical implications of this discrepancy are not discussed. How does the stability of a standard ReLU network relate to this theoretical model?
    *   **Normalization Term:** The CNN construction in Eq. (8) includes a normalization term `nk(u) = ||Pkxk−1(u)||`, which is necessary to fit the homogeneous kernel framework. This form of explicit normalization within the activation is not standard in CNNs. A discussion on the role of this term and the consequences of its absence in practice (as is typical) would be crucial for assessing the relevance of the results to real-world models. For instance, does batch normalization play a similar role implicitly?

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation (Quality):** The paper is entirely theoretical. While the theory is strong, the claims would be substantially more convincing with even simple empirical validation. This would help bridge the gap highlighted in the previous point. I would suggest the following experiments for a future version:
    *   Empirically measure the stability of a CKN or a CNN with a smooth activation to small, controlled diffeomorphisms and translations. Does the empirical error `||Φ(Lτx) - Φ(x)||` scale with `||∇τ||∞` and `||τ||∞/σn` as predicted by Theorem 6?
    *   Compare the stability of a network trained with the theoretically-required smooth, normalized activation versus a standard ReLU network. This would help understand if the theoretical properties are specific to the assumed model or if they are more general characteristics of deep convolutional architectures.

3.  **Norm Preservation and Signal Decay (Quality):** The authors correctly note that, unlike the scattering transform, their representation does not preserve the L2 norm due to the pooling operators `Ak`. They argue this is not a major issue for low-pass signals and can be mitigated. However, the potential for signal energy to decay exponentially with depth is a concern. A more detailed analysis of the conditions under which signal information is preserved would strengthen the work. For example, can one bound the energy loss in terms of the signal's frequency content and the pooling scales?

**Summary and Recommendation**

This paper presents a high-quality, theoretically sound analysis of invariance and stability in deep convolutional architectures. Its main strengths lie in the generalization of the stability analysis from scattering networks to a broader class of models via a kernel framework, and the novel connection it establishes to standard CNNs. This provides important insights into the geometry of the function classes learned by these models.

However, the work's impact is currently limited by the significant gap between the theoretical model of a CNN and the architectures used in practice (particularly regarding the activation function). The complete absence of experiments makes it difficult to gauge the practical relevance of the derived stability bounds.

Despite these limitations, the theoretical contribution is strong, original, and significant. It provides a new and valuable lens through which to study deep networks. The paper is well-written and would be of great interest to the theoretical machine learning community at NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly encourage the authors to address the gap between their theory and current practice in future work, ideally by incorporating empirical studies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and rigorous theoretical framework for analyzing the stability of deep convolutional representations.
*   Successfully extends the powerful analysis of scattering transforms to a more general, learnable setting.
*   Establishes an important conceptual link between a class of CNNs and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces, offering new geometric insights.
*   The work is clear, technically solid, and advances our fundamental understanding of deep learning models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The connection to practical CNNs is weakened by strong assumptions on activation functions that are not standard.
*   The complete lack of empirical results leaves the practical relevance of the theoretical findings in question.
