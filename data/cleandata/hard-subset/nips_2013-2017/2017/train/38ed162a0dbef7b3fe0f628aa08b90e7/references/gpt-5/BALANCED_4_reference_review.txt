Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that yields feature maps on continuous domains with a CNN-like structure: patch extraction, a pointwise RKHS map induced by homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. The authors analyze the resulting representation’s stability to diffeomorphisms and translation invariance, in the spirit of Mallat’s scattering transform, but for a broader family than fixed wavelet cascades. They prove Lipschitz stability of the representation to small deformations with constants that (i) depend linearly on the number of layers and (ii) achieve approximate translation invariance controlled by the final pooling scale. They discuss discretization (with subsampling and deconvolution-based recovery), kernel approximation (finite-dimensional projections as in CKNs), and generalize inner-layer and last-layer pooling to general groups G for equivariance and global invariance. Finally, they show that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (smoothed ReLU-like) lies in the RKHS associated with the multilayer kernel, and they bound the RKHS norm of such CNNs across layers, linking model norm to stability and potential generalization.

This work sits at the intersection of scattering networks (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat), convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.), and kernel perspectives on deep networks (Daniely et al.; Montavon et al.). Compared to scattering, it (a) replaces fixed wavelets with learned RKHS embeddings from homogeneous kernels, (b) relaxes exact energy preservation, and (c) covers a class of CNNs through RKHS inclusion. Compared to prior CKNs, it provides deformation stability guarantees and a principled route to group-equivariant/invariant constructions in the kernel setting.

Strengths
- Clear, unified operator-theoretic view of multilayer convolutional representations on continuous domains, directly comparable to scattering and compatible with CKN practice.
- The deformation stability theorem (Theorem 6) gives explicit control via commutators, with intuitive dependence on depth and pooling scales; translation invariance improves with final pooling scale.
- Thoughtful treatment of discretization and kernel approximation; non-expansiveness and norm control ensure stability is preserved under projections used in practical CKNs.
- Group-theoretic extension for equivariance/invariance on general locally compact groups is clean and broadly useful.
- Inclusion result for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations in the RKHS, with recursive norm bounds, provides a conceptual link between CNN stability and RKHS norms (hence margin/generalization).

Weaknesses
- Assumptions for the RKHS map (norm-preserving, non-expansive homogeneous kernels) and for CNN inclusion (smooth homogeneous activations, patch-norm normalization) are somewhat restrictive and diverge from standard CNN practice (non-smooth ReLU, no explicit patch renormalization).
- The main deformation stability bound grows linearly with depth and the constants can be loose (e.g., dependence on κ^(d+1)); the practical tightness of these constants is not evaluated.
- Translation invariance is traded off against loss of high-frequency content via Gaussian pooling; norm non-preservation vs. scattering is acknowledged but leaves open how to best select scales to balance invariance and information retention.
- Discretization analysis relies on stable deconvolution and ek ≥ sk; stability of deconvolution and boundary effects are not quantified, and the connection to common architectural choices (e.g., strides larger than kernel, padding) is only qualitatively discussed.
- The link to standard CNNs hinges on smoothed ReLU variants; quantitative approximation error between true ReLU networks and the admissible class is not provided.
- Limited empirical validation. The paper is theory-focused; still, even small-scale experiments demonstrating the predicted stability trends for CKNs or CNN surrogates would strengthen significance.

Quality
Technically careful, building on harmonic analysis and kernel operator tools. The commutator-based proof strategy is sound and extends Mallat’s approach to shifted pooling and general layers. Discretization and projection steps preserve non-expansiveness. However, several results depend on assumptions that may be hard to guarantee in standard CNNs, and quantitative constants are not explored empirically.

Clarity
Well written and organized. The operator notation is consistent, and the connection to prior work is clear. Some proofs are deferred to the appendix, which is acceptable, but the main text could better highlight the implications of constants and the effect of parameter choices (e.g., σk schedules). The CNN-to-RKHS inclusion is clearly stated but the practical meaning of the normalization term nk(u) could be further explained.

Originality
Solid and timely. The extension of scattering-style stability to a broader RKHS-based multilayer setting that subsumes CKNs and relates to a class of CNNs is novel. The group invariance construction is standard in equivariant literature, but embedding it into the kernel/CKN framework and tying it to stability is useful. The recursive RKHS norm bound for CNNs complements existing dual/kernel views.

Significance
Conceptually significant for understanding invariance and stability in learned convolutional representations beyond scattering. It clarifies how pooling scales and norms govern stability and suggests regularization via RKHS norms. Practical impact will depend on bridging gaps with standard CNNs (ReLU, batch norm, striding) and on demonstrating empirical relevance.

Arguments for acceptance
- General, rigorous stability analysis for a broad class of convolutional kernel representations.
- Bridges theory of scattering and practice of CKNs/CNNs, offering new insights into invariance, stability, and norms.
- Clean group-equivariant/invariant construction within the kernel framework.
- Potential to inform regularization and architecture design.

Arguments against acceptance
- Assumptions diverge from standard CNNs; inclusion requires smoothed activations and patch renormalization.
- Bounds may be loose; constants and depth dependence not empirically validated.
- Discretization and deconvolution stability lack quantitative guarantees.
- Limited experimental evidence of practical benefits.

Suggestions for improvement
- Quantify constants (C1, C2) for typical parameter choices and report empirical stability vs. σn, depth, and κ on synthetic deformations.
- Provide approximation-error bounds for replacing ReLU by the admissible smooth activation and for omitting patch renormalization; discuss the impact of batch normalization and strided convolutions.
- Extend stability results to a fully discrete setting, including boundary effects and common padding schemes; clarify when deconvolution is well-conditioned.
- Analyze projection error in kernel approximation quantitatively and its effect on stability; provide guidance for choosing subspace dimensions.
- Validate group-invariance construction on simple roto-translation tasks, comparing to group-equivariant CNNs.
- Discuss how to estimate or control RKHS norms in practice for CNNs, and provide at least a sketch of margin/sample-complexity implications.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a rigorous and insightful theoretical contribution that broadens deformation stability analysis from scattering to kernelized convolutional representations and a class of CNNs. The assumptions and lack of empirical validation limit immediate practical impact, but the conceptual advance and careful analysis justify acceptance.
