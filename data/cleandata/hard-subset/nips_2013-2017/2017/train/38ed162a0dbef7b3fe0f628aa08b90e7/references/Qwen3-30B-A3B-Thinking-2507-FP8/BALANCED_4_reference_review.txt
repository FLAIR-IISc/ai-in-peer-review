

This paper studies the theoretical properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through a kernel framework, with a focus on invariance and stability properties of representations. The authors generalize convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to a continuous domain and establish theoretical guarantees for stability to diffeomorphisms (small deformations) and translation invariance. They demonstrate that their functional space contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, and show that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a technically sound theoretical framework that extends previous work on the scattering transform [17] to a more general class of CNNs. The stability results (Theorem 6) are rigorous and provide explicit bounds on stability to diffeomorphisms, which is a significant contribution to understanding CNN representations.

2. The connection between the kernel framework and standard CNNs (Proposition 7) is novel and insightful, showing how stability properties apply to a broader class of networks. This bridges the gap between theoretical understanding and practical CNN architectures.

3. The paper carefully addresses limitations, including the restriction to smooth homogeneous activations (which excludes ReLU) and the need for additional non-linear prediction layers for norm preservation. The related work section is thorough and places the paper in context with relevant NIPS and other literature.

4. The mathematical exposition is clear and well-organized, with consistent notation throughout. The lemmas and propositions are well-structured, and the appendix provides necessary technical details.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks empirical validation of the theoretical claims. While theoretical guarantees are important, some experiments demonstrating how these stability properties manifest in actual CNN training would significantly strengthen the paper. For example, comparing the stability of different architectures to deformations would provide concrete evidence.

2. The connection to standard CNNs is limited to smooth homogeneous activations, which excludes ReLU, the most commonly used activation function. While the paper acknowledges this and suggests smoothed variants, it would be stronger to provide a more concrete connection to ReLU-based networks or discuss the practical implications of this limitation.

3. The discretization discussion (Section 2.1) is somewhat brief and doesn't fully address how the theoretical results translate to practical implementations. A more detailed treatment would help bridge the gap between theory and practice.

4. The paper doesn't sufficiently compare its approach with other recent theoretical work on CNNs, such as convexified CNNs [27] or the kernel methods of Zhang et al. [28]. This would better position the work within the current literature.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs (in the appendix). The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis, though the lack of empirical validation is a notable limitation.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation. The main ideas are accessible to readers familiar with the background, though some technical details might be challenging for non-specialists.

Originality: The paper extends the scattering transform framework to a more general kernel-based approach, providing theoretical guarantees for a broader class of CNNs. This represents a meaningful advancement over previous work, particularly in how it generalizes the scattering transform framework.

Significance: The results provide important theoretical insights into why CNNs work well, which could inform better network design and regularization techniques. The connection between stability and generalization via RKHS norm is particularly significant for understanding sample complexity in deep learning.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of CNN representations. While the lack of empirical validation and the limited connection to standard CNNs with ReLU are notable weaknesses, the theoretical framework and results are valuable and well-executed. 

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the empirical validation gap and strengthen the connection to practical CNN implementations. Specifically, the authors should:

1. Add some empirical experiments demonstrating stability properties in practice (e.g., comparing stability of different architectures to image deformations)
2. Provide a more detailed discussion of how the smooth homogeneous activation assumption affects practical CNN design
3. Expand the discretization discussion to address practical implementation details
4. Add a comparison with other recent theoretical approaches to CNNs

With these revisions, the paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS, providing both theoretical depth and practical relevance to the understanding of CNN representations.
