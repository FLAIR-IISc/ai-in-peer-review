This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors aim to provide a more principled understanding of invariance and stability in these networks, bridging the gap between their empirical success and theoretical foundations. They construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate that it encompasses a broad class of CNNs, enabling them to derive stability guarantees similar to those established for scattering transforms, but for more general architectures.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question in deep learning – understanding the geometry of the functional spaces induced by CNNs. The connection to kernel methods is a natural and potentially fruitful approach. The extension of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains and the subsequent analysis are well-executed.  The authors correctly identify and build upon relevant prior work, including scattering transforms, CKNs, and kernel methods for deep learning. The mathematical treatment is rigorous, with a clear notation and careful derivations (although relegated to the appendix). The results, specifically the stability guarantees, are significant as they offer a theoretical basis for the observed robustness of CNNs and provide a link between stability and generalization. The discussion of group invariance is also a valuable contribution.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses.  The primary concern is the practicality and broad applicability of the derived results.  While the theory is solid, the assumptions required to apply it (e.g., smooth homogeneous activations, specific kernel properties) may be restrictive in practice.  The paper acknowledges these limitations but doesn't fully address how prevalent they are in commonly used CNN architectures.  Furthermore, the connection to *actual* CNNs, beyond demonstrating that they fall within the defined functional space, feels somewhat tenuous.  The authors show that CNNs *can* be cast in this framework, but don't deeply analyze how well the framework captures the behavior of typical CNNs trained on large datasets. The discussion of "homogeneous activations" while theoretically important, feels somewhat disconnected from the predominantly non-homogeneous activations used in practice (ReLU being a prime example, although the paper briefly addresses smoothed variants). The extensive use of appendices, while understandable given the mathematical complexity, can make it difficult for the reader to follow the main line of reasoning.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a mathematically sound analysis of CNNs using kernel methods.
* **Novelty:** The construction of the multilayer convolutional kernel and the extension of stability results to a wider class of CNNs are novel contributions.
* **Connection to Prior Work:**  The paper effectively integrates and extends existing research on scattering transforms, CKNs, and kernel methods.
* **Significance:** The stability guarantees and their link to generalization are potentially significant for understanding and improving CNNs.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear notation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality:** The assumptions required for the theory to hold may limit its applicability to real-world CNNs.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:**  There’s a lack of empirical evidence demonstrating the relevance of the theory to standard CNN architectures and datasets.
* **Tenous Connection to Common CNNs:** Showing CNNs are *within* the framework isn’t the same as showing the framework fully *explains* their behavior.
* **Appendix-Heavy:**  The extensive reliance on appendices can hinder readability and understanding.
* **Homogeneous Activations:** The focus on homogeneous activations feels somewhat divorced from prevalent practices.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of CNNs, filling a gap in our understanding of their underlying principles.
* The extension of stability results beyond scattering transforms is a valuable contribution.
* The connection to kernel methods offers a new perspective on CNNs and opens up avenues for further research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The restrictive assumptions and lack of empirical validation raise concerns about the practical relevance of the results.
* The paper primarily focuses on theoretical contributions and lacks a strong connection to real-world applications.
* The connection between the theoretical framework and commonly used CNNs feels weak, leading to questions about the framework’s descriptive power.




**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the paper presents a technically sound and theoretically interesting analysis, its practical relevance and applicability to real-world CNNs are questionable. The assumptions are restrictive, and the lack of empirical validation limits its impact.  

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* **Address the practicality concerns:**  Discuss the prevalence of the assumptions in commonly used CNN architectures.
* **Include empirical validation:** Conduct experiments to demonstrate the relevance of the theory to standard CNNs and datasets.  Even a small-scale empirical study would strengthen the paper significantly.
* **Strengthen the connection to real-world CNNs:**  Provide a more in-depth analysis of how the framework captures the behavior of typical CNNs.
* **Reduce reliance on appendices:**  Consider incorporating more key results and explanations into the main body of the paper.
* **Explore Relaxations:** Investigate possibilities of relaxing the assumptions on activation functions or kernel properties.
