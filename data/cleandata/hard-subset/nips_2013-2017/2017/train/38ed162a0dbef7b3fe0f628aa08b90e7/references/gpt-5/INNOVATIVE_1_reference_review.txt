Summary
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that mirrors CNN architectures (patch extraction, pointwise nonlinear mapping, and pooling) and studies its invariance and stability properties via RKHS geometry. The authors prove Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms with a bound depending linearly on depth and inversely on the final pooling scale; they also show how to achieve global invariance to general transformation groups via group-equivariant patching and pooling. The kernel construction subsumes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) and, through a specific class of homogeneous dot-product kernels and smooth homogeneous activations, contains a broad class of CNNs; the RKHS norm then simultaneously controls prediction stability and generalization. The paper also addresses discretization, kernel approximation (finite-dimensional projections), and signal preservation under subsampling.

Relation to prior work
The work is clearly inspired by Mallat’s scattering transform (invariance and Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms) and by CKNs, extending both: scattering provides stability but uses fixed wavelets; CKNs provide a learnable multilayer kernel approximation without a full stability theory. This paper gives stability results for a broader kernelized CNN class and shows how many CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations can be embedded in the associated RKHS with explicit norm bounds. The group invariance construction relates to group-equivariant CNNs (e.g., Cohen & Welling) and invariant kernels, but here it is integrated into a kernel-RKHS framework tied to multilayer convolutional structure. The commutator analysis extends some scattering lemmas to shifted integral operators.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: a single RKHS framework that simultaneously captures CKNs and a nontrivial class of CNNs, tying invariance/stability to norm control and generalization.
- Theoretical advances: a clear diffeomorphism stability bound (Theorem 6) for multilayer convolutional kernels with depth and pooling dependence; compatibility with finite-dimensional kernel approximations; extension to group actions with equivariant patching/pooling.
- Technical novelty: commutator bound for [LcAσ, Lτ]; careful handling of discretization and signal preservation under subsampling; explicit conditions (A1–A3) that are interpretable from an architectural perspective.
- Potential impact: provides a principled lens on regularization (via RKHS norm), robustness, and the design of invariant architectures; bridges kernel methods and deep convolutional models.

Weaknesses
- Practical gap to standard CNNs: the required homogeneous, smooth activations and per-patch normalization nk(u) diverge from typical ReLUs/BatchNorm; ReLU is not covered (only smoothed variants), which may limit direct applicability.
- Assumption sensitivity: signal recovery via deconvolution (Lemma 2) may be ill-conditioned; the norm-preserving kernel mappings and A3 (patch size tied to pooling scale) constrain architecture choices; constants (C1, C2) may be large (dependence on κ, d), and the stability bound grows with depth.
- Limited empirical validation: no experiments illustrating stability/invariance numerically or demonstrating improved sample complexity/robustness for CKNs or CNNs within the proposed class.
- Group section is largely an elegant rephrasing of known equivariance/invariance constructions, albeit in the RKHS setting, with limited new theory beyond the integration into the framework.
- Clarity: the conditions for Cσ finiteness (Appendix B) and concrete kernel/activation pairs that satisfy (A1–A2) could be made more explicit in the main text; guidance on σk schedules and patch sizes in realistic grids would help.

Quality
The theoretical development appears sound and carefully argued; proofs are deferred but the main steps and assumptions are well stated. The link to CKN approximations and the preservation of non-expansiveness are convincing. Lack of empirical evidence is a weakness but acceptable for a primarily theoretical contribution.

Clarity
Generally well organized and readable for an expert audience. Some parts could be clearer: present a concrete instantiation (e.g., inverse polynomial kernel + smoothed ReLU) early; summarize the dependence of constants on problem parameters; discuss discretization stability quantitatively.

Originality
Moderately strong. The stability of scattering is known; group equivariance is known. The novelty lies in (i) extending stability analysis to a general multilayer convolutional kernel class close to learned CNNs, (ii) embedding a class of CNNs into the induced RKHS with explicit norm bounds, and (iii) connecting norm control to stability and generalization in this unified setting. This is a meaningful conceptual advance.

Significance
High for theory: provides tools and perspective likely to influence analysis of learned convolutional representations, and to motivate regularization/design choices. Practical immediate impact is less certain due to activation/normalization constraints and absence of experiments, but the direction is important.

Questions and suggestions
- Please provide a concrete kernel/activation choice (formula and plot) that satisfies (A1–A2) and Cσ < ∞, and show a toy experiment measuring ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ versus ‖τ‖∞, σn, and depth.
- Can the framework be extended to subhomogeneous activations or to ReLU via limiting arguments? Any bounds for piecewise-linear activations?
- How sensitive is Lemma 2’s recovery to noise and filter bandwidth? A condition number estimate would be useful.
- Can you instantiate the norm bound in Proposition 7 for a simple 2-layer network to provide intuition on how filter norms and channel counts affect Bk,i?
- Discuss boundary conditions and stride/padding in the discretized setting and how they affect stability.

Pros
- Unified RKHS view of convolutional representations with stability guarantees.
- Novel link embedding a class of CNNs in the induced RKHS with norm control.
- Clean, general diffeomorphism stability bound; principled group invariance construction.
- Compatibility with practical CKN approximations.

Cons
- Departures from standard CNN practice (activations/normalization).
- No empirical validation of the theoretical predictions.
- Constants and assumptions may limit sharpness/practicality.
- Group section incremental relative to known equivariance literature.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper advances a significant conceptual direction by unifying scattering-style stability with learnable convolutional architectures within a kernel/RKHS framework and by tying stability to norm control. Despite limited experiments and some practical gaps, the novelty and potential impact merit acceptance.

Confidence
High.
