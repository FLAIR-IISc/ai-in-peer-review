**Summary**

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel framework, generalizing Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), to operate on continuous-domain signals. Within this framework, they derive theoretical guarantees on the stability of the representation to small C1-diffeomorphisms and its invariance to translations. The stability bounds are shown to be analogous to those of scattering networks, with invariance increasing with network depth (specifically, the scale of the final pooling layer). The framework is also extended to handle global invariance to group actions. A key contribution is linking this kernel construction to a class of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with smooth, homogeneous activation functions, thereby extending the stability analysis to these models. The authors argue that the RKHS norm of a CNN predictor in this space controls both its generalization and its stability to deformations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a principled and technically sound theoretical investigation into the geometric properties of deep convolutional architectures. It builds upon established work in harmonic analysis and kernel methods to formalize intuitions about invariance and stability.
2.  **Generalization of Prior Work:** The work successfully extends the stability analysis of Mallat [17] from scattering networks (with fixed filters) to a more general class of architectures that includes learned representations, such as CKNs and a specific class of CNNs. This is a non-trivial and valuable contribution.
3.  **Novel Connection between Kernels and CNNs:** Section 5 establishes an explicit connection showing that certain CNNs are contained within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) of the proposed convolutional kernel. This provides a powerful theoretical tool for analyzing CNNs and links the network's stability directly to its RKHS norm, which has known connections to generalization.

**Weaknesses**

While the theoretical direction is commendable, the paper suffers from a significant number of minor flaws, unaddressed technical details, and a lack of polish that collectively undermine its contribution.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The paper's readability is hampered by the decision to relegate all proofs to the appendix. While common, key proof sketches or intuitions for central results like Proposition 3 and Theorem 6 should be included in the main text to make the paper more self-contained.
    *   The transition from the continuous model (Section 2) to the discrete model (Section 2.1) is abrupt. The subsection "Links between the parameters of the discrete and continuous models" is a single sentence and does not adequately explain the crucial relationship between parameters like patch size (`ek`) and pooling scale in the two domains.
    *   There is a conspicuous formatting error on the first page, where "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017), Long Beach, CA, USA." appears as a footnote to the title, suggesting a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   The notation `‖ · ‖` is overloaded to represent Hilbert space norms, L2 norms, and L2→L2 operator norms. While stated, this simplification can lead to ambiguity for the reader trying to carefully follow the derivations.

2.  **Technical Assumptions and Omissions:**
    *   The stability results rely on the restrictive assumption `‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2`, inherited from [17]. The paper does not discuss the practical implications of this constraint or whether it limits the analysis to unrealistically small deformations.
    *   The connection to CNNs in Section 5 is based on a non-standard "homogenized" activation function (Eq. 8), which includes a data-dependent normalization term `nk(u) = ‖Pkxk-1(u)‖`. The paper's argument that this is related to ReLU is tenuous. It is unclear if a CNN with this specific activation behaves similarly to standard CNNs or if this construction is merely a theoretical convenience. This critical point is not explored or justified.
    *   The signal preservation claims in Lemma 1 and 2 are overstated. Lemma 2 states that signal recovery is possible via deconvolution, but notes this is only stable when the scale parameter is "small enough." Deconvolution is a notoriously ill-posed problem, and without a more rigorous analysis of the stability conditions, this claim of perfect recovery is questionable.
    *   The discussion of kernel approximation in Section 2.2 is superficial. It correctly states that projection is non-expansive, preserving the stability bounds, but it completely ignores the trade-off between the quality of the finite-dimensional approximation and the preservation of signal information, which is a central issue in practical kernel methods.
    *   The claim that border effects are "negligible when dealing with large realistic images" is an unsubstantiated assertion.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:**
    *   The paper is entirely theoretical. While theory papers are welcome at NIPS, the claims made here have direct, testable implications. For instance, does the RKHS norm bound from Proposition 7 correlate with the empirically measured stability of a CNN to small deformations? Does a CKN trained with a regularizer on the final layer's norm exhibit greater stability as predicted? The absence of any experiments, even simple synthetic ones to validate the core theoretical claims, makes the work feel incomplete and its practical significance speculative.

**Recommendation**

I recommend **rejection**.

This paper tackles an important problem and presents an elegant theoretical framework. The connection between the kernel construction and CNNs is a promising idea. However, the execution is flawed. The work feels more like a position paper or an initial theoretical exploration than a complete, polished piece of research. The combination of restrictive and not fully justified assumptions, glossed-over technical details (e.g., deconvolution stability, the non-standard CNN activation), multiple minor presentation issues, and a complete lack of empirical validation prevents me from recommending acceptance. While the core ideas have merit, the paper in its current form does not meet the high standards of quality and completeness expected for NIPS.
