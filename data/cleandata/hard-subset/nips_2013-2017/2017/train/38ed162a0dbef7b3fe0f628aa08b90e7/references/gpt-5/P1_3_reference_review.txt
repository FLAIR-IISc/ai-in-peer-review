Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that mirrors CNN architectures while living in RKHSs. Each layer applies (i) patch extraction, (ii) a pointwise kernel map built from homogeneous dot-product kernels, and (iii) Gaussian pooling. The resulting representation is analyzed in the continuous domain and shown to be (i) stable to small C1 diffeomorphisms with a Lipschitz-type bound and (ii) increasingly translation-invariant as the last pooling scale grows. The analysis extends the scattering transform framework to a broader class of architectures beyond wavelet-based networks, and shows how to obtain global invariance to compact groups by designing equivariant patch extraction and pooling on groups. The discretization is addressed via sampling and subsampling operators with information-preservation lemmas under mild conditions. The paper also connects the kernel RKHS to a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations: such CNNs are shown to belong to the RKHS with an explicit norm bound, implying that their prediction functions inherit the stability properties and that their generalization can be controlled via RKHS norms. The kernel approximation view links directly to Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), arguing that practical finite-dimensional approximations preserve stability up to approximation error.

Relation to prior work
The work is grounded in Mallat’s scattering transform (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat) and its stability to diffeomorphisms, but replaces fixed wavelets by learned or generic homogeneous kernels. It builds on CKNs (Mairal et al.), extends scattering-style proofs (e.g., commutator bounds) to shifted pooling operators and to more general kernels, and connects to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling) by constructing group-covariant patching and pooling. It also relates to invariant kernels, shallow invariance, and stability/robustness literature.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main stability bound (Theorem 6) is carefully derived by decomposing the representation into linear operators and bounding commutators [PkAk−1, Lτ] and LτAn − An. The extension of Mallat’s commutator analysis to shifted pooled operators (Lemma 4) is nontrivial and valuable. The group invariance construction is correct and standard in harmonic analysis on groups. The embedding of CNNs with homogeneous smooth activations into the RKHS (Proposition 7) is interesting and the recursive norm bound is plausible.
- Assumptions: The framework assumes an initial low-pass operator A0 (argued to be compatible with acquisition), homogeneous smooth activations (ReLU excluded, though smoothed variants apply), and patch sizes proportional to pooling scales (A3). Signal preservation in the discrete setting requires deconvolution with a Gaussian and ek ≥ sk; stability of deconvolution is asserted for small scales, but conditioning/noise sensitivity is not discussed.
- Empirical support: The paper is entirely theoretical; there are no experiments validating the constants, the invariance/stability trade-offs in practice, or the RKHS norm’s role in generalization for CKNs/CNNs.
- Completeness: Proofs are said to be in the appendix. The main claims (stability, invariance, CNN–RKHS link) appear sufficiently supported, although explicit constants and tightness are not explored.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable given the mathematical content. The continuous/discrete correspondence is clearly laid out with careful notation (P/M/A vs. P̄/M̄/Ā). The role of pooling scale in translation invariance is transparent. Some sections could benefit from more intuition:
- Discuss conditioning of the deconvolution used in Lemma 2 and robustness to noise.
- Provide concrete kernel examples satisfying (A1–A2) and the corresponding Cσ(a) (Appendix B is referenced but a short table in the main text would help).
- Clarify the practical impact of the homogenization factor nk(u) in CNNs and how it relates to standard implementations.
- The group invariance section could include a concrete example (e.g., roto-translation SE(2)) with the precise pooling kernel.

Originality
The work extends scattering stability beyond wavelet convolutions to a large family of convolutional kernel representations, refines commutator bounds to shifted pooling, and provides a rigorous CNN–RKHS connection for multilayer convolutional architectures with homogeneous activations. The combination is novel and timely (relative to prior CKN and scattering literature), and the group invariance construction in this kernelized multilayer setting is a useful unification.

Significance
The results give principled conditions under which multilayer convolutional representations are stable to deformations and increasingly translation-invariant, and they unify CNNs/CKNs within an RKHS where stability and generalization are controlled by the same norm. This can inform architectural and regularization choices (e.g., pooling scales, normalization, Lipschitz control), and provides theoretical grounding for CKN-style models. While immediate practical impact may be limited by assumptions (smooth activations, homogenization) and lack of experiments, the conceptual contribution is likely to influence further theory on deep representations.

Pros
- Clear, general stability bound for multilayer convolutional kernel representations; translation invariance scales as 1/σn.
- Extension of Mallat’s analysis to shifted Gaussian pooling; careful operator-theoretic treatment.
- Discrete-to-continuous bridge with information-preservation lemmas and guidance on subsampling.
- Group-equivariant construction enabling global invariance to compact groups.
- Nontrivial CNN–RKHS inclusion with explicit norm bounds, tying stability to generalization.

Cons
- No empirical validation (even small-scale) to illustrate constants, trade-offs, or the CNN–RKHS norm implications.
- Activation restriction to smooth homogeneous functions; ReLU excluded (only smoothed variants apply).
- The homogenization nk(u)=||patch|| in the activation deviates from standard CNNs; practical implications unclear.
- The stability bound grows linearly with depth (1 + n) and constants depend on κ and dimension; tightness and practical magnitudes not discussed.
- Signal recovery via deconvolution with small σ may be ill-conditioned and noise-sensitive; assumptions for stability not quantified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add small experiments with CKNs or standard CNNs equipped with smoothed ReLU and normalization to empirically verify deformation stability and translation invariance trends vs. σn and depth.
- Quantify constants (C1, C2) for typical settings and discuss their dependence on κ, dimension, and kernel choice; provide numerical ranges.
- Elaborate on the impact of kernel approximation and projection error on stability (bounds including approximation error terms).
- Discuss how to regularize CNN training to control the induced RKHS norm (e.g., Parseval constraints, spectral norm penalties) and connect to generalization.
- Provide a concrete SE(2) example for group pooling and clarify sampling/discretization on groups.
- Comment on robustness of Lemma 2 to noise and deconvolution conditioning; suggest practical parameter regimes (ek, sk, σk).

Recommendation
This is a solid theoretical contribution that extends and unifies important lines of work on invariance and stability in convolutional representations. Despite the lack of experiments and some restrictive assumptions, the novelty and rigor warrant acceptance at NIPS as a theory paper.
