**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel framework for signals defined on continuous domains, which generalizes the Convolutional Kernel Network (CKN) model. The core of the work is to use this kernel framework to study the geometry of the associated functional space (an RKHS). Drawing heavily on the analytical techniques developed for scattering transforms (Mallat, 2012), the authors prove that their representation is stable to the action of small diffeomorphisms. Specifically, they derive a Lipschitz-style bound showing that the change in the representation is controlled by the norm of the deformation and its gradient. The framework is also shown to achieve near-invariance to translations, with the degree of invariance being controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer. This is further generalized to achieve invariance to the action of arbitrary compact groups. A key contribution is establishing a formal link between this kernel framework and standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). The authors show that the RKHS associated with their kernel contains a class of CNNs that use smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This result allows the stability and invariance guarantees derived for the kernel representation to be directly applied to this class of CNNs, connecting the RKHS norm of a CNN function to its stability and, by extension, its generalization properties.

**Strengths**

1.  **Technical Soundness and Significance:** The paper provides a rigorous and valuable theoretical contribution to understanding deep convolutional networks. By placing a class of CNNs within a well-defined RKHS, it provides a principled framework for analyzing their geometric properties. The stability analysis (Theorem 6) formally captures the widely-held intuition that deep convolutional architectures are robust to small input deformations. This result is significant because it extends the powerful stability guarantees of scattering transforms, which use fixed filters, to a setting that accommodates learned representations. The connection between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization is particularly insightful, offering a potential theoretical explanation for the effectiveness of regularization in deep learning.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The construction of the multilayer kernel is presented methodically, starting from a continuous signal domain and then discussing practical considerations like discretization. The theoretical analysis proceeds logically from bounding individual operator commutators to the final stability theorem. The distinction between the theoretical construction and its practical CKN implementation is handled well. The arguments are easy to follow for a reader familiar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis.

3.  **Originality:** The main novelty lies in bridging the gap between the theoretical analysis of scattering networks and the practical architectures of CKNs and CNNs. While the proof techniques are inspired by Mallat (2012), their application and extension to a kernel framework that encompasses learnable models is a novel and important step. The formal result in Proposition 7, which embeds a class of CNNs into the RKHS of the proposed convolutional kernel, is a significant and original contribution that provides a new lens through which to view these models.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Applicability to Standard CNNs:** The connection to CNNs, while a major contribution, is restricted to a specific class of models. The requirement for smooth, homogeneous activation functions and the introduction of a specific normalization term `nk(u) = ||Pkxk-1(u)||` (Section 5) deviates from standard CNN architectures. The most popular activation, ReLU, is homogeneous but not smooth and thus falls outside the direct scope of the RKHS construction, requiring a smoothed approximation. The practical implications of the required normalization term are not explored, making it unclear how closely these theoretical results apply to widely used models like ResNets or VGG networks in their standard form.

2.  **Absence of Experimental Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is a valid approach for a foundational work, the claims could have been substantially strengthened by empirical evidence. For instance, experiments could verify the stability bounds for the specified class of CNNs, or explore the trade-off between the final pooling scale (`σn`) and empirical translation invariance. Demonstrating a correlation between the RKHS norm bound (from Proposition 7) and the measured stability of a trained network would provide a powerful validation of the theory.

3.  **Signal Preservation Claims:** The claim of signal preservation and recovery (Lemmas 1 and 2) may be overstated. The pooling operators are fundamentally low-pass filters and cause irreversible loss of high-frequency information. While the authors state that recovery is possible via deconvolution, this process is known to be ill-posed and numerically unstable, especially when the filter has zeros in its frequency response. A more nuanced discussion of information loss and its effect on the representation would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a rigorous and novel theoretical framework for analyzing the stability of deep convolutional representations, extending results from scattering transforms to models with learned components.
*   Establishes a significant formal connection between a class of CNNs, CKNs, and kernel methods, contributing to the theoretical understanding of deep learning.
*   The work is of high technical quality, clearly written, and addresses a fundamental question in the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical results for CNNs are confined to a non-standard class of architectures, limiting the immediate practical relevance of the findings.
*   The lack of any experimental validation leaves the empirical relevance of the derived bounds as an open question.
*   The analysis relies heavily on existing techniques from the scattering transform literature.
