Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework on continuous domains that mirrors CNN architectures (patch extraction → pointwise nonlinear “kernel mapping” → local pooling) and induces an RKHS representation Φn(x). Within this framework, the authors (i) establish stability to C1 diffeomorphisms in the sense of Mallat: the representation error is bounded by C1(1+n)||∇τ||∞ + (C2/σn)||τ||∞ times ||x||, showing increased translation invariance with larger final pooling scale; (ii) show how to obtain invariance to general (locally compact) groups by choosing group-covariant patch extraction and pooling, akin to group convolutions; (iii) connect the RKHS induced by their multilayer convolutional kernel to a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, giving norm bounds that control Lipschitz stability of the induced predictors; and (iv) discuss discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations, arguing that CKN-style projections preserve non-expansiveness and thus maintain stability.

The work builds squarely on scattering networks and Mallat’s stability analysis (Bruna & Mallat 2013; Mallat 2012/2013), and on Convolutional Kernel Networks (Mairal et al. 2014/2017). It also relates to invariance via group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling 2016) and the invariant kernels literature (e.g., Haasdonk & Burkhardt 2007; Anselmi et al. 2015). The novelty lies in extending Mallat’s analysis to a broader kernelized convolutional setting that encompasses CKNs and a class of CNNs, and in making the RKHS norm the common control knob for both stability and generalization.

Quality
- The theoretical development is careful and leverages standard tools (operator norms, Schur’s test, commutators). The stability bound is clear and parallels scattering results while handling shifted pooling kernels and multilayer patch extraction.
- Assumptions (norm-preserving and non-expansive kernel maps; patch sizes scaling with pooling; initial anti-aliasing A0) are reasonable in the continuous model, and the discretization section provides a plausible bridge (though largely qualitative).
- The CNN–RKHS embedding for smooth homogeneous activations is interesting; the recursive bound on the RKHS norm makes the stability–generalization connection explicit.
- However, there are no empirical validations or concrete finite-sample generalization results; “improved sample complexity” remains a qualitative claim. Some technical claims (signal recovery with deconvolution in Lemma 2) are fragile under noise and discretization. Constants and tightness of the depth dependence are not discussed, and the class of admissible activations is somewhat restrictive (smooth variants of ReLU).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and well organized. Notation is heavy but consistent; the decomposition into operators helps.
- Key examples of admissible kernels and activations are relegated to the appendix; a brief table in the main text would help.
- The continuous/discrete connection is described, but quantitative discretization errors and boundary effects are not analyzed. The “signal preservation” results read stronger than what is practical; caveats could be emphasized.

Originality
- The main stability statement closely parallels scattering theory but in a more general multilayer kernel setting and with an explicit link to CNNs via RKHS containment. The group invariance construction is standard. The CNN norm bound leveraging kernel feature maps is related to prior dual/kernel views of deep nets (e.g., Daniely et al., Montavon et al., Anselmi et al.), but the present mapping for convolutional architectures with explicit operator-level analysis is a useful synthesis.
- Overall, the contribution is incremental but nontrivial: it unifies and extends known ideas to a broader class and clarifies the role of RKHS norms.

Significance
- For the theory community, the work offers a principled lens on invariance and stability beyond scattering, potentially informing regularization and architecture design. The RKHS norm as a joint control of Lipschitz stability and generalization is conceptually appealing.
- Practical impact is unclear without experiments demonstrating: (i) stability of CKN/CNN predictors trained with norm regularization; (ii) trade-offs between σn, depth, approximation quality, and accuracy; (iii) robustness to diffeomorphic and adversarial perturbations.
- The scope of CNNs covered (smooth homogeneous activations) limits immediate applicability; the relationship to standard ReLU nets is via smoothed approximations.

Pros
- Clear, operator-theoretic stability bound for a multilayer convolutional kernel representation.
- Unified framework encompassing CKNs and a class of CNNs with explicit RKHS norm control.
- Discussion of group invariance via equivariant patch/pooling; compatible with existing G-CNN ideas.
- Careful treatment of discretization and kernel approximation preserving non-expansiveness.

Cons
- Limited novelty versus scattering and prior invariant kernel/CNN analyses; group invariance section is standard.
- No empirical validation; claims about sample complexity and robustness are not backed by experiments or generalization theorems.
- Assumptions (smooth homogeneous activations, norm-preserving maps) and signal recovery via deconvolution reduce practicality.
- Stability bound grows with depth; constants and tightness are not characterized.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide concrete examples and computed Cσ for the kernels used, and quantify the resulting RKHS norm bounds for typical architectures.
- Add experiments with CKNs and CNNs on synthetic diffeomorphisms and adversarial perturbations to validate the stability claims and the effect of σn and depth.
- Give quantitative discretization error bounds and discuss noise sensitivity of Lemma 2’s deconvolution-based recovery.
- Clarify the coverage of standard ReLU CNNs: how tight is the smoothed-activation approximation, and how does the RKHS norm relate to usual weight norms?
- Compare explicitly to scattering and G-CNNs: where does the present framework strictly generalize or differ?
- Discuss practical regularizers or training procedures that approximately control the induced RKHS norm.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is technically solid and synthesizes known ideas into a coherent framework with some new bounds, but lacks empirical validation and stronger novelty beyond scattering/CKN theory. Strengthening with experiments and tighter, more quantitative results would make it a compelling NeurIPS contribution.

Confidence
High for the assessment of theory and positioning; medium for the practical implications pending experiments.
