

# Review: Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a kernel framework for analyzing the theoretical properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs), specifically focusing on invariance and stability to deformations. The authors generalize the convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) framework to develop a multilayer convolutional kernel that operates on continuous signals, allowing them to study translation-invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms. They establish theoretical guarantees similar to the scattering transform [17], but within a more general framework that encompasses a wide class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The paper builds upon Mallat's work on scattering networks and extends it to more standard CNN architectures through a kernel perspective, while also connecting to related work on group invariance and kernel methods for deep learning.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound theoretical framework with rigorous mathematical proofs (many relegated to the appendix). The authors carefully address the transition from continuous to discrete representations, which is crucial for practical applications. They demonstrate how their framework can be used to analyze both CKNs and standard CNNs, providing a unifying perspective on their theoretical properties. The paper's mathematical treatment is thorough, with well-defined assumptions (A1-A3) and clear lemmas establishing stability bounds.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by extending the scattering transform framework to more general CNN architectures through a kernel approach. The key insight is showing that the functional space defined by their kernel contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, which allows them to transfer stability results from the kernel framework to these CNNs. This provides a new theoretical lens for understanding why CNNs work well, going beyond the specific architecture of the scattering transform. The connection to RKHS norms controlling both stability and generalization is particularly novel.

**Significance:** The theoretical results have important implications for understanding the properties of CNNs, particularly regarding their invariance to deformations. The authors establish that stability to diffeomorphisms can be controlled through the norm in the functional space, which has direct relevance to generalization performance. This provides a principled theoretical foundation for regularization in CNNs, potentially leading to improved sample complexity. The work bridges the gap between theoretical analysis and practical deep learning, offering insights that could guide future architectural designs.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem statement to theoretical development to connections with CNNs. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the authors provide sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with the scattering transform framework. The introduction effectively motivates the work by highlighting the gap in understanding the theoretical properties of CNNs.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Empirical Validation:** While the paper provides strong theoretical results, it lacks empirical validation of these claims. The connection to practical CNNs is somewhat limited, as most modern CNNs use ReLU activations that are not smooth (though the authors acknowledge this and discuss smoothed variants). The paper would benefit from experimental results demonstrating how the theoretical insights translate to improved performance in practice.

**Technical Accessibility:** The paper assumes a strong background in functional analysis and harmonic analysis, which may limit its accessibility to the broader NIPS audience. While this is understandable for a theoretical paper, more intuitive explanations of key concepts would enhance the paper's impact.

**Computational Considerations:** The paper discusses the connection to CKNs and their computational approximations, but doesn't fully address the computational trade-offs between the theoretical framework and practical implementations. The relationship between the theoretical stability guarantees and computational efficiency of the resulting models could be explored more deeply.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the invariance and stability properties of CNNs through a novel kernel framework. The mathematical development is rigorous and well-executed, and the results have clear implications for understanding why CNNs work well. The connection to RKHS norms provides a principled way to control stability and generalization, which is a valuable theoretical insight.

While the lack of empirical validation is a notable limitation, the paper is clearly intended as a theoretical contribution, and the theoretical results are strong and well-supported. The authors have made a substantial effort to relate their work to previous contributions (scattering transform, CKNs, group invariance) and to clearly articulate their novel contributions.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Pro-accept arguments:**
- Strong theoretical contribution that provides new insights into why CNNs work well
- Rigorous mathematical development with clear connections to previous work
- Novel framework that unifies analysis of CKNs and standard CNNs
- Important implications for understanding stability, invariance, and generalization in deep learning

**Con-accept arguments:**
- Limited empirical validation of theoretical claims
- Requires strong mathematical background for full understanding
- Connection to practical CNNs with non-smooth activations (ReLU) is somewhat indirect

The paper advances the theoretical understanding of deep learning architectures in a meaningful way, providing a principled framework for analyzing invariance and stability properties. This work is of high quality, original, and significant for the deep learning community, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
