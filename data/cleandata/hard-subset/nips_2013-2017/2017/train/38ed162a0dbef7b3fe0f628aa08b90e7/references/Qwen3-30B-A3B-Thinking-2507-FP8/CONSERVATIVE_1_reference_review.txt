

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

## Summary
This paper proposes a kernel framework for studying invariance and stability properties of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and convolutional kernel networks (CKNs). The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes CKNs and uses reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to theoretically analyze stability to diffeomorphisms and translation invariance. They prove that their framework provides stability guarantees similar to the scattering transform, but extends to more standard CNN architectures. The paper also establishes a connection between certain CNNs with homogeneous activation functions and the RKHS defined by their kernel.

## Strengths
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of CNNs. The mathematical framework is well-constructed, with careful attention to the details of the kernel construction and stability proofs. The authors provide a clear connection between their kernel framework and previous work on scattering transforms. The paper is well-written, with rigorous proofs and adequate references to related work.

## Weaknesses
The primary weakness of this paper is the lack of empirical validation. The authors present a comprehensive theoretical analysis but fail to demonstrate how their framework translates to improved performance in practical applications. While they claim that stability may lead to improved sample complexity, they provide no experimental evidence to support this assertion.

The connection to standard CNNs is problematic. The authors restrict to homogeneous activation functions, which are not commonly used in modern CNNs. They acknowledge that ReLU, the most successful activation function, is not smooth and thus not directly covered by their framework, requiring a smoothed variant. This significantly limits the applicability of their theoretical results to the CNNs widely used in practice.

The paper also fails to adequately differentiate its contribution from existing work. The stability results bear strong resemblance to those in Mallat's scattering transform work (cited as [17]), with the authors claiming to extend them to more standard CNN architectures. However, the extension appears theoretical rather than practical, as they don't demonstrate how their framework leads to better models or improved performance on real tasks.

The paper makes a theoretical claim about the relationship between stability and generalization (via RKHS norms), but doesn't provide evidence that this relationship holds in practice. The connection between the theoretical stability guarantees and improved sample complexity remains speculative without empirical validation.

## Specific Criticisms
1. **Lack of empirical validation**: The paper presents theoretical results but provides no experiments on standard datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) to demonstrate that their framework leads to better models or improved stability in practice.

2. **Limited applicability to standard CNNs**: The requirement for homogeneous activation functions significantly restricts the practical relevance of the results, as modern CNNs primarily use ReLU or variants that don't satisfy the smoothness conditions required by the authors.

3. **Questionable significance**: While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate how these insights translate to practical improvements in deep learning. The connection between stability and improved sample complexity remains unverified.

4. **Overstated novelty**: The paper's main contribution appears to be a theoretical extension of existing work on scattering transforms to a more general kernel framework, but it doesn't provide evidence that this extension leads to new practical insights or improvements.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection of this paper. While the theoretical contribution is technically sound and well-executed, the paper fails to meet NeurIPS standards for significance and empirical validation. The authors present a novel mathematical framework but do not demonstrate how it leads to practical improvements in deep learning. The lack of empirical validation and the limited applicability to standard CNN architectures significantly undermine the paper's contribution to the field.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Providing empirical results showing that the theoretical stability properties translate to improved performance on standard benchmarks
2. Demonstrating how their framework can be applied to common CNN architectures without requiring restrictive activation functions
3. Better differentiating their contribution from existing work on the theoretical properties of CNNs and scattering networks

Without these elements, the paper remains a theoretical exercise that doesn't advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. Given the high bar for NeurIPS acceptance, the lack of empirical validation and limited practical relevance make this paper unsuitable for publication.
