- Constructs a multilayer convolutional kernel whose RKHS contains a broad class of CNNs (including CKNs).  
- Proves stability of the kernel representation to diffeomorphic deformations and establishes translation invariance.  
- Connects representation stability to improved generalization/sample complexity for predictors in the RKHS.  
- Frames CNN analysis through positive definite kernels and harmonic-analysis tools, linking to scattering transforms.  
- Positions the work within prior literature on CKNs, scattering, group invariance, and kernel–deep-learning intersections.  
- Theoretical analysis is mathematically rigorous, using RKHS and harmonic-analysis-based proofs.  
- Bridges kernel methods and practical CNN architectures, offering a new theoretical perspective on why deep learning works.  
- Framework is fairly general, covering smooth, homogeneous activations and allowing extensions to other transformation groups.  
- Addresses discretization and shows how sampling affects signal preservation, improving practical relevance of results.  
- Limitation: highly technical presentation requires strong background in functional analysis and signal processing.  
- Limitation: practical implications are underexplored and computation in infinite-dimensional RKHS remains challenging.  
- Limitation: focus on smooth, homogeneous activations excludes common non-smooth activations like ReLU.  
- Limitation: largely theoretical work with limited empirical validation linking stability to generalization.  
- Ambiguity: discussion of norm preservation versus energy preservation is unclear and underexplored.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, rigorous framework that meaningfully connects stability and generalization and bridges kernels and CNNs.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility concerns, limited practical validation, and activation-function restrictions.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: add discussion on practical algorithms/implementation, intuitive RKHS-norm/generalization explanation, extensions to non-smooth activations, and a small empirical illustration.
