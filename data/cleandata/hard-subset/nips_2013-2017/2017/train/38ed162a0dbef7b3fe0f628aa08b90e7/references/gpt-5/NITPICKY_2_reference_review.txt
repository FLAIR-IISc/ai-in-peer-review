Summary
The paper studies invariance and stability of multilayer convolutional representations through a positive definite kernel framework that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains. It constructs a multilayer mapping via patch extraction, kernel mapping to RKHSs, and Gaussian pooling; defines a corresponding kernel and function space; and proves Lipschitz stability to C1 diffeomorphisms in the spirit of Mallat’s scattering transform. It also proposes a group-equivariant construction by defining patch extraction and pooling on locally compact groups. Finally, it claims that a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, yielding norm-based control of stability and generalization.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual link between CKNs/CNNs and RKHS geometry, enabling a clean separation between representation and predictor and yielding Lipschitz-type stability bounds.
- The multilayer kernel construction on continuous domains and the commutator-based analysis extend scattering-style arguments beyond wavelet filters.
- Theorem 6 gives an interpretable trade-off: translation invariance grows with σn, deformation sensitivity grows with depth.
- Group-equivariant layer design aligns with prior group-convolution approaches and scattering on groups.
- The RKHS containment result for a class of CNNs (Proposition 7) provides an explicit norm control pathway.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Novelty vs. scattering and prior kernel analyses: Technically, the main stability bound (Theorem 6) largely mirrors Mallat’s framework, with similar assumptions and a depth factor; the main new element is handling general homogeneous kernels and the commutator [LcAσ, Lτ]. While useful, this feels incremental without empirical or sharper bounds (e.g., improved dependence on n, κ, or d). The group-equivariant construction essentially re-derives known equivariance via group convolutions (cf. Bruna & Mallat, Cohen & Welling) without new stability results for general groups.
- Key claims depend on appendices/extended version: Many essential proofs and details (e.g., norm lower bounds, last-layer Gaussian mapping stabilizing norm, Lemma B.2 conditions and constants Cσ, proofs of Lemma 1/2, commutator analysis) are deferred to appendices or an “extended version [3]”. For NeurIPS, the submission should be self-contained; relying on an external preprint for core arguments and properties is problematic.
- Lemma 1 (Signal preservation) and Lemma 2 (recovery with subsampling) are underspecified/overstated:
  - In the continuous case, recovering xk−1 from samples of xk = AkMkPkxk−1 requires invertibility of Mk and Ak and sufficient sampling density. The condition “Hk contains linear functions ⟨w,·⟩ ⇒ recoverability” is not sufficient as stated, because Ak is smoothing and generally non-invertible on L2; precise conditions on bandlimitation induced by A0 and on the sampling set (Nyquist-like) are needed. The statement “can be reconstructed from such a sampling” needs a constructive inverse and stability constants.
  - In the discrete case, deconvolution of a Gaussian after subsampling is ill-conditioned. The text asserts stability “when its scale parameter… is small enough,” but antialiasing typically requires σk scaled to the subsampling factor, which worsens deconvolution conditioning; the trade-off is not addressed, and no quantitative condition (Fourier-domain bound) is given.
- Proposition 3: The bound includes both ‖[An,Lτ]‖ and ‖LτAn − An‖. It is unclear why both terms are needed (the latter is not a commutator). Please clarify the derivation and whether one of these terms is redundant or double-counted in the telescoping argument.
- Stability with kernel approximation (Section 3.2): Projecting with Πk is non-expansive, but Πk and Lτ generally do not commute. The effect of projection adds additional commutator/approximation error terms that can accumulate with depth. The text asserts Theorem 6 “remains valid,” but the approximation error should appear explicitly (e.g., additive terms depending on supu approximation residuals and their propagation).
- CNN containment (Section 5): The constructed CNN class differs materially from standard practice:
  - The activation is σ(⟨w, z⟩/‖z‖) scaled by nk(u)=‖Pkzk−1(u)‖. This local homogenization is not how conventional CNNs operate; it effectively introduces input-dependent normalization at every pre-activation. Claiming the RKHS contains a “large class of CNNs” seems overstated; it contains a nonstandard, normalized variant with smooth homogeneous σ.
  - ReLU is excluded due to non-smoothness; the use of smoothed ReLU with the inverse polynomial kernel may be theoretically convenient but diverges from common practice. The practical implications of replacing ReLU with smoothed variants and adding the normalization are not evaluated.
  - The recursive norm bound (Proposition 7) depends on pk and Cσ, but constants and their growth are opaque. Without examples quantifying these constants for realistic architectures, the bound is hard to interpret.
- Depth and constants: Theorem 6 has deformation sensitivity scaling as O((1+n)‖∇τ‖∞), and constants C1 depend on κ as roughly κd+1. This suggests poor scaling with depth, receptive field growth, and dimension; no discussion of how to choose σk, Sk to mitigate this, nor comparisons with scattering’s path-length dependence.
- Clarity and notation: The choice to use the same ‖·‖ symbol for L2 norms, Hilbert norms, and operator norms invites confusion. Some statements (e.g., “‖Ak‖ ≤ 1 by Schur’s test”) are correct but would be clearer via Fourier-domain arguments for translation-invariant kernels (‖Aσ‖L2→L2 = sup|H(ω)| = 1 for normalized Gaussians).
- Empirical evaluation: There are no experiments showing the predicted stability/invariance on real data, nor demonstration that CKNs trained with the proposed design enjoy improved robustness/sample complexity. Given the paper’s practical motivations, at least minimal empirical support would strengthen significance.
- Presentation and formatting: Numerous typos and inconsistencies (see below), missing figures referenced (Figure 1, 2), and a very sloppy bibliography (missing years/venues, repeated commas, “R ©” artifacts, inconsistent capitalization).

Minor comments and typos

- Many occurrences of “L 2” with stray spaces; same for “R d”, “R pk”.
- Section 2.1: “discrete analogues of their discrete counterparts” likely “discrete analogues of their continuous counterparts.”
- “It is then be possible to derive…” grammatical error.
- Notation: e−1/2 k, s 1/2 k spacing issues; ensure consistent use of subscripts/superscripts.
- References have missing years/venues and artifacts (e.g., “Foundations and Trends R © in Machine Learning”). Please fix all entries.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide precise, stable reconstruction conditions and operators for Lemma 1/2, with quantitative bounds (e.g., bandlimitation, sampling density, deconvolution conditioning).
2. Clarify Proposition 3 derivation and the necessity of both ‖[An,Lτ]‖ and ‖LτAn − An‖ terms.
3. In Section 3.2, quantify the additional errors due to projections Πk not commuting with Lτ, and provide a full stability bound including approximation terms.
4. Quantify constants C1, C2 for the chosen h and typical κ, d, and discuss depth trade-offs.
5. For Proposition 7, give concrete Cσ for kernels in Appendix B and bound growth with depth/width; can you handle standard ReLU without homogenization?
6. Include at least a small empirical study validating the stability predictions on deformations, and a comparison to scattering/CNN baselines.

Recommendation
While the theoretical direction is interesting, the submission relies heavily on prior frameworks with incremental extensions, omits crucial details to the supplement/extended version, makes overstated claims about CNN coverage, and lacks empirical support. Numerous technical and presentation issues remain. I recommend rejection in its current form; a revised version with complete proofs, clarified assumptions, quantified constants, proper handling of approximation errors, and minimal empirical validation would be much stronger. Confidence: high.
