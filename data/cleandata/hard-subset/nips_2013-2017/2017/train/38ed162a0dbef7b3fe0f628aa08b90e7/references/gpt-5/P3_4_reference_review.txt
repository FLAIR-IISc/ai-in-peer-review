Summary
The paper studies invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations through a kernel/RKHS lens. It builds a multilayer convolutional kernel on continuous domains by composing patch extraction, a homogeneous dot-product kernel map that is norm-preserving and non-expansive, and Gaussian pooling. It analyzes (i) stability to C1-diffeomorphisms à la Mallat via operator commutators, yielding a bound that decays with the final pooling scale and grows linearly with depth; (ii) translation invariance; and (iii) group-equivariant/invariant constructions by defining group-covariant patch extraction and pooling on locally compact groups. The paper discusses discretization and kernel approximation (CKN) while preserving stability via non-expansive projections. Finally, it connects the RKHS associated to the multilayer kernel to a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, providing RKHS norm bounds for such CNNs, thus linking stability and generalization via the RKHS norm.

Relation to prior work
- Extends Mallat’s scattering theory to a broader class of convolutional architectures using positive definite kernels, borrowing commutator techniques but handling shifted kernels and multilayer compositions.
- Builds on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) by formalizing their continuous-domain limit and showing stability properties akin to scattering while allowing learned finite-dimensional approximations.
- Places group equivariance/invariance (e.g., Cohen & Welling) into the kernel framework with explicit equivariant patch extraction and pooling.
- Relates to recent analyses linking Lipschitz stability and robustness, and to kernel views of deep nets.

Quality (technical soundness)
Strengths:
- The stability result (Theorem 6) is clear and interpretable: the deformation term scales with (1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ and translation term decays like ‖τ‖∞/σn. The commutator-based proof strategy is appropriate and carefully adapted from scattering to convolution-plus-shifted-kernel operators.
- Assumptions (A1–A3) are well-motivated and map to common design choices (Gaussian pooling, increasing pooling scales and receptive fields).
- The discretization and projection discussions are sensible: non-expansive projections preserve the stability bounds; links between discrete parameters and continuous scales are spelled out.
- The RKHS embedding of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (Proposition 7), together with recursive norm bounds, is a useful bridge between CNN practice and RKHS theory.

Weaknesses / potential issues:
- The “signal preservation” lemmas (continuous and discrete) rely on deconvolution/invertibility of smoothing, which is delicate. In continuous L2, Gaussian convolution does not admit a bounded inverse; in discrete settings, the stability of deconvolution depends on filter bandwidth and noise. The text acknowledges stability when σ is “small enough,” but more precise conditions or counterexamples would help; as stated, Lemma 1 may read stronger than warranted.
- The general stability bound grows linearly with depth (1+n). In contrast to the scattering transform’s energy-preserving construction, pooling in this framework can progressively attenuate the signal, making comparison across depths more fraught. Some quantification of the trade-off between increased invariance (σn) and energy loss would be valuable.
- The class of CNNs embedded in the RKHS requires homogenized activations with explicit dependence on input patch norms (nk(u) factors). This deviates from standard CNNs and weakens the claim of covering a “large class of CNNs.” The treatment of ReLU requires smoothing and a specific kernel; it would be helpful to state explicitly which activations/kernels satisfy Lemma B.2 and how close they are to practical ReLU networks in behavior.
- The CKN approximation analysis ensures non-expansiveness but gives no rates linking projection error to stability or prediction accuracy. Similarly, the discussion of sample complexity via RKHS norms is qualitative; no explicit generalization bounds are provided for the multilayer kernel class.
- Practical pooling in CNNs often uses striding or max-pooling; the theory here assumes Gaussian linear pooling. Some comments on how the results might extend (or not) to these common choices would improve applicability.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable at a theoretical level. The operator-theoretic exposition is clear and the relation to scattering is made explicit.
- Several key proofs and kernel assumptions are deferred to appendices. Including a compact catalog of admissible kernels/activations with concrete examples in the main text would help.
- The statement of Lemma 1 and its practical implications could be clarified with explicit invertibility/stability conditions.

Originality
- The extension of scattering-style stability to a broad kernelized multilayer conv architecture, including the commutator bound with shifted kernels, is novel.
- The RKHS embedding of certain CNNs with recursive norm bounds in a multilayer convolutional kernel space is a nontrivial and useful contribution.
- The group invariance construction is standard in spirit but cleanly integrated into the kernel framework.

Significance
- Conceptually advances our understanding of invariance/stability in learned convolutional representations beyond fixed wavelet-based scattering.
- Provides a theoretical foundation for CKN-type architectures and suggests norm-based control for stability and generalization in convolutional RKHSs.
- Lacks empirical validation and explicit generalization/sample complexity bounds; thus immediate impact on practice may be limited, but the theoretical insights are likely to be influential.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, careful theoretical contribution extending an important line of work (scattering) to learned convolutional kernel representations.
- Clear stability guarantees with interpretable dependence on depth and pooling scale.
- Bridges CNNs and RKHSs, offering a principled lens on stability/generalization.

Cons for acceptance
- No experiments validating the theory or demonstrating practical benefits/trade-offs.
- Some assumptions/claims (signal preservation; coverage of “large class of CNNs”) are stronger than what is standard in practice and need sharpening.
- Missing quantitative bounds on approximation/generalization; limited discussion of non-Gaussian pooling commonly used in CNNs.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify the precise conditions under which Lemma 1 (and Lemma 2) hold stably. Can you quantify reconstruction error versus σk and noise?
- Provide explicit examples of admissible kernels/activations (with Cσ finite), and a discussion of how close the smoothed activations are to ReLU in practice.
- Can you sketch a Rademacher complexity or margin bound for the multilayer kernel class using the recursive norm bounds in Prop. 7?
- Discuss whether similar stability bounds can be obtained for strided/average pooling and for max-pooling.
- Include small empirical illustrations (e.g., measuring feature stability vs. σn and n, or comparing smoothed-ReLU CNNs vs. RKHS predictions).

Overall assessment
A strong theoretical paper that meaningfully generalizes scattering-style stability to a kernelized multilayer convolutional setting and forges a useful connection to CNNs. While somewhat idealized and lacking empirical validation, it is technically solid and significant for the theory of deep representations. I lean toward acceptance, provided the authors clarify the reconstruction claims and better delimit the scope of CNNs covered by their RKHS construction.
