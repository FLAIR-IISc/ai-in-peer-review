- Paper builds a multilayer continuous-domain convolutional kernel (patch extraction + homogeneous dot-product map + Gaussian pooling) and studies its invariance/stability via RKHS tools.
- Proves stability to C1-diffeomorphisms using commutator bounds, showing deformation error scales with (1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ and translation error decays like ‖τ‖∞/σn.
- Analyzes translation invariance and constructs group-equivariant/invariant kernels via group-covariant patch extraction and pooling.
- Discusses discretization and CKN approximations, showing non-expansive projections preserve stability bounds.
- Embeds a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations into the RKHS and provides recursive RKHS norm bounds linking stability and generalization.
- Extends Mallat’s scattering theory to kernelized multilayer architectures and adapts commutator techniques to shifted kernels and multilayer composition.
- Formalizes the continuous-domain limit of CKNs and shows scattering-like stability for learned finite-dimensional approximations.
- Integrates group equivariance (e.g., Cohen & Welling) into the kernel framework with explicit constructions.
- Stability result (Theorem 6) is clear, interpretable, and technically well-justified; assumptions A1–A3 are well-motivated.
- Discretization/projection arguments are sensible and preserve non-expansiveness; RKHS embedding of smoothed CNNs is a valuable bridge to practice.
- Signal-preservation lemmas rely on delicate deconvolution/invertibility claims; continuous Gaussian convolution lacks a bounded inverse and discrete stability depends on filter bandwidth/noise.
- General stability bound grows linearly with depth, raising trade-off concerns between increased invariance (larger σn) and signal energy loss.
- The RKHS-covered CNN class requires homogenized activations dependent on patch norms, deviating from standard CNNs and weakening coverage claims for practical ReLU networks.
- CKN approximation analysis lacks rates linking projection error to stability/prediction, and sample-complexity discussion is qualitative without explicit generalization bounds.
- Theory assumes Gaussian linear pooling; applicability to common practical choices like strided or max-pooling is unclear.
- Paper is well organized and readable, but key proofs/assumptions are deferred to appendices; a main-text catalog of admissible kernels/activations would help.
- Novel contributions: commutator-based stability for kernelized multilayer conv architectures, RKHS embedding with recursive norm bounds, and clean kernel-based group invariance integration.
- Significance: advances theoretical understanding of invariance/stability beyond wavelet scattering and provides a principled foundation for CKNs, but lacks empirical validation and explicit sample-complexity bounds.
- Pros for acceptance: solid theoretical contribution, interpretable stability guarantees, and useful CNN–RKHS connection.
- Cons for acceptance: no experiments, some overstated assumptions (signal preservation, CNN coverage), and missing quantitative approximation/generalization bounds.
- Suggested revisions: clarify stability/invertibility conditions for Lemmas 1–2; list admissible kernels/activations and how close smoothed activations are to ReLU; provide Rademacher/margin-style bounds using Prop.7; discuss extensions to strided/average/max-pooling; add small empirical illustrations.
- Overall: Strong, technically solid theoretical paper generalizing scattering-style stability to kernelized multilayer convolutions with useful CNN connections; lean accept if reconstruction claims and CNN coverage are clarified.
