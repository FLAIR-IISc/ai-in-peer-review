

# Review: Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations

This paper presents a theoretical study of convolutional architectures using positive definite kernels, aiming to characterize the invariance and stability properties of deep representations. The authors introduce a functional space derived from a kernel for multi-dimensional signals that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), demonstrating that this space contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. They prove stability results to diffeomorphisms and translation invariance, showing how these properties can be translated to predictive models through RKHS norm control.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by extending the scattering transform framework to more standard convolutional architectures. The connection between kernel methods and CNNs is novel, and the stability results (Theorem 6) provide valuable theoretical guarantees that align with the practical success of convolutional networks. The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical development and appropriate references to related work, particularly Mallat's scattering transform.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Formatting inconsistencies:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting errors that undermine its professionalism. Mathematical notation is inconsistent (e.g., "‖x‖ 2" vs "‖x‖^2"), spacing around operators is irregular (e.g., "‖f‖H‖ϕ(z)− ϕ(z ′)‖H"), and the use of en-dashes versus hyphens for indices ("k–1" vs "k-1") is inconsistent throughout the paper. These are avoidable errors that should have been caught during proofreading.

2. **Theoretical precision:** The paper claims "the most successful activation function, namely rectified linear units, is homogeneous" but this is mathematically inaccurate. ReLU is homogeneous of degree 1, but the paper conflates this with the mathematical definition of homogeneous functions. This inaccuracy is particularly problematic in Section 5, where the authors build their theoretical framework around homogeneous activations.

3. **Missing technical details:** Lemma 1 states "Hk contains linear functions 〈w, ·〉 with w in Pk" but fails to clarify what "w in Pk" means for linear functions, since Pk was defined as L2(Sk, Hk-1). This ambiguity in the mathematical definition weakens the theoretical foundation.

4. **Citation oversight:** The paper cites "Group invariance and stability to deformations of deep convolutional representations" by Bietti and Mairal (arXiv:1706.03078) but fails to discuss this work in the related work section, despite its direct relevance to the current paper's topic.

5. **Unsubstantiated claims:** The paper states "the results apply more broadly to CNNs that are contained in the functional space associated to the kernel" but provides no clear characterization of which CNNs are contained in this functional space, making the claim appear unsubstantiated.

6. **Reference inconsistency:** The paper refers to "the extended version of the paper [3]" but fails to include this extended version in the bibliography, creating confusion for readers.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents theoretically interesting results with potential significance for understanding convolutional architectures, the cumulative effect of these minor technical and presentation flaws is substantial. The paper is not technically rigorous enough for NIPS, where precision in mathematical formulation is paramount. The inconsistent notation, inaccurate claims about ReLU, and missing technical details undermine the paper's credibility as a scientific contribution.

The authors have made a commendable effort to connect kernel methods with convolutional architectures, but the paper requires significant revision to address these fundamental issues before it can be considered for publication. The minor flaws identified here are not merely typographical errors but represent a lack of attention to mathematical precision that is essential for a theoretical contribution at NIPS.

I recommend rejection. The paper's contribution is interesting but the current version is not sufficiently polished to meet NIPS standards. The authors should address these technical inconsistencies before resubmitting.
