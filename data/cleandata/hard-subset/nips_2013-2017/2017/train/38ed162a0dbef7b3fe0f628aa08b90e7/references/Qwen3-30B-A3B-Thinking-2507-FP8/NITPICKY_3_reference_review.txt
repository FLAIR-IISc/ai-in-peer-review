

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

## Summary
This paper proposes a kernel framework to study the invariance and stability properties of convolutional architectures. The authors generalize convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains, showing that their framework provides stability guarantees similar to the scattering transform but for more standard CNN architectures. They establish theoretical connections between their kernel framework and CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, demonstrating that stability properties translate to predictive models through RKHS norm control.

## Strengths
The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by formalizing the relationship between kernel methods and convolutional architectures. The stability analysis extends previous work on scattering networks to a broader class of models, providing a principled way to understand invariance properties. The connection between RKHS norm and generalization is particularly insightful, offering theoretical justification for regularization techniques used in practice.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Technical Details
1. The paper contains a formatting error in the author affiliation line: "∗Univ. Grenoble Alpes, Inria, CNRS, Grenoble INP, LJK, 38000 Grenoble, France" appears mid-text rather than as a proper footnote or author line.

2. The abstract contains a grammatical error: "the same digit with different handwritings may correspond to the same images up to small deformations" should be "the same class label" instead of "same images."

3. The reference to "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" is incorrectly formatted (should be "2nd" or "The").

### Theoretical Nuances
1. The paper claims to generalize CKNs to continuous domains but fails to clearly articulate the novelty of this extension. The statement "the construction follows closely that of convolutional kernel networks [15] but generalizes it" is insufficient justification for a theoretical contribution.

2. The proof of Lemma 1 relies on a strong assumption that "Hk contains linear functions ⟨w, ·⟩ with w in Pk" without justification. This assumption is critical to the signal preservation property but isn't properly motivated or explained.

3. In Section 5, the authors acknowledge that ReLU (the most successful activation function) is homogeneous but not smooth, and they mention a "smoothed variant" that satisfies their conditions. However, they provide no details about this variant or how it would be implemented, creating a significant gap in their theoretical claims.

4. The paper references "the extended version of the paper [3]" for details about norm preservation with Gaussian kernel mappings but doesn't provide a citation or link to this extended version, making verification difficult.

### Clarity and Presentation
1. The notation is confusing in multiple places. The paper uses "|| · ||" for Hilbert space norms, L2 norms, and L2 → L2 operator norms without clear distinction, which could mislead readers.

2. In the definition of the patch extraction operator (Section 2), the notation Pkxk-1(u) = (v ↦ xk-1(u+v))v∈Sk is ambiguous. It would be clearer to write Pkxk-1(u) = xk-1(u + ·)|Sk.

3. The paper claims "the norm of Φ(x) can decrease with depth due to pooling layers" but doesn't adequately explain why this is significant for practical applications or how it differs from the scattering transform's behavior.

4. In Section 3.2, the authors state that "the stability properties we have obtained previously are compatible with these steps" (referring to kernel approximations) but provide no proof or reference for this claim, which is a significant omission.

### Related Work and Novelty
1. The paper cites "Group invariance and stability to deformations of deep convolutional representations, A. Bietti, J. Mairal, preprint arXiv:1706.03078" as related work, but this appears to be the authors' own work (Bietti and Mairal) that they are submitting. This is a serious citation error that should be corrected.

2. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work on stability of CNNs, particularly from the scattering transform literature they cite.

### Experimental Support
The paper makes significant theoretical claims about how stability leads to improved sample complexity, but provides no empirical evidence to support this claim. Theoretical assertions should be either supported by theoretical results or empirical evidence.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical framework that connects kernel methods to CNNs, it contains multiple minor but significant flaws that undermine its technical rigor. The formatting errors, unclear notation, unjustified assumptions, and lack of detail on key claims (particularly regarding ReLU activation and the "smoothed variant") collectively suggest insufficient attention to detail. The paper also fails to properly distinguish its contributions from related work, including its own previous work.

These issues, while individually minor, collectively indicate a lack of thoroughness in the preparation of the manuscript. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these flaws substantial enough to warrant rejection, as they detract from the paper's scientific contribution despite its potentially valuable theoretical insights.

The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field, but as submitted, it does not meet the technical standards expected at NIPS. I recommend rejection with the hope that the authors will address these concerns in a revised version with more careful attention to detail.
