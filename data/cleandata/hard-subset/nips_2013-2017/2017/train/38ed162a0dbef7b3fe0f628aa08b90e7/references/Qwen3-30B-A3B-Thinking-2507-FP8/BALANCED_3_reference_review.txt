

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of convolutional neural network representations using a kernel-based framework. The authors propose a multilayer convolutional kernel construction that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and enables theoretical analysis of invariance and stability properties. The work builds on Mallat's scattering transform [17] and CKNs [15,16], extending their stability analysis to more standard CNN architectures with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The main contribution is a rigorous framework showing that the kernel representation provides stability guarantees to diffeomorphisms (smooth deformations), with the stability properties translating to predictive models through RKHS norms.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous mathematical analysis of stability properties under diffeomorphisms, with careful proofs of key theorems. The authors clearly establish how their framework extends the scattering transform's stability guarantees to more standard CNN architectures.

2. **Novel Unifying Framework**: The paper successfully bridges the gap between kernel methods and CNNs, showing that the RKHS defined by their kernel construction contains both CKNs and standard CNNs with homogeneous activation functions. This provides a unified theoretical perspective on these architectures.

3. **Clear Connection to Generalization**: The authors effectively demonstrate how stability (via RKHS norm control) relates to generalization performance, providing a principled theoretical basis for why CNNs generalize well. This connection between stability and generalization is particularly valuable.

4. **Thorough Related Work**: The paper carefully situates its contributions relative to previous work on scattering transforms [17], CKNs [15,16], and group invariance [6,9,22], making it clear how this work advances the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Practical Impact**: While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper lacks concrete experimental validation demonstrating how these insights could improve practical CNN architectures. The connection to recent CNN architectures (e.g., ResNets, attention mechanisms) is not explored.

2. **Accessibility**: The paper is highly technical, relying on advanced functional analysis and harmonic analysis concepts. This may limit its accessibility to the broader NIPS audience, especially those without strong mathematical backgrounds. A more intuitive explanation of key concepts would help.

3. **Handling of Non-Homogeneous Activations**: The paper focuses on homogeneous activation functions, which excludes ReLU (the most popular activation in CNNs). While the authors acknowledge this limitation and discuss smoothed variants, the connection to standard ReLU-based CNNs remains somewhat tenuous.

4. **Minimal Experimental Validation**: The paper is almost entirely theoretical, with no experiments demonstrating the practical utility of the framework. This is a significant gap given NIPS's emphasis on both theoretical and empirical contributions.

5. **Discretization Discussion**: While the paper addresses discretization in Section 2.1, the connection between the continuous theoretical framework and practical discrete implementations is not sufficiently developed to provide clear guidance for practitioners.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding the invariance and stability properties of CNN representations. The mathematical framework is sound, the connections to previous work are well-established, and the relationship between stability and generalization provides important theoretical insights. However, the lack of experimental validation and limited discussion of practical implementation make it difficult to assess the paper's broader impact on the field.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection** for this paper. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper's limited practical relevance and lack of experimental validation make it less compelling for NIPS, which values both theoretical rigor and empirical significance. The paper would benefit significantly from:

1. Adding experimental results demonstrating how the theoretical insights could guide the design of more stable CNN architectures.
2. Providing a more detailed discussion of how the framework applies to standard ReLU-based networks (not just homogeneous activations).
3. Including a clearer connection to recent advances in CNN architecture design.
4. Adding a more accessible introduction to the key mathematical concepts for readers without strong functional analysis backgrounds.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these gaps, particularly by demonstrating concrete applications of the theoretical framework to practical CNN design. With these improvements, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the NIPS community.
