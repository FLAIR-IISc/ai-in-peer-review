## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors construct a multilayer convolutional kernel, generalizing convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), and demonstrate that this framework contains a large class of CNNs. They prove stability results, akin to those established for scattering transforms, regarding invariance to diffeomorphisms and robustness to perturbations. Crucially, they connect this stability to potential improvements in sample complexity, arguing that stable representations lead to better generalization.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon a rich history of research attempting to understand the theoretical underpinnings of CNNs. It explicitly draws inspiration from the scattering transform ([17] & related works by Mallat), providing a kernel-based generalization applicable to a broader class of architectures. It also acknowledges connections to CKNs ([15, 16]) and recent kernel methods for deep learning ([2, 10, 18, 36]). The paper positions itself as an extension of existing work on group invariance ([6, 9, 22]) and Lipschitz stability ([8]) to a more general and theoretically grounded setting. 

**Strengths:**

* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical analysis, grounded in kernel theory and harmonic analysis. The proofs appear careful and well-structured (though full verification requires deeper dive, the organization and clear lemma statements inspire confidence).
* **Generalization of Existing Results:** Extending the stability properties of scattering transforms to a broader class of CNNs is a significant contribution. This bridges the gap between the theoretically well-understood scattering transforms and the more commonly used, but less understood, deep learning architectures.
* **Connection to Generalization:** The linking of stability to sample complexity is a compelling argument and offers potential insights into why stable representations are desirable. The RKHS norm as a regulator is a valuable observation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the research problem, and the notation is consistent throughout.
* **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of a section on discretization and approximation for practical implementation (CKNs) is important and demonstrates an awareness of the challenges of translating theoretical results into real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is heavily theoretical and lacks any empirical validation of the proposed framework. While the authors acknowledge the difficulty of directly verifying the theoretical results, some experiments, even on simple datasets, demonstrating the stability properties or improved sample complexity would significantly strengthen the work.
* **Assumptions and Constraints:** The results rely on several assumptions (e.g., smoothness of activations, regularity of diffeomorphisms, the norm preservation properties of kernels). The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not fully explored. Specifically, the requirement of smooth, homogeneous activations is a strong constraint.
* **Complexity of the Kernel Construction:** While the kernel construction is theoretically elegant, it might be difficult to implement and scale to large datasets. The computational cost associated with these kernels needs further investigation.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** While the paper states that the stability properties may lead to improved sample complexity, it doesn’t delve deeply into how this could be leveraged in practice. What specific architectural choices or training procedures could be informed by these theoretical results?
* **Potential Redundancy:** The discussion of group invariance feels somewhat separate from the core stability arguments. While interesting, it feels less central to the main contribution and could be more concisely presented.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and mathematically rigorous analysis of CNNs.
* It bridges a gap between scattering transforms and more general deep learning architectures.
* It offers potential theoretical insights into the link between stability and generalization.
* The work is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation weakens the impact of the theoretical results.
* The strong assumptions and computational complexity of the framework raise concerns about its practical applicability.
* The connection to practical implications could be more thoroughly explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper with the potential to contribute significantly to our understanding of CNNs. However, the lack of empirical validation and the stringent assumptions are significant weaknesses. I believe the paper is a worthwhile contribution, but its impact would be greatly enhanced by even limited experimental results. 

**Rating:**  **Weak Accept**. I recommend acceptance with the strong encouragement that the authors include some empirical validation in a future version. Specifically, demonstrating the stability properties on a simple dataset or exploring the link between RKHS norm and generalization performance would be valuable.

**Further Questions/Suggestions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide any discussion of the computational cost of the proposed kernel and potential approximations to make it more practical?
* Can you comment on the sensitivity of the results to the assumption of smooth, homogeneous activations? What happens when using common non-smooth activations like ReLU?
* Are there any concrete guidelines or architectural choices that practitioners could adopt to exploit the stability properties derived in this work?
* Have you considered the relationship between your stability results and adversarial robustness? Could the stable representations offer inherent protection against adversarial attacks?
