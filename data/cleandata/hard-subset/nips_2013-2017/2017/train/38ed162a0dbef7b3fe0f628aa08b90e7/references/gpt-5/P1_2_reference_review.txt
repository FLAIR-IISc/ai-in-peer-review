Summary and contributions
The paper develops a rigorous kernel-based framework to analyze invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. It defines a multilayer convolutional kernel for signals on Rd by composing three operators per layer: patch extraction Pk, a pointwise RKHS mapping Mk induced by a homogeneous dot-product kernel, and Gaussian pooling Ak. The resulting representation Φn(x) = AnMnPn ··· A1M1P1x induces a positive definite kernel whose RKHS contains linear predictors over the final feature map.

Main theoretical results:
- Signal preservation: under mild assumptions on the kernels (linear functionals are in the RKHS), the previous layer can be recovered from a sampled version of the current layer; a discrete construction with subsampling still allows layer-wise recovery when patch sizes exceed stride.
- Stability to diffeomorphisms and translation invariance: for C1-diffeomorphisms τ with ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2, the representation satisfies ‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖. The proof decomposes the variation into commutators of patch extraction/pooling with Lτ, bounding them via Schur’s test and a known bound on ‖LτAσ − Aσ‖. Translation invariance increases with the final pooling scale σn.
- Group equivariance/invariance: by defining patch extraction and pooling over a locally compact group with Haar measure, the layer is G-equivariant, enabling stability at intermediate layers (e.g., a subgroup) and global invariance via the last pooling over G.
- Link to CNNs: the RKHS associated with the multilayer kernel contains a class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations; a recursive bound upper-bounds the RKHS norm of such CNNs (Proposition 7). This connects norm control in the RKHS to Lipschitz stability of predictions and generalization via margin-style arguments.
- Practical aspects: discretization is treated explicitly, relating continuous patch/pooling scales to discrete patch sizes and strides; finite-dimensional approximations via projections (CKNs) preserve non-expansiveness and hence stability bounds.

Relation to prior work
The analysis extends Mallat’s scattering framework beyond wavelet-based fixed filters to learned, kernel-induced multilayer maps, retaining similar Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms and group-invariance constructions. It builds on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) by providing formal stability guarantees and a principled link to CNNs. Connections to group-equivariant CNNs, invariant kernels, and kernel analyses of deep nets are thoughtfully discussed; the work also relates stability to adversarial robustness via Lipschitz control.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: a clean RKHS framework that simultaneously captures CKNs and a broad class of CNNs, with precise assumptions (A1–A3).
- Rigorous stability analysis: transparent decomposition through operator commutators; explicit dependence on deformation magnitude, depth, and pooling scale.
- Group-theoretic generality: a neat, modular route to G-equivariance and global invariance with Haar-measure pooling.
- Practical relevance: discretization and finite-dimensional approximation are addressed; non-expansive projections preserve bounds.
- Insightful link between stability and generalization via RKHS norms, offering a theoretical lens on why more invariant/stable features can reduce sample complexity.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Restricted activation class: results require smooth homogeneous activations; ReLU is only covered via smoothed variants and specific kernels (e.g., inverse polynomial). This limits direct applicability to common CNNs with nonsmooth activations, biases, and batch normalization.
- Pooling model: Gaussian linear pooling is assumed; max-pooling or other nonlinear/learned pooling is not analyzed. The role of strides/aliasing is only partially addressed via σk and ek ≥ sk conditions.
- Potential looseness: constants in the stability bounds (growth with κd+1, linear in depth n) may be conservative; no empirical exploration of tightness.
- Continuous-domain emphasis: while discretization is discussed, boundary conditions and finite-support effects are deferred; practical implications for modern architectures (padding, dilations, residual connections) are not covered.
- Experiments: the paper is primarily theoretical; no empirical validation of the theoretical predictions (e.g., comparing stability versus σn or depth) is included in the main text.

Quality
The work is technically solid. Proofs are outlined with appropriate references (Mallat; Schur’s test) and detailed in appendices. The assumptions are explicit and reasonable for the class considered. The link to CNNs (Proposition 7) is carefully constructed, including a clear accounting of the nonstandard normalization factors nk(u).

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear and well organized. The operator viewpoint (Pk, Mk, Ak) and the commutator-based analysis are easy to follow. Suggestions:
- Provide a brief intuition for Lemma 1’s sampling condition and a concrete sampling scheme.
- Clarify the stability and conditioning of the deconvolution step in Lemma 2 and how σk relates to stride to avoid aliasing.
- Summarize in the main text the class of homogeneous kernels satisfying (A1–A2) and the key properties of Cσ used in Proposition 7.
- Discuss, even briefly, how biases or batch norm would affect the inclusion in the RKHS.

Originality
The combination of a multilayer convolutional kernel with a rigorous diffeomorphism-stability analysis beyond scattering is novel. The explicit embedding of a class of CNNs into the RKHS with norm bounds, tying stability and generalization, is original and timely. The group-equivariant pooling construction is consistent with prior art but presented in a unified kernel framework.

Significance
The results advance theoretical understanding of deep convolutional representations, offering design guidance (e.g., the role of final pooling scale on translation invariance) and a principled way to control stability via RKHS norms. While not directly improving benchmarks, the framework is likely to influence future analyses and methods, particularly for kernelized or hybrid architectures.

Questions and clarifications for the authors
- How sensitive are constants C1 and C2 to the choice of h and κ, and how do they scale in practice with depth n and dimension d?
- Can the framework accommodate max pooling or learned pooling filters while preserving stability bounds?
- Does Proposition 7 extend to include biases and residual connections, and how would nk(u) be handled in those cases?
- For discretization, can you provide guidance on choosing σk relative to stride to ensure stable deconvolution and minimal information loss?

Pros
- Clear, general, and rigorous stability and invariance analysis.
- Unified treatment of CKNs and a class of CNNs; insightful norm–stability–generalization link.
- Group-equivariant construction applicable beyond translations.
- Practical considerations for discretization and kernel approximation.

Cons
- Activation and pooling restrictions; limited coverage of standard ReLU/max-pooling CNNs.
- Potentially loose bounds; limited discussion of constants.
- No empirical validation in the main text.

Recommendation
Accept. This is a strong theoretical contribution that extends and unifies important strands of work on invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations, with clear relevance to the NeurIPS community. Minor clarifications as noted would further improve readability and applicability.
