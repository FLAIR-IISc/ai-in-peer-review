

# Peer Review: "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

## Summary

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations from the perspective of positive definite kernels. The authors propose a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and provides a unified theoretical foundation for understanding invariance and stability properties of convolutional architectures. The work builds upon Mallat's scattering transform theory [17] but extends it to encompass standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The paper makes three main contributions: (1) it develops a kernel framework for analyzing translation-invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms, (2) it establishes connections between this framework and standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, and (3) it demonstrates how RKHS norms control both stability and generalization, providing theoretical justification for regularization.

The paper relates to previous NIPS work through its connection to the scattering transform [5, 17] and convolutional kernel networks [15, 16]. It also builds upon related work on group invariance in CNNs [6, 9, 22] and kernel methods for deep learning [2, 4, 7, 10, 18]. Unlike previous approaches, the authors provide a unified theoretical framework that applies to both CKNs and standard CNNs with smooth activations.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Rigor**: The paper presents a mathematically rigorous analysis with well-constructed proofs (though relegated to the appendix). The authors carefully establish bounds on stability under diffeomorphisms and connect these to RKHS norms, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The analysis of how pooling layers affect stability and invariance is particularly elegant.

2. **Novel Connection to Standard CNNs**: The paper successfully bridges the gap between the scattering transform framework and standard CNNs by showing that CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations are contained within the RKHS defined by their kernel construction. This is a major theoretical advance, as it extends the stability results to a broader class of models than previous work.

3. **Clear Theoretical Framework**: The paper establishes a clear connection between stability, generalization, and RKHS norms. The authors demonstrate that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization, providing a theoretical basis for regularization in CNNs. This insight has the potential to guide the development of better regularization techniques.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The paper includes a thorough discussion of discretization and kernel approximation, showing how the theoretical framework can be implemented in practice as convolutional kernel networks. The authors also address the relationship between their framework and practical CNN architectures.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Empirical Validation**: The paper is purely theoretical and lacks experimental validation of the proposed framework. While the theoretical results are sound, it would be valuable to see how these stability properties manifest in practical CNN training and whether the RKHS norm can predict generalization performance on standard datasets.

2. **Limited Connection to Modern CNN Architectures**: The paper focuses on CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, but most state-of-the-art CNNs use ReLU activations, which are not homogeneous in the required sense (though the authors note a smoothed variant). The connection to modern architectures like ResNet or EfficientNet is not established, which limits the practical relevance of the results.

3. **Complexity of Mathematical Framework**: The paper assumes a strong background in functional analysis and kernel methods. While the mathematical framework is elegant, it may be inaccessible to many machine learning researchers. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of key concepts like the RKHS framework and its relationship to CNNs.

4. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implementation**: While the paper discusses discretization and kernel approximation, it doesn't provide guidance on how to practically implement the stability-based regularization suggested by the theory. For instance, how would one measure or control the RKHS norm in a standard CNN?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the invariance and stability properties of convolutional representations. The rigorous analysis extends previous work on the scattering transform to encompass standard CNNs, providing a unified theoretical framework that is both elegant and insightful. The connection between RKHS norms and generalization is particularly valuable for understanding why certain architectures generalize better than others.

However, the paper's purely theoretical nature is its main limitation. While the theoretical results are sound and important, the lack of empirical validation and limited connection to modern CNN architectures reduces the paper's immediate practical impact. The theoretical insights could be more impactful with experimental validation showing how these stability properties translate to improved performance in practice.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed, and it addresses a fundamental question about why convolutional architectures work well. While the lack of empirical validation is a limitation, the paper's strength lies in its theoretical contribution, which is precisely what NIPS values. The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and makes important connections between different strands of research in representation learning.

The authors should consider addressing the following points in a revised version (though they are not necessary for acceptance):

1. Add a brief empirical demonstration showing how the RKHS norm correlates with generalization performance on a simple task.
2. Discuss more explicitly how their framework applies to modern CNN architectures with ReLU activations.
3. Include a more intuitive explanation of the RKHS framework for readers without strong functional analysis backgrounds.

This paper advances our theoretical understanding of deep convolutional representations and provides a valuable framework for future research on stability and generalization in CNNs. It is a strong theoretical contribution that should be of interest to the NIPS community.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Summary

**Pros for Acceptance**:
- Rigorous theoretical contribution that extends previous work on scattering networks
- Establishes important connection between RKHS norms and stability/generalization
- Clear mathematical framework that unifies analysis of CKNs and standard CNNs
- Well-written and well-organized paper

**Cons for Acceptance**:
- Lack of empirical validation limits practical impact
- Limited connection to modern CNN architectures (ReLU-based)
- Mathematical complexity may limit accessibility to broader ML community

Overall, the theoretical contribution outweighs the limitations, and I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field that deserves publication at NIPS.
