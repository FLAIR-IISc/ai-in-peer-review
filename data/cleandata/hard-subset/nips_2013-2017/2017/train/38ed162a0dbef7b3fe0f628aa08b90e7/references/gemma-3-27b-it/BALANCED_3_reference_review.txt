This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of positive definite kernels. The authors aim to characterize the invariance and stability of these representations, building on previous work with scattering transforms and convolutional kernel networks (CKNs). They construct a multilayer convolutional kernel and demonstrate that it contains a large class of CNNs, enabling them to derive theoretical results about both data representation and predictive models. The core idea is to study translation-invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms, linking these properties to generalization capabilities.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles a fundamental question in deep learning: understanding *why* CNNs work so well. Connecting CNNs to kernel methods is a promising avenue, offering tools from harmonic analysis and functional analysis to rigorously analyze their behavior. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, citing relevant work on scattering transforms, CKNs, and kernel methods. The extension of CKNs to multi-dimensional signals and the development of a theoretical framework for stability and invariance are significant contributions.  The detailed breakdown of the construction, lemmas, and theorems demonstrate a strong technical understanding.  The link to generalization via RKHS norm control is also a valuable insight.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper rigorously grounds its analysis in kernel theory and harmonic analysis.
* **Novel Extension of CKNs:** Extending CKNs to multi-dimensional signals and relating them to broader CNN architectures is a valuable contribution.
* **Clear Connection to Generalization:**  The paper establishes a connection between stability, RKHS norm, and generalization performance.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of the relevant literature.
* **Detailed Mathematical Analysis:** The derivations and proofs are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:**  The paper is heavily theoretical and lacks empirical evaluation. While the authors discuss potential implications for generalization, there are no experiments to support these claims. Demonstrating the practical relevance of the theoretical results through simulations or experiments on standard datasets would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Complexity and Accessibility:** The mathematical notation and derivations are dense and may be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with kernel methods and harmonic analysis. While detail is important, some streamlining or more intuitive explanations could improve accessibility.
* **Assumptions and Limitations:** Several assumptions are made (e.g., smoothness of activations, regularity of diffeomorphisms). A more thorough discussion of the impact of these assumptions on the results would be beneficial. The practical implications of these assumptions for real-world CNN architectures should also be addressed.
* **Incremental Nature:** While technically sound, the work feels somewhat incremental. The extension of existing techniques to a more general setting is valuable, but the overall impact might be greater if the authors identified and addressed a more significant open problem.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The construction and analysis involve potentially expensive kernel computations. A discussion of the computational complexity and potential approximation techniques would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of CNNs using a powerful kernel-based framework.
* The results contribute to a deeper understanding of the invariance and stability properties of deep representations.
* The connection to generalization performance is a significant insight.
* The paper is technically sound and well-written.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the theoretical results.
* The complexity of the analysis may hinder broader adoption.
* The incremental nature of the work may not justify acceptance at a highly competitive conference like NeurIPS.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the theoretical contribution is valuable and the technical quality is high, the lack of empirical validation is a major drawback. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including experimental results that demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical findings. Furthermore, streamlining the presentation and providing more intuitive explanations would improve accessibility. The authors should consider addressing the limitations of their assumptions and discussing the computational implications of their approach.  

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Include Empirical Validation:** Conduct experiments on standard datasets to demonstrate the theoretical results (e.g., showing improved robustness to perturbations or better generalization performance for models with low RKHS norms).
* **Improve Accessibility:** Simplify the mathematical notation and provide more intuitive explanations of key concepts.
* **Address Assumptions:**  Discuss the impact of the assumptions made in the analysis and their relevance to real-world CNN architectures.
* **Discuss Computational Complexity:** Analyze the computational cost of the proposed framework and explore potential approximation techniques.
* **Consider a Broader Impact:**  Explore potential applications of the theoretical results beyond standard image classification tasks.
