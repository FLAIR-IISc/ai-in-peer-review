

# Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations by framing them within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) framework. The authors generalize the convolutional kernel network (CKN) approach [15,16] to develop a multilayer kernel construction that provides a principled analysis of invariance and stability properties for deep convolutional architectures. The work builds on Mallat's scattering transform [17] but extends it to more standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by introducing a kernel-based formalism that preserves signal information while achieving near-invariance to groups of transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms.

The paper's main technical contribution is a rigorous analysis demonstrating that the proposed kernel representation satisfies stability bounds under diffeomorphic deformations, with the bound decaying as the pooling scale increases (Theorem 6). The authors further establish connections between this kernel framework and standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, showing that a large class of CNNs reside within the RKHS defined by their kernel construction. This theoretical bridge between kernel methods and CNNs is particularly valuable as it provides a unified framework for analyzing stability and generalization.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with thorough theoretical development. The authors carefully establish the conditions under which their stability results hold (Assumptions A1-A3) and provide detailed proofs in the appendix. The connection between the kernel framework and CNNs (Section 5) is particularly strong, demonstrating that the stability properties apply to a broad class of practical CNN architectures. The analysis of signal preservation (Lemma 1) and discretization (Lemma 2) addresses important practical concerns for implementing the theoretical framework. The paper successfully addresses the technical depth required for NIPS.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with careful notation and logical flow. The authors effectively explain complex concepts (e.g., the relationship between RKHS norms and stability) through well-chosen examples. The figures (not shown here) would likely aid understanding of the multilayer construction. However, the paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of how the theoretical framework translates to practical implementation challenges in deep learning systems.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the scattering transform framework [17] to a broader class of convolutional architectures through the kernel perspective. While the CKN paper [15,16] introduced a similar multilayer kernel construction, this work provides a deeper theoretical analysis of invariance and stability properties for a wider class of networks. The authors clarify the novelty by showing how their framework encompasses CKNs while also including CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, which was not previously established. The extension to group invariance (Section 4) further differentiates this work from prior approaches.

**Significance**: This work has substantial significance for both theory and practice. By establishing a connection between RKHS norms and stability/generalization, the authors provide a principled theoretical foundation for understanding why certain CNN architectures generalize well. The stability results suggest that controlling the RKHS norm of predictive functions could lead to improved sample complexity. This framework also opens new avenues for leveraging kernel methods to design more stable and interpretable deep learning models. The paper addresses a fundamental question about the geometry of deep representations that has been largely unexplored in the literature.

**Strengths**:
1. Provides the first comprehensive theoretical analysis of stability and invariance for a broad class of convolutional architectures
2. Establishes a clear connection between kernel methods and CNNs that enables transfer of theoretical insights
3. Demonstrates how the framework can be applied to practical CNNs with smooth activations
4. Offers a principled approach to understanding the relationship between stability and generalization
5. Carefully addresses discretization and implementation concerns

**Weaknesses**:
1. The paper could better articulate why the RKHS formulation is useful beyond theoretical interest. While the authors mention that RKHS norms control stability and generalization, they don't elaborate on how this might lead to practical improvements in training or model design.
2. The significance of "preserving signal information" is not sufficiently explained in the context of classification tasks (where only task-relevant information matters). The authors should clarify why preserving all signal information is beneficial for classification rather than just the discriminative features.
3. The connection to invariant kernel approaches (e.g., Burkhardt [13]) is not explicitly discussed, though the authors cite this work. A brief comparison would strengthen the novelty argument.
4. The paper would benefit from a more concrete example demonstrating how the theoretical framework could be applied to improve practical CNN design.

**Recommendation**: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of deep convolutional representations. The work is well-executed, rigorous, and addresses a fundamental question in deep learning theory. While the practical implications could be more clearly articulated, the theoretical insights are valuable and novel enough to warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points above.

The paper's main contribution is not merely a reformulation of CNNs in RKHS terms but rather a demonstration that a broad class of CNNs reside within a functional space with provable stability properties. This bridges a significant gap between kernel methods and deep learning theory, providing a foundation for future work on stable and generalizable deep architectures.
