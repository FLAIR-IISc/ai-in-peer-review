This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations through the lens of multilayer kernels and their associated Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors generalize the construction of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) to develop a framework for signals on continuous domains. The core of this framework is a multilayer architecture composed of patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators.

The main contributions are theoretical. First, the paper establishes that this representation is stable with respect to the action of small diffeomorphisms and near-invariant to translations. These stability guarantees are formally characterized in a manner similar to the analysis of scattering transforms, showing that the representation's variation is bounded by the magnitude of the deformation. Second, the authors demonstrate how to construct representations that are equivariant, and ultimately invariant, to the action of more general transformation groups (e.g., rotations). Finally, and significantly, the paper establishes a formal link between this kernel framework and standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). It shows that the constructed RKHS contains a large class of CNNs that use smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This connection allows the stability and invariance properties derived for the kernel representation to be directly applied to these CNNs, linking the network's RKHS norm to both its stability and its generalization properties.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor and Quality:** The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous mathematical analysis connecting the empirically successful architecture of CNNs to the well-established theoretical framework of kernel methods. The proofs and derivations build elegantly upon foundational work in signal processing and machine learning.

2.  **Significance of Contribution:** Understanding the fundamental properties of deep networks, such as invariance to transformations and stability to deformations, is a critical open problem. This work makes a valuable contribution by providing a unified framework to study these properties for both CKNs and a relevant class of CNNs. The link established between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization is particularly insightful.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The authors clearly define their multilayer kernel construction and systematically derive the stability and invariance results. The connection to practical CNN models in Section 5 is well-motivated and clearly explained.

4.  **Originality:** While the work builds upon the CKN framework [15] and the stability analysis of scattering transforms [17], it represents a novel and meaningful synthesis. Extending the stability analysis from the predefined filters of scattering networks to a framework that encompasses learned representations like CNNs is a significant step forward.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

The paper is strong, but its impact could be further enhanced by clarifying the following points.

1.  **Novelty and Practical Implications:** The main contribution is a sophisticated theoretical reformulation of convolutional architectures within an RKHS context. To better highlight the significance of this achievement, the paper could elaborate more on its potential practical implications. For example:
    *   Could this kernel perspective lead to new, principled regularization strategies based on controlling the RKHS norm of the network?
    *   Does the framework suggest novel algorithms for training or designing networks with guaranteed stability properties?
    *   Could it be used to derive tighter or more meaningful generalization bounds for CNNs?
    A more in-depth discussion on these potential future benefits would strengthen the paper's claims of significance. Furthermore, while the paper builds on CKNs [15, 16], a more explicit statement distinguishing the novel contributions of this work (i.e., the stability/invariance analysis and the link to general CNNs) from the prior work on the CKN architecture would be helpful.

2.  **On the Importance of Signal Preservation:** The authors highlight that their representation preserves signal information (Lemmas 1 and 2), allowing the original signal to be reconstructed. Could the authors comment on why this is a universally desirable property? For many discriminative tasks, such as image classification, the objective is to discard nuisance variability and extract a low-dimensional, task-relevant manifold. In such cases, preserving all signal information might not be necessary or even optimal. A brief discussion on the potential trade-off between information preservation and discriminative power would be welcome.

3.  **Connection to Invariant Kernels:** Section 4 discusses achieving invariance to group actions by using equivariant operators. This is closely related to the rich literature on invariant kernels. For completeness, it would be valuable to discuss the connection to foundational work in this area, such as that of Haasdonk and Burkhardt [13], and to clarify how the proposed multilayer, convolutional approach relates to or extends these classic single-kernel methods.

4.  **Generalizing Group Equivariance (Minor Point):** On a related note, the construction in Section 4 uses specific patch, kernel, and pooling operators that commute with the group action to achieve equivariance. Is this a general principle? That is, would a multilayer architecture built from *any* sequence of operators that are individually equivariant to the group action also result in an overall equivariant representation?

### **Summary of Recommendation**

This is a high-quality, rigorous, and well-written paper that provides significant theoretical insights into the stability and invariance properties of deep convolutional networks. The connection it forges between CNNs and RKHS theory is elegant and important. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to suggestions for improving the paper's positioning and clarifying the broader implications of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides a rigorous and novel theoretical framework for analyzing CNNs.
*   Successfully derives stability and invariance guarantees analogous to those for scattering transforms, but for a broader class of models.
*   Establishes an important and explicit link between CNNs, multilayer kernels, and RKHS theory.
*   The paper is clear, technically sound, and addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The immediate practical applications of the theoretical framework could be articulated more clearly.
*   The novelty with respect to prior work on CKNs and scattering transforms could be stated more sharply.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
