- Paper is well written, rigorous, and shows DCNN function spaces can be characterized via multilayer kernels with (approximate) group invariance and stability to diffeomorphisms; reviewer recommends acceptance.

- Main contribution appears to be an RKHS reformulation of DCNNs; authors should elaborate on why this reformulation is useful.

- Can the kernel viewpoint yield new or sharper generalization bounds?

- Does the RKHS perspective enable more efficient computation via kernel techniques or approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features) during training or inference?

- Does the novelty go beyond a mere reformulation — i.e., does it lead to new algorithms, analyses, or architectural insights inaccessible from the standard DCNN view?

- Clarify novelty relative to “Convolutional Kernel Networks” (Mairal et al.): what are the key conceptual/technical differences and which results are new?

- The paper emphasizes “not losing the signal information”; clarify why preserving the full signal is crucial versus preserving only task-relevant/discriminative components.

- Relate the group invariance construction to invariant kernels (e.g., work by Hans Burkhardt and collaborators): assumptions, groups handled, and computational implications.

- On equivariance: is commuting with all group elements sufficient for layerwise operators to guarantee global equivariance, or are additional conditions required (continuity, boundedness, locality, compatibility with multilayer kernel structure)?

- Addressing the above practical and theoretical benefits of the RKHS reformulation and the precise novelty relative to prior kernel-based CNN work would improve clarity and impact.
