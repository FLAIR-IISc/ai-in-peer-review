Summary
The paper analyzes deep convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and establishes two main properties: (i) near-invariance to transformations from certain groups and (ii) stability to diffeomorphisms, provided the patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators are appropriately designed. The authors further argue that, under suitable assumptions, analogous properties extend to standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs).

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: The framework formalizes invariance and stability properties for CKNs and offers conditions under which these properties hold.
- Conceptual bridge to CNNs: The discussion connecting CKN behavior to CNNs is valuable and may inform architectural choices.
- Technical soundness: The mathematical development appears rigorous and well structured.

Weaknesses
- Lack of empirical validation: The paper provides no experiments to corroborate the theoretical claims. This weakens the practical relevance and makes it difficult to assess how tight or meaningful the bounds are in realistic settings.
- Clarity on scope: “Near-invariance” and “stability” are not always quantified in an easily interpretable way, and the precise classes of transformations and diffeomorphisms covered could be more clearly delineated.
- Kernel approximation discussion is limited: It is unclear whether random Fourier features or other finite-dimensional approximations preserve the stated stability guarantees and, if so, how the constants degrade.

Main questions for the authors
- Which transformation groups are covered explicitly by your invariance guarantees (e.g., translations, rotations, scalings), and how does the degree of invariance depend on pooling scales and patch sizes?
- How do the stability constants depend on network depth, patch stride, kernel bandwidth, and pooling parameters? Are there cumulative effects across layers that could lead to degradation?
- Under what precise assumptions do the CNN results hold? For example, do they require the learned filters to approximate the kernel feature map up to a certain error, and if so, how does this error propagate through depth?
- Can your theory accommodate common finite-dimensional kernel approximations (e.g., random Fourier features, structured random projections, Nyström)? If yes, how do approximation errors affect the invariance/stability bounds? Are there explicit trade-offs as a function of the number of features?
- What is the computational complexity of the proposed CKN constructions compared to standard CNNs, and how does this affect scalability?

Suggestions for empirical evaluation
- Controlled transformations: Evaluate CKNs with different patch and pooling configurations on datasets subjected to known transformations (e.g., translated, rotated, or scaled versions of images). Report accuracy decay or feature-space distances as a function of transformation magnitude to validate “near-invariance.”
- Diffeomorphic deformations: Use synthetic smooth warps (e.g., elastic deformations) to test stability. Plot representation distance versus deformation amplitude and compare to theoretical predictions.
- Comparisons to CNNs: Implement CNNs designed to mirror the CKN architecture and compare invariance/stability behavior under the same transformations. This would substantiate the claim that the properties transfer to CNNs under your assumptions.
- Kernel approximations: Compare exact kernel mappings (when feasible) to random Fourier features or other explicit approximations. Study how the number of features affects empirical stability and performance, checking whether degradation matches theoretical rates.
- Ablations: Vary patch sizes, strides, kernel bandwidths, and pooling scales to illustrate trade-offs between invariance and sensitivity to fine details.

Relation to prior work
- Please clarify the relationship to scattering networks and their established stability to diffeomorphisms. What are the key differences in assumptions, operators, and resulting bounds? A concise comparison would help position the contribution.

Clarity and presentation
- Define “near-invariance” and “stability” early with precise norms and metrics on both input and feature spaces.
- Summarize the main theorems with intuitive statements and highlight how constants scale with architectural parameters.
- Minor issues: fix typos and spacing (e.g., “i.e. CKNs” and “diffeomorphisms”).

Overall assessment
This is a technically solid and timely theoretical paper that advances our understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations and draws insightful connections between CKNs and CNNs. However, the absence of empirical validation limits its impact, especially given the practical importance of the claims. I encourage the authors to add targeted experiments and to expand the discussion on kernel approximations and their effect on stability. With these additions and improved clarity on assumptions and constants, the paper would make a strong contribution.
