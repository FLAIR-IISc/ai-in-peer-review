This paper presents a compelling and rigorous analysis demonstrating how the functional spaces implemented by Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (DCNNs) can be described through the lens of multilayer kernels. The authors successfully establish that this perspective yields architectural properties such as (approximate) invariance to group transformations and stability under diffeomorphic transformations. Overall, I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the following points:

My primary understanding is that the core contribution of this work lies in reframing the DCNN architecture within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. While technically sound, the paper would benefit significantly from a more explicit discussion of the *utility* of this reformulation. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on potential advantages this perspective offers. For example, could this kernel formulation lead to tighter generalization bounds? Does it offer computational efficiencies through the application of standard kernel methods or approximation techniques? Expanding on these potential benefits will strengthen the motivation and impact of the work.

Furthermore, it's important to clearly delineate the novelty of this approach beyond simply representing DCNNs within an RKHS framework. Is this reformulation more than a change in perspective? Addressing this question directly will solidify the contribution. In particular, a comparison with, and clear distinction from, the work presented in "Convolutional Kernel Networks" by Mairal et al. is crucial. The authors should explicitly discuss how their approach differs and offers advantages over this prior work.

The authors repeatedly emphasize the importance of “not losing signal information.” This assertion warrants further explanation. While preserving all signal information is critical for signal reconstruction tasks, many applications of DCNNs, such as image classification, are concerned only with task-relevant information. A clarification regarding the context in which this preservation of signal information is crucial, and its implications for the broader application of DCNNs, would be valuable.

Regarding the demonstrated group invariance, I wonder if there’s a connection to the invariant kernel approach pioneered by Hans Burkhardt. Exploring this potential relationship could provide additional context and demonstrate a broader understanding of relevant existing work. 

Finally, as a point of curiosity, the paper defines patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action. This raises an interesting question: would *any* operator that commutes with all elements of the group action guarantee the equivariance property of DCNNs with respect to the given group transformations? Investigating this question – even briefly – could offer a deeper insight into the underlying principles governing equivariance in these networks.
