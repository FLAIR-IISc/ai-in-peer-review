

The paper investigates the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations through a kernel-based theoretical framework, extending the mathematical foundations of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15,16] and connecting them to the scattering transform literature [17]. The authors develop a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes CKNs to continuous signal domains, demonstrating how this construction yields representations stable to diffeomorphisms and near-invariant to group transformations. This work bridges theoretical insights from kernel methods with practical convolutional architectures, providing a unified framework to analyze the functional spaces where CNNs operate.

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from theoretical foundations to practical implications. The technical content is rigorous and carefully developed, with appropriate connections to related work including Mallat's scattering networks [17] and CKNs [15,16]. The authors successfully establish that the kernel representation preserves signal information while achieving the desired stability properties, with stability bounds explicitly depending on the pooling scale of the final layer.

Strengths:
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing that many standard CNNs reside within the functional space defined by the authors' kernel construction, thereby extending Mallat's stability results to a broader class of architectures.
- The connection between RKHS norm and generalization performance provides valuable theoretical insight, suggesting that stable representations (with small RKHS norm) may require fewer samples for good generalization.
- The discretization and kernel approximation sections (Sections 2.1-2.2) thoughtfully bridge theory and practice, showing how the abstract framework can be implemented in real-world CNNs.
- The extension to group invariance in Section 4 demonstrates the framework's versatility beyond translation invariance.

Weaknesses:
- The motivation for studying stability is underdeveloped. The authors briefly mention that Lipschitz stability is important for robustness to adversarial examples [8], but this single sentence fails to justify the extensive theoretical analysis. A more thorough discussion of how stability impacts practical model performance (e.g., generalization, robustness to natural deformations) would strengthen the paper's significance.
- Notation conflict: The symbol κ is overloaded in Assumption (A3) [line 193] and Equation (10), where it represents both a constant in the patch size constraint and a kernel function. These should be denoted with distinct symbols.
- The displayed equation between lines 384-385 contains a redundant condition ("and ∀v...") that is identical to the first constraint due to kernel symmetry. This should be removed for clarity.
- Equation (10) should explicitly define the kernel mapping φ as K(z,z') = ⟨φ(z),φ(z')⟩_H(K), rather than omitting φ's definition.
- The inequality on lines 427-428 contains an error: |‖z‖ - ‖z‖|² should be |‖z‖ - ‖z'‖|².

Regarding references, the following require complete publication details:
- [3,6,8,13,14,18,25-27,30,31] are missing page numbers or publication venues
- [9] should cite Daniely et al.'s NIPS 2016 paper (pages 2253-2261)
- [17] should cite Muandet et al.'s Foundations and Trends paper (10(1-2):1-141)
- [19] should cite Raj et al.'s AISTATS 2017 paper (PMLR 54:1225-1235)
- [32] should note its ICML 2017 acceptance status

Originality: The paper offers a novel theoretical framework connecting kernel methods with standard CNN architectures, showing that many CNNs naturally reside within the functional space defined by the authors' kernel construction. This extends beyond previous work on CKNs [15,16] and scattering networks [17] by providing a unified perspective applicable to a broader range of convolutional architectures.

Significance: The results have clear implications for understanding why CNNs generalize well and for designing more robust models. The theoretical connection between stability and generalization provides a principled approach to model design, with potential applications in improving robustness to natural deformations and adversarial examples.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and appropriate mathematical assumptions (A1-A3). The authors carefully address the relationship between theoretical stability guarantees and practical implementations.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with consistent notation (except for the κ conflict). The theoretical development is accessible to readers familiar with kernel methods and convolutional networks.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of deep convolutional representations. While the motivation for stability analysis requires strengthening and notational issues need resolution, the paper makes a valuable addition to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the noted weaknesses.
