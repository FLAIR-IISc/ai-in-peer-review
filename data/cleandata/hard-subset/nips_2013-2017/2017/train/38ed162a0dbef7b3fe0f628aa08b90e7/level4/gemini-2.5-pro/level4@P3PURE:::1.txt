This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), demonstrating that with careful design of the patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators, CKNs can achieve near-invariance to groups of transformations and stability to the action of diffeomorphisms. Furthermore, the authors show that under certain assumptions, these properties can also extend to conventional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound. Its primary weakness, however, is the absence of empirical experiments to validate the theoretical analysis. The conclusions would be significantly strengthened by experimental results that verify the claimed near-invariance to transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms for CKNs constructed with the proposed design principles.

Additionally, a question for the authors is whether alternative kernel approximation methods, such as random projections or explicit kernel mappings with a finite number of Fourier components, could also be suitable. If so, what effect would these methods have on the stability properties analyzed in the paper?
