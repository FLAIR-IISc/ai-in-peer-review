This paper investigates appealing theoretical properties of Deep Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), demonstrating that they exhibit near-invariance to groups of transformations and stability under diffeomorphic deformations when utilizing carefully designed patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators. The authors further establish, under specific assumptions, that these beneficial properties extend to standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and well-written contribution. The primary weakness lies in the lack of empirical validation for the theoretical findings. It would significantly strengthen the work to include experiments demonstrating the claimed invariance of CKNs—configured with appropriate operator choices—to transformations and diffeomorphisms. Showing this experimentally would build confidence in the theoretical results and illustrate their practical impact.

A further avenue for exploration concerns alternative kernel approximation techniques. Specifically, it's worth considering whether random projections or explicit kernel mapping using a finite number of Fourier series terms could also serve as viable solutions. If so, it would be valuable to analyze the implications of these approaches on the stability properties discussed in the paper, and to compare them to the CKN approach. Understanding the trade-offs between different approximation methods would provide a more complete picture of the landscape of stable and invariant kernelized convolutional architectures.
