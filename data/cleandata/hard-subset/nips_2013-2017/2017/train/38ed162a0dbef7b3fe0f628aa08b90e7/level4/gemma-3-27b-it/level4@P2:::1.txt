This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on invariance and stability. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and analyze the geometry of the resulting reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). They demonstrate that this framework achieves near-invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms while preserving signal information. Importantly, they extend these results to a broader class of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with smooth, homogeneous activation functions.

The work builds upon previous research in several areas. It leverages the theory of scattering transforms [5, 17] which provides a strong foundation for understanding invariance and stability, but extends this work to more general CNN architectures and a kernel framework. The authors also connect to prior work on CKNs [15, 16], group invariance [6, 9, 22], and the interplay between kernels and deep learning [2, 7, 10]. The paper carefully positions itself within this existing literature, demonstrating an understanding of relevant prior work.

The strengths of this paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and its potential to provide a deeper understanding of the properties of deep convolutional networks. The authors clearly define their assumptions and carefully derive their results. The connection between the kernel framework, stability, and generalization is a compelling contribution, potentially offering insights into regularization and sample complexity. The extension of the theoretical results from CKNs to a wider class of CNNs is also significant. The presentation is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful notation and a logical flow of ideas.

However, the paper’s main weakness is the complete lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical results are well-developed, there is no experimental evidence to support the claims. Demonstrating the invariance and stability properties on real-world datasets would significantly strengthen the paper. For example, showing that a CKN or CNN trained with the proposed framework exhibits improved robustness to transformations or diffeomorphisms would be highly persuasive.

Another point for consideration is the exploration of alternative kernel approximation methods. The paper focuses on the approach used in CKNs, but briefly mentioning the potential of other techniques, such as random projections or finite Fourier series expansions, and discussing their potential impact on stability could broaden the scope of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous analysis.
*   Novel contribution to understanding the geometry of deep convolutional representations.
*   Extends existing theoretical frameworks (scattering transforms, CKNs) to a broader class of CNNs.
*   Potential to provide insights into generalization and regularization.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation.
*   Limited discussion of alternative kernel approximation methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of deep convolutional networks. However, the absence of empirical results is a significant limitation. While the theoretical analysis is compelling, it remains speculative without experimental support. I would recommend acceptance with the strong encouragement that the authors include experimental validation in a future version of the work. A score of 6/10 reflecting potential but limited by the lack of experiments.
