The paper is well written and rigorous. It convincingly shows that the function spaces implemented by DCNNs can be characterized via multilayer kernels, and that the resulting architecture enjoys desirable properties: (approximate) invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphic transformations. I recommend acceptance, with the following comments and questions:

- To my understanding, the main contribution is a reformulation of DCNN architectures within the RKHS framework. Please elaborate on why this reformulation is or will be useful. For example:
  - Can the kernel viewpoint yield new generalization bounds or sharpen existing ones?
  - Does it enable more efficient computation via standard kernel techniques or approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features), either during training or inference?
  - More generally, does the novelty go beyond a reformulation—does it lead to new algorithms, analyses, or architectural insights that are not accessible in the standard DCNN perspective?
  - Please also clarify the novelty relative to “Convolutional Kernel Networks” (Julien Mairal, Piotr Koniusz, Zaid Harchaoui, and Cordelia Schmid). What are the key conceptual or technical differences, and which results here are new compared to that work?

- The paper emphasizes “not losing the signal information.” Why is preserving the full signal crucial in this context? DCNNs are typically optimized for downstream tasks (e.g., image classification), where preserving only task-relevant information may suffice, unless the goal is reconstruction or invertibility. Please clarify the role of preserving the entire signal versus preserving discriminative components, and how this affects the theory and the practical design of the architecture.

- Regarding group invariance: is there a connection to invariant kernels as developed by Hans Burkhardt and collaborators? If so, please relate your construction to that line of work (assumptions, types of groups handled, computational implications), and clarify whether your multilayer kernel viewpoint recovers or extends those invariant kernel constructions.

- A curiosity about equivariance: you define patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action. Would any operator that commutes with all group elements ensure equivariance of the DCNN with respect to the group transformations, or are additional conditions required (e.g., continuity, boundedness, locality, or compatibility with the multilayer kernel structure)? Clarifying the minimal sufficient conditions for layerwise operators to guarantee global equivariance would strengthen the presentation.

Overall, the theoretical framing is elegant and the results are promising. Addressing the points above—especially the practical and theoretical benefits of the RKHS reformulation, and the precise novelty relative to prior kernel-based CNN formulations—would further improve the paper’s clarity and impact.
