This paper analyzes deep convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and establishes that, with appropriately designed patch extraction, kernel feature mapping, and pooling operators, CKNs are nearly invariant to certain transformation groups and stable under diffeomorphisms. The authors further show that, under suitable assumptions, these properties also extend to conventional convolutional neural networks (CNNs).

Overall, the paper is clearly written and technically sound. The theoretical results are well motivated and appear rigorous. However, the main weakness is the absence of empirical validation. It would significantly strengthen the work to include experiments demonstrating:
- Invariance of CKNs (with the proposed design choices) to the considered transformation groups.
- Stability to diffeomorphisms, with quantitative assessments of how performance degrades with increasing deformation.
- Sensitivity analyses showing how different choices of patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling affect the observed invariance and stability.
- A comparison to standard CNNs configured according to the paper’s assumptions, to support the claimed transfer of properties.

I also have a question regarding kernel approximation. Could random projections or explicit finite-dimensional kernel mappings (e.g., via random Fourier features) serve as practical approximations in this setting? If so:
- How do these approximations affect the stated stability and near-invariance guarantees?
- Are there bounds on the number of features required to preserve the theoretical properties up to a specified tolerance?
- Do the approximations introduce additional sources of instability or bias that interact with the pooling and patch extraction operators?

Addressing these points—especially with empirical evidence—would enhance the paper’s impact and clarify the practical implications of the theory.
