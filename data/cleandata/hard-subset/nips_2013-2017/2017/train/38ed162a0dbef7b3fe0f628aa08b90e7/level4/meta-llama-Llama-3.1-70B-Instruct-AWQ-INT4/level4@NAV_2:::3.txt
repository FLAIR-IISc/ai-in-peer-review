The paper "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations" presents a rigorous study of deep signal representations that are near-invariant to groups of transformations and stable to the action of diffeomorphisms without losing signal information. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel introduced in the context of convolutional kernel networks and study the geometry of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert space. The paper is well-written and provides a thorough analysis of the theoretical properties of deep convolutional architectures from the point of view of positive definite kernels.

The main contribution of the paper is a rewriting of CNN architectures in the context of RKHS, which provides a new perspective on the properties of these models. The authors show that this functional space contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, allowing them to obtain theoretical results for both classes of models. The paper also discusses the connection between the kernel representation and CNNs, showing that the RKHS obtained from the kernel construction contains a set of CNNs on continuous domains with certain types of smooth homogeneous activations.

The paper is related to previous work on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and the study of group-invariance and stability to the action of diffeomorphisms of scattering networks. However, the authors extend these results to more general CNN architectures and to the kernel framework, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the properties of deep convolutional representations.

The authors stress the importance of "not losing the signal information" in their construction, which is a key aspect of their approach. This is because the goal of the paper is to study the theoretical properties of deep convolutional architectures, rather than to propose a new method for image classification or other tasks. The authors aim to provide a deeper understanding of how these models work, which can be useful for a variety of applications.

Regarding group invariance, the authors define patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action, which ensures the equivariance property of DCNNs w.r.t. the group transformations. This is a key aspect of their approach, as it allows them to study the properties of deep convolutional representations in a more general setting.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with the topic. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the theoretical properties of deep convolutional architectures, which is a significant contribution to the field.
