The paper "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations" presents a theoretical framework for understanding the properties of deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs) in terms of multilayer kernels. The authors demonstrate that the functional space implemented by DCNNs can be described using a kernel formulation, which provides a rigorous and well-written treatment of the subject.

The main contribution of the paper is the rewriting of the DCNN architecture in the context of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). While this reformulation is elegant and provides new insights into the properties of DCNNs, it would be beneficial to include comments in the text on why this reformulation is useful. For instance, the authors could discuss how the kernel formulation may lead to interesting results on generalization bounds or whether it is computationally more efficient to implement using standard techniques/approximation on kernels.

The novelty of the approach seems to go beyond a simple reformulation of DCNNs in the context of RKHS, as it provides new insights into the properties of DCNNs, such as invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphic transformations. However, it is not entirely clear how this work differs from the paper "Convolutional Kernel Networks" by Julien Mairal et al. The authors should clarify the novelty of their approach with respect to this previous work.

The authors emphasize the importance of "not losing the signal information" in their construction. However, it is not entirely clear why this is crucial. DCNNs are often used in relation to specific tasks, such as image classification, where only task-relevant information needs to be preserved. The authors should comment on this point and provide more context on why preserving signal information is essential.

Regarding group invariance, it would be interesting to explore connections with the invariant kernel approach adopted by Hans Burkhardt. The authors should discuss whether their approach is related to or builds upon this previous work.

The construction of patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action is elegant. However, it is not clear whether any other operator that commutes with all group elements would ensure the equivariance property of DCNNs with respect to group transformations. The authors may want to discuss this point further.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a rigorous treatment of the subject. With some additional comments and clarifications, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, pending the authors' response to these comments.
