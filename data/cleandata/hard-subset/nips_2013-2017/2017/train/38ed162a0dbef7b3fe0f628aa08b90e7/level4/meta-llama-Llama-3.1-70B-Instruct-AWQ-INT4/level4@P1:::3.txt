This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations, focusing on their invariance and stability properties. The authors study a functional space derived from a kernel for multi-dimensional signals, which generalizes the construction of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs). They show that this functional space contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, allowing them to obtain theoretical results for both classes of models.

The main contribution of the paper is the rewriting of CNNs architecture in the context of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors demonstrate that the kernel representation described in the paper creates invariance to translations by commuting with the action of translations at intermediate layers, and how the last pooling layer on the translation group governs the final level of invariance. They also show how this can be achieved by defining adapted patch extraction and pooling operators that commute with the action of a transformation group G.

The paper is well-written and rigorous, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the properties of the kernel representation. However, there are a few points that could be clarified or expanded upon.

Firstly, the authors stress the importance of "not loosing the signal information". However, it is not entirely clear why this is so. CNNs are often used in relation to specific tasks, such as image classification, and it is not obvious why signal information needs to be preserved in these cases. The authors could provide more context or explanation for why this is an important consideration.

Secondly, the authors mention the connection between the kernel representation and the scattering transform, but they do not explicitly discuss the relationship between their approach and other invariant kernel methods, such as those developed by Hans Burkhardt. It would be helpful to clarify the novelty of their approach in this context.

Finally, the authors define patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action, but it is not clear whether any other operator that commutes with all group elements would ensure the equivariance property of CNNs with respect to group transformations. This is more of a curiosity, but it would be interesting to see a discussion of this point.

In terms of novelty, the paper appears to build on the work of Julien Mairal et al. on Convolutional Kernel Networks. However, the authors could provide more context or explanation for how their approach differs from or improves upon this earlier work.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a thorough analysis of the properties of the kernel representation. With a few clarifications and expansions, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
