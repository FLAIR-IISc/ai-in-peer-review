This paper presents a theoretical study of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel framework of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) to analyze signals in a continuous domain. The central contribution is to show that this representation, through a principled construction of patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators, is nearly invariant to group transformations and stable with respect to the action of small diffeomorphisms, without losing signal information.

The analysis heavily builds upon and extends the formalism introduced for the scattering transform, particularly the notion of Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms. A key result is that the stability of the representation can be bounded, with translation-invariance controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer. The authors then establish a connection to a broad class of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) by showing that CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions can be seen as functions within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) generated by their kernel construction. This allows the stability guarantees derived for the kernel representation to be extended to these CNNs.

**Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Technical Quality and Significance:** The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous analysis of important theoretical properties of deep convolutional models. It makes a significant contribution by bridging the gap between the theoretical analysis of scattering networks and the more widely used CKNs and CNNs. Understanding the principles of invariance and stability is fundamental to explaining the success of these architectures, and this work provides valuable insights into how these properties arise from the models' structure. The connection drawn between the RKHS norm of a model, its stability to deformations, and its generalization capacity is particularly insightful.

*   **Originality and Clarity:** The approach of using a unified multilayer kernel framework to study the properties of both CKNs and a class of CNNs is novel and elegant. Extending the stability proof techniques from the scattering transform literature to these more general architectures is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with the multilayer construction presented in a clear, step-by-step manner. The discussions on discretization and kernel approximation effectively connect the continuous-domain theory to practical implementations.

**Weaknesses and Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The primary weakness of this paper is the complete absence of experimental validation. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the claims would be far more compelling if supported by empirical evidence. For example, experiments could be designed to measure the stability of a CKN or a corresponding CNN to controlled diffeomorphisms, verifying the bound in Theorem 6. One could also empirically demonstrate how the degree of translation invariance varies with the scale of the final pooling layer (σn). Without any empirical results, the work remains purely theoretical and feels somewhat incomplete.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper's connection to CNNs relies on using smooth, homogeneous activation functions (e.g., a smoothed ReLU) and a specific normalization term `nk(u) = ||Pkxk-1(u)||`. How critical is this specific form of normalization? State-of-the-art CNNs commonly use batch normalization or other schemes instead. Could the analysis be extended to encompass these more practical normalization techniques?

*   In Section 2.2, the paper discusses kernel approximation via projection onto a finite-dimensional subspace, which is key to the practical CKN model. Could the authors comment on whether other common kernel approximation techniques, such as random Fourier features or the Nyström method, could be used within this framework? It seems that the stability guarantees would hold as long as the approximation mapping is non-expansive. It would be valuable to have the authors' perspective on the suitability of these alternative methods and their effect on the stability bounds.

In summary, this is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes an important contribution to our understanding of deep convolutional architectures. The theoretical framework is elegant and the results are significant. The main drawback is the lack of experiments to validate the theoretical findings. Nonetheless, the strength of the theoretical contribution makes a strong case for acceptance.
