This paper is well written and technically rigorous. It shows that a class of functional spaces implemented by deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs) can be described via multilayer kernels within a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) framework, and it establishes desirable architectural properties: (near) invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphic transformations. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the clarifications below.

Major comments and questions

- Contribution and usefulness of the RKHS reformulation:
  Please articulate more explicitly why casting DCNNs in an RKHS/multilayer kernel framework is useful. For instance:
  - Does the kernel view enable sharper generalization bounds (e.g., via norms of functions in the corresponding RKHS, margin-based arguments, or Rademacher/covering number control that scales with depth/architecture)?
  - Are there computational implications? Can standard kernel approximation techniques (Nyström, random features, inducing points) be leveraged to implement or analyze these architectures more efficiently, or to derive practical training or model selection procedures?
  - Beyond a reformulation, what new insights or results become accessible (e.g., principled design of pooling/nonlinearities to balance invariance/selectivity; architectural regularization via RKHS norms; new stability guarantees)?

- Novelty relative to prior work, especially Convolutional Kernel Networks:
  Please clarify the conceptual and technical differences with “Convolutional Kernel Networks” (Mairal, Koniusz, Harchaoui, Schmid). Specifically:
  - How do your multilayer kernels compare to the convolutional kernels proposed there (assumptions, expressivity, handling of pooling and nonlinearity)?
  - Are your invariance and stability results stronger, more general, or proved under different hypotheses? If so, state this precisely.
  - Do you obtain new theoretical guarantees (e.g., Lipschitz constants with respect to diffeomorphisms, group actions) that were not available in CKN or related works?
  - If there is overlap, please delineate what is new in your analysis, proofs, or architectural prescriptions.

- “Not losing the signal information”:
  The paper emphasizes preserving signal information. Please explain why this is necessary or desirable in your framework. In many applications (e.g., classification), it is sufficient to preserve task-relevant information while discarding nuisances. How does your requirement relate to notions such as sufficiency, invariance vs. selectivity, or information bottleneck trade-offs? Is signal preservation required to establish your stability/invariance results, or is it a design choice that can be relaxed without harming performance on downstream tasks? Clarifying when and why preservation is beneficial would strengthen the narrative.

- Group invariance connections:
  Is there a direct link to invariant kernel approaches as developed by Hans Burkhardt and collaborators? Under what conditions do your group-averaged or group-equivariant constructions coincide with, generalize, or differ from invariant kernels (e.g., kernels integrated over group orbits)? If applicable, provide a mapping between your multilayer kernel definitions and classical invariant kernel formulations, and discuss advantages (e.g., hierarchical composition, stability to non-group deformations).

- Operators commuting with group actions:
  You define patch extraction, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action. More generally, does any operator in the commutant of the group action ensure equivariance of the corresponding DCNN layer? If not, what additional properties are required (e.g., linearity, boundedness, locality, or specific continuity/Lipschitz conditions) to retain your stability and invariance guarantees through depth? Please also clarify how pointwise nonlinearities fit into this framework—what conditions on the nonlinearity preserve equivariance or controlled deviation from it?

Suggestions for clarity and completeness

- Precisely define “almost invariance”: provide a formal statement (e.g., Lipschitz or Hölder bounds) that quantifies the deviation from perfect invariance and how it scales with depth, layer parameters, and group/deformation magnitude.

- State all regularity and curvature assumptions on diffeomorphic transformations and on signals needed for the stability results. Indicate which constants depend on architecture (filters, pooling scales, bandwidths) and how these trade off invariance vs. sensitivity.

- Summarize the main theoretical contributions up front, distinguishing:
  (i) RKHS/multilayer-kernel representation of DCNN feature maps,
  (ii) equivariance/invariance properties under group actions,
  (iii) stability to diffeomorphisms,
  (iv) any implications for generalization or complexity control (if available).
  A concise theorem-level summary would help readers.

- Discuss computational tractability: if the multilayer kernels are to be used in practice, how are they chosen or approximated? Are there concrete algorithms (e.g., finite-dimensional feature maps) that instantiate the theory and suggest practical designs? If not, delineate this as a theoretical contribution and outline potential paths for approximation.

- If possible, provide a small illustrative example (even synthetic) showing how the RKHS perspective prescribes specific choices of pooling/nonlinearity that improve invariance/stability while controlling information loss.

- Clarify notation early (group action on signals vs. on spatial domains; definition of patches; measure on the group if averaging is used) to keep the proofs self-contained and easy to follow.

Overall assessment

The paper offers a compelling and rigorous perspective by recasting DCNN architectures within an RKHS of multilayer kernels and by proving desirable invariance and stability properties. With clearer articulation of the benefits of this reformulation, a sharper comparison to prior kernel-based convolutional models (notably Convolutional Kernel Networks), and precise statements regarding operator classes that preserve equivariance, the contribution will be both clearer and stronger. I recommend acceptance after addressing the points above.
