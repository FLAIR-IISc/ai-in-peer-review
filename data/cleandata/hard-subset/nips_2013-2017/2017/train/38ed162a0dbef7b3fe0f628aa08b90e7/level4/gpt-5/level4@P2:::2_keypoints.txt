- Paper generalizes convolutional kernel networks to continuous domains and studies the RKHS geometry of deep convolutional representations.  
- Core result: representation is Lipschitz-stable to C1 diffeomorphisms (including translations) under assumptions A1–A3 and norm-preserving/non-expansive kernel mappings.  
- Increasing pooling scale yields near-translation invariance; construction extends to locally compact groups for equivariance and global invariance via group pooling.  
- Discusses discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations, preserving non-expansiveness.  
- Shows a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lie in the same RKHS and inherit the RKHS stability guarantees when their RKHS norm is controlled.  
- Builds on CKNs and Mallat’s scattering transform, extending stability notions to more kernels, architectures, and group actions beyond translations.  
- Connects to group-equivariant CNNs, invariant kernels, and kernel-based views of deep networks; links RKHS norms, stability, and generalization.  
- Theoretical analysis is technically solid and well-structured; Theorem 6 cleanly quantifies sensitivity to deformations and pooling effects.  
- Discretization and projection steps are shown to be non-expansive, making results relevant to practical implementations.  
- Paper is generally well written and organized, though a few notational/editorial issues hinder readability.  
- Contribution is original in synthesizing kernel frameworks and embedding a class of CNNs into the RKHS with stability guarantees.  
- Providing stability and invariance guarantees for common convolutional architectures is timely and potentially influential for regularization and architecture design.  
- Strengths: unified theoretical framework, provable diffeomorphism stability, tunable translation invariance, group-extension, and practical relevance via discretization and CNN embedding.  
- Weaknesses: needs stronger motivation for why deformation stability matters in practice (e.g., sample complexity, domain shift, augmentation, nuisances).  
- Notation issue: κ is overloaded in A3 and Eq. (10); rename to avoid confusion.  
- Minor correctness/clarity fixes suggested (redundant constraint at lines 384–385, missing φ definition at line 416, typo in lines 427–428).  
- Limitations to acknowledge: stability constant grows with depth (1 + n), pooling reduces norm preservation, and assumptions rely on Gaussian pooling and smooth activations (unclear extension to ReLU/non-Gaussian pooling).  
- Reference corrections and missing page info requested for multiple citations; specific fixes for [9], [17], [19], and status update for [32].  
- Pros for acceptance: solid, general theoretical guarantees; clear link to CNNs and practical CKNs; valuable group-equivariant extension.  
- Cons for acceptance: motivation could be expanded, some notational/editorial issues, and assumptions may limit applicability to all modern CNN variants.  
- Overall recommendation: lean toward acceptance contingent on addressing minor clarity, reference fixes, and strengthening motivation.
