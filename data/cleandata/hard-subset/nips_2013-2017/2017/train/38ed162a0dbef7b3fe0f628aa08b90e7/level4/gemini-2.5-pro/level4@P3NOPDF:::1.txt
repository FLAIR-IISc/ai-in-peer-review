This paper provides a theoretical analysis of deep Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), investigating their invariance and stability properties. The authors demonstrate that with carefully designed components—specifically the patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators—CKNs can achieve near-invariance to groups of transformations and stability with respect to the action of small diffeomorphisms. Furthermore, the paper establishes a valuable connection to conventional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), showing that under certain assumptions, these desirable theoretical guarantees can be extended from CKNs to CNNs.

The paper is well-written, and the theoretical arguments are presented with clarity and rigor. The technical contributions are significant, providing a formal mathematical framework for understanding the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional architectures. Bridging the gap between kernel methods (CKNs) and deep neural networks (CNNs) is a valuable contribution, offering a new theoretical lens through which the success of CNNs can be interpreted. The analysis is thorough and provides a solid foundation for future work in this area.

The primary weakness of the paper is the complete absence of empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis is elegant and compelling, its practical implications remain speculative without experimental support. The claims of near-invariance to transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms are powerful, but they would be substantially strengthened by experiments designed to quantify these properties. For instance, one could measure the change in the output representation of a CKN or a corresponding CNN as an input image is subjected to various transformations (e.g., rotations, translations) or deformations. Such experiments would not only validate the theoretical bounds but also provide valuable insights into how tightly these bounds hold in practice and under what conditions the underlying assumptions are met. The inclusion of even simple, controlled experiments would significantly elevate the impact of this work.

Additionally, I have a question regarding the kernel mapping operator. The analysis appears to rely on a specific construction. Could the theoretical framework be extended to other common kernel approximation techniques? Specifically:

1.  Could methods like random Fourier features or other explicit kernel mappings based on a finite number of basis functions be used as the kernel mapping operator within the CKN architecture?
2.  If so, how would the use of such approximation methods affect the derived stability and invariance guarantees? Would the stability bounds degrade gracefully with the number of features used in the approximation?
