

The paper investigates theoretical properties of deep convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), demonstrating that under appropriate design of patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators, CKNs achieve near invariance to group transformations and stability under diffeomorphisms. The authors further establish that these properties extend to convolutional neural networks (CNNs) under specific assumptions. The work presents a technically sound and well-structured theoretical contribution, offering valuable insights into the geometric foundations of deep learning architectures.

While the theoretical analysis is rigorous and compelling, the paper lacks empirical validation to substantiate its core claims. The absence of experiments to demonstrate the stated invariance properties (e.g., via transformations on standard datasets) significantly weakens the practical relevance of the findings. For instance, visualizing how CKNs respond to controlled transformations (e.g., rotations, elastic deformations) would directly corroborate the theoretical stability guarantees. The authors should include such experiments to bridge the gap between theory and practice, as empirical evidence is essential for establishing the real-world applicability of the proposed framework.

A secondary concern pertains to the practical implementation of the kernel mapping. The paper relies on implicit kernel representations, but it does not explore whether finite-dimensional approximations—such as random Fourier features or explicit kernel expansions using finite Fourier series—could serve as viable alternatives. If feasible, these methods would significantly reduce computational complexity. Crucially, the authors should address whether such approximations preserve the invariance and stability properties derived in the theoretical analysis. Specifically, it would be valuable to analyze the impact of finite-dimensional kernel approximations on the network’s stability to diffeomorphisms, as this directly affects the practical utility of the framework.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear theoretical exposition and the elegant extension of results to CNNs. However, the omission of empirical results represents a notable limitation. For a venue like NIPS, where both theoretical and empirical contributions are highly valued, the lack of experimental support diminishes the paper’s overall impact. Including even minimal experiments (e.g., on small-scale transformations of MNIST or CIFAR-10) would substantially strengthen the submission.

Regarding the kernel approximation question, the authors should clarify whether random Fourier features (or similar methods) maintain the theoretical guarantees. If not, they should identify the conditions under which stability is compromised. This is not merely a technical footnote—it directly influences whether the framework can be deployed in resource-constrained settings. A brief discussion or preliminary experiment comparing implicit vs. explicit kernel mappings would be highly beneficial.

In summary, the paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to understanding the geometric properties of deep networks. Its primary weakness is the absence of empirical validation, which is a critical omission for a work claiming to establish generalizable properties. Addressing this gap would significantly elevate the paper’s quality. The question about kernel approximations is also pertinent and warrants a more thorough discussion. With these additions, the work would be a strong candidate for publication. As it stands, the theoretical insights are compelling but lack the empirical grounding required to fully convince the NIPS community of their practical significance.
