This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors achieve this by defining and studying a multilayer convolutional kernel, which generalizes the construction of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs). The main contribution is a formal proof that the representations derived from this kernel framework are stable with respect to the action of C1-diffeomorphisms. Specifically, the paper shows that the change in the representation is bounded by a function of the deformation's magnitude and gradient norm (Theorem 6). This result is analogous to stability guarantees previously established for scattering networks.

A key aspect of this work is that it connects this kernel-based analysis to standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). The authors demonstrate that the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) associated with their construction contains a broad class of CNNs that use smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This allows the stability guarantees derived for the kernel representation to be extended to these CNN models, providing a valuable theoretical insight into their behavior. The paper also sketches an extension of this framework to achieve equivariance and invariance to the action of locally compact groups.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of deep convolutional architectures, which are often treated as black boxes. The stability and invariance guarantees are a significant contribution toward a better mathematical understanding of these models.
*   **Novel Bridge between Kernels and CNNs:** The work successfully bridges the gap between kernel methods and deep learning. By showing that a class of CNNs resides within the RKHS of the proposed convolutional kernel, the authors provide a principled framework to analyze properties like stability and connect them to the RKHS norm, which is in turn related to generalization.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the arguments are easy to follow. The construction of the multilayer kernel is presented methodically, building from patch extraction to pooling, and the theoretical development is sound.
*   **Relevance:** The study of stability is highly relevant, particularly with the growing interest in the robustness of neural networks (e.g., to adversarial examples) and their generalization capabilities. This work provides a formal tool for reasoning about these properties.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The motivation for the stability analysis could be strengthened in the introduction. The link to adversarial robustness is mentioned only briefly, but the importance of stability to diffeomorphisms is a more general concept tied to generalization and robustness to natural variations in data. Expanding on why this property is crucial for modern deep learning models would further highlight the paper's impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a rigorous and novel theoretical analysis of stability for deep convolutional representations.
2.  Establishes an important formal connection between Convolutional Kernel Networks, a class of standard CNNs, and the theory of scattering networks.
3.  The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a significant and timely problem in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
The arguments against acceptance are minor. The primary contribution is a theoretical analysis that unifies and extends existing frameworks (CKNs, scattering transforms) rather than proposing a completely new architecture or empirical state-of-the-art result. However, this type of theoretical contribution is valuable and well-suited for NIPS.

**Detailed Comments and Typos:**

*   In assumption (A3), the notation `κ` is used for a constant, which is also used for the function defining the kernel in Equation (2). Using a different symbol would avoid potential confusion.
*   In Appendix B, Lemma B.1, the second constraint `(∀v ∈ Pk, ...)` in the displayed equation appears to be redundant, as it is identical to the first constraint due to the symmetry of the kernel `k`.
*   In Appendix B, after Lemma B.2, the definition of the feature map `φ` in the context of `K(z, z') = <φ(z), φ(z')>` could be stated more explicitly alongside the introduction of the kernel `K` for clarity.
*   In Appendix C.2, in the proof of non-expansiveness for the inverse polynomial kernel, there appears to be a typo. The term `| ||z|| - ||z|| |^2` should likely be `| ||z|| - ||z'|| |^2`. Furthermore, the inequality preceding this term seems to hold with equality.
