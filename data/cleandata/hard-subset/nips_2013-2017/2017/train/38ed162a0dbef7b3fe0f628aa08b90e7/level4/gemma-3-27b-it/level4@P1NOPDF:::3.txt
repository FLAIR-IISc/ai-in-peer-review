The paper is well-written and rigorous, successfully demonstrating how the functional spaces implemented by Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (DCNNs) can be described in terms of multilayer kernels. It also highlights appealing properties of this architecture: near-invariance to group transformations and stability under diffeomorphic transformations. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the following comments:

*To my understanding, the main contribution of this paper is a reformulation of DCNN architecture within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. Please elaborate on the practical benefits of this reformulation. For example, might this kernel formulation yield interesting results regarding generalization bounds? Or could it offer computational advantages through the application of standard kernel techniques and approximations? 

More broadly, does the novelty of this approach extend beyond simply reinterpreting DCNNs in the context of RKHS? Please also clarify the novelty relative to the paper “Convolutional Kernel Networks” by Mairal, Koniusz, Harchaoui, and Schmid.

* The authors emphasize the importance of “not losing signal information.” Could you explain *why* this is so critical? DCNNs are frequently employed for tasks like image classification, where only task-relevant information needs to be preserved – not necessarily the full original signal. Please comment on this point.

* Concerning group invariance: is there any connection to the invariant kernel approach developed by Hans Burkhardt?

* As a point of curiosity: you define patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action. Would any other operator (within the set of operators that commute with all group elements) also guarantee the equivariance property of DCNNs with respect to these group transformations?
