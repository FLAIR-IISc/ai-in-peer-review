Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation whose feature maps are near-invariant to translations, stable to C1 diffeomorphisms, and amenable to group-equivariant and globally group-invariant constructions. Building on the convolutional kernel network (CKN) framework, the authors define a sequence of patch-extraction, kernel-mapping, and pooling operators on continuous domains, and study the geometry of the induced RKHS. Under norm-preserving and non-expansive kernel mappings (A1–A2) and a coupling between patch size and pooling scale (A3), they establish stability bounds of the form ‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖, showing translation invariance increases with the last pooling scale and stability scales with the diffeomorphism’s Jacobian. They also discuss discretization, kernel-map approximations, and show that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations are contained in the RKHS, transferring stability guarantees to these CNNs via norm control. Finally, they outline how to achieve equivariance and global invariance for general locally compact groups by adapting patch extraction and pooling on the group.

The work is clearly positioned relative to scattering transforms (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) that establish stability and invariance for wavelet networks, and to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling). It extends scattering-style analysis to more general kernels and architectures, including CKNs and certain CNNs, and links RKHS norm control to both stability and generalization, resonating with classical kernel theory and recent analyses of deep networks as kernel machines (e.g., Poggio et al., Daniely et al.).

Strengths
- Solid, careful theory. The stability development leverages commutator bounds (Schur’s test), disentangles the roles of patch extraction/pooling, and recovers intuitive scaling with depth and final pooling scale.
- Unification and scope. The kernel view covers CKNs and, via smooth homogeneous activations, a meaningful subset of CNNs, thereby transporting stability properties beyond scattering networks.
- Group perspective. The construction for locally compact groups cleanly recovers equivariance and shows how to combine intermediate stability (subgroup pooling) with final global invariance.
- Practical bridge. The discussion of discretization, kernel approximations, and information preservation clarifies how the continuous theory maps to implementable models.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Motivation for stability could be strengthened. Beyond a brief mention of adversarial robustness, the paper would benefit from articulating why diffeomorphism stability matters empirically (e.g., sample complexity, data augmentation, robustness to viewpoint and nonrigid deformations) and how the presented bounds inform architectural choices (e.g., setting σk and patch sizes).
- Assumptions and constants. A brief discussion of the tightness of the (1+n) dependence and how C1, C2 scale with dimension and κ would add insight. Similarly, comments on the necessity/sufficiency of A3 and practical guidance for choosing σk and patch sizes would help practitioners.
- Discretization and deconvolution. The signal recovery via deconvolution in Lemma 2 relies on small pooling scales; a short stability remark or pointer to conditions ensuring numerically stable inversion would be useful.
- CNN inclusion. The mapping of common activations to the RKHS via smooth homogeneous surrogates is interesting; adding a concise example (e.g., a smoothed ReLU) with explicit Cσ and a short discussion of approximation error relative to standard ReLU CNNs would clarify practical impact.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance
- Quality: Technically sound and carefully argued. Proof techniques appear standard but well adapted; all main proofs are deferred to the appendix, which is acceptable. Minor correctness/clarity issues noted below.
- Clarity: The paper is well organized and readable. A few notational and editorial issues could be fixed (see Specific comments).
- Originality: Extends scattering-style invariance/stability analysis to a broader kernel framework and to a nontrivial class of CNNs. The group treatment is clean and aligns with, but is distinct from, G-CNNs.
- Significance: Provides theoretical grounding for multilayer kernel representations and offers principled guidance on invariance/stability in CNN-like architectures. Likely to influence subsequent theoretical and methodological work on robust representations.

Specific comments and minor issues
- Motivation: Expand the discussion around lines 56–57 to more fully motivate diffeomorphism stability (sample complexity, robustness, data augmentation), with pointers to empirical literature beyond adversarial examples.
- Notation: κ is overloaded. In (A3) κ denotes a geometric constant, while κk in Eq. (2) denotes the kernel’s scalar function. Consider renaming one of them to avoid confusion.
- Eq. between lines 384–385: The second constraint (“and ∀v …”) is redundant given kernel symmetry; one is sufficient.
- Line 416: Define ϕ explicitly when introducing the kernel map K(z,z′) = ⟨ϕ(z), ϕ(z′)⟩H.
- Lines 427–428: The expression after “=” should also hold with equality. Moreover, replace |‖z‖ − ‖z‖|2 with |‖z‖ − ‖z′‖|2.
- Lemma 1: Since recovery relies on coverage by patch centers, it would help to state the sampling density condition explicitly and remark on robustness to small violations.
- Lemma 2: Add a brief note on stability of the deconvolution with Gaussian kernels and the requirement that ek ≥ sk, possibly citing conditions for invertibility/stability.
- Theorem 6: A sentence quantifying how C1 depends on κ and d (as hinted in the appendix) would clarify the trade-off in (A3).
- References: Several entries lack pages or final publication details. Please update:

  • [17] Muandet et al., Foundations and Trends in Machine Learning, 10(1–2):1–141.
  • [19] Raj et al., AISTATS 2017, PMLR 54:1225–1235.
  • [32] Zhang et al., ICML 2017 (Convexified CNNs). Also complete page ranges for [3,6,8,13,14,18,25–27,30,31].

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Rigorous and general stability guarantees for a broad kernelized multilayer representation.
- Clear path from continuous theory to practical discretized architectures (CKNs), with extensions to certain CNNs.
- Conceptually unifying view linking RKHS norm control, stability, and generalization.
- Clean treatment of group equivariance/invariance.

Cons:
- Limited motivation and empirical context for why diffeomorphism stability matters in practice.
- Minor notational and editorial issues; limited guidance on constants and parameter choices.
- CNN inclusion relies on smooth homogeneous activations; the gap to standard ReLU CNNs could be discussed more concretely.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and timely theoretical contribution that bridges scattering theory, kernels, and CNNs, with clear relevance to understanding invariance and stability in deep representations. With improved motivation and minor clarifications, this paper would make a strong addition to NIPS.
