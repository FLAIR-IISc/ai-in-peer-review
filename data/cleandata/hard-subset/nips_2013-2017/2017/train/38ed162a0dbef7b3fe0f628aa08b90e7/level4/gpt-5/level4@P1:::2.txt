Summary
The paper studies deep convolutional representations built from multilayer kernels that generalize convolutional kernel networks (CKNs). The authors analyze the geometry of the associated RKHS and prove that the resulting representations are stable to C1 diffeomorphisms and can be made nearly invariant to group actions without losing signal information. Each layer applies patch extraction, a norm-preserving, non-expansive kernel map (via homogeneous dot-product kernels), and Gaussian pooling; composing these yields a feature map Φn. A signal preservation lemma shows that information is retained and can be recovered from discrete sampling, motivating a practical discretization. Kernel maps are then approximated by projections onto finite-dimensional subspaces, yielding the CKN architecture while preserving non-expansiveness.

The core stability result (Theorem 6) shows that, under assumptions (A1)–(A3) and ‖∇τ‖∞ ≤ 1/2, the representation satisfies
‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1 + n)‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞)‖x‖,
so translation invariance improves with the scale σn of the last pooling layer. Unlike the scattering transform, the norm is not preserved due to pooling, but the authors discuss circumstances where most energy is retained and how a final Gaussian kernel layer restores unit norm while keeping stability.

Section 4 extends the construction from (Rd,+) to general locally compact groups with a left-invariant Haar measure. By defining group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling, the network achieves equivariance in intermediate layers and global invariance through the last pooling. Finally, the authors show that the RKHS associated with their multilayer kernel contains a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (e.g., smoothed ReLU variants), and they provide RKHS norm bounds (Proposition 7). This connects stability and generalization via the RKHS norm, aligning with margin-based generalization insights.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on CKNs, which approximate multilayer kernel maps with finite-dimensional projections, and extends scattering transform analyses (Mallat) to a broader class of convolutional architectures. It relates to group-equivariant CNNs and invariant kernels, while providing a kernel-based, functional-space perspective that separates representation from prediction.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized, and technically sound paper that bridges deep networks and kernels.
- A general stability result for multilayer convolutional kernel representations, comparable to scattering but applicable to CKNs and certain CNNs.
- Clean separation of representation and prediction; stability controlled via RKHS norms with implications for generalization and sample complexity.
- Group-theoretic extension that neatly captures equivariance and global invariance.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Motivation for stability: The manuscript would benefit from stronger motivation beyond the brief remark on adversarial robustness (lines 56–57). Please expand on why Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms matters empirically (e.g., robustness to natural image deformations, sample complexity, domain shifts) and how it informs architecture/regularization choices.
- Notation: κ is overloaded in (A3) (line 193), where it also denotes the kernel function κ used to define K in Eq. (10). Please rename one of these to avoid confusion.
- Redundant constraint: In the displayed equation between lines 384 and 385, the second condition (“and ∀v …”) is superfluous; by symmetry of k it is identical to the first (“∀u …”).
- Missing definition: Line 416—φ is undefined. It should be introduced in Eq. (10) via K(z, z′) = ⟨φ(z), φ(z′)⟩H(K).
- Typo in inequality: Lines 427–428—(i) the inequality under “=” appears to hold with equality; (ii) the term |‖z‖ − ‖z‖|2 should read |‖z‖ − ‖z′‖|2.
- References: Please add page numbers where missing for [3,6,8,13,14,18,25–27,30,31]. Update:
  - [9] Daniely, Frostig, Singer, NIPS 2016, pp. 2253–2261.
  - [17] Muandet, Fukumizu, Sriperumbudur, Schölkopf, Foundations and Trends in ML, 10(1–2):1–141.
  - [19] Raj, Kumar, Mroueh, Fletcher, Schölkopf, AISTATS 2017, PMLR 54:1225–1235.
  - [32] Zhang, Liang, Wainwright, ICML 2017 (accepted; poster).

Pros for acceptance
- Provides a principled, general stability result for deep convolutional representations with a clear link to CNNs.
- Solid theoretical contributions with careful operator analysis and group extension.
- Clarifies how RKHS norms can regulate both stability and generalization.

Cons for acceptance
- Motivation for the central stability analysis is underdeveloped; broader practical implications are only briefly mentioned.
- Minor but numerous presentation issues (notation clash, missing definitions, typos, and reference details) that should be fixed.

Overall assessment
A strong and timely theoretical paper that unifies ideas from kernels and deep learning, extending scattering-style guarantees to a wider class of convolutional architectures, including practical CKNs and certain CNNs. With improved motivation and minor presentation fixes, it would make a valuable NIPS contribution.
