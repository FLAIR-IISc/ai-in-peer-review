Summary
This paper analyzes deep convolutional representations built from a multilayer convolutional kernel, generalizing convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains and studying the geometry of the associated RKHS. The core result establishes Lipschitz stability of the representation to C1 diffeomorphisms (including translations), in the sense of Eq. (4), under norm-preserving and non-expansive kernel mappings (assumptions A1–A2) and suitably chosen patch sizes (A3). The authors further show how increasing the pooling scale yields near-translation invariance, and they extend the construction to general locally compact groups to obtain equivariance and global invariance through group pooling. The paper also discusses discretization and kernel approximation (via finite-dimensional projections), and shows that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the same RKHS, implying that these CNNs inherit the same stability guarantees when their RKHS norm is controlled.

Relation to prior work
The work builds directly on CKNs and on Mallat’s scattering transform, adopting similar stability notions but extending them to a wider class of kernels and architectures, and to group actions beyond translations. It connects to the literature on group-equivariant CNNs and invariant kernels, and complements recent theoretical analyses that view deep networks through a kernel lens. The link between RKHS norms, stability, and generalization echoes classical large-margin theory.

Evaluation
- Quality: The analysis is technically solid and carefully structured. The stability bound (Theorem 6) is clear: the sensitivity to local deformations scales with (1 + n) times the diffeomorphism Jacobian norm, while translation sensitivity decays with the last-layer pooling scale. The discretization and projection steps preserve non-expansiveness, making the theory relevant to practical CKNs. Assumptions are reasonable for common Gaussian pooling and homogeneous dot-product kernels. Proofs are deferred but appear standard and well-motivated.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and well organized. A few notational and editorial issues (listed below) hinder readability and are easily fixable. Additional contextual motivation for why stability matters would further improve accessibility.
- Originality: The contribution extends scattering-style stability analysis to a more general kernel framework that encompasses CKNs and a nontrivial subset of CNNs. While the techniques build on known tools (harmonic analysis, RKHS theory), the synthesis and the CNN embedding are original and valuable.
- Significance: Providing stability and invariance guarantees for widely used convolutional architectures is important and timely. The connection between RKHS norm, stability, and generalization could influence regularization and architecture choices. The group-equivariant extension is also practically relevant.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical framework unifying kernels and deep convolutional architectures.
- Provable stability to diffeomorphisms and tunable near-invariance to translations via pooling.
- Extension to general groups with a clean equivariance/invariance construction.
- Practical relevance: discretization and kernel approximations maintain non-expansiveness; CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations reside in the same RKHS.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Motivation: The paper would benefit from a stronger discussion of why deformation stability matters in modern practice. Beyond the single sentence on adversarial robustness (lines 56–57), consider adding pointers to sample complexity, domain shifts, data augmentation, and robustness to nuisance variability.
- Notation: κ is overloaded in A3 and also denotes the scalar function defining the kernel in Eq. (10). Please rename one of them to avoid confusion.
- Minor correctness/clarity edits:
  - Between lines 384–385: the second constraint (“for all v …”) is redundant given kernel symmetry and duplicates the first constraint.
  - Line 416: the definition of φ is missing; it should be introduced in Eq. (10) through K(z, z′) = ⟨φ(z), φ(z′)⟩ in the RKHS.
  - Lines 427–428: the expression under “=” should likely be an equality; replace | ||z|| − ||z|| |^2 with | ||z|| − ||z′|| |^2.
- Limitations to acknowledge: The stability constant grows with depth (1 + n), and the representation does not preserve the signal norm due to pooling (though a top Gaussian mapping mitigates this). Assumptions depend on Gaussian pooling and smooth homogeneous activations; comments on how far these extend to common nonsmooth activations (e.g., ReLU) and to non-Gaussian pooling would help.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Solid and general theoretical guarantees for deep convolutional representations within an RKHS framework.
- Clear link to CNNs and practical CKN implementations.
- Useful and elegant extension to invariance under general groups.

Cons:
- Motivation for stability’s practical impact could be expanded.
- A few notational and editorial issues.
- Some assumptions (smooth activations, Gaussian pooling) may limit immediate applicability to all modern CNN variants.

Overall, this is a well-executed and relevant contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the minor clarity and reference issues and strengthening the motivation section.
