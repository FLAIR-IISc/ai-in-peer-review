

This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the theoretical underpinnings of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by framing them within a multilayer kernel framework. The authors successfully demonstrate how deep convolutional representations can be characterized as elements of a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS), showing that these representations exhibit both near-invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphic deformations without losing signal information. The work builds upon and extends the scattering transform framework of Mallat [17] while providing theoretical guarantees applicable to standard CNN architectures, rather than just pre-defined filter networks.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and rigorous, making a clear and compelling case for the theoretical properties of convolutional representations. The main contribution lies in generalizing convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16] to continuous domains and establishing stability properties that apply to a broader class of CNNs. This theoretical framework provides valuable insights into why CNNs work well, particularly regarding their stability to small deformations and invariance properties.

**Strengths:** The paper excels in Quality, demonstrating strong technical soundness through rigorous proofs of stability under diffeomorphisms and group invariance. The theoretical results are well-supported by mathematical analysis, and the connections to existing work (particularly Mallat's scattering transform and CKNs) are clearly articulated. The paper also makes a strong case for Significance by showing how these stability properties relate to generalization performance, with the RKHS norm controlling both stability and generalization.

**Originality** is well-justified, as the authors demonstrate how their kernel framework extends beyond previous work by providing theoretical guarantees applicable to standard CNN architectures rather than just pre-defined filter networks. The work distinguishes itself from Mairal et al.'s CKNs by showing that the functional space they define contains not just CKNs but also a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The authors should clarify the novelty relative to Mairal et al.'s work in the text, particularly regarding the broader applicability to standard CNNs.

**Clarity** is excellent, with well-organized presentation and clear mathematical exposition. The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of why the kernel framework is practically useful beyond theoretical insights. The authors should elaborate on how this formulation might lead to better generalization bounds or computational advantages, and why kernel-based approaches might provide new implementation opportunities compared to standard CNN training.

A key point requiring clarification is the claim that "signal information must be preserved." While this is mathematically important for the theoretical framework, the authors should explain why this matters for practical applications beyond signal reconstruction tasks. In classification tasks, only task-relevant information needs preservation, not the entire signal. The authors should address this distinction and explain how their framework handles this practical consideration.

The connection to Burkhardt's invariant kernel approach [13] should be explicitly discussed. The authors should clarify whether their group-invariance framework shares conceptual similarities with Burkhardt's work or represents a distinct theoretical approach.

The question about whether other operators beyond the patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling could ensure equivariance to group transformations is insightful. The authors should address whether their framework is generalizable to other operators that commute with group actions, or if the specific operators they define are necessary for the theoretical guarantees.

The paper's main limitation is that it doesn't sufficiently articulate why the kernel formulation provides new practical insights or advantages beyond the theoretical understanding. The authors should clearly state how this framework might lead to improved model design, better regularization strategies, or novel algorithmic approaches for CNNs. Specifically, they should address:

1. How the kernel framework enables better generalization bounds for CNNs
2. Whether the theoretical insights can translate to practical implementation advantages
3. The computational implications of this formulation compared to standard CNN training

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to understanding CNNs, but the practical significance could be made more compelling. The authors should more explicitly connect their theoretical results to potential applications and improvements in CNN design, training, or analysis.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a meaningful advance in understanding the mathematical properties of convolutional representations. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important theoretical question in deep learning. With minor revisions to clarify the practical significance and novelty relative to prior work, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
