

The paper presents a rigorous and well-structured analysis of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of multilayer kernels in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors successfully demonstrate how CNN architectures can be formalized using kernel-based representations, revealing key properties: near-invariance under group transformations (e.g., translations) and stability under diffeomorphic deformations. This theoretical framework offers a compelling reinterpretation of CNNs, and the technical execution is sound. I recommend acceptance pending clarification of the following points, which are crucial for establishing the paper’s contribution and impact.

**1. Clarify the novelty and utility beyond RKHS reformulation.**  
The core contribution appears to be a reformulation of CNNs within RKHS theory. While mathematically elegant, the manuscript does not sufficiently justify *why* this perspective is transformative. The authors should explicitly address:  
- *Practical utility*: How does this kernel formulation enable new insights into generalization bounds, approximation efficiency, or computational advantages over standard CNN implementations? For instance, could kernel approximation techniques (e.g., random features, Nyström methods) streamline training or inference?  
- *Novelty vs. prior work*: The approach bears resemblance to Mairal et al.’s "Convolutional Kernel Networks" (ICML 2014), which also embeds CNNs in RKHS via kernel composition. The authors must clearly differentiate their work: Is the novelty in the *specific kernel construction* (e.g., multilayer composition rules), the *theoretical guarantees* (e.g., stability bounds), or the *interpretation* of group invariance? A direct comparison with Mairal et al. is essential to avoid redundancy.  

**2. Justify "preserving signal information" for discriminative tasks.**  
The paper emphasizes preserving "signal information" as a critical design principle. However, for typical CNN applications (e.g., image classification), the goal is to retain *task-relevant features*, not the full signal. The authors should clarify:  
- Why full signal preservation is necessary for classification tasks (e.g., does it prevent loss of discriminative details under transformations?), or whether this emphasis applies primarily to reconstruction tasks.  
- If the framework is intended for *both* reconstruction and classification, how does it adapt to prioritize task-specific information? Without this, the claim risks being misaligned with standard CNN usage.  

**3. Connect group invariance to existing invariant kernel methods.**  
The paper discusses group invariance under transformations like translations. This echoes Burkhardt’s work on *invariant kernels* (e.g., "Invariant Kernels for Pattern Recognition," 2000), where kernels are designed to be invariant to group actions. The authors should:  
- Explicitly discuss how their kernel-based invariance mechanism relates to or differs from Burkhardt’s approach.  
- Address whether their formulation generalizes or extends prior invariant kernel techniques, particularly regarding the role of pooling and multilayer composition.  

**4. Generalize the operator commutation requirement.**  
The paper defines patch, pooling, and kernel maps as operators commuting with group actions to ensure equivariance. This raises a natural question:  
- Are the *specific* operators chosen (e.g., max pooling) necessary for equivariance, or do *any* operators commuting with the group action suffice? If the latter, the framework could be broader; if not, why are these operators uniquely suited?  
- The authors should clarify whether this commutation condition is both *necessary* and *sufficient* for the equivariance properties they derive, or if additional constraints are required.  

**Overall assessment:**  
The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by formalizing CNNs via multilayer kernels, offering new perspectives on invariance and stability. Its clarity and mathematical rigor are commendable. However, the current draft risks being perceived as a rephrasing of existing kernel-CNN connections without sufficient justification for its distinct value. Addressing the points above—particularly the novelty gap relative to Mairal et al. and the practical utility of the RKHS reformulation—will significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.  

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The authors must refine the introduction and related work to explicitly contrast their approach with Mairal et al.’s framework, add a dedicated discussion on the utility of the kernel perspective (e.g., for generalization or computation), and clarify the signal preservation rationale. These edits will ensure the paper’s novelty and relevance are unambiguously established for the NIPS audience.
