This paper is well-written and rigorous, and it successfully demonstrates how (some types of) functional spaces implemented by DCNNs can be described in terms of multilayer kernels. It also shows that the architecture has good features: (near) invariance for group transformations and stability for deformable transformations. I recommend acceptance, given the comments below:

*The main paper contribution for the reviewer's understanding is the rewriting of the CNNS architecture in terms of RKHS.
Please add some comments in the text explaining why this restructuring is useful/will be useful (for example, can the kernel formulation give interesting results on generalization limits?, is it computationally more efficient to implement using standard techniques/ अनुमान on the kernel?).
More generally, what is the novelty of the approach going beyond the rewriting of DCNNs in terms of RKHS?
Also clarify the novelty in relation to the paper "Convolutional Kernel Networks" by Julian Merrell, Piotr Konieczyk, Zaid Harachoui, and Cordelia Schmid.
*The authors emphasize the importance of "losing signal information."
Why is that? DCNNs are often used in relation to certain tasks (such as image classification). Unless the task is signal reconstruction, only task-relevant information needs to be preserved. Please comment on this.
*Regarding group invariance: Is there any connection with the invariant kernel approach (invariant kernel) adopted by Hans Burkhardt?
*Further curiosity: You define patches, pooling, and kernel maps that communicate with the group action. Will any other operator (in the set of operators that communicates with all group elements) ensure the invariance property of DCNNs in relation to group transformations?
