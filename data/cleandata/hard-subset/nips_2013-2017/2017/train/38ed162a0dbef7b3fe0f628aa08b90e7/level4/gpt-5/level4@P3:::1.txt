Summary
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals that (i) builds near-invariance to transformation groups through pooling on the group and (ii) is Lipschitz-stable to diffeomorphic deformations while preserving signal information. The construction generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains: each layer performs patch extraction, a kernel map with homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. The authors show signal recoverability from sampled feature maps (discretization-friendly), provide sufficient conditions under which the representation is non-expansive, and derive a stability bound to diffeomorphisms: the deviation scales linearly with the deformation’s Jacobian and with the translation magnitude, the latter controlled by the pooling scale at the last layer (σn). They further extend pooling and equivariance to general (locally compact) groups, yielding global invariance at the final layer while using intermediate pooling for stability. A key contribution is to show that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS associated with the multilayer kernel; thus, the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization through the standard Hilbert-space Lipschitz bound. The analysis relates and extends prior theory for scattering networks [Mallat; Bruna & Mallat] and connects to CKNs [Mairal et al.], group-equivariant CNNs [Cohen & Welling], and kernel views of deep networks.

Quality
- The theoretical development is careful and technically sound. The stability result (Theorem 6) gives an interpretable trade-off: sensitivity to local deformations scales with depth (via commutators bounded by ‖∇τ‖∞), while translation invariance improves with the last pooling scale (∝ 1/σn). The signal-preservation lemmas support discretization and layerwise sampling.
- The link to CNNs (Proposition 7) is well-motivated: by embedding CNN feature maps with homogeneous activations into the RKHS, the Lipschitz stability of predictions follows from the RKHS norm control.
- Assumptions (A1–A3) are explicit. The use of homogeneous dot-product kernels ensures norm preservation and non-expansiveness at the kernel map. The Gaussian pooling permits clean use of Schur’s test and commutator bounds.
- One limitation is the lack of empirical validation. Even simple synthetic experiments would increase confidence in how the constants and depth dependence behave for realistic architectures. In particular, the effectiveness of the discretized/approximated constructions (with small ek and sk) and the practical tightness of the stability bounds remain untested.
- The discussion on norm loss due to pooling is reasonable, but the paper could better quantify energy preservation (e.g., under 1/f^2-like spectra) and its impact on the magnitude of stability bounds for typical images.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and clearly written, with proofs in the appendix. The operator pipeline (Pk, Mk, Ak) and commutator-based stability analysis are presented cleanly.
- Suggestions for clarity:
  - Make the dependence of constants C1 and C2 more explicit (on κ, dimension d, kernel h, and possibly depth).
  - In Section 2.1, quantify the effect of deconvolution noise when recovering x̄k−1 from x̄k and give a practical range of scales for stable inversion.
  - In Section 2.2, explicitly state how projection/approximation error propagates through layers and adds to the stability bound (e.g., an additive term per layer).
  - Minor typos and small grammatical corrections would improve readability.

Originality
- The work extends the scattering framework’s invariance and deformation stability guarantees to a broader convolutional kernel setting and shows how CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations can be viewed within the induced RKHS. The general group-equivariant pooling formulation and stability transfer to predictive models via RKHS norms add novelty beyond prior analyses of scattering, CKNs, and kernel analyses of deep networks.

Significance
- The results provide principled conditions and tools to reason about invariance, stability, and generalization for convolutional representations, unifying kernel and deep learning perspectives. This can inform architecture design (e.g., pooling scales, patch sizes) and regularization (via RKHS norms) and may contribute to understanding sample complexity. Empirical support would further strengthen the impact.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Kernel approximation beyond RKHS projection: Could random feature maps (e.g., random Fourier features for shift-invariant kernels, or random Maclaurin/spherical-harmonics features for dot-product kernels) or Johnson–Lindenstrauss-type random projections be used in place of orthogonal projections? Since your stability transfer leverages non-expansiveness of Πk, arbitrary random features need not be non-expansive in the RKHS, though they can be near-isometries with high probability. Can you provide a variant of Theorem 6 with an additional approximation error term that accumulates across layers when using such features?
- Discretization constants: Can you provide bounds that make the ek ≥ sk condition more concrete for 2D images and standard CNN hyperparameters, and quantify the stability of deconvolution with realistic Gaussian scales?
- Activations: The RKHS inclusion requires smooth homogeneous activations; can you comment on how closely smoothed ReLUs approximate standard ReLU networks in practice, and whether the RKHS norm bounds remain informative?
- Depth dependence: The linear dependence on n in Theorem 6 could be pessimistic. Is there a refined bound when pooling scales grow geometrically, or when filters have limited spatial support?

Pros
- Solid theoretical contribution that unifies and extends scattering and CKN analyses.
- Clear path from continuous to discrete settings, with signal preservation guarantees.
- Explicit group-equivariant construction and translation invariance scaling with σn.
- Meaningful link to CNNs via RKHS inclusion and norm-based stability/generalization.

Cons
- No empirical validation (even small-scale experiments) to illustrate constants, tightness, and practical behavior under discretization/approximation.
- Stability under non-orthogonal kernel approximations (e.g., random features) not analyzed.
- Some constants and assumptions could be made more explicit and interpretable.

Overall evaluation
This is a strong theoretical paper with clear contributions to understanding invariance and stability in convolutional representations and their relation to CNNs. I lean toward acceptance, while strongly encouraging the authors to add a brief empirical section (synthetic deformations, comparison to scattering and standard CNNs/CKNs) and to discuss stability under practical finite-dimensional kernel approximations beyond orthogonal projection.
