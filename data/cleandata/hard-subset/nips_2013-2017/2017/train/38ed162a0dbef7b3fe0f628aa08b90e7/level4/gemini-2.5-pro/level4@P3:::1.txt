This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The core of the work is the construction of a multilayer convolutional kernel, which generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) and defines a corresponding Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors leverage this framework to study the behavior of representations under input transformations. The main contributions are twofold. First, the paper demonstrates that the resulting signal representation is stable under the action of C1-diffeomorphisms and near-invariant to translations. It provides a formal bound (Theorem 6) showing that stability to deformations is controlled by the network's structure, while invariance to translations increases with the scale of the final pooling layer. This analysis extends similar results from scattering transforms (Mallat, 2012) to a more general setting. Second, and perhaps most significantly, the paper establishes a formal link between this kernel framework and standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), showing that CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions are contained within the constructed RKHS. This connection allows the stability guarantees derived for the kernel representation to be extended to this class of CNNs.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper addresses the fundamental and highly relevant problem of understanding the geometric properties of deep neural networks. Providing theoretical guarantees for stability to deformations is a crucial step towards explaining the success of CNNs and designing more robust models.

2.  **General and Elegant Framework:** The use of a multilayer kernel and its associated RKHS provides a powerful and elegant framework for the analysis. This approach successfully generalizes previous work on scattering transforms to a broader class of models that includes CKNs and, importantly, conventional CNNs. The connection established in Section 5 is a key strength, bridging the gap between kernel methods and mainstream deep learning architectures.

3.  **Clarity and Rigor:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound. The mathematical construction is presented methodically, from the continuous signal domain to its discrete implementation. The assumptions are clearly stated, and the proofs (relegated to the appendix) seem solid. The work provides valuable theoretical insights, for instance, by linking the RKHS norm of a model to both its generalization capabilities and its stability to input deformations.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the complete absence of empirical results. While the theoretical analysis is valuable, it remains abstract without any experimental validation. The claims about stability and invariance are amenable to empirical verification. For instance, experiments could have been conducted to:
    *   Measure the empirical stability of CKNs or compatible CNNs to controlled diffeomorphisms and translations, and see how well these measurements align with the theoretical bounds.
    *   Demonstrate the practical effect of architectural choices (e.g., pooling scale `σ_n`, patch size) on the trade-off between invariance and performance.
    *   Showcase whether regularizing the RKHS norm of a CNN (as suggested by Proposition 7) during training leads to demonstrably more robust models on a practical task.

Without such experiments, it is difficult to assess the practical relevance and tightness of the derived bounds.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  Regarding the kernel approximation discussed in Section 2.2, the authors mention projecting onto a finite-dimensional subspace, which is compatible with the CKN model. Could other popular large-scale kernel approximation methods, such as those based on random Fourier features or Nyström sampling, be integrated into this framework? If so, what would be the effect of such approximations on the stability analysis and the bounds presented in Theorem 6?

2.  The bound on the CNN function norm in Proposition 7 depends recursively on the norms of the filter weights at all layers. This provides a theoretical connection but offers limited practical intuition on how to control this norm during training. Could the authors elaborate on potential regularization strategies that could explicitly encourage a smaller RKHS norm and, consequently, greater stability?

3.  The link to CNNs requires smooth homogeneous activation functions. While the paper provides examples and discusses smoothed variants of ReLU, it would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on the practical gap between these required activations and the non-smooth ReLU function, which is dominant in modern CNN architectures.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that provides novel and important insights into the stability of deep convolutional models. The framework is elegant and the connection to CNNs is particularly compelling. However, the lack of any experimental validation is a major shortcoming that limits the paper's impact. The addition of even a simple set of experiments to support the theoretical claims would significantly strengthen the submission.
