The paper investigates the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and their connection to more standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors generalize the multilayer kernel construction from CKNs and analyze the geometry of the associated reproducing kernel Hilbert space. They demonstrate that this framework achieves near-invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms, providing theoretical guarantees comparable to those of the scattering transform, while accommodating the more flexible architecture of CNNs.

This work builds upon a strong foundation of prior research in convolutional kernel methods [15, 16], the theory of scattering transforms [5, 17], and group-invariant representations [6, 9, 22]. It effectively extends the analytical tools developed for scattering transforms to a broader class of CNNs via a kernel perspective. The introduction of a kernel framework allows for a separation of data representation and predictive modeling, enabling a focused analysis of stability and its impact on generalization. The paper also correctly positions itself within the broader landscape of deep learning with kernels [2, 3, 7, 8, 36].

The strengths of the paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and the clarity of its mathematical presentation. The authors carefully define the necessary operators and assumptions, and the proofs appear well-structured (though detailed examination requires reading the appendix). The connection established between the kernel representation and standard CNNs is a significant contribution, suggesting that the theoretical results may have practical implications for understanding and regularizing CNN training. The discussion of group invariance and stability is thorough and builds on existing work in harmonic analysis.

However, a notable weakness is the complete lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical results are compelling, they would be significantly strengthened by experimental evidence demonstrating the predicted invariance and stability properties of CKNs or standard CNNs. It would be beneficial to see experiments illustrating the robustness of models trained within this framework to transformations and perturbations. Furthermore, the paper does not explore alternative methods for kernel approximation, such as random projections or explicit kernel mappings using finite Fourier series, and a discussion of their potential impact on stability would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous mathematical analysis.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of complex concepts.
*   Novel extension of scattering transform analysis to a broader class of CNNs.
*   Potentially impactful insights into the geometry of deep learning function spaces and its connection to generalization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of empirical validation to support the theoretical claims.
*   Limited discussion of alternative kernel approximation techniques.

Overall, this is a technically sound and theoretically interesting paper. While the absence of experimental results is a concern, the rigor and novelty of the analysis warrant consideration. Addressing the empirical validation and exploring alternative kernel approximations would substantially strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors acknowledging the need for future experimental work.
