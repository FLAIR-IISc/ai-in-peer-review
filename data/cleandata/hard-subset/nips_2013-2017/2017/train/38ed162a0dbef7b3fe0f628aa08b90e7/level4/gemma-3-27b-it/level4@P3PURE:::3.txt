The paper is well-written and rigorous, successfully demonstrating how the functional spaces implemented by Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (DCNNs) can be described in terms of multilayer kernels. It also establishes that this architecture possesses desirable properties, including (almost) invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphic transformations. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the comments below.

To my understanding, the primary contribution of this paper is a reformulation of the DCNN architecture within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. Please elaborate on the potential benefits of this reformulation—for example, could this kernel formulation lead to improved generalization bounds? Or, might it offer computational advantages through standard kernel techniques or approximations? Furthermore, it would be valuable to clarify whether the novelty of this approach extends beyond simply representing DCNNs within the RKHS context. Specifically, please distinguish this work from the paper "Convolutional Kernel Networks" by Mairal et al. 

The authors emphasize the importance of “not losing signal information.” However, DCNNs are frequently employed in tasks such as image classification, where only task-relevant information needs to be preserved, rather than reconstructing the original signal. Please comment on this point and the implications for the proposed framework.

Regarding group invariance, is there a connection to the invariant kernel approach developed by Hans Burkhardt? Exploring this relationship could provide valuable context.

Finally, as a point of curiosity, you define patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action. Would any other operator (within the set of operators that commutes with all group elements) also guarantee the equivariance property of DCNNs with respect to the given group transformations?
