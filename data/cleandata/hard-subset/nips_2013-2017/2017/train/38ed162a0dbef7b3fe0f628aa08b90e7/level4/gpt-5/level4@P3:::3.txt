Summary and relation to prior work
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals and analyzes its invariance and stability properties. The construction mirrors convolutional architectures: at each layer, the model performs patch extraction, a pointwise kernel map into an RKHS, and Gaussian pooling. The authors prove that the representation is stable to C1-diffeomorphisms and becomes increasingly translation-invariant as pooling scale grows. They further generalize pooling and patch extraction to arbitrary locally compact groups to obtain equivariance and global invariance to group actions. Importantly, the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel contains a broad family of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations; for these networks, the RKHS norm controls Lipschitz stability and thus relates stability and generalization. Discretization and finite-dimensional approximations yield convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), for which the stability properties largely carry over.

The work draws heavily on the scattering transform framework (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat) and on convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.), but extends them by: (i) providing stability bounds for a more general multilayer kernelized architecture (not restricted to wavelets), (ii) giving a principled group-equivariant construction beyond translations, and (iii) embedding a class of CNNs into the RKHS with explicit norm bounds. It also connects to invariant kernels (Haasdonk & Burkhardt), orbit-averaging ideas, and group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling).

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous analysis with precise operator-theoretic bounds. Theorem 6 captures the desired Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms with increasing translation invariance as the last pooling scale grows.
- Conceptual unification: the kernel viewpoint separates representation from prediction and yields a common language covering CKNs and a wide class of CNNs (Proposition 7).
- The group-theoretic section provides a clean recipe to obtain equivariance/invariance beyond translations using group convolution/pooling, harmonizing scattering, group-equivariant CNNs, and kernel methods.
- The discretization and finite-dimensional approximation steps are carefully motivated and compatible with the stability results via non-expansive projections.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty beyond reformulation: While the kernelization of multilayer CNN-like operators is well presented, the paper would benefit from a sharper positioning versus CKNs [Mairal et al., NIPS 2014, 2016] and scattering. What new phenomena or guarantees emerge here that were not available there?
- Practical implications: The manuscript emphasizes “not losing signal information,” yet most applications prioritize task-relevant information over full invertibility. Please clarify when invertibility is beneficial (e.g., theoretical control of norms and stability, regularization, robustness) and whether it has empirical consequences.
- Quantitative constants: The stability bound scales with depth and includes constants from Schur’s test and group pooling. More discussion on their magnitude and tightness, and on dependence on κ and dimensionality, would aid interpretation.
- Approximation error: For the finite-dimensional CKN-like implementation, can you quantify the loss due to projections (e.g., as a function of subspace dimension and sample coverage), and how it interacts with stability and generalization?
- Experimental illustration: Even a small synthetic experiment could help calibrate the depth and pooling-scale trade-offs predicted by the bounds.

Specific requests for clarification
- Usefulness of the reformulation: Beyond conceptual clarity, can the kernel framework lead to explicit generalization bounds? For instance, combining Lipschitz stability with RKHS margin bounds would yield sample-complexity statements; please discuss what can be stated here (even informally) and whether norm control suggests new regularizers. Also comment on computational aspects: does the kernel/RKHS view suggest practical training or model-selection benefits compared to standard CNNs (e.g., via subspace learning in CKNs or Nyström/RFF-style approximations)?
- Novelty relative to CKNs: Please make explicit what is new compared to Mairal et al. (2014, 2016): e.g., the continuous-domain analysis, the diffeomorphism stability bound for the full multilayer kernel (Theorem 6), the group-equivariant construction, and the embedding of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations into the RKHS with recursive norm bounds (Proposition 7).
- “Not losing signal information”: Explain why layerwise recoverability (Lemmas 1–2) matters in this setting. One perspective is that preserving information allows the RKHS norm to control both stability and generalization for any downstream task; losing information may preclude stable decision boundaries for tasks that depend on discarded components. Please articulate this rationale.
- Connection to invariant kernels: Relate your group pooling/integration to invariant kernel constructions (e.g., orbit-averaging, Haasdonk & Burkhardt). In what sense does your multilayer kernel generalize those approaches (e.g., local patch structure, intermediate stability vs. global invariance at the last layer), and can your theory recover their invariance properties as special cases?
- Commuting operators and equivariance: Beyond the specific patch/pooling/kernal maps you define, any bounded linear operator that commutes with the group action yields equivariance. Are there necessary/sufficient conditions on operators used within layers to preserve equivariance and stability (e.g., locality, boundedness, smoothness of kernels)? Please comment on the design space.

Quality
- Technically sound with careful assumptions (A1–A3). The proofs leverage standard tools (Schur’s test) and extend prior scattering arguments. Please consider adding an intuitive discussion of the (1 + n) depth factor and the role of σn in (7), and summarize the dependence of constants on κ and dimension.

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable. A brief table/figure summarizing the operators, assumptions, and resulting properties by layer would help. Define all constants where they first appear, and state explicitly the conditions for Lemmas 1–2 (e.g., stability of deconvolution).

Originality
- The combination of a multilayer kernel on continuous domains, diffeomorphism-stability bounds for that kernel, the group-equivariant construction, and the inclusion of a class of CNNs via RKHS norms appears novel beyond prior CKN and scattering work.

Significance
- Provides a principled bridge between CNNs, scattering, and kernels, with implications for understanding invariance/stability and for norm-based regularization. The potential to derive generalization bounds and to inform architecture/regularizer design is significant.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution clarifying invariance and stability of deep convolutional representations in an RKHS framework.
- Unifying perspective that includes both CKNs and a broad class of CNNs.
- Clean group-equivariant construction with clear takeaways about where invariance should be enforced.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited discussion of quantitative constants and practical implications.
- Limited empirical illustration of the theoretical trade-offs.
- Some novelty aspects vs. prior CKN/scattering work could be made crisper.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper is rigorous, conceptually unifying, and advances our theoretical understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations. With the clarifications suggested above, it will be a valuable contribution.
