

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically establishing that they can be made near-invariant to groups of transformations and stable to diffeomorphisms when appropriate kernel constructions are employed. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15,16] and analyze the geometry of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). Building on Mallat's scattering transform [17], which provided stability guarantees for wavelet-based architectures, the paper extends these results to more standard CNN architectures through a kernel framework. The work also connects to group-invariance studies [6,9,17,22] and demonstrates how stability properties can be translated to predictive models by controlling their RKHS norm.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured, making significant contributions to understanding the functional spaces underlying CNNs. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful proofs establishing stability bounds under specific assumptions (A1-A3), and the authors successfully demonstrate that these stability properties extend to a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The connection between stability and generalization is particularly noteworthy, as it provides a principled explanation for why stable representations may lead to better sample complexity.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-organized mathematical exposition that makes complex concepts accessible. The authors provide clear definitions of key operators (patch extraction, kernel mapping, pooling) and effectively relate their construction to existing work. The discussion of discretization and practical implementation (Section 2.1-2.2) demonstrates strong technical grounding.

In terms of originality, the paper offers a novel theoretical framework that bridges CKNs and standard CNNs, extending stability results beyond the scattering transform to more general architectures. This represents a meaningful advancement over previous work, as the authors properly situate their contribution within the NIPS proceedings by demonstrating how their results differ from related papers like Mallat [17] and Mairal [15].

The significance of this work is substantial, as it provides theoretical foundations for understanding why CNNs work well and offers potential pathways for improved regularization through stability control. The connection between RKHS norm, stability, and generalization has clear implications for both theory and practice.

The primary weakness is the lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis is compelling, the authors do not provide any experimental results to demonstrate the invariance and stability properties they claim. It would be valuable to see empirical evidence showing how CKNs with appropriate settings achieve the claimed invariance to transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms.

A related question concerns kernel approximation methods. The paper discusses finite-dimensional approximations for practical implementation, but it would be helpful to address whether alternative approaches like random Fourier features or explicit kernel mapping with finite Fourier series could also be used for kernel approximation. If so, what would be the effect on the stability guarantees? This question is particularly relevant given the growing interest in scalable kernel methods.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of CNNs' functional properties. With the addition of empirical validation and discussion of alternative kernel approximation methods, this paper would be even more impactful.
