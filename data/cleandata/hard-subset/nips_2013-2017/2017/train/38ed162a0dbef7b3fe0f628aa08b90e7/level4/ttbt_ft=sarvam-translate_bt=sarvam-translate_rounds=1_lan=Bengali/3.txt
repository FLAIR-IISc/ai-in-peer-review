This paper is well-written and rigorous. It successfully demonstrates how functional spaces (of some kind) implemented by DCNNs can be described in terms of multilayer kernels. It also shows some excellent features of the architecture: (almost) invariance to group transformations and stability to deformable transformations. Considering the comments below, I recommend accepting it:

* The main contribution of the original paper to the reviewer's understanding is the rewriting of the CNNS architecture in the context of RKHS.
Please add some comments on why/what makes this reconstruction useful (e.g., can the kernel formulation give interesting results on generalization bounds?, is it computationally more efficient using approximate kernels on standard techniques/kernels?).
More generally, what is the novelty of the approach to go beyond the reconstruction of DCNNs in the context of RKHS?
Also, clarify the novelty compared to the papers "Convolutional Kernel Networks" by Julien Mieral, Piotr Koniasz, Jaid Harchawi, and Cordelia Schmidt.
* The authors emphasize the importance of "signal information loss."
Why is this? DCNNs are often related to certain tasks (e.g., image classification). If the task is not just signal reconstruction, then only task-related information needs to be stored. Please comment on this.
* Regarding group invariance: Is there any connection with the invariant kernel method (invariant kernel) adopted by Hans Burkhardt?
* More curiosity: You define patches, pooling, and kernel maps that interact with group actions. Will any other operator (within the set of operators that interact with all group elements) ensure the invariance property of DCNNs with respect to group transformations?
