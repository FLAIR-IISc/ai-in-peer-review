

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, focusing on their near-invariance to groups of transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16] to develop a comprehensive functional space that includes both CKNs and a broad class of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with smooth homogeneous activation functions. Building on Mallat's scattering transform theory [17], they establish stability guarantees under the action of diffeomorphisms while preserving signal information—a significant advancement over previous approaches that either lacked learning capability or did not connect to standard CNN architectures.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a rigorous theoretical framework for understanding the invariance properties of convolutional representations through the lens of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). Second, it demonstrates that the stability properties of the kernel representation can be translated to predictive models by controlling their RKHS norm, linking stability to generalization. Third, it establishes a concrete connection between the proposed kernel framework and standard CNNs, showing that many CNN architectures live within the functional space defined by their kernel construction. This connection is particularly significant because it extends theoretical results from specialized architectures (like scattering networks) to the more widely used CNNs.

The technical development is strong and well-structured. The authors carefully define the multilayer convolutional kernel construction, including patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators, and prove that the representation preserves signal information at each layer. The stability analysis under diffeomorphisms is thorough, with precise bounds that incorporate the number of layers and pooling scales. The paper also extends its results to group invariance, showing how to achieve equivariance to various transformation groups through appropriate operator design. The connection to CNNs via homogeneous activation functions is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates that the theoretical framework applies to practical architectures commonly used in deep learning.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making complex theoretical concepts accessible. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, and the proofs (while relegated to appendices) appear sound. The related work section adequately contextualizes the contributions within the NIPS proceedings, particularly citing Mallat's scattering transform [17] and CKNs [15, 16].

However, the paper has one notable limitation: it lacks empirical validation of its theoretical claims. While the theoretical results are elegant and well-supported, the absence of experimental results makes it difficult to assess how these properties manifest in practice. For instance, the paper claims that CKNs with appropriate settings are invariant to transformations and stable to diffeomorphisms, but provides no visualizations, quantitative measurements, or comparative experiments to demonstrate this. The authors could strengthen their contribution by including experiments showing how the proposed kernel framework achieves invariance to specific transformations (e.g., rotations, translations) or stability to small deformations on standard datasets.

A second point of clarification concerns the kernel approximation methods. The paper mentions that convolutional kernel networks use finite-dimensional approximations of the RKHS layers but does not explore alternative approximation methods. It would be valuable to investigate whether techniques like random Fourier features or explicit kernel mappings with finite Fourier series could serve as viable alternatives, and how they might affect the stability properties. The authors should comment on whether these approaches would preserve the theoretical guarantees or introduce additional approximation errors.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous stability and invariance analysis
- Clear connection between kernel methods and practical CNN architectures
- Comprehensive treatment of group invariance and diffeomorphism stability
- Well-organized presentation with clear mathematical exposition

Weaknesses:
- Lack of empirical validation to support theoretical claims
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations for kernel approximation

The paper makes a significant contribution to our theoretical understanding of convolutional representations. By establishing that many standard CNNs reside within a functional space with provable stability properties, it provides a principled foundation for understanding why these architectures work well. The results have potential implications for regularization strategies, sample complexity, and the design of more robust deep learning models. While the absence of empirical validation is a notable limitation, the theoretical advances are substantial enough to warrant consideration for acceptance.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution that extends scattering transform theory to standard CNN architectures, with clear connections to existing work at NIPS.
Con: Lacks empirical validation to demonstrate the practical implications of the theoretical results.
