The paper is well-written and rigorous and successfully shows how (some type of) functional spaces implemented by DCNNs can be described in terms of multilayer kernels. It also demonstrates that this architecture possesses desirable properties: (almost) invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphic transformations. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the comments below.

To my understanding, the main contribution of the paper is a reformulation of CNN architectures within the context of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). Please elaborate on the utility of this reformulation – for example, could this kernel formulation lead to interesting results regarding generalization bounds? Is it computationally more efficient to implement using standard techniques or approximations common in kernel methods? 

More broadly, does the novelty of this approach extend beyond simply reformulating DCNNs in the context of RKHS? Also, please clarify the novelty of this work relative to the paper "Convolutional Kernel Networks" by Mairal et al. 

The authors emphasize the importance of “not losing signal information.” Why is this a critical concern? DCNNs are frequently employed in task-specific applications (e.g., image classification). Unless the task is signal reconstruction, only task-relevant information needs to be preserved. Please comment on this design choice.

Regarding group invariance, is there any connection to the invariant kernel approach developed by Hans Burkhardt and others? 

Finally, a point of curiosity: you define patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action. Would any operator (within the set of operators that commutes with all group elements) guarantee the equivariance property of DCNNs with respect to the group transformations?
