

This paper investigates the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations through the lens of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), extending the framework of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to provide theoretical guarantees for a broader class of convolutional architectures. The authors establish that the multilayer convolutional kernel framework preserves signal information while achieving near-invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms, with stability bounds that generalize the scattering transform results [17] while accommodating standard convolutional neural network (CNN) architectures. This work builds upon recent NIPS contributions on scattering networks [5, 17] and group-invariant representations [9, 22], while extending the kernel-based approach of CKNs [15, 16] to provide a unified theoretical foundation for understanding stability properties in convolutional architectures.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through rigorous proofs of stability under diffeomorphisms (Theorem 6), with clear connections between kernel representation theory and predictive models. The mathematical framework is sound, with careful attention to the conditions required for stability (assumptions A1-A3), and the analysis successfully bridges the gap between theoretical representation learning and practical CNN implementations. The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with well-organized sections that logically progress from theoretical foundations to practical implications, though some notation could be made more precise. The originality is significant, as the authors extend the scattering transform's theoretical framework to a broader class of convolutional architectures beyond pre-defined filter networks, providing a novel perspective on the geometric properties of deep representations. The significance of the results is substantial, as the stability guarantees directly relate to improved sample complexity and robustness to adversarial examples, which are critical concerns in deep learning applications.

Strengths:
- The theoretical framework provides a unified perspective on stability and invariance for both CKNs and standard CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations
- The paper establishes a clear connection between RKHS norm and generalization properties, demonstrating how stability controls sample complexity
- The extension to group-invariant representations (Section 4) is well-motivated and technically sound
- The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical exposition, making complex concepts accessible to the ML community

Weaknesses:
- The motivation for stability analysis could be strengthened; the current justification (line 56-57) is insufficient to fully justify the paper's main contribution, especially given the growing importance of stability in adversarial robustness [8]
- Notation confusion in assumption (A3) where κ is used for both the patch size parameter and as a function defining the kernel (Eq. 10)
- The displayed equation between lines 384-385 contains a redundant constraint (the second part 'and ∀v...' is identical to the first constraint due to kernel symmetry)
- The definition of the kernel mapping φ is missing from Eq. (10) where it should be introduced as ⟨φ(z), φ(z')⟩_H(K)
- The inequality in lines 427-428 should read |‖z‖ - ‖z'‖|² rather than |‖z‖ - ‖z‖|²

The paper meets all four quality criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims (Quality), clearly written with logical organization (Clarity), introduces a novel extension of previous work on scattering networks (Originality), and addresses a significant problem with clear implications for robust model design (Significance).

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to understanding the geometric properties of convolutional representations, with results that apply to both CKNs and standard CNNs. The stability guarantees provide valuable theoretical insights with practical implications for model robustness and generalization. The work builds upon and extends recent NIPS contributions on scattering networks and group invariance in a meaningful way.

Arguments against acceptance: The limited motivation for stability analysis weakens the paper's impact, and some technical details (notation, equation errors) require correction before the paper can be considered for publication. However, these issues are fixable and do not undermine the core theoretical contribution.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep convolutional representations, with results that advance the field beyond the scattering transform framework. The theoretical insights have clear implications for designing more robust deep learning models, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
