- Presents a multilayer convolutional kernel (RKHS) framework that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to analyze deep CNNs.  
- Formalizes the functional space underlying CNNs and shows this space admits stability guarantees.  
- Proves representations in this RKHS are stable to diffeomorphisms, with stability bounds decaying as pooling scales increase (Theorem 6).  
- Extends Mallat’s scattering-transform-style guarantees to more standard CNN architectures with smooth homogeneous activations.  
- Distinguishes kernel representations from predictive models and argues RKHS norms control stability and generalization, with implications for sample complexity.  
- Theoretical analysis is thorough, technically sound, and mathematically careful.  
- Successfully links abstract RKHS theory to practical CNN architectures, including CKNs and standard CNNs.  
- Fills a gap by extending stability theory beyond scattering networks to a broader class of CNNs.  
- Provides clear foundations for why certain CNNs exhibit desirable invariance properties.  
- Needs a more explicit discussion of the practical significance of the kernel formulation (e.g., for regularization, tighter generalization bounds, or architecture design).  
- Requires clearer articulation of novelty relative to Mairal et al.’s CKNs, specifying new theoretical insights beyond that work.  
- Should contextualize the “preserving signal information” claim for typical tasks like classification, where task-relevant features suffice.  
- Lacks comparison to invariant kernel methods (e.g., Burkhardt) despite focus on group invariance.  
- Would be strengthened by concrete examples or empirical comparisons showing how the proved stability compares to other stable representations (e.g., scattering networks).  
- Pro-acceptance: provides a rigorous theoretical framework, extends stability theory to standard CNNs, connects to practical architectures, and links RKHS norms to controllable stability/generalization.  
- Con-acceptance: practical significance of the kernel view is under-articulated, novelty over CKNs needs clarification, and information-preservation claims need contextualization.  
- Overall recommendation: technically strong and relevant to the community — recommend acceptance.
