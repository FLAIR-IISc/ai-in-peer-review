This paper explores the theoretical properties of deep convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and their connection to convolutional neural networks (CNNs). The authors demonstrate that CKNs can be designed to be near-invariant to groups of transformations and stable to the action of diffeomorphisms, which is a desirable property for many applications. The paper also shows that under certain assumptions, these properties can be extended to CNNs.

The main strength of the paper lies in its rigorous mathematical analysis, which provides a solid foundation for understanding the behavior of CKNs and CNNs. The authors carefully define the construction of the multilayer convolutional kernel and provide a detailed analysis of its properties, including stability to deformations and translation invariance. The connection to CNNs is also well-established, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how the properties of CKNs can be applied to CNNs.

However, the paper lacks empirical experiments to validate the theoretical analysis. It would be beneficial to include some experimental results to demonstrate the effectiveness of CKNs with appropriate settings in achieving invariance to transformations and diffeomorphisms. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the practical implications of the theoretical results.

Another potential area for exploration is the use of random projection or explicit kernel mapping with a finite number of Fourier series as alternative solutions for kernel approximation. The authors may want to consider investigating the effect of these alternatives on the stability of CKNs and CNNs.

Overall, the paper is well-written and technically sound, providing a valuable contribution to the understanding of CKNs and CNNs. With the addition of empirical experiments and further exploration of alternative kernel approximation methods, the paper could be even more comprehensive and impactful.
