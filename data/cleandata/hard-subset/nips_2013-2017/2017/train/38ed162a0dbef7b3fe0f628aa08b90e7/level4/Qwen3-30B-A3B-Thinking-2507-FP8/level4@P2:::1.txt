

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, focusing on their invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors extend the scattering transform framework [17] to a more general kernel-based approach by introducing a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16]. Their main contribution is establishing theoretical guarantees for the stability of representations under diffeomorphisms while preserving signal information, and demonstrating that these properties extend to a broad class of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with smooth homogeneous activation functions.

The paper makes a significant contribution by bridging kernel methods with deep learning theory. It builds upon Mallat's work on scattering networks [17] and CKNs [15, 16], but generalizes these approaches to provide a more comprehensive theoretical framework. The authors establish a connection between the RKHS norm of the representation and both stability properties and generalization performance, showing how the RKHS norm controls variations in prediction functions. This is particularly valuable for understanding why CNNs work well in practice, as it provides a principled approach to regularization and sample complexity. The paper also addresses group invariance by showing how to design patch extraction and pooling operators that commute with group actions, which generalizes previous work on rotation, translation, and reflection invariance [9, 17, 22].

The paper is technically sound and well-organized, with clear mathematical development and rigorous proofs. The authors demonstrate that their framework encompasses both CKNs and a broad class of CNNs, establishing that the stability properties derived for CKNs apply to these networks as well. The theoretical results are well-motivated and connect to practical concerns such as robustness to adversarial examples [8]. The mathematical tools are appropriately chosen and well-explained, with the paper providing sufficient detail for experts to follow the arguments.

Regarding quality, the paper provides strong theoretical foundations that are well-supported by mathematical analysis. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach, noting that while the representation does not preserve norm (unlike scattering transforms), this is mitigated for natural signals with low-frequency energy. The paper also acknowledges the practical considerations of discretization and kernel approximation, showing how the theoretical framework can be implemented in practice through convolutional kernel networks.

In terms of clarity, the paper is exceptionally well-written for a theoretical contribution. The mathematical notation is consistent and carefully defined, and the structure follows a logical progression from theoretical foundations to practical implications. The figures and examples (particularly the comparison of activation functions in Figure 2) help illustrate key concepts. The paper would benefit from slightly more intuitive explanations of the connection between the RKHS norm and practical generalization performance, but this is a minor point for a highly technical paper.

The originality of the work is substantial. While previous papers have studied invariance in CNNs [9, 22] or explored kernel-based approaches to deep learning [15, 25], this paper uniquely combines these threads to provide a unified theoretical framework that applies to both CKNs and standard CNNs. The extension of the scattering transform framework to this more general setting represents a meaningful advance over prior work.

The significance of this work is considerable. It provides a theoretical foundation for understanding why CNNs work well, with direct implications for regularization, generalization, and robustness. The connection between stability properties and generalization performance is particularly valuable for practitioners, as it suggests that stable representations (with small RKHS norm) may require fewer samples for good performance. This work could guide future research on designing more robust and efficient CNN architectures.

The main weakness of the paper is the lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the authors do not provide any experimental results to demonstrate the invariance and stability properties they claim. For instance, it would be valuable to see experiments showing how CKNs with appropriate design parameters maintain classification accuracy under various transformations and diffeomorphisms. This would strengthen the connection between theory and practice and provide concrete evidence of the claimed properties.

Additionally, the paper does not address alternative approaches to kernel approximation, such as random Fourier features or explicit kernel mappings. It would be valuable to discuss whether these methods could preserve the stability properties established in the paper, and if so, what trade-offs exist in terms of computational efficiency versus theoretical guarantees. This is particularly relevant for practical implementation of the theoretical framework.

To summarize, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the properties of deep convolutional representations. It provides rigorous analysis of invariance and stability under diffeomorphisms, connects these properties to generalization performance, and demonstrates their applicability to a broad class of CNNs. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important theoretical question in deep learning. The main limitation is the absence of empirical validation, which would strengthen the practical significance of the work. With empirical results, this paper would be an outstanding contribution to NIPS. As it stands, it is a strong theoretical paper that would benefit from additional experimental validation to fully realize its potential impact.
