This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations using the framework of multilayer positive definite kernels, generalizing the construction of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs). The central goal is to study and formally prove two desirable properties of these representations: near-invariance to group transformations and stability to the action of small diffeomorphisms, all while preserving the original signal information. The authors construct a sequence of feature maps in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) by successively applying patch extraction, a kernel mapping, and a linear pooling operator. They then derive a stability bound showing that the representation's sensitivity to diffeomorphisms is controlled by the Jacobian and magnitude of the deformation, with the invariance to pure translations increasing with the scale of the final pooling layer. This work connects directly to the CKN literature [15, 16] by providing a theoretical underpinning for its properties, and to the scattering transform [17] by extending similar stability analyses to a broader class of learnable architectures. Finally, the paper demonstrates that the functional space defined by this kernel construction contains a large class of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with smooth homogeneous activation functions, thereby extending the stability guarantees to these practical models.

**Strengths:**
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides a rigorous mathematical framework for analyzing the geometric properties of deep convolutional architectures. The proofs and derivations appear solid.
*   **Important Theoretical Results:** The main results—formally proving stability to diffeomorphisms (Theorem 6) and providing a construction for group invariance (Section 4)—are significant contributions. They offer a principled explanation for properties that are often empirically observed but poorly understood in standard CNNs.
*   **Bridge between Theory and Practice:** The connection established in Section 5 between the proposed RKHS and a class of CNNs is a key strength. It successfully translates the theoretical guarantees from the abstract kernel world to more conventional deep learning models, shedding light on the relationship between generalization (controlled by the RKHS norm) and stability.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Motivation and Novelty:** The main contribution is the development of an RKHS framework to analyze CNN properties. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of why this reformulation is beneficial beyond the theoretical analysis itself. For example, does this kernel perspective open up new avenues for deriving generalization bounds, designing principled regularizers, or creating more efficient algorithms through kernel approximations? Additionally, while building on [15, 16], the paper could more clearly delineate its novel contributions relative to the original CKN work.
*   **Relevance of Signal Preservation:** The authors repeatedly highlight that their representation preserves signal information (Lemma 1 and 2), allowing for reconstruction. It is unclear why this is a critical property for most common applications of DCNNs, such as classification or detection, where the primary goal is to abstract away task-irrelevant information. A comment clarifying the practical importance of this property in the context of discriminative tasks would be valuable.
*   **Connection to Related Work:** The work on group invariance in Section 4 develops an elegant G-equivariant layer. It would be beneficial to connect this to the broader literature on invariant kernels, such as the work by Haasdonk and Burkhardt [13], to better position the contribution within the existing body of research.
*   **Minor Clarification on Equivariance:** In Section 4, the paper constructs equivariant layers using specific patch, kernel, and pooling operators that commute with the group action. As a point of curiosity, it might be helpful to clarify if any operator that commutes with the group action would suffice to ensure the desired equivariance property for the layer.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper provides a strong, rigorous theoretical framework for understanding stability and invariance in deep convolutional models, which is a fundamental question in the field.
2.  The main results are significant, well-supported, and provide valuable insights into the geometry of the function spaces implemented by CNNs.
3.  The work successfully connects an abstract kernel construction to a practical class of CNNs, making the theoretical analysis relevant to the broader deep learning community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The practical implications of the kernel reformulation could be more clearly articulated, making the work feel more like a theoretical exercise than a framework with immediate downstream benefits.

Overall, the strengths of this paper in providing a rigorous and insightful theoretical analysis of DCNNs far outweigh its weaknesses. The questions raised are primarily for clarification and to further strengthen the paper's impact. I recommend acceptance.
