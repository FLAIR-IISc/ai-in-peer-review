- Proposes a multilayer convolutional kernel representation producing feature maps stable to C1 diffeomorphisms and increasingly translation-/group-invariant via pooling.  
- Each layer: patch extraction, pointwise RKHS kernel map, and Gaussian pooling, mirroring convolutional architectures.  
- Derives Lipschitz-type stability bounds: ‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖ with σn the last pooling scale.  
- Shows group-equivariance/invariance via group-covariant patch extraction and Haar-measure pooling.  
- Addresses discretization: subsampling with recoverability condition ek ≥ sk and deconvolution considerations.  
- Provides finite-dimensional kernel approximations (projections) that preserve non-expansiveness and thus stability.  
- Proves a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lie in the RKHS with explicit norm bounds, extending stability to these CNNs.  
- Builds on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and scattering transform, extending scattering-style stability to a general kernel framework.  
- Connects to group-equivariant CNN literature and invariant kernels, and to Lipschitz robustness/adversarial stability discussions.  
- Technical development is careful and sound, using commutator bounds, Schur’s test, and clear kernel-map assumptions (norm preservation, non-expansiveness).  
- Includes an initial anti-aliasing operator A0 to control high frequencies and ensure assumptions hold.  
- Translation-invariance term decays with pooling scale σn as expected; discretization/recovery lemmas clarify subsampling conditions.  
- Kernel projection approximations maintain non-expansiveness, supporting practical implementation via CKN-style projections.  
- Inclusion of CNNs into the RKHS is well-motivated but requires smooth homogeneous activations or smoothed ReLU variants.  
- Main limitation: constants C1, C2 and their dependence on κ, depth, and filters are not empirically calibrated.  
- Paper is clearly written with rigorous definitions; notation is heavy but consistent.  
- Would benefit from discussion on magnitude/tightness of constants, linear depth dependence in stability, and deconvolution stability under noise.  
- Novelty: extending scattering-style guarantees to a multilayer kernel framework that subsumes CKNs and certain CNNs is nontrivial and original.  
- Group-equivariant pooling is standard, but its integration and analysis in this kernel setting adds value.  
- Significance: offers theoretical insight into invariance/stability and suggests RKHS-norm regularization and architectural guidance.  
- Practical relevance is untested due to lack of empirical validation and unclear tightness of bounds.  
- Strength: rigorous explicit stability and invariance bounds unifying CKNs and a class of CNNs in an RKHS framework.  
- Strength: pragmatic treatment of discretization and kernel approximation preserving stability.  
- Strength: general group-equivariant/invariant construction via Haar pooling.  
- Weakness: no experiments to validate theoretical claims, constants, or practical gains (e.g., effect of σn or depth).  
- Weakness: reliance on smooth homogeneous activations limits direct applicability to standard ReLU networks.  
- Weakness: stability bound grows linearly with depth; practical implications not analyzed.  
- Weakness: deconvolution-based recovery may be ill-conditioned and robustness to noise is not analyzed.  
- Questions for authors: can RFF, Nyström, or other finite maps be used instead of projections, and under what conditions do stability guarantees hold?  
- Questions: can you empirically estimate C1 and C2 for typical kernels and show dependence on depth n?  
- Questions: how stable is deconvolution recovery under noise/model mismatch, and how does that affect post-subsampling stability?  
- Questions: are there approximation/error bounds when using standard non-smooth ReLU versus smoothed ReLU in practice?  
- Questions: how sensitive are results when ek < sk or when pooling/stride choices deviate from assumptions?  
- Suggestions: add experiments with CKNs/CNNs demonstrating stability to diffeomorphisms, invariance vs σn, and compare kernel approximations (projection, Nyström, RFF).  
- Suggestions: report how RKHS norm control correlates with generalization and stability empirically.  
- Suggestions: discuss constants and depth dependence to guide architectural choices.  
- Pros for acceptance: solid theoretical extension of scattering guarantees to CKNs/CNNs, clear RKHS-based framework linking stability, invariance, and generalization.  
- Cons for acceptance: no empirical validation and restrictive assumptions (smooth activations, ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2, anti-aliasing) may limit applicability.
