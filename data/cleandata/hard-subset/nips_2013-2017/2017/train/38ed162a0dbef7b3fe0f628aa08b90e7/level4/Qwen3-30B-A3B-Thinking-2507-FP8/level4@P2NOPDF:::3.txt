

The paper presents a well-written and rigorous analysis demonstrating how specific functional spaces implemented by deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs) can be characterized through multilayer kernels within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). It effectively establishes key architectural properties, including near invariance to group transformations and stability under diffeomorphic deformations. These contributions are significant for theoretical understanding of DCNNs, and I recommend acceptance pending the following clarifications:

1.  **Novelty and Utility of RKHS Reformulation:** The core contribution appears to be a reformulation of DCNN architecture within RKHS. To strengthen the paper, the authors should explicitly articulate *why* this reformulation is beneficial. Specifically, they should address: (a) whether the kernel formulation enables novel generalization bounds or theoretical insights not accessible via standard DCNN analysis; (b) potential computational advantages (e.g., leveraging kernel approximation techniques for efficiency). Furthermore, the novelty must be clearly distinguished from Mairal et al.'s "Convolutional Kernel Networks" (CVPR 2014). The paper should explicitly state how this work differs in approach, scope, or theoretical grounding from that prior work.

2.  **"Preserving Signal Information" Clarification:** The emphasis on "not losing signal information" requires justification. DCNNs are typically deployed for task-specific objectives (e.g., image classification), where only task-relevant features matter, not the full signal reconstruction. The authors should clarify *why* preserving the entire signal is crucial for their theoretical framework or the intended applications, especially given the common practice of discarding irrelevant information in downstream tasks.

3.  **Connection to Invariant Kernels:** The discussion of group invariance warrants a connection to existing work on invariant kernels. Specifically, the authors should address whether their approach relates to or builds upon the invariant kernel framework pioneered by Burkhardt (e.g., *Invariant Kernel Methods*), and if so, how their method differs or complements it.

4.  **Generality of Commuting Operators:** The paper defines patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with group actions to ensure equivariance. A natural question arises: would *any* operator commuting with all group elements guarantee the equivariance property for DCNNs under the group transformations considered? The authors should clarify whether their specific choice of operators (patch, pooling, kernel maps) is necessary or if broader classes of commuting operators suffice, and why.

The paper's theoretical contribution is valuable, but the above points are essential for a comprehensive presentation. Addressing them will significantly enhance the paper's impact by clarifying its novelty, practical relevance, and theoretical depth. The proposed reformulation is promising, but its significance hinges on these clarifications. The authors should explicitly state the *added value* of the RKHS perspective beyond a mere rephrasing of existing DCNN structure. The connection to Mairal et al. must be unambiguous to establish the work's position in the literature. The justification for signal preservation should be grounded in the specific theoretical goals or applications the paper targets. Finally, the discussion of commuting operators should explicitly address the scope of operators that ensure equivariance. With these revisions, the paper will make a strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of DCNNs.
