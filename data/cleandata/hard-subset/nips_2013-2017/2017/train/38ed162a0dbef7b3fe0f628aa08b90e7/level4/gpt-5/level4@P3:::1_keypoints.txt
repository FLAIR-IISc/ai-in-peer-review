- Develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation that builds near-invariance to groups via pooling and is Lipschitz-stable to diffeomorphic deformations while preserving signal information.  
- Generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains; each layer does patch extraction, homogeneous dot-product kernel mapping, and Gaussian pooling.  
- Proves signal recoverability from sampled feature maps, making the construction discretization-friendly.  
- Provides sufficient conditions for the representation to be non-expansive and derives a stability bound: deviation scales with the deformation Jacobian and translation magnitude (the latter controlled by final pooling scale σn).  
- Extends pooling and equivariance to general locally compact groups to obtain global invariance at the final layer using intermediate pooling for stability.  
- Shows a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lie in the induced RKHS, so the RKHS norm controls stability and generalization.  
- Theoretical development is careful and technically sound; Theorem 6 gives an interpretable depth vs. pooling trade-off and signal-preservation lemmas support discretization.  
- The CNN-to-RKHS link (Proposition 7) is well-motivated, enabling Lipschitz stability of predictions via RKHS norm control.  
- Assumptions A1–A3 are explicit; homogeneous dot-product kernels ensure norm preservation and non-expansiveness; Gaussian pooling enables clean analytic bounds.  
- Main limitation: no empirical validation—simple synthetic experiments would clarify constants, depth dependence, and discretized/approximate behavior.  
- Discussion of norm loss from pooling is reasonable but would benefit from quantifying energy preservation (e.g., for 1/f^2 spectra) and its effect on stability bound magnitudes.  
- Clarity is good overall; operator pipeline and commutator-based stability analysis are presented clearly with proofs in the appendix.  
- Suggested clarifications: make constants C1 and C2 explicit in terms of κ, d, kernel h, and depth.  
- Suggested clarifications: quantify deconvolution noise when recovering x̄k−1 from x̄k and give practical stable inversion scale ranges.  
- Suggested clarifications: explicitly state how projection/approximation error propagates through layers and adds to stability bounds (e.g., additive per layer).  
- Minor typos and grammatical fixes would improve readability.  
- Originality: extends scattering invariance and deformation stability to a broader convolutional-kernel setting and connects CNNs to the RKHS framework; group-equivariant pooling and stability transfer are novel extensions.  
- Significance: provides principled tools for reasoning about invariance, stability, and generalization that can inform architecture design and RKHS-based regularization; empirical support would increase impact.  
- Question: can random feature maps or JL-type projections replace orthogonal projections, and can Theorem 6 be extended with an accumulated approximation-error term?  
- Question: can discretization constants (ek ≥ sk) and deconvolution stability be made concrete for 2D images and standard CNN hyperparameters?  
- Question: how closely do smoothed ReLUs approximate standard ReLUs in practice, and do RKHS norm bounds remain informative?  
- Question: is the linear dependence on depth in Theorem 6 pessimistic—are refined bounds possible with geometrically growing pooling scales or limited filter support?  
- Pros: solid theoretical contribution unifying scattering and CKN analyses; clear continuous-to-discrete path and signal-preservation guarantees; explicit group-equivariant construction and RKHS link to CNNs.  
- Cons: lacks empirical validation; stability under non-orthogonal kernel approximations (e.g., random features) not analyzed; some constants and assumptions need clearer interpretation.  
- Overall: strong theoretical paper, lean toward acceptance, with a strong recommendation to add brief empirical experiments and discuss stability under practical finite-dimensional approximations.
