

This paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) through the lens of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15,16] and demonstrates how this framework characterizes the invariance and stability properties of CNN representations. Building upon Mallat's scattering transform [5,17], the work extends theoretical guarantees to more standard CNN architectures, providing formal stability bounds under diffeomorphic deformations and invariance to group transformations.

The paper's main contribution lies in formalizing the functional space underlying CNNs and establishing that this space admits stability guarantees. The authors prove that representations in this space are stable to diffeomorphisms, with stability bounds decaying as pooling scales increase (Theorem 6). They further demonstrate that the framework encompasses a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, thereby extending theoretical results to standard architectures beyond CKNs. The distinction between the kernel representation and predictive models is well-motivated, showing how RKHS norms control both stability and generalization, with potential implications for sample complexity.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical analysis is thorough and technically sound, with careful attention to mathematical details.
2. The paper successfully bridges theoretical insights with practical CNN architectures, establishing meaningful connections between abstract functional spaces and real-world models.
3. The extension of stability theory to standard CNNs addresses a significant gap in the literature, complementing the more limited scope of scattering networks.
4. The work provides clear theoretical foundations for understanding why certain CNN architectures achieve desirable invariance properties.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
1. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the practical significance of the kernel formulation. While the authors note that RKHS norms control stability and generalization, it would be valuable to more clearly articulate how this framework enables new insights or practical advantages (e.g., for developing improved regularization strategies, deriving tighter generalization bounds, or designing more stable architectures). A brief discussion of potential applications of these theoretical insights would strengthen the paper's significance.

2. The novelty relative to Mairal et al.'s "Convolutional Kernel Networks" [16] requires clearer articulation. While the paper acknowledges that CKNs represent a specific implementation of the proposed framework, it would be helpful to explicitly state what new theoretical insights the current work provides beyond the CKN framework. Specifically, the authors should better distinguish how the current work extends the CKN approach by providing stability guarantees applicable to a broader class of CNNs.

3. The emphasis on "preserving signal information" should be contextualized for typical CNN applications. For classification tasks, only task-relevant features matter rather than the complete signal. The authors should clarify how information preservation relates to task performance, particularly for classification where irrelevant signal components may be discarded without affecting accuracy.

4. The connection to invariant kernel methods (e.g., Burkhardt [13]) is not addressed. Given the paper's focus on group invariance, a comparison with existing invariant kernel approaches would provide valuable context and help position the work within the broader literature.

5. The claim that "the representation is stable" would be strengthened by concrete examples or comparisons with existing stable representations. While the theoretical bounds are well-established, illustrating how the stability properties compare to those of other approaches (e.g., scattering networks) would provide additional context for readers.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of CNN properties. The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and makes a valuable addition to the literature on the theoretical foundations of deep learning. The theoretical insights are particularly relevant to the NIPS community's interest in understanding the mathematical properties of deep architectures.

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- Provides rigorous theoretical framework for understanding CNN properties
- Extends stability theory to a broader class of CNNs beyond scattering networks
- Connects theoretical insights to practical architectures (CKNs and standard CNNs)
- Demonstrates how stability can be controlled via RKHS norms, with implications for generalization

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Could better articulate the practical significance of the kernel formulation
- Needs clearer differentiation from prior work (especially CKNs)
- Should contextualize information preservation for typical CNN applications

Given the technical quality of the work, its theoretical contribution to understanding CNN properties, and its relevance to the NIPS community, I recommend acceptance.
