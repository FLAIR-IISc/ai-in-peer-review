This paper investigates key properties of deep Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), demonstrating that they can be designed to be nearly invariant to transformation groups and stable to the action of diffeomorphisms. These properties are achieved through the careful design of the patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators. Furthermore, the authors show that under certain assumptions, these properties also apply to standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).

In general, the paper is well-written and technically sound. Its primary weakness is the lack of empirical experiments to validate the theoretical analysis. The paper would be significantly strengthened by experimental results demonstrating that CKNs with appropriate settings achieve the claimed invariance to transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms.

Additionally, could alternative kernel approximation methods, such as random projections or explicit kernel mappings using a finite Fourier series, be considered? If so, what effect would these methods have on the stability guarantees presented in the paper?
