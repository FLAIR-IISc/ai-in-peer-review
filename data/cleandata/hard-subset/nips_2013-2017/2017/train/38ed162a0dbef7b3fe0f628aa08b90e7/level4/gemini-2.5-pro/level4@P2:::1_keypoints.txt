- Presents a theoretical analysis of multilayer convolutional kernels and associated RKHS, proving stability to small diffeomorphisms and near-invariance to transformation groups, with results extending to many CNNs using smooth homogeneous activations.  
- Extends prior CKN and scattering-transform work, generalizing Mallat’s harmonic analysis to learnable architectures.  
- Technical quality is high: rigorous proofs, clear use of functional analysis and signal-processing tools, and an elegant multilayer kernel construction.  
- Significant and original: addresses why CNNs are robust to small input transformations and shows the RKHS contains a class of practical CNNs, increasing the results’ relevance.  
- Clear and well-organized exposition, moving logically from continuous kernel definition to discretization and connection with CNNs.  
- Main weakness: complete absence of empirical validation; practical relevance and tightness of bounds remain speculative.  
- Suggested experiments: numerically verify Theorem 6, test the σn (final pooling scale) vs. translation-invariance trade-off, and compare a CKN per the paper’s principles with a standard CNN on distorted tasks (e.g., elastic MNIST).  
- Question for authors: can large-scale kernel approximations (random Fourier features, Nyström) be incorporated, and would they preserve non-expansiveness (Assumption A2) and the stability bounds in Theorem 6?  
- Pros for acceptance: novel rigorous framework, successful extension of scattering stability to learnable models, addresses a fundamental theoretical question, and is technically strong.  
- Con for acceptance: lack of experiments makes it hard to assess practical impact and bound tightness.  
- Recommendation: strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS based on theoretical strength and clarity, with experiments suggested for future work.
