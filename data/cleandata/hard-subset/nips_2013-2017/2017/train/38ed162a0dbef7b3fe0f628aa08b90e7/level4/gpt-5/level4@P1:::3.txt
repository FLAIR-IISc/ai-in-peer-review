Summary
This paper builds a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals on continuous domains and studies its geometry. Each layer applies patch extraction, a kernel mapping to an RKHS via homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. The authors prove (i) a signal preservation property across layers under suitable sampling (and an analogous discrete result), (ii) stability to C1 diffeomorphisms with a bound that scales linearly with deformation size and inversely with the final pooling scale for translations, and (iii) a mechanism for global invariance to general transformation groups via group-covariant patch extraction and pooling. They further show that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, so predictive models defined in that space inherit the same stability via RKHS norm control. They also argue that common finite-dimensional kernel approximations (CKNs) preserve non-expansiveness and thus the stability conclusions.

Quality
The paper is technically careful and largely rigorous, with clear assumptions (A1–A3), precise operator-norm bounds, and a clean diffeomorphism stability theorem. The translation of stability from representation to prediction via the RKHS norm is well motivated. Some constants and depth dependencies could be discussed more (e.g., the (1 + n) factor and dependence on κ), but the statements are sound given the stated assumptions.

Clarity
The exposition is clear and well organized. The continuous-to-discrete discussion and the connection to CKNs/CNNs are helpful. A few points below would benefit from explicit clarification to better position the contribution.

Originality
The work extends the scattering framework to a more general multilayer kernel construction that encompasses CKNs and certain CNNs, and provides stability/invariance guarantees in that setting. While the analysis draws on prior techniques from scattering, the generality of the kernel framework and the explicit inclusion of CNNs in the induced RKHS appear to be the main novelty. Please delineate more sharply what is new relative to prior CKN work and group-invariant kernels.

Significance
The kernel viewpoint can help separate representation and prediction, clarify when stability/invariance arise, and suggest norm-based regularization that simultaneously controls generalization and stability. The results are primarily theoretical but potentially impactful for understanding and designing robust architectures.

Strengths
- Rigorous stability bounds to diffeomorphisms with clear dependence on deformation size and pooling scale; intuitive translation-invariance scaling with 1/σn.
- A general multilayer kernel construction that subsumes CKNs and captures a class of CNNs via RKHS inclusion, providing a unified analysis framework.
- Careful treatment of discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations while preserving non-expansiveness and stability.
- Group-equivariant construction yielding global invariance on compact groups through the final pooling layer.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Usefulness of the RKHS reformulation: Please expand on why this viewpoint is practically valuable beyond conceptual unification. For example:
  - Can it lead to explicit generalization bounds or sample complexity results in terms of RKHS norms for the CNN class you embed?
  - Does the kernel formulation enable computational benefits via standard kernel approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features, low-rank factorization), and how does this compare to conventional CNN training?
  - Beyond a reformulation, in what concrete ways does the analysis suggest new design or regularization principles for CNNs?
- Novelty relative to CKNs: The representation and discretization closely follow CKNs. Please make the incremental contributions explicit:
  - What new stability/invariance results are obtained that were not established in the original CKN work?
  - Is the inclusion of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations in the induced RKHS (Proposition 7) the primary new theoretical bridge?
  - How does your functional-space analysis differ from or extend kernel analyses of deep networks in prior work (e.g., Montavon et al., Daniely et al.)?
- “Not losing signal information”: You emphasize recoverability of signals (Lemmas 1–2). For task-driven learning (e.g., classification), full invertibility is not obviously necessary. Please motivate why signal preservation is an important design criterion here. For instance:
  - Is invertibility primarily used to argue that the representation is not discarding potentially task-relevant information while still enabling stability?
  - Do your stability/generalization arguments materially rely on this property, or is it ancillary?
- Relation to invariant kernels: Please clarify connections to invariant kernel methods (e.g., Haasdonk & Burkhardt). How does your group pooling and induced RKHS compare to constructing kernels by integrating over group orbits? Are there equivalences, or is your framework strictly more/less general?
- Operators commuting with group actions: You implement patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling that commute with Lg to ensure equivariance. In principle, any bounded linear operator that commutes with all Lg is G-equivariant. Could you comment on what additional properties (e.g., non-expansiveness, locality, smoothness) are required to retain your stability results, and whether more general commuting operators could be substituted without breaking the theory?

Minor suggestions
- Clarify how constants C1 and C2 depend on the pooling kernel and κ, and discuss practical implications for depth and patch sizes.
- Briefly discuss the impact of boundary effects introduced by finite support and how they affect the theorems (even qualitatively).
- When claiming improved sample complexity via stability, it would help to point to or sketch a concrete margin-based bound in your setting.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, well-presented theory connecting multilayer kernels, CKNs, and a class of CNNs under a common RKHS framework.
- Meaningful stability and invariance guarantees with clear architectural levers (pooling scale, group pooling) and compatibility with practical approximations.
- Potential to inform regularization and architecture design through norm-based control.

Arguments against acceptance
- The practical benefits of the reformulation are under-explained, and novelty over prior CKN and invariant kernel work could be articulated more clearly.
- Limited discussion of empirical implications or validation, and some constants/depth dependencies remain abstract.

Overall recommendation: accept, contingent on addressing the clarification points above.
