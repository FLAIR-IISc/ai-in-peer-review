This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), establishing that they can achieve near-invariance to transformation groups and stability to the action of diffeomorphisms. The authors demonstrate that these desirable properties are contingent upon the careful design of the patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators. Furthermore, the paper shows that under certain assumptions, these theoretical guarantees can be extended from CKNs to conventional Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the technical analysis appears sound. A significant limitation, however, is the absence of empirical validation for the theoretical claims. The analysis is purely theoretical, and the conclusions would be substantially more compelling if supported by experimental results. The inclusion of experiments demonstrating that appropriately configured CKNs are indeed nearly invariant to specific transformations and stable under diffeomorphisms would greatly strengthen the paper's contribution.

Additionally, I have a question regarding the kernel mapping operator. The authors could clarify whether alternative methods for kernel approximation, such as random projections or explicit kernel maps using a finite number of Fourier features, could be employed. If such methods are applicable, it would be valuable to understand how their use would impact the stability and invariance properties established in the paper.
