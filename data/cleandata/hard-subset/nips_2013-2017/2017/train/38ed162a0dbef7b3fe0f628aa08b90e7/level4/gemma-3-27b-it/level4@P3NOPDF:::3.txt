## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

This paper presents a compelling and rigorous analysis of Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (DCNNs), successfully demonstrating how their functionality can be described through the lens of multilayer kernels and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors establish important theoretical properties of this framework, specifically (almost) invariance to group transformations and stability under diffeomorphic transformations. Overall, I recommend acceptance, contingent upon addressing the comments detailed below.

The core contribution, as I understand it, is a reformulation of the DCNN architecture within the RKHS framework. While technically sound and well-executed, the paper would benefit significantly from a more detailed discussion of the *practical* implications of this reformulation. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on why adopting this kernel-based perspective is beneficial. Potential avenues to explore include: could this formulation lead to tighter generalization bounds? Does it open the door to leveraging established kernel methods for improved performance or efficiency? Is there potential for computational advantages through the utilization of standard kernel approximation techniques? A more thorough exploration of these aspects would strengthen the motivation for this work.

Furthermore, it is important to clearly articulate the novelty of this approach beyond simply re-framing DCNNs within an RKHS context. While the technical connection is established, the paper needs to emphasize what *new* insights or capabilities arise from this perspective. A critical point of comparison is the related work "Convolutional Kernel Networks" (Mairal et al.). The authors should clearly delineate the differences and advantages of their approach compared to this prior work. A direct comparison – even a brief one – would be highly valuable.

The authors repeatedly emphasize the importance of “not losing signal information.” However, this statement requires further clarification. DCNNs are rarely utilized for perfect signal reconstruction; they are typically employed for discriminative tasks such as image classification. In such scenarios, preserving only task-relevant information is sufficient, and indeed, often desirable. The authors should acknowledge this and explain why maintaining *all* signal information is considered crucial within their framework. Is this a requirement for analyzing the theoretical properties they derive, or is there a more general principle at play? 

Regarding the demonstrated group invariance, I wonder if there is a connection to the established work on invariant kernels, particularly that of Hans Burkhardt. Exploring this relationship could provide valuable context and potentially reveal synergies between the two approaches. A brief discussion would be informative.

Finally, a more theoretical curiosity: the authors define patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action, leading to equivariance. It raises the question – and the authors might consider briefly addressing this – whether *any* operator that commutes with all elements of the group would guarantee the desired equivariance property. While this may not represent a significant contribution, acknowledging this broader perspective would demonstrate a thorough understanding of the underlying principles.

In summary, this is a well-written and theoretically solid paper. Addressing the points raised above – specifically, clarifying the benefits and novelty of the RKHS reformulation, and providing a more nuanced discussion of signal preservation – would significantly strengthen the contribution and warrant a strong acceptance.
