This paper presents a theoretical framework for analyzing deep convolutional representations by defining a multilayer convolutional kernel. This construction is shown to encompass a class of deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The authors then leverage this reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) perspective to prove two key properties of the representation: near-invariance to group transformations and stability to small diffeomorphisms, while also preserving signal information. The work extends ideas from Convolutional Kernel Networks and the stability analysis of scattering transforms to a broader and more common class of deep learning architectures.

The paper is well-written, technically rigorous, and its results are well-supported. The theoretical framework it provides for understanding the properties of CNNs is a valuable contribution to the field. In particular, the stability and invariance proofs extend important theoretical guarantees to architectures that are more representative of modern CNNs. I recommend this paper for acceptance, though I believe it could be further strengthened if the authors address the following points:

*   **Significance and Novelty of the RKHS Reformulation:** The paper's primary contribution appears to be the reformulation of CNN architectures within an RKHS framework. While this is a valuable theoretical exercise, the paper could be strengthened by explicitly discussing the practical or future theoretical benefits of this perspective. For instance, could this kernel formulation lead to new insights on generalization bounds for CNNs? Does it suggest more computationally efficient training methods by leveraging standard kernel approximation techniques? A more detailed discussion on the utility of this framework would help clarify its significance beyond the stability analysis presented.

*   **Clarification of Novelty Relative to Prior Work:** The paper builds heavily on Mairal et al.'s "Convolutional Kernel Networks" (CKN). The manuscript would benefit from a clearer and more explicit statement of its novel contributions over this prior work. Beyond extending the CKN framework to establish stability and invariance properties, are there other fundamental novelties in the approach?

*   **Motivation for Preserving Signal Information:** The authors emphasize the preservation of signal information as a key property of their representation. This point warrants further discussion. For many common applications of DCNNs, such as image classification, the goal is precisely to discard information that is irrelevant to the task. Why is preserving the full signal information considered a desirable property in this context? Clarifying this would help readers understand the motivation, especially when the end goal is not signal reconstruction.

*   **Connection to Invariant Kernels:** In the discussion on group invariance, it would be beneficial to connect the proposed approach to the broader literature on invariant kernels, for example, the work by Haasdonk and Burkhardt. Situating the work within this established context would provide a more complete picture of its relationship to prior art.

*   **A Note on Group Equivariance (Minor Point):** This is more a point of curiosity. The paper constructs patch, pooling, and kernel mapping operators that commute with the group action to ensure equivariance. As a general principle, would *any* operator that commutes with all group elements suffice to guarantee the equivariance of the resulting DCNN layers? A brief comment on this could add to the completeness of the theoretical discussion.

In summary, this is a strong paper that provides an elegant framework and significant theoretical results for understanding deep convolutional representations. Addressing the points above would further improve the manuscript's clarity and impact.
