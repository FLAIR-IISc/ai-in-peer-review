This paper presents a rigorous and well-written theoretical framework for Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (DCNNs), successfully demonstrating how the functional spaces they implement can be described in terms of multilayer kernels. A key strength of the work is the theoretical analysis showing that the proposed architecture possesses desirable properties, such as near-invariance to group transformations and stability to small diffeomorphic transformations. The mathematical formalism is elegant and provides a valuable new perspective on the operations within DCNNs.

Despite the strengths of the theoretical treatment, the paper could be significantly improved by addressing the following points regarding its motivation, novelty, and broader impact.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Clarification of Novelty and Practical Utility:** The main contribution appears to be a reformulation of the DCNN architecture within the language of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). While this is a valuable theoretical exercise, the paper should more explicitly articulate why this reformulation is useful. What new possibilities or insights does it unlock? For instance:
    *   Does the kernel formulation lead to new or tighter generalization bounds for DCNNs?
    *   Does it suggest computationally more efficient implementation strategies, perhaps by leveraging standard techniques for kernel approximation?
    *   Is the primary novelty of the approach confined to this reformulation, or does it enable capabilities beyond what is achievable with standard DCNNs?

    Furthermore, the novelty with respect to prior work needs to be more clearly established. Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed comparison with the "Convolutional Kernel Networks" paper by Mairal et al. to precisely delineate this paper's unique contributions.

2.  **Motivation for Preserving Signal Information:** The authors repeatedly stress the importance of the architecture's ability to avoid "losing signal information." The motivation for this goal is unclear, especially in the context of the tasks where DCNNs are most commonly applied, such as image classification. In these discriminative settings, the objective is typically to discard task-irrelevant information (e.g., background, specific lighting conditions) and preserve only the features necessary for the task. Complete signal preservation seems more aligned with reconstruction tasks. The authors should comment on why preserving the full signal is a desirable property for a broader range of applications and clarify if their framework is primarily intended for a specific class of problems.

**Minor Comments and Questions:**

1.  **Connection to Invariant Kernels:** The discussion of group invariance is interesting. Is there any connection between the proposed approach and the established literature on invariant kernels, such as the work by Hans Burkhardt? A brief discussion of the relationship to this line of research would help place the work in a broader context.

2.  **Generalization of the Equivariance Property:** As a point of curiosity, the paper defines specific patch, pooling, and kernel maps that commute with the group action to ensure equivariance. Would any operator that commutes with all elements of the transformation group suffice to ensure the equivariance property of the overall DCNN architecture? A brief comment on the generality of this principle would be insightful.

**Recommendation:**

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that provides a valuable new perspective on DCNNs. I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the points above. Clarifying the practical implications and novelty of the RKHS reformulation, as well as the motivation for signal preservation, would substantially strengthen the paper's impact and make its contributions more accessible to the wider machine learning community.
