- Paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations via multilayer positive definite kernels, generalizing Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs).
- Main goals: prove near-invariance to group transformations and stability to small diffeomorphisms while preserving signal information.
- Constructs RKHS feature maps by successive patch extraction, kernel mapping, and linear pooling.
- Derives a stability bound showing sensitivity to diffeomorphisms is controlled by the deformation Jacobian and magnitude; translation invariance grows with final pooling scale.
- Connects to CKN literature and extends scattering-transform-style stability analyses to learnable architectures.
- Shows the kernel functional space includes a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, extending stability guarantees to practical models.
- Strength: technically rigorous, well-written, with solid proofs and derivations.
- Strength: significant theoretical results (stability—Theorem 6; group invariance—Section 4).
- Strength: bridges theory and practice by linking RKHS construction to CNNs and relating RKHS norm to generalization and stability.
- Weakness: motivation and novelty need clearer justification of practical benefits from the RKHS reformulation and differentiation from prior CKN work.
- Weakness: unclear practical importance of preserving full signal information for discriminative tasks; needs clarification.
- Weakness: should better situate the group-invariance layer within existing invariant-kernel literature (e.g., Haasdonk & Burkhardt).
- Weakness: clarify whether any operator commuting with the group action suffices for equivariance.
- For acceptance: provides a strong theoretical framework, significant results, and relevant connections to CNNs.
- Against acceptance: practical implications of the kernel reformulation are not clearly articulated, risking perception as mainly theoretical.
- Overall recommendation: accept.
