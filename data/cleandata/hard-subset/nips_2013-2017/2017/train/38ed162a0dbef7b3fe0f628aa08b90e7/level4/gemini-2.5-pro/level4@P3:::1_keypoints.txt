- Presents a multilayer convolutional kernel and associated RKHS that generalizes CKNs and enables analysis of deep convolutional representations.  
- Proves stability to C1-diffeomorphisms and near-invariance to translations, with a formal bound (Theorem 6) relating stability to network structure and final pooling scale.  
- Extends scattering-transform style results to a broader setting beyond scattering networks.  
- Establishes a formal connection showing CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activations lie in the constructed RKHS, allowing stability guarantees to transfer to those CNNs.  
- Significant theoretical contribution addressing geometric properties and stability of deep networks, important for robustness understanding and design.  
- Provides a general and elegant kernel/RKHS framework that bridges kernel methods (CKNs) and mainstream CNNs (Section 5).  
- Paper is clear, rigorous, well-organized, with solid mathematical constructions and proofs.  
- Major weakness: complete absence of empirical validation to test the practical relevance and tightness of theoretical bounds.  
- Suggest empirically measuring stability to controlled diffeomorphisms/translations and evaluating effects of architectural choices (pooling scale, patch size).  
- Suggest testing whether RKHS-norm regularization of CNNs improves robustness in practice.  
- Asks whether other kernel approximation methods (random Fourier features, Nyström) can be integrated and how they affect stability bounds.  
- Requests elaboration on practical regularization strategies to control the recursive CNN RKHS norm from Proposition 7.  
- Requests discussion on the practical gap between required smooth homogeneous activations and the commonly used non-smooth ReLU, and implications for applying results to real CNNs.  
- Overall: strong and novel theoretical work with compelling insights and connections, but limited impact due to missing experimental support.
