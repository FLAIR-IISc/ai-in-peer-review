Summary
This paper develops a multilayer kernel representation for signals that mirrors the structure of deep convolutional networks (CNNs), and studies its geometry in an RKHS. Each layer applies patch extraction, a norm-preserving kernel map induced by a homogeneous dot-product kernel, and Gaussian pooling. The authors prove stability of the representation to C1-diffeomorphisms and near-invariance to translations, with a bound that depends linearly on the deformation (via ∥∇τ∥∞) and on the translation magnitude scaled by the last pooling size (∥τ∥∞/σn). They also show how to obtain global invariance to general locally compact groups by designing layers (patch, kernel map, pooling) that commute with the group action. Discretization and kernel-approximation steps (as in convolutional kernel networks, CKNs) are discussed, with non-expansive projections preserving the stability analysis. Finally, they show that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies within the RKHS of the multilayer kernel, so that predictor stability and generalization can be controlled via the RKHS norm.

Relation to prior work
This work generalizes ideas from the scattering transform (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat) by proving Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms for a more general, learnable multilayer kernel/CNN class, rather than fixed wavelets. It extends CKNs (Mairal et al.) from a practical finite-dimensional approximation to a continuous-domain kernel construction with formal stability and group-invariance guarantees, and connects these guarantees to predictors through RKHS norms. It relates to invariant kernels (Haasdonk & Burkhardt) and group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling) by providing a kernel/RKHS perspective and stability to local deformations alongside global group invariance.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main stability result is derived via commutator bounds and Schur’s test, borrowing key lemmas from Mallat and extending them to shifted integral operators. Assumptions (A1–A3) are clearly stated (norm preservation/non-expansiveness of layer kernels; patch radius proportional to pooling scale). The translation-invariance term decays as 1/σn, aligning with intuition.
- Completeness: The theory covers continuous signals, discretization, and kernel approximations, and connects to CNN predictors through Proposition 7. Proofs are deferred but the roadmap is convincing.
- Caveats: “Signal preservation” in discrete layers relies on deconvolution after Gaussian pooling; this can be ill-conditioned unless scales are small relative to subsampling, and practical robustness deserves discussion. Norm non-preservation due to pooling is acknowledged; the suggested remedy (a final normalized kernel map) is reasonable but could be illustrated empirically. The smooth homogeneous activation requirement excludes standard ReLU; the smoothed variants covered by Lemma B.2 should be instantiated with concrete choices and constants.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. Suggestions:
- State the main stability theorem (Theorem 6) and its implications earlier, with a brief proof sketch before the detailed operator analysis.
- Give explicit examples of admissible kernels and activations satisfying (A1–A2) and Lemma B.2, with typical values of C1, C2.
- Clarify the conditioning of the deconvolution step in Lemma 2 and practical parameter ranges (ek, sk, σk).
- Minor typos: “loosing” → losing; “discusssed”; some formatting artifacts in references.

Originality
The contribution goes beyond a reformulation:
- New stability and near-invariance results for a multilayer kernel architecture that subsumes CKNs and a class of CNNs, extending scattering-style guarantees to learned (non-wavelet) features.
- A general group-equivariant kernel layer construction with global invariance at the last layer and stability to local deformations in earlier layers.
- Embedding of CNN predictors with smooth homogeneous activations into the RKHS, linking RKHS norm to Lipschitz stability and potential generalization control.

Significance
The work provides principled tools to analyze invariance, stability, and generalization of deep convolutional representations. The RKHS viewpoint cleanly separates representation from prediction and suggests norm-based regularization for stability and sample complexity. While primarily theoretical, the results are relevant to both kernelized and standard CNNs and complement group-equivariant architectures. Some empirical illustration (even small-scale) would further strengthen impact.

Pros
- Rigorous and clear stability analysis with interpretable bounds (∥∇τ∥∞, ∥τ∥∞/σn).
- Unifying RKHS framework that includes CKNs and a class of CNNs.
- General treatment of group equivariance and global invariance.
- Bridges representation stability and predictor generalization via RKHS norms.

Cons
- Practical aspects of “signal preservation” via deconvolution may be fragile; discuss numerical stability.
- The activation class excludes vanilla ReLU; provide concrete smooth surrogates and their impact.
- Limited empirical validation; examples illustrating constants, depth dependence, and discretization effects would help.
- Dependence on kernel/patch parameters (κ, σk) and depth n in constants is only qualitatively discussed.

Requests for clarification and suggestions
- Usefulness of RKHS reformulation: Please expand on how the RKHS norm can yield generalization bounds (e.g., margin/Rademacher control for the final linear layer), and whether this leads to practical regularizers or sample-complexity guarantees for the included CNN class. Are there computational benefits from kernel approximations (e.g., Nyström) in training or analysis?
- Novelty beyond CKN: Clearly delineate what is new relative to Mairal et al. (NIPS 2014; 2016)—in particular, the continuous-domain construction, diffeomorphism stability for learned multilayer kernels, group invariance, and the inclusion of CNN predictors with norm control.
- “Not losing signal information”: In supervised tasks, only task-relevant information is necessary. Please clarify why information preservation is emphasized: e.g., to ensure stability and equivariance analyses apply layerwise; to avoid discarding discriminative cues before learning; for transfer or inverse problems; and to guarantee that later supervised layers can select relevant features.
- Connection to invariant kernels: Relate your last-layer group pooling to invariant kernel constructions (averaging over group orbits). How does your local pooling and multiscale stability compare to globally invariant kernels in terms of expressivity and computational cost?
- Group-equivariant operators: Beyond the proposed patch/pool maps, any bounded linear operator that commutes with the left action on G will preserve equivariance. Do your stability and norm-control results require non-expansiveness or specific kernel smoothness? Please discuss the class of admissible commuting operators (e.g., group convolutions with square-integrable kernels) and trade-offs.
- Activations and constants: Provide explicit smooth relu-like activations satisfying Lemma B.2 and the corresponding Cσ(a). Include concrete kernel choices (e.g., inverse polynomial, Gaussian) and typical numerical values for C1, C2.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a rigorous and useful extension of scattering-style stability to a broader, practically relevant class of convolutional representations and connects them to predictor generalization through an RKHS lens. With the clarifications above, it will be a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
