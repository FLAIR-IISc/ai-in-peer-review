

The paper "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations" investigates the theoretical properties of deep signal representations, specifically focusing on stability to diffeomorphisms and invariance to group transformations. The authors extend the framework of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to develop a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes the scattering transform [17] to more standard convolutional architectures. They establish that representations derived from this kernel framework maintain signal information while achieving stability to small deformations, and demonstrate how these stability properties can be translated to predictive models through RKHS norm control. This work bridges two important areas in machine learning: deep learning architectures and kernel methods.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that builds upon Mallat's scattering transform [17] and CKNs [15, 16]. While the scattering transform provides a theoretical framework for wavelet-based representations with proven stability properties, it lacks the learning capability of modern CNNs. The authors successfully extend these stability guarantees to a broader class of architectures by introducing a kernel-based framework that encompasses both CKNs and a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. This is a notable advancement over previous work [5, 17], which focused on pre-defined filters rather than learning-based architectures.

The paper excels in technical quality. The theoretical results are carefully developed with appropriate assumptions and well-justified proofs. The authors provide a clear analysis of how stability properties transfer from the representation to predictive models through RKHS norm control. The connection between stability and generalization is particularly valuable, as it offers theoretical justification for why stable representations may lead to better sample complexity. The extension to group invariance in Section 4 is a thoughtful addition that demonstrates the framework's flexibility.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from theoretical foundations to practical implementation considerations. The authors effectively use mathematical notation to convey complex ideas without unnecessary clutter. However, some notational choices could be improved: the symbol κ is overloaded in Equation (10) and Assumption (A3), which may confuse readers. The draft review correctly notes that the superfluous phrase in the displayed equation between lines 384-385 should be removed, and the inequality on lines 427-428 should contain |‖z‖ - ‖z'‖|² rather than |‖z‖ - ‖z‖|².

The motivation for studying stability could be strengthened. While the authors briefly mention the relevance to adversarial robustness (line 56-57), they could better articulate why stability to diffeomorphisms is particularly important for practical machine learning applications beyond adversarial examples. A more detailed discussion of how these stability properties might translate to improved performance on real-world datasets would strengthen the significance argument.

The paper's originality is strong, as it successfully extends the scattering transform framework to a broader class of architectures while maintaining theoretical guarantees. This represents a meaningful advancement over previous work at NIPS, as the authors carefully distinguish their contributions from related papers [5, 17, 22] and demonstrate how their framework encompasses previous approaches. The connection to CNNs through Proposition 7 is particularly novel and valuable.

In terms of significance, the paper provides theoretical insights that could inform the design of more robust and sample-efficient deep learning models. The demonstration that stability properties transfer to predictive models through RKHS norm control offers a principled approach to regularization, potentially reducing the need for ad-hoc techniques like dropout. The results also provide theoretical justification for the empirical success of CNNs in vision tasks.

Strengths:
- Solid theoretical contribution extending scattering transform to more general architectures
- Clear connection between stability and generalization through RKHS norm
- Well-organized and technically sound presentation
- Demonstrates applicability to a broad class of CNNs
- Addresses the important problem of understanding deep network representations

Weaknesses:
- Motivation for stability analysis could be strengthened with more concrete applications
- Some notational choices (overloading of κ) could be clearer
- Minor technical errors in equations that need correction
- Missing page information for several references

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical implications and applications, as well as a clearer presentation of the key contributions relative to prior work. However, these are relatively minor issues that could be addressed with minor revisions.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in our understanding of the stability and invariance properties of deep convolutional representations. The paper successfully bridges kernel methods and deep learning, providing theoretical insights with clear connections to practical architectures. The results are technically sound, well-presented, and offer valuable theoretical justification for the success of convolutional networks. While the motivation for stability analysis could be strengthened, the paper makes a strong case for its significance to the machine learning community.

Pro-acceptance arguments: Theoretical contribution extends previous work on scattering transforms to a broader class of architectures, provides clear connections between stability and generalization, and offers principled regularization through RKHS norm control. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful advancement in understanding deep learning representations.

Con-acceptance arguments: The motivation for stability analysis is somewhat limited, and some minor notational issues need correction. However, these are relatively minor concerns compared to the paper's strong theoretical contribution.
