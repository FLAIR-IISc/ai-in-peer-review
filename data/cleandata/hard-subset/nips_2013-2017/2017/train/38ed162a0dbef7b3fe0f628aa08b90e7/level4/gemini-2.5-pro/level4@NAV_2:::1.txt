This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep signal representations, focusing on their invariance and stability properties. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel framework, which generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), to operate on signals in continuous domains. Drawing inspiration from the analysis of scattering transforms, they demonstrate that this representation is stable to the action of small C1-diffeomorphisms and can be made near-invariant to translations. The degree of translation invariance is shown to be controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer. This framework is then extended to achieve global invariance to the action of any locally compact group by adapting the patch extraction and pooling operators. A key contribution is showing that the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) associated with this kernel construction contains a large class of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This establishes a connection that allows the stability and invariance guarantees to be applied directly to these CNNs, linking their stability to their RKHS norm.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper's primary strengths lie in its technical quality, originality, and potential significance.

*   **Quality & Clarity:** The paper is technically sound and rigorously presented. The mathematical framework, built upon operator theory in Hilbert spaces, is clear and allows for precise statements about stability and invariance. The arguments are well-structured, progressing logically from the definition of the multilayer kernel in a continuous setting to its discretization, practical approximations, and eventual connection to CNNs. The paper is well-written and easy to follow for readers with a background in harmonic analysis and kernel methods.

*   **Originality & Significance:** The work is original in its synthesis of concepts from scattering networks, kernel methods, and deep learning. While stability analysis has been performed for scattering transforms, this work extends it to a broader and more practical class of models that includes learnable components, like CKNs and certain CNNs. This provides a valuable theoretical bridge between the well-understood properties of scattering networks and the less-understood, empirically successful CNNs. The results are significant as they offer a principled understanding of why CNNs are robust to small deformations of their inputs. The connection between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization provides a formal basis for regularization and could inspire new architecture designs.

The main weakness of the paper is the absence of empirical validation.

*   **Lack of Experiments:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the analysis is compelling, the claims would be substantially strengthened by experiments. For instance, empirical results demonstrating that the stability of a CKN or a corresponding CNN to diffeomorphisms is indeed controlled by the pooling scale or the model's norm would provide crucial validation. Showing how these theoretical properties correlate with performance on a practical task (e.g., classification under transformations) would greatly enhance the impact of the work.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  In Section 2.2 and 3.2, you discuss kernel approximation by projecting the feature map onto a finite-dimensional subspace, noting that this preserves the stability guarantees due to the non-expansive nature of projection. Could you comment on whether other common large-scale kernel approximation techniques, such as random Fourier features or the Nyström method, would also be compatible with your framework? Would the stability results still hold under these alternative approximation schemes?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and rigorous theoretical framework for analyzing the stability and invariance of deep convolutional architectures.
*   Successfully extends the stability analysis of scattering networks to a broader class of models with learned filters, including CKNs and a class of CNNs.
*   The connection between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization is an important conceptual contribution to the understanding of deep networks.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and technically solid.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The complete lack of experimental results leaves the theoretical contributions without empirical grounding. It is difficult to assess the practical relevance and tightness of the derived bounds without any validation.
