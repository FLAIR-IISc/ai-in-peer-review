Summary
This paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation that yields near-invariance to transformation groups and Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms while preserving signal information. The construction generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous domains through three operators per layer: patch extraction, a kernel map into an RKHS using homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling. The authors analyze translation equivariance of intermediate layers and show that increasing the last-layer pooling scale improves global translation invariance. They also extend the construction to general groups via group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling. Stability bounds to diffeomorphisms are derived in the style of scattering transforms, with constants depending on the Jacobian norm of the deformation and the pooling scale. The paper further addresses discretization and kernel approximation (including finite-dimensional projections as in practical CKNs), proving that stability is preserved under non-expansive projections. Finally, it shows that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the RKHS of the proposed kernel, so that the same stability results apply to these CNNs and their prediction Lipschitz constants can be controlled by the RKHS norm.

Relation to prior work
The analysis builds on and extends scattering theory (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat) to the CKN framework (Mairal et al.), and connects to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling), invariant kernels (Haasdonk & Burkhardt), and kernel views of deep networks. Compared with scattering, this work accommodates learned kernels/filters through RKHS projections and covers CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, closing part of the gap between handcrafted scattering and learned deep models.

Strengths
- Clear, principled framework that unifies CKNs and a class of CNNs under an RKHS lens.
- Rigorous stability bounds to diffeomorphisms with intuitive dependence on deformation magnitude and pooling scale; translation invariance increases with depth via final pooling.
- Careful treatment of discretization and kernel approximation, with non-expansive projections preserving stability.
- Group-equivariant construction provides a clean path to global invariance for general transformation groups.
- Insightful connection between RKHS norm, stability, and generalization.

Weaknesses
- No empirical validation. The theory would be strengthened by experiments showing stability/invariance for practical CKN/CNN instantiations and by quantifying constants.
- Assumptions restrict scope: smooth homogeneous activations (smoothed ReLU variants) and specific pooling (Gaussian). The effect of common nonsmooth activations and practical choices (stride, padding) is not fully explored.
- Constants in the bounds can depend on patch radius and dimension (via κ and d), and scale with depth; guidance on practical parameter choices is limited.
- The assumption of initial anti-aliasing A0 and boundary effects are acknowledged but not empirically assessed.

Quality
The theoretical claims are well supported with precise constructions and references. Proofs are deferred but appear standard given the framework. The link to CNNs is carefully derived via inclusion in the RKHS and norm control. However, experimental corroboration is missing, especially for finite-dimensional approximations and discrete implementations.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, though notation is dense. Suggestions:
- Provide more intuition for the dependence of constants on κ and d, and for the role of nk(u) in homogenized activations relative to standard CNN practice.
- Summarize key assumptions (A1–A3) and their practical implications in a compact table or box.
- Clarify boundary handling and padding choices in discretization.

Originality
The extension of scattering-style stability to a general multilayer kernel representation and the inclusion of certain CNNs in the associated RKHS are original and valuable. The group-equivariant kernel-layer formulation is clean and general.

Significance
The results deepen theoretical understanding of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations and may inform design and regularization of practical architectures. With empirical validation, the impact could be substantial.

Questions and suggestions
- Kernel approximation: Could random Fourier features or other explicit finite-dimensional mappings (e.g., trigonometric features for shift-invariant kernels) replace the learned subspace projections? If yes, under what conditions would non-expansiveness hold, and how would the stability bounds degrade with approximation error and feature dimension? Please discuss RFF vs Nyström in terms of stability constants and projection norms.
- Activations: How sensitive are results to using (leaky-)ReLU without smoothing? Can you bound the approximation gap between smoothed and standard ReLU in the RKHS norm and its effect on stability?
- Discretization: Provide experiments quantifying reconstruction error from subsampled pooled features (Lemma 2) as a function of ek, sk, and σk, and assess stability numerically under small diffeomorphisms.
- Practical guidance: Offer rules of thumb for choosing σk and patch sizes to balance invariance, information preservation, and stability constants.
- Comparisons: Include empirical comparisons with scattering and group-equivariant CNNs on benchmarks with controlled deformations (e.g., rotated MNIST, small elastic deformations).

Pros
- Strong, general theoretical framework unifying CKNs and certain CNNs.
- Clear stability guarantees to diffeomorphisms and path to group invariance.
- Insightful connections between norm control, stability, and generalization.

Cons
- No experiments; practical relevance and constants remain untested.
- Assumptions on activations and pooling may limit immediate applicability.
- Limited practical guidance on parameter choices and boundary conditions.

Overall
A technically solid and timely theoretical contribution. I would be inclined to accept if accompanied by minimal empirical validation demonstrating the predicted stability/invariance and clarifying the impact of finite-dimensional kernel approximations (including RFF/Nyström) on stability.
