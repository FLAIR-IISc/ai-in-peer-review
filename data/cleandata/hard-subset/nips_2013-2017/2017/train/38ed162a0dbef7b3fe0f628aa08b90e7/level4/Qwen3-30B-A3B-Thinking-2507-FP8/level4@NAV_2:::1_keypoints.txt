- Establishes that deep convolutional representations can be made near-invariant to transformation groups and stable to diffeomorphisms using appropriate kernel constructions.  
- Generalizes the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and analyzes the geometry of the corresponding RKHS.  
- Extends Mallat’s scattering-transform stability results to more standard CNN architectures via a kernel perspective.  
- Connects to prior group-invariance work and shows how stability properties translate to predictive models by controlling RKHS norms.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous and technically sound, with careful proofs under assumptions (A1–A3).  
- Demonstrates stability extends to a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions.  
- Highlights a principled link between stability and generalization/sample complexity.  
- Exposition is clear and well-organized, with precise definitions of patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators.  
- Strong treatment of discretization and practical implementation details (Sections 2.1–2.2).  
- Original contribution: novel theoretical framework bridging CKNs and standard CNNs, advancing beyond prior scattering/CKN work.  
- Significant implications for understanding CNNs and for designing regularization based on stability control.  
- Main weakness: absence of empirical validation demonstrating the claimed invariance and stability in practice.  
- Open question/weakness: limited discussion of alternative kernel approximation methods (e.g., random Fourier features, finite Fourier series) and their effect on stability guarantees.  
- Overall: a strong theoretical contribution that would be more impactful with experiments and broader discussion of approximation strategies.
