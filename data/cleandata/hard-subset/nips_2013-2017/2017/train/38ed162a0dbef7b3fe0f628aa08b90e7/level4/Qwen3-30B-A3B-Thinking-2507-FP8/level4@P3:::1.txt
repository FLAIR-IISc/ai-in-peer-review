

# Review for "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel construction from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16] to continuous-domain signals, establishing a theoretical framework for analyzing the geometry of functional spaces associated with convolutional architectures. Their work extends the scattering transform [5, 17] by providing a kernel-based approach that applies to both CKNs and a broad class of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with smooth homogeneous activation functions.

The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions. First, it demonstrates that the kernel-based representation preserves signal information at each layer while achieving stability to small deformations under the action of diffeomorphisms, with the stability bound decaying as the pooling scale increases. Second, it establishes a formal connection between the kernel framework and standard CNNs, showing that CNNs with appropriate smooth homogeneous activations (such as smoothed ReLU variants) belong to the same functional space, thus inheriting the stability properties. Third, it generalizes the framework to handle group invariance for various transformation groups (rotations, reflections, etc.) through carefully designed group-covariant operators.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs throughout, particularly for the stability analysis. The authors carefully distinguish between signal preservation and representation stability, and they address the relationship between the RKHS norm and generalization properties. The theoretical analysis is well-motivated and builds logically on prior work in scattering networks [17] and kernel methods. The paper also adequately references relevant prior work, including the foundational scattering transform [5, 17], CKNs [15, 16], and group-invariant architectures [9, 17, 22].

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with clear definitions of mathematical constructs and a logical flow from the theoretical foundation to the stability analysis. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the multilayer construction. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical results for practitioners.

Regarding originality, this work provides a significant theoretical advance over existing literature. While the scattering transform [5, 17] established stability properties for wavelet-based representations, this paper extends these results to more standard convolutional architectures through a kernel framework. The connection between CKNs and CNNs, showing that stability properties apply to a broad class of CNNs, represents a novel contribution. The paper also extends the theoretical analysis to group invariance for arbitrary transformation groups, which is more general than previous approaches.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a theoretical foundation for understanding why convolutional architectures work well, particularly regarding their robustness to deformations and invariance properties. By establishing that stability properties translate to improved sample complexity (via RKHS norm control), the paper offers a principled approach to regularization that could guide future model design. The connection between stability and generalization is particularly valuable for understanding the success of deep learning in practice.

Strengths:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis of stability and invariance
- Clear connection between kernel-based representations and standard CNNs
- Generalization to arbitrary transformation groups
- Careful distinction between signal preservation and representation stability
- Strong relation to the scattering transform literature [5, 17]

Weaknesses:
- Lack of empirical validation of the theoretical claims (the authors acknowledge this in the draft)
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations for the kernel approximation
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with related work on stability (e.g., [8] on adversarial robustness)
- The connection between the theoretical results and practical CNN architectures could be made more concrete

The main concern is the absence of empirical results to validate the theoretical claims. While theoretical work is valuable, demonstrating the practical relevance of the stability properties would strengthen the paper significantly. For instance, it would be valuable to see experiments showing how the proposed framework improves robustness to deformations or achieves better generalization compared to standard CNNs.

Regarding the kernel approximation question raised in the draft, the paper briefly discusses approximation via finite-dimensional subspaces (Section 2.2), but does not explore alternatives like random Fourier features or Nyström methods. The authors should consider discussing whether these approximation techniques would preserve the stability properties, as this would be relevant for practical implementation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides a significant theoretical advance on understanding convolutional representations
- Establishes rigorous stability and invariance properties for a broad class of architectures
- Connects kernel methods with standard CNNs in a meaningful way
- Has clear implications for regularization and generalization

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks empirical validation of theoretical claims
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges
- The connection to standard CNNs requires additional justification for non-homogeneous activations

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to our understanding of convolutional representations. While the absence of empirical results is a notable limitation, the theoretical depth and significance of the work warrant consideration for acceptance. The paper would be strengthened by additional experimental validation and more detailed discussion of practical implementation. However, as a theoretical contribution, it stands as a significant advance in the field. I recommend acceptance with the suggestion that the authors consider adding empirical results in future work.
