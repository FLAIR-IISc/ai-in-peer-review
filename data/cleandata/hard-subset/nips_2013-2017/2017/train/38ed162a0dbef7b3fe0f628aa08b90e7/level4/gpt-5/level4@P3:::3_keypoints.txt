- Proposes a multilayer convolutional kernel representation mirroring CNNs (patch extraction, RKHS pointwise map, Gaussian pooling) with stability and invariance analysis.  
- Proves stability to C1-diffeomorphisms and increasing translation invariance as pooling scale grows (Theorem 6).  
- Generalizes pooling and patch extraction to arbitrary locally compact groups to obtain equivariance and global group invariance.  
- Shows the induced RKHS contains a broad family of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations and gives RKHS-norm controls that relate stability and generalization (Proposition 7).  
- Shows discretization and finite-dimensional approximations yield CKNs, with stability properties largely preserved via non-expansive projections.  
- Builds on scattering transforms and prior CKN work but extends them by providing a more general multilayer kernel analysis, group-equivariant construction, and CNN embedding in RKHS.  
- Strength: clear, rigorous operator-theoretic analysis with precise stability bounds.  
- Strength: conceptual unification separating representation from prediction via a kernel viewpoint covering CKNs and many CNNs.  
- Strength: clean group-theoretic recipe for equivariance/invariance beyond translations that unifies scattering, G-CNNs, and kernel methods.  
- Strength: careful motivation and compatibility of discretization/finite-dimensional approximations with stability results.  
- Weakness: novelty vs. existing CKN and scattering literature needs sharper positioning—what new phenomena or guarantees are obtained?  
- Weakness: practical implications unclear—when is layerwise invertibility useful empirically versus just theoretically?  
- Weakness: stability bounds include depth-dependent constants and Schur/group pooling terms; their magnitude, tightness, and dependence on κ and dimension need more discussion.  
- Weakness: approximation error from finite-dimensional projections not quantified—how loss scales with subspace dimension and sample coverage and interacts with stability/generalization.  
- Weakness: lacks experimental illustration to calibrate trade-offs between depth and pooling scale predicted by the theory.  
- Request: clarify if the kernel framework yields explicit generalization/sample-complexity bounds (e.g., combine Lipschitz stability with RKHS margin bounds) and whether norm control suggests new regularizers.  
- Request: explain computational/model-selection benefits of kernel/RKHS view versus standard CNNs (e.g., subspace learning, Nyström/RFF approximations).  
- Request: explicitly state novelty relative to Mairal et al. (2014, 2016), e.g., continuous-domain analysis, full multilayer diffeomorphism stability (Theorem 6), group-equivariant construction, and recursive RKHS norm bounds embedding CNNs.  
- Request: justify the importance of layerwise recoverability (“not losing signal information”) for controlling stability and generalization across downstream tasks.  
- Request: relate group pooling/integration to invariant kernel constructions (orbit-averaging) and show how the multilayer kernel generalizes those approaches.  
- Request: discuss conditions on layer operators that guarantee equivariance and stability (e.g., locality, boundedness, kernel smoothness) beyond the specific choices made.  
- Quality: technically sound with careful assumptions and proofs leveraging known tools; suggest more intuition on the (1+n) depth factor and σn role in bounds.  
- Clarity: generally well organized and readable; recommend a summary table/figure of operators, assumptions, and properties by layer and define constants early.  
- Originality: combination of continuous multilayer kernel, diffeomorphism-stability, group-equivariant construction, and CNN inclusion via RKHS appears novel.  
- Significance: provides a principled bridge between CNNs, scattering, and kernels with implications for invariance/stability and norm-based regularization.  
- Pros for acceptance: solid theoretical contribution, unifying perspective including CKNs and CNNs, and clear group-equivariant construction.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited quantitative constant discussion, limited empirical validation, and some novelty aspects vs. prior work need clearer framing.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept, contingent on addressing clarifications and tightening comparisons to prior work.
