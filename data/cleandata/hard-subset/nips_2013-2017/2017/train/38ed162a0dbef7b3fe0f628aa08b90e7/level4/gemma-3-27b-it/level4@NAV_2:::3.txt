**Summary:** This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, framing them within the context of positive definite kernels and reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors generalize convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and demonstrate stability to diffeomorphic transformations and near-invariance to group transformations.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous mathematical analysis supporting the claims. The connection between the kernel framework and CNNs is clearly articulated. The work appears complete, offering a solid theoretical foundation. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and weaknesses, particularly concerning signal preservation and the implications of the kernel approximation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, despite the complexity of the mathematical concepts. The notation is consistent, and the flow of ideas is logical. Providing more intuitive explanations for the motivation behind certain choices (e.g., signal preservation) would be helpful.

**Originality:** The work builds upon existing research in scattering transforms and CKNs but extends these concepts to a broader class of CNNs and provides a unified kernel-based perspective. The extension to more general CNN architectures and the kernel framework represent a novel contribution. The paper adequately references related work, though a more explicit comparison with Mairal et al. (2016) would strengthen the originality argument. 

**Significance:** The results are potentially significant, as they offer a theoretical understanding of the properties of deep convolutional networks. The connection between stability and generalization could lead to improved regularization techniques and sample complexity bounds. This work advances the state of the art by providing a kernel-based analysis of CNNs, which could open up new avenues for research in this area.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with strong mathematical foundations.
*   Novel extension of existing concepts (scattering transforms, CKNs) to a broader class of CNNs.
*   Potential for significant impact on understanding generalization and regularization in deep learning.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical implications of the kernel reformulation are not fully explored.
*   A more detailed comparison with related work, especially Mairal et al. (2016), is needed.
*   The rationale for signal preservation needs further clarification in the context of task-specific applications.
