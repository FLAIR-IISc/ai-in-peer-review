

The paper investigates the stability and invariance properties of deep convolutional representations using a kernel framework. Building on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15,16] and the scattering transform [5,17], the authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes CKNs to continuous domains. The main contribution is showing that representations formed by this kernel framework are stable to C^1 diffeomorphisms (such as translations), with stability bounds that decay with the pooling scale. The work also demonstrates how these stability properties extend to group-invariant representations and connects the kernel framework to standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with smooth homogeneous activation functions.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written, with a logical flow that builds from theoretical foundations to practical implications. The authors provide a thorough treatment of stability under diffeomorphisms, showing how the kernel representation preserves signal information while achieving stability. The connection to CNNs is particularly valuable, as it demonstrates that theoretical stability properties apply to a broad class of practical models.

Quality: The technical content is strong and well-supported. The authors establish a rigorous theoretical framework for analyzing stability, with careful attention to mathematical details. The proofs are sound, and the assumptions (A1-A3) are clearly stated and justified. The main strength is the unified framework that bridges kernel methods and deep learning, providing theoretical insights into why CNNs work well. The connection to existing work on scattering networks [5,17] and group-invariance [9,17,22] is well-articulated, demonstrating the paper's place in the literature.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of technical concepts and a logical organization. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the figures effectively illustrate the key concepts. The authors do an excellent job of explaining complex ideas in an accessible manner. The only minor weakness is that the notation for the kernel parameter κ is overloaded (used both in the kernel definition and assumption A3), which could cause confusion for readers.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the scattering transform framework to standard CNN architectures through a kernel approach. While the scattering transform [5,17] provides stability guarantees for wavelet-based networks, this work shows that similar guarantees apply to a much broader class of models, including standard CNNs with smooth activations. The connection between CKNs and CNNs is novel, and the extension to group-invariance (Section 4) is a valuable contribution. The paper is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers, particularly those on scattering networks and kernel-based deep learning.

Significance: The work addresses a fundamental question about why deep convolutional networks generalize well, providing theoretical insights that could inform better network design. The stability results have practical implications for improving robustness to deformations and adversarial examples. The authors show how stability relates to generalization (through the RKHS norm), which could guide future work on regularization techniques. The paper is highly relevant to the ML community, as it provides a theoretical foundation for understanding and improving deep learning models.

Strengths:
- Provides a unified theoretical framework for stability in deep convolutional representations
- Connects kernel methods with standard CNN architectures
- Shows how stability properties extend to a broad class of practical models
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Rigorous mathematical treatment with sound proofs

Weaknesses:
- The motivation for stability analysis could be strengthened (currently only one brief sentence mentions relevance to adversarial robustness)
- The notation for κ is overloaded (used as both a kernel parameter and in assumption A3)
- A minor typographical error in the displayed equation between lines 384-385
- The definition of φ should be introduced in Equation (10) as ⟨φ(z), φ(z')⟩_H(K)
- The inequality in lines 427-428 should have |‖z‖ - ‖z'‖|² instead of |‖z‖ - ‖z‖|²
- Some references lack page numbers (e.g., [3,6,8,13,14,18,25-27,30,31])

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the properties of deep convolutional representations. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a fundamental question in deep learning. The connection between kernel methods and CNNs is particularly valuable, as it provides theoretical justification for why CNNs work well. The paper would benefit from slightly strengthening the motivation for stability analysis and clarifying the notation for κ, but these are minor issues.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances theoretical understanding of deep learning models while connecting to practical applications. The quality of the work meets NIPS standards, and the contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance. The authors have made a valuable addition to the literature on deep learning theory, with clear connections to both kernel methods and convolutional neural networks.
