This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on invariance and stability. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel construction used in Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) and leverage tools from reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) to demonstrate near-invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. A key contribution is showing that these properties extend to a broad class of CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions. The work builds on prior research in scattering transforms [17], kernel methods [15, 16], and group invariance [6, 9], offering a unified kernel-based perspective.

The paper is technically sound and well-written, presenting a clear and logical progression of ideas. The mathematical treatment appears rigorous, and the connections drawn between kernel theory and CNNs are insightful. The authors carefully lay out the assumptions and provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to follow the arguments. A particular strength lies in extending existing results from scattering transforms to the more general setting of CNNs and providing a framework for analyzing stability through the lens of RKHS norms. The exploration of how kernel design influences invariance is also valuable.

However, a significant weakness is the complete lack of empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis is compelling, the paper would be substantially strengthened by experimental results demonstrating the predicted invariance and stability properties. Demonstrating these properties on standard datasets (e.g., image classification with various transformations applied) would provide crucial support for the theoretical claims. Without experiments, the practical relevance of the findings remains unclear.

Another potential area for exploration, briefly touched upon by the authors, concerns alternative approaches to kernel approximation. The paper focuses on subspace learning within the RKHS. Investigating whether other approximation techniques, such as random projections or explicit kernel mapping with finite Fourier series, could also maintain stability and offering a comparison of computational costs would be beneficial. Exploring the trade-offs between approximation accuracy and computational efficiency is a relevant direction for future work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations.
*   **Novel perspective:** The kernel-based approach offers a unifying framework for understanding the properties of CKNs and CNNs.
*   **Extension of existing work:** The results generalize previous findings from scattering transforms to a broader class of models.
*   **Potential for impact:** The insights gained from this work could inform the design of more robust and generalizable deep learning architectures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of empirical validation:** The absence of experiments weakens the practical impact of the theoretical results.
*   **Limited exploration of approximation methods:**  The paper primarily focuses on one approach to kernel approximation, leaving open the possibility of more efficient alternatives.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. The theoretical contributions are valuable and the analysis is rigorous. However, the lack of experimental validation is a significant drawback. If the authors can address this concern by including some preliminary experiments, or at least outlining a clear experimental plan, I would be more inclined to recommend acceptance. The paper’s strengths suggest it could be a valuable contribution to the field, but the absence of empirical evidence limits its immediate impact. The work feels incomplete without some evidence supporting the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.
