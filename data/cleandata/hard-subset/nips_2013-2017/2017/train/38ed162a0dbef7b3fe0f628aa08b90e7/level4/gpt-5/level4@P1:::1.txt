Summary
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation for signals that yields feature maps which are (i) stable to C1 diffeomorphisms and (ii) increasingly near-invariant to translations (and more generally to group actions) through pooling. The construction mirrors convolutional architectures: each layer applies patch extraction, a pointwise kernel map into an RKHS, and Gaussian pooling. The authors derive Lipschitz-type stability bounds: for a diffeomorphism τ with ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2, the representation Φ satisfies ‖Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1 + n)‖∇τ‖∞ + C2‖τ‖∞/σn)‖x‖, where σn is the last pooling scale. They also show how to achieve equivariance/invariance to general groups by defining group-covariant patch extraction and pooling with respect to a Haar measure. Practical considerations are addressed: discretization with subsampling (signal recoverability under ek ≥ sk), and finite-dimensional kernel approximations via projections (as in CKNs), which preserve non-expansiveness and thus stability. Finally, they prove that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lie in the associated RKHS, with explicit norm bounds, so the same stability properties extend to these CNNs and connect to generalization via RKHS norms.

Relation to prior work
This work builds directly on convolutional kernel networks (Mairal et al.) and the scattering transform (Bruna & Mallat; Mallat), extending scattering-style stability guarantees to a more general kernel framework that includes CKNs and a class of CNNs. It relates to group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling) and invariant kernels, and connects to Lipschitz robustness considerations relevant to adversarial stability. The group pooling formalism echoes prior work on invariance via group averaging, but here is integrated into the multilayer kernel construction with explicit bounds.

Quality
The technical development is careful and sound. The stability analysis relies on commutator bounds (via Schur’s test), clear assumptions on kernel maps (norm preservation and non-expansiveness), patch sizes relative to pooling scales, and an initial anti-aliasing operator A0 to control high frequencies. The translation-invariance term decays with σn, as expected. The discretization/recovery lemmas make clear when subsampling is information-preserving (ek ≥ sk) and how kernel projections maintain non-expansiveness. The inclusion of CNNs into the RKHS with norm control is well-motivated, although it requires smooth homogeneous activations or smoothed ReLU variants. A limitation is that constants (C1, C2) and their dependence on κ, depth, and filters are not calibrated empirically.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, with rigorous definitions and helpful links to prior analyses. Notation is heavy but consistent. It would help to discuss the magnitude/tightness of constants and the practical impact of linear dependence on depth in the stability bound, as well as a brief discussion on the stability of the deconvolution step in Lemma 2 under noise.

Originality
The extension of scattering-style guarantees to a general multilayer kernel framework that subsumes CKNs, together with the embedding of CNNs in the same RKHS with explicit norm bounds, is novel and nontrivial. The group-equivariant pooling construction is standard, but its integration and analysis in this kernel setting adds value.

Significance
The results provide theoretical insight into invariance/stability of deep convolutional representations beyond scattering, suggest principled regularization via RKHS norms, and potentially inform architectural choices (patch and pooling scales). However, the lack of empirical validation leaves open the practical relevance and tightness of the bounds, as well as the impact on real-world performance and robustness.

Strengths
- Clear, rigorous analysis yielding explicit stability and invariance bounds.
- Unifies CKNs and a class of CNNs within an RKHS framework, enabling transfer of guarantees.
- Addresses discretization and kernel approximation pragmatically; non-expansive projections preserve stability.
- General group-equivariant/invariant construction with Haar pooling.

Weaknesses
- No experiments to validate theoretical claims, quantify constants, or demonstrate practical gains (e.g., stability vs. σn, depth, or approximation quality).
- Assumes smooth homogeneous activations; applicability to standard ReLU networks is indirect.
- Stability bound grows linearly with depth; practical impact not discussed.
- Recovery via deconvolution (Lemma 2) can be ill-conditioned; robustness to noise not analyzed.

Questions for the authors
- Kernel approximation: Can random Fourier features, Nyström, or other explicit finite-dimensional maps be used instead of projections? Since RFF is not a contraction/projection, under what conditions (e.g., uniform kernel approximation error, Lipschitz constants of the feature map) do your stability guarantees still hold, and how do constants degrade with feature dimension?
- Constants: Can you provide empirical estimates of C1 and C2 for typical choices of h and κ, and illustrate dependence on depth n?
- Robustness: How stable is the deconvolution-based recovery in Lemma 2 under noise and model mismatch, and how does this affect downstream stability after subsampling?
- Activations: Do you have approximation/error bounds when using standard (non-smooth) ReLU? How tight are the results with smoothed ReLU in practice?
- Practical design: How sensitive are the results to cases where ek < sk or where pooling/stride choices deviate from the stated assumptions?

Suggestions
- Add experiments with CKNs (and, if possible, CNNs in your RKHS class) demonstrating stability to controlled diffeomorphisms and improved invariance with larger σn, and comparing different kernel approximations (projection, Nyström, RFF).
- Report how RKHS norm control correlates with generalization and stability in practice.
- Discuss constants and depth dependence to guide architectural choices.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution extending scattering-style guarantees to CKNs and certain CNNs.
- Clear framework linking stability, invariance, and RKHS norm-based generalization.
- Broad relevance to understanding deep convolutional representations.

Cons for acceptance
- No empirical validation; practical implications and tightness of bounds remain untested.
- Restrictions on activations and assumptions (‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2, anti-aliasing) may limit immediate applicability.
