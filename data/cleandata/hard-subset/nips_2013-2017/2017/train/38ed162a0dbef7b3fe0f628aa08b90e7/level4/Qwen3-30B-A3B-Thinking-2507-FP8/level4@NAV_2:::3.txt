

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations, successfully characterizing them through multilayer kernel constructions in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). It demonstrates that these representations achieve near-invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphic transformations without losing signal information, making a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of convolutional neural networks.

The main contribution involves formalizing CNN architectures within the RKHS framework, extending previous work on convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to provide theoretical guarantees for stability and invariance. While the paper effectively shows how standard CNNs fit within this functional space, it would benefit from clearer articulation of the practical utility of this reformulation. The authors should elaborate on how this kernel perspective leads to new insights regarding generalization bounds, computational efficiency, or alternative implementation strategies compared to standard CNNs. Specifically, does this formulation enable more principled regularization approaches or provide new computational advantages through established kernel approximation techniques?

The novelty of the approach relative to Mairal et al.'s "Convolutional Kernel Networks" (NIPS 2014) requires more explicit clarification. What specific theoretical advances or broader applicability does this work provide beyond the CKN framework? The connection to previous invariant kernel approaches, particularly Burkhardt's work (Invariant Kernel Functions for Pattern Analysis and Machine Learning, 2007), should be explicitly addressed.

Regarding the claim that "not losing signal information" is important, the authors should clarify why preserving the complete signal is beneficial for practical applications. For most computer vision tasks (e.g., classification), the goal is to preserve task-relevant information rather than the full signal. The authors should explain how preserving complete signal information relates to task performance or provides advantages for practical implementations.

The paper's theoretical results on group invariance are well-developed, but it would be valuable to explore whether any operator that commutes with all group elements would suffice for achieving equivariance, or if the specific choice of patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators is necessary for the theoretical guarantees.

Despite these points, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides valuable theoretical insights into convolutional representations. The results on stability to diffeomorphisms and group invariance represent significant contributions to the theoretical understanding of CNNs. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors address these points in the final version.
