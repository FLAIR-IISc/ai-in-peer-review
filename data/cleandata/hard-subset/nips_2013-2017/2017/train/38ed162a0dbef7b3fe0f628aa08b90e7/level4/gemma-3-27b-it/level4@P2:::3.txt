This paper presents a rigorous and well-written analysis establishing a connection between deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs) and multilayer kernels within the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors successfully demonstrate that the functional spaces implemented by certain DCNN architectures exhibit desirable properties, namely approximate invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphic transformations. Overall, I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the points outlined below.

The primary contribution, as I understand it, is a reformulation of DCNN architectures in the language of RKHS. While technically sound, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of *why* this reformulation is valuable. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on potential benefits such as: Could this kernel formulation lead to tighter generalization bounds? Does it offer computational advantages through established kernel approximation techniques? Expanding on these potential applications would strengthen the paper’s impact. Moreover, a clearer articulation of the novelty beyond simply reformulating DCNNs within an RKHS framework is needed.

The paper draws heavily on “Convolutional Kernel Networks” (Mairal et al.). A more detailed comparison highlighting the distinctions between the two approaches is crucial. What advancements does this work offer *over* the prior work by Mairal et al.? Clarifying this will solidify the paper’s contribution.

The authors emphasize the importance of "not losing signal information." While theoretically interesting, this point feels somewhat disconnected from typical DCNN applications. DCNNs are frequently employed for tasks like image classification where only task-relevant information needs preservation, not necessarily a perfect signal reconstruction. A discussion addressing this point – clarifying why preserving all signal information is beneficial in the context of the analyzed architectures and potential applications – would improve the argument.

Regarding group invariance, it would be worthwhile to briefly discuss connections to the work on invariant kernels by Hans Burkhardt and others. Is there a relationship between the proposed approach and existing invariant kernel methods? Acknowledging and contrasting with these related works would demonstrate a comprehensive understanding of the field.

Finally, a somewhat more theoretical curiosity: the paper defines patch extraction, pooling, and kernel maps designed to commute with the group action. This suggests a general principle. Would *any* operator commuting with all group elements guarantee the equivariance property of DCNNs with respect to those transformations? Exploring this question, even briefly, could offer further insights.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation linking DCNNs to kernel methods.
*   **Interesting Properties:** Demonstrating invariance and stability properties is valuable for understanding and potentially improving DCNNs.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the mathematical arguments are presented clearly.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Clear Motivation:** The utility of the RKHS reformulation is not fully explained.
*   **Insufficient Novelty Discussion:** The paper needs to more clearly delineate its novelty compared to prior work, particularly Mairal et al.
*   **Limited Practical Implications:** The focus on signal preservation feels somewhat abstract without connection to common DCNN tasks.
