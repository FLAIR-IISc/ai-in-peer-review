This paper presents a theoretical framework for analyzing deep convolutional representations through the lens of multilayer reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors generalize the construction of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) to operate on continuous signals and use this framework to study two fundamental properties of deep representations: near-invariance to group transformations and stability under the action of diffeomorphisms. The core theoretical result demonstrates that the proposed representation is Lipschitz stable to small deformations, with an invariance factor that improves with network depth. Furthermore, the paper establishes a formal connection showing that the constructed RKHS contains a broad class of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with smooth homogeneous activation functions, thereby extending the stability guarantees to these models. This work builds directly upon the CKN framework [15, 16] and draws heavily on the analytical techniques developed for the scattering transform [17].

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of deep convolutional representations. The proofs and derivations appear solid, successfully extending concepts from harmonic analysis and kernel methods to a CNN-like architecture.
*   **Bridging Framework:** A significant strength is the connection established between the practical architecture of CNNs and the theoretical machinery of RKHS. This provides a valuable perspective for understanding properties like stability and invariance in a principled manner.
*   **Important Guarantees:** The analysis of stability to diffeomorphisms and invariance to group actions addresses fundamental questions in deep learning. Showing that these desirable properties can be controlled by the RKHS norm of the learned function provides a link between generalization (low norm) and robustness.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Clarification:**

The main weaknesses relate to the motivation behind certain choices and the positioning of the work's novelty and significance.

*   **Novelty in Relation to Prior Work:** The paper is presented as a generalization of CKNs [15, 16]. It would be beneficial for the authors to more explicitly delineate the novel contributions beyond the original CKN papers. Is the primary contribution the extension to continuous signals and the subsequent stability analysis, which was not the focus of the original CKN work, or are there other fundamental advances?
*   **Significance of the Reformulation:** The paper successfully reformulates a class of CNNs within a kernel framework. However, the practical implications or future benefits of this reformulation are not fully articulated. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could comment on why this perspective is useful beyond the theoretical analysis presented. For example:
    *   Could this framework lead to new, principled regularization techniques?
    *   Does it open the door to new generalization bounds for deep networks?
    *   Could it suggest computationally different training algorithms based on standard kernel approximations (e.g., Nyström method)?
*   **Motivation for Signal Preservation:** The authors stress the importance of the representation "not losing signal information" (Lemma 1 and 2). This is an interesting theoretical property, but its practical motivation is unclear. For many discriminative tasks (e.g., image classification), the goal of a representation is precisely to discard irrelevant variability in the signal to achieve invariance. A comment on why complete, lossless reconstruction is a desirable property, or for which specific tasks it is most relevant (e.g., generative models, signal reconstruction), would help clarify this point.
*   **Connection to Invariant Kernels:** The discussion on achieving global invariance to group actions (Section 4) is well-constructed. However, there is a rich literature on constructing group-invariant kernels. It would be valuable to connect the proposed approach to this existing work, for instance, the methods discussed by Haasdonk and Burkhardt [13], to better situate the contribution.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

1.  In Section 4, you define patch extraction, kernel mapping, and pooling operators that commute with the group action to ensure equivariance. This is a very clear construction. As a point of curiosity, would any operator from the commutant algebra (the set of all operators that commute with every group action `Lg`) suffice to build an equivariant layer, or is there something special about this specific sequence of operations?

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong theoretical paper that provides valuable insights into the geometric properties of deep convolutional networks. The connection between CNNs and RKHS is elegant and the stability analysis is rigorous. While the paper could be improved by better articulating its practical significance and more clearly positioning its novelty with respect to prior work, the contributions are solid and of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
