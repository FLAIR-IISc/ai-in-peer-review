This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations through the lens of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The authors generalize the multilayer kernel construction from Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) to signals defined on a continuous domain. This kernel representation is built by composing three operators layer-wise: patch extraction, a norm-preserving kernel mapping, and a linear pooling operator (Gaussian convolution). The main contributions are threefold. First, drawing inspiration from the analysis of scattering transforms (Mallat, 2012), the authors prove that their representation is stable to the action of small diffeomorphisms and is nearly invariant to translations. The degree of translation invariance is shown to be controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer. Second, the framework is extended to achieve equivariance and invariance to general group actions (e.g., rotations) by defining appropriate patch and pooling operators on the group. Third, and perhaps most importantly, the paper establishes a formal link between the constructed RKHS and a class of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This demonstrates that such CNNs are functions within the RKHS, and thus the stability and invariance results derived for the kernel representation apply directly to them.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Significance:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to our understanding of CNNs. By placing a class of CNNs within a well-defined functional space (an RKHS), the authors provide a principled way to analyze their properties. The stability analysis (Theorem 6) offers a clear and elegant result, analogous to that for scattering transforms, which formally characterizes why these architectures are robust to small deformations of the input, a crucial property for tasks like object recognition. This provides a rigorous foundation for intuitions that have long guided the design of deep networks.

2.  **Unification of Concepts:** The work successfully unifies several important concepts: the architectural principles of CNNs, the theoretical framework of kernel methods, and the stability analysis from harmonic analysis (scattering transforms). The demonstration that CNNs can be viewed as elements of an RKHS (Proposition 7) is particularly powerful, as it connects the network's parameters to the RKHS norm, which in turn controls both stability (via Eq. 1) and generalization (via margin bounds). This provides a compelling link between robustness and generalization.

3.  **Generality of the Framework:** The proposed kernel construction is general. The extension to arbitrary locally compact groups in Section 4 is a valuable addition, providing a unified perspective on building representations that are invariant to transformations beyond simple translations, which is a very active area of research (e.g., Cohen & Welling, 2016).

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of building up the multilayer kernel construction step-by-step and motivating each component. The connections to prior work, especially CKNs and the scattering transform, are clearly articulated, which helps to place the contributions in their proper context.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Gap between Theory and Practice:** While the link established between the RKHS and CNNs is a major strength, the class of CNNs covered by the analysis is somewhat constrained. The activation functions must be smooth and homogeneous, and the construction involves a specific normalization term `n_k(u) = ||P_k x_{k-1}(u)||`. The most common activation, ReLU, is homogeneous but not smooth, and standard CNNs do not include this explicit normalization. The authors acknowledge this and suggest smoothed ReLU variants, but a more detailed discussion on the implications of these differences would be beneficial. For example, could batch normalization or other common normalization schemes be interpreted as approximations of this term? Clarifying the gap between the analyzed "RKHS-CNNs" and state-of-the-art architectures would help readers better assess the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper is entirely theoretical. While this is acceptable for a work of this nature, the inclusion of even simple experiments could have significantly strengthened the claims. For instance, empirically measuring the stability of a CKN or a smoothed-ReLU CNN to small, controlled deformations and comparing it to the bound in Theorem 6 would provide a powerful validation of the theory. Another possibility would be to demonstrate the trade-off between the RKHS norm of a trained model and its stability, providing concrete evidence for the link between regularization, stability, and generalization discussed in the paper.

3.  **Signal Norm Preservation:** The authors note that, unlike the scattering transform, their representation does not preserve the L2 norm of the signal due to the pooling operators. They argue that this is not a critical issue for natural signals and suggest a potential fix. However, since the pooling operators are low-pass filters, there is a risk of significant signal energy loss, especially in very deep networks where many such operators are cascaded. The statement `||Φ(x)|| ≥ ||A_n · · ·A_0 x||` is mentioned as a partial reassurance but is cited from a preprint. A more self-contained and formal discussion of the conditions under which a substantial portion of the signal norm is preserved would strengthen the paper's completeness.

4.  **Discretization Details:** The discussion on discretization in Section 2.1 is important for connecting the continuous-domain theory to discrete implementations. However, the link between the parameters of the continuous and discrete models could be made more explicit. A concrete example illustrating how a standard discrete convolutional layer (e.g., with a 3x3 filter, stride 2) relates to the continuous parameters (patch size `S_k`, pooling scale `σ_k`, subsampling factor `s_k`) would improve the clarity and accessibility of this crucial connection.

In summary, this is a high-quality, technically sound paper that makes an important theoretical contribution to the field. It provides a rigorous and elegant framework for analyzing the stability and invariance properties of deep convolutional architectures. The main weaknesses relate to the gap between the analyzed models and practical CNNs, and the absence of empirical validation. However, these do not detract from the significance of the theoretical results. The paper advances our fundamental understanding of deep learning and is very likely to inspire future theoretical and practical work. I recommend acceptance.
