Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework for signals on continuous domains that yields representations that are (i) near-invariant to translations and more generally to actions of transformation groups via pooling on the group, and (ii) Lipschitz-stable to small diffeomorphisms. The construction generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to continuous-time signals and studies the geometry of the associated RKHS. Key steps per layer are: patch extraction, a norm-preserving homogeneous kernel map, and linear Gaussian pooling. The main theoretical results show that (a) the representation preserves signal information (with appropriate sampling) and that stability bounds analogous to the scattering transform hold, with translation-invariance improving with the last pooling scale; (b) discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations (as in practical CKNs) retain stability; (c) the framework extends to general locally compact groups via group-equivariant patching and pooling; (d) a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lie in the RKHS induced by the multilayer kernel, so their predictions inherit the same stability via a Lipschitz bound controlled by the RKHS norm, connecting stability and generalization.

This work is positioned between Mallat’s scattering transform (Bruna & Mallat, 2013; Mallat, 2012) and kernelized analyses of deep architectures (Mairal et al., 2014/2016 CKNs; Montavon et al., 2011; Daniely et al., 2016). It complements group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling, 2016) by providing a kernel/RKHS route to equivariance and invariance with explicit stability guarantees, and relates to invariant kernels (Haasdonk & Burkhardt, 2007). Compared to scattering, which uses fixed wavelets and preserves energy, the proposed framework accommodates learned filters (via kernel approximations) and covers a class of CNNs, at the cost of not being norm-preserving due to pooling.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: precise multilayer kernel construction, bounds on commutators with diffeomorphisms, and a final stability theorem with explicit dependence on ∥∇τ∥∞, ∥τ∥∞, depth, and pooling scale.
- Unification: connects CKNs and a nontrivial class of CNNs via an RKHS, showing CNN predictions belong to the RKHS under smooth homogeneous activations and providing norm bounds.
- Group generality: clean formulation of patching and pooling on locally compact groups yielding equivariance and a route to global invariance at the last layer.
- Practical tie-ins: discretization scheme with information preservation under mild conditions; compatibility of stability with finite-dimensional kernel projections; discussion of how norm control relates to generalization.
- Technically careful: leverages harmonic analysis tools (Schur’s test, commutator bounds) and extends scattering lemmas to shifted integral operators.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Assumptions on activations: the inclusion of CNNs in the RKHS requires smooth homogeneous activations; ReLU requires smoothing. The practical impact of smoothing on approximation error and training is not quantified.
- Norm preservation and energy: unlike scattering, pooling reduces energy; while the authors argue this is acceptable and propose an optional top Gaussian layer, empirical or theoretical guidance on how much energy is lost for typical signals would help.
- Constants and tightness: stability constants C1 and C2 depend on kernel/pooling choices and κ; explicit, practically interpretable bounds and their scaling with dimension d and depth would improve relevance.
- Discretization and deconvolution: Lemma 2 relies on deconvolution stability with Gaussian filtering; in practice this can be ill-conditioned. The robustness of recovery under noise and boundary effects is not analyzed.
- Experimental validation: no empirical evaluation to illustrate the sharpness of bounds, the trade-off between invariance and information loss, or the practical inclusion of CNNs via the RKHS norm. Even small-scale experiments (synthetic diffeomorphisms) would strengthen the case.

Quality
The paper is technically sound overall. The stability result (Theorem 6) is derived cleanly via commutator bounds; the generalization to groups is standard but well executed. The RKHS inclusion for CNNs (Proposition 7) is plausible and useful; the recursive norm bound is informative though potentially loose. Some assumptions (A1–A3) are reasonable for the chosen homogeneous kernels and Gaussian pooling, but the necessity vs. convenience could be discussed more.

Clarity
The exposition is mostly clear and well structured: construction → stability → approximations → groups → CNN link. Sketches in the main text with proofs in the appendix are appropriate. A few places could benefit from added intuition: (i) the role and typical magnitude of κ in A3, (ii) a diagram of the group-equivariant pipeline, (iii) a concrete list of activations/kernels satisfying Lemma B.2 and their Cσ. The discretization section is helpful but terse on conditioning.

Originality
Moderate to high. The work meaningfully extends scattering-style stability analysis to a broader kernel/CNN setting and gives a novel inclusion of a class of CNNs in the RKHS of a multilayer convolutional kernel with norm control. While building on established ideas, the synthesis and generalization are nontrivial.

Significance
Potentially significant for theory: provides a framework linking invariance/stability, RKHS geometry, and generalization for deep convolutional representations, covering both kernelized models and a family of CNNs. Practical significance would be bolstered by empirical demonstrations or concrete guidance for architecture/regularization design (e.g., how to control the RKHS norm via weight penalties or spectral constraints).

Specific questions and suggestions
- Activation class: Please quantify the approximation error between ReLU and the smoothed variant used to ensure inclusion in the RKHS, and discuss its effect on the norm bound and stability.
- Constants: Can you provide explicit expressions or bounds for C1 as a function of κ, d, and the pooling kernel, and discuss the depth dependence (1 + n) tightness? Is logarithmic dependence achievable with alternative constructions?
- Assumption A3: To what extent do common CNN settings (e.g., 3×3 filters with stride/pooling 2) satisfy supc∈Sk |c| ≤ κσk−1? Any guidance on choosing σk and patch sizes jointly?
- Discretization: How stable is the recovery in Lemma 2 under additive noise and boundary truncation? Is regularized deconvolution required, and does it affect the invariance/stability results?
- Generalization link: Can you relate the RKHS norm bounds to data-dependent complexity (e.g., Rademacher complexity) or margin bounds in this multilayer setting?
- Empirics: A small experiment measuring ∥Φ(Lτx) − Φ(x)∥ as a function of τ for CKNs and standard CNNs would be illuminating, as would comparisons with scattering.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, well-motivated theoretical advance generalizing scattering-style stability to a broader, practically relevant family.
- Clear bridge between CNNs and RKHS geometry with norm-based control of stability.
- Conceptually unifies invariance via group pooling and stability via intermediate pooling.

Cons for acceptance
- No empirical validation; practical implications and constants are not quantified.
- Restrictive activation assumptions limit immediate applicability to standard ReLU CNNs without smoothing.
- Some technical assumptions (A0 initial pooling, deconvolution-based recovery) may be idealized for real data.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and technically competent theoretical paper that should be of interest to the NeurIPS community studying foundations of deep representation learning. Empirical illustrations or more explicit constants would strengthen it further.
