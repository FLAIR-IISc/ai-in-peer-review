## Review of "Invariance and Stability of Deep Convolutional Representations"

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, focusing on invariance to transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors build upon the framework of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and generalize it to a broader class of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with smooth homogeneous activation functions. They demonstrate that their proposed kernel framework yields representations with stability guarantees similar to those of scattering transforms, while allowing for the learning-based flexibility of standard CNNs.

The core contribution lies in establishing a connection between kernel methods and CNNs, providing a theoretical foundation for understanding the stability and invariance properties inherent in these architectures. The authors convincingly argue that controlling the norm in the associated Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) can lead to improved generalization, linking stability to sample complexity. They extend previous work on scattering transforms to a more general setting, addressing a crucial gap in understanding the geometry of the functional spaces inhabited by CNNs.

The paper is technically sound, with a clear presentation of the mathematical framework and rigorous proofs (relegated to the appendix). The authors demonstrate a strong grasp of relevant literature in kernel methods, signal processing, and deep learning. The extension of existing results to encompass a wider class of CNNs and the careful consideration of both theoretical properties and practical implications (discretization, kernel approximation) are strengths. 

However, the paper's clarity suffers somewhat from its density and reliance on mathematical notation. While thorough, the technical details might pose a barrier to readers not deeply familiar with kernel methods. The discussion of the relationship between the theoretical framework and practical CNN architectures could be more concrete with specific examples.  Furthermore, the assumption of smooth homogeneous activations, while allowing for theoretical tractability, might limit the applicability of the results to commonly used activation functions like ReLU (although they do address smoothed variants).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a valuable theoretical framework for understanding CNNs through the lens of kernel methods.
*   Extends previous work on scattering transforms to a more general and practical setting.
*   Establishes a link between stability, invariance, and generalization performance.
*   Technically rigorous and demonstrates a strong understanding of relevant literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The density and mathematical notation might hinder accessibility for some readers.
*   The assumption of smooth homogeneous activations limits the direct applicability to standard CNNs.
*   The connection between theory and practical CNN design could be further strengthened.

**Overall:** This paper presents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep convolutional representations. Despite some limitations in clarity and scope, the strong technical foundation and potential for future work make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and provide more concrete examples linking the theory to practical CNN architectures.
