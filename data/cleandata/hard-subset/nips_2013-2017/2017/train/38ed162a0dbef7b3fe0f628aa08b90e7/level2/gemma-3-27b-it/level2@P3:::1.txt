This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically focusing on invariance and stability. The work generalizes the multilayer kernel introduced in convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and leverages reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) geometry to analyze these properties. The central goal is to understand the characteristics of functional spaces where CNNs reside, moving beyond the “black box” view. The paper successfully establishes connections between stability, invariance, and generalization, demonstrating that models within this functional space can inherit desirable properties like Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms.

The strengths of this paper lie in its rigorous mathematical treatment of a crucial topic in deep learning – understanding the underlying geometry of CNNs. The use of kernel methods provides a powerful framework for analyzing invariance and stability. Extending results from scattering transforms to a broader class of CNNs, including CKNs, is a significant contribution. The analysis of the impact of different approximations, such as discretization and kernel approximation, enhances the practical relevance of the theoretical findings. The clear presentation of the mathematical derivations and the inclusion of detailed proofs in the appendix are commendable. The link between stability and generalization, suggesting improved sample complexity, is a compelling result. The discussion of group invariance, highlighting how to achieve both stability and invariance, adds further depth to the work.

However, the paper could be improved in several areas. While the theoretical results are strong, the connection to practical CNN architectures could be made more explicit. The discussion focuses heavily on the theoretical framework, and it's not immediately clear how these findings translate into concrete design choices for CNNs in real-world applications. Providing examples of CNN architectures that demonstrably fall within the analyzed functional space would strengthen the argument. The reliance on the assumption of smoothness and homogeneity of activation functions is somewhat limiting. While the paper acknowledges this, a discussion of the implications for commonly used non-smooth activations like ReLU, beyond mentioning smoothed variants, would be valuable. While the paper highlights the importance of low RKHS norm for generalization, a more in-depth exploration of techniques for controlling the RKHS norm during training could be beneficial. Expanding the experimental validation beyond theoretical analysis would further solidify the contributions. For instance, demonstrating the improved robustness or sample complexity on standard datasets with CNNs designed based on these insights would be impactful. The connection to adversarial robustness, touched upon briefly, could be explored in more detail. Finally, the numerous references (72) could be better organized for clarity and easier navigation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and rigorous mathematical analysis.
*   Novel extension of existing theoretical frameworks (scattering transforms) to a broader class of CNNs.
*   Clear connection between stability, invariance, and generalization.
*   Significant contribution to understanding the geometry of functional spaces underlying CNNs.
*   Well-written and detailed presentation, including comprehensive proofs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of practical implications and concrete design choices for CNNs.
*   Assumptions about activation function smoothness may limit applicability.
*   Lack of experimental validation to support the theoretical findings.
*   Potentially dense mathematical notation may hinder accessibility for some readers.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes significant contributions to the understanding of deep convolutional representations. The rigorous mathematical analysis and novel extensions of existing frameworks are commendable. While the connection to practical applications could be strengthened, the theoretical results are compelling and provide valuable insights into the underlying properties of CNNs. The paper is suitable for acceptance, with the recommendation that the authors address the aforementioned weaknesses in future work or through expanded discussion within the manuscript. Specifically, clarifying how the theoretical insights can inform CNN design choices and providing a more detailed discussion of the limitations related to activation function smoothness would be beneficial.
