This paper presents a theoretical framework for analyzing the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The analysis is grounded in the theory of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS). The central idea is to construct a multilayer convolutional kernel, which generalizes the construction of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), and to study the geometric properties of its associated functional space.

The main contributions are:
1.  A proof that the constructed representation is stable with respect to the action of small C1-diffeomorphisms, providing an explicit bound on the variation of the representation. This result is analogous to stability guarantees previously established for scattering transforms.
2.  A demonstration that the representation achieves progressive invariance to translations, with the degree of invariance controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer.
3.  An extension of the framework to achieve equivariance and invariance to general group actions, such as rotations, by defining appropriate patch extraction and pooling operators on the group.
4.  A key result connecting this theoretical kernel framework to practical Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). It is shown that the RKHS associated with the proposed kernel contains a large class of CNNs that use smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This allows the stability guarantees derived for the kernel representation to be extended to these CNN models.

The work builds directly upon two significant lines of research: the stability analysis of scattering networks (Mallat, 2012) and the framework of Convolutional Kernel Networks (Mairal et al., 2014, 2016). It successfully extends the rigorous harmonic analysis techniques from the former to the more flexible, learnable architectures related to the latter, thereby bridging a critical gap between theoretical signal processing models and deep learning practice.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental question in deep learning: what are the theoretical underpinnings of the excellent performance of CNNs, particularly their robustness to small input variations? By providing a rigorous mathematical framework that proves stability to diffeomorphisms and connects it directly to a class of CNNs, the work offers valuable theoretical insight. The link between the RKHS norm, stability, and generalization (Section 5) provides a principled way to think about regularization in these models.
*   **Quality:** The technical content is of high quality. The mathematical development is rigorous, and the claims are well-supported by detailed proofs (provided in the appendix). The assumptions required for the main stability theorem are clearly stated (A1-A3). The derivation, which involves bounding commutators of operators, is technically sound and represents a non-trivial extension of prior work to this kernel-based setting.
*   **Originality:** The primary originality lies in the synthesis of ideas from kernel methods, scattering transforms, and deep networks. While the tools are not entirely new, their application to build a unified framework that encompasses both CKNs and standard CNNs is novel. Showing that a functional space defined by a constructive kernel contains a class of CNNs is a powerful and elegant approach to transfer theoretical properties from the former to the latter.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with the construction of the kernel, proceeds to the stability analysis, and concludes with the important connection to CNNs. The notation, though dense, is consistent and well-defined. The motivation and relation to prior work are clearly articulated in the introduction.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Theoretical Nature and Lack of Empirical Validation:** The work is entirely theoretical. While this is not inherently a flaw for a contribution of this nature, the impact of the paper could be strengthened by some empirical evidence. For example, an experiment investigating the correlation between the derived RKHS norm bound for a CNN (from Proposition 7) and its empirically measured robustness to deformations or its generalization performance would provide a valuable link between the theory and practice. This is a "nice-to-have" suggestion that could be explored in future work.
*   **Assumptions on Activation Functions:** The connection to CNNs in Section 5 requires the use of smooth homogeneous activation functions. The most common activation, ReLU, is homogeneous but not smooth. The paper rightly points to smooth approximations of ReLU, but a more in-depth discussion of the practical implications of this choice would be beneficial. For instance, how does the performance of a network with a smoothed ReLU compare to one with a standard ReLU? Clarifying the extent to which this theoretical requirement limits the applicability of the results to state-of-the-art CNNs would be helpful.
*   **Interpretation of Stability Bounds:** Theorem 6 provides a clear stability bound, but the constants `C1` and `C2` are abstract. A more detailed discussion on how these constants depend on network parameters (e.g., patch size, properties of the pooling filter `h`) would enhance the practical interpretability of the result. This would help a reader better understand the trade-offs involved in designing a network for greater stability.
*   **Signal Norm Preservation:** The paper notes that, unlike scattering transforms, the proposed representation does not preserve the signal norm due to the pooling operators. While it is argued that this may not be a significant concern, the discussion could be slightly expanded. Lemma 1 suggests signal preservation is possible, but this relies on the invertibility of the pooling convolution, which can be ill-conditioned. A brief comment on the trade-off between achieving stability/invariance via pooling and the potential loss of signal information would be welcome.

In summary, this paper provides a significant and elegant theoretical contribution to our understanding of deep convolutional architectures. It establishes a rigorous connection between the geometry of a specially constructed RKHS and the desirable properties of stability and invariance in CNNs. The strengths in significance, quality, and originality are substantial. The weaknesses are minor and primarily relate to the scope of the work (being purely theoretical) and opportunities for further clarification. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance.
