- Paper develops a multilayer RKHS-based kernel representation for continuous-domain signals using patch extraction, norm-preserving kernel mapping, and Gaussian pooling.  
- Proves stability to small diffeomorphisms and near-translation invariance, with translation invariance controlled by final pooling scale.  
- Extends the framework to achieve equivariance/invariance to general group actions (e.g., rotations) via group-specific patch and pooling operators.  
- Establishes that a class of CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activations lie in the constructed RKHS, so the RKHS stability/invariance results apply to them.  
- Theoretical significance: provides a rigorous foundation explaining CNN robustness to small deformations, analogous to scattering transform analysis.  
- Unification: connects CNN architectures, kernel methods, and scattering-transform stability, linking network parameters to an RKHS norm that controls stability and generalization.  
- Generality: kernel construction extends to arbitrary locally compact groups, giving a unified perspective on building invariant representations.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and clearly situates contributions relative to prior work (CKNs, scattering).  
- Theory-practice gap: analysis restricts CNNs to smooth, homogeneous activations and a specific normalization term, leaving questions about applicability to common architectures (e.g., ReLU, batch norm).  
- Lack of empirical validation: entirely theoretical work would be strengthened by experiments validating stability bounds or relating RKHS norm to stability/generalization.  
- Signal norm preservation concern: pooling breaks L2 norm preservation and could cause significant energy loss in deep cascades; more formal analysis of norm retention is needed.  
- Discretization details need expansion: clearer mapping from continuous parameters (patch size, pooling scale, subsampling) to standard discrete layers (e.g., 3x3 filters, stride 2) would aid practical relevance.
