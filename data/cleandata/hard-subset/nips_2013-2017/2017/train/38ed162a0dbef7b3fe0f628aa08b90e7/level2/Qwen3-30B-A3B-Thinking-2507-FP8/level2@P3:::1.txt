

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, focusing on invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) to establish a functional space that contains both CKNs and a broad class of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The main contribution is a theoretical analysis demonstrating that representations from this functional space are stable to diffeomorphisms while preserving signal information, with stability properties directly transferable to predictive models through RKHS norm control.

The paper builds upon key prior work including Mallat's scattering transform for wavelet-based architectures [17], which provides stability guarantees but lacks learning capability, and CKNs [15, 16], which offer a kernel interpretation of CNNs but with limited theoretical analysis of stability properties. The authors successfully bridge these approaches by extending Mallat's stability framework to encompass standard CNN architectures through the kernel perspective.

The theoretical results are well-structured and rigorously proven. The main stability theorem (Theorem 6) provides a clear bound on the representation's sensitivity to diffeomorphisms, showing that stability is achieved through appropriate pooling operations and that translation invariance improves with layer depth. The authors carefully address the connection between the continuous theoretical framework and practical discretization through the CKN implementation. The proof techniques are well-motivated and extend Mallat's work on scattering transforms, with appropriate attention to the additional complexities introduced by the kernel framework.

The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing a precise mathematical connection between CNNs and kernel methods. Proposition 7 demonstrates that a broad class of CNNs with homogeneous activation functions resides within the RKHS defined by the authors' kernel construction. This provides a theoretical foundation for understanding why CNNs generalize well, as the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization. The paper effectively argues that stable representations (with small RKHS norm) should generalize better to unseen data, particularly for tasks involving natural signals where small deformations preserve class labels.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation throughout. The connection between theoretical analysis and practical implementation (Section 2.1 and 2.2) is well-executed, addressing the transition from continuous theory to discrete practice. The authors also thoughtfully discuss limitations, such as the lack of norm preservation in the representation, and propose a solution using an additional non-linear prediction layer.

The significance of this work lies in its theoretical unification of several strands of research on invariance and stability in convolutional architectures. By providing a common theoretical framework that encompasses both scattering transforms and CNNs, the paper offers new insights into why deep convolutional models work well in practice. The connection between stability and generalization has important implications for regularization strategies in deep learning, suggesting that stability constraints could be incorporated into training objectives to improve sample efficiency.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of the kernel approach versus standard CNN training, particularly regarding the practical feasibility of the kernel approximation scheme. While the authors acknowledge this as a potential limitation, a brief discussion of computational costs would strengthen the practical relevance of the framework. Additionally, the paper could more explicitly address how the theoretical stability guarantees translate to empirical performance on standard vision benchmarks, which would help contextualize the practical significance of the theoretical results.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep convolutional representations. The mathematical framework is sound, the results are novel and significant, and the connection between theory and practice is well-maintained. The work advances the state of the art by providing a unified theoretical perspective on invariance and stability in convolutional architectures that encompasses both CKNs and a broad class of CNNs. The results have the potential to guide future research on stable and sample-efficient deep learning models.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality at NIPS: it is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims, clearly written with appropriate organization, original in extending the scattering transform framework to encompass standard CNNs, and significant in establishing a theoretical foundation for understanding why convolutional architectures work well. The work represents a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of deep learning models.
