- Multilayer convolutional kernel framework producing near-invariance to group actions and Lipschitz stability to C1-diffeomorphisms while preserving signal information.

- Representation built by composing patch extraction, homogeneous dot-product kernel mappings to RKHSs, and Gaussian pooling; continuous-domain Φn and kernel Kn analyzed.

- Large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations shown to lie in the same RKHS, transferring stability guarantees via RKHS norm control.

- Extends scattering-transform stability results to a broader kernel-derived class that subsumes CKNs and captures a subset of CNNs.

- Main stability result (Theorem 6): ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞)‖x‖ under regularity and non-expansiveness assumptions.

- Proof approach: bound commutators [PkAk−1, Lτ] via Schur’s test and handle residual translation via standard bounds; extension to shifted integral operators is sound.

- Lemmas on signal preservation and discretization plausible, highlighting conditions (e.g., deconvolution stability, ek ≥ sk) for information retention.

- Proposition 7 links CNN stability/generalization to RKHS norms via recursive Cσ-dependent bounds, making norm control explicit.

- Technical development is careful and largely self-contained, though several proofs and refinements are deferred to appendices/extended version.

- Manuscript is generally clear and well organized; notation is dense but consistent; some constants (C1, C2) and admissible activation class need more explicit main-text treatment.

- Original contributions include commutator extension to shifted pooling, RKHS norm control for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, and group-equivariant pooling on general groups.

- Theoretical significance: offers a unifying perspective on invariance, stability, and generalization across kernel deep architectures and certain CNNs; practical impact would benefit from experiments.

- Pros: rigorous diffeomorphism stability with depth and pooling dependence; unified framework covering CKNs and some CNNs; careful continuous-to-discrete analysis; general group-equivariant layer design; clear assumptions.

- Cons: applicability limited to smooth homogeneous activations (standard ReLU handled only via smoothing); some constants and depth dependence may be loose; no empirical validation; signal preservation depends on strong deconvolution/anti-aliasing assumptions; border/discretization effects under-analyzed.

- Suggested improvements: list concrete admissible activations/kernels and plots; quantify constants and typical σ schedules for 2D; add small empirical deformation-robustness study; discuss projection errors across depth; clarify 2D discretization and border handling.

- Overall assessment: strong theoretical paper extending stability and invariance guarantees to kernel-derived deep representations and connecting them to CNNs via RKHS norms; merits acceptance despite missing experiments and restrictive activation assumptions.
