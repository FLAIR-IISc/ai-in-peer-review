Summary and relation to prior work: The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel representation that generalizes Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs) to continuous domains and analyzes its invariance to group actions and stability to diffeomorphisms. Using RKHS geometry, the authors prove a Lipschitz stability bound to C1-diffeomorphisms with explicit dependence on deformation magnitude and on the last pooling scale, paralleling and extending scattering theory (Mallat; Bruna & Mallat). They further design group-equivariant patch/pooling operators to obtain global invariance to compact groups, and show that a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lies in the induced RKHS, transferring the stability guarantees to these CNNs. The paper connects to prior NIPS work on CKNs, kernel views of deep nets, and group-equivariant CNNs, and positions the results relative to scattering (norm preservation vs pooling-induced attenuation), kernel approximations (non-expansive projections), and robustness (Parseval networks).

Strengths:
- Clear and rigorous theoretical framework that unifies scattering-style stability with kernelized deep representations.
- Thoughtful treatment of discretization and kernel approximation, with signal preservation lemmas and compatibility of stability with finite-dimensional projections.
- Valuable link showing CNNs with homogeneous smooth activations reside in the RKHS, tying stability to RKHS norms and suggesting generalization implications.
- Group-equivariant construction is principled and aligns with established harmonic analysis.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical validation is absent; no numerical evidence illustrating tightness of bounds, constants, or practical impact on robustness/generalization versus CNNs/scattering/CKNs.
- Assumptions may limit practical scope: smooth homogeneous activations (smoothed ReLU), normalization factors nk(u), A3 coupling patch and pooling scales, and the small-deformation regime ‖∇τ‖∞ < 1/2.
- The stability bound scales with depth (1+n) and depends on κ; discussion of how constants behave for realistic architectures is limited.
- The group-invariance section assumes signals on groups and ideal Haar-convolution; guidance for finite grids, boundary effects, and non-compact groups is brief.
- No quantitative sample-complexity results despite the RKHS-norm narrative.

Usefulness and significance: The results provide conceptual clarity and tools for reasoning about invariance/stability in CNN-like architectures, likely to influence theory and design of robust representations. Practical utility would be strengthened by experiments and recipes for choosing σk, ek, and projections.

Constructive suggestions:
- Include controlled experiments comparing deformation stability of CKNs/CNNs with and without the proposed design choices; quantify C1, C2 behavior.
- Discuss extensions to common components (ReLU, batch norm, strides, residual connections) and how to approximate them within the RKHS.
- Provide guidelines for setting σk and patch sizes under discretization, with sensitivity analyses.
- Explore finite-sample generalization bounds using the induced RKHS norm for the CNN class.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, novel theoretical contribution extending scattering-like guarantees to broader deep architectures.
- Bridges kernels and CNNs with clear insights into invariance, stability, and generalization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of empirical substantiation.
- Practical coverage limited by assumptions and unquantified constants.
