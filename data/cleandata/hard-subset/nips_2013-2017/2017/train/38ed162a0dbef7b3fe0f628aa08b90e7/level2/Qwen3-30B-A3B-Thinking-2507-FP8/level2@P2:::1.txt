

This paper investigates the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations through a kernel-based framework, extending the scattering transform theory to more general convolutional architectures including CNNs. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel that generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and prove stability guarantees under diffeomorphisms while preserving signal information. The key contribution is showing that this functional space contains a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions, allowing theoretical results to be translated to practical models.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs (provided in the appendix) for stability and invariance properties. The authors carefully distinguish between theoretical properties and practical implementations, addressing discretization and kernel approximations through convolutional kernel networks. The connection between RKHS norm and stability/generalization is particularly valuable, as it provides a principled way to control model complexity.

The work builds effectively on prior research, particularly Mallat's scattering transform [17] and Mairal's convolutional kernel networks [15]. The paper demonstrates clear understanding of the field's context and properly situates its contributions relative to existing literature. The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach (e.g., norm preservation is not maintained due to pooling, which they address through an additional non-linear prediction layer), demonstrating scholarly integrity.

The main strength of the paper is its elegant theoretical unification of scattering transforms and CNNs through a kernel framework. The stability results (Theorem 6) provide a precise mathematical characterization of how CNNs handle deformations, with clear dependence on pooling scales and network depth. The connection between RKHS norm and generalization is particularly insightful, suggesting that stable functions (with small RKHS norm) should generalize better.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its impact. First, the theoretical results are not validated with empirical evidence. While the authors discuss practical implementation through CKNs, they provide no experiments demonstrating how these stability properties manifest in actual CNN training and performance. This is a significant omission given the field's emphasis on empirical validation.

Second, the requirement for homogeneous activation functions (e.g., smoothed ReLU variants) is somewhat restrictive. While the authors acknowledge that ReLU is homogeneous, they note it's nonsmooth and thus doesn't directly fit their framework. The connection to standard CNNs could be strengthened by discussing how common activations might approximate their homogeneous requirements or by providing examples of CNN architectures that satisfy their assumptions.

Third, the paper is quite mathematically dense without sufficient intuitive explanations. While the mathematical rigor is appropriate for NIPS, some key concepts could benefit from more intuitive explanations or visualizations to enhance accessibility for a broader audience.

The significance of this work is substantial for understanding the theoretical properties of deep learning models. The framework provides a principled way to analyze and potentially design more stable CNNs, which could lead to improved sample complexity and robustness. The connection between stability and generalization via RKHS norm is particularly valuable for practitioners seeking to understand model behavior.

The paper's clarity is generally good, with consistent notation and logical flow. However, the density of mathematical notation might hinder readers who are not experts in functional analysis or harmonic analysis. Some of the technical details about the multilayer construction could be better motivated with simple examples.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a clear contribution by extending the scattering transform framework to a broader class of convolutional architectures. While related to prior work on CKNs [15, 16] and scattering networks [5, 17], the specific kernel-based framework that unifies these approaches with theoretical guarantees for stability and invariance represents a significant advancement.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding deep convolutional representations. To strengthen it further, the authors should consider adding a brief intuitive explanation of key concepts for non-specialists and addressing the empirical validation gap. The connection to standard CNN architectures could also be more explicitly developed.

The paper's main contribution lies in providing a theoretical framework that explains why certain CNN architectures exhibit stability to deformations, with clear mathematical characterization. This work will likely influence future research on the theoretical foundations of deep learning, particularly regarding stability and generalization.
