

This paper studies the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations through the lens of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS), generalizing the multilayer kernel from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) [15, 16]. The authors establish theoretical guarantees for stability to diffeomorphisms and invariance to group transformations, demonstrating that these properties extend to a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions. The work builds on Mallat's scattering transform [17] but extends it to more standard CNN architectures by using a kernel framework, which allows them to separate representation learning from prediction.

The main contributions include: (1) a generalization of the multilayer kernel to continuous domains; (2) stability bounds for the representation under diffeomorphisms; (3) a connection showing that CNNs with homogeneous activation functions reside in the same RKHS; and (4) an interpretation linking RKHS norms to stability and generalization.

Strengths:
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending stability results from the scattering transform to a broader class of CNNs.
- The mathematical analysis is rigorous, with well-motivated assumptions and careful proofs (e.g., Theorem 6 provides explicit stability bounds).
- The connection between RKHS norms and generalization is insightful, providing theoretical grounding for why CNNs generalize well.
- The authors properly contextualize their contributions relative to prior work on invariance (e.g., [6, 9, 17, 22]) and kernel methods (e.g., [15, 16, 27]).
- The paper carefully addresses limitations, noting that the representation doesn't preserve norm like the scattering transform does, but that this is less relevant for natural signals.

Weaknesses:
- The focus on continuous domains requires discretization for practical use. While Section 2.1 discusses discretization, a more detailed analysis of how theoretical guarantees translate to discrete implementations would strengthen practical relevance.
- The connection to practical CNNs relies on homogeneous activation functions, which are not standard in most modern CNNs (ReLU is homogeneous but non-smooth). While the authors note smoothed variants exist, a more concrete discussion of how this applies to popular architectures would be helpful.
- The paper lacks empirical validation of theoretical claims. While theoretical work is valuable, some experimental results demonstrating stability properties in practice would strengthen the contribution.
- The related work section thoroughly covers relevant literature but could better distinguish this approach from recent kernel-based CNN approaches (e.g., [2, 3, 10, 18]).

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical arguments are clear. The organization flows logically from problem statement to theoretical development to practical implications. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges and how theoretical results translate to discrete CNNs.

Regarding the four criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims (4/4).
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though some sections could benefit from more concrete examples (3/4).
- Originality: The paper extends previous work on invariance to a broader class of CNNs through a kernel framework, providing new theoretical insights (4/4).
- Significance: The results provide theoretical grounding for why CNNs work well, connecting stability to generalization with important implications for designing more robust architectures (4/4).

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding deep convolutional representations. The stability results provide a valuable framework for analyzing and improving CNNs. While the paper could be strengthened with more practical implementation details and empirical validation, the theoretical contributions are substantial and well-executed. I recommend acceptance.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, rigorous analysis, novel connection between RKHS norms and stability/generalization, well-contextualized in related work.
Con: Limited discussion of practical implementation details, lacks empirical validation, homogeneous activation function requirement may limit immediate applicability to standard CNNs.
