

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, focusing on invariance to group transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms. The main claim is that by generalizing the multilayer kernel framework from convolutional kernel networks (CKNs), the authors establish a functional space containing a large class of CNNs that exhibit stability properties similar to the scattering transform [17], while preserving signal information. The paper demonstrates that the RKHS norm controls both stability and generalization, potentially leading to improved sample complexity.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution, providing rigorous mathematical proofs for stability bounds under diffeomorphisms (Theorem 6) and showing how these results extend to CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations (Proposition 7). The connection between the kernel framework and CNNs is particularly valuable, as it bridges theoretical analysis with practical deep learning architectures. The paper effectively builds on Mallat's work [17] while extending it to more general CNN architectures, demonstrating a clear understanding of the relevant literature.

The theoretical analysis is sound, with well-constructed proofs and appropriate mathematical tools. The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, noting that while the continuous framework provides theoretical insights, practical implementations require discretization and kernel approximations (Section 2.1-2.2). The authors also correctly note that their norm preservation results differ from the scattering transform but are mitigated by using additional non-linear prediction layers.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it provides a principled theoretical foundation for understanding why CNNs work well, potentially guiding future model design. The connection between RKHS norm, stability, and generalization offers new insights for practitioners seeking to build more robust models. While the paper is purely theoretical with limited experimental validation (as expected for a theoretical contribution), the mathematical rigor and clear exposition make it a valuable addition to the literature.

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear mathematical notation and sufficient detail for experts to follow the proofs. The references to related work are comprehensive and appropriately contextualized, demonstrating strong field knowledge.

Overall, this is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of deep convolutional representations. It makes a significant original contribution by connecting kernel methods to CNN theory and establishing stability properties for a broad class of models. I recommend acceptance for its theoretical soundness, novelty, and significance to the field.
