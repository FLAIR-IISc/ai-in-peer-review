This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional representations, specifically their invariance and stability. The authors generalize the multilayer kernel introduced in the context of convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and analyze the geometry of the associated reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). They aim to provide theoretical guarantees for invariance to transformations and stability to diffeomorphisms, extending results from the scattering transform to a broader class of CNNs. The work is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question about the geometry of functional spaces underlying deep networks.

**Summary of Related Work:** The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, referencing key contributions in kernel methods, CKNs, and the scattering transform. The discussion of related work is comprehensive, covering both theoretical and empirical studies of invariance and stability in CNNs.  The connection to prior work on kernelization of deep networks is also appropriately highlighted.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper presents a mathematically rigorous analysis of invariance and stability, building on solid foundations in kernel methods and harmonic analysis. The proofs appear carefully constructed, and the authors provide sufficient detail (with the majority relegated to appendices).
*   **Generalization of Scattering Transform:**  Extending stability results from the scattering transform, which relies on pre-defined filters, to more general CNN architectures is a significant contribution.
*   **Connection to CNNs:** Establishing a link between the kernel framework and CNNs with smooth homogeneous activation functions allows the theoretical results to be applied to practical architectures. This bridges the gap between theoretical analysis and commonly used models.
*   **Clear Presentation:** Despite the mathematical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the main ideas are clearly explained.
* **Discretization discussion:** The detailed discussion about discretization and how to preserve signal information in the discrete setting adds to the practical relevance of the theoretical work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The stability results rely on several assumptions (e.g., smoothness of activations, regularity of diffeomorphisms). While common in this type of analysis, it would be beneficial to discuss the sensitivity of the results to violations of these assumptions, or to investigate relaxations. The assumption on homogeneous activation functions is restrictive, and more discussion on how this affects applicability is needed.
*   **Practical Implications:** While the paper establishes theoretical guarantees, the practical implications are not fully explored. It remains unclear how these results can be directly used to improve the design or training of CNNs. For example, the authors mention the RKHS norm controlling generalization, but lack a practical method to control this norm during training.
*   **Computational Cost:** The kernel-based approach, while theoretically insightful, may be computationally expensive in practice.  The discussion of CKNs as a practical approximation is helpful, but the scalability of these approximations to large-scale datasets remains a concern. The paper doesn't fully address this challenge.
* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper is heavily theoretical and lacks empirical validation. Some experiments demonstrating the stability properties on real-world datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **Homogenization:** The need for homogenization of activations seems artificial, and potentially limiting to real-world applications. While the authors acknowledge the limitation (e.g., ReLU is not directly included), the rationale and impact need further justification.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound analysis of invariance and stability in deep convolutional representations.
*   It extends important results from the scattering transform to a broader class of CNNs.
*   It provides valuable insights into the geometry of the functional spaces underlying deep learning models.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper relies on several restrictive assumptions.
*   The practical implications of the theoretical results are not fully explored.
*   The lack of empirical validation weakens the claims.
*   The computational cost of the kernel-based approach may be prohibitive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of deep convolutional representations. While the assumptions and lack of empirical validation are weaknesses, the theoretical rigor and novel insights outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance, but I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses in future work by exploring the sensitivity of the results to different assumptions and investigating practical applications of the theoretical findings, potentially through some targeted experiments.  I rate this paper as a **Strong Accept**.
