- Paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel on continuous domains that is Lipschitz-stable to diffeomorphisms and nearly translation/group-invariant via equivariant patch extraction and pooling.
- Construction generalizes CKNs and embeds a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations into the associated RKHS.
- Theorem 6 gives Lipschitz-type deformation stability with explicit dependence on depth and last-layer pooling scale; commutator analysis extends scattering proofs to shifted integral operators.
- Discretization and finite-dimensional kernel projections are treated, with non-expansive projections preserving stability.
- Proposition 7 embeds CNNs into the RKHS with a recursive RKHS-norm bound linking stability and generalization.
- Theoretical development is careful, using established tools (Schur’s test, Haar integration) and matching desired stability properties.
- Commutator analysis and extension of scattering-style arguments to the kernel setting are technically sound.
- Discretization and projection steps are well tied to the continuous theory, ensuring stability is not degraded.
- Embedding of CNNs into the RKHS with norm control is technically interesting and potentially impactful for norm-based generalization.
- Signal-preservation and deconvolution claims rest on assumptions that make inversion ill-posed in practice; stability under noise/discretization is not quantified.
- Lemma 2 mentions stability only for sufficiently small scales but provides no explicit condition-number or quantitative bound.
- Restrictions to smooth homogeneous activations exclude plain ReLU; ReLU is only treated via smoothing, leaving coverage of standard CNNs unclear.
- Stability constants depend on kernel shape and κ with possibly unfavorable κ^(d+1) scaling; magnitudes are not discussed.
- No empirical validation is provided to illustrate bound tightness or practical usefulness versus scattering or equivariant CNNs.
- Paper is well organized and readable with clear operator notation and consistent layer pipeline (P, M, A).
- Some sections (signal preservation, discretization) need more explicit statements on stability vs identifiability and noise assumptions.
- Novelty includes extending scattering-theoretic stability to broader multilayer kernels and embedding CNNs into RKHS with commutator analysis for shifted operators.
- Group-equivariant layer construction within the kernel/RKHS framework adds originality beyond prior CKN and equivariant CNN work.
- Conceptually unifies kernels, CNNs, and scattering under a single stability framework and connects representation stability to predictor smoothness via RKHS norms.
- Practical impact would be stronger with empirical evidence and tighter constants/assumptions.
- Pros: principled generalization of scattering stability, bridges CKNs and CNNs via RKHS, clean general group-invariance construction, stability preserved under approximations.
- Cons: no experiments, unstable deconvolution-based preservation claims, restrictions to smooth activations and unclear coverage of engineering practices (strides, max pooling), unquantified constants/dimensional dependencies.
- Suggestion: add empirical illustrations measuring stability to diffeomorphisms and correlation of RKHS norm with generalization on deformed datasets.
- Suggestion: clarify handling or extension to strided convolutions, max pooling, and non-Gaussian bounded pooling.
- Suggestion: quantify constants C1, C2 and dependence on kernel parameters/dimension with example numeric values.
- Suggestion: analyze stability of deconvolution recovery under discretization and noise or reformulate preservation as injectivity with stability margins.
- Suggestion: detail how smoothed ReLU approximates ReLU in the RKHS and provide concrete smoothing choices and bounds.
- Suggestion: compare RKHS-norm-based control to spectral/Parseval Lipschitz controls and state when RKHS offers complementary advantages.
- Recommendation: lean toward acceptance—solid theoretical contribution contingent on addressing clarity issues and, ideally, minimal empirical validation.
