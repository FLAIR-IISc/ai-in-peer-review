Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework on continuous domains that yields signal representations which are (i) stable to diffeomorphisms in the Lipschitz sense and (ii) can be made nearly invariant to translations and, more generally, to actions of locally compact groups via appropriate group-equivariant patch extraction and pooling. The construction generalizes convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and is shown to contain, as elements of the associated RKHS, a broad class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations. The authors derive bounds showing that deformation sensitivity scales linearly with the deformation’s Jacobian and that translation sensitivity decays with the last pooling scale; they extend scattering-style arguments (Mallat 2012, Bruna & Mallat 2013) to their kernel setting, while decoupling representation stability from predictor smoothness via RKHS norms. They also discuss discretization, kernel approximations (projections), and prove that stability is preserved under these approximations. Section 4 formulates global invariance to group actions by building layers that commute with the group action, akin to (but more general than) group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling 2016), and relates inner-layer pooling to local stability vs last-layer pooling to global invariance. Finally, they embed CNNs with suitable activations into the RKHS of their multilayer kernel and bound their RKHS norms, linking generalization and stability.

Quality:
- The theoretical development is careful and builds on established tools (Schur’s test, commutator bounds, Haar integration). The main stability theorem (Theorem 6) matches the desired Lipschitz-type stability to diffeomorphisms with an explicit dependence on depth and last-layer pooling scale. The commutator analysis extends the scattering proofs to shifted integral operators.
- The discretization and finite-dimensional projection steps are discussed and tied back to the continuous theory. The non-expansiveness of projections ensures stability is not degraded.
- The embedding of CNNs into the RKHS (Proposition 7) with a recursive norm bound is technically interesting and potentially impactful for norm-based generalization control.

Caveats:
- Several key claims about “signal preservation” (Lemma 1) and recovery via deconvolution rely on assumptions that make inversion ill-posed in practice (e.g., Gaussian blur is invertible in L2 but unstable). Stability of these inversions under noise and discretization is not quantified; later Lemma 2 acknowledges stability only “when the scale is small enough,” but no explicit condition-number analysis is provided.
- The assumptions for the CNN link restrict to smooth homogeneous activations; ReLU is handled only via smoothed variants. It would help to clarify how tight the resulting function class is relative to standard CNNs with ReLU, striding, and max pooling.
- The constants in the stability bound depend on kernel shape and the κ parameter controlling patch diameter relative to pooling, with a potentially unfavorable κ^(d+1) scaling; no discussion of their magnitude is given.
- No empirical validation is provided to illustrate the tightness or usefulness of the bounds, or to compare with scattering and group-equivariant CNNs on tasks where deformations matter.

Clarity:
The paper is well organized and readable, with clear operator notation and a consistent pipeline (P, M, A) across layers. The relation to prior work is appropriate, and the technical choices (homogeneous dot-product kernels, Gaussian pooling) are well motivated. Some sections (signal preservation, discretization) could benefit from more explicit statements of stability vs identifiability and explicit assumptions on noise.

Originality:
The work extends scattering-theoretic stability guarantees beyond wavelet-based, norm-preserving transforms to a broader multilayer kernel class and shows how a family of CNNs sits within the corresponding RKHS with norm control. The commutator analysis for shifted integral operators and the explicit group-equivariant layer construction within the kernel/RKHS framework add novelty beyond prior CKN papers and group-equivariant CNNs.

Significance:
Conceptually, the paper contributes to our theoretical understanding of deep convolutional representations by unifying kernels, CNNs, and scattering under a single stability framework, and by connecting representation stability to predictor smoothness via RKHS norms. This can inform regularization strategies (e.g., norm control) and architectural choices (pooling scales). The practical impact would be strengthened by empirical evidence and tighter constants/assumptions.

Pros:
- Clear, principled generalization of scattering stability to a broader class of convolutional representations.
- Bridges CKNs and a nontrivial class of CNNs through RKHS embeddings and norm bounds.
- Group-invariance construction is clean and general, aligning with equivariance/invariance literature.
- Stability preserved under finite-dimensional kernel approximations and discretization (non-expansive projections).

Cons:
- No experiments; empirical relevance and tightness of bounds are not demonstrated.
- “Signal preservation” and recovery results hinge on unstable deconvolutions; practical stability is not analyzed.
- Restrictions to smooth homogeneous activations; unclear coverage of standard ReLU networks with common engineering choices (strides, max pooling, batch norm).
- Constants and dimensional dependencies are not quantified, making practical guidance difficult.

Questions and suggestions:
- Provide empirical illustrations (e.g., CKN or CNN instantiations) showing measured stability to diffeomorphisms vs pooling scale, and whether RKHS norm correlates with generalization on deformed datasets.
- Clarify the extent to which strided convolutions and max pooling fit into the framework or how the results extend to them; can non-Gaussian pooling with bounded support be accommodated?
- Quantify constants C1, C2 and their dependence on kernel parameters and dimension; provide example numerical values.
- Discuss stability of the deconvolution-based recovery steps under discretization and noise; if ill-posed, reformulate “signal preservation” as injectivity up to bandlimit with explicit stability margins.
- Elaborate on how close smoothed ReLU activations can approximate ReLU within the RKHS while maintaining manageable Cσ; provide concrete σ-smoothing choices and bounds.
- Compare and contrast with Parseval/spectral norm-based Lipschitz control for CNNs; when does the RKHS norm offer a sharper or complementary handle?

Recommendation:
This is a solid theoretical paper with a clear contribution to the analysis of deep convolutional representations. Despite the lack of experiments and some restrictive assumptions, the conceptual advances and technical results are valuable. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the clarity issues and, ideally, adding minimal empirical validation.
