This paper presents a theoretical analysis of deep convolutional representations, focusing on their invariance to transformations and stability to small input deformations. The authors achieve this by generalizing the convolutional kernel network (CKN) framework to define a multilayer convolutional kernel for signals on continuous domains. The primary contributions are: 1) proving that the resulting signal representation is Lipschitz stable to the action of diffeomorphisms, a property previously established for scattering networks; 2) showing that near-invariance to translations is achieved, with the degree of invariance controlled by the scale of the final pooling layer; and 3) demonstrating that the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) associated with this kernel contains a broad class of standard CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions, thereby extending the stability guarantees to these models.

The work is technically sound and builds upon a strong foundation of prior research, most notably adapting the analytical framework of Mallat's scattering transforms to the more general and learnable setting of CKNs. This unification of signal processing perspectives, kernel methods, and deep learning is a significant strength and represents a novel contribution to our theoretical understanding of CNNs. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, guiding the reader through the complex construction of the kernel and the subsequent stability analysis. The theoretical results are important, as they provide a principled connection between the RKHS norm of a network function, its stability to deformations, and, by extension, its generalization properties.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses. The primary limitation is the gap between the theoretical model and practical CNN implementations. The link to CNNs requires specific assumptions, such as smooth homogeneous activations and a non-standard normalization term, which deviate from the widely used (non-smooth) ReLU activation. While smoothed alternatives are discussed, a more thorough exploration of this discrepancy would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the analysis is entirely theoretical. The inclusion of even simple experiments to empirically validate the stability bounds or demonstrate the trade-off between pooling scale and invariance would make the theoretical claims more concrete and impactful.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides a rigorous and novel theoretical framework for analyzing the stability and invariance of CNNs.
*   Successfully unifies concepts from scattering networks, kernel methods, and deep learning.
*   The results offer important insights into the relationship between network regularization, robustness, and generalization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The connection to practical CNNs relies on assumptions that create a gap with common practice.
*   The lack of any empirical validation leaves the theoretical findings uncorroborated by practice.

Despite these minor shortcomings, the paper's theoretical novelty and significance are substantial, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
