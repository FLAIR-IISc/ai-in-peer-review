- Studies invariance and stability of deep convolutional representations via RKHS, generalizing the multilayer kernel from CKNs.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees for stability to diffeomorphisms and invariance to group transformations for CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations.  
- Extends Mallat’s scattering transform to more standard CNN architectures using a kernel framework that separates representation learning from prediction.  
- Main contributions: (1) multilayer kernel generalized to continuous domains; (2) stability bounds under diffeomorphisms; (3) CNNs with homogeneous activations lie in the same RKHS; (4) RKHS norms linked to stability and generalization.  
- Strength: significant theoretical extension of scattering transform stability to a broader class of CNNs.  
- Strength: rigorous mathematical analysis with well-motivated assumptions and explicit bounds (e.g., Theorem 6).  
- Strength: insightful connection between RKHS norms and why CNNs generalize well.  
- Strength: thorough contextualization relative to prior invariance and kernel literature.  
- Strength: authors acknowledge and discuss limitations (e.g., representation not norm-preserving) and relevance to natural signals.  
- Weakness: continuous-domain focus requires discretization for practice; translation of guarantees to discrete implementations needs more detail.  
- Weakness: reliance on smooth homogeneous activations limits applicability to standard CNNs (ReLU is homogeneous but non-smooth); more concrete discussion needed.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical validation demonstrating theoretical claims in practice.  
- Weakness: related work could better distinguish this approach from recent kernel-based CNN methods.  
- Writing and organization: well-written, consistent notation, clear proofs and logical flow, but could use more concrete examples and implementation discussion.  
- Evaluation scores: Quality 4/4, Clarity 3/4, Originality 4/4, Significance 4/4.  
- Recommendation: important theoretical contribution and recommend acceptance; pros—strong theory and novel RKHS link to stability/generalization; cons—limited practical details, no experiments, activation-function constraints.
