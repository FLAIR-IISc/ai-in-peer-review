This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the invariance and stability properties of deep convolutional representations. The authors introduce a multilayer convolutional kernel, generalizing the framework of Convolutional Kernel Networks (CKNs), to operate on continuous signals. The main contributions of the paper are threefold. First, by analyzing the geometry of the corresponding Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), the authors prove that this representation is stable to the action of small C1-diffeomorphisms and nearly invariant to translations. This result is analogous to the stability guarantees established for the scattering transform, but it applies to a more general, learnable architecture. Second, the framework is extended to achieve equivariance and invariance to general locally compact group actions, such as rotations. Third, and perhaps most importantly, the paper establishes a formal link between this kernel framework and standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). It demonstrates that the RKHS associated with the proposed kernel contains a large class of CNNs with smooth, homogeneous activation functions. This connection allows the stability and invariance guarantees derived for the kernel representation to be directly applied to these CNNs, providing a principled understanding of their geometric properties.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** This paper provides a rigorous and much-needed theoretical foundation for understanding the stability and invariance of deep convolutional architectures. It successfully bridges the gap between the purely mathematical framework of the scattering transform (which uses fixed filters) and more practical, learnable models like CNNs. The analysis offers valuable insights into how architectural choices, such as pooling scale and network depth, directly impact these crucial geometric properties.

2.  **Novel Connection between CNNs and Kernel Methods:** The most compelling aspect of this work is the connection established in Section 5 between the proposed RKHS and a class of CNNs. By showing that certain CNN functions are elements of this RKHS, the paper provides a powerful theoretical tool. The RKHS norm is shown to control the stability of the function, which elegantly links the concepts of regularization, generalization (via margin bounds), and geometric stability to input deformations. This is a profound and useful insight for the deep learning community.

3.  **Clarity and Rigor:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and mathematically precise. The construction of the multilayer kernel is laid out logically, and the proofs for the stability theorems are well-structured, building upon and extending previous work from harmonic analysis and signal processing in a clear manner. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the connections between the abstract continuous-domain theory and its practical discrete-domain counterparts (CKNs).

4.  **Generality:** The proposed framework is general. It is not limited to 2D images but applies to d-dimensional signals. Furthermore, Section 4 demonstrates how the core ideas of patch extraction and pooling can be generalized to achieve invariance to arbitrary group actions, which is a topic of growing interest in the field.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Gap between Theory and Practice for CNNs:** The class of CNNs shown to reside in the RKHS requires a non-standard activation function of the form `nk(u)σ(〈wik, Pkzk-1(u)〉/nk(u))`, which includes a patch-norm-dependent term `nk(u)`. While the authors correctly point out that the popular ReLU activation is homogeneous, it is non-smooth, and standard CNNs do not include this explicit normalization within the activation. It would be helpful if the authors could comment on the significance of this architectural deviation. How critical is this normalization for the theory to hold, and what are the potential implications of this for understanding standard, off-the-shelf CNN architectures?

2.  **Absence of Empirical Validation:** The paper is purely theoretical. While the quality of the theory is high, the claims could have been further strengthened with some empirical validation. For instance, an experiment demonstrating the trade-off between the final pooling scale (`σn`) and translation invariance, or showing that regularizing a CNN's weights in a way that relates to the derived RKHS norm bound (Proposition 7) leads to improved stability against deformations, would make the work's practical relevance more tangible.

3.  **Impact of Norm Reduction:** Unlike the scattering transform, the proposed representation is not norm-preserving due to the pooling operators (`Ak`). The authors argue that this may not be a major concern for natural signals or can be mitigated by an additional layer. However, since the stability bound `‖Φ(Lτx)− Φ(x)‖` is relative to `‖x‖`, a significant reduction in `‖Φ(x)‖` due to repeated pooling could mean that the representation loses signal energy. Could the authors elaborate on how this progressive loss of energy might affect the quality of the representation and the interpretation of the stability bounds, especially in very deep networks?

**Overall Evaluation:**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of deep learning. The work is original, technically sound, and provides deep insights into the geometric properties of convolutional networks by elegantly leveraging tools from kernel methods and harmonic analysis. The connection it forges between the stability of a network, its RKHS norm, and generalization is particularly insightful. Despite the minor weaknesses concerning the gap with current CNN practices and the lack of experiments, the theoretical contributions are strong enough to stand on their own. This paper is of high quality and is likely to be influential. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
