This paper investigates the theoretical properties of deep convolutional architectures, aiming to understand their invariance and stability from a kernel perspective. The authors generalize convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) and study the geometry of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). They demonstrate stability to diffeomorphisms and near-invariance to group transformations, and connect these properties to potential improvements in sample complexity.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question regarding the inner workings of CNNs. It’s positioned well within the existing literature, referencing relevant works on scattering transforms, CKNs, and kernel methods for deep learning. The authors clearly articulate their goals and how their approach extends previous research. The connection to stability and generalization, via the RKHS norm, is a compelling contribution. The thoroughness of the theoretical analysis, including detailed proofs (relegated to the appendix), is commendable. 

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of the properties of the proposed kernel framework. The use of RKHS theory and the detailed proofs contribute to the credibility of the findings.
* **Generalization of CKNs:** Extending the concepts of CKNs to more general convolutional architectures is a valuable contribution. Showing that a large class of CNNs fall within this framework broadens the applicability of the theoretical results.
* **Stability and Invariance Properties:**  Demonstrating stability to diffeomorphisms and near-invariance to group transformations provides theoretical justification for the robustness observed in CNNs.
* **Link to Generalization:**  Connecting stability (via RKHS norm) to generalization potential offers a novel perspective on regularization and sample complexity in deep learning.
* **Clear Presentation:** Despite the mathematical complexity, the paper is relatively well-written and organized.  The use of notation is consistent, and the structure facilitates understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The paper primarily focuses on theoretical results. While the authors suggest potential implications for practical applications, there's a lack of empirical validation to support these claims. It would be beneficial to demonstrate these properties on real-world datasets, even with a small-scale experiment.  Specifically, the practical implications of the theoretical bounds (e.g., Theorem 6) are unclear without some experimental validation.
* **Complexity & Accessibility:** While well-organized, the paper's mathematical depth makes it challenging for readers unfamiliar with RKHS theory and harmonic analysis. More intuitive explanations or simplified examples could improve accessibility.
* **Practicality of Kernel Approximation:** The discussion of approximating the kernel in Section 2.2 touches on the computational challenges of using the full kernel framework. However, a more detailed discussion of practical methods for approximation and their impact on the theoretical guarantees would be valuable. 
* **Homogeneous Activations Restriction:**  The requirement of homogeneous activations (Section 5) feels somewhat limiting. While the authors acknowledge ReLU and point to smoothed variants, it's unclear how restrictive this constraint is in practice and whether the theoretical benefits extend to commonly used non-homogeneous activations.
* **Comparison with Existing Regularization Techniques:** The link between RKHS norm and generalization is interesting, but the paper doesn’t deeply connect this to existing regularization techniques (e.g., weight decay, dropout) in the CNN literature. How does controlling the RKHS norm relate to these established methods?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental question in deep learning with a rigorous theoretical approach.
* The results are novel and contribute to a deeper understanding of the properties of CNNs.
* The work extends existing concepts and provides a valuable framework for analyzing convolutional architectures.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of empirical validation limits the practical impact of the theoretical findings.
* The mathematical complexity might hinder accessibility for a broad audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. While the lack of empirical validation is a concern, the theoretical contributions are significant and the analysis is thorough. The paper provides a valuable framework for understanding the stability and invariance properties of CNNs, and the link to generalization is a promising avenue for future research. Addressing the weaknesses in future work (e.g., through experiments and improved accessibility) would further strengthen the contribution. I encourage the authors to consider incorporating empirical results or a more detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges in a revised version.
