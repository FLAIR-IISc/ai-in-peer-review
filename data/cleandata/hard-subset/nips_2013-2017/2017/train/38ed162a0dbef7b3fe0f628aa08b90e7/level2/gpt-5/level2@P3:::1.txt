Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops a multilayer convolutional kernel framework that yields deep signal representations with two key properties: near-invariance to group actions (e.g., translations and more general groups) and Lipschitz stability to C1-diffeomorphisms, while preserving signal information along the hierarchy. The construction parallels convolutional kernel networks (CKNs) by composing patch extraction (Pk), kernel mappings to RKHSs (Mk) via homogeneous dot-product kernels, and Gaussian pooling (Ak). A continuous-domain analysis yields a representation Φn whose induced kernel Kn defines an RKHS; stability and invariance of Φn are then established via commutator bounds and operator norms. Importantly, a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations is shown to lie in the same RKHS, so the same stability guarantees extend to these predictive models with norm-controlled Lipschitz constants.

The work builds on the scattering transform literature (Bruna & Mallat, PAMI 2013; Mallat, 2012), which established Lipschitz stability to diffeomorphisms and translation invariance for wavelet-based, non-learned deep transforms with norm preservation. It also connects to CKNs (Mairal et al., NIPS 2014; Mairal, NIPS 2016), group-equivariant CNNs (Cohen & Welling, ICML 2016), and hierarchical kernel viewpoints (Anselmi et al.). The main advance is extending scattering-style stability guarantees to a broader, kernel-derived class that includes CKNs and a subset of CNNs, together with a careful continuous-to-discrete bridge and group-equivariant pooling on general locally compact groups.

Quality:
The technical development is careful and largely self-contained. The key stability result (Theorem 6) gives ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ ≤ (C1(1+n)‖∇τ‖∞ + (C2/σn)‖τ‖∞)‖x‖, under standard regularity (‖∇τ‖∞ ≤ 1/2) and kernel mapping non-expansiveness assumptions. The proof strategy—bounding commutators [PkAk−1, Lτ] via Schur’s test and handling the residual translation term via a standard bound (Lemma 5)—is sound and extends prior scattering analyses to shifted integral operators. Lemmas on signal preservation and discretization are plausible and highlight conditions (e.g., deconvolution stability, ek ≥ sk) under which information is not lost. The link to CNNs (Proposition 7) provides norm bounds via a recursive Cσ-dependent quantity, making precise how stability/generalization can be controlled by the RKHS norm. Some proofs and refinements are deferred to appendices or an extended version, which is common for NIPS-length papers.

Clarity:
The manuscript is generally clear and well organized. The continuous model, discretization (Section 2.1), and kernel approximation (Section 2.2) are delineated, and assumptions (A1–A3) are explicit. Notation is dense but consistent. A few places could be improved: making Cσ and the admissible activation class more explicit in the main text; providing more intuition for constants (C1’s dependence on κ and d, C2 on ‖∇h‖1); and tightening the bridge from continuous to 2D discrete images, including border effects.

Originality:
The analysis extends scattering-style diffeomorphism stability to a new kernel-based deep representation that subsumes CKNs and captures a class of CNNs via RKHS embedding. The commutator extension to shifted pooling operators and the RKHS norm control of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations add novel connections between deep networks, kernels, and stability/generalization. The group-equivariant pooling construction on general groups is consistent with but distinct from prior equivariant CNN constructions.

Significance:
The theoretical contribution is relevant to the NeurIPS community, offering a unifying perspective on invariance, stability, and generalization across kernel deep architectures and certain CNNs. It may inform principled design choices (pooling scales σk, patch sizes, activation smoothing) and suggests that stability can be regulated via RKHS norms. The practical impact would be strengthened by empirical illustrations, but the theoretical advance stands on its own.

Pros:
- Rigorous stability to diffeomorphisms with explicit dependence on depth and final pooling scale; translation invariance increases with σn.
- Unified kernel framework encompassing CKNs and a class of CNNs, transferring stability via RKHS norm control.
- Careful continuous-to-discrete analysis and conditions for information preservation under subsampling and pooling.
- General group-equivariant layer design enabling global invariance and local stability on groups beyond translations.
- Solid technical tools and clear assumptions (non-expansiveness, homogeneous kernels).

Cons:
- Applicability restricted by activation class (smooth, homogeneous); standard ReLU is handled only via smoothed variants.
- Some constants and depth dependence (linear in n, κd+1 growth) might be loose; no quantitative evaluation of bounds.
- No empirical validation (e.g., deformation robustness curves, CKN/CNN comparisons) to illustrate theoretical claims.
- Signal preservation relies on stable deconvolution and an initial anti-aliasing assumption x0 = A0x; border/discretization effects in 2D are not analyzed in detail.
- Several details deferred to appendices or an extended version.

Suggestions for improvement (feasible within a revision cycle):
- Provide concrete examples of admissible activations and kernels in the main text, with plots and a brief discussion of approximation to ReLU and its implications.
- Quantify typical constants for 2D images and standard σ schedules, and discuss practical ranges for κ and ek, sk.
- Add a small empirical study: measure ‖Φ(Lτx)−Φ(x)‖ versus ‖∇τ‖∞ and σn on synthetic deformations; compare smoothed-ReLU CNNs/CKNs to standard CNNs on deformation robustness.
- Elaborate on how projection errors in finite-dimensional CKN layers compound across depth and interact with stability.
- Clarify the discretization mapping for 2D images, including handling of borders and padding.

Overall assessment:
A strong theoretical paper that extends stability and invariance guarantees to a broad, kernel-derived class of deep convolutional representations and connects these properties to CNNs via RKHS norms. Despite the lack of experiments and some restrictive assumptions on activations, the contribution is solid and of clear interest to NeurIPS. Arguments favor acceptance.
