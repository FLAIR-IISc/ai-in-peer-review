- Goal: The paper analyzes invariance and stability of deep convolutional representations by generalizing multilayer kernels and studying the RKHS geometry.  
- Positioning: Related work is well-situated, citing kernel methods, CKNs, and scattering transform literature.  
- Theoretical rigor: Presents careful, mathematically rigorous proofs grounded in kernel methods and harmonic analysis.  
- Generalization of scattering: Extends stability results from scattering transforms to broader CNN architectures.  
- Connection to CNNs: Links the kernel framework to CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations, bridging theory and practice.  
- Presentation: Clear writing, consistent notation, and well-organized exposition with detailed appendices.  
- Discretization: Provides a useful discussion on discretization and preserving signal information in discrete settings.  
- Assumptions: Stability results depend on restrictive assumptions (smooth activations, regular diffeomorphisms, homogeneity) that limit applicability.  
- Homogenization concern: Requiring homogeneous activations (excluding common functions like ReLU) seems artificial and potentially limiting.  
- Practical implications: Lacks concrete methods to leverage theoretical guarantees (e.g., how to control RKHS norm during training).  
- Computational cost: Kernel-based approach may be computationally expensive and scalability of CKN approximations is not fully addressed.  
- Empirical validation: Heavily theoretical with limited or no experiments to demonstrate claimed stability on real data.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound contributions that extend scattering results and deepen understanding of function-space geometry.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Reliance on restrictive assumptions, weak practical guidance, limited empirical evidence, and potential computational infeasibility.  
- Overall assessment: Strong theoretical paper meriting acceptance, but authors should address assumption sensitivity, practical applicability, and add empirical validation in future work.
