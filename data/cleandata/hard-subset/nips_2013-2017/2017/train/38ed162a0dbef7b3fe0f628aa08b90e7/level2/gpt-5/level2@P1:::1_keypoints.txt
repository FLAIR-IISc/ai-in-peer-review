- Proposes a multilayer convolutional kernel for continuous-domain signals yielding group-pooling invariance and Lipschitz stability to small diffeomorphisms.  
- Layer pipeline: patch extraction, norm-preserving homogeneous kernel map, and linear Gaussian pooling; generalizes CKNs and studies the RKHS geometry.  
- Main theoretical results: information preservation under sampling, scattering-like stability bounds with translation-invariance improving with pooling scale.  
- Shows stability is retained under discretization and finite-dimensional kernel approximations used in practice.  
- Extends framework to general locally compact groups via group-equivariant patching and pooling for equivariance/invariance.  
- Proves a large class of CNNs with smooth homogeneous activations lie in the induced RKHS, linking CNN predictions’ stability to RKHS norm bounds.  
- Positions work between Mallat’s scattering transform and CKN/kernel analyses, complementing group-equivariant CNNs with a kernel/RKHS perspective.  
- Strength: clear, precise multilayer kernel construction with explicit commutator and stability bounds depending on ∥∇τ∥∞, ∥τ∥∞, depth, and pooling scale.  
- Strength: unifies CKNs and certain CNNs via RKHS inclusion and norm control connecting stability and generalization.  
- Strength: clean group-general formulation enabling equivariance and a route to global invariance at final layer.  
- Strength: practical considerations—discretization scheme, information preservation conditions, and compatibility with finite-dimensional approximations.  
- Strength: technically careful, leveraging harmonic analysis and extending scattering lemmas to shifted integral operators.  
- Weakness: requires smooth homogeneous activations for CNN inclusion; ReLU needs smoothing and approximation/training impact is unquantified.  
- Weakness: pooling breaks norm preservation (unlike scattering); lack of guidance on typical energy loss or mitigation besides an optional top Gaussian layer.  
- Weakness: stability constants (C1, C2) depend on kernel/pooling choices and κ; explicit, interpretable bounds and scaling with d/depth are missing.  
- Weakness: deconvolution-based discretization (Lemma 2) may be ill-conditioned in practice; robustness to noise and boundary effects is not analyzed.  
- Weakness: no empirical validation to illustrate bound sharpness, invariance-information loss trade-offs, or practical RKHS inclusion of CNNs.  
- Quality: technically sound with clean derivations; some assumptions are reasonable but could be better justified as necessity vs convenience.  
- Clarity: well-structured exposition; could add intuition on κ, a group-equivariant pipeline diagram, and concrete activation/kernel examples with constants.  
- Originality: moderate-to-high—nontrivial synthesis extending scattering-style stability to kernel/CNN settings with RKHS inclusion results.  
- Significance: potentially important theoretical link between invariance/stability, RKHS geometry, and generalization; practical impact needs empirical guidance.  
- Key questions: quantify ReLU smoothing approximation error and its effect on norms/stability; provide explicit bounds for constants like C1 in terms of κ, d, pooling.  
- Key questions: clarify Assumption A3 in common CNN setups (patch sizes, σk choices) and analyze discretization robustness to noise/boundaries and need for regularized deconvolution.  
- Key questions: relate RKHS norm bounds to data-dependent generalization measures (Rademacher complexity/margins) and provide small-scale empirical tests of stability vs τ.  
- Pros for acceptance: solid theoretical advance generalizing scattering stability, clear RKHS-CNN bridge, and unified view of invariance via group pooling.  
- Cons for acceptance: lack of experiments, restrictive activation assumptions for ReLU, and some idealized technical assumptions for discretization/recovery.
