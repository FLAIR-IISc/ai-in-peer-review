The paper reduces nonparametric CI testing to binary classification using a 1-NN bootstrap that swaps Y from nearest neighbors in Z-space to approximate fCI, with a bias-corrected variant that trains an extra (Y,Z) classifier.

Theoretical contributions: a TV bound showing bootstrap closeness to fCI with error O(n^{-1/dz}) (up to tail/log terms), ERM generalization bounds under near-independence, and a link between classification error and dTV(f, fCI).

Empirically, CCIT outperforms kernel-based tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic post-nonlinear models and flow-cytometry-derived CI relations, particularly as dim(Z) increases.

Strength: clear, practical reduction to classification that avoids heavy optimization required by permutation/LP methods.

Strength: provides non-asymptotic theoretical guarantees (TV closeness and generalization under near-independence).

Strength: includes a bias-corrected variant (CCITv2) to mitigate finite-sample bootstrap bias.

Strength: modular design lets improvements in classifiers benefit CI testing; nearest-neighbor search is computationally efficient.

Weakness: lacks principled calibration — no p-values or Type I error control, τ is chosen heuristically, and bootstrap aggregation is ad hoc.

Weakness: TV convergence rate exhibits the curse of dimensionality (O(n^{-1/dz})), so guarantees deteriorate quickly with high-dimensional Z.

Weakness: near-independence analysis relies on strong/opaque assumptions (e.g., Lipschitzness, curvature bounds) and handles sample dependence abstractly, with unclear constants.

Weakness: comparisons omit CMI/KNN-based CI tests that are natural NN baselines; runtime and sensitivity to classifier choice/hyperparameters are underreported.

Weakness: fixed use of 1-NN — k-NN smoothing and metric choice/learning are not explored theoretically or empirically.

Weakness: flow cytometry evaluation uses uncertain ground truth, complicating interpretation of significance.

Quality: technically sound with explicit but somewhat restrictive assumptions and plausible proofs; finite-sample calibration is not treated theoretically.

Quality: empirical methodology shows performance gains but lacks controlled hypothesis testing (e.g., permutation/null calibration or conformal methods).

Clarity: paper is generally well-written with clear algorithms, but notation consistency and specification of τ, bootstrap repetitions B, and tuning/training protocols need clarification.

Originality: the NN bootstrap approximating fCI with TV guarantees is novel and simpler than prior LP/permutation approaches; near-independence generalization analysis adds novelty.

Significance: practically significant by enabling CI testing to leverage modern classifiers and scale well, though lack of calibration limits immediate use in scientific settings.

Arguments for acceptance: novel/simple reduction with theoretical support, strong empirical gains over common kernel CI tests, and meaningful generalization analysis.

Arguments against acceptance: no principled calibration for Type I error/p-values, theoretical rates suffer from curse of dimensionality, and some important baselines and sensitivity analyses are missing.

Suggestion: provide a calibrated test (e.g., null distribution via repeated label-swapping or nested resampling) to obtain p-values and control error rates.

Suggestion: explore k-NN variants and metric learning for Z to reduce variance and improve rates, and analyze their theoretical impact.

Suggestion: include comparisons to CMI/KNN-based CI tests and report runtimes and sensitivity to classifier hyperparameters.

Suggestion: detail hyperparameter tuning protocols with strict sample splitting to avoid leakage and add sensitivity analyses.

Suggestion: quantify finite-sample bias magnitude and offer data-driven guidance for τ or a bias correction with confidence intervals.
