## Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing the problem as a binary classification task and leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The core idea is to overcome the difficulty of obtaining samples from the conditional independence distribution, fCI, by introducing a nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure and theoretically demonstrating its closeness to fCI in terms of total variational distance. The authors then derive generalization bounds for classification in this non-i.i.d. setting, translating them into error bounds for CI testing.

The work builds upon recent advances in model-powered statistical inference, particularly the work of Lopez-Paz et al. (2016) which utilizes classification for independence testing, but extends it to the more challenging CI testing scenario. It differentiates itself from kernel-based approaches like KCIT (Zhang et al., 2012) and RCIT (Strobl et al., 2017) by avoiding expensive linear programming and offering theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap procedure. The theoretical contribution, specifically the analysis of Rademacher-type classification bounds in the presence of near-independent samples, is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined problem statement, a clear methodology, and rigorous theoretical analysis. The proposed nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is innovative and the theoretical results demonstrating its accuracy are impressive. The empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets shows performance gains over existing methods. The paper is well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the complex ideas presented. The inclusion of detailed algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1, 2, & 3) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the assumptions required (smoothness of f(y|z) and f(z)) may be restrictive in practice. The practical implications of these assumptions, and the sensitivity of performance to their violation, aren’t fully explored. The empirical evaluation, while showing improvements, doesn't delve deeply into scenarios where the assumptions are likely to be violated. The connection between the observed performance scaling better than the theoretical worst-case analysis isn't fully elaborated. Additionally, while related work is discussed, the connection to work on causal discovery beyond the cited papers could be strengthened.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to CI testing with strong theoretical foundations.
*   Demonstrated empirical performance improvements over existing methods.
*   Valuable contribution to the field of non-i.i.d. generalization bounds.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of complex ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Restrictive assumptions may limit practical applicability.
*   Limited exploration of scenarios where assumptions are violated.
*   More in-depth discussion of the discrepancy between theoretical and empirical scaling would be beneficial.

**Overall:** I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and recommend acceptance. The theoretical and methodological novelty, coupled with the demonstrated empirical performance, outweigh the minor weaknesses. Addressing the assumptions and expanding the empirical analysis could further strengthen the work.
