- Novel approach: frames continuous conditional independence testing as a binary classification problem using generated fCI samples.  
- Core method: generate approximate fCI samples and train a classifier to distinguish true joint vs generated samples.  
- Theoretical contributions: provides total variation bounds for bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1).  
- Theoretical contributions: provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independence (Theorem 2).  
- Empirical results: shows consistent improvements over KCIT and RCIT, especially in higher dimensions and on a flow cytometry dataset.  
- Motivation and impact: well-motivated for causal inference and leverages modern classifiers (GBTs, deep nets).  
- Notation/clarity: presentation is dense; heavy notation in theoretical sections is hard to follow.  
- Smoothness assumptions: theory relies on strong smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 & 2) with limited discussion of practical implications or robustness to violations.  
- Dimensionality concern: theoretical bounds depend on Z's dimensionality, potentially limiting scalability despite good empirical scaling.  
- Bias correction (Algorithm 3): description and intuition for subtracting the Y|Z classifier loss feels abrupt and under-motivated.  
- Related work: comparison focuses on kernel methods; lacks deeper critique and comparison to other nonparametric approaches (e.g., kNN or mutual information estimators).  
- Minor factual critique: statement about KCIPT requiring an expensive linear program is oversimplified.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel idea, strong theoretical guarantees, convincing empirical gains, and potential to leverage classifier advances.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dense theory presentation, strong/unexamined smoothness assumptions, abrupt bias-correction description, and limited related-work coverage.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity, discuss assumptions, better motivate bias correction, and broaden related-work comparisons.
