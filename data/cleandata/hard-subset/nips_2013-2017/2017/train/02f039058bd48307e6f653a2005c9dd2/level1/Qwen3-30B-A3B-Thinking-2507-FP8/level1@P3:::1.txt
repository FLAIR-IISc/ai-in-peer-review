

# Peer Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper presents a novel approach to non-parametric conditional independence testing by reducing the problem to a binary classification task. The authors propose a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), which they then use to train classifiers to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution and the conditionally independent distribution. The work builds on recent advances in using classification for independence testing [19], extending them to the more challenging conditional independence setting where permutation must be Z-dependent.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a theoretically grounded method for generating samples from f_CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, with a proof showing that the generated samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance under smoothness assumptions (Theorem 1). Second, it develops novel generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples, which is a significant theoretical contribution beyond just CI testing. Third, it demonstrates empirical superiority over state-of-the-art kernel-based methods [28, 32] on both synthetic data and real protein signaling network data.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are well-motivated and correctly address the key challenge of generating samples from f_CI. The proof techniques, particularly the analysis of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap and the generalization bounds for non-i.i.d samples, are sound. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, with both synthetic experiments showing scalability with dimensionality and real-world validation on the flow-cytometry dataset. The paper correctly acknowledges limitations, noting that the performance with respect to dimension of Z scales better than theoretical worst-case analysis suggests. The authors are careful to distinguish between theoretical guarantees and practical performance.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and logically structured. The problem statement, methodological contributions, and theoretical results are clearly presented. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-3) are precise and well-documented. The connection between the classification framework and CI testing is well-explained. The theoretical results are presented in a way that makes them accessible to NIPS readers. Minor improvements could be made in clarifying the intuition behind the nearest-neighbor bootstrap in the introduction and providing more concrete examples of the bias correction in Algorithm 3.

**Originality**: The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution to CI testing. While binary classification has been used for independence testing [19], extending this to conditional independence is challenging due to the need for Z-dependent permutations. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach provides a simple yet effective solution, and the theoretical analysis of the generated samples' closeness to f_CI is a significant advance. The paper correctly situates itself against related work, particularly [10] (which uses permutation but requires solving an expensive linear program), and [28, 32] (kernel-based methods). The theoretical analysis of classification under non-i.i.d samples is also novel and of independent interest.

**Significance**: The work addresses a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning with important applications in causal inference, Bayesian network structure learning, and feature selection. The authors demonstrate that their method scales much better with dimensionality than previous approaches, which is crucial for modern high-dimensional datasets. The empirical results show clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods, and the modular design allows practitioners to leverage advances in classification without modifying the core CI testing framework. The paper's approach opens up a promising direction for using machine learning tools for statistical testing more broadly.

**Strengths**:
1. Novel reduction of CI testing to binary classification with a practical bootstrap procedure
2. Strong theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap procedure and classification generalization
3. Comprehensive empirical validation showing clear performance gains
4. Clear connection to practical applications in causal discovery
5. Theoretical contribution on classification generalization under near-independence

**Weaknesses**:
1. The theoretical guarantee in Theorem 1 has a complex dependency on dimension (n^(-1/dz)) that might be challenging to interpret for practitioners.
2. The paper doesn't thoroughly address computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap (which is O(n^2) without approximate nearest neighbors).
3. While the bias correction in Algorithm 3 is well-motivated, the paper could better explain why the bias term is small in practice.
4. The relationship to recent work on conditional independence testing using neural networks (e.g., [33]) isn't sufficiently contrasted.

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- The paper makes a significant contribution to a fundamental problem in statistics
- The theoretical and empirical results are strong and well-supported
- The approach is novel and advances the state of the art
- The method is practical and could be widely adopted in causal discovery applications

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- The theoretical guarantees might be too complex for practical use
- The computational cost of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap could be prohibitive for very large datasets

**Overall assessment**: This paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to conditional independence testing. The authors successfully bridge statistical testing and machine learning by proposing a model-powered approach that leverages powerful classifiers while providing rigorous theoretical guarantees. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the approach's superiority over state-of-the-art methods. The paper is well-written and makes a strong case for the value of model-powered statistical testing. I recommend acceptance at NIPS 2017.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's of high quality with strong theoretical and empirical support, clear in presentation, original in its approach to CI testing, and significant for both theory and practice. The method could potentially be extended to other statistical testing problems, opening up broader research directions.
