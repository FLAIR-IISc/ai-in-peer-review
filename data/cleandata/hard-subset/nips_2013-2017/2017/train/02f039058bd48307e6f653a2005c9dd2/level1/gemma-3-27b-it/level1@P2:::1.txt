This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables, framing the problem as a binary classification task. The core idea is to generate samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution fCI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure and then train a classifier to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution and the bootstrapped samples. The theoretical contributions include bounds on the accuracy of the bootstrap and generalization bounds for the classifier in this non-i.i.d setting. Empirical evaluation demonstrates performance gains over existing methods on simulated and real datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental problem in statistical inference with applications in areas like causal discovery and feature selection. The idea of leveraging powerful classifiers for CI testing is sensible, given the limitations of traditional kernel-based methods, particularly in high dimensions. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap appears to be a clever technique for approximating fCI, and the theoretical analysis provides justification for its effectiveness. The inclusion of empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets strengthens the paper.

However, there are several weaknesses. The theoretical analysis, while impressive, is dense and relies on several assumptions regarding smoothness (Assumptions 1 and 2). The practical implications of these assumptions – how sensitive the method is to their violation – are not thoroughly discussed. The constant factors in the theoretical bounds are also not specified, making it difficult to assess the tightness of the results. The empirical section, while demonstrating improvements, lacks a detailed ablation study. For example, it is unclear how much each component contributes to the overall performance (e.g., the bootstrap vs. the choice of classifier). Further, while the performance is shown to scale better with the dimension of Z, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity would be valuable. The comparison to other methods is limited to KCIT and RCIT; including comparisons to more recent and potentially relevant approaches would strengthen the evaluation. The notation throughout the paper could also be improved for clarity. There is an instance of ambiguity surrounding the threshold ⌧ and the bias correction in Algorithm 3. The explanation relies heavily on “expected variance” and could be made more precise. The connection to the work in [19] (independence testing) could be made more explicit. This paper extends that work by creating the permutation dependence, so that aspect should be highlighted.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of formulating CI testing as a classification problem is a novel and promising direction.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical analysis supporting the proposed method, including bounds on the accuracy of the bootstrap and generalization performance.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate that the method outperforms existing methods on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** Although dense, the paper is generally well-written and the ideas are clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on strong smoothness assumptions, and their practical impact is not adequately discussed.
*   **Constant Factors:** The theoretical bounds lack constant factors, hindering assessment of tightness.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** The empirical section lacks a detailed ablation study to identify the key contributors to performance.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other methods is limited.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity is missing.
*   **Notational Clarity:** Some notation could be improved for clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to CI testing.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   The problem is important and the solution addresses a significant gap in the literature.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The strong assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis may limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The lack of a detailed ablation study and a more comprehensive comparison to other methods weakens the empirical evaluation.
*   The lack of concrete constant factors hinders the assessment of practical usefulness.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. While the theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions and the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the promising empirical results justify acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above, particularly regarding a discussion of the assumptions, a more detailed ablation study, and clarification of the notation.
