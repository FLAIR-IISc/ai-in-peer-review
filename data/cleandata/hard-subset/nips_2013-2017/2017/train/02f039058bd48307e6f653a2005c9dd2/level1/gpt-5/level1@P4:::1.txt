Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables by reducing the test to a binary classification problem. Given i.i.d. samples from f(x,y,z), the method generates synthetic samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap: partition the data, for each sample in U1 find its 1-NN in U2 in Z-space, and swap the Y coordinates. The original samples (label 1) and NN-bootstrapped samples (label 0) form a two-sample classification problem. If a trained classifier (e.g., gradient-boosted trees or deep nets) can distinguish the two sets on a test split, reject H0: X ⫫ Y | Z; otherwise fail to reject. A bias-corrected variant trains an additional classifier on (Y,Z) only to subtract the finite-sample bias induced by the non-i.i.d. bootstrap.

Theoretical contributions include: (i) a total variation (TV) bound showing the distribution of a bootstrapped sample is close to fCI under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z), with error O(n^{-1/dz}) up to tail and logarithmic terms; (ii) generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization with near-independent samples produced by the bootstrap, using VC/Rademacher-style analysis adapted to this dependence; (iii) a link between classification error and dTV(f, fCI). Empirically, the method (CCIT) outperforms kernel-based CI tests KCIT [32] and RCIT [28] on synthetic post-nonlinear models with high-dimensional Z and on CI relations derived from flow cytometry protein-signaling data. The approach builds on classifier two-sample tests in independence testing [19], nearest-neighbor techniques in information estimation [13], and contrasts with KCIPT [10], which uses a permutation via linear programming to emulate fCI.

Strengths:
- Clear reduction of CI testing to classification with a practical and efficient NN bootstrap, avoiding the heavy optimization of [10].
- Non-asymptotic theoretical guarantees: TV closeness of the bootstrap to fCI under stated smoothness assumptions, and generalization bounds under near-independence.
- A bias-corrected variant (CCITv2) that mitigates finite-sample bias by subtracting a classifier trained on (Y,Z).
- Strong empirical performance, especially as dim(Z) grows, leveraging modern classifiers.
- Modular design: improvements in classification directly benefit CI testing, and nearest-neighbor search is computationally efficient.

Weaknesses:
- Statistical calibration is underdeveloped. The procedure is framed as accuracy vs. 0.5 with a threshold τ; there is no principled p-value or Type I error control under H0. τ is chosen heuristically (e.g., 1/√n), and multiple bootstrap aggregations are used without a formal test.
- The TV convergence rate O(n^{-1/dz}) and dependence on tail mass G(·) reflect the curse of dimensionality; while empirics are favorable, the guarantees degrade rapidly with high-dimensional Z.
- The near-independence analysis is high-level; constants and conditions (e.g., Lipschitzness of f(z), curvature matrix bounds) may be strong and are not easy to verify in practice. Dependence across synthetic samples (shared neighbors) is handled abstractly; clearer intuition or simulations isolating this effect would help.
- Comparisons omit recent conditional mutual information (CMI)-based CI tests using KNN estimators that are natural baselines given the NN theme. Runtime comparisons and sensitivity to classifier choice/hyperparameters are limited.
- The choice of 1-NN is fixed; potential benefits of k-NN smoothing and its theoretical implications are not explored. Robustness to distance metric choice in Z is not discussed.
- The flow cytometry evaluation uses uncertain ground truth; while common, this complicates significance interpretations.

Quality:
- The paper is largely technically sound, with careful problem setup and plausible proofs sketched for the main statements (full details are in the appendix). Assumptions are explicit but somewhat restrictive, and finite-sample calibration is not addressed theoretically.
- The empirical methodology demonstrates clear performance gains; however, absence of controlled hypothesis testing (e.g., permutation-based null calibration or conformal methods) weakens inferential conclusions.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. Algorithms are clearly presented. Some notation could be streamlined (e.g., consistent use of subscripts/superscripts and symbols like dz). The role and selection of τ, the bootstrap repetition B, and training/tuning protocols for classifiers should be specified more clearly. Providing an algorithmic recipe for p-value computation (even via Monte Carlo) would improve clarity.

Originality:
- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap that approximates fCI with TV guarantees is novel and simpler than prior permutation/LP approaches. The generalization analysis under near-independent samples adds methodological novelty beyond standard i.i.d. bounds.

Significance:
- The approach is practically significant: it enables CI testing to benefit from advances in classification and scales well in many settings. The theoretical insights into bootstrap closeness and generalization are of independent interest. The lack of calibrated testing limits immediate adoption in scientific applications requiring controlled error rates, but this is addressable.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and simple reduction of CI testing to classification with theoretical support.
- Strong empirical improvements over widely used kernel CI tests, especially with higher-dimensional conditioning.
- Generalization bounds under near-independence contribute to theory.

Arguments against acceptance:
- No principled calibration (p-values/Type I error control); τ selection is ad hoc.
- Theoretical rates suffer from the curse of dimensionality; assumptions may be strong.
- Missing comparisons to CMI-based KNN CI tests; limited analysis of sensitivity to classifier/hyperparameters and distance metrics.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a calibrated test: e.g., null distribution via repeated label-swapping within the bootstrap framework, or a nested resampling scheme to obtain p-values and FWER/FDR control.
- Explore k-NN variants and metric learning for Z to mitigate variance and improve rates; analyze their theoretical impact.
- Include comparisons to CMI/KNN-based CI tests and report runtime.
- Detail hyperparameter tuning protocols with strict sample splitting to avoid leakage; report sensitivity analyses.
- Clarify the magnitude of the finite-sample bias and provide guidance for τ or a data-driven bias correction with confidence intervals.
