- Recasts conditional independence testing as a binary classification problem using powerful classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, DNNs).  
- Proposes a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate approximate conditionally independent samples, with theoretical guarantees on sample quality.  
- Provides generalization bounds for classification in the non-i.i.d. setting (Rademacher-complexity-based analysis).  
- Empirically outperforms state-of-the-art CI tests on simulated and real datasets, including flow cytometry.  
- Avoids expensive linear programs used in prior CI methods and leverages flexible high-dimensional classifiers.  
- Theoretical contributions extend classifier-based independence testing to the more challenging CI setting.  
- Application to biological data connects the method to causal discovery efforts.  
- Novel reduction enables exploitation of modern supervised learning advances.  
- Clear presentation with helpful algorithms (Algorithm 2 & 3) and a publicly available Python package.  
- Strong theoretical guarantees and rigorous analysis in a non-i.i.d. context.  
- Nearest-neighbor bootstrap may be computationally costly in high-dimensional, large-scale datasets.  
- Method may be sensitive to hyperparameters (e.g., τ) and classifier tuning; limited sensitivity analysis presented.  
- Strong smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 & 2) may not hold in practice and their violation is not fully explored.  
- Bias-correction in Algorithm 3 is useful but its subtraction-based justification appears heuristic and lacks rigorous backing.  
- Comparisons are limited—important alternatives (e.g., entropy-based CI tests) are not evaluated.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically strong, and reproducible implementation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability concerns, restrictive assumptions, and heuristic bias-correction needing stronger justification.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper recommended for acceptance subject to addressing computational cost, sensitivity analysis, and bias-correction justification.
