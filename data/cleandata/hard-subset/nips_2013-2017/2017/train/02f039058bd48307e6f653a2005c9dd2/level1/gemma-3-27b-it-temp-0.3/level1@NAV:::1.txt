## Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing the problem as a binary classification task. The core idea is to leverage powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, neural networks) to distinguish between the joint distribution and the conditionally independent distribution, addressing the challenge of generating samples from the latter via a nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap’s accuracy (total variational distance) and generalization bounds for the classification task, validating their approach empirically on simulated and real datasets.

The paper builds upon existing work in CI testing, particularly kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT, by offering a data-driven alternative that avoids expensive linear programming (as in KCIT) and potentially scales better with dimensionality. It also connects to recent work using classification for independence testing (e.g., [19]), extending the idea to the more complex conditional independence setting. The reduction to classification is a compelling approach, allowing the authors to benefit from advances in supervised learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The classification-based approach to CI testing is a fresh perspective, offering a potential advantage in high-dimensional settings.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides theoretical analysis of both the bootstrap procedure (total variational distance) and the generalization bounds for classification, demonstrating a strong understanding of the underlying statistical principles.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on synthetic and real datasets show promising performance gains over existing methods, suggesting the practical utility of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the methodology and theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, is quite involved and may be difficult for some readers to fully grasp.
* **Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantees rely on smoothness assumptions on the underlying distributions, which may not always hold in real-world scenarios. The sensitivity of the method to these assumptions isn't fully explored.
* **Computational Cost:** While potentially scaling better with dimensionality, the nearest neighbor bootstrap could be computationally expensive for very large datasets. This is not thoroughly discussed.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to existing methods is somewhat limited. A more comprehensive evaluation against a wider range of CI tests, particularly those designed for high-dimensional data, would strengthen the claims.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to CI testing.
* The empirical results demonstrate promising performance gains.
* The work has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in causal discovery and related fields.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical complexity and assumptions may limit the practical applicability of the method.
* The computational cost of the bootstrap procedure could be a concern for large datasets.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of CI testing. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical rigor, and promising empirical results outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the computational cost discussion and expanding the empirical comparison. The authors should also consider clarifying the practical implications of the smoothness assumptions.
