### Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

#### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel, non-parametric method for Conditional Independence (CI) testing, called the Model-Powered CI test (or CCIT). The core idea is to reframe the statistical hypothesis test of `X ⊥ Y | Z` as a binary classification problem. This reduction allows the authors to leverage the power and scalability of modern supervised learning models, such as gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks, which are known to perform well in high-dimensional settings.

The main technical challenge is generating samples from the conditionally independent distribution `f_CI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)` using only i.i.d. samples from the true joint distribution `f(x, y, z)`. The authors propose an elegant and efficient solution: a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. For each sample `(x, y, z)`, it finds another sample `(x', y', z')` whose `z'` is the nearest neighbor to `z`, and creates a new synthetic sample `(x, y', z)`. These synthetic samples, labeled as class 0, are contrasted with original samples, labeled as class 1. A classifier is then trained to distinguish them. The test accuracy serves as the test statistic: an accuracy near 0.5 suggests `f ≈ f_CI` (i.e., conditional independence), while a significantly higher accuracy suggests dependence.

The paper provides strong theoretical support for this framework. First, it proves that the distribution of the bootstrapped samples converges to `f_CI` in total variation distance. Second, it develops novel generalization bounds for classifiers trained on this data, which is non-trivially non-i.i.d. due to the bootstrap process. Empirically, the proposed method is shown to significantly outperform prior state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT), particularly as the dimension of the conditioning variable `Z` increases.

This work fits into the broader research theme of machine learning reductions and builds upon recent work using classifiers for two-sample and independence testing (e.g., Lopez-Paz et al., 2016). Its primary distinction is the successful extension of this paradigm to the more complex and crucial problem of conditional independence.

#### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The central idea of reducing CI testing to classification is both novel and highly significant. It opens the door for the CI testing literature to directly benefit from the rapid advancements in high-dimensional classification. The proposed nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a simple, intuitive, and effective method for solving the key sampling problem, a marked improvement over more complex proposals like the LP-based permutation in KCIPT [10]. This work has the potential to become a standard tool for CI testing in practice, especially in fields like causal discovery where conditioning on high-dimensional sets is common.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental validation is thorough and compelling. CCIT demonstrates a substantial performance gain over established kernel-based methods, especially in the challenging setting where the dimensionality of `Z` is high (Fig. 2a). This is a critical practical advantage, as the curse of dimensionality is a major bottleneck for most existing CI tests. The application to the flow-cytometry dataset further showcases its utility on real-world problems.

3.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide rigorous theoretical justification for their method. Theorem 1, which bounds the quality of the bootstrapped samples, is essential for the validity of the entire approach. Furthermore, Theorem 2 provides a novel analysis of Rademacher complexity for the specific "near-independent" data generated by the bootstrap. This result is of independent interest and strengthens the paper by formally connecting the classifier's empirical performance to the underlying statistical distance between distributions.

4.  **Practicality and Flexibility:** The proposed framework is modular and practical. It relies on two well-established components: fast nearest-neighbor search and standard binary classifiers. This makes the method relatively easy to implement and allows practitioners to choose the most appropriate classifier for their specific data domain (e.g., CNNs for images, XGBoost for tabular data), adding a valuable degree of flexibility not present in monolithic kernel methods. The proposed bias-correction algorithm (CCITv2) is a thoughtful addition that addresses a subtle but important practical issue.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance / Suggestions for Improvement):**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The theoretical convergence rate for the bootstrap procedure presented in Theorem 1, `O(1/n^(1/d_z))`, suffers exponentially from the curse of dimensionality with respect to `d_z`. While the authors rightly note that the empirical performance is much better, this discrepancy suggests the worst-case theoretical analysis may not fully explain the method's success. The strong empirical results likely stem from the ability of powerful classifiers to exploit lower-dimensional structure in the data (e.g., data lying on a manifold), a phenomenon not captured by the current theory. A discussion of this gap and potential avenues for tighter analysis under more specific structural assumptions would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Formal Hypothesis Testing:** The method's outcome depends on the choice of classifier, its hyperparameters, and the final decision threshold `τ`. The paper offers limited guidance on setting these. For instance, `τ` is set to `1/√n` in experiments, but a more principled way to set `τ` to control the Type I error at a desired level (e.g., α = 0.05) is needed for the method to be used as a formal hypothesis test. One potential solution, common in related literature, would be to use a permutation-based approach to compute a p-value, thereby obviating the need for a fixed threshold. A discussion of the sensitivity to the classifier's architecture and tuning would also be valuable.

3.  **Clarity on Bias Correction Mechanism:** The intuition behind the bias correction in Algorithm 3 is that a classifier trained on `(y, z)` captures the bias from the bootstrap. However, the explanation could be more precise. The bootstrap procedure preserves the `x` and `z` from an original sample `(x, y, z)` and pairs them with a `y'` from a neighbor. This process could create subtle artifacts in the joint distribution of `(x, y', z)` that are not present in the marginal distribution of `(y', z)`. It would be helpful to clarify why the bias is assumed to be fully captured by the `(y, z)` components alone.

---

#### Detailed Comments and Questions

*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper highlights the efficiency of the bootstrap due to fast nearest-neighbor algorithms. However, a formal complexity analysis is missing. How does the overall runtime of CCIT compare theoretically and empirically to competitors like KCIT and RCIT, especially as `n` and `d_z` grow? Adding a runtime comparison plot to the experiments section would be informative.
*   **Discrete Variables:** The current work is focused on continuous variables. Could the authors comment on the applicability or necessary modifications of their method for discrete or mixed-type variables? The nearest-neighbor concept might not translate directly to discrete `Z`.
*   **Proof of Theorem 1:** The bound in Theorem 1 depends on smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2). How restrictive are these in practice? For example, are distributions with sharp boundaries or discontinuities problematic?

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, powerful, and well-supported method for conditional independence testing. The central idea is elegant, the results are significant, and the work has the potential for high impact. The weaknesses identified are primarily areas for future refinement rather than fundamental flaws. I strongly recommend acceptance.
