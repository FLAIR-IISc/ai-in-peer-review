The paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables by framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea is to generate samples that approximate the conditional independence distribution fCI and then train a classifier to distinguish between these generated samples and real samples from the joint distribution. A nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is proposed to generate the fCI samples, and theoretical guarantees are provided for the quality of the generated samples and the generalization bounds for the classifier. The method is empirically validated on synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating performance gains over existing methods.

This work builds on the established research area of non-parametric CI testing, particularly kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT. It differentiates itself by leveraging the power of modern classifiers (gradient boosted trees and deep neural networks) and providing theoretical justification for the nearest-neighbor bootstrap. The reduction to a classification problem is a compelling approach, drawing inspiration from recent work using classifiers for independence testing (e.g., [19]), but extending it to the more complex CI setting. The theoretical analysis, including total variational distance bounds and Rademacher complexity analysis for non-i.i.d samples, is a significant contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem, coupled with the nearest-neighbor bootstrap, represents a novel approach in the field.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including bounds on the total variational distance between generated samples and the true conditional independence distribution, and generalization bounds for the classifier. The analysis of Rademacher complexity in a non-i.i.d setting is particularly noteworthy.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art techniques on both synthetic and real datasets. The scalability observed in experiments, despite the theoretical worst-case analysis, is promising.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the key contributions. The algorithmic descriptions (Algorithms 1, 2, and 3) are relatively easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) that may not always hold in practice. While acknowledged, the sensitivity of the method to these assumptions is not thoroughly investigated empirically.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The paper mentions a threshold (⌧) in Algorithm 3 but doesn't provide detailed guidance on how to set it appropriately. The choice of this parameter could significantly impact performance.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions the efficiency of the nearest neighbor search, the overall computational cost of the bootstrap procedure and classifier training might be substantial, especially for high-dimensional data. This aspect is not fully discussed.
*   **Comparison to Alternatives:**  The comparison to existing methods (KCIT, RCIT) is somewhat limited. A more comprehensive comparison, including a wider range of datasets and parameter settings, would strengthen the empirical evaluation.  It would be useful to see a computational time comparison as well.
*   **Redundancy:** Algorithms 2 and 3 are very similar; Algorithm 3 addresses a bias in 2. The flow of ideas feels slightly disjointed as a result.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to CI testing with strong theoretical foundations.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant performance gains over existing methods.
*   The theoretical analysis offers valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed algorithm.
*   The reduction to a classification problem opens up possibilities for leveraging advances in machine learning for statistical inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reliance on strong smoothness assumptions could limit the applicability of the method.
*   The lack of detailed guidance on parameter tuning and a thorough analysis of computational cost might hinder practical adoption.
*   The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical soundness, and the promising empirical results outweigh the identified weaknesses. While the assumptions and computational cost are concerns, they are acknowledged by the authors. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2017. A minor revision addressing the parameter tuning guidelines and providing a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity would further strengthen the paper.
