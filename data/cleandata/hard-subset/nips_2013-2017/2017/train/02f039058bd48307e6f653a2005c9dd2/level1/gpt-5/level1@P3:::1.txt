Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a “model-powered” reduction of nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing to binary classification. Given i.i.d. samples from f(x,y,z), the authors generate pseudo-samples intended to approximate the CI product fCI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap on Z: for each (x,y,z) in one half of the data, find its 1-NN z0 in the other half and replace y by the neighbor’s y0. A two-sample classification between original samples and NN-bootstrapped samples (with a held-out test set) then decides whether f differs from fCI. The authors (i) prove that the distribution of a single bootstrapped sample is close to fCI in total variation (TV) under smoothness assumptions, with a rate O(n^{-1/dz}); (ii) develop generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization when negative-class samples are “near-independent,” deriving risk bounds involving VC dimension and terms depending on dz; (iii) propose a bias-corrected variant that subtracts the performance of a classifier that only sees (Y,Z); and (iv) demonstrate empirical gains over KCIT and RCIT on synthetic PNL data with high-dimensional Z and a flow-cytometry dataset.

The work relates closely to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and permutation-based KCIPT, as well as recent classifier-based two-sample/independence tests. The main novelty versus KCIPT is replacing a Z-aware permutation computed via an LP by a simple, scalable Z-NN-based bootstrap with provable TV closeness to fCI, and using modern classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees) for the two-sample discrimination. On the theory side, the paper extends Rademacher/VC-style generalization guarantees to a setting with near-independent samples created by the NN bootstrap.

Strengths:
- Conceptual simplicity and modularity: reduces CI testing to a two-sample classification problem, unlocking strong practical classifiers, fast NN search, and potential improvements as ML advances.
- Solid and nontrivial theory: TV closeness of the NN bootstrap to fCI under smoothness, and generalization bounds in a non-i.i.d. setting are technically interesting and of independent value.
- Scalability: the NN bootstrap is computationally attractive compared to LP-based permutations; implementation and code are provided.
- Empirical performance: clear gains over KCIT/RCIT in regimes with moderate-to-high-dimensional Z on synthetic data; useful application to flow-cytometry.

Weaknesses and concerns:
Quality:
- Statistical testing calibration is underdeveloped. The decision rules based on held-out classification accuracy and a heuristic threshold τ (or bias-corrected difference) do not provide level-α control or p-values. ROC/AUC across many datasets is informative but does not address Type-I error for a single test. A permutation or sample-splitting-based calibration of the test statistic under H0 would strengthen the work.
- The TV rate O(n^{-1/dz}) for the bootstrap suffers severely from the curse of dimensionality in Z; while practice appears more forgiving, theory provides weak guarantees when dz is large. Assumptions (e.g., curvature matrix bound for f(y|z) and smoothness/integrability of f(z)) are nonstandard and somewhat opaque; more intuition and examples would help.
- The “near-independence” generalization analysis is promising but complex. Key dependencies (e.g., multiple U1 points sharing the same NN in U2, spatial clustering) and how they quantitatively affect mixing coefficients are abstracted into G(ε) terms; empirical diagnostics of the dependence strength would be useful.
- The bias-corrected method (comparing XYZ vs YZ classifiers) is intuitive, but its Type-I calibration is still heuristic; it may also reduce power when the extra signal from X|Z is weak relative to Y|Z nuisance. Clarify in which regimes CCITv2 improves over CCITv1.

Clarity:
- Some assumptions (Assumption 1’s “generalized curvature” Ia(z)) and constants are difficult to parse. Providing an intuitive restatement (e.g., Lipschitz/smoothness of log f(y|z) in z under f(y|z)) and concrete examples (Gaussian conditionals, additive noise models) would improve accessibility.
- Notation is heavy; minor typos/inconsistencies (e.g., set names U'2/U02) and overloaded symbols make reading harder. A succinct figure describing data flow and independence relations would help.

Originality:
- Using NN bootstrap to approximate fCI is, to my knowledge, novel and elegant relative to prior Z-aware permutations (KCIPT). The generalization bounds under near-independence also add novelty beyond standard VC/Rademacher results.

Significance:
- If properly calibrated, the approach could become a strong practical CI test in high-dimensional Z regimes where kernel tests struggle. The ability to “plug-in” powerful classifiers is appealing for practitioners. Theoretical tools for near-independence could benefit related bootstrap/synthetic data methods.

Specific suggestions:
- Provide a hypothesis-testing procedure with finite-sample Type-I control: e.g., conditional label permutation on the test set, cross-fitting, or a wild bootstrap that respects the generated data dependence; report empirical null distributions and false-positive rates.
- Explore m-NN or radius-based matching instead of 1-NN, and cross-fitting (multiple disjoint U2/U3) to reduce dependence and variance; analyze the trade-offs theoretically.
- Report runtime comparisons against KCIPT and kernel tests; include multivariate X and Y experiments and heterogeneous/noisy Z to probe robustness.
- Discuss extension to mixed discrete/continuous variables and to non-Euclidean Z metrics.
- Include sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters; guard against overfitting via nested CV.

Minor comments:
- The claim that edges are “never CI given any other conditioning set” is true for DAGs with a direct edge when Z excludes X,Y, but stating this explicitly would avoid confusion with collider/path effects.
- Please unify notation for datasets (U1, U2, U3, U’2) and for risks/losses; define b(n) where first used.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and simple reduction of CI to classification via a theoretically justified NN bootstrap.
- Useful, general-purpose approach with strong empirical results and open-source code.
- Interesting learning-theory contribution for near-independent samples.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of a principled, calibrated hypothesis test with controlled Type-I error; reliance on heuristics and ROC across datasets.
- Theoretical guarantees degrade quickly with dz; assumptions are strong and not easy to verify.
- Limited empirical breadth (few distributions, mostly univariate X,Y) and missing runtime comparisons.

Overall: A promising and original contribution with solid theory and practical appeal. I recommend acceptance if the authors can strengthen the testing calibration and clarify assumptions; otherwise, it is still a strong borderline paper with clear potential impact.
