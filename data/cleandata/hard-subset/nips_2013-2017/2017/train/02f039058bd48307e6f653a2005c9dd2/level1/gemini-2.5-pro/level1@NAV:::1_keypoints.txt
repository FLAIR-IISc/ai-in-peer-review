- Introduces CCIT: reduces conditional independence testing to a binary classification problem distinguishing true joint samples from synthetic samples approximating f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).
- Key technical innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate the synthetic conditionally independent samples.
- Extends classifier-based testing paradigms to the harder CI problem, offering a clear alternative to kernel-based methods like KCIT.
- Originality/significance: elegant reduction that lets practitioners leverage powerful high-dimensional classifiers to mitigate the curse of dimensionality.
- Technical quality: Theorem 1 bounds total variation between bootstrap and target distributions; Theorem 2 provides novel generalization bounds for classification on non-i.i.d. samples.
- Empirical performance: thorough experiments show state-of-the-art results on synthetic and real data, especially as the dimensionality of Z increases, outperforming KCIT and RCIT.
- Theory vs. practice gap: proven convergence rate O(n^{-1/dz}) predicts severe degradation with high-dim Z, which conflicts with empirical robustness and needs further explanation.
- Threshold selection is heuristic: CCITv2’s bias correction helps, but a principled significance procedure (e.g., permutation-based null and p-values) is lacking.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution combining a novel method, rigorous theory, and compelling empirical evidence.
