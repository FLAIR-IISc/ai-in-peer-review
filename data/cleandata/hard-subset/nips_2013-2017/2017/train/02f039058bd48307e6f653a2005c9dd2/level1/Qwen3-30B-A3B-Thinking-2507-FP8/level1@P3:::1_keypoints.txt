- Proposes reducing non-parametric conditional independence testing to a binary classification problem via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate f_CI samples.  
- Provides a theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) that bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation under smoothness assumptions.  
- Develops novel generalization bounds for classification with near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples (Theorem 2).  
- Empirically outperforms state-of-the-art kernel-based CI tests on synthetic and real protein signaling data.  
- Strong technical quality: proofs and analysis of the NN bootstrap and non-i.i.d. generalization are sound.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive, including scalability with dimensionality and real-world validation.  
- Paper is well-written and logically structured; algorithms are precise and clearly explained.  
- Minor clarity improvements suggested: better intuition for the NN bootstrap in the introduction and concrete examples for bias correction in Algorithm 3.  
- Original contribution: extends classification-based independence testing to the conditional setting with a simple, effective Z-dependent permutation via NN bootstrap.  
- Novel theoretical analysis of classification under non-i.i.d. samples is of independent interest.  
- Significant impact: relevant to causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection; scales better with dimensionality than prior methods.  
- Strength: modular framework lets practitioners use advanced classifiers without changing the CI test core.  
- Weakness: Theorem 1’s dimension-dependent rate (n^{-1/d_z}) is complex and may be hard to interpret for practitioners.  
- Weakness: Computational cost of the NN bootstrap is O(n^2) without ANN, potentially prohibitive for very large datasets.  
- Weakness: Explanation of why the bias term in Algorithm 3 is small in practice could be improved.  
- Weakness: Insufficient contrast with recent NN-based CI testing work (e.g., [33]).  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant, novel, and practically useful contribution with strong theoretical and empirical support.  
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical guarantees may be hard to apply in practice and computational cost could limit scalability.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, and significant paper; reviewer recommends acceptance (NIPS 2017).
