

# Peer Review: Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test

This paper proposes a novel approach to non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing by converting the problem into a binary classification task. The key innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure that generates samples from the conditional independence distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) using only samples from the true joint distribution f(x,y,z). The authors theoretically show that these generated samples are close to f_CI in total variational distance and derive generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d near-independent samples. They empirically demonstrate superior performance over state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic data and real-world flow cytometry datasets.

The work builds upon recent advances in using classification for independence testing [19], but significantly extends them to the more challenging conditional independence setting. Previous kernel-based CI testing methods [10, 28, 32] typically rely on expensive permutations or require strong assumptions about the underlying distributions. The authors' bootstrap procedure provides a theoretically grounded and computationally efficient alternative to these approaches.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The theoretical contributions are strong and well-motivated. Theorem 1 rigorously establishes the closeness of the bootstrap samples to f_CI under appropriate smoothness assumptions, while Theorem 2 provides novel generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d samples. The empirical validation is comprehensive, showing consistent performance gains across multiple datasets and increasing dimensionality of Z. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement, main contributions, and algorithmic details are clearly presented with helpful figures (Fig. 1) and pseudocode (Algorithms 1-3). The theoretical results are carefully stated with appropriate assumptions, and the connections between the theoretical guarantees and empirical performance are well-explained.

*Originality:* The model-powered approach to CI testing represents a significant conceptual shift from previous methods. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is novel, theoretically justified, and avoids the computational complexity of previous permutation-based methods [10]. The analysis of classification generalization under near-independent samples is of independent interest beyond CI testing.

*Significance:* CI testing is a fundamental problem with broad applications in causal inference, Bayesian networks, and feature selection. The authors' method demonstrates superior scalability to high-dimensional Z compared to existing approaches, making it practically valuable for real-world problems. The modular design allows leveraging advances in classification without modifying the core CI testing procedure.

**Weaknesses:**

The theoretical guarantees rely on strong smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2), which may limit applicability to some real-world distributions. While the paper mentions that the method works well in practice even when these assumptions are not perfectly satisfied, it would strengthen the paper to include a brief empirical investigation of sensitivity to assumption violations.

The paper mentions the computational efficiency of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap but lacks a detailed comparison of computational complexity with kernel-based methods. For very high-dimensional Z, nearest-neighbor search could become expensive, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of this trade-off.

The implementation details regarding classifier hyperparameter selection (e.g., XGBoost parameters) are somewhat sparse. While the paper mentions using XGBoost as the classifier, it doesn't specify how hyperparameters were chosen or tuned across different datasets, which could affect reproducibility.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pros:* Novel approach to CI testing with strong theoretical guarantees; superior empirical performance across multiple datasets; efficient implementation; broad applicability to statistical testing problems; well-written and organized.

*Cons:* Theoretical assumptions may be restrictive for some applications; limited computational complexity analysis; some implementation details could be more thorough.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions to the field of conditional independence testing. The theoretical results are rigorous, the empirical validation is thorough, and the method has clear practical advantages. The paper addresses a fundamental problem with a novel approach that could influence future work in statistical testing and estimation. While the theoretical assumptions could be more broadly applicable, and the computational complexity discussion could be expanded, these are minor concerns that do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the computational complexity discussion and provide more details about classifier hyperparameter selection. The paper is well-positioned to make a significant impact at NIPS, and the model-powered approach could inspire similar techniques for other statistical testing problems.
