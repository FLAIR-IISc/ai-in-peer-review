- Proposes reducing conditional independence testing X ⟂ Y | Z to a binary classification problem distinguishing true joint samples from conditionally independent samples.  
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap that swaps Y from a Z-nearest neighbor to efficiently approximate sampling from f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Theorem 1: proves bootstrapped sample distribution converges to f_CI in total variation, justifying the bootstrap.  
- Theorem 2: provides novel generalization bounds for classifiers trained on “near-independent” (not fully i.i.d.) bootstrapped samples.  
- Empirically, CCIT (with bias-corrected CCITv2) outperforms kernel-based baselines (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic and real datasets, especially as dim(Z) increases.  
- High technical quality: sound reduction, clever bootstrap, and thorough theoretical and experimental support.  
- Minor weakness: theoretical convergence rate O(n^{-1/dz}) implies a curse of dimensionality that contradicts strong empirical performance for dz up to 70.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with useful pseudocode; theoretical section is dense and could use more intuitive explanations of bound terms.  
- Originality: novel application of classification to CI via nearest-neighbor bootstrap and new Rademacher analysis for near-independent samples.  
- Significance: method is scalable, practical, and likely impactful for causal discovery and structure learning; code available.  
- Pro-accept arguments: novelty/significance, strong empirical results, solid theory, clarity and reproducibility.  
- Con-accept arguments: gap between theory and practice regarding dimensionality, and reliance on a heuristic decision threshold τ lacking a principled null-distribution approach.
