This paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables by framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea is to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution fCI and then train a classifier to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution and these generated samples. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the quality of the generated samples and the generalization ability of the classifier, and demonstrate empirical improvements over existing methods on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental problem in causal inference and graphical modeling. The reduction to a classification problem is a clever idea, allowing the exploitation of powerful modern classifiers (gradient boosted trees and deep neural networks). The theoretical contributions, particularly the total variational distance bounds on the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1) and the generalization bounds for classification under near-independence (Theorem 2), are significant.  The empirical results are convincing, showing consistent performance gains over state-of-the-art methods like KCIT and RCIT, especially in higher dimensions. The inclusion of a flow cytometry dataset adds further practical relevance.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, the notation is dense and can be difficult to follow, particularly in the theoretical sections. While the core idea is intuitive, the equations and definitions require careful examination, and the connection between them could be made clearer. The authors introduce a lot of notation (e.g.,  `dTV`, `G( )`, `Ia(z)`) with limited immediate context, requiring the reader to constantly refer back to the definitions.

Secondly, the theoretical analysis, while impressive, relies on several assumptions about the smoothness of the underlying distributions (Assumptions 1 & 2).  The practical implications of these assumptions are not thoroughly discussed.  How sensitive are the results to violations of these assumptions? Are there scenarios where the method is likely to fail or perform poorly due to these smoothness requirements? The authors mention the smoothness assumptions are analogous to those in existing entropy estimation work, but a more detailed discussion of their practical relevance is needed. The theoretical bounds also involve terms dependent on the dimensionality of Z, potentially limiting scalability in very high-dimensional scenarios.  While the empirical results show good scaling behavior, a more thorough theoretical investigation of this aspect would be beneficial.

Thirdly, the description of Algorithm 3 (bias correction) feels somewhat abrupt. The intuition behind subtracting the loss of the classifier trained on only Y and Z is plausible, but the motivation could be more clearly articulated.  Why is this bias present, and why does subtracting it improve performance?

Finally, the related work section, while extensive, could be more critical. The authors position their work primarily against kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT). However, there’s limited discussion of alternative non-parametric approaches, such as those based on nearest neighbor density estimation and mutual information estimation directly (as alluded to in the text).  A more comprehensive comparison to these approaches would strengthen the paper. The statement that KCIPT requires solving an expensive linear program is a bit of a simplification; while computationally demanding, optimizations exist.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to CI testing.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees for sample generation and classifier generalization.
*   Convincing empirical results demonstrating performance gains over state-of-the-art methods.
*   Potential to leverage advancements in binary classification for improved CI testing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense notation and challenging theoretical sections.
*   Strong assumptions about the smoothness of underlying distributions, with limited discussion of practical implications.
*   Abrupt introduction of the bias correction algorithm.
*   Somewhat limited comparison to alternative non-parametric methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of CI testing. The novel approach, strong theoretical foundations, and promising empirical results justify acceptance.  However, the authors should address the concerns regarding clarity, the discussion of assumptions, and the completeness of the related work section in a revised version. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Improve the clarity of the notation and presentation, particularly in the theoretical sections.
2.  Provide a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of the smoothness assumptions.
3.  Elaborate on the motivation and intuition behind the bias correction algorithm (Algorithm 3).
4.  Expand the related work section to include a more comprehensive comparison to alternative non-parametric methods.
