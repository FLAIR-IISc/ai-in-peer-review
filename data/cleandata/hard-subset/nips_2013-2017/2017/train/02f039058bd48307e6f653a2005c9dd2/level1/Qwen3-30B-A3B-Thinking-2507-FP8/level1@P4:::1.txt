

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper proposes a model-powered approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) to generate samples from the conditional product distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) when only samples from the true joint distribution f(x,y,z) are available. These generated samples are then used to train classifiers that distinguish between the true joint distribution and f_CI. The approach differs from previous kernel-based methods like KCIT [32] and RCIT [28], and from KCIPT [10] which uses a more complex permutation-based method to simulate f_CI. The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrap samples (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d samples (Theorem 2), with empirical validation showing improved performance over state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness:** The paper makes significant theoretical contributions. Theorem 1 provides a rigorous bound on the total variational distance between the bootstrapped samples and f_CI under smoothness assumptions, which is a novel contribution to the field. Theorem 2 extends generalization bounds to non-i.i.d samples, which is of independent interest and addresses a key challenge in the proposed approach. The theoretical results are carefully developed and supported by appropriate assumptions.

**Novelty:** The conversion of CI testing to a classification problem using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a novel approach. The paper clearly differentiates from previous work, particularly KCIPT [10], by providing theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrap procedure and by using classifiers instead of kernel-based two-sample tests. The authors also make a valuable contribution in analyzing classification under near-independent samples, which is a gap in the literature.

**Significance:** The method addresses an important problem in statistical learning with applications in causal inference, Bayesian network structure learning, and feature selection. The empirical results demonstrate substantial performance gains over state-of-the-art methods, especially in high-dimensional settings where the dimension of Z increases. The paper shows that the approach scales better in practice than theoretical worst-case bounds suggest, which is a significant practical contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, methodology, and theoretical results are presented logically. The algorithms are described with pseudocode, and the experimental setup is detailed. The theoretical results are well-explained with appropriate definitions and context.

## Weaknesses

**Practical Implementation Details:** While the paper provides a clear theoretical framework, it lacks sufficient detail about the practical implementation of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap. For instance, the paper does not discuss how the nearest-neighbor search is implemented for high-dimensional Z, which could significantly impact computational efficiency. The paper mentions "fast algorithms for identifying nearest-neighbors [24]" but does not elaborate on how these are applied in practice.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper cites relevant work but does not adequately compare with the most recent methods in the field. For example, the paper does not discuss how their approach compares to the more recent work by Strobl et al. [32] on approximate kernel-based conditional independence tests, which was published in 2017 and could be relevant to their comparison.

**Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on strong smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2). The paper does not provide evidence that these assumptions are reasonable for real-world data, which could limit the applicability of the theoretical guarantees.

## Recommendation

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel approach that successfully reduces CI testing to classification
- Strong theoretical contributions with rigorous guarantees
- Empirical results showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods
- Clear presentation of the methodology and results

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited practical implementation details for high-dimensional Z
- Incomplete comparison with the most recent methods in the field
- Strong theoretical assumptions that may not hold in practice

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing by introducing a novel model-powered approach that converts the problem to binary classification. The theoretical results are sound and well-motivated, and the empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages over existing methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The paper addresses a well-known challenge in CI testing (the need to simulate samples from f_CI) with a theoretically justified and computationally efficient method. The theoretical analysis of classification under near-independent samples is particularly valuable and could be of interest beyond CI testing.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses in implementation details and comparison with the most recent work, these do not undermine the overall quality of the contribution. The paper's novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical significance make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS 2017. The approach could inspire similar model-powered reductions for other statistical testing problems, extending the paper's impact beyond CI testing.
