- Proposes reducing conditional independence (CI) testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to simulate f_CI(x,y,z) from joint samples.  
- Introduces Algorithm 1 for nearest-neighbor bootstrap and trains classifiers to distinguish joint vs. bootstrapped samples.

- Theorem 1 provides a novel bound on total variation distance between bootstrap-generated samples and f_CI under smoothness assumptions.  
- Theorem 2 extends generalization bounds to non-i.i.d. (near-independent) samples, addressing a key challenge for the method.  
- Novelty: first work to combine nearest-neighbor bootstrap with classification for CI testing and to give theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap.  
- Significance: empirical results show substantial gains over state-of-the-art, especially as Z’s dimension grows, and the method scales better in practice than worst-case bounds.  
- Clarity: paper is well-organized, with clear problem statement, pseudocode, and detailed experimental setup.

- Lacks practical implementation details for nearest-neighbor search in high-dimensional Z and how fast NN algorithms are applied.  
- Incomplete comparison with the most recent related work (e.g., Strobl et al. 2017 on approximate kernel CI tests).  
- Theoretical guarantees depend on strong smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) with no evidence these hold on real data.

- Recommendation (pro): novel, theoretically solid, empirically strong, and clearly presented—recommended for acceptance.  
- Recommendation (con): concerns about high-dimensional implementation, missing recent comparisons, and strong theoretical assumptions argue for caution.

- Overall: significant and well-supported contribution to CI testing with useful theoretical advances and strong empirical performance, despite some practical and comparative gaps.
