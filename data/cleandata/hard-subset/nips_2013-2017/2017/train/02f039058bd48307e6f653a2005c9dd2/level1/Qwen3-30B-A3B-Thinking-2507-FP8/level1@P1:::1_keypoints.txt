- Proposes converting non-parametric conditional independence testing into a binary classification task using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate f_CI-like samples.
- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is theoretically shown to produce samples close to f_CI in total variation under smoothness assumptions (Theorem 1).
- Provides novel generalization bounds for classification with non-i.i.d. near-independent samples (Theorem 2).
- Empirical results show superior performance to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic and flow cytometry datasets, scaling better with dimensionality of Z.
- Method avoids expensive permutation procedures used by many kernel-based approaches and is computationally efficient in practice.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and includes helpful figures and pseudocode for reproducibility.
- The approach is original and conceptually shifts CI testing by leveraging classification models modularly.
- The classification-generalization analysis under near-independence is of independent theoretical interest.
- Theoretical guarantees depend on strong smoothness assumptions that may limit applicability to some real-world distributions.
- Lacks empirical sensitivity analysis to violations of the smoothness assumptions.
- No detailed computational complexity comparison with kernel methods; nearest-neighbor search may become costly in very high-dimensional Z.
- Sparse implementation details on classifier hyperparameter selection (e.g., XGBoost tuning), which could affect reproducibility.
- Pros: strong theory, novel method, superior empirical performance, practical scalability, and clear exposition.
- Cons: restrictive theoretical assumptions, limited complexity analysis, and insufficient hyperparameter implementation details.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to expand computational complexity discussion and clarify classifier hyperparameter selection.
