- Proposes reducing conditional independence testing to a binary classification task by distinguishing f(x,y,z) from f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) to generate samples approximating f_CI from i.i.d. joint samples.  
- Leverages powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, deep nets) for CI testing after bootstrap sample generation.  
- Theorem 1: bootstrap samples are provably close to f_CI in total variation (O(1/n^{1/d_z})) under smoothness assumptions.  
- Theorem 2: provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independence (Rademacher-complexity analysis).  
- Empirical results show substantial gains over KCIT and RCIT on synthetic data (scales to ~70 dimensions) and on flow-cytometry real data.  
- Paper is well-written with clear problem statement, precise pseudocode for Algorithms 1–3, and reproducible experimental details.  
- Clearly differentiates from permutation-based and kernel methods, and situates theoretical results relative to prior work.  
- Original contribution: novel reduction of CI testing to classification and a practical nearest-neighbor bootstrap with theoretical justification.  
- Significance: advances CI testing with practical gains in high-dimensional settings and applicability to causal inference and feature selection.  
- Strength: strong theoretical guarantees, comprehensive empirical validation, scalability, and clear presentation.  
- Weakness: theoretical results rely on smoothness assumptions that may not hold universally.  
- Weakness: sensitivity of nearest-neighbor bootstrap to distance metric choice is not thoroughly explored.  
- Weakness: computational complexity of the bootstrap for extremely large datasets is not fully addressed.  
- Weakness: bias correction analysis in Algorithm 3 is brief and could be expanded.  
- Pro for acceptance: high-quality, novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically strong contribution with clear practical utility.  
- Con for acceptance: limited scope of theoretical guarantees and insufficient discussion of computational issues for very large datasets.
