This paper presents a novel approach to non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing, which is a crucial problem in statistics and machine learning. The authors propose a model-powered CI test that reduces the problem of CI testing to binary classification. This approach allows them to leverage the power of supervised learning models, such as gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks, to handle complex probability distributions and achieve better performance compared to the state-of-the-art methods.

The main idea of the paper is to convert the CI testing problem into a classification problem by generating samples that are close to the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). The authors propose a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate these samples and provide theoretical guarantees on the closeness of the generated samples to fCI in terms of total variational distance.

The paper also provides theoretical results on the generalization bounds for classification under near-independence, which is a novel contribution to the field of machine learning. The authors show that the samples generated by the nearest-neighbor bootstrap are close to i.i.d, and they provide a novel analysis of Rademacher complexity bounds under near-independence.

The empirical evaluation of the proposed algorithm, CCIT, shows that it outperforms the state-of-the-art methods, KCIT and RCIT, on both synthetic and real datasets. The results demonstrate that CCIT can handle high-dimensional CI testing and scales better with the dimension of Z.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed approach and its theoretical guarantees. The empirical evaluation is thorough, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner.

Strengths:

* The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure may not be efficient for large datasets, as it requires computing the nearest neighbor for each sample in U1.
