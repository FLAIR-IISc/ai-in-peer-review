- Summary: Proposes reducing nonparametric CI testing to binary classification by NN-bootstrap of Z to approximate fCI and two-sample classify original vs bootstrapped samples.

- Theoretical results: Proves NN-bootstrap is TV-close to fCI with rate O(n^{-1/dz}), gives generalization bounds for near-independent negative samples, and proposes a bias-corrected classifier comparison.

- Empirics: Shows improvements over KCIT/RCIT on synthetic PNL data with moderate-to-high-dimensional Z and an application to flow-cytometry.

- Relation to prior work: Connects to kernel CI tests (KCIT/RCIT), KCIPT, and classifier-based two-sample tests; novelty is scalable Z-NN bootstrap replacing LP-based permutations and use of modern classifiers.

- Strength: Conceptual simplicity and modularity—turns CI testing into a classification problem enabling powerful off-the-shelf classifiers and fast NN search.

- Strength: Solid, nontrivial theory—TV closeness and extending VC/Rademacher-style generalization to near-independent samples are technically interesting.

- Strength: Scalability—NN bootstrap is computationally attractive versus LP permutations and authors provide code.

- Strength: Empirical performance—demonstrates clear gains in certain high-dimensional-Z regimes and a practical application.

- Weakness: Lack of statistical calibration—decision rules based on held-out accuracy and heuristic thresholds do not provide level-α control or p-values and ROC/AUC don’t address single-test Type-I error.

- Weakness: Curse of dimensionality—the TV rate O(n^{-1/dz}) is weak when dz is large and assumptions (curvature, smoothness/integrability) are nonstandard and opaque.

- Weakness: Complex near-independence analysis—dependencies like shared NNs and clustering are abstracted into G(ε) and lack quantitative diagnostics or empirical checks.

- Weakness: Bias-corrected method is heuristic—Type-I calibration is unclear and it may reduce power when X|Z signal is weak relative to Y|Z nuisance.

- Clarity issue: Some assumptions (e.g., generalized curvature Ia(z)) and constants are hard to parse; need intuitive restatements and concrete examples (e.g., Gaussian or ANM).

- Clarity issue: Heavy notation, minor typos/inconsistencies, and overloaded symbols; recommend a succinct figure showing data flow and independence relations.

- Originality: NN-bootstrap to approximate fCI and the near-independence generalization bounds are novel and elegant relative to prior Z-aware permutations.

- Significance: If calibrated, the method could be a strong practical CI test in high-dimensional Z settings and the theoretical tools may benefit related bootstrap/synthetic-data methods.

- Suggestion: Provide a hypothesis-testing procedure with finite-sample Type-I control (e.g., conditional label permutation, cross-fitting, wild bootstrap) and report empirical null/false-positive rates.

- Suggestion: Explore m-NN or radius-based matching and cross-fitting (multiple disjoint U2/U3) to reduce dependence/variance and analyze trade-offs.

- Suggestion: Report runtime comparisons against KCIPT and kernel tests, include multivariate X/Y experiments, and test robustness to heterogeneous/noisy Z.

- Suggestion: Discuss extensions to mixed discrete/continuous variables and non-Euclidean Z metrics.

- Suggestion: Include sensitivity analyses to classifier choice and hyperparameters and guard against overfitting via nested CV.

- Minor comment: Clarify the DAG/edge statement about conditional independence to avoid confusion with collider/path effects.

- Minor comment: Unify dataset notation (U1,U2,U3,U'2) and define b(n) at first use.

- Acceptance pro: Novel, simple, theoretically justified NN-bootstrap reduction with strong empirical results and open-source code.

- Acceptance con: No principled calibrated test for Type-I error, theory degrades with dz and relies on strong assumptions, and empirical evaluation is limited in breadth.

- Overall: Promising and original with solid theory and practical appeal; recommend acceptance if testing calibration and assumption clarity are strengthened, otherwise borderline.
