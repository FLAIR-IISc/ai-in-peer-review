- Proposes reducing continuous conditional independence testing to a binary classification problem using generated fCI samples.
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate fCI samples and trains classifiers to distinguish them from real joint samples.
- Provides theoretical guarantees: total variation bounds for generated samples and generalization bounds via Rademacher complexity in a non-i.i.d. setting.
- Leverages modern classifiers (gradient boosted trees, deep nets) and demonstrates empirical improvements on synthetic and real datasets.
- Frames a novel extension of classifier-based independence testing to the more complex CI setting.
- Writing and algorithm descriptions are clear and well organized.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis, notably the non-i.i.d. Rademacher complexity treatment.
- Strength: empirical performance and observed scalability despite conservative worst-case theory.
- Weakness: relies on smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) that may not hold broadly; sensitivity not thoroughly tested.
- Weakness: the threshold parameter (τ) in Algorithm 3 lacks practical tuning guidance.
- Weakness: potential computational cost of bootstrap and classifier training, especially in high dimensions, is under-discussed.
- Weakness: empirical comparisons to KCIT/RCIT are limited; no comprehensive datasets, parameter sweeps, or runtime comparisons.
- Weakness: Algorithms 2 and 3 are similar, giving a slightly disjoint presentation and redundancy.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach with strong theory, empirical gains, and potential to leverage ML advances for statistical inference.
- Arguments against acceptance: strong assumptions, insufficient tuning and computational analysis, and a less comprehensive empirical evaluation.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution worth acceptance with minor revision to address parameter tuning guidance and computational complexity discussion.
