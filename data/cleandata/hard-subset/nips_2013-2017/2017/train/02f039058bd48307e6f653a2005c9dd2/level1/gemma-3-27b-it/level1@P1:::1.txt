This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach for conditional independence (CI) testing of continuous random variables. The core idea is to recast CI testing as a binary classification problem, leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks to distinguish between the joint distribution and the conditionally independent distribution. A key technical contribution is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution, coupled with theoretical guarantees on the quality of these samples and generalization bounds for the classification task. The paper provides empirical validation on both simulated and real datasets, demonstrating performance gains over existing methods.

The paper builds upon prior work in CI testing, particularly kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT [32, 28]. It distinguishes itself by avoiding expensive linear programs [10] and utilizing flexible, high-dimensional classifiers. The reduction to classification is inspired by recent work using classifiers for independence testing [19], but extends this approach to the more challenging CI setting.  The theoretical contributions relating to generalization bounds in the presence of non-i.i.d samples are also a significant contribution, drawing on related work in Rademacher complexity [4, 21]. The application to flow cytometry data connects the method to a relevant biological application, building on existing causal discovery efforts in that domain [26].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem is a conceptually appealing and potentially powerful approach, allowing for the exploitation of advancements in supervised learning.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical analysis of the bootstrap procedure and generalization bounds, establishing a formal foundation for the method. The analysis of Rademacher complexity in a non-i.i.d setting is particularly noteworthy.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art alternatives on both synthetic and real datasets, showcasing its practical effectiveness.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key ideas and technical details.  Algorithm 2 and 3 are particularly helpful in understanding the method.
* **Practical Implementation:** Providing a publicly available Python package for the implementation is a valuable contribution to reproducibility and accessibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the nearest neighbor bootstrap is presented as efficient, the computational cost of finding nearest neighbors in high-dimensional spaces could become a bottleneck for very large datasets. This is not thoroughly discussed.
* **Sensitivity to Parameters:** The performance may be sensitive to the choice of parameters, such as the threshold ⌧ in Algorithm 3 and the parameters of the chosen classifier (e.g., XGBoost). A more in-depth sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions:** The smoothness assumptions required for the theoretical guarantees (Assumptions 1 & 2) are strong and may not hold for all real-world datasets. The implications of violating these assumptions are not fully explored.  The discussion of the limitations of these assumptions in practice would strengthen the paper.
* **Bias Correction Improvement:** The second version of the algorithm (Algorithm 3) with bias correction is a worthwhile addition, but the justification for subtracting the test loss of the classifier trained only on Y and Z feels somewhat heuristic. A more rigorous theoretical justification could improve this aspect.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the paper compares against KCIT and RCIT, it doesn't comprehensively compare against other relevant methods, such as those based on entropy estimation [13].



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to CI testing.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant performance gains over existing methods.
* The theoretical contributions are valuable and advance our understanding of classification in non-i.i.d settings.
* The publicly available implementation promotes reproducibility and wider adoption.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap could be a limitation.
* The strong smoothness assumptions may restrict the applicability of the theoretical guarantees.
* The heuristic justification for the bias correction in Algorithm 3 could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of CI testing. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the theoretical guarantees and empirical validation, justifies acceptance. While the weaknesses mentioned above are valid concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the paper. I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017.  I suggest the authors address the comments regarding computational cost, sensitivity analysis, and the justification for the bias correction in a final revision.
