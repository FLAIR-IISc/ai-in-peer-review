

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors convert the CI testing problem into a classification task where the goal is to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution f(x,y,z) and samples from the conditional independent distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). The key innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) that generates samples close to f_CI from i.i.d. samples of the true joint distribution. This allows them to leverage powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, deep neural networks) for CI testing. The paper provides theoretical guarantees showing that the bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance and establishes generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. Empirical results demonstrate significant performance gains over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real-world flow-cytometry data.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is sound, with Theorem 1 establishing that the bootstrap samples are close to f_CI (d_TV(,f_CI) = O(1/n^(1/d_z))), and Theorem 2 providing generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. The empirical validation is comprehensive, with extensive experiments on synthetic data showing scalability to high dimensions (up to 70) and real-world data demonstrating practical utility. The authors carefully address the theoretical limitations of their approach, particularly regarding the smoothness assumptions. The experimental setup is well-designed, with proper comparison to relevant baselines (KCIT, RCIT) and appropriate metrics (ROC AUC).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement, methodology, and theoretical contributions are clearly presented. The pseudocode for Algorithms 1-3 is precise and easy to follow. The relation to previous work (particularly [10], [28], [32]) is well-explained, with clear differentiation of their approach from permutation-based methods. The theoretical results are properly contextualized, and the empirical results are presented with sufficient detail to understand the methodology and findings. The paper provides enough information for an expert to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The paper makes significant original contributions. The core idea of reducing CI testing to binary classification is novel, and the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure provides a practical solution to the key challenge of generating samples from f_CI. This differs fundamentally from prior work that relies on permutations [10] or kernel-based methods [28, 32]. The theoretical analysis of Rademacher complexity under near-independence (Theorem 2) is also novel and of independent interest. The authors clearly demonstrate how their approach differs from previous work, showing that their bootstrap method is both theoretically justified and computationally more efficient than alternatives.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem with wide-ranging applications in causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection. The authors demonstrate that their method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches, particularly in high-dimensional settings where previous methods struggle. By leveraging advances in classification, their approach naturally benefits from improvements in that field. The empirical results on the flow-cytometry dataset show practical utility in a real-world biological application. The paper advances the state of the art in CI testing in a demonstrable way.

**Strengths:** 
- Novel reduction of CI testing to binary classification
- Theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap procedure and classification generalization
- Strong empirical results showing scalability to high dimensions
- Clear, well-organized presentation
- Practical implementation with good performance on real data

**Weaknesses:**
- Theoretical guarantees assume specific smoothness conditions that may not hold for all distributions
- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap's sensitivity to distance metric choice isn't thoroughly explored
- The paper could better address the computational complexity of the bootstrap procedure for extremely large datasets
- The analysis of the bias correction in Algorithm 3 is somewhat brief

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* This is a high-quality, novel contribution that makes a significant advance in a fundamental statistical problem. The theoretical and empirical results are strong, and the approach has clear practical utility. It aligns well with NIPS' interest in novel machine learning approaches to statistical problems.

*Con:* The theoretical guarantees are somewhat limited in scope due to the smoothness assumptions, and the paper could better address computational aspects for very large datasets.

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of statistical testing. The model-powered approach is elegant and effective, and the theoretical analysis provides rigorous justification for the method. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages over existing methods, especially in high-dimensional settings. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the state of the art in conditional independence testing. I recommend acceptance.
