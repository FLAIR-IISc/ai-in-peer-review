

This paper introduces a novel model-powered approach to non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors propose a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) when only samples from the true joint distribution are available. Their key theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which establishes that the generated samples are close to f_CI in total variational distance under smoothness assumptions, and Theorem 2, which provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. The method leverages powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and outperforms state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic and real-world datasets, particularly excelling in high-dimensional settings.

The paper demonstrates strong quality through rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical validation. The theoretical guarantees on the bootstrap procedure and classification generalization are well-motivated and address a critical gap in existing CI testing literature. The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with well-structured algorithms and clear connections to prior work, particularly distinguishing their approach from KCIPT [10] which requires solving expensive linear programs. The originality is significant—the conversion of CI testing to classification is novel, and the nearest-neighbor bootstrap provides a theoretically grounded alternative to permutation-based methods for high-dimensional Z.

The significance is clear: CI testing is fundamental to causal inference and Bayesian network learning, and this work provides a scalable solution that leverages advances in classification. The empirical results on the flow-cytometry dataset demonstrate practical utility for real-world causal discovery problems.

A minor weakness is that the theoretical analysis focuses on worst-case guarantees, while the empirical results show better performance than expected, suggesting room for tighter theoretical bounds. Additionally, the connection to recent work on non-i.i.d generalization (e.g., [21]) could be strengthened.

Overall, this is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The model-powered approach is elegant, theoretically sound, and empirically validated, with clear potential for broad adoption in causal inference and machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
