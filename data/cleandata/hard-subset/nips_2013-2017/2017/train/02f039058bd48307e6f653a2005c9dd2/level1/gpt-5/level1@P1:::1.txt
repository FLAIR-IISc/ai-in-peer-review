Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “model-powered” nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables by reducing CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from f(x,y,z), the method (i) holds out n samples as positives, (ii) constructs n “negative” samples meant to approximate the conditionally independent product fCI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap that swaps Y within nearest neighbors in Z, and (iii) trains a classifier (e.g., XGBoost, DNNs) to distinguish the two; poor accuracy implies X ⊥ Y | Z. Because the bootstrap negatives are only approximately i.i.d. and only approximately from fCI, the authors prove: (a) the generated samples are close to fCI in total variation (TV) under smoothness assumptions, with rate O(n^{-1/dz}); (b) generalization bounds for ERM classifiers under “near-independence,” extending Rademacher/VC-style results to this setting; and (c) a bias-corrected variant that subtracts the performance of a classifier trained on (Y,Z)-only features. Empirically, CCIT outperforms kernel-based methods KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear data (scaling to large dz) and shows favorable ROC/AUC on flow cytometry conditional relations.

The work is related to and extends: classifier two-sample testing (C2ST) and independence testing via permutation (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016), where permutation yields exact factorization for independence but not CI; KCIT and RCIT (kernel CI tests); and KCIPT (Doran et al., UAI), which also attempts to emulate fCI via a permutation/optimal transport step but is computationally heavy and lacks TV guarantees. The paper also engages with generalization bounds under dependent data (Mohri & Rostamizadeh, NIPS 2008) and with kNN-based density/entropy estimation (Gao et al.).

Quality
Strengths:
- Provides a simple and computationally efficient nearest-neighbor bootstrap that approximates fCI without solving an LP, with a rigorous TV bound under clear assumptions.
- Novel generalization analysis for ERM classifiers with near-independent samples and an explicit bound that connects to dTV(f,fCI), supporting the classification reduction.
- Bias-corrected testing procedure that is intuitive and improves finite-sample behavior.
- Strong empirical performance in high-dimensional Z and publicly released code.
- Clear modularity: improvements in classifiers directly translate into stronger tests.

Weaknesses:
- The TV approximation rate O(n^{-1/dz}) is extremely slow in the worst case; the theory degrades sharply with dz and relies on fairly strong smoothness assumptions (curvature of f(y|z), bounded Hessian and Lipschitz properties of f(z)).
- The generated negatives are not i.i.d., and while the authors bound generalization error, they do not provide a formal level-α test or finite-sample type-I error control. The choice τ≈1/√n is heuristic, and the bias-correction (subtracting (Y,Z)-only accuracy) lacks a formal guarantee that the resulting test is well-calibrated.
- Power and size as functions of sample size, classifier capacity, and hyperparameters are not systematically characterized; the method’s dependence on the choice of classifier (e.g., XGBoost vs DNNs) is a practical challenge.
- The experimental section could be stronger: limited ablations (e.g., varying noise level, sample size n, and using k>1 in kNN), limited runtime comparisons against RCIT/KCIT, and limited reporting of empirical type-I/II error under controlled α.
- The flow cytometry evaluation uses proxy ground truth and ad-hoc negative examples; conclusions about real-world performance should be tempered.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The reduction and algorithms (CCITv1/v2) are easy to follow.
- Assumption 1 (generalized curvature matrix I_a(z)) is nonstandard and could use more intuition and examples linking it to familiar smoothness notions.
- Notation occasionally heavy; a schematic of the data splits and dependence structure would help.

Originality
- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap to emulate fCI is conceptually simple yet original and practically attractive, improving on KCIPT’s computational burden.
- The generalization bounds under near-independent sampling for ERM are novel in this CI context and of independent interest.
- The classification-based CI reduction extends C2ST-style ideas to the harder CI setting with a principled data-generation step.

Significance
- Provides a practical, flexible, and scalable CI testing framework that leverages advances in classification. Likely to be useful in causal discovery and graphical models.
- The theory, while conservative in dz, establishes a foundation for model-powered testing and may stimulate further work on improved conditional bootstraps (e.g., kNN, local regression, optimal transport approximations with guarantees) and calibrated testing.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Calibrated testing: Provide a permutation/wild bootstrap over the DataGen step or cross-fitting to empirically estimate the null distribution of the accuracy gap and control type-I error at a target α. Alternatively, report empirical size across n.
- Use k-NN with k>1 or local kernel smoothing when forming Y-swaps to reduce variance and potentially improve the approximation rate; analyze the bias-variance tradeoff.
- Discuss and, if possible, relax Assumption 1 with more interpretable smoothness conditions (e.g., Lipschitz of conditional log-density).
- Add ablations: effect of n, classifier choice and depth, dz, noise variance, and strength of dependence c. Report runtime comparisons.
- Provide guidance for τ selection, or replace with a data-dependent threshold via holdout/permutation.
- Consider reporting test power/size rather than only AUC for synthetic experiments.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and effective method with strong empirical performance.
- New theoretical tools: TV closeness of a practical CI bootstrap and generalization bounds under near-independence.
- Bridges modern classification with a core statistical testing problem; likely community interest.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of formal α-level control and limited calibration discussion.
- Conservative theory in dz and strong assumptions; gap between theory and practice not fully explored.
- Experimental evaluation could be more thorough and reproducibility details (hyperparameters) expanded.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a practical, conceptually clean contribution with nontrivial theory and promising empirical results. Strengthening calibration and experimental analysis would make it even more compelling.
