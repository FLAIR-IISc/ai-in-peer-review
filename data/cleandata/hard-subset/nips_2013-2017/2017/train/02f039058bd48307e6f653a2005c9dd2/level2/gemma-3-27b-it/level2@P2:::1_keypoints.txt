- Proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing CI testing to a binary classification problem.  
- Uses powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, deep nets) to perform the converted CI test.  
- Addresses the key challenge of generating fCI samples via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap samples (total variation bounds) and classifier generalization (including Rademacher complexity in non-i.i.d. setting).  
- Empirically validates the method on simulated and real datasets, showing strong performance, especially in high-dimensional settings.  
- Well-motivated problem and conceptually sound framing aligned with model-driven inference trends.  
- Nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a reasonable practical solution to generate fCI samples.  
- Theoretical analysis (TV bounds, generalization error) is a significant contribution and increases method reliability.  
- Experimental results show promising gains over existing methods and demonstrate real-data utility (flow cytometry).  
- Related work is adequately covered, citing kernel-based and permutation-based CI tests and theoretical analyses.  
- Novelty: combination of classification-based CI testing, nearest-neighbor bootstrap, and theory is new.  
- Theoretical soundness: rigorous guarantees including non-i.i.d. Rademacher analysis.  
- Empirical performance: outperforms baselines in high dimensions and on real data.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with helpful pseudocode and clear explanations.  
- Computational cost: overall runtime of bootstrap plus training powerful classifiers may be high and is not thoroughly discussed.  
- Parameter sensitivity: sensitivity to nearest-neighbor and classifier hyperparameters is not extensively explored or analyzed.  
- Assumptions: theoretical results rely on smoothness assumptions; robustness to assumption violations is not investigated.  
- Algorithm 3 complexity: bias-correction via two classifiers adds complexity and its benefit over Algorithm 2 is not convincingly demonstrated.  
- Missing baseline: lacks comparison to simpler nonparametric CI tests (e.g., mutual information–based) as additional baselines.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound, strong theory, promising empirical gains, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unresolved computational cost and parameter sensitivity, reliance on smoothness assumptions, and unclear benefit of the more complex Algorithm 3.  
- Overall assessment: significant contribution deserving acceptance with minor revisions to address computational aspects, parameter tuning, robustness to assumption violations, and clearer comparison between Algorithm 2 and Algorithm 3.
