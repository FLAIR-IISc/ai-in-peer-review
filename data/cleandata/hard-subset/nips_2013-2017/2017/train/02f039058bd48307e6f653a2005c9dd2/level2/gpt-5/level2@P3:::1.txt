Summary
The paper addresses nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables by reducing CI testing to binary classification. Given samples from f(x,y,z), the method constructs a “null-like” sample set approximating fCI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap: partition the data, for each (x,y,z) in one part find its 1-NN in z among the other part and swap the y-coordinates, yielding (x,y’,z). CI testing is then a two-sample classification problem between original samples and NN-bootstrapped samples; powerful classifiers (e.g., XGBoost, DNNs) provide the test statistic. A bias-corrected variant trains (i) a classifier on all features and (ii) a classifier on (y,z) only, using their difference to reduce finite-sample bias.

Theory is provided on two fronts. First, the distribution of a bootstrapped sample is proved close to fCI in total variation, with a rate O(n^{-1/d_z}) under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z). Second, generalization error bounds for empirical risk minimization are developed under near-independence (since bootstrapped samples are not i.i.d.), yielding classification guarantees comparable to standard VC/Rademacher results up to additional terms reflecting dependence. Empirically, the method outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear setups as d_z grows (to ~70), and on flow cytometry protein network data. A code repository is provided.

Relation to prior work
This fits within model-powered reductions that leverage advances in supervised learning. It extends classifier-based two-sample and independence testing (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016) to CI testing by crafting an approximate conditional-product sample. Compared to permutation-based KCIPT (Doran et al., 2014), the proposed NN bootstrap avoids solving a linear program and comes with total variation guarantees. The analysis connects to non-i.i.d. generalization bounds (Mohri & Rostamizadeh, 2008) and to NN-based information/entropy estimation (Gao et al., 2016; 2017).

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with strong practical appeal: reduction of CI testing to classification enables use of state-of-the-art learners and domain knowledge.
- Novel and efficient NN bootstrap to approximate fCI, with explicit total variation bounds.
- Generalization analysis under near-independence that adapts classical VC/Rademacher tools to this setting.
- Empirical gains over KCIT and RCIT, especially in higher-dimensional Z; real-data evaluation on flow cytometry.
- Open-source implementation; clear algorithmic description including a bias-corrected variant.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Statistical calibration: the testing procedure is presented primarily as a classifier-accuracy thresholding and ROC analysis. A principled level-α test with valid p-values and finite-sample Type I control is not fully developed. The choice τ=1/√n is heuristic; ROC AUCs, while informative, do not substitute for controlled error rates.
- Assumptions and rates: the smoothness assumptions (e.g., the generalized curvature matrix for f(y|z)) are nonstandard and could use more intuition and examples. The total-variation rate O(n^{-1/d_z}) exhibits a severe curse of dimensionality; guidance on when the assumptions hold and whether milder conditions suffice would help.
- NN search and high-dimensional Z: practical nearest-neighbor search degrades in high dimensions. The method scales to d_z≈70 empirically, but details on exact vs approximate NN, distance metrics, and their impact on the guarantees are missing.
- Baselines and runtime: KCIPT is highly relevant; even if expensive, small-scale comparisons would be informative. Conditional mutual information estimators (e.g., kNN-based CMI) and CHSIC variants could broaden baselines. Runtime and scalability comparisons are not reported despite claims of efficiency.
- Sensitivity and ablations: the role of classifier choice G, hyperparameters, and training protocol is not systematically explored. Type I error under H0 across settings, sensitivity to distributional non-smoothness, and to d_x/d_y are underreported.

Quality
The technical development is careful, with nontrivial proofs for the NN bootstrap’s closeness to fCI and for near-i.i.d. generalization bounds. The empirical section supports the main claims, though calibration and runtime analyses are limited. Statements are generally supported by theory or experiments; claims about superiority over prior work are backed by comparisons to KCIT/RCIT and references to KCIPT’s computational burden.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The algorithmic pipeline is easy to follow. Some assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1) would benefit from more intuition and concrete examples in the main text. Practical guidance on choosing τ and G, and on implementing NN search in high dimensions, should be expanded.

Originality
The reduction of CI testing to classification via a tailored NN bootstrap for approximating fCI appears novel. The near-independence generalization analysis tailored to this construction is also original and of independent interest.

Significance
CI testing is central in structure learning and causal discovery. A simple, scalable method that leverages modern classifiers is potentially impactful for practitioners. The theoretical framework may inspire further “model-powered” reductions in hypothesis testing.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Provide a calibrated testing procedure with valid p-values and Type I error control. Options include: (a) a binomial test on test-set accuracy under H0 with label exchangeability; (b) permutation or split-conformal calibration of the accuracy difference in CCITv2; (c) repeated sample-splitting with aggregation. Report empirical Type I error and power at fixed α.
- Clarify and illustrate Assumption 1 with families where it holds (e.g., additive noise models with smooth link, exponential families). Discuss robustness to violations.
- Document the NN search details (exact vs approximate, metric), and assess the effect of approximate NN errors on theory and practice.

Important but optional:
- Include runtime scaling and memory comparisons versus KCIT/RCIT; attempt small-scale KCIPT runs if feasible.
- Add baselines based on conditional mutual information estimators and CHSIC variants.
- Provide ablations across classifier classes (e.g., XGBoost vs DNNs) and guidance for selecting G; analyze sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Explore k-NN or local resampling variants in Algorithm 1 and their effect on bias/variance.

Minor:
- Specify tie-breaking and handling of rare z regions in Algorithm 1.
- Expand intuition for the bias-correction in CCITv2 and formalize when it removes the finite-sample bias.

Overall assessment
A strong contribution with a simple, practical algorithm, nontrivial theory, and promising empirical results. With improved statistical calibration and additional empirical analyses, this work would be a valuable addition to the CI testing literature. Recommended for acceptance after addressing essential points.
