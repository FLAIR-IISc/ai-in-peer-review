- Proposes reducing conditional independence testing to binary classification using powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, neural nets).  
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI.  
- Theoretically proves the bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance.  
- Provides generalization bounds for classification under the near-i.i.d. samples produced by the bootstrap (Theorems 1 and 2).  
- Theorem 1: d_TV(bootstrap, f_CI) = O(1/n^{1/d_z}); Theorem 2: generalization bounds for near-independent samples.  
- Empirical results show clear advantages over KCIT and RCIT on synthetic and real datasets.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and situates results within the nonparametric CI testing literature.  
- Strength: enables use of advanced classifiers to handle high-dimensional data, practically scaling better than expected.  
- Strength: nearest-neighbor bootstrap is simpler and more efficient than prior methods like KCIPT (which uses an expensive LP).  
- Weakness: lacks explanation for why empirical performance outperforms the worst-case theoretical rates—needs investigation.  
- Weakness: bias correction in Algorithm 3 is empirically effective but lacks theoretical justification or guarantees.  
- Weakness: computational complexity of nearest-neighbor search in high dimensions is not sufficiently discussed; scalability concerns and approximations should be addressed.  
- Weakness: empirical comparisons omit some recent relevant methods (e.g., Strobl et al. 2017 approximate kernel CI test).  
- Significance: high—advances CI testing with implications for causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection; reviewer recommends acceptance.
