## Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing for continuous variables. The core idea – framing CI testing as a classification problem leveraging powerful classifiers like boosted trees and neural networks – is promising and aligns well with the trend of model-driven approaches in statistical inference. The authors address the key hurdle of generating samples from the conditional independence distribution (fCI) using a novel nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure, and importantly, provide theoretical guarantees on its accuracy (in terms of total variational distance) and generalization bounds for the resulting classification task. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the applications of CI testing and the limitations of existing methods. The theoretical contributions, particularly the generalization bounds in a non-i.i.d setting, are a significant strength. The empirical evaluation on both simulated and real (flow cytometry) datasets demonstrates performance gains over state-of-the-art methods like KCIT and RCIT, though the gains appear more substantial in the synthetic data. The inclusion of Algorithm 3 with bias correction is a thoughtful addition, acknowledging and addressing potential issues with finite sample sizes.

However, the paper suffers from a degree of verbosity, with substantial repetition of concepts and a lengthy presentation of related work. The theoretical analysis, while strong, is dense and may be inaccessible to readers not deeply versed in statistical learning theory. Furthermore, while the flow cytometry results show improvement, a more in-depth discussion of the biological relevance of the discovered CI relationships would strengthen the impact. The performance gains in real datasets seem moderate and the discussion about discrepancies in the ground truth (edges present in all three graphs) could be expanded.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to CI testing with strong theoretical backing.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over existing methods.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in causal inference and related fields.
*   Provides useful insights into classification under near-independence.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Verbosity and repetitive presentation.
*   Dense theoretical analysis potentially limiting accessibility.
*   Moderate performance gains on real datasets.
*   Limited biological interpretation of results.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution to the field. While the paper could benefit from increased conciseness and a more accessible presentation of the theoretical results, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the empirical validation suggest it merits acceptance, perhaps with a request for revisions to address the points mentioned above. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
