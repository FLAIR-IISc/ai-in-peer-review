- Reduces non-parametric conditional independence testing to binary classification by distinguishing real joint samples from synthetic conditionally independent samples.
- Generates synthetic samples via a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap: replace y_i with y_j from the z-nearest neighbor to z_i to approximate f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).
- Proves the bootstrapped sample distribution is close to f_CI in total variation distance with error depending on the dimension of Z (Theorem 1).
- Derives new Rademacher-complexity-based generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the near-independent (non-i.i.d.) bootstrapped samples (Theorem 2).
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance: CCIT outperforms kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT), especially as the dimension of Z increases, and succeeds on real flow cytometry data.
- Introduces a bias-corrected variant (CCITv2) and shows practical applicability using powerful classifiers (trees, neural nets).
- Strength — originality/significance: elegantly connects CI testing to supervised learning, enabling scalable, model-powered tests.
- Strength — theoretical foundation: solid proofs for sample closeness and novel learning-theory results for near-i.i.d. data.
- Strength — empirical validation: comprehensive synthetic and real-data experiments supporting claimed advantages.
- Strength — clarity: well-written, intuitive exposition and clear algorithmic presentation.
- Weakness — threshold τ: practical choice τ=1/√n lacks full theoretical justification and guidance for Type I error control; permutation calibration suggested.
- Weakness — assumptions: reliance on smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1–2) could be clarified intuitively and discussed for restrictiveness.
- Weakness — computational complexity: lacks a formal overall complexity analysis and direct comparison to KCIT/RCIT costs.
- Weakness — classifier dependence: performance hinges on classifier choice and tuning, introducing researcher degrees of freedom and potential sensitivity.
