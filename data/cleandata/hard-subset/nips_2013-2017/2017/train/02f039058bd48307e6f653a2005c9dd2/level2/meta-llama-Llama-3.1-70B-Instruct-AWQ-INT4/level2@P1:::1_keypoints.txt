- Proposes CCIT: converts non-parametric conditional independence testing into a binary classification problem.  
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees on the closeness of bootstrap samples to the true distribution.  
- Derives generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples.  
- Empirical results show CCIT outperforms state-of-the-art methods on synthetic and real data.  
- Novelty: reduction of CI testing to supervised classification is a significant departure from kernel-based methods.  
- Novelty: nearest-neighbor bootstrap for generating conditionally independent samples is a new contribution.  
- Strength: enables use of powerful supervised learning tools and different classifiers.  
- Strength: solid theoretical analysis supporting the method.  
- Strength: demonstrated empirical improvements over existing CI tests.  
- Weakness: theoretical guarantees depend on smoothness assumptions that may not hold in practice.  
- Weakness: nearest-neighbor bootstrap could be computationally expensive on large datasets.  
- Weakness: requires hyperparameter tuning (e.g., threshold), which may be challenging.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with standard, well-defined notation.  
- Quality: technically sound analysis and convincing experimental evaluation.  
- Significance: relevant to ML, statistics, causal discovery, feature selection, and Bayesian network learning.  
- Pro arguments: novel approach, theoretical guarantees, and empirical superiority support acceptance.  
- Con arguments: reliance on smoothness assumptions, computational cost, and hyperparameter sensitivity argue for caution.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address assumptions, computational complexity, and hyperparameter tuning details.
