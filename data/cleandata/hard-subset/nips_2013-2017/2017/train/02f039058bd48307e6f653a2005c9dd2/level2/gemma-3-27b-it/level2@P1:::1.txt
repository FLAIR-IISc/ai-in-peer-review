This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables by framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea is to generate samples resembling the conditional independence distribution, fCI, using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure, and then train a classifier to distinguish between these generated samples and original samples. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the quality of the generated samples and generalization bounds for the classifier, and demonstrate improved performance on both simulated and real-world datasets compared to existing methods.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental problem in statistical inference with broad applications. The reduction to a classification problem is a clever idea, allowing the leveraging of powerful modern classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, deep neural networks). The theoretical analysis, including the total variational distance bound for the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1) and the generalization bounds (Theorem 2), is a significant contribution. The empirical results provide convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly its scalability with dimensionality. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world experiments strengthens the evaluation. The detailed related work section is also commendable.

However, several aspects could be improved. 

1. **Clarity of Algorithm 3:** Algorithm 3 (CCITv2) with bias correction is presented, but the rationale for subtracting the test loss of the classifier trained solely on Y and Z coordinates is not fully explained. It relies on the assumption that the classifier can perfectly distinguish between the distributions only based on Y and Z when H0 holds. A more intuitive explanation connecting this to the bias in the original classifier's error would be helpful.  Also, the specific value of the threshold ⌧ = 1/sqrt(n) feels somewhat arbitrary. A justification or sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.

2. **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implications of the parameters involved (e.g., the smoothness assumptions in Theorem 1, the VC dimension in Theorem 2) need further discussion.  How sensitive is the performance to violations of these assumptions in realistic scenarios?  What guidance can be provided for choosing appropriate hyperparameters for the nearest-neighbor bootstrap (e.g., the number of neighbors) and the classifier?

3. **Comparison to Recent Work:** The related work section is comprehensive but slightly dated in some areas.  Recent advances in kernel-based CI tests (beyond KCIT and RCIT mentioned) and non-parametric causal discovery should be discussed. For example, methods based on energy-based models or neural ordinary differential equations might offer relevant comparisons.

4. **Computational Cost:** The nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) can be computationally expensive, especially in high dimensions. While the authors mention the efficiency of fast nearest-neighbor algorithms, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, particularly as a function of sample size and dimensionality, is needed. How does this compare to the computational cost of existing methods like KCIT?

5. **Empirical Evaluation Depth:** While the empirical results are positive, the comparison could be more thorough.  Specifically, the impact of different classifiers (beyond XGBoost) on performance should be explored. Also, a more detailed analysis of the results on the flow cytometry dataset, including a comparison of the discovered CI relations to existing biological knowledge, would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Novel and insightful approach to CI testing by framing it as a classification problem.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees for the generated samples and classification accuracy.
*   Empirically demonstrates improved performance over state-of-the-art methods.
*   Well-written and clearly presented, with a comprehensive related work section.
*   Addresses a fundamental problem with broad applications.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   Clarity of the bias correction mechanism in Algorithm 3 could be improved.
*   Practical implications of the theoretical assumptions need further discussion.
*   Computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a potential concern.
*   Comparison to more recent CI testing methods would be beneficial.
*   Empirical evaluation could be more thorough, exploring different classifiers and a deeper analysis of the real-world data.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing.  The proposed method is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While some aspects could be further clarified and expanded upon, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly those concerning the clarity of Algorithm 3 and a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical assumptions.
