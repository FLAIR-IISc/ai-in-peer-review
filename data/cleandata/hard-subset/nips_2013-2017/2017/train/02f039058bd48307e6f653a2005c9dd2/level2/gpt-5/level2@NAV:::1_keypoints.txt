- Proposes a model-powered CI test reducing CI testing to binary classification via a 1-NN bootstrap that approximates fCI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Core claim 1: A simple 1-NN swap generates samples close to fCI in total variation with an explicit bound under smoothness assumptions.  
- Core claim 2: Provides generalization guarantees for ERM classifiers trained on near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples, giving misclassification risk bounds that map to CI testing error.  
- Core claim 3: Introduces a bias-corrected variant using a Y,Z-only classifier.  
- Core claim 4: Shows empirical ROC AUC improvements over KCIT and RCIT on synthetic and flow cytometry datasets.  
- Theoretical development is careful and nontrivial, with well-motivated TV and near-i.i.d. generalization results.  
- Limitation: The TV bound rate O(n^{-1/dz}) suffers from the curse of dimensionality and relies on strong smoothness assumptions for f(y|z) and f(z).  
- Empirical strength: Clear AUC improvements; empirical weakness: no demonstrated level-α control, p-values, or Type I error calibration—τ chosen heuristically.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally clear and well-organized with helpful pseudocode; missing practical guidance on τ, classifier/hyperparameter choices, and runtime.  
- Notation around near-independence analysis could be streamlined for readability.  
- Originality: NN bootstrap for approximating fCI and ERM bounds under near-independent sampling appear novel and distinct from kernel tests and KCIPT.  
- Significance: Potentially impactful for causal discovery pipelines if robustly calibrated; public code increases practical relevance.  
- Pro: Simple, general reduction with strong empirical performance and new theoretical contributions.  
- Pro: Practical algorithm with publicly available code.  
- Con: Lacks principled level-α testing/p-value calibration.  
- Con: Performance rates degrade with dim(Z); assumptions may limit applicability.  
- Con: Limited real-data evaluation, no runtime/memory benchmarks, and no comparisons to KCIPT/CMIknn.  
- Suggestions: Provide a level-α procedure (e.g., label permutations or conformal methods) to control Type I error and produce p-values.  
- Suggestions: Explore k-NN/radius bootstraps, sensitivity to metric choice, runtime/memory benchmarks, ablations, and robustness to classifier/hyperparameters.  
- Suggestions: Extend to mixed/discrete variables, compare against KCIPT and CMIknn, and offer guidance for τ selection and multiple-testing control.  
- Overall: Promising and innovative; with calibration and expanded evaluation the paper would be very strong—reviewer leans toward acceptance.
