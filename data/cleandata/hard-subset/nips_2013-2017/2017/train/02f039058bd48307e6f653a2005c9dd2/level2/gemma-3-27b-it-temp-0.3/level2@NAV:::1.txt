## Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing the problem as a binary classification task and leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The core contribution lies in a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, fCI, given only samples from the joint distribution. The authors also provide theoretical analysis of generalization bounds for this classification-based CI test.

The paper is well-motivated, as CI testing is fundamental to various fields like causal inference and Bayesian network discovery. The idea of leveraging modern classification techniques is promising, particularly for high-dimensional data where traditional methods struggle. The theoretical analysis, including the total variational distance bound for the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1) and the generalization bounds (Theorem 2), adds significant value and rigor. The empirical validation on both simulated and real (flow cytometry) datasets demonstrates performance gains over existing methods like KCIT and RCIT, particularly as the dimension of the conditioning variable (Z) increases.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical results are impressive, the assumptions required for them to hold (smoothness of f(y|z) and f(z)) might be restrictive in practice. The discussion of these limitations could be more thorough. Furthermore, the presentation is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the technical details, especially regarding the nearest-neighbor bootstrap and the generalization bounds. The connection between the theoretical bounds and the observed empirical performance isn't always clear. The experimental setup, while comprehensive, could be improved by comparing against more recent CI testing methods. The code availability is a strong positive, facilitating reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach leveraging powerful classifiers for CI testing.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous analysis.
*   Empirically validated performance gains over existing methods.
*   Code availability promotes reproducibility.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Restrictive assumptions for theoretical guarantees.
*   Dense presentation and lack of intuitive explanations.
*   Limited comparison to the most recent CI testing methods.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a novel and theoretically sound approach to CI testing. While the presentation could be improved and the assumptions acknowledged more explicitly, the contributions are significant and warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the limitations.
