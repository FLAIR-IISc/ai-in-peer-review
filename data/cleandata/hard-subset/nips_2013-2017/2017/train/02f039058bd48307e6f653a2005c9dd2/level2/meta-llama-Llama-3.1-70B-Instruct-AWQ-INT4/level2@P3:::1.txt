**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper proposes a model-powered approach for Conditional Independence (CI) testing by converting it into a binary classification problem. The central idea is to harness the power of supervised learning models, such as gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks, to improve the performance of CI testing. The authors introduce a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditional product distribution, which is a crucial step in the classification-based CI testing approach. The paper also provides theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and risk generalization bounds for the classification problem under non-i.i.d near-independent samples.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The paper proposes a new approach to CI testing by converting it into a binary classification problem, which is a departure from traditional kernel-based methods.
2. **Theoretical guarantees**: The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and risk generalization bounds for the classification problem, which is a significant contribution to the field.
3. **Empirical evaluation**: The paper presents extensive empirical evaluations on synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Flexibility**: The approach allows for the use of various classification models, including gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks, which can be chosen based on domain knowledge.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Assumptions**: The theoretical guarantees rely on smoothness assumptions on the joint distribution, which may not always hold in practice.
2. **Computational complexity**: The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure may be computationally expensive, especially for large datasets.
3. **Hyperparameter tuning**: The approach requires careful tuning of hyperparameters, such as the choice of classification model and threshold values.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel approach to CI testing, which is distinct from traditional kernel-based methods. The use of a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditional product distribution is a new contribution to the field.

**Significance**

The paper has significant implications for various applications, including causal inference, feature selection, and Bayesian network structure learning. The proposed approach has the potential to improve the performance of CI testing in high-dimensional settings.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed approach, including the theoretical guarantees and empirical evaluations. The notation is consistent, and the figures and tables are well-organized.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed approach. The theoretical guarantees are well-established, and the empirical evaluations are extensive and well-designed.
