

This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables by reducing the problem to binary classification. The key innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure that generates samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), which is necessary to distinguish between H0 (X ⊥ Y | Z) and H1 (X ⊁ Y | Z). The authors theoretically show that their bootstrap procedure produces samples close to f_CI in total variational distance (Theorem 1), and provide generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d samples (Theorem 2), which translate to error bounds for CI testing. Empirical results demonstrate clear performance gains over state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic data and a real-world protein signaling network dataset.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) It provides a theoretically grounded approach to CI testing that leverages powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks, which is particularly valuable for high-dimensional settings where previous methods struggle; (2) The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is simpler and more theoretically justified than permutation-based approaches used in prior work (e.g., KCIPT); (3) The theoretical analysis of generalization bounds under near-independent samples is novel and of independent interest to machine learning theory.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement, technical contributions, and empirical evaluation are well-structured, with appropriate pseudocode (Algorithms 1-3) and theoretical results. The related work section effectively contextualizes the paper's contributions relative to previous kernel-based methods and classification-based independence testing approaches. The experimental setup is thorough, with both synthetic experiments that scale with dimensionality of Z and a meaningful real-world application to flow-cytometry data.

The main strengths of the paper include its strong theoretical guarantees, practical empirical validation, and the clear insight that powerful classification models can be harnessed for statistical testing problems. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach scales better with dimensionality than existing methods, which is particularly important for modern high-dimensional datasets.

A few minor weaknesses could be addressed to strengthen the paper. First, the computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) could be more explicitly discussed, particularly regarding the scalability of the nearest-neighbor search as dimensionality increases. While the paper mentions "fast algorithms for identifying nearest-neighbors," a brief discussion of computational complexity would help readers understand the practical limitations of the approach.

Second, while the bias correction in Algorithm 3 is a useful improvement, the paper could provide more intuition about why the bias exists and how it relates to the theoretical guarantees. A short discussion of the expected bias magnitude and its relationship to the theoretical bounds would enhance the practical understanding of the method.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of statistical testing by demonstrating how to leverage advances in supervised learning for fundamental statistical problems. The approach is not only theoretically sound but also practically useful, as evidenced by the clear performance improvements on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The problem of CI testing is important for applications in causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection, and this paper provides a valuable new tool for addressing it.

The paper satisfies all four evaluation criteria: it is technically sound with strong theoretical guarantees (Quality); clearly written with appropriate organization (Clarity); offers a novel reduction to classification with a new bootstrap procedure (Originality); and demonstrates significant practical improvements over state-of-the-art methods (Significance).

I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound approach to an important problem with clear empirical validation. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the practical implications of the work are substantial for the machine learning and statistics communities. The minor suggestions for improvement could be addressed in a revised version, but do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.
