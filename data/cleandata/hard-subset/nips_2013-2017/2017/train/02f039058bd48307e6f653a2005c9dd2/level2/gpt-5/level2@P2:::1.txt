Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test, CCIT, that reduces CI testing to binary classification by generating pseudo-samples from the conditionally independent product distribution fCI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). The key technical component is a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that, for each sample in one partition, replaces Y with the Y of its nearest neighbor in Z from a second partition. The authors prove that a single such bootstrapped sample is close in total variation (TV) to fCI under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z), with a rate O(n^{-1/dz}) (Theorem 1). Since the bootstrapped samples are not i.i.d., they further provide generalization bounds for ERM classification under a form of near-independence, yielding risk bounds involving VC dimension and terms that scale with dz (Theorem 2). A bias-corrected testing variant subtracts the performance of a classifier trained on (Y,Z) alone from one trained on (X,Y,Z). Empirically, CCIT with XGBoost outperforms kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic post-nonlinear data with high-dimensional Z and on flow cytometry protein signaling data.

This work builds on binary-classifier two-sample testing (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab for independence testing) and contrasts with kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and KCIPT (Doran et al.), which uses an expensive LP-based permutation. The proposed NN bootstrap offers a simple, scalable alternative with new theoretical guarantees for the CI-testing reduction.

Strengths:
- Conceptual simplicity and modularity: reduces CI testing to two-sample classification, enabling direct leverage of modern classifiers.
- Novel NN bootstrap to approximate fCI without solving a global matching; theoretically justified via TV bounds.
- Generalization analysis for classification under near-independence is technically interesting and broadly relevant.
- Strong empirical performance, particularly as dz increases, with practical bias correction that is easy to implement.
- Clear comparison to kernel-based CI tests and discussion of related reductions.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Assumptions and rates: The TV bound scales as O(n^{-1/dz}), reflecting a severe curse of dimensionality in Z. While the authors note better empirical behavior, the theory suggests slow convergence in high-dz regimes.
- Assumption 1 (generalized curvature of f(y|z)) is nonstandard and difficult to interpret/verify; guidance or examples where it holds would help.
- Theorem 1 controls the marginal distribution of a single bootstrapped sample; dependence across bootstrapped samples is handled in Theorem 2 with additional assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz f(z)), but the resulting bounds are complex and appear loose. A clear statement of consistency (Type I control and power→1) under explicit conditions is missing.
- Hypothesis testing calibration: The procedure uses a threshold τ (suggested τ=1/√n) but does not provide a principled way to control test level α or compute p-values. Reported results focus on ROC/AUC rather than Type I error at a fixed α.
- Empirical scope: X and Y are one-dimensional in synthetic tests; it is unclear how performance scales with dx, dy or varying noise levels. Runtime/scalability with dz and n is not quantified, and NN search in high-dz may be costly or unreliable.
- Bias-corrected variant: The intuitive subtraction L(X,Y,Z)−L(Y,Z) is compelling, but a formal guarantee (e.g., bound on the residual bias and test validity) is not given.
- Flow cytometry evaluation: The “non-CI” negatives are constructed by picking edges and random conditioning sets; without careful d-separation analysis, some negatives may accidentally be CI or nearly so, potentially confounding conclusions.

Quality:
The technical development is careful, with nontrivial proofs for TV closeness and near-i.i.d. generalization bounds. However, the testing aspect lacks a clear calibration framework. The assumptions warrant more discussion and examples, and some important practical questions (classifier choice sensitivity, hyperparameters, K-NN vs 1-NN, computational cost) are underexplored.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and organized. Pseudocode is helpful. Assumption 1 could be better motivated (connect it more transparently to Fisher information or Lipschitz properties of log f(y|z)). The implications of Theorem 2 for an actual test (power and level) should be summarized more explicitly.

Originality:
The reduction to classification per se is known for independence and two-sample tests, but the proposed NN bootstrap for CI and the near-independent generalization analysis appear novel. The bias-corrected variant is a simple but practical contribution.

Significance:
If properly calibrated, this approach can make CI testing more robust in high-dimensional, nonlinear settings by leveraging modern classifiers. The method is promising in practice, as evidenced by empirical gains, and the theoretical analysis of near-independent generalization is of independent interest.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a formal consistency theorem for the full CCIT (and CCIT-v2), including explicit conditions under which Type I error is controlled and power→1. If exact level control is hard, propose a resampling-based calibration (e.g., repeated NN bootstraps with label randomization to estimate the null distribution of the test statistic).
- Discuss and, if possible, relax or replace Assumption 1 with more interpretable conditions (e.g., Hölder smoothness of conditional densities). Include examples where the assumptions hold.
- Analyze the effect of K in K-NN bootstrapping (and/or radius neighbors) on bias-variance and TV rate; consider adaptive K.
- Report computational complexity and wall-clock runtimes versus KCIT/RCIT, and include high-dz nearest neighbor search choices (brute force vs approximate).
- Extend experiments to higher-dimensional X and Y, and include sensitivity to sample size, noise, and classifier family. An ablation comparing XGBoost vs DNNs would strengthen the “model-powered” claim.
- For the flow cytometry study, ensure that non-CI labels are validated via d-separation relative to the assumed graphs, or supplement with interventional subsets to reduce ambiguity.
- Provide guidance on setting τ, or better, replace thresholding with an estimated p-value via repeated random splits/bootstraps and label permutations that respect the constructed two-sample setting.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, practical CI testing reduction with strong empirical performance.
- Novel bootstrap construction with theoretical justification.
- Technically interesting generalization bounds under near-independence.
- Clear potential impact by leveraging advances in classification.

Cons for acceptance:
- Lack of calibrated testing procedure with controlled Type I error.
- Heavy and somewhat opaque assumptions; theoretical rates degrade with dz.
- Limited empirical analysis of X/Y dimensionality and computational costs.
- Bias-corrected variant not theoretically justified.

Overall:
A promising, well-motivated contribution with solid theory and notably strong empirical results. With improved calibration and broader empirical validation, this could be an influential approach to nonparametric CI testing.
