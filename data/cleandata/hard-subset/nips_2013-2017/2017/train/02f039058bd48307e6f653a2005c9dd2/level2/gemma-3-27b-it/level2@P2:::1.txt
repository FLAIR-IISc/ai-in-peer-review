This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables. The core idea is to convert the CI test into a binary classification problem, leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. A key challenge is generating samples from the conditional independence distribution fCI, which is addressed through a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. Theoretical guarantees are provided for both the bootstrap samples and the generalization bounds of the classifier, and the method is empirically validated on simulated and real datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, as CI testing is crucial for various statistical learning problems. The approach of framing CI testing as a classification problem is conceptually sound and aligns with the broader trend of model-driven inference.  The authors correctly identify the difficulty of generating samples from fCI and their proposed nearest neighbor bootstrap is a reasonable solution. The theoretical analysis, including the total variational distance bounds and generalization error analysis, is a significant contribution. The empirical evaluation demonstrates promising performance gains over existing methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.  Related work is adequately covered, citing relevant papers in kernel-based CI testing, permutation-based independence testing, and related theoretical analyses.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The model-powered approach to CI testing, combined with the nearest neighbor bootstrap and theoretical analysis, represents a novel contribution to the field.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the generated samples and the generalization error of the classifier, which is crucial for establishing the reliability of the method. The analysis of Rademacher complexity in a non-i.i.d setting is particularly valuable.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms existing approaches, especially in high-dimensional settings.  The application to the flow cytometry dataset demonstrates the practical utility of the algorithm.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core ideas and technical details. The inclusion of algorithms as pseudocode is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the efficiency of nearest neighbor search, the overall computational cost of the bootstrap procedure and training powerful classifiers might be significant, especially for very high-dimensional data.  This is not thoroughly discussed.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of parameters for the nearest neighbor bootstrap (e.g., number of neighbors) and the classifier could significantly impact performance. The paper doesn’t extensively explore the sensitivity of the method to these parameters.  A more detailed discussion of parameter tuning would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on smoothness assumptions on the underlying distributions (Assumptions 1 & 2). While common in such analyses, the impact of these assumptions on the practical performance of the method isn't fully investigated. How robust is the method to violations of these assumptions?
* **Algorithm 3 Complexity:** While Algorithm 3 addresses the bias correction, it introduces additional complexity by requiring training two classifiers. The improvement gained by using this more complex algorithm compared to Algorithm 2 isn’t convincingly demonstrated in the provided results. A clearer comparison of their performance is warranted.
* **Missing Baseline:**  The comparison to kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT is helpful, but including a simpler, non-parametric CI test (e.g., based on mutual information estimation) as a baseline would further highlight the benefits of the proposed approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in statistical inference.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong justification for the method's reliability.
* The empirical results demonstrate promising performance gains over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost and parameter sensitivity of the method are not fully addressed.
* The reliance on smoothness assumptions could limit the applicability of the method in some scenarios.
* The benefits of Algorithm 3 over Algorithm 2 aren’t clearly demonstrated.


**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The combination of a novel approach, strong theoretical guarantees, and promising empirical results makes it a worthy candidate for acceptance. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly concerning computational cost and parameter sensitivity, would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on providing a more detailed discussion of computational aspects, parameter tuning, and the robustness of the method to violations of the smoothness assumptions.  A more direct comparison between Algorithm 2 and Algorithm 3 would also be helpful.
