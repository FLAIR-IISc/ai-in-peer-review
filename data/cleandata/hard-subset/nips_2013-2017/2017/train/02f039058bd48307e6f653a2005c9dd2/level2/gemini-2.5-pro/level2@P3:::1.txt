### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables. The core contribution is a novel, "model-powered" approach that reduces the CI test `X ⊥ Y | Z` to a binary classification problem. The key insight is to formulate a two-sample test between the original data distribution `f(x, y, z)` and the conditionally independent distribution `f_CI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)`.

Since samples from `f_CI` are not directly available, the paper proposes a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) to generate samples that approximate it. Given a set of i.i.d. samples, a portion is set aside (labeled '1', from `f`). The remaining samples are used to generate a new dataset (labeled '0') where for each point `(x, y, z)`, its `y` value is swapped with the `y'` from a different point `(x', y', z')` whose `z'` is the nearest neighbor to `z`. The paper provides a theoretical analysis showing that the distribution of these generated samples is close to `f_CI` in total variation distance.

With these two labeled sets of samples, a powerful classifier (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, deep neural networks) is trained to distinguish between them. If the classifier achieves an accuracy significantly better than random guessing, the null hypothesis `H0: X ⊥ Y | Z` is rejected. The work provides theoretical generalization bounds for the classifier in the non-i.i.d setting induced by the bootstrap procedure. Empirically, the proposed method (CCIT) is shown to outperform state-of-the-art kernel-based methods, particularly as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable `Z` increases.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work.

*   **Theoretical Contributions**: The theoretical results are a significant strength. Theorem 1 provides guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples by bounding the total variation distance to the target distribution `f_CI`. This formally justifies the core mechanism of the proposed test. Furthermore, Theorem 2 provides a novel analysis of classifier generalization bounds for the "near-independent" samples generated by the bootstrap, adapting Rademacher complexity analysis to this setting. This is a non-trivial result of potential independent interest.
*   **Methodological Contributions**: The proposed method is well-motivated and includes a thoughtful bias-correction step (Algorithm 3). Recognizing that the bootstrap procedure might introduce a small, systematic bias even under H0, and correcting for it by training a second classifier on only the `(Y, Z)` variables, is a clever and practical improvement.
*   **Empirical Evaluation**: The experiments are well-designed, comparing against relevant state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real-world data. The synthetic experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's strength in handling higher-dimensional conditioning sets, a known challenge for many CI tests. The analysis on the flow cytometry dataset provides a compelling application to a real scientific problem.

A point for consideration is the gap between the theoretical rate of convergence in Theorem 1, which scales as `O(n^(-1/dz))`, and the excellent empirical performance in high `dz`. The theoretical bound is quite pessimistic for large `dz`. A brief discussion on why the practical performance seems to overcome this "curse of dimensionality" would be valuable. Is it because the smoothness assumptions are worst-case, or are modern classifiers particularly robust to the type of approximation error introduced by the bootstrap?

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and organized. The main idea is communicated effectively, aided by Figure 1 and clear pseudocode for the proposed algorithms. The problem setup, related work, and contributions are clearly articulated.

To further improve clarity and reproducibility, a few details could be added:
*   The selection of the threshold `τ` is a critical step in the final hypothesis test. The paper mentions using `τ = 1/sqrt(n)` in the experiments. It would be helpful to provide more justification for this choice. Is it a heuristic, or is it motivated by the expected variance of the test statistic under the null hypothesis? A brief discussion of the sensitivity to this hyperparameter would be beneficial.
*   In the experiments, the algorithm is run `B` times. It is not specified how the results from these `B` runs are aggregated to produce a single test statistic or p-value. Clarifying whether this involves averaging the accuracies, a voting scheme, or another method would improve reproducibility.
*   The description of the bias-correction algorithm (CCITv2) is clear, but specifying the type and parameterization of the classifier `ĝ0` used for bias estimation would be a helpful detail for implementation.

**Originality**

The proposed approach is highly original. While classifier-based two-sample tests have been explored for unconditional independence, their application to the conditional case is not straightforward due to the difficulty of sampling from `f_CI`. The central contribution—the nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate these samples—is a novel and effective idea. This work successfully frames a classical statistical testing problem in the language of modern supervised learning, which is a creative and promising research direction. The connection to machine learning reductions is aptly noted. The paper does an excellent job of situating the work within the context of prior kernel-based and permutation-based CI tests, clearly articulating how this approach differs.

**Significance**

The work is significant and likely to have a substantial impact.
*   It provides a new, powerful, and flexible tool for a fundamental statistical problem that is a building block for many areas in machine learning and statistics, including causal discovery, feature selection, and graphical model learning.
*   The "model-powered" philosophy is very appealing. By reducing CI testing to classification, the method can immediately leverage any advances in high-dimensional, non-linear classification. This modularity ensures that the approach will remain relevant and can be improved as classification methods evolve.
*   The strong empirical results, especially in the high-dimensional `Z` setting, suggest that this method addresses a key limitation of some existing approaches and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. Researchers and practitioners in need of a scalable, non-parametric CI test are likely to use this method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance**: The paper introduces a novel and elegant reduction of CI testing to binary classification, a significant conceptual contribution with high practical potential.
2.  **Strong Performance**: The method is shown to outperform strong, established baselines, particularly in challenging high-dimensional settings.
3.  **Solid Theory**: The approach is supported by a solid theoretical foundation, including a novel analysis of the proposed bootstrap procedure and the subsequent classifier generalization.
4.  **Flexibility**: The modular design allows practitioners to plug in state-of-the-art classifiers, making the framework powerful and future-proof.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Theory-Practice Gap**: There is a notable gap between the pessimistic theoretical convergence rates with respect to the dimension `dz` and the strong empirical performance. While not a flaw, a deeper discussion of this would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Implementation Details**: Some practical details regarding hyperparameter selection (`τ`) and experimental procedure (aggregation over bootstrap runs) could be clarified for better reproducibility.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and high-performing method for a fundamental problem, backed by both theory and experiments. The suggestions for improvement are minor and can be addressed in a revision.
