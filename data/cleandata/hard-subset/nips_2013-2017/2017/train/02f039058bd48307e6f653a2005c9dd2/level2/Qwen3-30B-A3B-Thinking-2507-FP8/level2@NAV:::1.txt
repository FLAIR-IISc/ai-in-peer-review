

This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The key innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure that generates samples from the conditional independence distribution f_CI, enabling the use of powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The authors provide theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1-2) showing the bootstrapped samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance and derive generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. Empirical results on synthetic data and flow-cytometry show significant performance gains over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT).

The paper's main strength is its novel approach to CI testing that leverages advances in classification. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap elegantly addresses a key limitation of previous permutation-based methods (KCIPT), which required solving expensive linear programs. The theoretical results are rigorous and provide a solid foundation for the method. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating clear advantages particularly in high-dimensional settings where previous methods struggle.

However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of limitations. While it notes that practical performance scales better than theoretical worst-case analysis, it doesn't adequately address potential failure cases of the nearest-neighbor approach (e.g., when the Z-distribution is highly non-uniform or clustered). A more nuanced analysis of the relationship between the theoretical bounds and practical performance would strengthen the paper.

The work is highly significant for causal inference and structure learning applications, where CI testing is fundamental. The model-powered approach opens new possibilities for leveraging advances in machine learning for statistical testing. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and properly contextualized within the literature.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that makes a significant advance in CI testing. The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the approach is both novel and practically useful. With minor improvements to the discussion of limitations, this would be an excellent paper for NIPS.
