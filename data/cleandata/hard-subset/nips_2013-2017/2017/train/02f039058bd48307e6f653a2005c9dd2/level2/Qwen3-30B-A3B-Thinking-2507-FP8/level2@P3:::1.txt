

This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach for non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The key innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure that generates samples from the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) using only samples from the true joint distribution f(x,y,z). The authors theoretically demonstrate that these generated samples are close to fCI in total variational distance, and provide generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d near-independent samples. Empirical results show significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real datasets.

The work makes several strong contributions. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) is both theoretically justified and computationally efficient, addressing a key challenge in CI testing where direct sampling from fCI is infeasible. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) is comprehensive, providing precise bounds on the distance between generated samples and fCI, as well as generalization guarantees for the classification problem. The paper clearly differentiates from related work, particularly KCIPT [10] which requires solving an expensive linear program for permutation-based sampling. The empirical validation is thorough, with results demonstrating substantial performance gains over KCIT [32] and RCIT [28] in high-dimensional settings (Fig. 2a), and successful application to protein signaling network analysis (Fig. 2b, Table 2c).

The paper excels in quality with technically sound theoretical foundations and well-designed experiments. The clarity is excellent, with a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm design to theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The presentation of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is particularly clear, and the connection between CI testing and classification is well-motivated. The significance of the work is high, as CI testing is a fundamental problem with applications in causal inference and Bayesian networks, and the model-powered approach opens new possibilities for leveraging advances in supervised learning for statistical testing.

Some minor weaknesses could be addressed. The theoretical analysis in Theorem 1 shows a rate of O(1/n^(1/dz)), which may become slow in high dimensions. While the paper acknowledges this, it would strengthen the argument to better explain why the method performs well in practice despite this theoretical limitation. The bias correction in Algorithm 3 is somewhat ad-hoc; a more rigorous justification for this step would be valuable. The empirical section could include more discussion about the sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters, as the paper currently uses XGBoost without exploring alternatives.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with appropriate references to related work including KCIT, RCIT, and KCIPT. The contributions are clearly distinguished from previous work, and the theoretical novelty in analyzing classification under near-independent samples is particularly valuable. The method's flexibility to use different classifiers (e.g., gradient boosted trees or neural networks) is a significant practical advantage.

The empirical results are convincing, with clear performance gains across multiple datasets and problem settings. The flow cytometry application demonstrates practical utility, though the paper could further elaborate on how this method might contribute to causal discovery beyond merely verifying existing causal graphs.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of statistical testing. The model-powered approach is innovative and well-executed, with both theoretical rigor and empirical validation. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is a key technical advance that addresses a fundamental challenge in CI testing. The paper's clear presentation, thorough theoretical analysis, and significant performance improvements over prior work make it a compelling contribution to NIPS.

The work advances the state of the art in CI testing, particularly in high-dimensional settings where previous methods struggle. The connection between CI testing and classification opens new avenues for leveraging advances in supervised learning for statistical testing problems. The paper's contributions are significant enough to warrant acceptance, as it provides both theoretical foundations and practical tools that will be valuable to researchers working on statistical inference and causal discovery.
