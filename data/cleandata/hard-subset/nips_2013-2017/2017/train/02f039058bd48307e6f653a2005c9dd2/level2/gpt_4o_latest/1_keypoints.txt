- Reframes non-parametric conditional independence testing as a binary classification problem using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate the conditional product distribution.
- Utilizes powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, deep neural networks) to handle high-dimensional continuous data.
- Provides theoretical guarantees including total variation bounds between generated and true conditional product distributions and generalization bounds for classifiers on near-independent samples.
- Empirical results outperform kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic and real-world datasets, including protein signaling networks.
- Novelty: introduces a fresh, practical reduction of CI testing to supervised learning, diverging from traditional kernel methods.
- Theoretical contribution: rigorous analysis of bootstrap accuracy and classifier generalization.
- Empirical validation: strong experimental evidence of superiority over state-of-the-art methods.
- Scalability: method shows robustness in high-dimensional settings where traditional methods struggle.
- Flexibility: modular design permits use of any classifier, adaptable to domain-specific needs.
- Computational complexity: reliance on powerful classifiers may introduce significant overhead for large datasets.
- Bias correction: Algorithm 3’s bias correction is theoretically justified but lacks extensive empirical robustness checks.
- Limited discussion of limitations: sensitivity to hyperparameters and the effect of classifier choice are not thoroughly explored.
- Comparative analysis gap: more detailed comparisons on computational efficiency and scalability versus kernel methods are needed.
- Pros for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem with a novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated approach that bridges CI testing and supervised learning.
- Cons against acceptance: potential computational costs and insufficient sensitivity/limitations analysis reduce completeness.
- Recommendation: accept, with authors urged to discuss limitations and provide a more detailed computational analysis.
