This paper addresses the problem of non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing it as a classification problem and leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The key technical contribution lies in a novel nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution fCI. Theoretical results on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for the classification task are provided, along with empirical validation on simulated and real datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, as CI testing is a fundamental problem with broad applications in areas like Bayesian network discovery and causal inference. The reduction to a classification problem is a natural and promising approach, particularly given the advances in machine learning. The nearest neighbor bootstrap method appears to be a clever way to address the challenge of obtaining samples from fCI, given only samples from the joint distribution.  The theoretical analysis, including the total variational distance bound (Theorem 1) and the generalization bounds (Theorem 2), is a significant contribution, demonstrating a rigorous understanding of the method’s properties. The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm, showing performance gains over existing methods.

A strength of this work is the thoroughness of the theoretical analysis. The authors carefully consider the non-i.i.d nature of the bootstrapped samples and derive generalization bounds accordingly. This is a crucial aspect, as standard generalization bounds do not apply directly in this setting. The connection to Rademacher complexity is also valuable. The experimental evaluation, including both synthetic and real-world datasets, strengthens the claims made by the authors. The comparison to existing methods (KCIT and RCIT) provides a good benchmark for assessing the performance of the proposed algorithm. The inclusion of Algorithm 3, addressing the bias correction, demonstrates a thoughtful refinement of the initial approach.

However, there are areas where the paper could be improved. While the theoretical results are comprehensive, the assumptions required for them to hold (specifically, the smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z)) might be strong. A discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to violations of these assumptions would be beneficial.  The empirical results section could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the scaling behavior of the algorithm with respect to the dimensionality of Z.  While the paper mentions that performance scales well in practice, a more systematic investigation of this aspect would be valuable. It would be helpful to show the computational costs of each algorithm for a range of problem sizes (i.e. dataset sizes and dimension of Z), to better assess practical applicability. Furthermore, while the choice of XGBoost is reasonable, a brief justification for its selection, or perhaps a comparison with other classifiers, would strengthen the argument. The related work section is extensive but could perhaps be more focused, prioritizing the most closely related papers and providing a more critical assessment of their limitations.  The notation, while generally clear, could be made more consistent throughout the paper.  For example, the use of different symbols for distributions (f, q) could be clarified.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to CI testing leveraging the power of modern classification techniques.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous analysis of the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds.
*   Empirically validated performance gains over existing methods on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   Addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference with broad applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong assumptions on the smoothness of the underlying distributions may limit the applicability of the theoretical results.
*   The computational cost and scaling behavior of the algorithm could be a concern for high-dimensional problems.
*   Some minor issues with notation and clarity could be addressed.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of CI testing. The combination of theoretical rigor and empirical validation is particularly impressive. While there are some areas for improvement, they are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. This reviewer recommends acceptance.
