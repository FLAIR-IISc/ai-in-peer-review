

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem, leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and neural networks. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI, which they theoretically show are close to f_CI in total variation distance. They then provide generalization bounds for classification under the non-i.i.d samples generated by their bootstrap procedure.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it successfully converts CI testing into a classification problem, allowing the use of advanced classifiers that handle high-dimensional data well. Second, the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is elegant and efficient compared to previous methods like KCIPT, which requires solving an expensive linear program. Third, the theoretical guarantees in Theorem 1 (showing d_TV(, f_CI) = O(1/n^{1/d_z})) and Theorem 2 (generalization bounds under near-independent samples) are well-motivated and carefully developed. Finally, the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) in both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and detailed pseudocode. The theoretical results are properly contextualized within the literature on non-parametric CI testing, and the authors appropriately cite relevant prior work, particularly comparing their approach to kernel-based methods and previous classification-based approaches.

Strengths include the novel reduction of CI testing to classification, the elegant bootstrap procedure, and the strong empirical performance. The authors effectively demonstrate that their method scales better with dimensionality than previous approaches, even though their theoretical guarantees suggest a curse of dimensionality (d_TV bound depends on d_z).

However, there are several areas for improvement. First, the paper claims that "performance with respect to dimension of Z scales much better than expected from our worst case theoretical analysis" but doesn't adequately explain this discrepancy. The authors should investigate why their method outperforms theoretical expectations in practice, as this would strengthen their argument and provide valuable insights for future work.

Second, while the bias correction in Algorithm 3 is empirically effective, the paper lacks theoretical justification for this procedure. The authors should either provide theoretical guarantees for the bias correction or acknowledge it as an empirical improvement without theoretical backing.

Third, the paper doesn't sufficiently address the computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. For large datasets, finding nearest neighbors in high dimensions can become computationally expensive, which might limit the practical applicability of the method. The authors should discuss this limitation and potentially suggest approximations for large-scale applications.

Fourth, while the paper compares with KCIT and RCIT, it doesn't compare with the most recent methods in the field, such as the "Approximate kernel-based conditional independence test" by Strobl et al. (2017), which is directly relevant to their approach. Including this comparison would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

The paper's significance is high, as CI testing is fundamental to causal inference, Bayesian network structure learning, and feature selection. By providing an efficient, scalable method that leverages advances in classification, the authors enable practitioners to apply powerful machine learning tools to a central statistical problem.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper with novel contributions that advance the state of the art in CI testing. The theoretical foundations are solid, the empirical results are compelling, and the approach is well-motivated. With some additional theoretical analysis of the bias correction, a more detailed discussion of computational complexity, and comparisons with the most recent methods, this paper would be even stronger. The paper represents a significant contribution to the field and would be valuable for researchers working on causal inference and statistical testing. I recommend acceptance.
