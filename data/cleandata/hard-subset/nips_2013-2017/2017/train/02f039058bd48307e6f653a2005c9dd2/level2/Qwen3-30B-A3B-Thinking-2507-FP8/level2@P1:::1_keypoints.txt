- Proposes a novel reduction of conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables to binary classification using powerful classifiers.
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap that generates samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).
- Theorem 1: shows the bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance.
- Theorem 2: provides generalization bounds for classification under near-i.i.d. samples, yielding CI testing error bounds.
- Empirical results: outperforms kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic benchmarks and a protein signaling dataset.
- Main contribution: a theoretically grounded, model-powered CI test that scales better to high-dimensional Z using classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and neural nets.
- Nearest-neighbor bootstrap is simpler and more theoretically justified than prior permutation-based approaches (e.g., KCIPT).
- Novel theoretical analysis of generalization under near-independence is of independent interest to ML theory.
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized with helpful pseudocode (Algorithms 1–3) and contextualized related work.
- Experimental setup is thorough, including dimensionality-scaling synthetic experiments and a real-world flow-cytometry application.
- Strengths: strong theoretical guarantees, practical empirical validation, and clear insight on leveraging supervised models for statistical testing.
- Demonstrated better scaling with dimensionality than existing methods, important for modern high-dimensional datasets.
- Minor weakness: computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap and scalability of nearest-neighbor search should be discussed more explicitly.
- Minor weakness: bias correction (Algorithm 3) needs more intuition on why the bias arises and its expected magnitude relative to theoretical bounds.
- Overall significance: makes a substantial contribution to statistical testing with applications in causal inference, Bayesian networks, and feature selection.
- Recommendation: accept; minor suggested clarifications can be addressed in revision but do not undermine the paper’s quality.
