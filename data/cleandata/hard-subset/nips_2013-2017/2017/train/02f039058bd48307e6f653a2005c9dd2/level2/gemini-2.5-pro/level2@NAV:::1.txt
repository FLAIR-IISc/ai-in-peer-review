This paper proposes a novel method for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning. The core idea is to reduce the CI test to a binary classification problem. The authors frame the task as distinguishing between samples from the true joint distribution f(x, y, z) and samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). The main technical contribution is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples that approximate f_CI, which is non-trivial as one only has access to samples from the true joint distribution. This approach cleverly positions the CI test to leverage the power of modern high-dimensional classifiers like gradient-boosted trees. The work builds upon the recent trend of using classifiers for two-sample testing but extends it from simple independence to the more complex conditional independence setting, providing a compelling alternative to established kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The reduction of CI testing to classification via the novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap is an elegant and powerful contribution. It allows the field to harness rapid advancements in supervised learning for a classical statistical problem, which could have a significant impact on areas like causal discovery and feature selection.
*   **Technical Quality:** The claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1) and, impressively, derive novel generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the resulting non-i.i.d data (Theorem 2). This theoretical rigor is a major strength.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experiments are thorough and demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over state-of-the-art methods, especially as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases. The application to the flow cytometry dataset showcases the method's practical utility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Theory-Practice Gap:** The theoretical bounds show a strong dependence on the dimension of Z (d_z), suggesting a curse of dimensionality. However, the empirical results are much stronger. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of this gap. Why do modern classifiers like XGBoost seem to mitigate this theoretical limitation so effectively in practice?
*   **Threshold Selection:** The performance of the proposed test depends on a threshold parameter τ. While a heuristic is suggested, a more principled approach for setting this parameter, or an analysis of the test's sensitivity to it, would strengthen the paper's practical guidance for users.
*   **Clarity on Bias Correction:** The bias-corrected algorithm (CCITv2) is an important practical improvement. It would be helpful to more explicitly connect the need for this correction to the theoretical bounds on the bootstrapped sample distribution (Theorem 1), clarifying how small deviations from f_CI can create a discernible signal even under the null hypothesis.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a novel idea, strong theoretical foundations, and compelling empirical results. The proposed method represents a significant advance in CI testing.
