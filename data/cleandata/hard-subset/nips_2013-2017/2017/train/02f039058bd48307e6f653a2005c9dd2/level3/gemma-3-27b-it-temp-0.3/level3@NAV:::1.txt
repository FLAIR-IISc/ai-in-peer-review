## Review of “Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test”

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing it as a classification problem. The core idea is to generate samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution *f*<sup>CI</sup> using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, and then train a classifier to distinguish these bootstrapped samples from the original data. If the classifier performs no better than random, the null hypothesis (CI) is not rejected. The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (total variational distance) and generalization bounds for the classifier, and demonstrates empirical improvements over existing methods on both simulated and real datasets.

The approach is well-motivated, addressing a crucial problem in areas like causal inference and Bayesian network learning. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the methodology accessible. The theoretical contributions, particularly the bounds on the total variation distance and the generalization analysis in a non-i.i.d setting, are valuable. The empirical validation provides evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing gains over state-of-the-art techniques like KCIT and RCIT. 

However, several points require further consideration. **(Major 1)** The paper doesn’t thoroughly explore alternatives to the nearest-neighbor bootstrap. Given the success of permutation-based generation in independence testing [7], and the existence of kernel two-sample tests, a comparative analysis of these approaches within the proposed classification framework is crucial. **(Major 2)** The method's ability to detect *weak* conditional dependence is unclear. If the NN-bootstrapped distribution is already close to *f*<sup>CI</sup> in finite samples, how does the classifier reliably discern a deviation indicating dependence? **(Major 3)** The symmetry (or lack thereof) of the method with respect to *x* and *y* is not addressed. In causal settings, the directionality (*x* → *y* vs. *y* → *x*) matters. How does the algorithm handle this asymmetry when deciding which variable to sample during bootstrap? **(Major 4)** The choice of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is a significant concern. The paper lacks a clear justification or guidance on how to select an appropriate value for τ, which could significantly impact performance. 

**(Minor)** The empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by dividing by the sample size, a standard practice in risk estimation.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper tackles an important problem with a novel and theoretically grounded approach. The empirical results are promising, and the clarity of presentation is commendable.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The lack of comparative analysis with alternative generation methods, the unclear handling of weak dependence and causal asymmetry, and the ambiguity surrounding the parameter τ raise significant concerns.



**Overall Assessment:**  I believe the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution, but the major concerns outlined above need to be addressed before acceptance. I recommend a rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit, focusing on the points raised in this review.
