This paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing the problem as a binary classification task. This is a significant contribution, as it leverages the power of modern classification techniques – such as gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks – to tackle a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning with applications in causal inference, Bayesian network discovery, and feature selection. The core idea is elegant: generate samples approximating the conditional product distribution (necessary if X and Y are conditionally independent given Z), and then train a classifier to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution and those from the approximated conditionally independent distribution. 

The method builds on recent work that converts statistical tests into classification problems, notably "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests." However, a key challenge in CI testing is generating these samples under the null hypothesis, which this paper addresses with a novel nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure.  Crucially, the authors provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating that the generated samples are close to the desired conditional product distribution in terms of total variational distance. They further develop generalization bounds for classification in the non-i.i.d setting induced by the bootstrap procedure, translating these bounds into error bounds for the CI test itself. This rigorous theoretical analysis is a major strength of the paper.

The authors empirically validate their method on both simulated and real datasets, demonstrating performance gains over existing state-of-the-art CI tests like KCIT and RCIT. The application to flow cytometry data further illustrates the practical utility of the approach for analyzing complex biological systems. The scalability shown in the empirical results is encouraging, particularly as the dimension of Z increases. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem and the proposed nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure represent a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees on the quality of the generated samples and the generalization performance of the classifier, which is commendable.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The results on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Practical Relevance:** The method has clear applications in important areas like causal inference and network discovery.
* **Well-Connected to Literature:** The authors demonstrate awareness of related work and clearly position their contribution within the existing landscape.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Algorithm 2 Clarity:** Algorithm 2, describing the CI testing process, could be presented more concisely. The steps feel somewhat verbose, and streamlining could improve readability.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not extensively explore the sensitivity of the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters. While XGBoost is used in the experiments, it’s unclear how different classifiers or hyperparameter settings might influence the results and potentially alter the acceptance/rejection decisions. 
* **Section 1.1 Redundancy:** Section 1.1 repeats information already present in the introduction, making it somewhat redundant.
* **Minor Typos:** A few minor typos were observed ("classifier" on line 157 and “graphs” on line 280), but these are easily corrected.
* **Discrete Variable Extension:**  The authors do not discuss the potential for extending the method to discrete variables, nor whether the bootstrap procedure might become more powerful when Z is discrete, which would be a valuable future direction.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel and theoretically well-grounded approach to CI testing.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method compared to existing techniques.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the performance of the algorithm.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact research in areas like causal inference and network discovery.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a detailed analysis of the sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters is a minor concern.
* The redundancy in section 1.1 and the minor typos detract slightly from the overall polish of the paper.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The novel approach, rigorous theoretical analysis, and compelling empirical results outweigh the minor weaknesses. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity of Algorithm 2, the redundancy in Section 1.1, and the identified typos.  Further discussion regarding the sensitivity to classifier choice would also be beneficial, potentially as a direction for future work.
