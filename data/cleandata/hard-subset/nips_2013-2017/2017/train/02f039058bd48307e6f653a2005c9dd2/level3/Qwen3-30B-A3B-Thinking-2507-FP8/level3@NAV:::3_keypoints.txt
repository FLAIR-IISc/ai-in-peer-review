- Proposes a CI test by reducing conditional independence to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).
- Theorem 1: proves bootstrap samples are close to the conditional product distribution in total variation distance.
- Theorem 2: establishes generalization bounds for classifiers trained on near-independent (bootstrap) samples.
- Extends classifier two-sample tests (Lopez-Paz & Oquab) to the conditional setting.
- Empirically outperforms kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic and flow-cytometry datasets.
- Shows good scalability with the dimensionality of Z, which is valuable for causal inference.
- Paper is well-structured and accessible to practitioners, though some theoretical proofs lack transparency.
- Major strengths: novel theoretical contributions, strong empirical validation, clear practical utility for causal discovery.
- Presentation weaknesses: Section 1.1 is redundant and Algorithm 2 needs a more succinct description.
- Reproducibility concern: sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters is not thoroughly explored.
- Missing discussion on whether a discrete-variable analog exists, an important extension.
- Minor issues: typos (e.g., "classifier" on line 157) and other small edits needed; overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
