This paper addresses the problem of non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing it as a classification problem and leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The core contribution lies in a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution, coupled with theoretical guarantees on the total variational distance between the generated and true distributions. The work builds upon a growing body of research utilizing model-powered approaches to statistical inference, notably related to independence testing [19] and kernel-based CI tests [32, 28, 10]. It distinguishes itself by avoiding expensive linear programs (as in [10]) and providing theoretical backing for the bootstrap procedure’s accuracy.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of reducing CI testing to classification, the specific nearest-neighbor bootstrap algorithm, and the accompanying theoretical analysis (particularly Theorem 1) represent a significant contribution. The bound on the total variational distance and the generalization bounds for classification under near-independence are insightful and non-trivial.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a substantial theoretical foundation, demonstrating a clear effort to justify the proposed method. The attempt to derive generalization bounds in a non-i.i.d setting is particularly commendable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate performance gains over existing methods on both synthetic and real-world data, suggesting practical applicability. The flow cytometry data analysis is a good demonstration of the method’s potential.
* **Potential Significance:** Successfully bridging CI testing with the advances in modern classification offers a promising avenue for improved performance, especially in high-dimensional settings.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is dense and challenging to follow. The presentation often lacks intuitive explanation, making it difficult to grasp the core ideas without considerable effort. The numerous definitions and theorems contribute to this complexity.
* **Lack of Intuition & Discussion:** While theoretically sound, the paper lacks in-depth discussion of the implications of its results. The connection between the small error probability in 1.1(iii) and the classifier’s ability to distinguish f and f^CI when G is limited is not adequately addressed. The example with r0=0.1 highlighted in the prompt raises a valid concern – if the classifier class is insufficiently rich, how can a small error probability arise? This point needs elaboration.
* **Algorithm 3 – Incremental Benefit:** The introduction of Algorithm 3 with bias correction feels somewhat tacked on. While theoretically motivated, the practical benefit isn't clearly demonstrated; the empirical results don't explicitly show the improvement achieved by using Algorithm 3 versus Algorithm 2.
* **Limited Comparison:** The empirical comparison primarily focuses on KCIT and RCIT. Expanding the comparison to more recent and relevant methods would strengthen the evaluation.



**Detailed Comments:**

* **Theorem 1:** The assumptions (1) and (2) regarding smoothness are substantial. A discussion of how sensitive the performance is to violations of these assumptions would be helpful. It would also be valuable to provide some intuition for what these conditions imply about the underlying data distributions.
* **Section 1.1(iii):** This section is crucial but difficult to understand. A more intuitive explanation of how the generalization bounds translate into error bounds for CI testing would be highly beneficial. The relationship between the VC dimension (V) and the complexity of the classifier class needs to be made clearer.
* **Algorithm 2 & 3:** The notation is sometimes inconsistent and the steps could be broken down further for improved clarity.
* **Empirical Results:**  The synthetic experiments would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the parameter settings and their impact on performance. Providing confidence intervals for the ROC AUC scores would also be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to CI testing.
* The combination of theoretical analysis and empirical validation is strong.
* The potential for significant impact on the field is high.
* The authors address a challenging problem with a creative solution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper's readability is a significant concern.
* The lack of intuitive explanation hinders understanding of the core concepts.
* The practical benefit of Algorithm 3 is not convincingly demonstrated.
* The comparison with existing methods is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of non-parametric CI testing. The technical soundness and novelty of the approach are commendable. However, the lack of clarity and intuitive explanation significantly detract from its accessibility. Addressing these issues through a more pedagogical presentation and a more thorough discussion of the results would substantially strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should prioritize improving the clarity and readability of the paper, providing more intuitive explanations of the key concepts, and elaborating on the practical implications of their theoretical results. A more detailed comparison with a wider range of existing methods would also be beneficial.
