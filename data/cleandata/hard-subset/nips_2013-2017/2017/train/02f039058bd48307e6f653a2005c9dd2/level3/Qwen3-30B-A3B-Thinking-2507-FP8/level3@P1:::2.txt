

This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditional independence distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), which is the key technical challenge in the problem. The main idea is to use these generated samples (labeled 0) alongside original samples (labeled 1) as training data for classifiers, with rejection of H0 when the classifier achieves significantly better than random accuracy. The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing that their bootstrap procedure produces samples close to f_CI in total variational distance under smoothness assumptions, and derive generalization bounds for classification with near-independent samples.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a theoretically grounded approach to generating samples from f_CI, which is a critical step missing in prior work. The authors prove Theorem 1 showing that the variational distance between the bootstrap samples and f_CI is O(1/n^(1/dz)) under appropriate smoothness assumptions, which is a novel theoretical contribution. Second, they develop a novel analysis of Rademacher complexity bounds for classification under non-i.i.d near-independent samples (Theorem 2), which is of independent interest beyond CI testing. Third, the empirical results demonstrate superior performance over state-of-the-art methods like KCIT and RCIT, especially in high-dimensional settings where previous methods struggle.

The paper's main strength lies in its technical novelty and strong theoretical foundation. The smoothness assumptions made in Theorem 1 are well-motivated and enable the authors to establish meaningful bounds on the quality of the bootstrap samples. The connection between the classification error and the total variational distance between distributions (Theorem 2) provides a clean theoretical framework for understanding the CI test's behavior. The empirical results are thorough and demonstrate clear practical advantages, particularly in high-dimensional settings where the authors' method outperforms competitors by significant margins.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses in clarity and exposition. The theoretical results are dense and difficult to follow, with insufficient intuitive explanations to help readers grasp the key insights. The presentation of the main contributions (Section 1.1) would benefit from more concrete examples or simpler explanations of why the nearest-neighbor bootstrap works. The connection between the theoretical bounds and the practical performance of the algorithm is not sufficiently explained.

The most significant concern relates to the interpretation of the classifier error bounds. The authors claim that when f ≈ f_CI (H0 holds), the optimal classifier's error rate should be close to 0.5, but they provide limited intuition for why this should hold when the classifier class G is too simple to distinguish between distributions that are close in total variational distance. For instance, if r0 = 0.1 (meaning the Bayes optimal error rate is 0.4), the bound in Theorem 2(i) suggests that the classifier's error could be as high as 0.4 + b(n) + ε, which might still be significantly different from 0.5. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the classifier class G should be chosen to ensure that the error rate is close to 0.5 under H0, which is critical for the test's validity.

The paper is also somewhat unclear about how the bias correction in Algorithm 3 works in practice, and the connection between the theoretical bounds and the choice of threshold τ is not fully explained. While the authors note that "powerful binary classifiers perform well in high-dimensions," they don't provide sufficient guidance on which classifiers to use or how to tune them for optimal CI testing performance.

In terms of significance, the work represents an important step toward leveraging modern machine learning tools for statistical testing. The model-powered approach is generalizable to other statistical testing problems and could inspire similar reductions in other domains. The empirical results on the flow-cytometry dataset demonstrate the method's practical utility in a real-world causal inference setting.

Strengths: Strong theoretical foundation, novel bootstrap procedure, clear empirical advantages over state-of-the-art methods, significant practical implications for high-dimensional CI testing.

Weaknesses: Poorly explained theoretical results, unclear connection between theory and practice, insufficient guidance on classifier selection, and unresolved concerns about the interpretation of the error bounds under H0.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the CI testing problem with strong empirical validation. While the theoretical presentation could be significantly improved for clarity, the core ideas and results are sound and impactful. The authors should revise the paper to better explain their theoretical results and address the concerns about the classifier error interpretation. With these improvements, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
