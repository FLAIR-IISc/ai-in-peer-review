- Proposes reducing continuous-variable conditional independence testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap that swaps Y across samples with similar Z to create a “product” class.  
- Bootstrap yields samples provably close in total variation to f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Uses a classifier (e.g., XGBoost or DNN) trained to distinguish joint vs. product samples as the test statistic.  
- Introduces a bias-corrected variant comparing classifiers trained with and without X to improve test behavior.  
- Theoretical guarantee: TV-distance error for the bootstrap scales as n^(−1/dz) under smoothness assumptions.  
- Provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independent, non-i.i.d. samples via VC/Rademacher analysis.  
- Empirically outperforms kernel CI baselines (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic and flow-cytometry datasets.  
- Connects clearly to classifier two-sample tests (C2ST) and permutation-based conditional tests (e.g., KCIPT), offering a simpler bootstrap and stronger conditional guarantees.  
- Quality: technically sound with nontrivial proofs and comprehensive experiments; near-i.i.d. generalization analysis is a notable strength.  
- Limitation: limited sensitivity/robustness analyses—results may depend on classifier choice, capacity, regularization, hyperparameters, and thresholding.  
- Limitation: size control and calibration (p-values, Type I error control) need more thorough empirical and method-level treatment.  
- Clarity: generally well-written and accessible, but proofs are dense, Section 1.1 is repetitive, and Algorithm 2 could be more concise.  
- Minor typos noted (e.g., “classifier” line 157, “graphs” line 280).  
- Originality: novel extension of C2ST to the conditional setting with a new nearest-neighbor bootstrap and non-i.i.d. generalization bounds.  
- Significance: high—CI testing is central to causal discovery and the method is practical, scalable, and likely to be adopted.  
- Suggested follow-ups: discrete-variable version, robustness ablations and recommended defaults, empirical Type I error reporting and calibration (cross-fitting, conformal or within-Z permutations), and discussion of high-dimensional Z (kd-tree/ANN complexity, metric learning to mitigate curse of dimensionality).
