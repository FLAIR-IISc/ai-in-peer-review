- Novel CI testing method: reframes conditional independence testing as a binary classification problem using a model-powered approach.
- Nearest-neighbor bootstrap: proposes a bootstrap procedure to generate conditionally independent-like samples and proves bounds on total variation distance to the true null.
- Generalization analysis: develops generalization bounds for the classification task under non-i.i.d., near-independent samples.
- Main theoretical contributions: new smoothness assumption on the conditional density, TV-distance bound between null and bootstrap density, and a theorem combining smoothness, classifier achievability, and error gap.
- Empirical performance: shows the proposed algorithm outperforms state-of-the-art methods on synthetic and real-world datasets.
- Strength — novelty: insightful reduction of CI testing to a classification framework.
- Strength — theory: thorough theoretical analysis including TV and generalization bounds.
- Strength — experiments: strong empirical results demonstrating effectiveness across datasets.
- Weakness — readability: paper is hard to read and provides little interpretation or intuition for results.
- Weakness — assumptions: smoothness assumption on the conditional density may be restrictive and not generally valid.
- Weakness — empirical comparisons: needs more comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods and deeper result analysis.
- Originality: introduces a new nearest-neighbor bootstrap and a classification-based CI testing paradigm.
- Originality: provides theoretical guarantees (TV bounds, non-i.i.d. generalization) not commonly seen in CI testing work.
- Significance: addresses an important problem (CI testing) with potential applications in causal inference and feature selection.
- Significance: approach could be widely applicable if assumptions hold and practicalities are addressed.
- Quality: technically sound, with rigorous theory and empirical validation; overall well-organized and logically structured.
- Clarity: conflicting assessments — technically clear in structure but practically hard to read, lacking intuition, figures, and explanatory discussion.
- Argument for acceptance: novel, theoretically-grounded method with strong empirical evidence addressing a significant problem.
- Argument against acceptance: readability issues, potentially restrictive assumptions, and insufficient benchmarking/analysis.
