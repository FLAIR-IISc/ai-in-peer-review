

This paper presents a novel model-powered approach for conditional independence (CI) testing that converts the problem into a binary classification task, leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The authors propose a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditional product distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), which is necessary for testing whether X ⊥ Y | Z. The key insight is that the problem reduces to a two-sample test between the original samples and the bootstrapped samples, which can be effectively solved using binary classifiers.

The main technical contributions are threefold: (i) a theoretical guarantee showing that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap produces samples close to f_CI in total variation distance (Theorem 1), (ii) generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d near-independent samples (Theorem 2), and (iii) extensive empirical validation on synthetic and real datasets. The authors demonstrate significant performance gains over state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) in high-dimensional settings, particularly when the dimension of Z increases.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The approach is conceptually elegant and practically useful for causal inference tasks, where CI testing is fundamental for structure learning and unconfoundedness verification. The theoretical guarantees are impressive, extending the classifier-based two-sample testing framework to the conditional setting with rigorous analysis. The empirical results on both synthetic data (with varying dimensions of Z) and the flow cytometry protein signaling network dataset demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed method, especially in high-dimensional scenarios where previous methods struggle. The authors effectively connect their work to prior literature, particularly the "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" paper, and provide clear implementation details.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The theoretical proofs are dense and difficult to follow for readers without strong statistical learning backgrounds, which may limit accessibility. The presentation could be improved by streamlining redundant sections (like the repetitive Section 1.1) and providing a more concise description of Algorithm 2. The authors acknowledge these limitations but don't sufficiently address how to make the theoretical contributions more accessible.

Two important questions arise from the paper: (1) Is there a discrete-variable analog of this approach, and would the bootstrap procedure become more powerful when Z is discrete? (2) How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and hyperparameters? The paper uses XGBoost as the classifier but doesn't explore how different classifiers or hyperparameter choices might affect the results, which could be critical for practitioners.

The authors note minor typos ("classifier" in line 157, "graphs" in line 280) but these are easily fixable. The paper is well-written overall and accessible to practitioners, though the theoretical sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with strong theoretical guarantees and thorough empirical validation. The methodology is complete and carefully evaluated, with appropriate attention to both strengths and weaknesses. The clarity is generally good, though the theoretical sections could be improved. The originality is high, as it extends classifier-based two-sample testing to the conditional setting in a non-trivial way that addresses a significant limitation of prior work. The significance is substantial, as CI testing is a fundamental problem in causal inference and structure learning, with clear practical applications.

The paper advances the state of the art in CI testing by providing a method that scales better to high-dimensional settings than existing approaches, while leveraging the power of modern supervised learning tools. This is particularly valuable for real-world applications where high-dimensional conditioning sets are common.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field that addresses a fundamental problem with practical relevance. The model-powered approach represents a promising direction for future work in statistical testing. While the theoretical exposition could be more accessible, the empirical results and methodological innovation are compelling. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound, clearly written (with room for improvement in theoretical exposition), highly original, and of significant importance to the field.

Pro: Novel conversion of CI testing to classification problem, strong theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap procedure, excellent empirical performance especially in high dimensions, practical utility for causal inference.
Con: Dense theoretical proofs, some redundant presentation, insufficient exploration of classifier sensitivity, no discussion of discrete-variable extension.

The paper should be accepted as it makes a significant contribution to a core problem in statistical learning with clear practical value.
