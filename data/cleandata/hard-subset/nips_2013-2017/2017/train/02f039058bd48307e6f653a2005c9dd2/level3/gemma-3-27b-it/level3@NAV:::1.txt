## Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing the problem as a binary classification task. The core idea is to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution *fCI* using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap and then train a classifier to distinguish these from samples from the true joint distribution. The method is theoretically grounded with bounds on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and *fCI*, and empirical risk bounds for the classifier. 

The paper addresses a significant problem in causal inference and related fields. The motivation for moving beyond kernel-based methods, particularly for high-dimensional data, is well-articulated. The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized, making the paper easy to follow. The authors appropriately reference related work, including recent advances in CI testing using permutation-based techniques [19] and kernel methods [32, 28]. The theoretical contributions, particularly the bounds on the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1), are valuable.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. **(Major 1)** The authors don’t sufficiently justify their choice of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap for generating *fCI* samples. A detailed comparison to alternative generation methods like those based on permutation (e.g., [7, 10]) or kernel two-sample tests would strengthen the paper. It’s unclear why the proposed approach is preferred, beyond its relative simplicity. **(Major 2)** Detecting weak conditional dependence presents a challenge. If the bootstrapped distribution already closely approximates *fCI*, how does the method reliably detect even small deviations indicative of dependence? This is not discussed. **(Major 3)** The method’s symmetry is questionable. The choice of which variable to sample from during bootstrap (X or Y) implicitly assumes a directionality that may not be justified. This is particularly relevant in causal inference where distinguishing *x→y* from *y→x* is crucial. **(Major 4)** The parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is critical, yet its justification and sensitivity analysis are lacking. A more rigorous discussion of how to select this parameter, potentially through cross-validation or theoretical guidelines, is necessary.

**(Minor)** The empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by dividing by the sample size; otherwise, the loss will scale with the dataset size. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper addresses an important problem, presents a novel approach, and provides theoretical guarantees. The empirical results are promising.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The lack of comparison with alternative generation methods, the unclear handling of weak conditional dependence, the lack of discussion on symmetry/causal asymmetry, and the unexplained parameter τ are significant concerns.

**Overall:** While the paper presents a promising approach, the concerns raised above need to be adequately addressed before it can be considered for acceptance. I recommend a rejection with an invitation to resubmit after addressing these points.
