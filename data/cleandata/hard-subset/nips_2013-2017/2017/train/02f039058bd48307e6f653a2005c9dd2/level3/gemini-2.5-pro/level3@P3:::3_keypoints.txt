- Core idea: Reduce CI testing to binary classification by comparing true joint samples to nearest-neighbor bootstrap samples approximating f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).
- Nearest-neighbor bootstrap: For each (x_i,y_i,z_i), find a close z_j and form (x_i,y_j,z_i) to generate product-like samples.
- Test statistic: Classification error of a powerful classifier; error ≈0.5 accepts H₀, significantly lower rejects H₀.
- Strength — Practicality: CCIT is scalable and leverages modern supervised models to handle high-dimensional conditioning sets.
- Strength — Theory: Theorems show generated samples are close in total variation and provide Rademacher-complexity bounds for learning on the near-i.i.d. data.
- Strength — Empirics: Strong empirical gains over kernel methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially as dim(Z) grows, and success on flow cytometry data.
- Strength — Clarity: Well-written, intuitive presentation and modular pipeline easy to implement.
- Weakness — Hyperparameter sensitivity: Test outcome may depend on classifier choice, architecture, and tuning; paper lacks analysis of this sensitivity.
- Weakness — Presentation: Redundant material in Sec.1.1 and verbosity in Algorithm 2 description.
- Weakness — Typos: Minor errors (e.g., "class of function" → "class of functions"; "The three graph are" → "The three graphs are").
- Questions: How sensitive are results to classifier/hyperparameter choices and can the test be calibrated across classifiers? Is there a natural nearest-neighbor bootstrap analog and stronger guarantees for discrete or mixed variables (Z discrete simplifies matching)?
