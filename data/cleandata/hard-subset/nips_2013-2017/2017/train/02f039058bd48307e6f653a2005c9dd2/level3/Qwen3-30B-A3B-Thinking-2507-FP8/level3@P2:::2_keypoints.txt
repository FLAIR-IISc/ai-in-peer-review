- Proposes converting non-parametric conditional independence testing into a binary classification problem using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate the conditional product distribution fCI(x,y,z).
- Key insight: test CI by a two-sample classification between original joint samples (label 1) and bootstrapped fCI samples (label 0), solvable with modern classifiers (GBDT, neural nets).
- Theoretical contribution (Theorem 1): nearest-neighbor bootstrap samples are close to fCI in total variation distance under smoothness assumptions.
- Theoretical contribution (Theorem 2): provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independent (bootstrapped) samples.
- Claims these are the first theoretical guarantees for bootstrapping fCI samples in the CI testing context.
- Empirical results show improvements over state-of-the-art methods (e.g., KCIT, RCIT), including on synthetic data and a flow cytometry protein signaling dataset.
- Well-positioned for the NIPS community with clear applications in causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection.
- Differentiates from prior kernel-based and LP-based methods by leveraging supervised learning tools and avoiding expensive linear programs.
- Strength: novel and compelling reduction of CI testing to supervised classification using modern ML advances.
- Strength: substantial theoretical guarantees addressing a key technical challenge in CI testing.
- Strength: strong empirical validation, particularly in high-dimensional settings and a real biological dataset.
- Weakness: paper is difficult to read and lacks clear interpretation of key concepts and assumptions (e.g., smoothness vs practical performance).
- Weakness: theoretical concerns about limitations of the classifier class G—unclear how small error probabilities are achieved if G is too simple to distinguish f and fCI.
- Weakness: presentation of theoretical results needs more intuitive explanation and possibly simpler proofs.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of practical limitations such as computational complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve clarity and address theoretical concerns about classifier class limitations.
