

This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution f^{CI}, then use classifiers to distinguish between observed samples and these bootstrapped samples. The key insight is that if the classifier performs near-randomly, the variables are conditionally independent. The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees, including bounds on total variation distance between bootstrapped samples and f^{CI} (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2).

The paper addresses an important problem in statistical learning with clear presentation and strong empirical validation. The authors demonstrate significant performance gains over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real datasets, particularly for high-dimensional settings where previous methods struggle.

However, several critical concerns must be addressed. First, the paper fails to compare with alternative sample generation methods (e.g., permutation-based approaches in [10]), leaving open whether the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is truly necessary or could be replaced by simpler methods. Second, the method's ability to detect weak conditional dependence is questionable since the NN-bootstrapped distribution is already close to f^{CI} in finite samples, potentially making weak dependence indistinguishable from independence. Third, the approach is not symmetric in X and Y, raising concerns about causal asymmetry when deciding which variable to sample (X→Y vs Y→X). Fourth, the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 lacks proper justification, with no clear guidance for selection across different datasets.

Minorly, the empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by sample size for proper comparison.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical quality with well-supported claims and significant practical impact, addressing an important problem with clear significance. The clarity is excellent, and the approach represents a novel combination of nearest-neighbor techniques with classification. While the core idea is promising, the unresolved methodological questions regarding τ selection, symmetry, and comparison with alternatives represent substantial limitations that would need addressing before acceptance. The work is technically sound and important, but the current presentation requires significant clarification on these critical points.
