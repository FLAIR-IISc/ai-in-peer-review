This paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables that reduces CI testing to binary classification via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from f(x,y,z), the method: (i) holds out n samples (U1), (ii) constructs n “conditionally independent” surrogates U2′ by replacing Y in each sample of U2 with the Y of its 1-NN in Z from a disjoint split U3, and (iii) trains a classifier to distinguish U1 (label 1) from U2′ (label 0). If the classifier cannot do better than chance, the test fails to reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. The authors prove that the NN-bootstrap distribution is close in total variation to fCI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) under smoothness conditions on f(y|z) and f(z), with a rate O(n^{-1/dz}). They also develop generalization bounds for ERM under “near-independent” (non-i.i.d.) samples produced by the bootstrap, adapting Rademacher/VC-style arguments. Empirically, the method (CCIT) outperforms kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic post-nonlinear data and on flow cytometry protein signaling data. The work connects to reductions to classification in two-sample testing (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab) and to kernel CI tools (Fukumizu et al., KCIT/RCIT, KCIPT), replacing kernel statistics by powerful classifiers (XGBoost) and replacing LP-based permutations (KCIPT) by a simple NN bootstrap with theory.

Quality: The paper is technically sound and complete. The bootstrap TV bound is carefully stated under explicit smoothness/tail conditions and the “near-independence” learning bounds are novel and relevant. The experimental section is thorough enough to demonstrate practicality and scalability in dz. Some aspects could be strengthened: calibration of Type I error, sensitivity to classifier choice/capacity, and more diagnostics for weak-dependence regimes.

Clarity: The manuscript is generally well written and organized. The pipeline and algorithms are clear, assumptions are explicit, and theorems are interpretable. The bias-corrected version (Algorithm 3) is well motivated. A few choices (e.g., τ selection) need clearer guidance and a principled calibration procedure.

Originality: Converting CI to two-sample testing via a learned discriminator is fresh in the CI context, building on classifier-based independence tests while addressing the harder Z-conditional setting. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap as a surrogate for the Z-conditional permutation, paired with TV and generalization guarantees under non-i.i.d. sampling, is a novel and useful combination. The work is distinct from KCIT/RCIT and KCIPT, which either rely on RKHS operators or expensive permutations.

Significance: CI testing is central to causal discovery and graphical models. A practical and scalable alternative to kernel CI with solid theory is impactful. The empirical evidence suggests better power at higher dz than common kernels. If calibration and weak-signal sensitivity are addressed, the method could become a widely used CI routine.

Major comments and questions
1) Modularity and ablations. The method decomposes into a generator (NN bootstrap approximating fCI) and a discriminator (two-sample test via a classifier). It would be valuable to:
- Hold the generator fixed and compare classifiers (GBDT vs DNN vs linear/logistic) for power/type I error.
- Hold the classifier fixed and replace the discriminator with a kernel two-sample test (e.g., MMD) on the same generated pairs; conversely, use the KCIPT generator with your classifier. This would clarify where the gains arise (generator vs discriminator), and relate more directly to KCIPT.
- Report computational costs for NN bootstrap vs KCIPT’s LP.

2) Sensitivity to weak conditional dependence and finite-sample closeness. Since the NN bootstrap is designed to be close to fCI, its closeness in finite n (especially under high dz where the bound is slow) may attenuate signal. Please analyze power under local alternatives (Pitman-type), and empirically sweep effect sizes (e.g., the c parameter in Y=cos(b^T Z + cX + noise)) at fixed n, reporting power curves. Practical enhancements to consider:
- Increase k in k-NN with distance weighting to reduce variance; compare to 1-NN.
- Aggregate over multiple random splits/bootstraps and use an averaged test statistic (e.g., cross-validated AUC) to stabilize power.
- Use representation learning for Z (e.g., supervised metric or autoencoder) before NN search to mitigate curse of dimensionality, with a discussion of validity.

3) Symmetry in X and Y. The generator swaps Y conditioned on Z while keeping X, Z fixed. CI is symmetric in X and Y, but the finite-sample procedure may not be. Is the test invariant if we swap the roles (i.e., generate by swapping X instead of Y)? If not, I recommend reporting both directions and combining them (e.g., by averaging test statistics or taking the maximum) to enforce symmetry and possibly improve power. Please discuss implications for causal asymmetry: while CI itself is symmetric, your choice of which coordinate to resample can introduce asymmetric finite-sample bias; the bias-correction step using (Y,Z) mitigates this but an explicit symmetry check would be reassuring.

4) Choice and calibration of τ. The recommended τ=1/√n is heuristic and depends on classifier capacity, sample splitting, and the bootstrap bias. To provide Type I error control and p-values, a data-driven calibration is preferable:
- Null calibration by repeating the entire pipeline under synthetic nulls: within each training/test split, re-generate label-0 sets by additional NN bootstraps or conditional permutations, compute the test statistic (e.g., held-out loss or AUC), and form an empirical null to set τ or compute a p-value.
- Alternatively, permute labels within D while keeping the generator fixed to estimate the finite-sample bias b and variance directly.
Please document what was used in experiments (the text mentions τ=1/√n) and provide sensitivity analyses.

Minor comments
- Algorithm notation: ensure empirical risk is normalized by sample size everywhere; in Algorithms 2–3 the definition includes 1/|D|, but later references to “loss” and comparisons should be explicit about normalization and whether the loss is error rate or AUC.
- Complexity: discuss approximate NN and its effect on TV bounds and power.
- Assumptions: provide intuition/examples where Assumptions 1–2 hold (e.g., Gaussian copula, smooth conditionals) and guidance for practitioners on diagnostics.
- Consider adding baselines based on conditional mutual information estimators (e.g., KSG variants) for completeness.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, modular reduction of CI testing to classification with practical benefits.
- Theoretical guarantees: TV closeness of the generator and learning bounds under near-independence.
- Strong empirical performance, especially in higher-dimensional Z, with real-data validation.
- Simple, scalable implementation using off-the-shelf classifiers and NN search.

Cons against acceptance
- Calibration of τ and Type I error not fully principled; lack of p-value computation.
- Limited ablation isolating contributions of generator vs discriminator.
- Power under weak dependence and local alternatives not analyzed; asymptotic rate in dz is pessimistic.
- Potential asymmetry in practice (X vs Y) not explicitly addressed.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying τ calibration and adding modular ablations; the contribution is novel and promising, with solid theory and encouraging empirical results.
