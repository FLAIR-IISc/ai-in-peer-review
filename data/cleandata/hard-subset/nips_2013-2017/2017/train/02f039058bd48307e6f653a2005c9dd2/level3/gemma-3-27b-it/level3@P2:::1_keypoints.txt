- Paper introduces a novel reduction of conditional independence (CI) testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate f_CI samples.  
- The classifier’s ability to distinguish true joint vs. bootstrapped samples is used to accept/reject conditional independence.  
- Theoretical guarantees are provided for the quality of the bootstrap and generalization bounds for the classifier.  
- Empirical results on synthetic and real data show improved performance over state-of-the-art CI tests.  
- Addresses an important problem (CI testing) with broad applications like causal inference and feature selection.  
- Novel approach: classification reduction is clever and potentially powerful.  
- Strong theoretical contributions: analysis of NN bootstrap accuracy and classifier generalization.  
- Strong empirical performance: outperforms existing methods in experiments.  
- Clear presentation and well-explained technical details.  
- Major concern: insufficient comparison with alternative methods for generating f_CI (e.g., permutation-based) and alternative two-sample tests (e.g., kernel tests).  
- Major concern: unclear performance for detecting weak conditional dependencies, especially in finite samples where f_CI may be close to the true distribution.  
- Major concern: asymmetry in treatment of X and Y is not discussed; implications for causal direction detection are unclear.  
- Major concern: threshold parameter τ selection is under-addressed; τ = 1/sqrt(n) may not be optimal and needs principled guidance.  
- Minor issue: empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by sample size.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated method with strong theory and promising empirical gains on a fundamental problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks comprehensive component comparisons, weak-dependence evaluation, treatment of asymmetry, and threshold selection guidance.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution recommending acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: compare alternative f_CI generators and two-sample tests, evaluate weak-dependence detection, discuss X/Y asymmetry and causal implications, provide principled τ selection, and fix empirical risk normalization.
