## Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper tackles the problem of non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing it as a classification problem and leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The core contribution lies in a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, coupled with theoretical guarantees on the total variational distance between the generated and true distributions. The authors also present generalization bounds for classification in the context of these near-independent samples.

The approach of reducing CI testing to a classification problem is a natural and promising direction, building on recent work like [19] which utilizes classification for independence testing. The novelty here is extending this to the more complex conditional independence scenario, requiring a method to approximate the conditional independence distribution – which the bootstrap procedure attempts to address. The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 1 combining smoothness assumptions, achievability of the bootstrapped distribution, and error bounds, are insightful and non-trivial. Establishing a bound on the total variation distance is a significant step towards providing theoretical backing for the method.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The writing is dense and lacks intuitive explanations. While the mathematical formulations are present, the connection to the overall CI testing process isn't always clear. The discussion around the small error probability in 1.1(iii) is particularly concerning. The example with r0=0.1 raises legitimate questions about how a classifier can effectively distinguish between distributions when the classifier class G is demonstrably too simple to capture the underlying relationship. This suggests a potential disconnect between the theoretical guarantees and practical applicability. 

Furthermore, while the paper references related work, the discussion feels somewhat cursory. A more in-depth comparison with existing kernel-based methods [32, 28, 10] – particularly regarding computational complexity and scalability – would strengthen the contribution. The empirical evaluation, while present, could be more comprehensive, with a more detailed analysis of the impact of different classifier choices and parameter settings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to CI testing leveraging powerful classifiers.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with insightful results (Theorem 1).
*   Potential for improved performance in high-dimensional settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Poor clarity and readability.
*   Lack of intuitive explanation and discussion of limitations.
*   Concerns about the practical relevance of the theoretical guarantees.
*   Insufficiently detailed comparison with existing methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically interesting contribution with potential, but its lack of clarity and the unresolved concerns regarding practical applicability significantly detract from its overall quality. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and address the clarity issues, provide a more thorough discussion of the limitations, and strengthen the empirical evaluation. A more intuitive explanation of the theoretical results and their implications would also be beneficial.
