This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables. The core idea is to recast the CI test as a binary classification problem, leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The critical challenge – estimating samples from the conditional independence distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) given only samples from the joint distribution f(x, y, z) – is addressed with a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (in terms of total variational distance) and generalization bounds for the classification task, ultimately translating into error bounds for the CI test. Empirical validation on simulated and real datasets demonstrates performance gains over existing methods.

This work builds upon a long line of research in non-parametric CI testing. Kernel-based methods [28, 32, 10] represent a dominant approach, often utilizing covariance operators in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces. The paper appropriately contrasts its method with existing work, particularly KCIPT [10], highlighting the advantage of avoiding expensive linear programming for permutation calculation. The connection to recent work leveraging classification for *independence* testing [19] is also clearly established. The authors correctly position their contribution as extending these ideas to the more challenging CI setting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem, coupled with the nearest-neighbor bootstrap, is a creative and potentially impactful approach.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides theoretical analysis, including bounds on the total variational distance between the bootstrapped distribution and the target fCI distribution, and generalization bounds for the classifier. This is a significant strength, especially compared to purely empirical approaches.
*   **Empirical Performance:**  The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing state-of-the-art algorithms on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The robustness across different graph structures in the flow cytometry data is particularly encouraging.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the key ideas and technical details.  The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1, 2, and 3) are helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Component Comparison (Major 1):** While the overall approach is innovative, the paper lacks a thorough comparison of individual components with existing alternatives. For example, could the nearest-neighbor bootstrap be replaced with the permutation-based generation strategy from [7], and how would this impact performance and theoretical guarantees? Similarly, could a kernel-based two-sample test be used instead of a classifier, and how would the results compare? Exploring these alternatives would strengthen the justification for the authors’ specific choices.
*   **Sensitivity to Weak Dependence (Major 2):**  A potential issue arises when the true relationship between X and Y given Z is weak.  In such cases, the bootstrapped distribution may already be very close to fCI, making it difficult for the classifier to distinguish between the two distributions and potentially leading to a failure to detect the dependence. The paper doesn’t adequately address how the method performs in these scenarios.
*   **Symmetry and Causal Asymmetry (Major 3):** The method’s symmetry (or lack thereof) with respect to X and Y is not discussed. In causal inference, the direction of dependence matters (X→Y is different from Y→X). Does the choice of which variable to sample during the bootstrap process impact the results, and if so, how is this addressed?  This is particularly important when applying the method to causal discovery.
*   **Threshold Selection (Major 4):** The choice of the threshold parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is crucial, and the paper’s suggestion of τ = 1/sqrt(n) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more principled approach to selecting this parameter is needed, potentially based on cross-validation or theoretical considerations. Further guidance on setting τ in practice would be valuable.
*   **Minor Implementation Detail:** The empirical risk calculations in Algorithms 2 and 3 appear to be missing normalization by dividing by the sample size (n).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to CI testing.
*   The empirical results demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   The problem is important and the proposed solution has the potential to advance the field.
*   The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of comparison with alternative components weakens the justification for the specific choices made.
*   The method’s performance in detecting weak conditional dependencies and handling causal asymmetry are not fully addressed.
*   The threshold selection remains unclear and could significantly impact the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The innovative approach, theoretical rigor, and promising empirical results outweigh the identified weaknesses. However, addressing the major concerns regarding component comparison, weak dependence detection, causal asymmetry, and threshold selection would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** contingent upon the authors addressing these issues in a revised version. I suggest a score of **7/10**.
