- Proposal: reduce conditional independence testing to binary classification by generating null samples via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap on Z.  
- Core theory: prove a total-variation bound between the bootstrap distribution and fCI under a smoothness condition on f(y|z) and regularity of f(z).  
- Rate: obtain dTV = O(n^(−1/dz)) up to log factors.  
- Generalization analysis: provide near-i.i.d Rademacher/VC-type bounds linking empirical minimizer risk ĝ to Bayes risk r0 = 0.5(1−dTV).  
- Empirics: method instantiated with boosted trees/deep nets outperforms KCIT/RCIT and a permutation-based approach.  
- Relation to prior work: appropriately contrasts kernel- and permutation-based CI tests and connects to classifier two-sample testing and kNN MI/entropy techniques.  
- Novelty: high — NN-bootstrap CI null with rigorous TV control and tailored near-i.i.d bounds is novel.  
- Significance: potentially high, enabling modern classifiers for CI testing, but practical rates degrade with dz.  
- Technical quality: main theorems are non-trivial and insightful, especially Theorem 1.  
- Clarity problems: manuscript is hard to read; key intuitions for Assumption 1, role of G, and TV→power translation are under-explained.  
- Missing assumptions: Section 1.1(iii) needs explicit capacity/separation conditions on G (VC dimension, sample size, relation to dTV) for the small-error claim.  
- Practical concern: r0≈0.1 example shows the small-error event may be implausible if G is too simple to separate f from fCI.  
- Bias-correction: included but its effect size and choice of τ require calibration and clearer explanation.  
- Sensitivity: method sensitivity to intrinsic dimension dz (n^(−1/dz)) and nearest-neighbor choice (k=1) is not fully explored.  
- Pros summary: novel TV bound, near-i.i.d generalization analysis, strong empirical performance.  
- Cons summary: poor exposition, insufficient conditions on model capacity, and unexplored sensitivity to dz/k.  
- Recommendation: weak accept, contingent on clarifying the role of G, adding intuition/examples for r0 and small-error regimes, and improving exposition.
