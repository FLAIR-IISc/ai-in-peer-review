

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by converting the problem into a binary classification task. The authors generate samples from the conditional independence distribution f^CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure, then train a classifier to distinguish between original samples and bootstrapped samples. The method rejects the null hypothesis of conditional independence (X ⊥ Y | Z) if the classifier performs significantly better than random guessing.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a theoretically grounded nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) with guarantees on the total variation distance between bootstrapped samples and f^CI. Theorem 1 establishes that d_TV(, f^CI) = O(1/n^{1/dz}) under appropriate smoothness assumptions. Second, it develops generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the near-independent samples generated by the bootstrap (Theorem 2), addressing an important gap in the literature. Third, it demonstrates substantial empirical improvements over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) in high-dimensional settings, particularly when the dimension of Z grows.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presented, making a strong case for the method's advantages. The authors effectively relate their work to prior research on kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and permutation-based approaches (KCIPT), highlighting how their nearest-neighbor bootstrap provides both theoretical guarantees and computational advantages over existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical guarantees are comprehensive and well-motivated, addressing both the quality of the bootstrapped samples and the generalization properties of the classifier.
- The method's modular design allows leveraging advances in classification algorithms, which is a significant practical advantage.
- The empirical results are thorough, with extensive synthetic experiments showing superior performance as the dimension of Z increases, and validation on the flow-cytometry dataset.
- The paper clearly articulates the key technical challenge: generating samples from f^CI when only samples from the true joint distribution are available.

**Major Weaknesses:**
1. **Component Replacement Analysis:** The paper decomposes the method into sample generation and classification components but fails to investigate whether alternative methods for either component (e.g., permutation-based generation or kernel two-sample tests) could be substituted and how they would compare. This is critical for understanding the method's relative advantages.

2. **Weak Dependence Detection:** The paper doesn't address how the method handles weak conditional dependence when the bootstrapped distribution is already close to f^CI in finite samples. If the bootstrapped distribution is nearly indistinguishable from f^CI, the classifier may fail to detect even moderate dependence, limiting the method's sensitivity.

3. **Causal Asymmetry:** The approach appears asymmetric in X and Y, but the paper doesn't clarify how it handles causal asymmetry (X→Y vs Y→X) when deciding which variable to sample. This is particularly important in causal discovery applications where the direction matters.

4. **Parameter Selection:** The choice of parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not justified. The paper uses τ = 1/√n as an upper bound but doesn't explain how to select τ in practice for different problem settings, which could significantly impact performance.

**Minor Concern:**
The empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by the sample size (as is standard practice), rather than being reported as absolute counts. This would make the results more interpretable and consistent with standard machine learning practice.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing with a well-motivated, theoretically grounded approach that outperforms prior methods. The quality of the work is high, with strong theoretical guarantees and compelling empirical validation. The clarity of presentation is excellent, and the significance of the problem addressed is clear. The method's modular nature and ability to leverage advances in classification algorithms are particularly valuable.

While the major weaknesses regarding component substitution, weak dependence detection, causal asymmetry, and parameter selection should be addressed, these are important considerations for the field rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed method. The paper represents a substantial advancement over existing work, particularly in high-dimensional settings where kernel-based methods struggle.

I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the major concerns in a revised version, particularly regarding the theoretical limits of the method for detecting weak dependence and the practical choice of the τ parameter. The work is clearly original and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of causal inference and statistical testing.
