- Proposes converting conditional independence testing for continuous variables into a binary classification task using generated samples approximating f_CI(x,y,z).
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to produce samples close to the conditional product distribution f_CI.
- Trains classifiers to distinguish true joint samples from bootstrap samples, enabling CI testing via classification.
- Main theoretical contribution: proves bootstrap guarantees (d_TV(., f_CI) = O(1/n^(1/d_z))) ensuring generated samples approximate f_CI.
- Extends generalization bounds to near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples, adapting classifier two-sample test theory to the conditional setting (Theorem 2).
- Builds on and extends prior work (KCIT, RCIT, and classifier two-sample tests) by addressing Z-dependent permutation issues with a novel bootstrap.
- Empirically outperforms KCIT and RCIT, maintaining near-perfect performance with high-dimensional Z (up to 70D) on synthetic data.
- Outperforms existing methods on flow-cytometry causal graphs, even with low-dimensional conditioning sets.
- Provides a well-organized implementation and thorough experimental validation (GitHub released).
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis paired with comprehensive empirical results demonstrating scalability and practical utility.
- Strength: original nearest-neighbor bootstrap idea that advances beyond permutation-based methods.
- Strength: significant for causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection due to improved CI testing performance.
- Weakness: does not address extension to discrete variables, limiting applicability.
- Weakness: sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters not thoroughly explored, raising reproducibility concerns.
- Weakness: some theoretical sections (e.g., Theorem 1 proof) could use more intuitive exposition; minor typographical/presentation issues.
- Acceptance arguments: novel, strong theory, comprehensive experiments, practical implementation, clear relation to prior work.
- Rejection concerns: limited discrete-variable discussion, clarity in some theory sections, and minor presentation fixes needed.
- Overall recommendation: strong contribution with recommendation for acceptance.
