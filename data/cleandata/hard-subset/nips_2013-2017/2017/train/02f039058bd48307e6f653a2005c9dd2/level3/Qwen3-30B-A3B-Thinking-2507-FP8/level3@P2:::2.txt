

# Review for "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing by converting the problem into a binary classification task. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditional product distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) when only access to i.i.d. samples from the true joint distribution f(x, y, z) is available. The key insight is that the CI testing problem reduces to a two-sample test between original samples (labeled 1) and bootstrapped samples (labeled 0), which can be effectively solved using powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees or neural networks. The paper builds on recent work using classifiers for independence testing (e.g., [19]), but extends it to the more challenging conditional independence setting.

The paper's main theoretical contributions are Theorem 1, which establishes that the samples generated by the nearest-neighbor bootstrap are close to fCI in total variation distance under smoothness assumptions, and Theorem 2, which provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. These results are significant as they provide the first theoretical guarantees for bootstrapping samples from fCI in the CI testing context, addressing a key technical challenge. The authors also demonstrate empirical improvements over state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic data and the flow cytometry protein signaling network dataset.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community, as it addresses a fundamental problem with clear applications in causal inference, Bayesian network structure learning, and feature selection. It builds upon recent kernel-based methods like KCIT [32] and RCIT [28] but takes a fundamentally different approach that leverages modern supervised learning tools. The authors properly contextualize their work against related literature, particularly noting the limitations of KCIPT [10] which requires solving expensive linear programs.

Strengths:
1. The conversion of CI testing to classification is a compelling and novel approach that leverages advances in supervised learning.
2. The theoretical contributions (Theorem 1 and Theorem 2) are substantial, providing rigorous guarantees for the bootstrap procedure and classification generalization.
3. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods, especially in high-dimensional settings.
4. The practical application to the flow cytometry dataset provides meaningful validation of the method's utility.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper is difficult to read due to insufficient interpretation of key concepts. For instance, the connection between the smoothness assumptions and practical performance is not clearly explained.
2. The theoretical analysis raises concerns about classifier class limitations. As noted in the key points, it's unclear how the small error probability mentioned in Section 1.1(iii) can be achieved when the classifier class G is too simple to distinguish f and fCI (as illustrated with the r0=0.1 example). The paper doesn't adequately address this potential limitation.
3. The presentation of the theoretical results could be improved with more intuitive explanations and possibly simplified proofs.
4. The paper lacks sufficient discussion of the practical limitations of the method, such as computational complexity and sensitivity to hyperparameters.

The main theoretical contribution, Theorem 1, is particularly impressive as it combines smoothness assumptions with the error of the optimal classifier to provide meaningful bounds. However, the connection between the theoretical guarantees and the practical performance is not sufficiently clarified.

For acceptance, I note that the paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a well-established problem in statistics and machine learning, with clear empirical validation. The novelty of the approach, combined with the strong theoretical results, makes it a valuable contribution to the field. However, the paper would benefit substantially from improved clarity and addressing the theoretical concerns regarding classifier class limitations.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to conditional independence testing that leverages modern machine learning tools. The main weakness is the paper's lack of clarity in explaining key concepts and addressing potential limitations of the method. Despite this, the technical contributions are substantial, the empirical results are convincing, and the approach has clear practical applications.

Pro acceptance: Novel approach that successfully converts a fundamental statistical problem into a machine learning problem, strong theoretical guarantees, empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over state-of-the-art.

Con acceptance: Poorly written with insufficient interpretation, theoretical concerns about classifier class limitations not adequately addressed, lacks clarity on how the method works in practice.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the theoretical concerns raised. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and would be of interest to the NIPS community.
