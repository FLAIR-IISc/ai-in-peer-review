Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables by reducing CI testing to binary classification. The method (CCIT) constructs a “synthetic null” sample intended to approximate fCI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that swaps Y across points with nearby Z while keeping (X,Z) intact. It then performs a two-sample test between the observed data and the synthetic-null data using a powerful classifier (e.g., gradient-boosted trees or DNNs). A bias-corrected variant compares the test loss when classifying with (X,Y,Z) versus with (Y,Z) only, which aims to subtract the finite-sample bias arising from near-i.i.d. dependencies in the bootstrapped samples.

The paper provides: (i) a total variation (TV) distance bound between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and fCI under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z), scaling like O(n^{-1/dz}) up to constants and lower-order terms; (ii) generalization bounds for ERM classifiers trained on the near-independent samples produced by the NN bootstrap, adapting Rademacher/VC-style results to this dependence; and (iii) empirical comparisons on synthetic data and the flow cytometry protein-signaling dataset, showing improved performance over KCIT and RCIT. The work builds on the growing body of “machine learning reductions” and on classifier two-sample tests used for (unconditional) independence [Lopez-Paz & Oquab’16], and contrasts with kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and permutation-based CIPT [Doran et al., UAI’14], which solves a linear program to produce a Z-aware permutation.

Strengths
- Clear, modular reduction from CI testing to two-sample testing that leverages advances in classification. The bias-correction idea (YZ vs XYZ classifier) is intuitive and practical.
- Novel NN-bootstrap construction with a rigorous TV guarantee to fCI; the near-i.i.d. generalization analysis is technically interesting.
- Strong empirical performance, particularly in higher-dimensional Z regimes where kernel tests struggle; code is available.
- The paper is generally well written and motivates the approach within causal discovery and graphical modeling contexts common at NIPS/NeurIPS.

Weaknesses and questions
Major 1: Swappability of components and comparisons.
- The method decomposes into (a) synthetic-null sample generation and (b) a two-sample discriminator. Could the classifier be replaced with a kernel two-sample test (e.g., MMD) within your pipeline? Conversely, could your NN-bootstrap be compared to the CIPT permutation (Doran et al.) while keeping the same classifier, to isolate the benefit of your generator?
- A small ablation—CCIT+MMD and CIPT+classifier—would clarify where performance gains arise (generator vs discriminator) and help practitioners mix-and-match.

Major 2: Power for weak dependence.
- Since your NN-bootstrapped distribution is provably close to fCI and, under H1 with weak signal, the observed distribution may also be close to fCI, practical power hinges on the TV gap and classifier capacity. Please provide a power analysis vs signal strength (e.g., the coefficient c in your synthetic model) and sample size, ideally comparing to KCIT/RCIT. Are there regimes where the classifier saturates near 0.5 even though H1 holds?
- Methodological suggestions: (i) average over multiple independent NN-bootstraps and aggregate via a calibrated test (e.g., combine p-values or use a permutation-calibrated decision on the risk difference) to reduce variance; (ii) consider k-NN resampling (k>1) or locally weighted resampling for Y|Z to reduce variance at the cost of controlled bias; (iii) investigate learned conditional generative models for Y|Z (e.g., conditional density estimators) as a plug-in generator, comparing power and runtime.

Major 3: Symmetry in X and Y and causal asymmetry.
- The generator swaps Y across nearest neighbors in Z while leaving X fixed. Is the test symmetric with respect to X and Y? In principle CI is symmetric in X and Y given Z. Please clarify whether swapping X instead of Y yields the same test and whether running both directions and combining (e.g., taking the minimum p-value with Bonferroni/Fisher’s method) improves robustness.
- In causal applications (X→Y vs Y→X), does the choice of which variable to resample have practical effects on power or calibration? Some empirical analysis and guidance would be helpful.

Major 4: Choice of τ in Algorithms 2 and 3.
- The paper notes a finite-sample bias b and uses τ≥b, with τ=1/√n in experiments. This is ad hoc and data-dependent bias may vary. Please provide a principled calibration:
  - Null calibration by Monte Carlo: generate the synthetic-null twice (or permute labels) to estimate the null distribution of the risk (or of the loss difference in CCITv2), and set τ by a desired type-I error level.
  - Use a binomial (or McNemar’s) test on paired predictions to test whether loss < 0.5 beyond chance.
  - Report a p-value (rather than a fixed τ) for reproducibility across datasets and classifiers.
- Also, Algorithm 2 Line 7 appears to have a missing sign: one would typically reject H0 if the test loss is sufficiently below 0.5 (i.e., ˆL < 0.5 − τ), not above.

Minor
- Empirical risk in Algorithms 2–3 should be normalized by sample size (you already do this in the definition, but please ensure consistency in pseudocode).
- Please comment on computational complexity (NN search, number of bootstraps) and wall-clock comparisons to RCIT/KCIT.
- Assumption 1’s “generalized curvature” could use more intuition and an example class where it holds; clarifying constants and the role of G(·) in bounds would aid readability.

Quality
Technically solid overall. The TV bound and near-i.i.d. generalization result are nontrivial and, to my knowledge, new in this CI-testing context. Assumptions are reasonable but the n^{-1/dz} rate highlights the curse of dimensionality in theory; nevertheless, empirical results suggest benign structure and the strength of modern classifiers mitigate this in practice. Experimental evaluation is thoughtful, though ablations and power analyses (Majors 1–2) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, with helpful algorithms and intuition. A few notational glitches (threshold sign; normalization) and spots where intuition for assumptions could be expanded.

Originality
High. The NN bootstrap for approximating fCI with TV guarantees and the adaptation of generalization bounds to near-independent samples are novel, beyond prior classifier-based tests for unconditional independence and prior permutation-based CI testing.

Significance
Significant for practitioners in causal discovery and graphical modeling: a flexible, scalable CI tester that leverages off-the-shelf classifiers is impactful. The theoretical pieces are also of independent interest.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant reduction; practical and flexible.
- New theory: TV approximation to fCI and generalization under near-independence.
- Strong empirical performance and available code.

Arguments against acceptance
- Threshold calibration and finite-sample bias handling need a more principled treatment.
- Limited ablations to attribute gains to generator vs discriminator; open questions about symmetry and weak-signal power.
- Theoretical rates degrade with dz; more discussion of mitigation would help.

Overall evaluation
A strong and timely contribution. Addressing the calibration and ablation concerns would further solidify the paper. I lean toward acceptance.
