This paper proposes a novel model-powered conditional independence (CI) test for continuous random variables. The core idea is to reduce CI testing to a binary classification problem by generating samples from the conditional independence distribution *f*<sub>CI</sub> using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. A classifier is then trained to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution and the bootstrapped samples, with the performance of the classifier indicating whether to reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for the classification task. Extensive experiments on synthetic and real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The problem of CI testing is fundamental to many areas of statistical learning, including causal inference, Bayesian network structure learning, and feature selection. Existing non-parametric CI tests often struggle with high dimensionality. This work tackles this limitation by leveraging the power of modern machine learning classifiers (like gradient boosted trees and deep neural networks) within a principled statistical framework. The reduction to a classification problem is a clever approach, and the theoretical analysis providing guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and generalization performance is a significant contribution. The empirical results are also encouraging, showing performance gains over state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem is a novel and potentially powerful technique.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the accuracy of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap and the generalization performance of the classifier, which strengthens the credibility of the method.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed algorithm outperforms existing methods on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed approach.
*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** CI testing is a fundamental problem with broad applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Component Comparison (Major 1):** The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison of the proposed method with alternatives. Specifically, it is unclear whether existing techniques for generating *f*<sub>CI</sub>, such as the permutation-based method in [7], could be substituted for the nearest-neighbor bootstrap without significantly impacting performance. Similarly, exploring the performance of alternative two-sample tests (e.g., kernel-based tests) in place of the classifier would be valuable.
*   **Weak Dependence Detection (Major 2):** A key challenge in CI testing is reliably detecting *weak* conditional dependencies, especially when sample sizes are limited. The paper doesn't adequately address how well the proposed method performs in such scenarios. If the NN-bootstrapped distribution is already close to *f*<sub>CI</sub> in finite samples, the classification problem might become too difficult, leading to low statistical power.
*   **Symmetry and Causal Asymmetry (Major 3):** The method appears to be asymmetric in its treatment of X and Y, as it involves generating samples conditional on either X or Y. The paper does not discuss how this asymmetry affects its ability to detect causal relationships or how it handles scenarios where the causal direction is reversed (X → Y vs. Y → X). This is an important consideration, especially in the context of causal inference.
*   **Threshold Selection (Major 4):** The choice of the threshold parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is crucial for the performance of the test, but the paper provides limited guidance on how to set this parameter in practice. The suggestion of using τ = 1/sqrt(n) might not be optimal for all datasets, and a more principled approach to threshold selection is needed.
*   **Minor Implementation Detail:** The empirical risk calculations in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by dividing by the sample size to ensure a proper loss estimate.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to CI testing with strong theoretical foundations.
*   The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing techniques.
*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in statistical learning and has the potential to make a significant impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison with alternative methods and does not adequately address the challenges of detecting weak dependencies.
*   The asymmetry in the treatment of X and Y and the lack of guidance on threshold selection are significant limitations.
*   The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, might not fully capture the practical challenges of applying the method to real-world datasets.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The core idea of reducing the problem to a classification task is innovative, and the theoretical analysis provides strong support for the proposed approach. However, the limitations regarding component comparison, weak dependence detection, asymmetry, and threshold selection raise concerns. Therefore, I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Conduct a more thorough comparison with alternative methods for generating *f*<sub>CI</sub> and for performing the two-sample test.
2.  Investigate the performance of the method in detecting weak conditional dependencies.
3.  Discuss the implications of the asymmetry in the treatment of X and Y and how the method handles causal asymmetry.
4.  Provide a more principled approach to threshold selection.
5.  Correct the normalization issue in the empirical risk calculations.
