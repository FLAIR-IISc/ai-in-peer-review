This paper proposes a novel approach to non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing, which is a crucial problem in statistics and machine learning. The authors convert the CI testing problem into a binary classification problem, allowing them to leverage the power of supervised learning models. The key innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure that generates samples close to the conditionally independent distribution. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and risk generalization bounds for the classification problem under non-i.i.d near-independent samples.

Strengths:

* The paper addresses an important problem in statistics and machine learning, and the proposed approach is novel and well-motivated.
* The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of their approach, including guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and risk generalization bounds.
* The empirical results are impressive, showing that the proposed approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes that the joint distribution of the variables satisfies certain smoothness assumptions, which may not always be the case in practice.
* The choice of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not well-motivated, and it is not clear how to choose it in practice.
* The paper does not provide a clear comparison with other methods that use classification for CI testing, such as the permutation-based kernel conditional independence test.
* The empirical results are not fully reproducible, as the code for the experiments is not provided.

Minor issues:

* The empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be divided by the sample size (normalization) to ensure that the loss is properly scaled.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work, including a clear comparison with other methods that use classification for CI testing.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to CI testing, which is a crucial problem in statistics and machine learning.
* The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of their approach, including guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and risk generalization bounds.
* The empirical results are impressive, showing that the proposed approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper assumes that the joint distribution of the variables satisfies certain smoothness assumptions, which may not always be the case in practice.
* The choice of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not well-motivated, and it is not clear how to choose it in practice.
* The paper does not provide a clear comparison with other methods that use classification for CI testing.

Overall, I believe that the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of statistics and machine learning, and it is well-suited for publication in a top-tier conference. However, I would like to see the authors address the minor issues and provide a more detailed discussion of the related work before accepting the paper.
