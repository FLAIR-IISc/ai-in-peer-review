Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces a model-powered approach to nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables. Given i.i.d. samples from f(x,y,z), the authors construct a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap to generate pseudo-samples intended to mimic the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). They then reduce CI testing to a labeled two-sample classification problem: original samples are labeled 1, NN-bootstrapped samples labeled 0. If a classifier cannot distinguish them (test error near 0.5), the method fails to reject CI; otherwise it rejects. The authors provide (i) total variation bounds between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and fCI that scale as O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions, and (ii) generalization bounds for empirical risk under near-independence, yielding guarantees that the test behaves like random guessing under H0 and has power under H1 when the TV gap is nontrivial. Empirical comparisons against KCIT/RCIT show favorable performance, particularly in higher-dimensional Z, and an application to protein signaling data is presented.

The work is related to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT/RCIT), the permutation-based kernel test of Doran et al. (KCIPT), and classifier two-sample tests (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab). Compared to KCIPT, the proposed NN bootstrap is simple and avoids solving a linear program, while offering explicit TV-distance guarantees. Compared to kernel tests, the reduction to classification allows leveraging modern models (e.g., XGBoost, DNNs) and potentially improved power in complex, high-dimensional regimes.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling reduction of CI testing to a modular pipeline: conditional resampling + two-sample discrimination.
- Novel NN-bootstrap with explicit TV bounds to fCI, and careful learning-theoretic analysis under near-i.i.d. sampling; the latter is of independent interest.
- Strong empirical performance, especially for larger dz, with practical implementations leveraging fast NN search and off-the-shelf classifiers.
- Well-written and easy to follow; the problem is important and timely.

Weaknesses and major concerns
1) Modularity: alternatives for each component and comparative performance. The method decomposes into conditional sample generation and two-sample discrimination. It would strengthen the paper to:
- Compare the NN bootstrap with alternative conditional resampling schemes, e.g., (a) permutation-based conditional resampling as in KCIPT, (b) k-NN conditional permutations/weights (k>1), (c) residual-based resampling via flexible conditional regressors for X|Z and Y|Z, or (d) conditional GAN/VAEs trained to approximate f(x|z) and f(y|z).
- Compare the classifier-based discriminator to kernel two-sample tests (e.g., MMD with optimized kernels) using the same resampled data. This would clarify when classifiers vs kernels are preferable, both for power and calibration.
Given the pipeline’s modularity, head-to-head ablations would be very informative.

2) Power against weak conditional dependence. Theorem 1 shows the NN-resampled distribution is already close to fCI at rate O(n^{-1/dz}). When H1 is weak (small dTV(f,fCI)), finite-sample closeness of the resampled distribution to fCI plus variance in the classifier may mask dependence. Please clarify:
- Minimal detectable effect: can you translate Theorem 2 into a separation-rate condition on dTV(f,fCI) (or on a more interpretable quantity like conditional mutual information) as a function of n and dz?
- Practical sensitivity: experiments with very weak signal (vary c in Y=cos(b^T Z + cX + ε2) to near 0) and moderate/large dz would help quantify power decay.
- Tuning for power: discuss using k-NN (k>1) with local weighting, repeated resampling with aggregation, or cross-fitting to reduce variance and improve sensitivity.

3) Symmetry and causal asymmetry. The test resamples Y given Z and keeps X fixed, then compares classifiers trained on (X,Y,Z) vs (Y,Z) to correct bias. CI is symmetric in X and Y. Is the procedure symmetric?
- Does swapping the roles (resample X|Z, correct via (X,Z)) yield the same decision? If not, recommend a symmetrized statistic (e.g., max or average of both directions) and report its effect empirically.
- In causal discovery workflows (edges X→Y vs Y→X), users may be tempted to choose which variable to resample based on a hypothesized direction. Please discuss whether/why the test statistic should remain invariant to labeling of X and Y, and provide guidance.

4) Choice of threshold τ (Algorithms 2 and 3). The experiments set τ=1/√n, but the test as stated does not provide size control or a recipe for setting τ at a desired α-level.
- Please propose a calibration strategy: e.g., null calibration by re-generating multiple NN-bootstrapped datasets and computing the empirical null of the test statistic; label-permutation of the constructed D; or cross-fitting that isolates independent test folds for valid p-values. Reporting p-values or quantile-based thresholds would make the test usable in practice and in multiple-testing settings (e.g., graph discovery).
- Can the bounds in Theorem 2 be turned into a conservative τ(α,n,V) to control Type I error?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Statistical validity: clarify whether the train/test split D_r, D_e ensures that dependencies induced by using the same U2,U3 for resampling do not leak between train and test. If needed, generate independent U’2,U’3 for the test set or use cross-fitting across folds.
- Curse of dimensionality: the O(n^{-1/dz}) rate is pessimistic but fundamental for NN methods. Discuss when practice departs from worst-case (e.g., low intrinsic dimension of Z, manifold structure), and whether metric learning or representation learning for Z can help.
- Complexity: include time/memory scaling vs RCIT/KCIT for large n and dz; report k-NN indexing details.
- Multiple testing: recommend FDR/FWER control procedures for structure learning use-cases.

Minor
- Algorithms 2 and 3: ensure empirical risks are explicitly normalized by sample size when reported/computed, and make notation consistent across lines (the pseudo-code mixes descriptions).
- Notation: a brief intuitive explanation of Assumption 1 (curvature matrix) would help readers; consider adding examples where it holds (e.g., location-scale families).

Evaluation
- Quality: Technically solid. The TV and generalization bounds are carefully argued; empirical results are compelling. The main missing piece is calibrated testing (τ selection) and comparisons for alternative resampling/discriminator choices.
- Clarity: Very clear and well-organized. Minor clarifications as above would improve reproducibility.
- Originality: High. The conditional resampling via simple NN with TV guarantees and the generalization analysis under near-i.i.d. sampling are novel contributions.
- Significance: Significant. A practical, powerful CI test with strong empirical performance and a modular design is likely to influence practice and spur follow-on work.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, modular reduction with strong theory and practice.
- Simple, efficient algorithm; clear empirical gains in challenging regimes.
- Novel generalization analysis under near-independence.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of calibrated thresholds/p-values; unclear Type I error control.
- Limited ablations on the two modules; unclear when classifiers vs kernels are best.
- Open questions on symmetry and power in weak-signal regimes.

Questions for the authors
1) How would you set τ to guarantee level-α control? Can you report calibrated p-values?
2) Is the test symmetric in X and Y? What happens if you resample X|Z instead?
3) How does power scale in weak dependence and large dz; can you provide separation-rate guidance?
4) Can you compare against KCIPT-style permutations and MMD-based discrimination within your pipeline?
