### Summary

This paper introduces a novel, non-parametric method for Conditional Independence (CI) testing, named CCIT (Classifier CI Test). The core idea is to reframe the CI test (X ⊥ Y | Z) as a binary classification problem. The authors propose a procedure to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution f(x, y, z) and samples from an approximation of the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).

The key technical contribution is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap algorithm to generate samples approximating f_CI. Given a set of i.i.d. samples, this algorithm finds, for each point (x, y, z), a nearest neighbor in Z-space, (x', y', z'), and creates a new sample (x, y', z). The original samples are labeled '1' and the bootstrapped samples '0'. A powerful classifier (e.g., XGBoost) is then trained to distinguish between these two classes. If the classifier's accuracy on a test set is significantly better than random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is rejected.

The paper provides theoretical support for this approach. First, it bounds the total variation distance between the distribution of the bootstrapped samples and the true f_CI. Second, it provides generalization bounds for the classifier's risk, adapting Rademacher complexity analysis to the "near-independent" samples generated by their procedure. This work relates to a growing line of research on using classifiers for two-sample testing and to kernel-based CI tests like KCIT and permutation-based tests like KCIPT. Empirically, the proposed method is shown to outperform state-of-the-art CI tests, particularly as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a creative and powerful "model-powered" approach to a fundamental statistical problem. By reducing CI testing to classification, it allows the use of sophisticated, high-capacity supervised learning models that are well-suited for complex, high-dimensional data, a setting where many existing CI tests struggle. The strong empirical results, showing significant gains over established methods like KCIT and RCIT, demonstrate the promise of this direction.
2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The authors provide solid theoretical justification for their method. The bound on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped sample distribution and the target conditional product distribution (Theorem 1) is a key result that validates the sample generation procedure. Furthermore, the novel analysis of generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the non-i.i.d. bootstrapped samples (Theorem 2) is a valuable contribution of independent interest.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The main idea is explained intuitively with a clear diagram (Fig. 1) and detailed algorithms. The motivation, contributions, and connection to related work are laid out clearly.

### Weaknesses

My main concerns with the paper relate to the statistical soundness of the hypothesis test, its robustness to weak dependencies, and a clearer positioning relative to alternative methods.

1.  **Choice of Threshold `τ`:** A major concern is the lack of a principled method for choosing the decision threshold `τ` in Algorithms 2 and 3. For a method to be a valid hypothesis test, one must be able to control the Type I error rate at a desired significance level (e.g., α = 0.05). This typically requires understanding the null distribution of the test statistic. The paper provides no such analysis. The authors state they use `τ = 1/sqrt(n)` in experiments, which seems ad-hoc. Without a procedure to calibrate `τ` to a specific α-level, the proposed method is more of a dependency scoring metric than a formal statistical test. This is a critical omission that undermines its practical utility.

2.  **Distinguishing Weak Dependence from Approximation Error:** The nearest-neighbor bootstrap only generates an approximation of the f_CI distribution. The total variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and f_CI is bounded by b(n), which is non-zero for finite n. Consequently, even under the null hypothesis (H0), the two distributions being compared are slightly different. In a scenario with true but weak conditional dependence, the distributions will also be slightly different. It is unclear how the proposed test can reliably distinguish between these two cases. The bias correction in Algorithm 3 is a good step, but the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion and analysis of the test's power against weak dependencies in light of this inherent approximation error.

3.  **Modular Comparison to Alternatives:** The proposed method consists of two main modules: (1) a nearest-neighbor bootstrap for sample generation and (2) a classifier for a two-sample test. The paper would be significantly stronger if it analyzed the contribution of each module separately. For example:
    *   How does the proposed NN-bootstrap compare to the permutation-based sampling strategy proposed by Doran et al. [10]?
    *   Conversely, how does using a classifier compare to using a standard kernel two-sample test (e.g., MMD) on the samples generated by the NN-bootstrap?
    Such an ablation study would help pinpoint whether the performance gains come from the novel sampling scheme, the use of a powerful classifier, or the combination of both.

4.  **Symmetry of the Test:** The procedure described in Algorithm 1 resamples the Y-coordinates based on nearest neighbors in Z. Is the test symmetric with respect to X and Y? What would be the effect of resampling X-coordinates instead? This could be particularly relevant in causal discovery applications where the roles of X and Y may not be interchangeable.

5.  **Minor Point:** In the pseudocode for Algorithms 2 and 3, the definition of empirical risk minimization (`ˆL(g, D_r)`) is missing the normalization by the size of the training set, `1/|D_r|`. This should be corrected for clarity.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   Proposes a novel, elegant, and powerful framework for CI testing that effectively leverages modern machine learning classifiers.
*   The approach is supported by both theoretical analysis and strong empirical evidence of outperforming state-of-the-art methods, especially in high dimensions.
*   Addresses a problem of fundamental importance to machine learning, statistics, and causal inference.

### Arguments for Rejection
*   The lack of a principled method to set the decision threshold `τ` to control Type I error is a serious flaw for a paper proposing a hypothesis test.
*   The paper does not adequately address how the test distinguishes weak dependence from the inherent approximation error of the bootstrap method.
*   The contribution is not sufficiently disentangled from alternative combinations of sampling and two-sample testing methods.

In conclusion, while the core idea is excellent and the empirical results are impressive, the issue with the threshold selection is a major weakness that prevents me from giving a strong recommendation at this time. If the authors can convincingly address how to calibrate `τ` for a desired significance level, I would be inclined to raise my score.
