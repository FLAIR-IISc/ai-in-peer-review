- Introduces CCIT: reframes conditional independence testing as binary classification by distinguishing true joint samples from NN-bootstrap approximations of f_CI.
- Proposes a nearest-neighbor bootstrap that constructs (x, y', z) by pairing y from a Z-nearest neighbor to approximate f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).
- Trains a high-capacity classifier (e.g., XGBoost) on original vs. bootstrapped samples and rejects H0 if classifier outperforms chance.
- Provides a bound on total variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and true f_CI (Theorem 1).
- Derives generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the near-independent bootstrapped samples (Theorem 2).
- Empirically outperforms KCIT, RCIT, and related CI tests, especially as Z dimensionality increases.
- Well-written and clearly organized with intuitive explanations, diagrams, and algorithms.
- Lacks a principled method to choose threshold τ, so Type I error control at a desired significance level is not established.
- Finite-sample approximation error b(n) means bootstrapped null differs from true null, making it unclear how to separate weak dependencies from approximation bias.
- Needs ablation/modular analysis to disentangle contributions of NN-bootstrap vs. classifier (e.g., compare to permutation sampling or using MMD on bootstrap samples).
- Unclear whether the test is symmetric in X and Y and how resampling X instead of Y would affect results.
- Minor correction: empirical risk definition in Algorithms 2 and 3 should include normalization by 1/|D_r|.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, powerful framework that leverages modern classifiers, supported by theory and strong empirical gains in high dimensions.
- Arguments for rejection: no principled τ calibration for Type I control, insufficient analysis of weak-dependence vs. bootstrap error, and incomplete comparison to alternative sampling/test combinations.
