

# Review for NIPS 2017 Paper: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The authors convert the CI testing problem into a classification task where the goal is to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution and samples from the conditionally independent distribution f^CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). To generate samples from f^CI, they introduce a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) that swaps Y-coordinates with those of nearest neighbors in Z-space. The main technical contributions include theoretical guarantees on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped samples and f^CI, and generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples.

The paper builds on previous work in CI testing, particularly kernel-based methods like KCIT [32], RCIT [28], and KCIPT [10]. Unlike KCIPT, which requires solving an expensive linear program to generate samples from f^CI, the authors' nearest-neighbor approach is computationally efficient and comes with theoretical guarantees. The paper also relates to recent work on using binary classifiers for two-sample testing [19], but extends this to the more challenging conditional independence setting.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical contributions are well-motivated and rigorous. Theorem 1 establishes meaningful bounds on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped samples and f^CI, while Theorem 2 provides novel generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. The authors carefully address the non-i.i.d nature of the bootstrapped samples, which is a critical technical challenge.

2. **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real datasets. The method scales significantly better with dimensionality of Z, which is particularly important for high-dimensional causal discovery applications. The flow cytometry dataset results provide a compelling real-world application.

3. **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear exposition of the methodology and theoretical results. The algorithms are well-defined, and the connection between CI testing and classification is effectively illustrated with Figure 1.

4. **Significance:** CI testing is a fundamental problem with wide applications in causal inference, Bayesian network structure learning, and feature selection. The authors successfully leverage advances in supervised learning to address this problem, providing a method that can benefit from future improvements in classification algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Comparison with Alternatives:** The paper does not sufficiently address how their nearest-neighbor bootstrap compares to existing alternatives for generating samples from f^CI. For instance, it would be valuable to compare their method with the permutation-based approaches in [10] or with direct estimation of conditional mutual information using nearest-neighbor techniques [13]. The authors claim their method is "efficient" but don't provide a thorough comparison of computational complexity or performance trade-offs.

2. **Detection of Weak Dependence:** The paper doesn't adequately address how the method performs when the dependence between X and Y given Z is weak. Since the bootstrapped samples are already close to f^CI in finite samples (as shown in Theorem 1), the method's ability to detect subtle conditional dependence may be limited. The authors should discuss the trade-offs between sample size and the strength of dependence that can be reliably detected.

3. **Symmetry and Causal Asymmetry:** The method as presented is not symmetric in X and Y. This is a significant concern because CI testing is often used in causal discovery, where the direction of dependence matters (e.g., X→Y vs Y→X). The authors should address how their method handles causal asymmetry and whether the choice of which variable to "bootstrap" affects the results.

4. **Threshold Selection:** The parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is crucial for the test's performance, but the paper provides no guidance on how to choose it. The authors mention setting τ = 1/√n as an "upper-bound on the expected variance," but this seems arbitrary without justification or empirical exploration of different values.

5. **Minor Issue:** The empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by the sample size (i.e., divided by n), which is a standard practice in classification. This normalization is missing in the pseudocode, which could cause confusion for readers.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the problem of conditional independence testing. The model-powered approach is novel, technically sound, and empirically validated. The theoretical analysis of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap and the generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples are particularly impressive contributions. The paper addresses an important problem with clear applications, and the method's scalability with dimensionality is a major practical advantage.

The main concerns are about the lack of comparison with alternative sample generation methods, the handling of weak dependence, and the threshold selection for the test. These are important issues that should be addressed before publication. However, the paper's technical contributions and empirical results are substantial enough to warrant acceptance, and these concerns can be addressed through minor revisions.

**Recommendation:**
Accept

**Pro and Con Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel model-powered approach that reduces CI testing to classification
- Theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap procedure and classification performance
- Strong empirical results showing superior performance over state-of-the-art
- Clear, well-organized presentation of a complex problem

*Con:*
- Insufficient comparison with alternative sample generation methods
- Limited discussion of the method's ability to detect weak conditional dependence
- No guidance on choosing the critical threshold parameter τ
- Lack of symmetry in X and Y (important for causal inference)

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and with minor revisions addressing the concerns above, it would be an excellent addition to NIPS 2017.
