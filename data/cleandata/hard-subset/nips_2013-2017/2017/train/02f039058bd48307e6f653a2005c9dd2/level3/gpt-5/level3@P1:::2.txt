This paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables that reduces CI testing to a binary classification problem. Given 3n samples from f(x,y,z), the method (i) constructs a “conditionally independent” sample via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that swaps Y across 1-NN matches in Z, (ii) labels original samples as class 1 and NN-bootstrapped samples as class 0, and (iii) trains a classifier (e.g., XGBoost or DNN) to distinguish them. If the classifier cannot do better than chance, one fails to reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. The paper proves that the NN bootstrap produces samples close (in total variation) to fCI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), derives generalization bounds for ERM under near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples, and demonstrates strong empirical results on synthetic and flow-cytometry data, often outperforming kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT).

Relation to prior work: The paper is closely related to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT, KCIPT). Unlike KCIPT, which uses a Z-aware permutation via a linear program, this work uses a simple NN bootstrap with theoretical TV guarantees, and leverages advances in supervised learning for the two-sample subproblem, echoing classifier two-sample tests and recent independence testing via classifiers. The non-i.i.d. generalization analysis connects to Rademacher/VC theory and to bounds for mixing processes.

Main technical contributions:
- Smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) (via a generalized curvature bound) and on f(z) (twice-differentiable with Hessian and tail controls) that enable an explicit, dimension-aware TV bound between the bootstrap distribution and fCI. Theorem 1 yields a rate essentially O(n^(-1/dz)) up to constants and tail/mass terms G(·).
- Generalization guarantees for ERM under near-independence: the samples generated by the NN bootstrap are not i.i.d. but are “close enough” to obtain risk bounds akin to standard VC/Rademacher results. The analysis quantifies the approximation to the Bayes risk r0=0.5(1−dTV(f,fCI)), with an additional approximation term η capturing the gap between the Bayes classifier and the best-in-class classifier, plus near-independence error terms.
- A bias-corrected testing procedure (CCITv2) that subtracts a YZ-only classifier’s loss from the XYZ classifier’s loss to mitigate finite-sample bias introduced by the bootstrap.

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple reduction: CI testing to classification via a Z-aware bootstrap; practical and modular (plug in any strong classifier).
- Solid and non-trivial theory: TV closeness under smoothness; careful handling of non-i.i.d. samples; bounds that cleanly expose the roles of dTV, VC dimension V, dz, and the approximation gap η.
- Empirical performance: strong power in challenging high-dimensional-Z synthetic settings and competitive performance on real flow-cytometry data; implementation available.
- The NN bootstrap is computationally light compared to Z-aware permutations via linear programs, and scales with fast NN search.

Weaknesses and clarity concerns:
- Readability and interpretation: the paper is difficult to parse and offers limited intuition for how the pieces fit together in practice (e.g., how TV bounds translate into test size/power for finite n, how to set τ, and how to choose G).
- Role of classifier class G and η: While the bounds carry an explicit approximation term η (gap between Bayes and best-in-class), the paper gives little guidance on ensuring η is small. If G is too simple, the trained classifier may not approach r0 even when dTV(f,fCI) is large. For example, with r0=0.1 (large signal), a weak G could still yield test error near 0.5. This raises practical questions about adaptivity of G, diagnostics, and the conditions under which the claimed small error probability in 1.1(iii) is realized.
- Statistical calibration: Type I error control under H0 relies on choosing τ>b where b is an unknown finite-sample bias; in experiments τ=1/√n is used without a principled calibration. The paper lacks a clear recipe for p-values or guaranteed size control.
- Assumptions: The smoothness conditions (Assumption 1 curvature, Assumption 2 Hessian/tail controls) may be restrictive; the paper would benefit from examples where they hold, and discussion of robustness when they fail.
- Dimension dependence: Theoretical rates degrade with dz (O(n^(-1/dz))). While practice looks better, the paper does not explain why (e.g., benign structure in Z, manifold assumptions, or classifier adaptivity).

Quality: The technical development appears sound and thoughtfully executed. The TV analysis of the NN bootstrap and the non-i.i.d. generalization bounds are novel and carefully tied to the testing problem. However, statistical calibration and guidance for choosing G and τ need strengthening.

Clarity: The exposition is dense; key definitions (r0, η, G) and their practical implications are not sufficiently highlighted. The paper would benefit from an end-to-end schematic linking assumptions → TV closeness → generalization bounds → test decision, and from a dedicated section on practical guidance (choice of classifier, hyperparameters, τ, and diagnostics).

Originality: High. The reduction of CI testing to classification via a theoretically grounded NN bootstrap, plus generalization analysis under near-independence, goes beyond prior kernel/permutation-based approaches.

Significance: Potentially high. The approach offers a flexible, scalable alternative to kernel CI tests and can immediately leverage advances in classification. If calibration and guidance are improved, it could become a standard practical CI test.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Elegant, practical reduction with strong empirical gains.
- Non-trivial, broadly useful theory (TV closeness and near-i.i.d. ERM bounds).
- Simpler and faster construction than KCIPT with comparable or better accuracy.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited guidance on classifier choice and calibration; unclear conditions ensuring small η in practice.
- Weak treatment of Type I error control and p-value computation.
- Dense presentation with minimal intuition, making it hard for readers to implement correctly.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide actionable guidance: how to choose G (e.g., XGBoost vs DNN), how to detect when G is too weak (e.g., learning curves, cross-validated risk vs richer models), and how to select τ to control size (e.g., Monte Carlo null via repeated NN bootstraps, cross-fitting, or conditional randomization for calibration).
- Clarify the role of η with ablations varying G’s capacity and reporting power/size trade-offs.
- Discuss the smoothness assumptions with concrete examples and robustness checks.
- Analyze computational cost and scalability vs KCIT/RCIT/KCIPT.
- Add more interpretation of the empirical results, especially why performance remains strong as dz grows despite the pessimistic rate.

Overall recommendation: Borderline accept. The methodological novelty, theoretical contributions, and empirical performance are strong, but the paper needs clearer guidance on calibration and practical deployment, and improved clarity to maximize impact.
