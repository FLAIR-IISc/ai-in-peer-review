

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by converting it into a binary classification problem. The authors address the key challenge of generating samples from the conditionally independent distribution f^{CI}(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. Their approach (CCIT) generates samples that approximate f^{CI}, then trains classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees) to distinguish between original samples and bootstrapped samples. The intuition is that if the classifier performs significantly better than random, it indicates conditional dependence.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides theoretical guarantees for the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure, showing that the generated samples are close to f^{CI} in total variation distance (Theorem 1). Second, it develops novel generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), which is of independent interest. Third, it demonstrates significant empirical improvements over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real-world flow-cytometry datasets.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the problem, solution, and theoretical results. The connection to previous work on kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) is appropriate, and the authors correctly identify their method's advantages over permutation-based approaches in high-dimensional settings. The empirical results are comprehensive and demonstrate that CCIT scales better with dimensionality than competing methods.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. First, the authors do not adequately compare their nearest-neighbor bootstrap to existing alternatives. They do not address whether permutation-based approaches (as in [10]) or kernel two-sample tests could replace their bootstrap step while maintaining similar performance. The claim that their bootstrap is "more efficient" needs stronger justification beyond "simple" - they should provide computational complexity comparisons.

Second, the paper does not adequately address the fundamental issue of detecting weak conditional dependence. The authors show that their bootstrap samples are close to f^{CI}, but they do not explain how their method can distinguish between weak dependence (where d_TV(f, f^{CI}) is small) and independence. This is a critical concern because in practice, CI tests must be sensitive to small deviations from independence.

Third, the method is not symmetric in X and Y, which raises questions about causal asymmetry. The authors sample from f^{CI} by modifying Y coordinates using nearest neighbors in Z, but they do not explain how this would work if they had chosen to modify X instead. This asymmetry could lead to inconsistent results depending on which variable is treated as the "target" for modification.

Fourth, the choice of threshold τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is problematic. The paper states "we set the threshold in Algorithm 3 to τ = 1/√n" but provides no theoretical justification for this choice. The authors should derive τ based on their theoretical bounds to ensure proper control of Type I error rates.

Finally, there is a minor technical issue: the empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be divided by the sample size (as is standard) to properly represent the error rate. The current formulation computes the raw number of misclassifications rather than the rate.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical results and good empirical validation. The clarity is excellent, with clear presentation of methods and results. The originality is high, as the model-powered approach to CI testing is novel and effectively leverages advances in classification. The significance is strong, as CI testing is a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning with applications in causal inference and structure learning.

To summarize the pros and cons:

Pros:
- Novel model-powered approach to CI testing
- Strong theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap procedure
- Significant empirical improvements over state-of-the-art
- Well-written and clearly presented

Cons:
- Insufficient comparison to alternative bootstrap methods
- No discussion of how to detect weak dependence
- Asymmetric treatment of X and Y
- Arbitrary threshold choice without theoretical justification
- Minor technical issue with empirical risk calculation

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact, but it requires addressing the major concerns before acceptance. The authors should provide a thorough comparison to alternative bootstrap methods, derive a principled choice for τ, address the asymmetry issue, and clarify how their method handles weak dependence. With these revisions, this would be an excellent contribution to the field.
