

This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors convert CI testing into a two-sample test between original samples and those generated via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure, which simulates samples from the conditional independence distribution fCI. The main theoretical contributions include a novel smoothness assumption on conditional densities and a bound on the total-variation distance between the bootstrap samples and fCI (Theorem 1), along with generalization bounds for classifiers trained on these near-independent samples.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, showing significant performance gains over state-of-the-art methods on synthetic and real datasets. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with Theorem 1 being particularly novel and insightful. The connection between classification error and CI testing is well-motivated, and the authors provide appropriate theoretical guarantees for their approach.

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The theoretical results are presented in a dense, technical manner without sufficient interpretation. The connection between the theoretical error bounds and practical performance is unclear, particularly regarding the example with r0=0.1 where the classifier class G might be too simple to distinguish f and f^CI. The paper fails to explain how small error probabilities can realistically occur in such cases, raising concerns about the practical applicability of the theoretical guarantees.

The work demonstrates good quality through its theoretical contributions and empirical validation. Originality is strong, as the authors introduce a novel bootstrap procedure and analysis framework for CI testing, distinct from existing kernel-based approaches. The significance is high, as CI testing is a fundamental problem in causal inference and graphical models, and the approach leverages powerful classification tools.

The main weakness is poor clarity and lack of interpretive guidance, which undermines the otherwise strong technical contribution. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of the theoretical results, clearer discussion of practical implementation considerations, and a more detailed explanation of how the theoretical error bounds translate to real-world performance. While the technical content is valuable, the current presentation makes it difficult for readers to fully appreciate the contribution and its implications.
