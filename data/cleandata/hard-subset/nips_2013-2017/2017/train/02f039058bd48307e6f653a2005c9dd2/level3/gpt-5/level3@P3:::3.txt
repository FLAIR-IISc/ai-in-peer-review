Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test for three continuous random variables X, Y, Z that reduces CI testing to a supervised binary classification problem. The key idea is to construct a set of “conditionally independent” samples by a simple nearest-neighbor bootstrap: split the data, for each point in one split find its nearest neighbor in Z in the other split, and swap the Y coordinates. This produces samples that are provably close (in total variation) to the conditional product f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). The original joint samples are labeled as class 1 and the bootstrapped samples as class 0. A binary classifier (e.g., gradient-boosted trees or a deep net) is trained to discriminate the two classes; if it performs significantly better than chance on a held-out test set, H0: X ⟂ Y | Z is rejected. A bias-corrected variant subtracts the performance of a classifier that only sees (Y,Z) from a classifier that sees (X,Y,Z).

Theoretically, the paper proves: (i) the bootstrap samples are close to fCI in total variation at rate O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z); (ii) generalization bounds for ERM classifiers with near-independent samples, effectively extending Rademacher/VC guarantees to this setting; and (iii) that, under H0, the classifier’s error concentrates near 0.5. Empirically, the method (CCIT) outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic data with growing dim(Z) and on the flow cytometry data.

This work connects clearly to and extends “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (Lopez-Paz & Oquab), which handled unconditional independence via permutation, and to kernel CI tests (KCIT/RCIT) and the permutation-based KCIPT. Relative to KCIPT’s linear-program-based conditional permutation, nearest-neighbor matching is much simpler and comes with new total-variation guarantees.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Strong. The total-variation approximation and “near-i.i.d.” generalization analysis are nontrivial and, to my knowledge, new in the conditional setting. Assumptions are explicit (smoothness of f(y|z), regularity of f(z)) and the curse-of-dimensionality in Z is acknowledged by the O(n^{-1/dz}) rate. Empirical results are carefully executed and competitive.
- Claims support: The method is validated on both synthetic and real datasets; theoretical claims match the algorithmic design. One caveat: the paper primarily reports ROC/AUC and accuracy thresholds; explicit control of Type I error at a fixed level α is not fully developed (though the bootstrap suggests a path to calibration).
- Completeness: This is a complete piece of work with proofs and code.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and accessible to practitioners; the algorithm is easy to implement, and the empirical section is clear.
- Proofs, while impressive, are dense and will be hard to follow for non-experts.
- Presentation issues:
  - Section 1.1 largely repeats the intro; consider merging to avoid redundancy.
  - Algorithm 2 can be described more succinctly; the core is data splitting, neighbor match in Z, label construction, train/test split, and ERM—this can be compressed.
  - Minor typos: “classifier” (line 157) and “graphs” (line 280).

Originality
- The reduction of CI testing to classification using a nearest-neighbor conditional bootstrap is novel and elegant. The generalization bounds under near-independence and total-variation analysis for the conditional bootstrap appear original.
- The work thoughtfully builds on and contrasts with KCIT/RCIT/KCIPT and classifier two-sample tests.

Significance
- Practically useful: CI testing is central to causal discovery and unconfoundedness checks; a scalable, model-powered approach will be valuable to practitioners.
- Likely to be used and extended: the modular pipeline can leverage advances in classification, metric learning for Z, and approximate NN search.
- Theoretical advances extend a growing line of work on classifier-based testing to conditional settings.

Questions for the authors
- Discrete-variable analog: For discrete Z (or small-support Z), a natural analog is to permute Y within each Z stratum to exactly sample f(y|z) under H0, yielding finite-sample exactness. Do you consider such a variant, and would the bootstrap become strictly more powerful or easier to calibrate when Z is discrete? Can your theory cover the mixed discrete/continuous case?
- Calibration and p-values: How do you recommend setting τ to achieve a test at level α? Can one use repeated nearest-neighbor bootstraps under an estimated fCI to approximate the null distribution of the classifier test statistic and report p-values?
- Classifier/hyperparameter sensitivity: How sensitive is the accept/reject decision to the choice of classifier, depth/learning rate for XGBoost, network architecture, and early stopping? Could different seeds/hyperparameters flip outcomes? Please report robustness checks across models and hyperparameters, or provide recommended defaults.
- Nearest-neighbor design choices: What is the impact of k-NN versus 1-NN, metric choice or normalization for Z, and approximate NN search on Type I/II error? Any guidance for high-dimensional Z (e.g., metric learning or dimensionality reduction)?
- Sample complexity: Given the O(n^{-1/dz}) rate, can you provide practical rules of thumb for n vs dz to maintain power?

Pros
- Simple, modular algorithm that leverages state-of-the-art classifiers.
- Strong and original theory (TV closeness; near-i.i.d. generalization).
- Competitive and sometimes superior empirical performance.
- Clear connection to and acknowledgment of prior classifier two-sample tests.
- Practical relevance for causal inference (e.g., unconfoundedness testing).

Cons
- Theory reflects a severe dependence on dim(Z); guidance for high-dim Z could be expanded.
- Test calibration (fixed-α control, p-values) is not fully addressed.
- Some sensitivity to classifier/hyperparameters is likely, but not systematically studied.
- Minor redundancy and a few typos; some proofs are hard to parse.

Suggestions
- Add a section on calibration to obtain p-values or level-α tests (e.g., double bootstrap, label permutation within the synthetic fCI class, or cross-fitting).
- Include robustness analyses across classifiers and hyperparameters; report variability over random seeds/splits.
- Discuss discrete/mixed Z variants (within-stratum permutations), which may offer exact finite-sample validity.
- Tighten Section 1.1 and streamline Algorithm 2’s description.

Overall assessment
A strong, original, and practically impactful contribution that extends classifier-based testing to the conditional setting with solid theory and good empirical performance. I recommend acceptance.
