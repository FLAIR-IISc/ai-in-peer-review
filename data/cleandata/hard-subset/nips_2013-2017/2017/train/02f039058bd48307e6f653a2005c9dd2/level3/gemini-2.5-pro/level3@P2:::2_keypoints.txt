- Proposes reducing conditional independence testing X ⟂ Y | Z to a binary classification task using powerful supervised learners.  
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate null samples approximating f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) with provable total variation bounds.  
- Trains a classifier to distinguish real vs. bootstrapped samples and rejects H0 if accuracy exceeds chance.

- Novel and flexible framework that leverages advances in supervised learning for high-dimensional CI testing.  
- Theorem 1 provides a non-trivial TV-distance bound for the bootstrap under a smoothness assumption on f(y|z).  
- Theorem 2 gives Rademacher-type generalization bounds for classifiers trained on near-independent (non-i.i.d.) bootstrap samples.  
- Empirically outperforms KCIT and RCIT, especially as conditioning dimension grows; demonstrated on flow cytometry data.

- Presentation is dense and lacks intuition, making theoretical results (especially Theorem 2 and the role of approximation error η) hard to parse.  
- Connection between the smoothness assumptions and practical performance is under-explained.  
- Practical guidance is limited: lacks discussion of failure modes when assumptions fail, sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., threshold τ), and calibration details.

- Overall high-quality, original, and technically strong work with significant practical potential in causal inference and related areas.  
- Strengths (novel reduction, rigorous guarantees, strong empirical results) outweigh weaknesses that are mainly about clarity and exposition.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel reduction, rigorous theoretical support for bootstrap and non-i.i.d. learning, state-of-the-art empirical performance.  
- Argument against acceptance: paper is difficult to read and lacks accessible intuition for key theoretical claims.

- Recommendation: Accept, with revisions to improve clarity and provide more intuition and practical guidance.
