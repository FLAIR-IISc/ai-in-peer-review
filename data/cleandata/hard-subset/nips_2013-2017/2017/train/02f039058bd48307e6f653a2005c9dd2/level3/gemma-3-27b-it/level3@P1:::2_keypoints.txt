Paper reduces conditional independence testing for continuous variables to a binary classification problem.  
Samples approximating fCI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) are generated via a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap.  
Generated and original samples are used to train classifiers (gradient boosted trees or deep nets) to test CI.  
Theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) shows bootstrapped samples are close to fCI in total variation.  
Generalization bounds are derived for classification with non-i.i.d., near-independent samples.  
Empirical results on simulated and real (flow cytometry) data show performance gains over existing methods.  
Addresses computational bottleneck of KCIPT’s linear program by proposing a simpler NN bootstrap with theoretical guarantees.  
Leverages prior ML-for-inference work and explicitly tackles generating fCI samples, a gap in prior methods.  
Strength: conceptually elegant reduction to classification enabling flexible use of advances in ML.  
Strength: rigorous theoretical contributions, especially bounds under near-independence.  
Strength: strong empirical validation and public GitHub implementation.  
Weakness: paper is dense, lacks intuitive explanations and is hard to follow.  
Weakness: connection between theoretical error bounds and practical implications is not well explained.  
Weakness: insufficient practical guidance on choosing threshold τ in Algorithm 2.  
Weakness: discussion of bias correction in Algorithm 3 is cursory.  
Key concern: if classifier class G is under-expressive (example r0=0.1), paper doesn’t clarify how a small error probability is achievable.  
Weakness: large number of references makes the paper feel overwhelming.  
Arguments for acceptance: novelty, theoretical soundness, empirical validation, and high potential impact.  
Arguments for rejection: poor clarity/readability, limited interpretability, lack of practical guidance, and unaddressed limitations when G is simple.  
Overall recommendation: accept conditional on improving presentation, practical implementation details, and discussion of limitations and intuitive interpretation of error bounds.
