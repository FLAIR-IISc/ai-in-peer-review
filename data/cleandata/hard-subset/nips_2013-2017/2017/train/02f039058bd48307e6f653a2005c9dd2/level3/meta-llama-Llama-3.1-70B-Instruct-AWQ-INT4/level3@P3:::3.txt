This paper proposes a novel approach for non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing, reducing the problem to binary classification. The authors develop a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples close to the conditionally independent distribution and provide theoretical guarantees on the closeness of these samples. They also establish generalization bounds for classification under near-independence, extending existing results to the non-i.i.d setting.

The paper is well-written and accessible to practitioners, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and its advantages. The authors acknowledge and connect to prior work, including the "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" paper. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing the proposed method to existing CI tests on synthetic and real data.

The strengths of the paper include:

* A novel and efficient approach to CI testing, leveraging the power of binary classifiers
* Theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for classification under near-independence
* Empirical evaluation on synthetic and real data, demonstrating the method's performance gains over existing approaches
* A clear and accessible writing style, making the paper easy to follow for practitioners

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the choice of classifier and hyperparameters, as this may affect the test's performance and outcome
* The authors mention that the bootstrap procedure is efficient, but a more detailed analysis of its computational complexity would be helpful
* The paper assumes that the dimension of Z is fixed, but it would be interesting to explore how the method performs when Z is high-dimensional
* There are some minor typos and presentation issues, such as the redundancy of Section 1.1 and the description of Algorithm 2

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to CI testing, combining the power of binary classifiers with a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. While the idea of reducing CI testing to binary classification is not new, the authors' approach is distinct from existing methods and offers several advantages.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential impact on causal inference tasks, such as testing unconfoundedness. The proposed method is practically useful and can be applied to various domains, including protein signaling networks.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, as it presents a novel and efficient approach to CI testing, with strong theoretical guarantees and empirical evaluation. However, I suggest that the authors address the minor issues mentioned above to further improve the paper.
