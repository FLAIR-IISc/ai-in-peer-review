This paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test for three continuous variables by reducing CI testing to binary classification. The authors generate a “product” class by a nearest-neighbor bootstrap that swaps Y across samples with similar Z, yielding samples close in total variation to f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). A classifier (e.g., XGBoost or DNN) trained to distinguish joint vs. product samples provides the test statistic; a bias-corrected variant compares classifiers trained with and without X. Theoretical contributions include TV-distance guarantees for the bootstrap (scaling as n−1/dz under smoothness) and generalization bounds for classification under near-independent, non-i.i.d. samples via VC/Rademacher analysis. Empirically, the method outperforms kernel CI baselines (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic and flow-cytometry data. The approach connects clearly to classifier two-sample tests (C2ST; Lopez-Paz & Oquab) and to permutation-based conditional tests (e.g., KCIPT), while offering a simpler bootstrap and stronger conditional guarantees.

Quality: Technically sound with careful assumptions, nontrivial proofs, and comprehensive experiments. The near-i.i.d. generalization analysis is a notable strength. However, sensitivity analyses are limited: outcomes may depend on classifier choice, hyperparameters, and thresholding; size control under H0 and calibration (p-values) deserve more thorough treatment.

Clarity: Generally well-written and accessible. Proofs are dense and could use more intuition. Section 1.1 repeats content from the introduction; Algorithm 2 could be described more succinctly. Minor typos: “classifier” (line 157), “graphs” (line 280).

Originality: Extends C2ST methodology to the conditional setting with a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap and non-i.i.d. generalization bounds. Clear differentiation from KCIT/RCIT/KCIPT and appropriate citations.

Significance: High. CI testing is central to causal discovery and testing unconfoundedness; the method is practical, scalable in many regimes, and likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Questions and suggestions:
- Discrete analog: Is there a version when Z (or X,Y) are discrete? Would the bootstrap/permutation be exact and more powerful when Z has ties or small support?
- Robustness: How sensitive are results to classifier family, capacity, regularization, and hyperparameters? Provide ablations and recommended defaults.
- Size control: Report empirical Type I error across settings; consider cross-fitting, conformal calibration, or permutation within Z-bins.
- High-dimensional Z: Discuss kd-tree/ANN complexity and behavior when dz is large; the n−1/dz rate highlights a curse-of-dimensionality—can metric learning for Z help?

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong theory bridging CI and classification under near-independence
- Simple, implementable algorithm with open-source code
- Superior empirical performance and practical relevance

Cons:
- Limited robustness/size-control analyses
- Some presentation redundancies and dense proofs
- No discrete-variable treatment or guidance for extreme high-dimensional Z
