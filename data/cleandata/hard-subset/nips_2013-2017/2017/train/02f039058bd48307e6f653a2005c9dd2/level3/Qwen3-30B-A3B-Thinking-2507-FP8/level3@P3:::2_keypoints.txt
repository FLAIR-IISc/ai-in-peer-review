- Reduces conditional independence testing to binary classification by comparing original samples to nearest-neighbor bootstrap samples approximating f_CI.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees: Theorem 1 bounds total-variation distance between bootstrap and f_CI under smoothness; Theorem 2 gives generalization bounds for near-i.i.d. classification.  
- Leverages powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, neural nets) to detect departures from conditional independence.  
- Empirical results show substantial improvements over KCIT and RCIT on synthetic and flow-cytometry data and better scaling with Z dimensionality.  
- Practical utility: allows practitioners to use modern classification methods; includes bias-corrected Algorithm 3 to address initial bias.  
- Novelty: extends classification-based testing to the conditional setting and introduces a simple nearest-neighbor bootstrap for f_CI.  
- Theoretical contribution: non-trivial analysis of classification under non-i.i.d. sampling is of independent interest.  
- Strength: strong technical contributions and rigorous empirical validation.  
- Weakness: paper is difficult to read, poorly organized, and lacks intuitive explanation of key results.  
- Weakness: unclear interpretation of how classifier class G affects achievable error rates; the r0=0.1 example raises serious concerns.  
- Weakness: need clearer, more intuitive explanation of smoothness assumptions and their necessity.  
- Concern: claim that classifier error approaches 0.5 under H0 may fail when G is too simple, undermining a central claim.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound but weakened by lack of interpretation and unresolved concerns about classifier class limitations.  
- Clarity assessment: writing is dense; more intuitive examples and clearer exposition required.  
- Significance: approach could materially advance causal discovery, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection by enabling classifier-based CI tests.  
- Pro acceptance: novel theory, strong empirical gains, practical flexibility, and well-motivated problem.  
- Con acceptance: poor presentation, unresolved theoretical concerns about G, and example that undermines claims.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with major revisions to improve clarity, address classifier-class (G) concerns, and better connect theory to practice.
