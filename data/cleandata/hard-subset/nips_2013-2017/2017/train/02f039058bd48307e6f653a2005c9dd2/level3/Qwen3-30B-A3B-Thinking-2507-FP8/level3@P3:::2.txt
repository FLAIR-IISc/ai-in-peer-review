

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test" (NIPS 2017)

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors convert the CI testing problem into a two-sample test between original samples and bootstrap samples generated from a nearest-neighbor procedure that mimics the conditionally independent distribution f_CI. The key insight is that powerful classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, neural networks) can then distinguish between the true joint distribution and f_CI when conditional independence does not hold. The authors provide theoretical guarantees about the quality of the bootstrap samples (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d sampling (Theorem 2).

The work builds on recent advances in using classification for independence testing [19] but extends them to the more challenging conditional independence setting. It differs from prior kernel-based approaches [10, 28, 32] by using a bootstrap procedure that generates samples from f_CI without expensive linear programming, and by leveraging modern classification algorithms rather than kernel methods. The paper also relates to recent work on machine learning reductions [18, 2, 8] that convert complex learning problems into binary classification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Contributions:** The paper makes several strong theoretical contributions. Theorem 1 establishes a novel bound on the total-variation distance between the bootstrap samples and f_CI under smoothness assumptions, which is a significant technical advance. Theorem 2 provides non-trivial generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples, which is of independent interest to learning theory.

2. **Empirical Performance:** The empirical results demonstrate substantial improvements over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic data with varying dimensionality of Z and real-world flow-cytometry data. The authors show that their approach scales much better with dimension than prior methods, which aligns with their theoretical claims.

3. **Practical Utility:** The model-powered approach allows practitioners to leverage advances in classification algorithms, providing flexibility in choosing appropriate classifiers based on domain knowledge. The bias-corrected version (Algorithm 3) addresses a practical limitation of the initial approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Clarity and Interpretation:** The paper is difficult to read and lacks sufficient interpretation of key results. The explanation of how small error probability (1.1(iii)) can occur when the classifier class G is too simple is particularly unclear. The example with r0=0.1 raises serious concerns about the intuition behind the theoretical guarantees. The authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the classifier class G and the achievable error rates.

2. **Theoretical Presentation:** While Theorem 1 is novel and non-trivial, the paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the smoothness assumptions and their necessity. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical performance of the algorithm needs to be strengthened.

3. **Scope of Claims:** The paper claims that the classifier error rate approaches 0.5 under H0, but the example with r0=0.1 suggests this might not hold when the classifier class G is too simple to distinguish f and f_CI. This undermines a key claim of the paper and requires clarification.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The empirical results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach. However, the lack of clear interpretation of the theoretical results and the unresolved concerns about the classifier class G weaken the overall quality.

**Clarity:** The paper is poorly organized and lacks sufficient explanation of key concepts. The writing is dense and difficult to follow, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the specific technical details of the bootstrap procedure and theoretical analysis. More intuitive examples and a clearer exposition of the main ideas would significantly improve the paper.

**Originality:** The conversion of CI testing to binary classification is novel for the conditional independence setting, and the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure provides a simple yet effective solution to the challenging problem of generating samples from f_CI. The theoretical analysis of classification under non-i.i.d sampling is also novel and of independent interest.

**Significance:** The approach enables the use of powerful modern classifiers for CI testing, which could lead to significant practical advances in causal discovery, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection. The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods, and the model-powered approach could be extended to other statistical testing problems.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel theoretical contributions to CI testing
- Strong empirical results showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art
- Practical approach that leverages advances in classification
- Well-motivated problem with important applications

*Con:*
- Poorly written with insufficient interpretation of key results
- Critical concerns about the theoretical guarantees when classifier class G is limited
- Example with r0=0.1 undermines a key claim of the paper
- Theoretical results are not sufficiently connected to practical performance

**Overall Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of CI testing with novel theoretical insights and strong empirical results. However, the paper requires significant improvements in clarity and interpretation to properly convey its contributions. The authors should address the concerns about the classifier class G and provide a clearer explanation of how the theoretical results translate to practical performance. The paper would benefit from a more intuitive presentation of the key concepts and a deeper discussion of the limitations of the approach.
