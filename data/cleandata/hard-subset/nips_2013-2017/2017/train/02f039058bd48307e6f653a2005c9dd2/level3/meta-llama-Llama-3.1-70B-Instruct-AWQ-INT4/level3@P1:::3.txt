This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence testing for continuous random variables. The method converts the testing problem into a binary classification task, leveraging the power of supervised learning models such as gradient boosted trees and deep neural networks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive numerical experiments and real-world applications.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) reducing the problem of conditional independence testing to binary classification, (2) developing a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples close to the conditional product distribution, and (3) providing theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and the classification problem. The authors also present an efficient algorithm for conditional independence testing, which they call Classifier-based Conditional Independence Test (CCIT).

The paper is well-written and accessible to practitioners, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work in the field, connecting their approach to prior research on classifier two-sample tests.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to tackle high-dimensional conditional independence testing problems, which is a challenging task in the field. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on synthetic and real-world data, showing that their method outperforms existing conditional independence tests.

However, there are some minor issues with the presentation. Section 1.1 is redundant and could be removed or merged with other sections. Algorithm 2 could be described more succinctly, and there are a few minor typos throughout the paper.

Some potential questions and areas for further research include:

* Is there a discrete-variable analog of the proposed method, and how does the bootstrap procedure change when Z is discrete?
* How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and hyperparameters? Could this change the accept/reject outcomes?
* Can the method be extended to handle more complex conditional independence relationships, such as those involving multiple variables or non-linear relationships?

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing, providing a powerful and flexible approach to tackling high-dimensional problems. The authors' theoretical guarantees and empirical evaluations demonstrate the effectiveness of their method, making it a valuable tool for practitioners working in this area.
