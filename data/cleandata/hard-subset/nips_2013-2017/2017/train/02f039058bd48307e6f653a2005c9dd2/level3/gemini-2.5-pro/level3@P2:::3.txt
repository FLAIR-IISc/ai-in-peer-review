This paper proposes a novel, non-parametric method for testing conditional independence (CI) between continuous random variables, X ⟂ Y | Z. The core idea is to reframe the CI test as a binary classification problem, which allows the authors to leverage the power of modern high-dimensional classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks.

The method proceeds in two main stages. First, given samples from the true joint distribution f(x, y, z), it generates a new set of samples designed to approximate the conditional product distribution f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), which is the distribution under the null hypothesis of CI. This is achieved through a clever nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure: for each sample (x, y, z), it finds a different sample with the closest z-value and swaps their y-values. The paper provides a strong theoretical guarantee that the distribution of these generated samples is close in total variation distance to the true conditional product distribution. In the second stage, the original samples are labeled as class 1 and the generated samples as class 0. A classifier is trained to distinguish between these two classes. The test statistic is based on the classifier's prediction accuracy on a held-out test set. If the accuracy is close to random guessing (0.5), the null hypothesis of CI is not rejected; if the accuracy is significantly better, the null is rejected.

This work extends the recent line of research using classifiers for two-sample testing (e.g., Lopez-Paz et al., 2016) from the simpler case of marginal independence to the more challenging setting of conditional independence. This extension is non-trivial, as generating samples from the conditional product distribution is significantly harder than generating samples from the marginal product distribution (which can be done by simple permutation). The proposed nearest-neighbor bootstrap is an elegant and effective solution to this problem.

**Quality:** The paper presents a complete and technically sound piece of work. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and extensive empirical results. The theoretical contributions are particularly impressive. The authors provide a novel proof that their bootstrap procedure generates samples that are provably close to the target distribution. Furthermore, they develop new generalization bounds for classification using these non-i.i.d., "near-independent" samples, a result of independent interest that extends Rademacher complexity analysis. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against strong kernel-based baselines (KCIT, RCIT) and demonstrating superior performance, especially as the dimension of the conditioning variable Z increases. The bias-corrected version of the algorithm (CCITv2) is a thoughtful addition that addresses a potential practical issue.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the main idea is presented in an accessible manner. The motivation and algorithm are clearly described, making the core contribution easy for practitioners to understand. While the main ideas are clear, the theoretical sections and proofs can be dense and challenging to follow for a non-expert. Some of the presentation could also be tightened. For instance, Section 1.1 ("Main Contributions") largely repeats information from the introduction. The description of Algorithm 2 could also be made more succinct, as it follows a standard train/test split procedure for a classifier.
Minor typos: "classier" -> "classifier" (line 157); "graph" -> "graphs" (line 280).

**Originality:** The proposed approach is highly original. While classifier-based two-sample tests exist, their application and theoretical validation for the conditional independence setting are novel. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap method for sampling from the conditional product distribution is a key new technical contribution that enables this reduction to classification. The work is well-situated within the literature and appropriately acknowledges and builds upon prior art.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. CI testing is a fundamental tool in many areas of machine learning and statistics, including causal discovery, feature selection, and graphical model structure learning. This paper provides a powerful, practical, and scalable new tool for these tasks. By connecting CI testing to the rich and rapidly advancing field of supervised classification, the authors open up a promising research direction. Practitioners working on causal inference, in particular, will find this method practically useful for tasks such as testing for unconfoundedness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and powerful framework:** Reducing CI testing to a classification task is an elegant idea that allows leveraging state-of-the-art classifiers.
2.  **Strong theoretical guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous proofs for the validity of its bootstrap procedure and novel generalization bounds for the resulting classification problem.
3.  **State-of-the-art empirical performance:** The method is shown to outperform existing methods, especially in higher-dimensional settings, on both synthetic and real-world data.
4.  **High potential impact:** The method provides a valuable new tool for a fundamental statistical problem with broad applications in machine learning and causal inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The sensitivity of the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters is not explored. This is a critical practical issue, as the power of the test is directly tied to the performance of the chosen classifier.
2.  The clarity of the theoretical sections could be improved to make the results more accessible.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  How sensitive is the final test decision (or p-value) to the specific choice of classifier (e.g., XGBoost vs. a deep neural network) and its hyperparameters? Could an overly powerful or fine-tuned classifier lead to false rejections by detecting minuscule artifacts of the bootstrap procedure rather than true conditional dependence?
2.  Have you considered an analog of your test for discrete variables? In the case where Z is discrete, the nearest-neighbor search becomes an exact match within strata of Z. Does the bootstrap procedure and its theoretical guarantees become simpler or more powerful in this setting?

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a significant and novel contribution to an important problem. The strengths in originality, theoretical depth, and empirical performance far outweigh the minor weaknesses in presentation.
