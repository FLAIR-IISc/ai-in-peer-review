

# Review for "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables by converting the problem into a binary classification task. The authors develop a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples close to the conditional product distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) (which characterizes X ⊥ Y | Z), then train classifiers to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution and these generated samples. The core technical contribution is proving theoretical guarantees on the bootstrap procedure (showing d_TV(, f_CI) = O(1/n^(1/dz))) and deriving generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples, which extend previous work on classifier-based two-sample tests to the conditional setting.

The paper builds on recent advances in CI testing, particularly the kernel-based approaches of KCIT [32] and RCIT [28], and connects to the "revisiting classifier two-sample tests" work by Lopez-Paz and Oquab [19]. While prior work has used classifiers for independence testing (X ⊥ Y), this paper extends the approach to the more challenging conditional setting by introducing a novel bootstrap method that addresses the Z-dependent permutation problem. The theoretical analysis is impressive, with Theorem 1 providing strong guarantees about the bootstrap samples and Theorem 2 extending generalization bounds to non-i.i.d settings.

The paper demonstrates significant empirical advantages over state-of-the-art methods. On synthetic data with high-dimensional Z (up to dimension 70), CCIT maintains near-perfect performance while KCIT and RCIT degrade significantly. On the flow-cytometry dataset, CCIT outperforms existing methods in all three causal graphs tested, even with low-dimensional conditioning sets. The authors provide a well-organized implementation (https://github.com/rajatsen91/CCIT) and thorough experimental validation.

Strengths:
- **Quality**: The theoretical contributions are rigorous and well-motivated, with strong guarantees on the bootstrap procedure and generalization bounds. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and clearly demonstrates the method's advantages.
- **Originality**: The model-powered approach to CI testing is novel, with the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure representing a significant technical contribution that advances beyond previous permutation-based methods [10].
- **Significance**: CI testing is fundamental to causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection. This method provides a practical, scalable solution that outperforms existing approaches, particularly in high-dimensional settings where previous methods struggle.
- **Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and accessible to practitioners. The main ideas are clearly explained, though some theoretical sections (e.g., the proof of Theorem 1) could benefit from more intuitive exposition. Minor presentation issues include redundant content in Section 1.1 and Algorithm 2 could be described more succinctly.

Weaknesses:
- The paper doesn't address how the method would extend to discrete variables (a question noted in the key points), which limits its applicability to certain domains.
- The sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters is not thoroughly explored. It's unclear whether the test results could be affected by the choice of classifier (e.g., XGBoost vs. neural networks) or hyperparameters, which could impact reproducibility.
- Some minor typographical errors were noted (e.g., "classifier" on line 157, "graphs" on line 280).

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of statistical testing by leveraging modern machine learning tools in a principled way. The theoretical guarantees, empirical validation, and practical implementation collectively demonstrate that model-powered approaches can significantly advance statistical testing. The connection to causal inference applications is particularly compelling, as CI testing is foundational to causal discovery.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel approach to CI testing that significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods
- Strong theoretical guarantees with well-justified assumptions
- Comprehensive empirical validation on both synthetic and real data
- Practical implementation that can be easily adopted by practitioners
- Clear connection to previous work and identification of how this work differs

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited discussion of discrete-variable extension
- Some theoretical sections could be clearer
- Minor presentation issues that could be easily fixed

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of statistical testing with broad applications in causal inference and machine learning. The model-powered approach is elegant, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the state of the art in a fundamental statistical problem while leveraging modern machine learning techniques. The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the writing is generally clear and accessible. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the significant contribution of this work. I recommend acceptance.
