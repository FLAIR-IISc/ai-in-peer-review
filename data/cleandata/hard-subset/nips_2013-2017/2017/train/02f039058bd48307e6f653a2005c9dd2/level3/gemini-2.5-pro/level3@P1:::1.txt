This paper proposes a novel method for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning. The core idea is to reframe the CI test `X ⊥ Y | Z` as a binary classification problem. The method consists of two main stages. First, given i.i.d. samples from the true joint distribution `f(x, y, z)`, a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is used to generate samples that approximate the distribution under the null hypothesis, `f_CI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)`. This is achieved by, for each sample `(x_i, y_i, z_i)`, finding its nearest neighbor `(x_j, y_j, z_j)` in Z-space and creating a new sample `(x_i, y_j, z_i)`. Second, a powerful classifier (e.g., XGBoost) is trained to distinguish the original samples (labeled 1) from the bootstrapped samples (labeled 0). The intuition is that if `X` and `Y` are truly conditionally independent, the two distributions will be indistinguishable, and the classifier's accuracy will be close to random guessing (0.5). If they are dependent, the classifier will achieve a significantly lower test error.

The paper provides significant theoretical support for this approach. It establishes a bound on the total variation distance between the distribution of the bootstrapped samples and the true conditional product distribution `f_CI`. Furthermore, it provides generalization bounds for the classifier's empirical risk, extending Rademacher complexity analysis to the "near-independent" samples produced by the bootstrap procedure. This work relates to a large body of literature on CI testing, particularly kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT, and permutation-based tests like KCIPT. The key distinctions are the novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap for generating null samples and the use of general-purpose, high-performance classifiers for the two-sample test component.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a problem of fundamental importance. The "model-powered" approach of reducing the statistical test to a classification task is elegant and powerful. It allows the well-developed machinery of modern supervised learning (e.g., gradient boosted trees, deep networks) to be brought to bear on this classical problem, which is a significant conceptual contribution. This approach appears particularly promising for high-dimensional conditioning sets `Z`, where many existing methods struggle.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The submission is technically sound and provides non-trivial theoretical guarantees. Theorem 1, which bounds the quality of the bootstrapped samples, provides a solid foundation for the entire method. Theorem 2, which analyzes the generalization error for the classifier in the presence of non-i.i.d. data, is a valuable contribution in its own right and demonstrates a careful theoretical treatment.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The core idea is explained intuitively, and the algorithms are presented clearly in pseudocode. The connection between the bootstrap procedure, the classification task, and the final hypothesis test is easy to follow.

4.  **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world flow cytometry data are impressive. The proposed method, CCIT, consistently outperforms state-of-the-art kernel-based methods, especially as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable `Z` increases.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

1.  **(Major Concern) The Choice of Threshold τ:** A critical component of any hypothesis test is the decision threshold, which determines the trade-off between Type I and Type II errors. In Algorithms 2 and 3, the threshold `τ` is presented as a parameter without a principled method for its selection. The paper states that `τ` needs to be greater than a bias term `b`, but it is unclear how to estimate `b` or otherwise choose `τ` to control the Type I error rate at a desired level (e.g., α = 0.05). The experiments mention using `τ = 1/√n`, but it is not clear if this choice has theoretical justification or how it relates to a specific significance level. A more detailed discussion on calibrating `τ`, perhaps via a permutation procedure on the test statistic itself, is necessary for the method to be a practical statistical test.

2.  **(Major Concern) Sensitivity to Weak Dependence:** The method's ability to detect dependence relies on the classifier distinguishing `f(x, y, z)` from the bootstrapped distribution, let's call it `ψ(x, y, z)`. From Theorem 1, we know `d_TV(ψ, f_CI) = O(1/n^(1/dz))`. This means even under the null hypothesis (`f = f_CI`), the two sets of samples given to the classifier are not from the same distribution. If the true conditional dependence is weak, it is possible that `d_TV(f, f_CI)` is smaller than `d_TV(ψ, f_CI)`. In such cases, the classifier might fail to distinguish `f` from `ψ`, leading to a false negative (failure to reject H0). Could the authors comment on the statistical power of their test and how the magnitude of detectable dependence relates to the approximation error of the bootstrap?

3.  **Modularity and Comparison of Components:** The proposed method can be seen as a two-part pipeline: (1) a sample generation module (NN-bootstrap) and (2) a two-sample test module (classification).
    *   How does the proposed NN-bootstrap compare to other methods for generating samples from `f_CI`, such as the permutation-based approach in Doran et al. [10]? A discussion or empirical comparison would strengthen the case for the proposed generator.
    *   Given the generated samples, one could use other two-sample tests instead of a classifier, such as a kernel-based MMD test. A comparison against this alternative would help isolate whether the performance gains come from the sample generation step, the classification step, or the combination.

4.  **Symmetry in X and Y:** Algorithm 1 is asymmetric: it resamples the `Y` coordinates based on nearest neighbors in `Z`. Is the test's performance invariant to swapping the roles of `X` and `Y`? In applications like causal discovery, the distinction between `X` and `Y` can be meaningful. The authors should clarify the rationale for choosing one variable over the other and whether this choice impacts the results.

5.  **(Minor Point) Pseudocode Clarity:** In Algorithms 2 and 3 (lines 6 and 4-5 respectively), the definition of the empirical risk `L_hat` is missing a normalization factor (i.e., division by the size of the dataset, `|D_r|` or `|D'_r|`). This should be corrected for clarity.

**Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, intuitive, and powerful framework for CI testing by reducing it to binary classification.
*   The method demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming established baselines, particularly in high dimensions.
*   The approach is supported by solid theoretical analysis, including bounds on the sample generation procedure and the classifier's generalization error.
*   The paper is well-written and clear.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method for choosing the decision threshold `τ` is not principled, which is a significant drawback for a formal hypothesis test.
*   Potential issues regarding the test's statistical power for weak dependencies are not addressed.
*   The paper lacks a comparison with alternative components (e.g., permutation sampling) that could be used within its modular framework.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a creative and promising approach to a difficult problem. The strengths in novelty, theory, and empirical results are compelling. However, the weakness regarding the selection of the threshold `τ` is a serious concern that impacts the practical applicability of the method as a rigorous statistical test. I would be inclined to recommend acceptance if the authors can provide a more satisfactory answer to this point in their rebuttal.
