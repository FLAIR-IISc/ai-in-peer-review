- Proposes reframing non-parametric conditional independence testing as a binary classification problem using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate f^{CI}(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Distinguishes bootstrapped vs. observed samples with a classifier; inability to beat random guessing implies failure to reject conditional independence.  
- Builds on classifier-based two-sample tests (Lopez-Paz et al., 2016) and contrasts with kernel-based CI methods like KCIT and RCIT.  
- Provides theoretical results: total variation bounds between the bootstrap and target distributions and generalization bounds for classifiers on non-i.i.d. generated samples.  
- Strength: elegant, modular reduction that lets practitioners leverage powerful supervised models; clear presentation and solid theoretical backing.  
- Major concern: modularity demands comparisons — paper lacks evaluation of NN-bootstrap against other generative procedures (e.g., permutation methods) and comparison of the classifier step to other two-sample tests (e.g., MMD) on the same generated data.  
- Major concern: potential low power for detecting weak dependencies — bootstrapped distribution may be too close to empirical data in finite samples, causing high false-negative rates.  
- Major concern: bootstrap is asymmetric (resamples Y conditioned on Z but leaves X unchanged); paper does not address test symmetry or how resampling choice affects results, important for causal interpretation.  
- Critical concern: decision threshold τ is chosen ad hoc and not well-justified; test validity and performance are highly sensitive to this parameter.  
- Overall: promising and original framework, but needs to address comparisons, sensitivity/power, symmetry, and principled parameter selection to be reliable in practice.
