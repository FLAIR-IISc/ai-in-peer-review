This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by converting the problem into a binary classification task. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to approximate the conditionally independent distribution, and then utilize powerful classifiers to distinguish between observed and bootstrapped samples. The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and the empirical risks of the classifiers, and demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach through extensive numerical experiments.

The paper addresses an important problem in statistics and machine learning, and the proposed approach is clearly presented. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the method, including bounds on the total variation between the nearest-neighbor bootstrapped distribution and the conditionally independent distribution, as well as bounds on the empirical risks for ideal classifiers and near-independent samples.

However, there are several major concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the method decomposes into two components: sample generation and classification. It is unclear whether existing alternatives, such as permutation-based generation or kernel two-sample tests, can replace one or both components and how their performance compares. A thorough comparison with existing methods would strengthen the paper.

Secondly, the method relies on the nearest-neighbor bootstrapped distribution being close to the conditionally independent distribution in finite samples. However, it is unclear how the method detects weak conditional dependence when the bootstrapped distribution is already close to the conditionally independent distribution. The authors should provide more insight into this issue and discuss potential limitations of the approach.

Thirdly, the method is not symmetric in x and y, and it is unclear how it handles causal asymmetry (x→y vs y→x) when deciding which variable to sample. The authors should discuss this issue and provide guidance on how to choose the correct variable to sample.

Lastly, the choice of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not clearly justified. The authors should provide more insight into the choice of this parameter and discuss potential alternatives.

In terms of minor issues, the empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be divided by the sample size (normalization) to ensure that the risk is properly scaled.

Overall, the paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence testing and provides a thorough analysis of the method. However, there are several major concerns that need to be addressed to strengthen the paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper addresses an important problem in statistics and machine learning.
* The proposed approach is clearly presented and provides a novel solution to the problem.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the method, including theoretical guarantees and empirical results.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The method decomposes into two components, and it is unclear whether existing alternatives can replace one or both components.
* The method relies on the nearest-neighbor bootstrapped distribution being close to the conditionally independent distribution in finite samples, but it is unclear how it detects weak conditional dependence.
* The method is not symmetric in x and y, and it is unclear how it handles causal asymmetry.
* The choice of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not clearly justified.

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field, but it requires significant revisions to address the major concerns mentioned above.
