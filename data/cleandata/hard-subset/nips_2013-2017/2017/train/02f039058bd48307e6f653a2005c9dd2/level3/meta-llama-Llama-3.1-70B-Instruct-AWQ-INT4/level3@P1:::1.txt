This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by converting it into a binary classification problem. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution, which can then be distinguished from the original samples using a powerful classifier. The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and the generalization bounds for the classification problem, making it a significant contribution to the field of non-parametric CI testing.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The introduction provides a thorough background on CI testing and its applications, and the problem setting is clearly defined. The authors also provide a detailed description of their algorithm, including the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure and the classification step.

One of the major strengths of the paper is its ability to tackle high-dimensional CI testing problems, which is a significant improvement over previous methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive numerical experiments, including synthetic and real-world datasets. The results show that their method outperforms existing state-of-the-art algorithms, including KCIT and RCIT.

However, there are some concerns and questions that need to be addressed. Firstly, the method decomposes into two components: sample generation and classification. It is unclear whether existing alternatives, such as permutation-based generation or kernel two-sample tests, can replace one component and how their performance compares. This is an important question, as it could potentially simplify the method and improve its performance.

Secondly, the method relies on the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution. However, it is unclear how the method detects weak conditional dependence when the bootstrapped distribution is already close to the conditionally independent distribution in finite samples. This is a concern, as it could lead to false negatives.

Thirdly, the method is not symmetric in x and y, and it is unclear how it handles causal asymmetry (x→y vs y→x) when deciding which variable to sample. This is an important consideration, as it could affect the accuracy of the method.

Finally, there is a serious concern about how to choose the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The authors provide some guidance on how to choose τ, but it is unclear whether this is sufficient. A more thorough investigation of the effect of τ on the performance of the method is needed.

In terms of minor issues, the empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be divided by the sample size (normalization) to ensure that the risk is properly scaled.
