This paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables by (i) generating proxy samples from the CI factorization fCI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap and (ii) reducing CI testing to a labeled two-sample classification problem that contrasts observed versus NN-bootstrapped samples. Intuitively, if CI holds, the classifier should perform no better than random; improved accuracy implies detectable deviation from fCI. The authors provide (a) bounds on the total variation distance between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and fCI under smoothness assumptions, and (b) generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization with “near-i.i.d.” samples, extending standard VC/Rademacher analyses to this setting. Empirically, the approach outperforms KCIT/RCIT and scales favorably in high-dimensional Z. This is closely related to prior NIPS work on kernel CI tests (e.g., KCIT), classifier two-sample tests, and learning-theoretic bounds for non-i.i.d. processes.

Quality: The paper is technically sound overall, with careful theory connecting the NN bootstrap to fCI and risk bounds under dependence. However, statistical calibration and detectability for weak dependencies warrant deeper analysis. Clarity: The exposition is clear and well-organized; assumptions and algorithms are explicit. Originality: The specific combination of NN-based conditional shuffling with a learning-reduction and near-i.i.d. generalization analysis is novel. Significance: CI testing is important for causal discovery and graphical models; the method is practical and potentially impactful.

Major comments
1) Modularity and alternatives: Since the method decomposes into sample generation and discrimination, please compare replacing (a) NN bootstrap with alternative conditional permutation strategies (e.g., KCIPT-style optimal matching/permutation) and (b) classifier with kernel two-sample tests (MMD). An ablation would clarify where gains arise.
2) Power for weak dependence: When the NN-bootstrapped distribution is already close to fCI in finite samples, what is the detectability boundary? Provide power analyses or lower bounds as a function of n, dz, and effect size; discuss whether n−1/dz pessimism is the true bottleneck in practice.
3) Symmetry and causal asymmetry: The algorithm swaps Y conditioned on Z. Is the test symmetric in X and Y|Z? Consider symmetrizing by running both swaps (Y|Z and X|Z) and combining p-values; discuss behavior under asymmetric causal mechanisms (X→Y vs Y→X).
4) Threshold τ: Choosing τ is critical. Beyond the heuristic τ≈1/√n, please provide a data-driven calibration (e.g., null distribution via conditional permutations/knn-resampling, cross-fitting, or conformal calibration) with type-I error control.

Minor
- Normalize empirical risks explicitly by sample size in Algorithms 2–3 (and ensure consistent notation).
- Discuss sensitivity to the Z-metric, choice of k in kNN, and feature scaling; report ablations.

Pros for acceptance: clear, novel reduction; solid theory; strong empirical results; practical and scalable. Cons: limited guidance on τ and calibration; unclear sensitivity/power in weak-dependence regimes; asymmetry not addressed; missing modular ablations.
