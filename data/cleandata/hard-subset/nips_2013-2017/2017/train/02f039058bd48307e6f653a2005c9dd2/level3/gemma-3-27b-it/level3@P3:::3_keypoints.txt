- Introduces a novel approach that reduces continuous conditional independence (CI) testing to a binary classification task.
- Generates samples approximating the conditional product distribution via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap and trains a classifier to distinguish joint vs. product samples.
- Builds on classifier two-sample tests and extends them to the conditional setting with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Well-written, accessible presentation with clear motivation and a logical algorithm description.
- Empirical evaluation on synthetic and real data shows the method outperforms existing CI tests in several scenarios.
- Theoretical results provide bounds on bootstrap sample accuracy and classifier generalization in a non-i.i.d. setting.
- Connects to causal inference (e.g., testing unconfoundedness), demonstrating practical relevance.
- Theoretical proofs are dense and may be difficult for readers not versed in statistical learning theory.
- Section 1.1 is somewhat redundant with the introduction.
- Algorithm 2 is verbose and could be made more concise by referencing the nearest-neighbor bootstrap directly.
- A few minor typographical errors (e.g., "classifier" line 157, "graphs" line 280).
- Open question: Is there a natural discrete-variable analog, and does discreteness simplify the bootstrap?
- Open question: Sensitivity of the test to classifier choice and hyperparameters—could different choices change outcomes?
- Open question: Need for a detailed analysis of computational cost, especially in high-dimensional settings despite nearest-neighbor speedups.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound approach with impressive theoretical guarantees and strong empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical complexity may limit accessibility; minor redundancy and presentation issues.
