The paper "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test" presents a novel approach to non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables. The authors reduce the problem of CI testing to a binary classification problem, leveraging the power of supervised learning models. This approach allows them to harness powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks, which can handle complex probability distributions.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples close to the conditional product distribution. The authors theoretically show that these generated samples are indeed close to the conditionally independent distribution in terms of total variational distance. They also provide generalization bounds for classification under near-independence, which translate into error bounds for CI testing.

The authors empirically validate the performance of their algorithm on simulated and real datasets, showing significant improvements over previous methods. The paper is well-written and accessible for practitioners, although the theoretical proofs can be challenging to follow. The authors acknowledge and connect to prior work, including "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests."

Strengths of the paper:

* The approach is innovative and leverages the power of supervised learning models to tackle a challenging problem in statistics.
* The theoretical guarantees are strong and impressive, extending classifier two-sample tests to the conditional setting.
* The empirical results are convincing, showing significant improvements over previous methods.

Weaknesses of the paper:

* The presentation could be improved. Section 1.1 is redundant, and Algorithm 2 could be described more succinctly.
* Minor typos are present throughout the paper (e.g., "classifier" on line 157 and "graphs" on line 280).
* The authors do not discuss the sensitivity of the test to the choice of classifier and hyperparameters, which could impact the accept/reject outcomes.
* The question of whether there is a discrete-variable analog and how the bootstrap performs when Z is discrete is not addressed.
