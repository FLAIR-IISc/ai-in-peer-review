This paper introduces a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing the problem as a binary classification task. The core idea is to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) using a novel nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure. These generated samples are then paired with original samples for training a classifier (e.g., gradient boosted trees or deep neural networks) to distinguish between the null hypothesis (CI holds) and the alternative. The authors contribute a theoretical analysis of the generated samples, demonstrating their closeness to fCI in terms of total variational distance (Theorem 1), and derive generalization bounds for the classification problem under non-i.i.d. near-independent samples.  Empirical validation on simulated and real datasets demonstrates performance gains over existing methods.

The work builds upon a growing body of literature leveraging machine learning techniques for statistical inference, notably related work employing classifiers for independence testing [19]. Existing non-parametric CI tests often rely on kernel methods [28, 32, 10].  Specifically, KCIPT [10] attempts to emulate fCI through a sample permutation, requiring solving a linear program, a computationally expensive step. The authors address this limitation with a simpler nearest-neighbor bootstrap, providing theoretical guarantees for its accuracy.  The approach distinguishes itself by explicitly addressing the challenge of generating samples from fCI, a crucial step absent or less rigorously addressed in prior work.  Reducing CI testing to binary classification allows the authors to leverage advancements in that field, creating a flexible and potentially powerful framework.

The strengths of this paper are significant. The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem is a compelling and conceptually elegant approach. The theoretical analysis, particularly Theorem 1 establishing the proximity of the bootstrapped samples to fCI and the subsequent generalization bounds, represents a substantial contribution. These bounds are especially noteworthy given the near-independence of the generated samples. The empirical results, demonstrating performance gains over existing methods on both synthetic and real datasets (flow cytometry data), provide support for the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.  The authors also demonstrate an awareness of practical considerations, providing a public GitHub repository for their implementation. The inclusion of extensive related work demonstrates a good understanding of the field.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses, most notably regarding clarity and interpretability. The presentation is dense and often lacks intuitive explanation. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical implications of the error bounds (section 1.1(iii)) is not fully elucidated. The authors mention a threshold ⌧ in Algorithm 2 but do not provide sufficient guidance on how to choose an appropriate value, leaving a practical gap in implementation. The discussion surrounding the bias correction in Algorithm 3 is also somewhat cursory. A key concern arises from the example given where r0 = 0.1. If the classifier class G is insufficiently expressive to distinguish f and fCI, how can a small error probability realistically be achieved? The paper doesn't fully address this potential limitation, and clarification is needed.  The large number of references, while demonstrating thoroughness, also makes the paper feel overwhelming.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of reducing CI testing to a classification problem, coupled with the novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap and its theoretical justification, is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis, particularly Theorem 1 and the subsequent generalization bounds, is rigorous and insightful.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm compared to existing methods.
*   **Potential Impact:** This work has the potential to advance the field of CI testing, providing a flexible and powerful framework for applications in causal inference, feature selection, and Bayesian network learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is dense and difficult to follow, hindering comprehension of the core ideas.
*   **Interpretability:** The connection between the theoretical results and practical implications is not fully explained.
*   **Practical Guidance:** Insufficient guidance is provided on practical aspects, such as choosing the threshold ⌧.
*   **Potential Limitations:** The paper does not adequately address the potential limitations of the approach when the classifier class G is too simple.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses in clarity, this paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to conditional independence testing. The reduction to a classification problem and the associated theoretical guarantees are significant contributions. While improvements in presentation and practical guidance would be beneficial, the strengths of the work outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity concerns and providing more detailed discussion on the limitations of the approach and practical implementation details. A more intuitive explanation of how the error bounds translate into practical performance would also be valuable.
