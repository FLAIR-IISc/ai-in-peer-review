### Summary

This paper introduces a novel, model-powered approach for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing. The core idea is to reduce the statistical hypothesis test of X ⊥ Y | Z to a binary classification problem. This is achieved by generating a synthetic dataset that approximates samples from the null distribution, f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), and then training a powerful classifier (e.g., XGBoost, deep neural networks) to distinguish these synthetic samples from the original, true data samples.

The main technical challenge, and a key contribution of this work, is a method for generating these synthetic null samples. The authors propose a "nearest-neighbor bootstrap" procedure: for each sample (x, y, z) in a held-out set, they find its nearest neighbor (x', y', z') in another set based on the z-coordinate, and create a new sample (x, y', z). The intuition is that if z is close to z', then y' is approximately a sample from f(Y|Z=z). The test statistic is derived from the classifier's performance on a test set; if the classifier can distinguish the two distributions with accuracy significantly better than 0.5, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is rejected.

The paper provides strong theoretical support for this approach. First, it proves that under certain smoothness assumptions on the conditional and marginal densities, the distribution of the bootstrapped samples is close to the true null distribution in total variation (TV) distance (Theorem 1). Second, it provides generalization bounds for the classifier's error rate (Theorem 2), extending Rademacher complexity analysis to the non-i.i.d. setting induced by the bootstrap procedure. This connects the classifier's empirical performance to the TV distance between the true and null distributions, justifying the test.

This work relates to a large body of literature on CI testing, most notably recent kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT. It also builds on the idea of using classifiers for two-sample testing, as seen in Lopez-Paz et al. (2016) for unconditional independence, but extends it to the more challenging conditional setting, which requires a novel sample generation scheme.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is grounded in a solid theoretical framework.
- The theoretical analysis is a major strength. Proving that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure generates samples whose distribution is provably close to the target null distribution (Theorem 1) is a non-trivial and crucial result. The novel smoothness assumption on the conditional density (Assumption 1), which generalizes the Fisher Information matrix, is insightful.
- Furthermore, the analysis of the classifier's generalization error in the resulting non-i.i.d setting (Theorem 2) is a strong contribution. It correctly identifies that the bootstrap procedure breaks the i.i.d. assumption and provides a rigorous analysis to show that the empirical risk still concentrates appropriately. This provides a complete theoretical justification for the entire pipeline.
- The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating superior performance against state-of-the-art methods, particularly as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases. This highlights the practical benefit of leveraging powerful, high-dimensional classifiers.

**Clarity:** This is the main weakness of the paper. The writing is very dense and often lacks intuition, making it difficult to follow.
- The paper would benefit significantly from more interpretation of its theoretical results. For example, the bounds in Theorem 1 and 2 are presented with little discussion of how the various terms (e.g., dimensions, smoothness constants) affect the final result in an intuitive way.
- A critical point that is not clearly discussed is the role of the classifier's function class, G. The theoretical analysis (specifically Theorem 2, part (ii)) seems to rely on the assumption that G is rich enough to contain a classifier close to the Bayes optimal one (captured by the term η). The paper does not address the case of model misspecification, where G might be too simple. For instance, if the true distributions are very distinguishable (e.g., Bayes risk r0 = 0.1), but the best classifier *in G* can only achieve an error of 0.4, the test would likely fail to reject the null hypothesis. A discussion of the sensitivity of the test to the choice and capacity of the classifier is needed to make the work more complete and accessible to practitioners.
- The bias correction procedure in Algorithm 3 is presented as a practical improvement but lacks theoretical analysis. While intuitive, it is not clear how this correction affects the statistical properties of the test, such as its Type I error rate.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
- The reduction of conditional independence testing to a classification problem via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a novel and powerful idea. While classifiers have been used for unconditional independence tests, the proposed method for generating samples from the conditional product distribution f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) is new and elegant.
- The theoretical analysis, particularly the bound on the TV distance for the bootstrap samples and the generalization bounds for non-i.i.d. "near-independent" samples, represents a novel technical contribution.

**Significance:** The paper has the potential for high impact.
- It provides a flexible and powerful framework for CI testing that can immediately benefit from advances in supervised learning. As classification models for high-dimensional data improve, so will this CI test.
- The strong empirical performance, especially in higher dimensions, suggests that this method could become a go-to tool for CI testing in practice, with applications in causal discovery and graphical modeling.
- The theoretical tools developed for analyzing learning with "near-independent" samples may be of independent interest to the learning theory community.

### Arguments for acceptance:
1.  **Novel and Powerful Framework:** The core idea of reducing CI testing to classification via a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap is elegant, original, and highly significant.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides two non-trivial theoretical results: a guarantee on the quality of the bootstrapped null samples (Theorem 1) and a learning-theoretic justification for the test statistic via generalization bounds in a non-i.i.d setting (Theorem 2).
3.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The proposed method, CCIT, is shown to outperform existing state-of-the-art CI tests, especially in settings with a higher-dimensional conditioning set Z, demonstrating its practical value.

### Arguments for rejection:
1.  **Poor Clarity and Lack of Intuition:** The paper is very difficult to read. It presents complex theoretical results with minimal interpretation, making it hard to grasp the key insights and limitations.
2.  **Unaddressed Limitations:** The paper fails to discuss the crucial issue of model misspecification (i.e., when the chosen classifier class is not powerful enough), which is a key practical concern for a method that relies entirely on a classifier's performance.
3.  **Heuristic Bias Correction:** The improved algorithm (CCITv2) relies on a bias correction step that is heuristic and lacks theoretical justification regarding its impact on the test's statistical properties.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty, theoretical depth, and strong empirical results, outweigh its weaknesses in clarity. It presents a significant contribution to the field of statistical testing and machine learning. I recommend acceptance, with a strong suggestion that the authors revise the paper to improve clarity and add a discussion on the practical implications of choosing the classifier class.
