- Proposes CCIT: reduce conditional independence testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating f^{CI}(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Implements classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees) to distinguish original from bootstrapped samples; strong classifier performance indicates conditional dependence.  
- Theoretical guarantee: nearest-neighbor bootstrap samples are close to f^{CI} in total variation distance (Theorem 1).  
- Novel generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), of independent interest.  
- Empirically outperforms state-of-the-art KCIT and RCIT on synthetic and real flow-cytometry datasets and scales better with dimensionality.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly presents problem, solution, and theory, and appropriately situates itself relative to kernel-based CI tests.  
- Originality: model-powered approach to CI testing is novel and leverages advances in classification effectively.  
- Significance: addresses a fundamental problem with potential impact on causal inference and structure learning.  
- Major weakness: inadequate comparison of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap to alternative methods (permutation-based or kernel two-sample tests) and no computational complexity justification for calling the bootstrap “more efficient.”  
- Major weakness: no discussion of detecting weak conditional dependence — unclear how small d_TV(f,f^{CI}) deviations are distinguished from independence.  
- Major weakness: method is asymmetric in X and Y (modifies Y using Z-nearest neighbors) with no explanation of consequences or how results change if X were modified instead.  
- Major weakness: threshold choice τ = 1/√n in Algorithms 2 and 3 is arbitrary and lacks theoretical justification tied to Type I error control.  
- Minor technical issue: empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by sample size to represent misclassification rate rather than raw count.  
- Recommendation: before acceptance, provide thorough comparisons to alternatives, derive a principled τ from theoretical bounds, address asymmetry and weak-dependence sensitivity, and fix the empirical-risk normalization.
