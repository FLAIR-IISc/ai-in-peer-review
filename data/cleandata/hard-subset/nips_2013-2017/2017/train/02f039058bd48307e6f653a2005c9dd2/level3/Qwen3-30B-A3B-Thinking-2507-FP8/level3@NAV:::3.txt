

This paper introduces a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The authors propose a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples close to the conditional product distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), then train a classifier to distinguish between these generated samples (labeled 0) and original joint samples (labeled 1). The key theoretical contribution is proving that the bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance (Theorem 1), and establishing generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). This work builds upon recent classifier-based two-sample tests (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016) while extending them to the conditional setting.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical guarantees, with the method outperforming state-of-the-art kernel-based approaches (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real flow-cytometry data. The empirical results show significant scalability with dimensionality of Z, which is particularly valuable for causal inference applications. The paper is well-structured and accessible to practitioners, though the theoretical proofs could be more transparent.

Strengths include impressive theoretical contributions that extend classifier two-sample tests to conditional independence, strong empirical validation, and clear practical utility for causal inference tasks. The authors properly acknowledge related work, particularly the "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" paper.

Weaknesses include presentation issues: Section 1.1 is redundant, and Algorithm 2 could be described more succinctly. The sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters isn't thoroughly explored—this could impact reproducibility. The authors should address whether their method has a discrete-variable analog, as this is an important extension. Minor typos (e.g., "classifier" on line 157) also need correction.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a theoretically grounded, practically useful CI test that leverages modern machine learning. The theoretical guarantees are strong, the empirical results are convincing, and the method has clear applications in causal discovery. With minor revisions to address the presentation issues and sensitivity analysis, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
