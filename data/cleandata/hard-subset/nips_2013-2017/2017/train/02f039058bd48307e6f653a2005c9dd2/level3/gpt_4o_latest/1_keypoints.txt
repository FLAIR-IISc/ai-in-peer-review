- Presents a novel model-based approach that reframes non-parametric CI testing as a binary classification task using nearest-neighbor bootstrap samples.  
- Key innovation: a nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure that generates samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution f^{CI}.  
- Theoretically: provides bounds on the closeness of the bootstrapped distribution to f^{CI} and generalization error bounds for classification with near-independent samples.  
- Empirically: outperforms state-of-the-art kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic and real-world datasets, especially in high dimensions.  
- Modularity: method is flexible, allowing use of powerful classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, deep nets) and is clearly presented.  
- Strength: introduces a fresh, theoretically grounded reduction of CI testing to classification with rigorous analysis.  
- Strength: demonstrates strong empirical performance and scalability to high-dimensional problems.  
- Weakness: modularity raises the unresolved question whether alternative components could replace the nearest-neighbor bootstrap or classifier for better performance.  
- Weakness: robustness to weak dependencies between x and y given z in finite samples is not thoroughly evaluated.  
- Weakness: assumes symmetry between x and y, which may limit applicability in causal scenarios with asymmetric relations.  
- Weakness: sensitivity of the algorithm to the parameter τ is not justified; lacks sensitivity analysis.  
- Minor issue: empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by sample size for consistency.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions contingent on addressing component substitution, weak-dependency handling, causal asymmetry, and parameter sensitivity concerns.
