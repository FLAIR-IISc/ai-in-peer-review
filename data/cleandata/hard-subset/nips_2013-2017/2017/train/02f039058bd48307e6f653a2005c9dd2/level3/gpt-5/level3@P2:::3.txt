Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables by reducing CI testing to a supervised binary classification problem. Given samples from f(x,y,z), the authors construct a synthetic dataset intended to follow fCI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) using a nearest-neighbor “bootstrap”: partition the data, for each u=(x,y,z) in one part find the 1-NN of z in the other part and swap in the neighbor’s y to form u′=(x,y0,z). A classifier is then trained to distinguish “real joint” samples from “synthetic CI” samples. High classification accuracy implies dependence (reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z), while near-chance accuracy implies conditional independence. A bias-corrected version subtracts a classifier trained only on (y,z) to mitigate finite-sample bias induced by the non-i.i.d. synthetic samples.

Theoretically, they show the distribution of a single bootstrapped sample is close to fCI in total variation under smoothness conditions, with a rate O(n−1/dz) up to log/constant factors. They further derive generalization bounds for ERM classifiers trained on the non-i.i.d. synthetic data, extending VC/Rademacher-style bounds to a “near-independent” setting. Empirically, the method outperforms kernel CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic post-nonlinear benchmarks and on flow cytometry protein-signaling data.

This work builds on classifier two-sample testing (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016) and prior CI tests based on kernels (KCIT, RCIT) and permutation-based conditional MMD (KCIPT), improving on the latter by avoiding a costly permutation LP and by providing TV-distance guarantees for the bootstrap. The non-i.i.d. generalization analysis connects to and complements results for mixing processes.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and flexibility: reduces CI testing to a robust supervised learning pipeline, enabling use of gradient-boosted trees or neural nets.
- Theoretical contributions: (i) TV-distance control between the NN-bootstrap distribution and fCI; (ii) generalization bounds for ERM with near-independent samples; (iii) bias-corrected testing scheme.
- Strong empirical performance and scalability, especially as dim(Z) increases, with code provided.
- Clear positioning vis-à-vis KCIT/RCIT/KCIPT and classifier two-sample testing.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Finite-sample level control: the procedure uses a threshold τ (and a bias-corrected loss difference), but the paper does not provide guarantees on type-I error at a prespecified α or a principled way to set τ; ROC/AUC reporting sidesteps the testing-level question.
- Sensitivity to classifier choice/hyperparameters is not systematically studied; performance and acceptance decisions may vary with model class, regularization, and tuning procedures.
- Assumptions and rates: the TV bound scales as n−1/dz, reflecting a curse of dimensionality in Z; while practice looks better, guidance on when assumptions hold and how constants behave is limited.
- Non-i.i.d. bootstrap samples: while analyzed, the dependence structure and constants in the generalization bounds are not fully transparent; practical implications for confidence levels remain unclear.
- Minor presentation issues: some redundancy (Section 1.1) and overly verbose algorithm descriptions; a few typos.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is carefully justified under smoothness of f(y|z) and f(z). The generalization analysis for near-independent samples is a meaningful and nontrivial extension of standard classification bounds. Empirical comparisons are fair and show substantial gains. However, the lack of finite-sample level calibration (p-values or α-controlled tests) is a notable gap for a hypothesis testing method.

Clarity
Generally clear and accessible, with helpful algorithmic descriptions and references. Proofs are dense and could be streamlined; Section 1.1 repeats material from the intro; Algorithm 2/3 can be tightened. Some additional guidance on hyperparameter choices and τ selection would improve reproducibility.

Originality
High. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap construction for CI, with TV guarantees, and the generalization bounds under near-independence appear novel. The reduction to classification is known for independence and two-sample settings, but the conditional extension with theory is original and valuable.

Significance
High practical relevance for causal discovery, graphical models, and feature selection. The framework is likely to be adopted and extended, given its simplicity and empirical strength. The theoretical techniques for near-independent generalization may be of independent interest.

Questions for the authors
- Discrete and mixed variables: How would the method adapt when Z (or X/Y) is discrete or mixed? Would within-stratum permutation (for discrete Z) give exact fCI and stronger tests?
- Classifier sensitivity: How robust are decisions across different classifiers, architectures, and hyperparameters? Do you recommend a default (e.g., XGBoost with specified settings)?
- Calibration: Can you provide a permutation or sample-splitting scheme to estimate the null distribution of the test statistic and deliver p-values/α-controlled tests? How should τ be set in practice?
- k-NN vs. 1-NN: Would k-NN matching or local averaging reduce variance/bias and improve finite-sample behavior, especially when fZ is low in some regions?
- Distance metrics: Any guidance on metric choice for Z when features have different scales or are manifold-structured?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a calibration procedure (e.g., label permutation between U1 and U′2 or cross-fitting) to obtain p-values and control type-I error.
- Add a sensitivity analysis across classifiers/hyperparameters and default recommendations.
- Discuss k-NN variants and robustness to the choice of distance metric in Z.
- Streamline Section 1.1 and compress Algorithm 2/3 descriptions; move some details to the appendix.
- Expand on the practical implications of the near-i.i.d. generalization bounds (constants, sample sizes).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and extensible method with strong empirical gains.
- Solid and novel theoretical guarantees bridging CI bootstrapping and classification generalization under dependence.
- High relevance to NeurIPS community (causal inference, graphical models, testing with modern ML).

Cons for acceptance
- No principled level calibration or p-value computation provided.
- Theoretical rates deteriorate with dim(Z) and rely on smoothness/regularity that may be hard to verify.
- Limited analysis of classifier/hyperparameter sensitivity.

Minor typos
- “classifier” (line 157) and “graphs” (line 280) need correction.

Overall
A strong and timely contribution that advances CI testing by leveraging modern classifiers with new supporting theory. Addressing test-level calibration and classifier sensitivity would further strengthen the work. I lean toward acceptance.
