This paper presents a novel and promising approach to non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables. The core idea is to reduce the CI test to a binary classification problem by constructing samples approximating the conditional product distribution and then leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees or deep neural networks. This approach offers a significant advantage in high-dimensional settings where traditional methods struggle. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and empirical results.

The paper builds upon the growing trend of model-powered statistical inference, specifically relating to recent work on reducing independence testing to classification (referenced as [19]). Unlike previous kernel-based approaches like KCIT [32] and RCIT [28], which often involve computationally expensive procedures (e.g., solving linear programs [10]), this method proposes an efficient nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. The theoretical contributions are particularly noteworthy, providing guarantees on the bootstrapped samples in terms of total variational distance (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for classification under near-independence (Theorem 2). These bounds extend existing results for classifier two-sample tests to the more challenging conditional setting.

The strengths of this work are numerous. First, the proposed method is conceptually simple yet effective. By framing CI testing as a classification task, it naturally benefits from the advances in machine learning. Second, the theoretical analysis provides a strong foundation for the method, establishing its validity and providing insights into its performance. The generalization bounds are especially valuable, offering guarantees on the error rate of the CI test. Third, the empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach on both synthetic and real datasets, showing performance gains over state-of-the-art methods, particularly as the dimensionality of Z increases. Finally, the practical relevance to causal inference tasks, such as testing unconfoundedness, further enhances the significance of this work.

However, there are also some weaknesses to consider. While the paper acknowledges the dependence on the choice of classifier, a more in-depth analysis of the sensitivity of the test to different classifiers and their hyperparameters would be beneficial. Could different choices lead to inconsistent accept/reject decisions? Exploring this further could strengthen the robustness argument. Section 1.1 feels somewhat redundant, reiterating information already presented in the introduction.  Algorithm 2 could be described more concisely, focusing on the core steps and omitting unnecessary details. While the theoretical analysis is impressive, the proofs can be dense and challenging to follow for readers unfamiliar with the specific mathematical tools employed. A more intuitive explanation of the key ideas behind the proofs could improve accessibility. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem, coupled with the nearest-neighbor bootstrap, is a novel and effective approach.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and classification error provide a strong foundation for the method.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The method outperforms existing CI tests on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   **Practical Significance:** The method has clear applications in causal inference and other areas where CI testing is crucial.
*   **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** Limited investigation of the sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters.
*   **Theoretical Accessibility:** The theoretical proofs are dense and may be inaccessible to some readers.
*   **Minor Presentation Issues:** Section 1.1 is redundant and Algorithm 2 could be more concise.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of non-parametric CI testing. The proposed method is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically effective. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  The paper addresses a difficult problem in a clever and insightful manner, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above regarding classifier sensitivity and presentation clarity. I would rate this as a strong accept.
