This paper proposes a novel approach to non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing by converting the problem into a binary classification task. The authors leverage the power of supervised learning models, such as gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks, to tackle high-dimensional CI testing. The paper is well-written, and the ideas are clearly presented.

The key contribution of the paper is the development of a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples that mimic the conditionally independent distribution. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the total variational distance between the generated samples and the conditionally independent distribution. They also derive generalization bounds for classification under near-independence, which translates into error bounds for CI testing.

The paper addresses an important problem in the field of machine learning and statistics, and the proposed approach is novel and well-motivated. The authors provide extensive empirical evaluations of their algorithm, comparing it to state-of-the-art methods, and demonstrate its effectiveness in various settings.

However, there are some concerns and open questions that need to be addressed. Firstly, the method decomposes into sample generation and classification, and it is unclear whether existing alternatives, such as permutation-based generation or kernel two-sample tests, can replace one component and how their performance compares. Secondly, the method may struggle to detect weak conditional dependence when the nearest-neighbor bootstrapped distribution is already close to the conditionally independent distribution in finite samples. Thirdly, the method may not be symmetric in x and y, and it is unclear how it handles causal asymmetry (x→y vs y→x) when deciding which variable to sample. Finally, there is a serious concern about how to choose the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3.

In terms of the review criteria, the paper scores well on quality, clarity, and significance. The technical contributions are sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The paper is clearly written, and the ideas are well-organized and easy to follow. The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses an important problem in the field and provides a novel solution.

However, the originality of the paper is somewhat limited, as it builds upon existing work on classification-based testing and kernel methods. The paper does not provide a significant departure from previous contributions, but rather a novel combination of familiar techniques.

In conclusion, this paper proposes a novel approach to non-parametric CI testing by converting the problem into a binary classification task. While the paper has some limitations and open questions, it is well-written, and the ideas are clearly presented.
