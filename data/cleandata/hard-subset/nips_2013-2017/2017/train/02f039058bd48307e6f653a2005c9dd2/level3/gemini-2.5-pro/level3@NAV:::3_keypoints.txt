- Proposes CCIT: a novel nonparametric conditional independence test that reduces CI testing to a binary classification task.
- Generates "negative" samples approximating f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap and uses classifier test error as the test statistic.
- Builds on classifier two-sample test framework and extends it to the conditional setting.
- Theoretical contributions: guarantees on closeness of bootstrapped samples in total variation and new generalization bounds for classifiers on near-independent samples.
- Empirically outperforms kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT), especially as dimensionality of Z increases.
- Main strength: originality and practical significance by enabling use of modern supervised models for CI testing.
- Presentation issues: theoretical sections can be hard to follow; Section 1.1 feels redundant and Algorithm 2 could be more concise.
- Practical concern: sensitivity to choice of classifier and hyperparameters—needs discussion on how choices affect test conclusions.
- Question about extension to discrete variables, where neighbor-matching bootstrap might be even more effective.
- Minor typos noted: "classifier" on line 157 and "graphs" on line 280.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, theoretically sound, empirically strong, and likely influential contribution to causality and ML.
