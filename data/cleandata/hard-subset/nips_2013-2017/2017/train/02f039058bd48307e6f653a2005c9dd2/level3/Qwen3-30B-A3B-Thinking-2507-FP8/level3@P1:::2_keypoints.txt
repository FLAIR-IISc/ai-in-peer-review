- Proposes reducing conditional independence (CI) testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate f_CI samples.  
- Key procedure: label bootstrap samples 0 and original samples 1, train a classifier, reject H0 if classifier accuracy is significantly above random.  
- Theorem 1: bootstrap samples approximate f_CI in total variation distance O(1/n^(1/d_z)) under smoothness assumptions.  
- Theorem 2: novel Rademacher-complexity-style generalization bounds for classification with near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples.  
- Empirical results show clear advantages over KCIT and RCIT, notably in high-dimensional settings.  
- Main strength: strong technical novelty and solid theoretical foundation linking classification error to TV distance.  
- Smoothness assumptions are well-motivated and enable meaningful bounds on bootstrap quality.  
- Presentation and exposition are dense; theoretical results lack intuitive explanations and examples.  
- Insufficient explanation of why nearest-neighbor bootstrap works in simple terms and how bounds translate to practice.  
- Major concern: interpretation of classifier error bounds under H0 — unclear how to choose classifier class G so optimal error is near 0.5.  
- Algorithmic clarity issues: bias correction in Algorithm 3 and the choice/role of threshold τ are not well explained.  
- Lacks practical guidance on which classifiers to use and how to tune them for CI testing.  
- Significance: method is generalizable to other testing problems and demonstrates real-world utility (flow-cytometry).  
- Recommendation: revise to improve clarity, provide intuition/examples, and address classifier-selection and error-interpretation concerns.
