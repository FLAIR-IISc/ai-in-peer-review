## Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables, framing the problem as a classification task. The core idea – generating samples approximating the conditional product distribution via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap and then using a classifier to distinguish these from joint samples – is a clever and promising one. This allows leveraging the power of modern classifiers (like deep neural nets) for a traditionally challenging statistical problem. The authors demonstrate strong theoretical guarantees for their bootstrap procedure and provide empirical validation on both synthetic and real data, showing performance gains over existing methods. 

The work builds upon the recent trend of reducing statistical inference problems to classification, notably referencing “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests.” However, it extends this idea to the more complex conditional setting, which is a significant contribution. The theoretical analysis, particularly the generalization bounds in the presence of non-i.i.d. samples generated by the bootstrap, is impressive and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying statistical principles. The paper is generally well-written and accessible, making it valuable for practitioners interested in causal inference and related fields. 

However, the theoretical proofs are dense and can be difficult to follow without a strong background in statistical learning theory. While the authors acknowledge this, improving the clarity of these sections would broaden the paper’s impact.  A key strength is the practical relevance to causal inference, specifically testing unconfoundedness, which is explicitly mentioned. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of framing CI testing as a classification problem with a novel bootstrap procedure is innovative.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Strong theoretical guarantees are provided for both the bootstrap sampling and the classification performance.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The method outperforms existing CI tests on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   **Practical Significance:** The technique has direct applications in causal inference, a crucial area of research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Accessibility:** The theoretical proofs are challenging to follow for readers without a specialized background.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough investigation into the sensitivity of the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters. This is a crucial point, as different classifiers could potentially lead to different conclusions.
*   **Presentation Issues:** Section 1.1 is somewhat redundant, and Algorithm 2 could be presented more concisely.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Is there a discrete-variable analog to this method? Would bootstrapping become more powerful when Z is discrete, potentially simplifying the process?
*   How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and hyperparameters? Could different choices significantly alter the accept/reject outcomes, and if so, how can this be mitigated?

**Overall:**

Despite minor presentation issues and the need for further investigation into classifier sensitivity, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of CI testing. The combination of theoretical rigor, empirical validation, and practical relevance makes it a worthwhile addition to the conference. I recommend acceptance.
