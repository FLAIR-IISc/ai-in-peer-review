This paper proposes a novel, non-parametric method for Conditional Independence (CI) testing, which is a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning. The core idea is to reduce the CI test H0: X ⊥ Y | Z to a binary classification problem. This is achieved by generating a synthetic dataset that approximates the conditionally independent distribution f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). The main technical contribution is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure for generating these synthetic samples. Given samples from the true joint distribution f(x, y, z), the procedure matches each sample (x_i, y_i, z_i) with a nearest neighbor (x_j, y_j, z_j) in the Z-space and swaps their y-coordinates to create a new sample (x_i, y_j, z_i).

These synthetic samples are assigned label 0, while an independent set of true samples from f(x, y, z) is assigned label 1. A powerful classifier (e.g., gradient boosted trees, deep neural networks) is then trained to distinguish between these two classes. If the classifier achieves an accuracy significantly better than random guessing (0.5), it implies the two distributions are distinguishable, and the null hypothesis of conditional independence is rejected. This work extends the recent trend of using classifiers for two-sample testing (e.g., Lopez-Paz et al., 2016 for unconditional independence) to the more complex conditional setting. It differs from prior kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) by leveraging the power and scalability of modern classifiers.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical contributions are particularly strong. Theorem 1 provides an impressive guarantee that the distribution of the bootstrapped samples converges to the target conditionally independent distribution in total variation distance, under reasonable smoothness assumptions. This formally justifies the core mechanism of the proposed test.

Furthermore, the authors provide a novel analysis of Rademacher complexity bounds for classification with the "near-independent" samples generated by their bootstrap procedure (Theorem 2). Standard generalization bounds assume i.i.d. data, an assumption violated by the nearest-neighbor matching. Extending these bounds to the non-i.i.d. setting is a valuable contribution of independent interest.

The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing the proposed method (CCIT) against established baselines (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real-world data. The results convincingly show that CCIT outperforms previous methods, especially as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases, demonstrating the practical advantage of leveraging high-dimensional classifiers. The authors also thoughtfully propose a bias-corrected version of their algorithm (CCITv2) to handle finite-sample effects, which strengthens the practical applicability of the method.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-written and the main algorithm is presented in an accessible manner, making it understandable for practitioners. The motivation is clear, and the overall structure is logical. The authors do a good job of situating their work within the existing literature.

There are, however, some minor areas for improvement in the presentation:
- Section 1.1, "Main Contributions," is somewhat redundant as it largely repeats points already made in the introduction. This section could be integrated more seamlessly into the main text to improve flow.
- The description of Algorithm 2 could be presented more succinctly.
- Some minor typos were noted: "classier" should be "classifier" (line 157, in Algorithm 2 caption), and "graph" should be "graphs" in the caption for Table 2c (line 280 in the original PDF).

**Originality**
The work is highly original. While the idea of using classifiers for two-sample tests exists, this paper is the first to successfully and rigorously extend this paradigm to the challenging problem of *conditional* independence testing. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is a novel and clever technique to generate samples from the required conditional product distribution, which is a key obstacle in this setting. The theoretical analysis for non-i.i.d. generalization is also a novel contribution. The paper clearly articulates how it differs from and builds upon prior work, especially kernel-based methods and unconditional classifier-based tests.

**Significance**
The significance of this work is high. Conditional independence testing is a cornerstone of many machine learning and statistical tasks, including causal discovery, graphical model structure learning, and feature selection. The proposed "model-powered" approach provides a powerful, flexible, and scalable tool that is likely to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners. By framing the problem as a classification task, the method can directly benefit from ongoing advances in supervised learning models, ensuring its continued relevance. The demonstrated empirical superiority, particularly in higher dimensions, suggests it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Impact:** The reduction of CI testing to classification is an elegant and powerful idea with significant practical implications.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical justification for its core components, including convergence of the bootstrap procedure and generalization bounds for the resulting classifier.
3.  **Superior Empirical Performance:** The experiments clearly show the method outperforming state-of-the-art baselines, especially in challenging high-dimensional settings.
4.  **Broad Applicability:** The method provides a valuable tool for numerous fields that rely on CI testing, such as causal inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Sensitivity to Classifier Choice:** The test's performance may depend on the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters, which could introduce variability into the results. The paper could benefit from a discussion on this sensitivity.
2.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** As noted above, the clarity could be slightly improved in a few places.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated method for a fundamental statistical problem.

**Questions for Authors:**
1.  The test relies on the choice of a classifier (e.g., XGBoost). How sensitive is the test's outcome (the decision to accept or reject H0) to the specific classifier used and the tuning of its hyperparameters? This seems to introduce a degree of freedom that is not present in traditional statistical tests.
2.  The paper focuses on continuous variables. Could you comment on how this framework might extend to discrete or mixed-type variables? In particular, if Z is discrete, the nearest-neighbor search simplifies to exact matching. Does the bootstrap procedure become more powerful or even exact in that setting?
