This paper introduces a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables, framing the problem as a binary classification task. The core idea – generating samples approximating the conditional product distribution via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap and then training a classifier to distinguish joint vs. product samples – is clever and addresses a key challenge in non-parametric CI testing. This work builds upon recent advances in classifier two-sample tests (specifically referencing “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests”) and extends them to the conditional setting, offering strong theoretical guarantees.

The paper is generally well-written and accessible, particularly for practitioners familiar with machine learning techniques. The motivation is clearly articulated, and the algorithm is presented in a logical manner. The empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real data demonstrates the method’s effectiveness, outperforming existing CI tests in several scenarios. The theoretical results are impressive, providing bounds on the accuracy of the bootstrapped samples and generalization error of the classifier. The connection to causal inference – specifically testing unconfoundedness – highlights the practical relevance of this work.

However, the theoretical proofs are indeed dense and may be challenging to follow for readers not deeply versed in statistical learning theory. While the authors acknowledge this, simplifying or providing more intuitive explanations of key derivations would be beneficial. Additionally, Section 1.1 feels somewhat redundant, restating information already presented in the introduction. Algorithm 2 could also be described more succinctly, perhaps by directly referencing the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure instead of repeating its steps. Minor typos exist ("classifier" line 157 and "graphs" line 280).

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem is a significant contribution, allowing for the leverage of powerful modern classifiers.
* **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The theoretical analysis provides rigorous justification for the method and establishes generalization bounds in a non-i.i.d setting.
* **Empirical Performance:** The method demonstrably outperforms existing CI tests on both synthetic and real data.
* **Practical Relevance:** The application to causal inference tasks, such as testing unconfoundedness, highlights the method's practical value.
* **Clear Writing:** Generally well-written and accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Complexity:** The theoretical proofs are difficult to follow.
* **Redundancy:** Section 1.1 is somewhat redundant.
* **Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 2 could be more concise.
* **Minor Typos:** A few minor typographical errors exist.

**Open Questions/Areas for Consideration:**

* **Discrete Variable Analog:** Is there a natural discrete-variable analog to this approach? Does the bootstrap procedure become more powerful when Z is discrete, potentially simplifying the sample generation process?
* **Sensitivity Analysis:** How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters? Could different choices lead to different accept/reject outcomes? A more thorough investigation of this aspect would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention efficiency gains from fast nearest neighbor algorithms, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost, particularly in high dimensions, would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in statistical inference.
* The theoretical guarantees are impressive and contribute to our understanding of CI testing in a non-parametric setting.
* The empirical results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the method.
* The paper is well-written and addresses a relevant problem with clear implications for causal inference.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical complexity might limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
* The minor issues with redundancy and algorithm description, while not critical, detract slightly from the overall presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of non-parametric CI testing. The novel approach, strong theoretical foundations, and promising empirical results warrant acceptance. Addressing the minor weaknesses and clarifying the theoretical aspects would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
