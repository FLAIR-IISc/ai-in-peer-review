- Converts conditional independence testing for continuous variables into a binary classification problem using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate the CI product distribution.  
- Algorithm 1 generates bootstrap samples approximating f_CI(x,y,z) to serve as one class, with original samples as the other; test based on classifier error.  
- Theoretical contributions: proves bootstrap samples are close in total variation (Theorem 1) and gives generalization bounds for classifiers on non-i.i.d. samples (Theorem 2).  
- Builds on classifier two-sample tests and addresses limitations of kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) in high-dimensional Z.  
- Empirically outperforms state-of-the-art methods on synthetic and real datasets, especially when Z is high-dimensional.  
- Demonstrates practical utility on protein signaling network data for causal inference.  
- Strength: original, model-powered approach that leverages supervised learning advances for statistical testing.  
- Strength: strong theoretical grounding and technically sound analysis of TV distance and generalization.  
- Strength: thorough empirical evaluation with attention to Type I and Type II errors.  
- Strength: well-written and accessible with clear algorithm and theory exposition.  
- Weakness: Section 1.1 is redundant; Algorithm 2 and its bias correction could be more concise.  
- Weakness: minor typos (e.g., “classifier” line 157, “graphs” line 280) and appendix proofs may be hard for some readers.  
- Key question: Is there a discrete-variable analog, and how to handle discrete Z where nearest-neighbor bootstrap may fail?  
- Key question: How sensitive is the test to classifier choice and hyperparameters—need for sensitivity analysis.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept — significant, novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated; meets quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria.
