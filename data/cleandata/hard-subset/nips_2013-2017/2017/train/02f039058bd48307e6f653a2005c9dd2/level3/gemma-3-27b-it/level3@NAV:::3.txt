## Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables, framing the problem as a classification task. The core idea – constructing samples approximating the conditional product distribution via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap and then using a classifier to distinguish these from joint samples – is a clever and potentially powerful one. The method offers a promising avenue for CI testing, particularly in high-dimensional settings where traditional methods struggle. The authors convincingly position their work within the broader context of causal inference and relate it to existing literature, notably “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests.”

A key strength lies in the theoretical guarantees provided. Extending classifier two-sample tests to the conditional setting is a significant achievement and demonstrates a strong theoretical understanding. The analysis of Rademacher complexity bounds in the presence of non-i.i.d samples is also impressive and adds considerable value. The empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real data provides supporting evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing performance gains over existing techniques like KCIT and RCIT. The practical applicability to causal inference tasks, such as testing unconfoundedness, further enhances the paper’s significance.  The code availability is also a major plus.

However, the paper isn't without its weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the proofs are dense and challenging to follow, potentially limiting accessibility for some readers. The authors acknowledge this, but improving clarity in this area would be beneficial.  Regarding the questions raised by the supervisor, the lack of discussion on a discrete-variable analog is a notable omission. Exploring how bootstrap performance might change with a discrete Z is a relevant area for future work. Similarly, a more thorough sensitivity analysis regarding the choice of classifier and hyperparameters is needed.  The authors should demonstrate that the results are robust to these choices and don’t critically depend on specific configurations. 

Minor presentation issues, like the redundancy in Section 1.1 and the somewhat verbose Algorithm 2, also detract slightly from the overall reading experience. Finally, the typos (“classifier” and “graphs”) should be corrected.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to CI testing.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees and a rigorous analysis.
*   Empirically validated performance gains over existing methods.
*   Potential for practical impact in causal inference.
*   Well-written and accessible for practitioners (with the exception of the proofs).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Proofs are difficult to follow.
*   Lack of discussion on a discrete-variable analog.
*   Limited sensitivity analysis concerning classifier choice.
*   Minor presentation issues and typos.



**Overall:** Despite the minor drawbacks, this is a high-quality paper with significant potential.  I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the concerns regarding the clarity of the proofs and the sensitivity analysis. The contribution is substantial, and the work is likely to be influential in the field of causal inference and beyond.
