Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables by reducing CI testing to supervised binary classification. The key novelty is a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that, given 2n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, constructs n synthetic samples whose distribution is provably close in total variation (TV) to the conditional product distribution fCI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). The test then trains a classifier to distinguish the held-out real samples (label 1) from NN-bootstrapped samples (label 0). High accuracy implies f ≠ fCI and rejection of H0: X ⫫ Y | Z; low accuracy implies failure to reject H0. A bias-corrected variant subtracts a baseline classifier trained only on (Y,Z) to mitigate finite-sample bias from the NN bootstrap.

The theoretically oriented contributions are: (i) under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z), a TV bound between the bootstrap distribution and fCI of order O(n−1/dz) (Theorem 1); (ii) generalization bounds for ERM under near-independent samples (due to the bootstrap’s dependence), adapting VC/Rademacher-style analysis to this setting (Theorem 2). Empirically, the method (with XGBoost) substantially outperforms kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic post-nonlinear noise settings and shows strong ROC AUC on flow cytometry protein-signaling data.

The approach relates to prior kernel-based CI tests [KCIT/RCIT, KCIPT], classifier two-sample testing for independence via permutation [Lopez-Paz & Oquab], and non-i.i.d. generalization analyses [Mohri & Rostamizadeh]. Contrary to KCIPT’s permutation via a costly LP, this paper’s NN bootstrap is simple and efficient, and accompanied by a TV guarantee.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean reduction from CI testing to classification with a practical and efficient NN bootstrap. The reduction can leverage advances in modern classifiers.
- Theorem 1’s TV guarantee under explicit smoothness assumptions is technically non-trivial and useful; the nearest-neighbor construction is intuitive and well-motivated.
- The generalization results for near-independent samples are valuable beyond this application and bridge a gap between i.i.d. ERM theory and the dependence induced by the bootstrap.
- Strong empirical performance and scalability, with open-source code.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity: Several core ideas are hard to follow on first read. Assumption 1 (the generalized curvature/Fisher-like condition on f(y|z)) is non-standard and needs more intuition and examples. The roles of constants and the G(ε) terms in Theorem 1/2 obscure when the bounds become meaningful in practice.
- Power depends critically on classifier capacity G and approximation to the Bayes optimal rule. Section 1.1(iii)/Theorem 2(ii) involves r0 = 0.5(1−dTV(f,fCI)) and an achievability gap η, but it remains unclear how small η can be for realistic G and n. If G is too simple, the test may have low power even when r0 ≪ 0.5 (e.g., your r0 = 0.1 example); please clarify conditions on G (capacity, calibration, training) under which the gap to Bayes risk is controlled.
- Statistical calibration: The procedure is presented as a test but does not provide size control or p-values. Using a fixed threshold τ (e.g., 1/√n) does not guarantee level-α control. A permutation/Monte Carlo calibration with repeated bootstrap samples under an estimated null would strengthen the testing interpretation.
- Curse of dimensionality in Z: The TV rate O(n−1/dz) is pessimistic; while experiments scale well, the theory suggests bias may dominate for large dz. Discuss practical choices (e.g., k-NN with k>1, distance-weighted swapping, metric learning for Z) and provide sensitivity analyses.
- Dependence structure: While the near-independence analysis is appreciated, the magnitude of the o(n(1/ε))/n term and the choice/tradeoff of ε are not transparent. A simple corollary with explicit, usable constants/rates would help.
- Empirics: More ablations would improve credibility: effect of the classifier choice (e.g., DNNs vs. GBT), k in k-NN, sample size, Z-dimension, and Type I error under H0. On flow cytometry, the ground truth is uncertain; consider also synthetic data with known CI structure to report controlled FPR/TPR.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with novel and carefully reasoned proofs for the bootstrap TV bound and near-i.i.d. generalization. Some assumptions (smoothness, Lipschitz f(z)) may be restrictive but are stated. Experimental methodology is reasonable, though formal hypothesis-testing calibration is missing.

Clarity
- The paper would benefit from better organization and intuition:
  - Provide a high-level sketch for Theorem 1, with a figure explaining why swapping Y with the 1-NN in Z approximates sampling from f(y|z).
  - Interpret Assumption 1 in familiar terms (e.g., local Lipschitzness/Bregman curvature) and give concrete distributions that satisfy it.
  - In Theorem 2, restate bounds with explicit constants and a recommended ε to yield a readable big-O rate.
  - Clarify Sec. 1.1(iii): define hat R and η precisely, and describe how to choose G/training to ensure small η.

Originality
- The reduction to classification is known for two-sample and independence testing, but the NN bootstrap for CI together with a TV guarantee and the near-independent generalization analysis is, to my knowledge, novel and non-trivial.

Significance
- Practical and potentially impactful: easy to implement, scalable, and shows strong performance relative to popular kernel CI tests. The generalization analysis under near-independence could be useful in other learning-reduction pipelines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a calibrated testing procedure (p-values/critical values) via repeated NN bootstrap under H0-like resampling, reporting Type I error control.
- Discuss and evaluate the effect of classifier capacity G and training (e.g., XGBoost depth/trees, regularization), including scenarios where G is deliberately misspecified.
- Explore k>1 nearest neighbors or distance-weighted swaps; provide empirical bias-variance trade-offs vs. k.
- Provide more interpretation and examples for Assumption 1; relate it to Fisher information bounds in standard models.
- Include ablations on dz, n, and classifier choice; report runtime and memory vs. KCIT/RCIT.
- Minor: fix typos, unify notation, and add a brief “practitioner’s guide” for choosing τ, G, k, and sample splits.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-justified NN bootstrap for CI with a TV bound.
- Generalization bounds under near-independence are of independent interest.
- Strong empirical results and a simple, flexible implementation leveraging modern classifiers.

Arguments against acceptance
- Clarity issues impede accessibility; key assumptions and bounds are hard to interpret.
- Lack of formal test calibration and limited analysis of Type I error.
- Theoretical rates degrade with dz, and the link between Bayes risk gap and realizable classifier classes is underexplained.

Overall recommendation
- Lean accept, contingent on improved exposition and adding a calibrated testing procedure or, at minimum, an empirical Type I error study. This work is technically solid, practically useful, and likely to influence follow-up research and applications.

Reviewer confidence
- Moderate.
