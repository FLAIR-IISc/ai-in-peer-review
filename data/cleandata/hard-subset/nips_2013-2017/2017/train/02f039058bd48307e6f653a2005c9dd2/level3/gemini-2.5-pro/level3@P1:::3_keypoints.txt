- Proposes a novel non-parametric CI test by reducing H0: X ⟂ Y | Z to a binary classification problem using synthetic samples.  
- Generates synthetic samples via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap: match samples by Z and swap their y-coordinates to approximate f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Labels bootstrapped samples as 0 and independent true samples as 1, then trains a classifier; accuracy ≫ 0.5 implies rejection of conditional independence.  
- Main technical contribution: nearest-neighbor bootstrap with Theorem 1 proving convergence in total variation under smoothness assumptions.  
- Novel generalization analysis (Theorem 2): extends Rademacher complexity bounds to the near-independent, non-i.i.d. bootstrap samples.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough and shows CCIT (and bias-corrected CCITv2) outperforming kernel-based baselines (KCIT, RCIT), especially with high-dimensional Z.  
- Leverages power and scalability of modern classifiers (e.g., XGBoost, neural nets) unlike prior kernel-based CI tests.  
- Practical enhancement: introduces CCITv2 to correct finite-sample bias and improve applicability.  
- Writing is clear and accessible overall, with logical structure and good literature context.  
- Minor clarity/presentation issues: redundant “Main Contributions” section, verbose Algorithm 2 description, and small typos (“classier”, “graph” → “graphs”).  
- Highly original: first rigorous extension of classifier-based two-sample testing to conditional independence, with a clever bootstrap and novel non-i.i.d. analysis.  
- Significant impact: useful for causal discovery, graphical models, and feature selection; benefits from advances in supervised learning.  
- Argument for acceptance: elegant reduction, strong theoretical guarantees, superior empirical performance, and broad applicability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: potential sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters; minor presentation issues to fix.  
- Questions for authors: how sensitive are results to classifier selection and hyperparameter tuning; how does the method extend to discrete or mixed-type variables (e.g., exact matching when Z is discrete)?
