This paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing it as a classification problem. The core idea – utilizing nearest-neighbor bootstrapping to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution and then employing a classifier to distinguish these from true joint samples – is a compelling one. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, effectively explaining the motivation, methodology, and theoretical contributions. It addresses an important problem in causal inference and statistical learning, and builds upon related work, particularly kernel-based methods and recent advances in classification.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem, combined with the nearest-neighbor bootstrap, provides a fresh perspective.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical bounds on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and the true conditional independence distribution, alongside generalization bounds for the classifier. This analytical rigor strengthens the contribution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The algorithmic descriptions (Algorithms 1, 2, and 3) are clear, and the mathematical notation is appropriately used.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on both synthetic and real data demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showcasing performance gains over existing state-of-the-art techniques.
* **Practical Implementation:** The availability of the code further enhances the paper's utility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity (τ):** The choice of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is a significant concern. The paper acknowledges this but lacks a concrete guideline for selecting an appropriate value, leaving it somewhat ad-hoc. A more detailed discussion on parameter tuning and its impact on performance would be valuable.
* **Weak Dependence Detection:** The paper doesn't fully address how the method detects *weak* conditional dependence. If the NN-bootstrapped distribution is already very close to f<sup>CI</sup>, distinguishing between the two hypotheses becomes challenging. Further elaboration on this aspect, potentially through empirical analysis with varying degrees of dependence, is needed.
* **Symmetry and Causal Asymmetry:** The question of symmetry (or lack thereof) in x and y, and how the algorithm handles causal asymmetry (x→y vs. y→x) when deciding which variable to sample is important. The paper doesn’t delve into this. Specifying a default strategy or providing guidance on choosing the variable based on prior causal knowledge would strengthen the work.
* **Normalization in Empirical Risk:** The empirical risk calculations in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by dividing by the sample size. This omission doesn’t invalidate the theoretical results, but is a minor oversight in the practical implementation.
* **Comparison to Alternatives:** The paper states the modularity of the approach allows replacement of components. However, a more in-depth comparative analysis against alternatives – for example, using permutation-based generation (reference [7]) or kernel two-sample tests – would be highly beneficial. Quantifying the trade-offs in terms of accuracy, computational cost, and theoretical guarantees would be valuable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* The theoretical contributions are significant and well-justified.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The parameter sensitivity issue (τ) and the lack of guidance on its selection are a concern.
* The limited discussion on handling weak dependencies and causal asymmetry.
* The lack of a detailed comparison to alternative methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed method is novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity, weak dependence detection, and asymmetry are legitimate, they do not fundamentally undermine the contribution. Addressing these points in a revised version would further enhance the paper's quality. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the parameter tuning issue and expanding the discussion on the points raised above. The normalization of the empirical risk should also be corrected.
