This paper proposes a novel and powerful method for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables, which the authors call the Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test (CCIT). The core idea is to cleverly reduce the statistical testing problem to a binary classification task. The method first generates a set of "negative" samples designed to approximate the conditional product distribution f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. It then trains a powerful classifier, such as a deep neural net or gradient-boosted tree, to distinguish these generated samples from the original joint distribution samples. The classifier's test error serves as the test statistic to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. This work builds upon and significantly extends the framework of classifier two-sample tests (Lopez-Paz et al., 2016) to the more complex conditional setting.

**Strengths:**
The paper's main strength lies in its originality and practical significance. By framing CI testing as a classification problem, it allows practitioners to leverage the full power of modern supervised learning models for a fundamental problem in statistics and causal inference. The theoretical contributions are impressive and provide a solid foundation for the method. The authors offer strong guarantees on the closeness of the bootstrapped samples to the true conditional product distribution in total variation distance and provide novel generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the resulting near-independent samples. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating superior performance over established kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT, particularly as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**
While the paper is generally well-written and accessible, the theoretical sections can be difficult to follow. From a presentation standpoint, Section 1.1 feels redundant with the introduction, and Algorithm 2 could be described more succinctly.

A key practical concern is the method's sensitivity to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters. How might different choices affect the test's conclusion? A discussion on this would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, the work focuses on continuous variables; is there a natural analog for discrete variables? One might expect the neighbor-matching bootstrap to be even more powerful when Z is discrete, and it would be interesting to see the authors' perspective on this extension.

Minor typos were noted: "classifier" on line 157 and "graphs" on line 280.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a significant and original contribution that will likely be influential. The proposed method is elegant, theoretically sound, and empirically strong, making it a valuable tool for researchers in causality and machine learning.
