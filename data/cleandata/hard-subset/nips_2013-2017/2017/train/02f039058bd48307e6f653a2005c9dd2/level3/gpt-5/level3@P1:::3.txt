Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables by reducing CI testing to a binary classification problem. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method constructs a “null” sample set intended to mimic fCI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that swaps Y within 1-NN matches in Z across a held-out split. The test then trains a classifier to distinguish original samples (joint) from NN-bootstrapped samples (approximately fCI) and uses the test-set error to accept/reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. A bias-corrected variant (CCITv2) subtracts the error of a classifier trained on (Y,Z) only from that trained on (X,Y,Z).

The approach builds on classifier two-sample tests (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab) and on kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and the permutation-based KCIPT. Relative to KCIPT’s expensive linear program, the proposed NN bootstrap is simple and fast. Theoretical contributions include: (i) total variation (TV) bounds showing the NN-bootstrapped distribution is close to fCI under smoothness assumptions; (ii) generalization bounds for ERM under near-i.i.d. dependence induced by the bootstrap; and (iii) translation of these bounds into CI testing error guarantees. Empirically, the method outperforms KCIT/RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear models (especially as dim(Z) grows) and on flow-cytometry CI checks.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling reduction of CI testing to classification, enabling use of state-of-the-art learners (XGBoost, deep nets) in high dimensions.
- Elegant NN bootstrap that approximates the conditional product distribution without solving global matching/LP problems; computationally attractive with standard NN data structures.
- Strong theory: TV-distance control to fCI and novel Rademacher/VC-type generalization bounds under near-independent samples. The bias-corrected CCITv2 is a practical and principled fix for finite-sample bias.
- Extensive experiments: synthetic scaling in dim(Z) and a real-world application (flow cytometry) with consistent gains over KCIT/RCIT.
- Good integration with prior work and clear positioning relative to classifier two-sample tests and kernel CI tests.

Weaknesses and questions
- Level control and calibration: The procedure is presented via accuracy thresholds/ROC AUC rather than a test with guaranteed Type-I error at a chosen α. How should τ be chosen in practice to control false positives, and what is the finite-sample null distribution of the accuracy gap (with/without X)? A permutation over labels within the constructed dataset or repeated sample-splitting/bootstrapping could provide calibration; please clarify and report α-level performance in simulations.
- Dependence on Z dimension: Both the TV bound and classification generalization results degrade with dz (e.g., O(n−1/dz)). While the empirical performance is better than the worst-case rate, it would help to discuss regimes where the NN bootstrap may fail (very high-dimensional Z, low-density regions) and practical mitigations (k-NN with k>1, radius trimming, feature selection for Z).
- Assumptions and constants: The curvature and smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z) are nontrivial and the constants (e.g., through G(·)) are opaque for practitioners. A brief discussion on verifiability/robustness (e.g., heavytails, multimodality) would strengthen the practical message.
- Classifier sensitivity: Because the decision depends on a black-box classifier and hyperparameters, different choices could flip accept/reject outcomes. Please include sensitivity analyses (e.g., across XGBoost depth/trees, neural architectures) and guidance for default settings. Ensembling across classifiers could further stabilize results.
- Discrete variables: When Z is discrete, exact within-stratum permutations can generate fCI. Do the authors recommend a discrete variant (stratified permutation) and/or a hybrid where discrete coordinates of Z are permuted and continuous coordinates use NN matching? Does the bootstrap become more powerful in this setting?
- Data usage efficiency: The method uses three disjoint splits (U1,U2,U3), which can reduce power at small n. Consider and discuss cross-fitting to reuse data while preserving independence for theory.

Quality
Technically solid and careful. The bootstrap-to-fCI TV control and near-i.i.d. generalization analysis are notable. Proofs are involved; while likely correct, some steps (e.g., the curvature-based bound and handling low-density regions via G(·)) are dense—adding intuition and a proof roadmap in the main text would help.

Clarity
Well-written and accessible overall, with helpful algorithms and figures. Some redundancy (Section 1.1 repeats the pipeline) and Algorithm 2 could be described more succinctly. Proof sketches or intuition boxes would aid readers.

Originality
High. The reduction of CI testing to classification via an efficient NN bootstrap, together with generalization guarantees under near-independence, is novel and extends classifier two-sample testing to the conditional case.

Significance
High. CI testing is central to causal discovery and feature selection. A practical, scalable, model-powered test that outperforms kernel baselines is valuable to both researchers and practitioners. The analysis of classification with near-independent samples may also be of independent interest.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an α-calibrated testing procedure (null distribution via repeated splits/permutation) and report Type-I/Type-II errors at fixed α in experiments.
- Add guidance on classifier choice/hyperparameters and a robustness table across learners; consider an ensemble.
- Explore k-NN (k>1) or local-kernel variants of the bootstrap and report sensitivity to k and distance metric for Z.
- Include runtime comparisons with KCIT/RCIT/KCIPT.
- Discuss discrete-variable extensions and mixed discrete–continuous Z.
- Minor presentation edits: merge 1.1 with the introduction; condense Algorithm 2; add a high-level proof sketch for Theorems 1–2.

Typos and minor issues
- “classifier” capitalization (line ~157) and “graphs” agreement (line ~280). Please proofread for minor typos.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, simple idea with solid theory and clear empirical gains.
- Scales well with powerful off-the-shelf classifiers; broadly useful for causal inference workflows.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of α-calibrated testing procedure in the main empirical evaluation.
- Theoretical rates degrade in high-dimensional Z; assumptions may be strong in some applications.

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept. The methodological contribution, theoretical guarantees, and practical performance outweigh the concerns; addressing calibration and robustness would make the paper even stronger.
