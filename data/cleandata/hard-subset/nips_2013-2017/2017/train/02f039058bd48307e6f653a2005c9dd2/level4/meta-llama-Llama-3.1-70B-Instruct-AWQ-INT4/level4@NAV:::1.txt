This paper presents a model-powered approach for conducting conditional independence tests on iid data. The core concept involves utilizing nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that closely follow the f^{CI} distribution, and then training and testing a classifier to determine if it can distinguish between the observation distribution and the bootstrapped distribution. If the classification performance is comparable to a random guess, the null hypothesis that the data follows conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is accepted.

The authors have established bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution to f^{CI} in terms of total variational distance, as well as bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for near-independent samples.

In general, the paper tackles an important problem and is well-presented.

Detailed comments:

Major:

1. The proposed method appears to consist of two primary components: generating samples that mimic f_{CI} and employing a classifier for determination. A question arises as to whether either of these steps can be replaced with existing solutions from the literature. For instance, could the permutation-based method proposed in [7] be used in conjunction with the classification-based approach, or could the nearest neighbor bootstrap be combined with the kernel two-sample test approach? The performance of such alternatives is of interest.

2. In the finite sample size setting, the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution is close to f_{CI}. However, if the ground truth is that x and y are weakly dependent given z, it is unclear how the proposed method would perform.

3. The method's symmetry with respect to x and y is also a concern. From a causal perspective, x and y can be dependent given z due to different causal models (e.g., x causes y or y causes x). In such scenarios, it is unclear whether the method can determine which variable to sample. The authors have not addressed this issue.

4. The choice of parameter $\tau$ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is also a concern.

Minor:

In Algorithms 2 and 3, it appears that the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size.
