Summary
This paper proposes a model-powered approach to nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables by reducing CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from f(x, y, z), the method splits the data, applies a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap to construct synthetic samples that approximate fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z) f(y|z) f(z), and then trains a binary classifier to distinguish the original samples from the synthetic ones. If the classifier performs better than chance, H0: X ⟂ Y | Z is rejected. The authors provide (i) total variation (TV) distance bounds showing that the NN-bootstrap samples are close to fCI under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z), and (ii) generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization under near-independent samples, yielding testing error bounds. They also propose a bias-corrected variant that subtracts the residual distinguishability in (Y, Z) alone. Empirical results on synthetic data and the flow cytometry dataset show improved ROC–AUC over kernel-based baselines (KCIT, RCIT), particularly in higher-dimensional Z.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned against kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and a permutation-based approach (KCIPT). It extends the recent idea of using classifiers for two-sample and independence testing to the CI setting, where constructing samples from the null fCI is harder because permutations must respect Z. The NN-bootstrap offers a computationally simple alternative to the permutation-with-linear-programming in KCIPT and is accompanied by explicit TV bounds. The authors also connect to generalization theory beyond i.i.d. samples, complementing work on mixing processes and Rademacher complexity.

Strengths
- Conceptual reduction: Turning CI testing into a classification problem is elegant, modular, and practically powerful, leveraging off-the-shelf learners (e.g., gradient-boosted trees).
- Novel bootstrap construction: The nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate fCI is simple, efficient, and theoretically analyzed. The TV bounds are technically interesting and, to my knowledge, new in this setting.
- Generalization under dependence: The analysis of ERM generalization with near-independent samples is a valuable contribution that clarifies when training on NN-bootstrapped data can be justified.
- Practical performance: The method outperforms strong baselines in synthetic and real data, and scales more favorably in Z than many kernel tests in practice.
- Bias correction: The CCITv2 scheme is a practical fix for finite-sample bias arising from the bootstrap.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarity and exposition: The paper is dense and difficult to follow in places. The intuition behind the curvature assumption (Assumption 1), the set G(·), and the role of each term in the TV bound could be substantially clarified. A “roadmap” figure that explicitly ties Assumptions → Theorem 1 → Theorem 2 → testing decision would help.
- Practical guidance: The test relies on a threshold τ that must exceed a bias b(n), but there is limited guidance on choosing τ to control Type I error. A calibration procedure (e.g., sample-splitting with additional bootstraps, or a permutation over Y within Z-neighborhoods) would improve usability and provide nominal level control.
- Dependence on classifier class G: The guarantees require that the best classifier in G achieves error close to r0 = 0.5(1 − dTV(f, fCI)). If G is too weak (e.g., linear) the approximation error η can be large, making the bound vacuous and the test low-powered. Please discuss the choice of G, the role of η in Theorem 2(ii), and any diagnostics for underfitting.
- Dimensionality of Z: The TV bound scales as O(n−1/dz) (up to constants and lower-order terms), which is pessimistic in high dz. While experiments suggest better practical scaling, it would be helpful to articulate the regimes (e.g., manifold or low-effective-dimension Z, Lipschitz constants) under which performance matches practice.
- Bootstrap details: The algorithm uses 1-NN in Z. How sensitive are results to the choice of distance metric, the presence of ties, or using k-NN with averaging? Some empirical ablations and brief theoretical discussion would be useful.
- Statistical calibration: The paper reports ROC–AUC, but CI tests are commonly evaluated for type I control at fixed α. Please include size vs. power curves with calibrated thresholds.
- Comparisons: Consider adding a conditional mutual information baseline (e.g., kNN-based CMI estimators) and/or KCIPT if computationally feasible, to broaden the comparison.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with careful assumptions and proofs. The TV and generalization analyses are nontrivial and valuable. Some constants/terms in bounds are complex, and finite-sample implications could be discussed more explicitly. Empirical evaluation is solid but would benefit from level calibration and additional baselines.

Clarity
The main ideas are clear, but the manuscript would benefit from more intuition, cleaner notation, and a tighter presentation of assumptions. Provide a plain-language explanation of Assumption 1, a brief summary of Assumption 2’s implications, and a worked example illustrating how the NN-bootstrap approximates fCI.

Originality
High. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap for CI along with TV analysis and generalization bounds under near-independence appear novel. The reduction to classification is known in related contexts but is extended here in a substantive way.

Significance
Potentially high. A modular CI test that can leverage advances in classification is attractive for practice, especially in high-dimensional settings. The theoretical tools may be useful beyond CI testing.

Questions for the authors
- Section 1.1(iii) bound: Suppose H1 holds and r0 = 0.1, but G is a weak class (e.g., linear) that cannot distinguish f from fCI, so η ≈ 0.4. In this regime, how should we interpret the inequality? My reading is that the bound concentrates around r0 + η plus generalization terms, so if η is large the classifier error will stay near 0.5 and the test will be powerless. Please confirm and clarify the role of η and any conditions under which η is small (e.g., using sufficiently expressive G).
- Threshold selection: How do you recommend choosing τ to ensure type I error control at a desired α? Can the bias-correction step be combined with a data-driven calibration?
- k-NN vs. 1-NN: Do you have empirical or theoretical guidance on using k > 1 for the bootstrap?
- Runtime: Please report computational complexity and wall-clock time vs. KCIT/RCIT for varying n and dz.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean reduction; strong empirical performance; novel and useful theory; code available.

Cons for acceptance
- Exposition is challenging; lack of level-calibrated evaluation and guidance on τ; dependence on classifier capacity not fully addressed; pessimistic theoretical scaling in dz without clear practical conditions.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the role of η/G, providing threshold calibration guidance, and improving exposition.
