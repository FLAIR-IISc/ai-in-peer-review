This paper introduces a novel, distribution-free test for conditional independence between three continuous variables. The authors address a fundamental problem in machine learning, particularly within causal inference, where determining conditional independence is crucial for assessing unconfoundedness. Their approach cleverly leverages samples from the joint distribution to generate samples approximating the conditional distribution in terms of total variation distance. These generated samples are then used to train a binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network), and the classifier's predictive error serves as the basis for accepting or rejecting the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The method is thoroughly evaluated through comparisons with existing techniques, accompanied by both descriptive analysis and experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets.

Conditional independence testing is a cornerstone of modern machine learning, and its reliable performance has significant implications, particularly in causal reasoning. This proposed method appears both intuitive and sufficiently robust for a wide range of practical causal applications. 

The paper is generally well-written and accessible. While the theoretical details and proofs require careful attention, the authors effectively summarize the key findings and clearly articulate the methodology for practitioners. The most compelling aspect of this work lies in its theoretical guarantees. The method builds upon the framework established in “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf) – as the authors rightfully acknowledge – extending it to incorporate a third variable.  After appropriate matching based on this variable, the core methodology shares similarities with the referenced work; however, the theoretical bounds achieved in this paper are remarkably strong.

I have a few suggestions for improvement:

* **Discrete Case:** Could the method be adapted for discrete variables?  The bootstrap procedure often benefits from increased power when the conditioning variable is discrete, and exploring this extension would be valuable.
* **Classifier Sensitivity:**  An investigation into the test's sensitivity to the choice of classification algorithm and hyperparameter settings would strengthen the analysis. Demonstrating the robustness (or outlining the potential impact) of these choices via additional simulations would be highly beneficial.  Specifically, how do variations in classifier parameters influence acceptance or rejection of the null hypothesis?
* **Redundancy in Section 1.1:**  Section 1.1, outlining the main contributions, feels somewhat repetitive. The authors reiterate their approach multiple times, and this level of detail appears unnecessary given the clarity of the overall presentation.  Streamlining this section would improve the paper's flow.
* **Algorithm 2:** Presenting Algorithm 2 in full detail seems redundant. The procedure is already adequately described within the main text and could be summarized more concisely.
* **Minor Typos:** There are a couple of minor typographical errors: “classifier” should be used in line 157, and “graphs” in line 280.
