This paper introduces a novel, distribution-free test for conditional independence among three continuous variables. The authors circumvent strong parametric assumptions by constructing samples approximating the conditional product distribution via samples from the joint distribution, measured using total variation distance. A binary classifier – specifically, a deep neural network – is then trained to discriminate between these samples, with the resulting classification error serving as the basis for a hypothesis test regarding conditional independence. The method’s performance is evaluated through comparisons with existing techniques, supported by experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Conditional independence testing is a fundamental problem in machine learning, with particular relevance to causal inference and assessing unconfoundedness. The proposed approach demonstrates both conceptual simplicity and practical potential for a wide range of causal applications. 

The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the methodology, despite the theoretical details and proofs requiring careful consideration. The authors effectively summarize their key findings, making the approach accessible to practitioners.

The theoretical contributions are particularly noteworthy. While building upon the framework established in “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf) – as the authors acknowledge, by extending the test to incorporate a third variable and employing neighbor matching – the resulting theoretical guarantees are substantial.

Several points warrant further consideration:

- Does the method extend to a discrete setting? Leveraging the inherent advantages of bootstrapping when the conditioning variable is discrete would be a valuable addition.
- An investigation into the test’s sensitivity to the choice of classification method and hyperparameter settings would be beneficial. Assessing the robustness of the results to such variations, alongside the current simulations, would strengthen the analysis.
- Section 1.1, outlining the main contributions, feels somewhat repetitive; streamlining this section would improve clarity.
- The explicit presentation of Algorithm 2 appears unnecessary, given the process is adequately described elsewhere in the paper.
- Minor corrections are suggested: “classifier” (line 157) and “graphs” (line 280).
