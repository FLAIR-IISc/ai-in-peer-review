This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conducting conditional independence tests for iid data. The basic idea is to utilize nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that follow a distribution close to the conditional independence distribution (f^{CI}) and train a classifier to distinguish between the observation distribution and the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution. If the classification performance is close to random guessing, one fails to reject the null hypothesis that the data follows conditional independence; otherwise, one rejects the null hypothesis.

The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI} in terms of total variational distance and bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for near-independent samples.

In general, the paper addresses an important problem and presents the content in a clear manner.

Detailed comments:

Major:

1. It appears that the proposed method can be decoupled into two major components: generating samples that mimic f_{CI} and using a classifier for determination. A question arises as to whether either step can be replaced with existing solutions from the literature. For instance, could the permutation-based method proposed in [7] be used in conjunction with the classification-based approach to solve the problem? Alternatively, could the nearest neighbor bootstrap be combined with the kernel two-sample test approach? I am curious about the potential performance of such combinations.

2. The nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution approximates f_{CI} in the finite sample size setting. However, if the ground truth is that x and y are weakly dependent given z, how will the proposed method distinguish between this scenario and true conditional independence?

3. Is the method symmetric with respect to x and y? From a causal perspective, x and y can be dependent given z due to two different causal models: x causes y or y causes x. In these different scenarios, should one determine which variable to sample? The authors do not consider this issue.

4. I am concerned about the choice of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3.

Minor:

In Algorithms 2 and 3, it appears that the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size to obtain an unbiased estimate.
