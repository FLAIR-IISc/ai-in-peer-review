- Proposes CCIT: reduce conditional independence testing to a binary classification task distinguishing true joint samples from null (CI) samples.  
- Null-sample generation via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap: for each (x_i,y_i,z_i) find NN z_j and form (x_i,y_j,z_i).  
- Extends classifier-based two-sample testing to the conditional case, addressing non-trivial challenges where simple permutation fails.  
- Novelty/significance: leverages advances in high-dimensional classification, making CI testing model-powered and immediately improvable with better classifiers.  
- Theoretical contribution: requires a smoothness assumption on f(y|z) and proves a TV-distance bound (Theorem 1) between bootstrap and true CI distribution.  
- Generalization analysis: provides Rademacher-complexity-style bounds for classifiers on the near-i.i.d. bootstrap samples (Theorem 2).  
- Empirical results: CCIT significantly outperforms kernel-based tests (KCIT, RCIT) in synthetic high-dimensional Z settings and succeeds on flow cytometry causal discovery.  
- Clarity issue: paper is technically dense and needs more intuition and clearer interpretation of theoretical terms and practical implications.  
- Classifier capacity risk: method assumes classifier family G is expressive enough; insufficiently powerful classifiers can cause false negatives and should be discussed.  
- Bias-correction motivation: CCITv2 is practical but the paper should better explain why the bootstrap creates bias that a powerful classifier can exploit under H0.  
- Overall: strong, novel approach with rigorous theory and convincing empirical gains; presentation and explicit discussion of classifier/model-selection issues need strengthening.  
- Arguments for acceptance: elegant reduction to classification, efficient nearest-neighbor bootstrap, strong theoretical guarantees, and state-of-the-art empirical performance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dense presentation and incomplete discussion of failure modes when the classifier lacks sufficient capacity.
