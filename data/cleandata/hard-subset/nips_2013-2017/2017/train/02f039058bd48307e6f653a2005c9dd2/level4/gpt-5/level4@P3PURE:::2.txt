Summary
The paper proposes a new conditional independence test based on a smoothness assumption for conditional densities and provides theoretical guarantees via total variation bounds and a key theorem combining multiple assumptions.

Strengths
1) The paper identifies that, for a conditional independence test to work, one must assume some smoothness of the conditional density. The authors propose such an assumption and, under it, bound the total variation distance between the density under the null hypothesis and the density obtained via the bootstrap. This is novel.
2) The paper leverages this smoothness assumption to bound the error of the optimal classifier on the training set (Section 1.1 (iii)).
3) Theorem 1—combining the smoothness assumption, the assumption that hat R can be achieved with error eta, and the assumption on the gap between the optimal and trained classifier errors—is novel, insightful, and non-trivial.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- The paper is difficult to read and provides limited interpretation of the results. As a consequence, I am not fully confident I understand some parts correctly.
- In particular, consider the inequality in Section 1.1 (iii). Assume the alternative hypothesis holds and r0 = 0.1. Suppose G is a small hypothesis class (e.g., linear functions) that is not expressive enough to distinguish between f and f^CI at all. Can you explain, intuitively, how the small probability of error can still be achieved—i.e., how we can have hat R < 0.1 + o(1) < 0.2 for large n—under these conditions?

Overall assessment
- The theoretical contributions are novel and technically interesting, especially the total variation bound under the proposed smoothness assumption and the synthesis in Theorem 1.
- However, the exposition should be improved substantially. Please add more intuition, illustrative examples, and clearer explanations of the roles and interactions of the assumptions (particularly in Section 1.1 (iii)), and address the scenario where G is too limited to separate f from f^CI.
