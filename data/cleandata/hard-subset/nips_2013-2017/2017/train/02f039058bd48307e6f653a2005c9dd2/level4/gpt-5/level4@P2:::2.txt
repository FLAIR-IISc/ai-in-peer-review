Summary
The paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables that reduces CI testing to supervised binary classification. The key idea is to generate synthetic samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) using a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap: for each (x, y, z), replace y with the y of the nearest neighbor in z from a held-out set. The authors prove that the distribution of these bootstrapped samples is close in total variation (TV) to fCI under smoothness assumptions. With this reduction, CI testing becomes a two-sample classification problem between the original data and the NN-bootstrapped data, enabling the use of strong classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, deep nets). They provide generalization bounds for classification using near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples and propose a bias-corrected test statistic that compares a classifier using (x, y, z) to one using only (y, z). Empirically, the method outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic data and performs well on flow-cytometry protein signaling data.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned against kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT, KCIPT). Unlike KCIPT, which uses a permutation induced by solving a linear program to approximate fCI and performs a kernel two-sample test, this work uses a simple NN bootstrap with TV guarantees and leverages modern classifiers. It also connects to classifier-based two-sample testing for independence and to NN-based entropy/CMI estimation, but extends these ideas to conditional testing with nontrivial theoretical analysis for non-i.i.d. samples.

Strengths
- Novel reduction: Turning CI testing into classification via a nearest-neighbor swap conditioned on z is simple, elegant, and practically attractive. It enables the use of off-the-shelf high-capacity classifiers in high dimensions.
- Theoretical guarantees: Theorem 1 provides a bound on dTV between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and fCI, under clear smoothness assumptions. This addresses a core technical challenge that limited earlier classification-based reductions for CI.
- Learning-theoretic analysis under near-independence: The paper develops generalization bounds for classifiers trained on samples that are not exactly i.i.d. but “nearly” independent due to spatial separation in z, extending VC/Rademacher-style results.
- Practical bias correction: The comparison between classifiers trained on (x, y, z) and on (y, z) mitigates finite-sample bias due to the NN coupling, improving robustness.
- Empirical performance: The method scales well in the dimensionality of z in simulations and shows strong AUC on real protein data, outperforming established baselines.

Weaknesses and suggestions for clarity
- Readability and intuition: Several key results are hard to parse without more intuition. For example, provide a concise, high-level explanation of how the NN swap approximates sampling from f(y|z) and why this yields TV closeness, including a simple 1D illustration with smooth f(y|z) and regions of small f(z).
- Role of approximation error and function class G: In Section 1.1(iii) and Theorem 2, clarify the interplay between (a) the Bayes risk r0 = 0.5(1 − dTV(f, fCI)), (b) the approximation error η = infg∈G R(g) − R∗, and (c) the estimation/generalization terms. If G is small (e.g., linear) and cannot separate the classes under H1, the classifier’s risk may remain near 0.5 despite dTV(f, fCI) being large. Please state explicitly whether the bounds are with respect to the Bayes optimal classifier (unrestricted) or to the best-in-class g∈G, and where η enters the inequality. An intuitive explanation for the following scenario would help: under H1 with r0 = 0.1, but G is too limited to distinguish f from fCI, how can the bound imply small error (e.g., R̂ < 0.2 for large n)?
- Dependence on dz: The TV bound scales as O(n−1/dz), reflecting a curse of dimensionality in z. While experiments show good performance up to dz ≈ 70, discuss when the assumptions and constants make the bound informative, and provide guidance on expected behavior as dz grows without low-dimensional structure.
- Assumptions: Assumption 1 (curvature of f(y|z)) is nonstandard. Provide examples of models where it holds, and guidance on verifying or relaxing it. Similarly, clarify the Lipschitz/smoothness assumptions on f(z), and how sensitive the NN step is to deviations (e.g., multimodality, heavy tails).
- Practical testing aspects: The empirical evaluation reports ROC/AUC. For hypothesis testing, how should users set τ to control Type I error at a target α? Can the bias-corrected statistic be coupled with a calibration scheme (e.g., sample-splitting, permutation over labels, or a null bootstrap) to obtain p-values?
- Implementation details: Provide sensitivity analyses for (i) classifier choice and hyperparameters, (ii) number of bootstraps B and data splitting strategy, (iii) approximate vs exact NN search, and (iv) sample size dependence. A brief computational complexity discussion would be useful.

Quality
The technical development appears sound and addresses both the generative (bootstrap closeness to fCI) and discriminative (generalization under near-independence) aspects. However, clearer statements about approximation error relative to G and explicit conditions under which the classifier is guaranteed to outperform chance would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The paper would benefit from:
- A running toy example illustrating the NN bootstrap and bias correction.
- A figure or schematic contrasting KCIPT’s permutation with the proposed NN swap.
- A consolidated theorem that cleanly separates Bayes, approximation, and estimation errors.

Originality
The NN bootstrap for approximating fCI with TV guarantees and the generalization analysis under near-independent sampling are, to my knowledge, new and substantive contributions beyond prior kernel and permutation-based CI tests.

Significance
If widely adopted, the reduction enables practical, scalable CI testing using modern classifiers and fast NN search. The approach could influence causal discovery pipelines and high-dimensional testing settings. The worst-case dependence on dz tempers the theoretical rates, but the empirical gains are compelling.

Pros
- Simple, modular algorithm with strong empirical results
- Solid theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap and learning
- Bias-corrected statistic improves robustness

Cons
- Presentation is dense; key intuitions are under-explained
- Practical testing calibration (p-values/α-control) not fully addressed
- Dependence on classifier richness and on dz could be better clarified

Questions for the authors
- Please clarify the precise role of η and of the function class G in the generalization bounds, and address the scenario where G is too limited to separate under H1.
- How should τ be set to control Type I error, and can you provide a calibrated testing procedure with p-values?
- How sensitive is performance to approximate NN search, classifier choice, and the number of bootstraps?
- Can you provide examples where Assumption 1 is satisfied and discuss diagnostics for its plausibility in practice?
