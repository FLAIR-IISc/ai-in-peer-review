The paper proposes a novel conditional independence (CI) test based on a model-powered approach, reducing the problem to binary classification.

**Strengths**

1) The paper correctly identifies the need for smoothness assumptions on the conditional density to enable a data-driven CI test. The authors propose such an assumption and provide a bound on the total variational distance between the density under the null hypothesis and the density obtained from their bootstrap procedure. This is a valuable and novel contribution.
2) The paper leverages this smoothness assumption to establish generalization bounds for the classifier trained on the generated data (section 1.1 (iii)). This connection between the CI test and the performance of the classifier is well-motivated.
3) Theorem 1, which combines assumptions on smoothness, classifier error, and the distance between distributions, is insightful and non-trivial. It provides a theoretical foundation for the proposed method.

**Weaknesses**

The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in presentation, making it difficult to follow the technical arguments and interpret the results. This hinders a complete assessment of the work. For example, consider the inequality in section 1.1 (iii). Assuming the alternative hypothesis holds and r0=0.1, and supposing G is a limited class of functions (e.g., linear functions) that are insufficiently expressive to distinguish between f and fCI, it is unclear how the stated small probability of error can be achieved (i.e., how  ˆR can be less than 0.1 + o(1/n) for large n). A more intuitive explanation of this behavior would be beneficial. 

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

* **Introduction:** The introduction is verbose and could benefit from streamlining. Briefly motivating the importance of CI testing and highlighting the limitations of existing methods would improve readability.
* **Notation:** The notation is often heavy and could be simplified. For example, consistently using subscript notation (e.g., f<sub>X,Y,Z</sub>) throughout the paper would reduce ambiguity.
* **Algorithm Descriptions:** The descriptions of Algorithms 1, 2, and 3 could be more concise and focus on the key steps. Providing pseudocode with clear variable names is helpful.
* **Theoretical Results:** While the theoretical results are interesting, they are presented with limited intuition. Adding explanations of the key terms and their implications would significantly improve understanding. For example, the meaning of ‘d<sub>z</sub>’ and its impact on the bounds is not clearly explained.
* **Empirical Results:** The empirical results are presented without sufficient context. Providing a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, the choice of parameters, and the performance metrics would strengthen the conclusions. The claim that the performance scales better than expected from the theoretical analysis needs further justification.
* **Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive but could be more focused. Highlighting the specific advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches would be helpful.

**Arguments For Acceptance**

* Novel approach to CI testing by framing it as a classification problem.
* Theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for the classifier.
* Empirical results demonstrating performance gains over existing methods.
* The identification and formalization of smoothness assumptions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance**

* Lack of clarity in presentation makes the paper difficult to understand.
* Limited intuition and explanation of the theoretical results.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and better contextualized.
* The connection between the theoretical results and the practical performance of the algorithm is not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to conditional independence testing. However, the lack of clarity and the limited intuition surrounding the theoretical results hinder a complete assessment. While the potential impact of the work is significant, the paper requires substantial revisions to improve its readability and accessibility. I recommend **weak reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit, focusing on clarifying the presentation and providing more intuitive explanations of the theoretical findings. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential for acceptance.
