### **Summary and Significance**

This paper introduces a general, non-parametric test for assessing the conditional independence of three continuous random variables (X ⟂ Y | Z). The proposed method avoids strong parametric assumptions. Instead, it operates by first drawing samples from the joint distribution P(X, Y, Z) and then constructing a second set of samples from a distribution designed to approximate the conditional product distribution P(X|Z)P(Y|Z)P(Z). The core idea is to train a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network, to distinguish between samples from these two distributions. The trained classifier's predictive accuracy on a held-out test set serves as the test statistic, which is used to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors provide a thorough comparison of their method against existing approaches, supported by experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data.

Conditional independence testing is a fundamental problem in modern machine learning and statistics, with critical applications in causal inference, where researchers frequently need to verify assumptions of unconfoundedness. The proposed method appears to be a straightforward yet powerful tool that could be widely adopted in these domains.

### **Strengths and Contributions**

The paper is well-written, and the proposed methodology is both elegant and powerful. While the technical details of the theoretical results and proofs can be challenging to follow, the authors do an excellent job of summarizing the core findings and presenting the methodology in a manner that is clear and accessible to practitioners.

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, lies in its strong theoretical guarantees. The authors rightly acknowledge that their method is a well-conceived extension of the framework presented in "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests," adapting it from a two-variable independence test to a three-variable conditional independence test. This adaptation involves a clever nearest-neighbor matching step on the conditioning variable Z. However, the theoretical analysis provided for the conditional setting is non-trivial and impressive, establishing solid foundations for the test.

### **Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong submission, a few points could be clarified or improved:

1.  **Discrete or Mixed-Type Variables:** The paper focuses on the continuous variable setting. Could the authors comment on a potential analog for discrete or mixed-type variables? When the conditioning variable Z is discrete, the process of matching neighbors becomes exact rather than approximate. It seems the bootstrap procedure would be particularly powerful and straightforward in this setting. Any insights on this would be welcome.

2.  **Sensitivity to Classifier Choice:** The test's performance relies on a binary classifier. How sensitive are the final results (i.e., the p-values or accept/reject decisions) to the choice of the classifier's architecture and its hyperparameters? It is plausible that different hyperparameter settings could lead to different conclusions. Including a sensitivity analysis alongside the simulation results would significantly strengthen the paper's practical recommendations for users of this method.

3.  **Redundancy in Introduction:** Section 1.1, "Main Contributions," seems somewhat redundant. The authors summarize their approach multiple times throughout the introduction, and this section could be condensed or integrated more smoothly into the surrounding text to improve conciseness.

4.  **Redundancy of Algorithm 2:** The inclusion of Algorithm 2 in its full pseudo-code form feels unnecessary. The procedure it describes is already explained clearly and sufficiently in the main text. This space might be better utilized for further discussion or to elaborate on experimental details.
