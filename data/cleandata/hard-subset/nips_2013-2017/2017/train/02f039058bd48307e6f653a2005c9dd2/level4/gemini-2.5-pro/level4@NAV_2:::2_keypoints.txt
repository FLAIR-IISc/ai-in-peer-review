- Proposes CCIT, a novel non-parametric CI test that reduces conditional independence testing to binary classification via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap that approximates f_CI = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).

- Labels original samples as class 1 and bootstrapped samples as class 0; classifier accuracy serves as the test statistic (high accuracy ⇒ dependence).

- Theorem 1: provides a non-trivial bound on the total variation distance between the bootstrap-generated distribution and the true conditionally independent distribution under smoothness assumptions.

- Theorem 2: derives generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the near–non-i.i.d. bootstrap samples using Rademacher complexity, linking empirical risk to distributional distance.

- Practical advantage: method leverages modern supervised learners (e.g., gradient-boosted trees), scales better than kernel-based CI tests, and empirically outperforms them as Z’s dimensionality increases.

- Weakness: presentation and intuition are lacking—the technical sections are dense and the connection between theorems and practical behavior is not clearly explained.

- Specific concern: paper does not sufficiently address how the classifier function class G’s expressiveness affects test power; a limited G can yield large approximation error and fail to detect dependence despite large TV distance.
