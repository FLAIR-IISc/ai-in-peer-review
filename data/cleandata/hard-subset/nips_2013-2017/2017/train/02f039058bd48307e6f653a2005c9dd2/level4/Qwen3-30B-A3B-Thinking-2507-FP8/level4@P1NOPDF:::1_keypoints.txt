- Proposed method: nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate approximate f^{CI} samples, then train a classifier to distinguish observed vs. bootstrapped data; classification near random implies conditional independence.
- Theoretical contributions: bounds on total variation between bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI}, and bounds on empirical risks for ideal and near-independent samples.
- Major 1 — Methodological decoupling: approach splits into generation and classification; authors should compare hybrids using established components (e.g., permutation tests or kernel two-sample tests) to justify design.
- Major 2 — Weak dependence sensitivity: lacks analysis/experiments on performance when conditional dependence is weak (low mutual information), where detection is hardest.
- Major 3 — Causal asymmetry: treating X and Y symmetrically ignores causal directionality (X→Y vs. Y→X), which matters in causal inference and sampling choices.
- Major 4 — Parameter sensitivity: choice of τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is critical but unaddressed; need practical tuning guidance or robustness demonstrations.
- Minor — Empirical risk normalization: Algorithms 2 and 3 should normalize empirical risk by sample size (standard practice).
- Overall: novel framework with theory but unresolved issues (weak dependence, causal asymmetry, parameter selection) limit practical applicability and require addressing before publication.
