This paper proposes a model-powered approach to perform conditional independence (CI) tests for independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data. The core idea is to leverage nearest neighbor bootstrapping to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, *f*<sup>CI</sup>, and then train a classifier to distinguish between the observed data distribution and the bootstrapped distribution. If the classifier’s performance is close to random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is accepted.

The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution to *f*<sup>CI</sup>, expressed in terms of total variational distance, and provide bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for nearly-independent samples. Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is presented clearly.

**Detailed Comments:**

**Major:**

1. The proposed method appears to naturally decompose into two components: generating samples that approximate *f*<sub>CI</sub>, and employing a classifier for the decision. I question whether either of these steps could be replaced with existing methods from the literature. For example, could we combine the permutation-based method from [7] with a classification approach to solve the problem? Alternatively, could we integrate the nearest neighbor bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test? I am curious about the performance implications of such substitutions.

2. The analysis demonstrates the proximity of the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution to *f*<sup>CI</sup> in finite sample settings. However, if the true relationship is one of weak dependence between *x* and *y* given *z*, how would the proposed method differentiate this from true conditional independence?

3. Is the method symmetric with respect to *x* and *y*? From a causal perspective, dependence between *x* and *y* given *z* can arise from two distinct causal structures: *x* causing *y*, or *y* causing *x*. These scenarios may require different sampling strategies. The authors do not address this asymmetry.

4. I have serious concerns regarding the selection of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The sensitivity of the results to this parameter is unclear.

**Minor:**

In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculation appears to omit division by the sample size. This normalization seems necessary for a valid empirical risk estimate.
