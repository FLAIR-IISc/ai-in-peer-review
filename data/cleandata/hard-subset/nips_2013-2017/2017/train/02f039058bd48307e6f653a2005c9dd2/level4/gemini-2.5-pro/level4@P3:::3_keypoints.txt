- Proposes CCIT, a non-parametric CI test that reframes X ⊥ Y | Z as a binary classification problem.  
- Constructs class '1' from original joint samples and class '0' to approximate f_CI = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap that swaps y-components of nearest-z neighbors to generate near-i.i.d. samples under the null.  
- Uses classifier accuracy (e.g., GBDT or neural nets) as the test statistic: ~0.5 implies independence, higher implies dependence.  
- Proves bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation (Theorem 1).  
- Provides generalization bounds/Rademacher complexity results for classifiers trained on "near-independence" samples (Theorem 2).  
- Shows strong empirical performance on synthetic and flow cytometry data, outperforming KCIT/RCIT, especially with high-dimensional Z.  
- Highlights practical significance and modularity: leverages advances in supervised learning for CI testing.  
- Emphasizes strong theoretical contributions and novel analysis useful beyond this specific method.  
- Notes clear writing, good organization, and a useful bias-corrected variant (CCITv2).  
- Warns sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters; calls for robustness/ablation studies.  
- Questions novelty as conceptually incremental from prior classification-based two-sample tests; main novelty is the NN bootstrap.  
- Suggests manuscript could be more concise due to some redundancy in presentation.  
- Asks how the method extends to discrete or mixed variables (Z with discrete/high-cardinality support).
