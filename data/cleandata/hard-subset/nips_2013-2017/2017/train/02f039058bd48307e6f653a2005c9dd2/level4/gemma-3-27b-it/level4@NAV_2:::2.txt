The paper addresses the problem of non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables, framing it as a binary classification problem and leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The key contribution lies in a novel nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, coupled with theoretical guarantees on the quality of this approximation and generalization bounds for the resulting classification task.

The paper’s strengths are significant. First, it correctly identifies the crucial need for assumptions regarding the smoothness of conditional densities for effective CI testing and proposes a well-defined smoothness assumption, bounding the total variational distance between the true and bootstrapped distributions under this assumption. This is a novel and important contribution. Second, the paper provides theoretical guarantees relating the error of the optimal classifier on the training set to the underlying distributions (section 1.1 (iii)). Third, Theorem 1, combining smoothness assumptions, generalization error bounds, and the difference in errors between optimal and trained classifiers, is insightful, non-trivial, and represents a core technical achievement. 

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues. It is densely written and lacks sufficient intuitive explanations of the results, making it challenging to fully grasp the implications of the theoretical findings. For example, the inequality presented in section 1.1 (iii) warrants further clarification. Specifically, consider the alternative hypothesis holding (r0 = 0.1). If G represents a limited class of functions (e.g., linear functions) incapable of effectively distinguishing between *f* and *f<sup>CI</sup>*, how can a small probability of error be achieved (i.e.,  ˆ*R* < 0.1 + *o<sub>1</sub>* < 0.2 for large *n*)? An intuitive explanation would greatly enhance understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem with a principled bootstrap procedure is novel.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees, including bounds on the bootstrap approximation and generalization error.
*   **Potential Impact:** The approach can leverage advances in classification, potentially leading to significant improvements in CI testing, particularly in high dimensions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper's density and lack of intuitive explanations hinder understanding and reproducibility.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While empirical results are presented, the connection between the theoretical results and the practical performance could be strengthened. More detailed discussion of the experimental setup and parameter choices is needed.



Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and potentially impactful contribution. However, significant improvements in clarity and exposition are necessary to fully realize its value. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, specifically focusing on improving the readability and providing more intuitive explanations of the key theoretical results.
