This paper introduces a model-based approach for conditional independence testing with independent and identically distributed (iid) data. The core concept leverages nearest neighbor bootstrapping to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, f^{CI}. A classifier is then trained and evaluated to discern between the observed data distribution and the distribution generated by the nearest neighbor bootstrap. The null hypothesis of conditional independence is rejected if the classifier’s performance significantly exceeds random guessing; otherwise, it is accepted.

The authors establish theoretical bounds on the approximation quality of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution to f^{CI}, quantified by total variational distance, and derive bounds on empirical risks for both ideal classification scenarios and near-independent samples. 

Overall, the paper tackles a significant problem and is clearly presented. 

Detailed comments:

Major:

1. The proposed method appears to comprise two distinct components: sample generation approximating f_{CI}, and classification-based decision making. It is unclear if either component could be replaced with existing techniques from the literature without substantial performance loss. For instance, could permutation-based methods (e.g., [7]) be combined with a classification approach, or could nearest neighbor bootstrapping be used in conjunction with kernel two-sample tests? A comparative analysis of these alternative combinations would be valuable.
2. The paper demonstrates the proximity of the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution to f_{CI} in finite sample settings. However, if the true relationship between x and y is weak dependence conditional on z, how would the proposed method reliably detect this subtle dependence?
3. The method’s symmetry with respect to x and y is unclear. From a causal inference perspective, dependence between x and y given z can arise from distinct causal mechanisms (x causes y, or y causes x). The paper does not address whether or how the choice of which variable to sample influences the results in these asymmetric scenarios.
4. The selection of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 raises serious concerns, as its optimal value is not clearly justified or determined.

Minor:

In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculations appear to be missing normalization by the sample size.
