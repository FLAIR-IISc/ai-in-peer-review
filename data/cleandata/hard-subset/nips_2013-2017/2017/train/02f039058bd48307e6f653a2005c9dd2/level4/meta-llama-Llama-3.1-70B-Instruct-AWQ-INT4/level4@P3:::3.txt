The authors propose a general test to test conditional independence between three continuous variables, without strong parametric assumptions. The method uses samples from the joint distribution to create samples close to the conditional product distribution in total variation distance. These samples are then fed to a binary classifier, such as a deep neural net, to determine whether to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors compare their proposed method to existing ones in literature, both with descriptions and with experimental results on synthetic and real data.

Conditional independence testing is an important tool for modern machine learning, especially in the domain of causal inference, where claims of unconfoundedness are frequently of interest. The proposed method appears straightforward and powerful enough to be used for many causal applications.

The paper is well-written, with clear summaries of the results and methodology. While the details of the theoretical results and proofs can be hard to follow, the authors provide a good balance between technical rigor and accessibility to practitioners.

The most impressive part of the paper is the theoretical results, which provide guarantees for the proposed method. The method is an extension of the approach for testing independence in the paper "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests", but the guarantees of the theoretical results are impressive.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel and powerful method for conditional independence testing.
* The theoretical results provide strong guarantees for the method.
* The paper is well-written and accessible to practitioners.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on synthetic and real data.

Weaknesses:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the method.
* The sensitivity of the test to the classification method used is not thoroughly explored.
* Some sections, such as Section 1.1 of Main Contributions, appear redundant and could be condensed.

Suggestions for improvement:

* Consider adding a discussion of the discrete analog of the method, as the bootstrap may be even more powerful when Z is discrete.
* Provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the test to the classification method used, including the impact of hyperparameter tuning.
* Condense redundant sections, such as Section 1.1 of Main Contributions, to improve the overall flow of the paper.
* Correct minor typos, such as "classifier" in line 157 and "graphs" in line 280.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing and is well-suited for publication in NIPS.
