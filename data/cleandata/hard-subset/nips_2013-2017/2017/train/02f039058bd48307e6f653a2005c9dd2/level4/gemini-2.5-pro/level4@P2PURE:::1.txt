This paper proposes a classifier-based approach for conducting conditional independence tests on i.i.d. data. The core idea is to use a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate synthetic samples from a distribution that approximates the true conditional independence distribution ($f_{CI}$). A classifier is then trained to distinguish these bootstrapped samples from the original observed data. If the classifier's performance is close to random chance, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected. Conversely, if the classifier can distinguish the two distributions with high accuracy, the null hypothesis is rejected.

The authors provide theoretical justification by developing bounds on the total variation distance between the nearest-neighbor bootstrapped distribution and $f_{CI}$. They also provide bounds on the empirical risk for an ideal classifier under settings of both perfect and near conditional independence.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is clearly presented.

**Detailed Comments:**

**Major:**

1.  The proposed method appears to consist of two distinct components: a sampling procedure to approximate $f_{CI}$ and a classification-based test to distinguish distributions. Could either of these components be replaced with existing methods from the literature? For instance, could the permutation-based method from [7] be used for sampling, followed by the proposed classification test? Alternatively, could the nearest-neighbor bootstrap be paired with a standard kernel two-sample test? A comparison of performance with these hybrid approaches would be insightful.

2.  The paper argues that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap distribution closely approximates $f_{CI}$ with a finite sample size. Given this, how would the proposed method perform in the case of weak conditional dependence between X and Y given Z? Since the bootstrapped distribution is only an approximation, it is unclear if the test would have sufficient power to distinguish the observed distribution from the synthetic one in this scenario.

3.  Is the proposed method symmetric with respect to X and Y? From a causal perspective, conditional dependence can arise from different underlying causal structures (e.g., X causes Y or Y causes X). Does the choice of which variable to bootstrap (i.e., resampling X based on its neighbors in Z, or Y) matter in these different scenarios? The authors do not appear to address this potential asymmetry.

4.  A significant concern is the practical selection of the threshold parameter $\tau$ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The paper provides little guidance on how to choose this hyperparameter, which appears crucial for the test's performance.

**Minor:**

1.  In Algorithms 2 and 3, the definition of the empirical risk seems to be missing a normalization term; it should likely be divided by the sample size.
