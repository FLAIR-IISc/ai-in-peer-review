

This paper introduces a model-based approach for conditional independence testing on i.i.d. data. The core idea employs nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution $f_{CI}$, followed by training a classifier to distinguish between the observed data distribution and the bootstrap distribution. If classification performance is near random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is rejected. The authors establish theoretical bounds on the total variation distance between the bootstrap distribution and $f_{CI}$, as well as bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for nearly independent samples. Overall, the problem addressed is significant, and the paper is well-structured and clearly presented.

**Major Comments:**  
1. **Component Substitutability:** The method comprises two key components: (i) generating samples mimicking $f_{CI}$, and (ii) using a classifier for hypothesis testing. Could either component be replaced by established techniques without compromising performance? For instance, could the permutation-based approach from [7] be combined with the classifier, or could nearest-neighbor bootstrap be paired with a kernel two-sample test? The authors should compare the proposed method against such alternatives to justify its novelty and efficacy.  

2. **Performance under Weak Dependence:** The theoretical guarantees assume the bootstrap distribution closely approximates $f_{CI}$ in finite samples. However, if the true dependence between $X$ and $Y$ given $Z$ is weak (i.e., conditional independence is only approximately satisfied), how does the method behave? The current analysis does not address this critical scenario, which is common in practice.  

3. **Asymmetry in Causal Interpretation:** The method treats $X$ and $Y$ symmetrically, but in causal contexts, dependence given $Z$ may arise from distinct causal structures ($X \rightarrow Y$ or $Y \rightarrow X$). The approach does not account for this distinction. For example, if $X$ causes $Y$, sampling $X$ versus $Y$ could yield different bootstrap distributions. The authors should discuss whether the method can distinguish causal directions or if asymmetry is inherently unaddressed.  

4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of $\tau$ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is critical but not justified. How should $\tau$ be selected (e.g., via cross-validation, theoretical bounds, or domain knowledge)? The paper lacks guidance on this hyperparameter, raising concerns about reproducibility and practical deployment.  

**Minor Comments:**  
- In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size (not just computed as a sum) to ensure consistency with standard risk definitions. This is a straightforward correction.  

**Summary:**  
The paper presents a novel framework for conditional independence testing using nearest-neighbor bootstrap and classification. The theoretical contributions on distribution approximation and risk bounds are valuable, and the exposition is generally clear. However, the major concerns—particularly regarding weak dependence, causal asymmetry, component substitutability, and parameter selection—require substantial clarification. Addressing these would strengthen the method’s practical utility and theoretical grounding. The minor comment on empirical risk normalization is easily resolved. If these points are adequately addressed, the paper would make a meaningful contribution to the field.
