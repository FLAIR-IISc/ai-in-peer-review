This paper presents a novel, model-powered approach for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing of continuous random variables. The core idea is to reframe the CI test `X ⊥ Y | Z` as a binary classification problem. The authors tackle the main technical challenge—generating samples from the conditional product distribution `f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)`—by proposing a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. This procedure generates a dataset that is shown to be close in total variation distance to the target distribution. Subsequently, the original samples (labeled class 1) and the bootstrapped samples (labeled class 0) are used to train a powerful classifier, such as a gradient-boosted tree or a deep neural network. The classifier's test error serves as the test statistic: an error close to 50% indicates that the two distributions are indistinguishable, leading to a failure to reject the null hypothesis of CI, while a significantly lower error suggests conditional dependence. This work extends the recent paradigm of using classifiers for two-sample testing (as in Lopez-Paz et al., 2016 for independence testing) to the more complex conditional setting. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the non-i.i.d. samples produced by their method.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-founded, and the claims are strongly supported by both theoretical analysis and comprehensive experiments. The authors provide two key theoretical results: Theorem 1 establishes that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure generates samples that provably converge to the target conditional product distribution, and Theorem 2 provides novel generalization bounds for classification with the "near-independent" samples generated, which is a contribution of independent interest. The empirical evaluation is extensive, demonstrating superior performance over state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real-world data, particularly as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases. The authors are also careful to address potential pitfalls, such as the bias introduced by the bootstrap in the finite sample regime, and propose a practical bias-corrected version of their algorithm (Algorithm 3).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of building intuition for their approach before presenting the formal algorithms and theoretical results. The pipeline is illustrated effectively, and the methodology is described with sufficient detail to enable reproducibility. The paper successfully communicates a complex set of ideas to a broad machine learning audience. A few minor improvements could further enhance clarity:
*   The summary of contributions in Section 1.1 largely repeats information from the introduction and could be condensed.
*   The formal pseudo-code for Algorithm 2 could be described more succinctly in the text, as its steps are already detailed in the preceding paragraphs.
*   There are minor typos: e.g., "classier" should be "classifier" (line 157), and "graph" should be "graphs" (line 280).

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on the conceptual foundation of classifier-based two-sample tests, its extension to the conditional independence setting is non-trivial and novel. The primary original contributions are (1) the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure as a practical and efficient method for sampling from `f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)`, and (2) the accompanying theoretical analysis that guarantees the quality of these samples and provides learning guarantees for the classification task. This stands in contrast to related permutation-based kernel methods that require solving an expensive linear program. The theoretical analysis of Rademacher complexity for near-independent samples is also a significant and novel contribution.

**Significance:** The problem of conditional independence testing is of fundamental importance to machine learning, with direct applications in causal inference, feature selection, and graphical model structure learning. The proposed method, CCIT, represents a significant advance for practitioners. By leveraging the power and flexibility of modern classification models, it provides a highly effective tool that scales well to high-dimensional conditioning sets, a common challenge in real-world problems. This model-powered framework is modular and will naturally benefit from future advances in supervised learning. The work is likely to be influential and widely used by both researchers and practitioners.

**Questions and Suggestions for Authors:**
*   How sensitive is the test's performance to the choice of classifier and its associated hyperparameters? A brief discussion or experiment on the robustness of the results to changes in the classifier's configuration would be a valuable addition.
*   The paper focuses on continuous variables. Could the authors comment on how the method might be extended to handle discrete or mixed-type variables? The nearest-neighbor approach seems especially promising when Z is discrete, as exact matches can be found, potentially simplifying the analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, powerful, and practical method for the fundamental problem of conditional independence testing.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art empirical performance, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
*   Provides strong and non-trivial theoretical guarantees for the proposed methodology.
*   Presents a flexible "model-powered" paradigm that effectively connects statistical testing with modern supervised learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical implementation relies on a choice of classifier and hyperparameters, the sensitivity to which is not fully explored in the paper. However, this is a minor point that does not detract from the paper's core contributions.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions to an important problem. I strongly recommend acceptance.
