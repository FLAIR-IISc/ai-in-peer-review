Summary
This paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test (CCIT) that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from f(x,y,z), the method splits the data, uses a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap to construct pseudo-samples intended to mimic fCI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) by swapping Y values between 1-NN pairs in Z, and then trains a classifier to distinguish the original samples (label 1) from the bootstrapped ones (label 0). Under H0 (X ⟂ Y | Z), the two sets should be indistinguishable; under H1 they should be separable. The paper provides (i) a total variation (TV) bound showing that the NN-bootstrap distribution is close to fCI under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z), with rate O(n^{-1/dz}); (ii) generalization bounds for ERM classifiers trained on the resulting near-independent samples; and (iii) a bias-corrected variant (CCITv2) that subtracts the accuracy of a classifier trained only on (Y,Z) to mitigate finite-sample bias. Empirically, CCIT outperforms kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic data with large conditioning sets and on the flow cytometry protein network dataset. The approach is related to classifier two-sample tests for independence (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab), but addresses CI by constructing approximate fCI samples via a simple NN bootstrap rather than a costly permutation or linear program as in KCIPT.

Relation to prior work
- Kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT, KCIPT) dominate recent literature; KCIPT also tries to emulate fCI but requires solving an expensive LP. CCIT replaces this with a 1-NN swap in Z and leverages modern classifiers.
- Independence testing via classifiers has precedent, but CI requires Z-dependent permutation; CCIT’s NN bootstrap is a novel and practical solution.
- Theoretical analysis includes TV bounds for the NN bootstrap (related in spirit to nearest-neighbor entropy/MI estimation) and generalization bounds for non-i.i.d. samples, complementing results for mixing processes.

Strengths
- Novel and elegant reduction of CI testing to binary classification via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap that approximately samples from fCI.
- Theoretical guarantees: a nontrivial TV bound quantifying closeness to fCI under clear smoothness assumptions; generalization guarantees for classifiers trained on near-independent data, including a Rademacher/VC-style analysis adapted to this setting.
- Practical algorithm with strong empirical performance, scaling to high-dimensional Z where kernel methods degrade. The modularity allows one to plug in advances in classification (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, deep nets).
- A thoughtful bias-correction (comparing all-features vs. YZ-only classifiers) that addresses finite-sample deviations from 0.5 accuracy under H0.

Weaknesses and suggestions for clarity
- Exposition is challenging. Many readers will struggle to interpret the bounds and their practical implications. Please:
  - Provide intuitive explanations for Theorem 1: why 1-NN in Z makes Y approximately an independent draw from f(y|z) when fZ(z) is not too small, and how rare-z regions are controlled by G(·).
  - Clarify the dependence on dz and the curse-of-dimensionality in the O(n^{-1/dz}) rate. Discuss when this is a loose worst-case bound relative to observed performance.
  - Explain the role and calibration of the threshold τ, the finite-sample bias b(n), and how CCITv2 mitigates it in practice (including how to estimate or upper-bound b(n)).
  - State clearly whether Type I error is controlled at a user-specified level α. CCIT currently uses accuracy thresholds; a permutation or cross-fitting scheme to obtain p-values would improve interpretability and comparability with kernel tests.
  - Detail the computational complexity (1-NN search, number of bootstraps B) and sensitivity to the choice of metric on Z (Euclidean vs. learned/standardized metrics).
- The theoretical bounds hinge on smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z) that may be strong in some applications. A discussion of robustness to violations (e.g., heavy tails, local discontinuities) would help.
- The generalization bound presumes an expressive classifier class G; practical consequences of using a weak G (e.g., linear models) should be discussed alongside the theory and in ablation studies.
- Empirical section: include ablations on classifier choice, k in k-NN (k>1), and sensitivity to Z scaling/normalization.

Specific technical question
Section 1.1(iii): Suppose H1 holds and r0 = 0.1, but G is a small class (e.g., linear functions) that cannot distinguish f from fCI at all. Intuitively, how can the stated high-probability bound yield a small error (hat R < 0.1 + o(1) < 0.2 for large n)? In other words, if approximation error dominates (G is misspecified), the Bayes risk within G may be near 0.5 even when dTV(f, fCI) is large. Please clarify which term in your bound captures this approximation error (the gap between the Bayes risk over all measurable classifiers and over G), and how the bound degrades when G is not expressive enough.

Quality
Technically solid and carefully argued. The NN-bootstrap TV bound and non-i.i.d. generalization analysis are substantive contributions. However, the paper should better connect the theory to practice: how to choose τ and G, how to assess and control error rates, and how sensitive the method is to high-dimensional Z and metric choices.

Clarity
The paper would benefit from more intuition, clearer presentation of assumptions, and practical guidance. Streamlining notation and providing a high-level roadmap for proofs would also help.

Originality
High. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap for approximating fCI, coupled with non-i.i.d. generalization bounds for classification-based CI testing, is novel.

Significance
Significant. A flexible, scalable CI test that leverages modern classifiers is of broad interest for causal discovery and feature selection. The theoretical framework is also of independent value.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and practical reduction of CI testing to classification with strong empirical gains.
- Nontrivial theoretical guarantees (TV bounds and near-i.i.d. generalization).
- Simple, implementable algorithm with modular improvements possible.

Arguments against acceptance
- Presentation and interpretability gaps (especially error control and intuition behind bounds).
- Strong smoothness assumptions and unfavorable worst-case dimension dependence, with limited discussion of robustness.
- Limited guidance on classifier choice, calibration of τ, and finite-sample behavior.

Overall, I find the technical contributions compelling, but improving clarity and addressing the questions above would strengthen the paper considerably.
