

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

The paper introduces a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors address the key challenge of generating samples from the conditional product distribution $f_{CI}(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)$ given only samples from the true joint distribution $f(x,y,z)$. They propose a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples close to $f_{CI}$ in total variation distance, then use powerful classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, deep neural networks) to distinguish between original samples (labeled 1) and bootstrap-generated samples (labeled 0), with the classifier's error rate determining the CI test result.

This work builds on recent advances in using classifiers for statistical testing, particularly the "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" paper (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016), which addressed independence testing (X ⊥ Y) through Y-coordinate permutation. The authors extend this to CI testing (X ⊥ Y | Z) with a Z-dependent nearest-neighbor bootstrap, which is more complex than simple permutation due to Z's potential high dimensionality. The paper relates to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and permutation-based methods (KCIPT), but provides theoretical guarantees about the bootstrap samples' proximity to $f_{CI}$ that previous methods lack.

**Quality**

The paper presents technically sound contributions with rigorous theoretical results. The authors prove their nearest-neighbor bootstrap generates samples close to $f_{CI}$ in total variation distance (Theorem 1) and establish generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). The theoretical analysis properly addresses the non-i.i.d nature of the generated samples, a critical aspect of the method. The empirical evaluation demonstrates clear performance gains over state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real datasets, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The authors provide detailed experimental results with ROC AUC scores and analyze how performance scales with Z's dimension, showing their method outperforms alternatives when Z is high-dimensional.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and generally clear, though some technical details in the theoretical sections may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with advanced statistical learning theory. The methodology is well-motivated, with key ideas (bootstrap procedure, reduction to classification) clearly explained. The paper would benefit from a more concise presentation of Algorithm 2, as it is somewhat redundant with earlier descriptions. The authors effectively summarize main contributions and results, making the paper accessible to practitioners.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel approach to CI testing by leveraging modern classifiers through a model-powered framework. While reducing statistical testing to classification is not entirely new, the authors extend it to CI testing with a theoretically justified bootstrap procedure. The key innovation is the nearest-neighbor bootstrap that handles Z-dependent sampling, theoretically analyzed in terms of total variation distance. This approach differs significantly from previous permutation-based methods (KCIPT) that require solving linear programs. The paper clearly articulates how their work differs from prior contributions and provides a compelling analysis of advantages.

**Significance**

Conditional independence testing is fundamental to causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection. The authors' method enables the use of powerful modern classifiers for CI testing, particularly valuable in high-dimensional settings where traditional methods struggle. The empirical results show clear performance gains, especially as Z's dimension increases—a common scenario in real-world applications. The model-powered framework could be extended to other statistical testing problems, making it broadly applicable with significant potential impact.

**Strengths**

1. Strong theoretical foundation with novel contributions to the bootstrap procedure and generalization bounds.
2. Clear empirical advantages over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
3. Flexible approach that leverages advances in classification without requiring major modifications.
4. Well-structured paper with clear summaries of key results, accessible to practitioners.

**Weaknesses**

1. Redundant presentation of the main approach in multiple sections (e.g., Section 1.1).
2. Overly detailed description of Algorithm 2, which is already explained in the text.
3. Insufficient discussion of classifier sensitivity to hyperparameters (would different hyperparameters lead to different test results?).
4. Missing discussion of the discrete Z case, which could be a natural extension where the bootstrap might be even more effective.

**Pro and Con Acceptance List**

*Pro:*
- Strong theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs of bootstrap accuracy and classification bounds.
- Clear empirical advantages over existing methods, especially in high-dimensional settings.
- Well-motivated approach that leverages modern classification tools for a fundamental statistical problem.
- Good balance between theoretical rigor and practical applicability.

*Con:*
- Some redundancy in method description that could be streamlined.
- Insufficient analysis of classifier sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- No discussion of the discrete Z case, which could be a natural extension.

**Overall Recommendation**

This paper makes a significant contribution to conditional independence testing with a theoretically sound, empirically validated approach. The model-powered framework is a promising direction for statistical testing that could be extended to other problems. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and addresses an important problem with practical significance. While minor issues exist with presentation and a few unanswered questions, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality. I recommend acceptance.
