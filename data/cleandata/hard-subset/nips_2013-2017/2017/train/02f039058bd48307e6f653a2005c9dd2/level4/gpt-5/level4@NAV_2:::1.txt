This paper presents a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for i.i.d. continuous data. The key idea is to transform CI testing into a binary classification task: use a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap to synthesize samples whose distribution is close to fCI, and then train a classifier to distinguish the original samples from the bootstrapped ones. If the classifier’s accuracy is near random guessing, one fails to reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence; if the classifier significantly outperforms chance, one rejects the null.

The authors develop bounds on how close the NN-bootstrapped distribution is to fCI in total variation distance, as well as generalization bounds on empirical risk both in an ideal i.i.d. setting and under the near-independence induced by the bootstrap.

Overall, the paper tackles an important problem and is clearly presented.

Detail comments:
Major:
1. The method decomposes naturally into two components: (a) generating samples that approximate fCI, and (b) using a classifier for the decision. Can either component be swapped with existing techniques? For example, could one use the permutation-based approach from prior work [7] together with the classification step, or combine the NN bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test? A comparison of these hybrids would be informative.
2. The NN bootstrap is only approximately fCI under finite samples. If the ground truth exhibits weak dependence of X and Y given Z, how sensitive is the proposed test in this near-null regime?
3. Is the method fully symmetric in X and Y? From a causal perspective, dependence given Z might arise via X → Y or Y → X. In such settings, does one need to decide which variable to resample or treat differently? This aspect is not discussed.
4. The choice of the threshold parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is a significant practical concern. Clear guidance or a data-driven calibration procedure would help.

Minor:
- In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be normalized by the sample size.
