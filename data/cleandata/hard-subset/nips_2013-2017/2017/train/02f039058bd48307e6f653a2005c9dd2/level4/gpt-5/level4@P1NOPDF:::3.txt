The paper proposes a general test of conditional independence among three continuous variables (X, Y, Z). It avoids strong parametric assumptions: the method uses samples from the joint distribution to construct proxy samples that are close, in total variation distance, to the conditional product distribution P(X|Z)P(Y|Z)P(Z). A binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network) is then trained to distinguish between samples from the joint and from this proxy distribution; the classifier’s predictive error serves as the test statistic to accept or reject the null H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. The authors compare their method to existing approaches, both conceptually and through experiments on synthetic and real data.

Conditional independence testing is important in modern machine learning, especially for causal inference, where questions of unconfoundedness are central. The proposed test appears straightforward and potentially powerful for many causal applications.

The paper is generally well written. Although some of the theoretical details and proofs are dense, the authors do a good job summarizing the results and presenting the methodology clearly for practitioners.

The theoretical results are, in my view, the most impressive aspect. The approach is a natural extension of the classifier two-sample test of “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as the authors acknowledge: they incorporate a third variable Z, and after matching on neighbors in Z, the procedure closely parallels the original test. Nonetheless, the theoretical guarantees established here are strong and compelling.

Comments and suggestions:
- Discrete analog: Is there a discrete version of the method? When Z is discrete, the resampling/bootstrap strategy should be even more powerful; a discussion would be useful.
- Sensitivity to classification choices: How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters? Could modest changes in tuning flip accept/reject decisions? Reporting robustness checks or sensitivity analyses alongside the simulations would strengthen the empirical section.
- Redundancy: Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) repeats the summary multiple times; consider tightening to avoid redundancy.
- Algorithm 2: Writing out Algorithm 2 in full seems unnecessary; the process can be described more succinctly and is already explained earlier in the paper.
- Typos: “classifier” (line 157) and “graphs” (line 280).
