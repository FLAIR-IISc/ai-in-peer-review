This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence testing for i.i.d. data. The core idea is to use a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples from a distribution close to f^{CI} (the distribution under conditional independence), and then train a classifier to distinguish the observed joint distribution from the nearest-neighbor–bootstrapped distribution. If classification performance is close to random guessing, one fails to reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence; otherwise, one rejects it.

The authors develop bounds on the total variation distance between the nearest-neighbor–bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI}, as well as bounds on empirical risk under idealized classification settings and for nearly independent samples.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is clearly presented.

Detailed comments:

Major:
1. The method decomposes naturally into two components: (i) generating samples that mimic f^{CI}, and (ii) using a classifier to make a decision. Can either component be replaced by existing methods from the literature? For example, could one use the permutation-based approach in [7] combined with classification, or pair the nearest-neighbor bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test (e.g., MMD)? I am curious how these hybrids would perform in practice, both in terms of power and Type I error, relative to the proposed pipeline.

2. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap approximates f^{CI} only in finite samples. If, in truth, X and Y are weakly dependent given Z, how will the proposed method behave? In particular, how sensitive is the test to small departures from conditional independence? It would be helpful to understand the power in this “near-CI” regime and whether the classifier-based decision rule can reliably detect weak conditional dependence, or if the approximation error of the bootstrap masks it.

3. Is the method fully symmetric with respect to X and Y? From a causal perspective, dependence given Z can arise from different causal structures (X → Y or Y → X). In such scenarios, does the procedure require choosing which variable to resample or condition on in the nearest-neighbor step, and does that choice affect performance? The paper does not discuss this design issue. Clarifying whether the test is invariant to swapping X and Y, and if not, providing guidance, would strengthen the contribution.

4. I am seriously concerned about how to choose the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3. How should τ be set in practice? Is there theoretical guidance (e.g., scaling with sample size or dimension) or a recommended data-driven selection procedure? The test’s validity and power may be sensitive to τ, so practical recommendations are important.

Minor:
- In Algorithms 2 and 3, it appears that the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size (i.e., reported as an average loss), not left unnormalized.
