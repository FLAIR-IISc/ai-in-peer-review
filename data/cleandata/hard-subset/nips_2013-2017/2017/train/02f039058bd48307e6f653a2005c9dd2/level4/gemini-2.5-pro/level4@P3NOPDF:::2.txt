This paper introduces a novel method for conditional independence (CI) testing based on a classification framework. The goal is to distinguish between samples from the joint distribution and samples from the product of conditional distributions. The central contribution is the introduction of a new smoothness assumption on the conditional density function. The authors leverage this assumption to provide theoretical guarantees for their test, specifically by bounding the total variation distance between the null and bootstrap distributions.

### Strengths

1.  The paper astutely identifies the necessity of a smoothness assumption on the conditional density for CI tests to be effective. The authors formalize such an assumption and use it to derive a novel and significant result: a bound on the total variation distance between the density under the null hypothesis and the density obtained from the bootstrap procedure. This theoretical foundation is a key strength.

2.  The main theoretical result, Theorem 1, is non-trivial and insightful. It elegantly connects three crucial components: (i) the proposed smoothness assumption, (ii) the ability of a chosen classifier family to approximate the optimal Bayes classifier (i.e., achieving a risk `R*` close to the minimum possible), and (iii) the generalization error of the trained classifier (the difference between its empirical risk `hat R` and true risk `R`). This theorem provides a clear and comprehensive framework for understanding the conditions under which the proposed CI test is guaranteed to work.

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of the paper is its clarity and presentation. The writing is dense, and the theoretical results are presented with limited intuition or interpretation, making them difficult to parse. This lack of explanation makes it challenging for the reader to gain a deep understanding of the method's mechanics and the implications of the theorems. Consequently, I am uncertain if I have correctly interpreted some key aspects of the proposed framework.

To illustrate my main point of confusion, please consider the following scenario based on the inequality in Section 1.1 (iii). Assume the alternative hypothesis holds, and the optimal Bayes risk is, for example, `r0 = 0.1`. Now, suppose the class of classifiers `G` we use for the test is not sufficiently expressive to capture the dependence between the variables. For instance, `G` could be the class of linear functions, while the true dependence is highly non-linear.

In this case, any classifier `g` in `G` would be unable to distinguish between the joint distribution `f` and the product of conditionals `f^CI`, and its risk `R(g)` would be close to 0.5. The empirical risk of the trained classifier, `hat R`, should also concentrate around 0.5 for large `n`. However, the test appears to rely on `hat R` being small (e.g., `hat R < 0.1 + o(1) < 0.2`) to correctly reject the null hypothesis.

Could the authors provide an intuitive explanation for how the test can succeed in this scenario? It seems that the validity of the test implicitly requires that the chosen function class `G` is rich enough to approximate the Bayes optimal classifier, an assumption that should be stated more explicitly and discussed as a practical consideration for users of the method. Clarifying this point would significantly improve the paper's impact and accessibility.
