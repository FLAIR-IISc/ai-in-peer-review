The paper proposes a novel conditional independence test, offering several key strengths.

Firstly, the authors recognize the necessity of assuming smoothness of the conditional density for the test to be effective. They introduce such an assumption and establish a bound on the total variation distance between the density under the null hypothesis and the density obtained from bootstrap sampling. This contribution is particularly noteworthy.

Secondly, the paper utilizes this assumption to derive a bound on the error of the optimal classifier on the training set, as presented in equation (1.1 iii). This result is significant and demonstrates the practical implications of the proposed assumption.

Lastly, Theorem 1 is a novel and insightful result that combines the smoothness assumption, the achievability of the estimated risk with error η, and the difference in errors between the optimal and trained classifiers. This theorem is non-trivial and showcases the authors' rigorous approach to conditional independence testing.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The writing is dense and challenging to follow, which hinders the interpretation of the results. As a consequence, it is difficult to grasp the implications of the theorems and proofs presented. For instance, considering the inequality in Section 1.1 iii), if we assume the alternative hypothesis holds and set r0 = 0.1, it is unclear how a small probability of error can be achieved when the class of functions G is not expressive enough to distinguish between f and f^CI. Specifically, if G is a small class, such as linear functions, and these functions fail to capture the underlying differences, it is not immediately apparent how the condition hat R < 0.1 + o1 < 0.2 can be satisfied for large n. A more intuitive explanation of this result would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and impact.
