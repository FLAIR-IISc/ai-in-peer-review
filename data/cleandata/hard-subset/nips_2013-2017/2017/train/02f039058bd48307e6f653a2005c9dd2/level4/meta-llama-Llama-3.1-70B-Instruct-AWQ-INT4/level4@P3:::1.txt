This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conduct conditional independence tests for i.i.d. data. The basic idea is to use nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that follow a distribution close to the conditionally independent distribution f^{CI} and train a classifier to distinguish between the observed distribution and the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution. If the classification performance is close to random guessing, one fails to reject the null hypothesis that the data follows conditional independence; otherwise, one rejects the null hypothesis.

The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI} in terms of total variational distance and bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for near-independent samples.

The paper addresses an important problem and is presented in a clear manner. However, several concerns and questions arise:

Major:

1. The method can be decoupled into two major components: generating samples that mimic f^{CI} and using a classifier for determination. It is unclear whether existing solutions from the literature can be used in place of either component. For example, using the permutation-based method proposed in [7] and then the classification-based approach to solve the problem, or using the nearest neighbor bootstrap and the kernel two-sample test approach together to solve the problem. The performances of these alternative approaches are unknown.

2. The nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution is close to f^{CI} in the finite sample size setting. However, if the ground truth is that x and y are weakly dependent given z, it is unclear how the proposed method will perform.

3. The method may not be totally symmetric with respect to x and y. In the causal perspective, x and y can be dependent given z due to two different causal models: x causes y or y causes x. In these two different scenarios, it may be necessary to determine which variable to sample. The authors do not consider this problem.

4. The choice of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is unclear and may require further investigation.

Minor:

In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size.

Overall, while the paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence testing, further clarification and investigation are needed to address the concerns and questions raised above.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence testing using nearest neighbor bootstrap and classification.
* The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI} in terms of total variational distance and bounds on empirical risks.
* The paper is well-presented and easy to follow.

Weaknesses:

* The method may not perform well when x and y are weakly dependent given z.
* The choice of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is unclear.
* The method may not be symmetric with respect to x and y.
* The paper does not consider the causal perspective and the potential need to determine which variable to sample.
