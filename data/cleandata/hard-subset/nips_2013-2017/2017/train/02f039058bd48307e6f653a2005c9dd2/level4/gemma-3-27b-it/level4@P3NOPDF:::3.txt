## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

This paper presents a novel and generally applicable test for conditional independence between three continuous random variables. The authors circumvent strong parametric assumptions by constructing samples approximating the conditional product distribution using samples from the joint distribution, achieving a total variation distance bound. These generated samples are then fed into a binary classifier – such as a deep neural network – and the classifier’s performance is used to determine whether to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The approach is thoroughly evaluated through comparisons with existing methods, supported by both synthetic and real-world experimental results.

Conditional independence testing is a fundamental tool in modern machine learning, and particularly vital in causal inference where assessing unconfoundedness is frequently required. This work offers a promising addition to the existing toolkit. The proposed method appears both conceptually straightforward and practically powerful, with the potential for broad applicability across numerous causal modeling scenarios. 

The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex method. While the theoretical details and accompanying proofs require careful consideration, the authors effectively summarize the key results and clearly articulate the methodology for practitioners. The strength of this work lies in its theoretical guarantees. The method builds upon the framework established in “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as acknowledged by the authors, extending it to accommodate a third variable *Z* through a neighbor-matching procedure. Despite this extension, the resulting theoretical bounds are impressively strong and provide solid justification for the method’s efficacy. 

I have several suggestions that could further strengthen the paper:

* **Discrete Variable Extension:**  It would be valuable to consider the performance of the method when *Z* is a discrete variable. The bootstrap approach, for example, often gains power in discrete settings. Exploring this could broaden the applicability and understanding of the technique.

* **Classifier Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the test to the choice of classification method and its hyperparameters warrants investigation. Different classifiers and hyperparameter configurations could potentially lead to differing conclusions (acceptance or rejection of the null hypothesis). Including a sensitivity analysis alongside the existing simulations would strengthen confidence in the robustness of the proposed test.

* **Streamlining the Presentation:** Section 1.1, outlining the main contributions, feels somewhat redundant. The authors reiterate their approach multiple times, and the length of this section could be reduced without loss of clarity. 

* **Algorithm Conciseness:** The detailed presentation of Algorithm 2 is arguably unnecessary. The process is adequately described elsewhere in the paper, and a more concise description would improve readability.

* **Minor Corrections:** I identified a few minor typos: “classifier” should be used in line 157, and “graphs” in line 280.



Overall, this is a strong submission with significant potential. The theoretical contribution is substantial, the method is clearly explained, and the empirical evaluation is thorough. Addressing the suggestions above would further refine the paper and solidify its contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
