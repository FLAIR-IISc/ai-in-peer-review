This paper introduces a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables. The core idea is to transform the CI test into a binary classification problem, leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The key technical challenge – generating samples from the conditional product distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) – is addressed through a new nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure, with theoretical guarantees on its accuracy in terms of total variational distance. The authors then develop generalization bounds for the resulting classification task, leading to error bounds for the CI test. Empirical validation on both simulated and real datasets demonstrates performance gains over existing methods.

CI testing is a fundamental problem with applications in areas like causal inference, Bayesian network structure learning, and feature selection. The proposed method offers a compelling, non-parametric approach, particularly valuable when strong distributional assumptions are undesirable. The approach is particularly appealing due to its potential to benefit from advancements in binary classification techniques; improvements in classifiers directly translate to improvements in the CI test.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The authors effectively summarize their contributions and methodology, making it accessible to both researchers and practitioners. While the theoretical details are mathematically dense, the explanations are generally clear and well-motivated. The empirical results provide strong support for the proposed method, showcasing its superior performance compared to state-of-the-art alternatives. The inclusion of a real-world application to flow cytometry data further strengthens the practical relevance of the work.

A significant strength of the paper lies in its theoretical grounding. The authors provide rigorous guarantees for both the accuracy of the bootstrapped samples and the generalization performance of the classifier, going beyond a purely empirical evaluation. The extension of existing generalization bounds to the near-independent sample setting is a valuable contribution in itself.  The connection to and differentiation from prior work, such as “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” [Lopez-Paz et al., 2016], is clearly articulated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The model-powered approach to CI testing, coupled with the nearest neighbor bootstrap and theoretical analysis, represents a novel contribution to the field.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the accuracy of the bootstrap procedure and the generalization performance of the classifier.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The empirical results demonstrate significant performance gains over existing CI testing methods on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The method is applicable to a wide range of problems in machine learning and statistics, particularly those involving causal inference.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and theoretical results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Algorithm Complexity:** While the core idea is intuitive, the full algorithm, including the bootstrap and classifier training, may be computationally demanding, especially for high-dimensional datasets.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** The sensitivity of the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters could be further investigated. A more detailed analysis of this aspect would enhance the practical utility of the method.
*   **Redundancy:** Some sections (e.g., the repeated summarization of contributions in Section 1.1) could be streamlined for improved conciseness.
* **Algorithm 2 & 3 detail:** The inclusion of detailed pseudocode for Algorithms 2 & 3 is somewhat verbose and detracts from the flow of the paper.

**Specific Suggestions:**

*   Explore the potential benefits of applying the bootstrap procedure when Z is discrete, as this could simplify the sample generation process.
*   Conduct a sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of different classifiers and hyperparameter settings on the performance of the CI test.
*   Consider streamlining Section 1.1 to avoid redundant summaries of the main contributions.
*   Remove or condense the detailed pseudocode for Algorithms 2 and 3, relying instead on a more concise textual description.
*   Correct minor typos (e.g., "classifier" in line 157, "graphs" in line 280).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The novel approach, rigorous theoretical analysis, and compelling empirical results warrant acceptance. Addressing the minor concerns outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
