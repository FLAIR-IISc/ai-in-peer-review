This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables. The core idea is to leverage supervised learning – specifically, classification – to distinguish between the joint distribution and the conditional independence distribution. A novel nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure is introduced to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, enabling this classification-based approach. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (in terms of total variational distance) and establish generalization bounds for the classification step, accounting for the near-independence introduced by the bootstrap.

The paper addresses an important problem in statistical inference with applications in causal discovery, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection. The approach of framing CI testing as a classification problem is interesting and allows the utilization of powerful, modern machine learning techniques. The presentation is generally clear and well-structured. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work, particularly in kernel-based CI testing and recent advances in classification.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** Framing CI testing as a classification problem offers a fresh perspective and leverages the power of modern classifiers.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical results regarding the quality of the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for the classifier, adding rigor to the proposed method.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate promising performance gains over existing methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the methodology is described in a reasonably accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modularity and Alternatives:** The method’s two-stage nature (bootstrap sample generation and classification) invites the question of whether individual components could be replaced with existing solutions. The authors do not adequately address this, and a comparative analysis against alternatives – such as combining the permutation method from [19] with classification, or the bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test – would strengthen the paper.
*   **Weak Dependence & Sensitivity:** When the true relationship between X and Y given Z is weak, the proposed method might struggle to reliably distinguish between the distributions. The impact of this weakness, and potential remedies, are not fully explored.
*   **Symmetry and Causal Direction:** The method appears symmetric with respect to X and Y, potentially obscuring causal directionality. In causal inference, distinguishing X -> Y | Z from Y -> X | Z is crucial. The paper doesn't address whether or how to adapt the method to identify causal direction.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity (τ):** The choice of the threshold parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is critical but lacks sufficient justification. A more principled approach to setting τ or a sensitivity analysis demonstrating robustness to its value is needed.
*   **Minor Technical Point:** In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculation appears to be missing normalization by the sample size (i.e., division by *n*).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and promising approach to CI testing with strong theoretical foundations.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the method’s effectiveness, particularly in high-dimensional scenarios.
*   The work addresses a significant problem in statistical inference with broad applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper does not thoroughly investigate the performance implications of replacing individual components with existing methods.
*   The handling of weak dependencies and causal directionality is limited.
*   The sensitivity of the method to the parameter τ is a concern.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The proposed method is innovative, theoretically sound, and empirically promising. However, the concerns regarding modularity, weak dependencies, causal direction, and parameter sensitivity prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **weak reject** with encouragement to address the raised concerns in a revised version. Specifically, the authors should: (1) provide a comparative analysis against alternative approaches; (2) discuss the method's behavior under weak dependencies; (3) consider how to incorporate causal directionality; and (4) provide guidance on selecting the threshold parameter τ. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and its contribution to the NIPS community.
