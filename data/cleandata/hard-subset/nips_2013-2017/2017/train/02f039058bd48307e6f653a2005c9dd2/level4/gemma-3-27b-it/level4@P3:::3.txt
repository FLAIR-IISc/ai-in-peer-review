This paper addresses the important problem of non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables. The authors propose a novel model-powered approach, framing the CI test as a binary classification problem. The core idea is to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure, and then leverage powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees or deep neural networks) to distinguish between the joint and conditionally independent distributions. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the generated samples and classification performance, and demonstrates empirical improvements over existing methods on both simulated and real datasets.

This work builds upon a rich history of CI testing methods, particularly kernel-based approaches [32, 28, 10] and recent work utilizing classification for independence testing [19]. The proposed method offers a compelling alternative by capitalizing on the strengths of modern classification techniques, potentially scaling better to high-dimensional data. The reduction to a classification problem is a clever and promising direction, allowing the authors to harness recent advancements in that field. The theoretical analysis, particularly the generalization bounds for classification under near-independence, is a significant contribution and distinguishes this work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The model-powered approach, reducing CI testing to a classification problem, is a fresh perspective.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including bounds on the total variational distance between generated and target distributions, and generalization bounds for the classifier.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches on both synthetic and real data. The scaling observed with respect to the dimension of Z is particularly encouraging.
* **Clarity (Overall):** The paper is generally well-written and the core methodology is clearly explained, even if some theoretical details are complex.
* **Practicality:** The availability of a public code repository enhances the reproducibility and usability of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Redundancy:** Section 1.1 (“Main Contributions”) feels repetitive. The core ideas are already well-established in the introduction.
* **Algorithm Presentation:**  Presenting Algorithm 2 in detail seems unnecessary given its prior explanation. A more concise description would suffice.
* **Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper lacks an investigation into the sensitivity of the test's performance to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters. Demonstrating robustness to these factors would strengthen the claims.
* **Discrete Case:** The potential benefits of applying the bootstrap procedure when Z is discrete are not discussed. Exploring this aspect could be valuable.
* **Minor Issues:** There are a few minor typos ("classifier" in line 157, "graphs" in line 280) that should be addressed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to a fundamental problem in machine learning and statistics.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the performance of the proposed method.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over existing approaches.
* The code availability promotes reproducibility and wider adoption of the method.
* The work addresses a challenging problem and makes a demonstrable advance in the state of the art.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The redundancy in Section 1.1 and the detailed presentation of Algorithm 2 detract slightly from the paper's overall conciseness.
* The lack of a sensitivity analysis regarding classifier selection is a concern.
* While the theoretical guarantees are strong, they rely on smoothness assumptions that may not hold in all practical scenarios.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The proposed method is novel, theoretically well-grounded, and empirically effective. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not outweigh the paper's contributions. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2017.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** 

I recommend the authors address the redundancy in Section 1.1, consider streamlining the presentation of Algorithm 2, and potentially add a brief discussion regarding the method’s performance with discrete Z variables. A sensitivity analysis of the classifier selection would further bolster the paper’s claims.
