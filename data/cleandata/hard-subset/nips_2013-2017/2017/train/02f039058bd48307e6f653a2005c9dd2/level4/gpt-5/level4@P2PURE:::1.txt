This paper proposes a model-powered approach to testing conditional independence for i.i.d. data. The core idea is to use a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap to generate samples from a distribution that is close to f^{CI}, and then train a classifier to distinguish the observed data distribution from the NN-bootstrapped distribution. If the classifier’s performance is close to random guessing, one fails to reject the null hypothesis that the data are conditionally independent; otherwise, one rejects the null.

The authors develop bounds on the total variation distance between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI}, as well as bounds on empirical risks under idealized classification settings and for nearly independent samples.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is clearly presented.

Detailed comments:
Major:
1. The method decomposes into two main components: (i) generating samples that approximate f^{CI}, and (ii) using a classifier to make a decision. Can either component be replaced by existing methods from the literature? For instance, could one use the permutation-based approach from [7] together with the classification-based decision rule? Conversely, could one combine the NN bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test? I am curious about the comparative performance of such hybrids.
2. The NN-bootstrap distribution is only approximately f^{CI} in finite samples. If, in truth, x and y are only weakly dependent given z, how will the proposed method behave in this regime? In particular, how sensitive is it to weak conditional dependence when the bootstrap approximation error is non-negligible?
3. Is the method fully symmetric with respect to x and y? From a causal perspective, x and y may be dependent given z due to different causal structures (x → y or y → x). In these two scenarios, do we need to decide which variable to sample or model? The paper does not discuss this issue.
4. I am seriously concerned about how to choose the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3.

Minor:
- In Algorithms 2 and 3, it appears that the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size.
