This paper proposes a model-powered approach to perform conditional independence (CI) tests for independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data. The core idea leverages nearest neighbor bootstrapping to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, *f*<sup>CI</sup>. A classifier is then trained and evaluated to determine whether it can distinguish between observations drawn from the true data distribution and those generated via the nearest neighbor bootstrap. If classification performance is near chance level, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is. The authors develop theoretical bounds on the closeness of the bootstrapped distribution to *f*<sup>CI</sup>, specifically in terms of total variational distance, and provide bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification scenarios and for nearly-independent samples. 

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem – efficient and statistically sound CI testing – and presents the approach in a relatively clear and accessible manner. However, several significant concerns require addressing before acceptance.

**Major Concerns:**

1. **Modularity and Existing Alternatives:** The proposed method appears decomposable into two main components: sampling to approximate *f*<sup>CI</sup>, and classification to assess distributional similarity. I question whether each component necessitates the specific approach taken in this work. Could either component be replaced by existing, well-established methods from the literature without significantly compromising performance? For example, one could consider utilizing permutation-based tests (e.g., as proposed in [7]) coupled with a classification framework. Alternatively, the nearest neighbor bootstrap could be combined with a kernel two-sample test. A comparative analysis demonstrating the advantages of the proposed combination over these alternatives is crucial.

2. **Weak Dependence and Detection:** The theoretical guarantees demonstrate the proximity of the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution to *f*<sup>CI</sup>, but this is established within a finite sample size setting.  If the ground truth is that *x* and *y* are *weakly* dependent given *z*, how would the proposed method reliably distinguish this weak dependence from the noise inherent in the bootstrapping process? The potential for false negatives in the presence of subtle dependencies warrants further investigation and discussion.

3. **Symmetry and Causal Direction:** The method’s symmetry with respect to *x* and *y* is unclear. From a causal perspective, dependence between *x* and *y* given *z* can arise from *x* causing *y*, or *y* causing *x*. These scenarios may require different sampling strategies. Does the method inherently favor one direction of influence over the other? The authors do not address the implications of causal directionality or offer guidance on which variable to sample in differing causal scenarios. Addressing this asymmetry would significantly strengthen the work.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity ($\tau$):** I have serious concerns regarding the selection of the parameter *τ* in Algorithms 2 and 3. The paper lacks a clear justification for how this parameter should be chosen in practice. What is the sensitivity of the method’s performance to different values of *τ*? Providing guidelines or a principled method for selecting an appropriate *τ* is essential for the practical applicability of the proposed algorithms.

**Minor Comments:**

* In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculations appear to omit division by the sample size. This normalization is standard practice and should be included to obtain a properly scaled risk estimate.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting approach to conditional independence testing, grounded in theoretical analysis. However, the concerns raised above – particularly regarding the modularity of the approach, the handling of weak dependencies, the lack of consideration for causal directionality, and the parameter selection issue – represent substantial weaknesses.  Addressing these points through additional analysis, experimentation, and a more thorough discussion would substantially improve the paper’s contribution and justification for publication. Currently, I lean towards a **weak reject**. Significant revisions addressing the major concerns are necessary for reconsideration.
