- Proposes reducing conditional independence testing to binary classification using samples from the joint labeled 1 and samples from a nearest-neighbor bootstrap approximating the CI distribution labeled 0.  
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) to generate samples close to the conditionally independent distribution from i.i.d. joint samples.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees that bootstrapped samples approximate f_CI in total variation distance and gives generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d. samples.  
- Leverages powerful classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees) and shows superior empirical performance over kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) in high-dimensional settings.  
- Addresses an important problem with applications to causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection.  
- Original methodological contribution by extending classifier-based independence testing to the conditional case with a novel bootstrap and theoretical analysis.  
- Strong theoretical quality: proofs are sound under stated smoothness assumptions and non-i.i.d. generalization analysis is valuable.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough, including synthetic experiments and real-world flow cytometry data, demonstrating near-perfect performance up to 70 dimensions.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and reproducible with precise definitions and algorithmic detail.  
- Weakness: choice of threshold parameter τ in Algorithms 2/3 is not well-justified and appears arbitrary; sensitivity analysis is missing.  
- Weakness: method’s power when X and Y are weakly dependent given Z (small d_TV) is not addressed and may lead to low detection power.  
- Weakness: asymmetry between X and Y in the method is problematic for causal interpretation and could cause misinterpretation in causal discovery.  
- Weakness: theoretical smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1–2) are strong; more intuition or evidence of realism for applications is needed.  
- Pros for acceptance: significant empirical improvements, novel and theoretically justified bootstrap, leverages ML classifiers, and a clear contribution to an important problem.  
- Cons for acceptance: lack of principled τ selection, untested performance on weak conditional dependencies, asymmetry concerns for causal use, and potentially restrictive theoretical assumptions.  
- Overall recommendation: accept, with suggested follow-up on principled parameter selection and investigation of X/Y asymmetry.
