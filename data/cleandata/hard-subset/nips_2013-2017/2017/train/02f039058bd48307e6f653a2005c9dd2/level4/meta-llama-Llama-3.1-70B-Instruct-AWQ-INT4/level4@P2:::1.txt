This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conduct conditional independence tests for i.i.d. data. The basic idea is to use nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that follow a distribution close to the conditionally independent distribution (f^{CI}) and train a classifier to distinguish between the observation distribution and the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution. If the classification performance is close to random guessing, one fails to reject the null hypothesis that data follows conditional independence; otherwise, one accepts the null hypothesis.

The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI} in terms of total variational distance and bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for near-independent samples. The paper addresses an important problem and is presented in a clear way.

Major Comments:

1. The method can be decoupled into two major components: generating samples that mimic f_{CI} and using a classifier for determination. It is unclear whether existing solutions from literature can replace either of these steps. For example, using the permutation-based method proposed in [7] and then the classification-based approach to solve the problem, or using the nearest neighbor bootstrap and the kernel two-sample test approach together to solve the problem. The performance of these alternatives is unknown.

2. The nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution is close to f_{CI} in the finite sample size setting. However, if the ground truth is that x and y are weakly dependent given z, it is unclear how the proposed method will perform.

3. The method does not seem to be totally symmetric with respect to x and y. In the causal perspective, x and y can be dependent given z due to two different causal models: x causes y or y causes x. In these two different scenarios, it is unclear whether one needs to determine which variable to sample.

4. The choice of the parameter $\tau$ in algorithms 2 and 3 is not clear and may be a concern.

Minor Comment:

In algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size.

Strengths:

* The paper addresses an important problem in conditional independence testing.
* The approach is novel and leverages powerful supervised learning tools.
* The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and empirical risk bounds.

Weaknesses:

* The method's performance in the finite sample size setting is unclear, particularly when x and y are weakly dependent given z.
* The choice of the parameter $\tau$ is not clear and may be a concern.
* The method may not be symmetric with respect to x and y, which could be a limitation in certain applications.

Originality:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence testing using nearest neighbor bootstrap and classification.
* The authors provide new theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples and empirical risk bounds.
