- Proposes a general nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test for three continuous variables by constructing surrogate samples approximating f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) and framing CI testing as a binary classification task.
- Uses high-capacity classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, deep nets) to distinguish true joint samples from surrogates and decides accept/reject based on predictive error.
- Avoids strong parametric assumptions, making the method broadly applicable to causal analysis and unconfoundedness checks.
- Paper is clearly written with practitioner-friendly methodology despite some dense theoretical proofs.
- Strong theoretical contributions and guarantees, extending classifier-based two-sample tests to the conditional case.
- Empirical evaluation includes synthetic experiments and a real flow cytometry dataset, comparing to prior methods conceptually and empirically.
- Relates directly to prior work (“Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests”) and acknowledges that after matching on Z the procedures align.
- Requests clarification on a discrete Z analogue and whether a tailored bootstrap/variant would be more effective for discrete conditioning.
- Raises concern about sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters and recommends a robustness analysis.
- Recommends condensing repetitive content in Section 1.1 and shortening Algorithm 2’s full presentation.
