- Core idea: use an NN-bootstrap to generate samples approximating f^{CI} and train a classifier to distinguish observed vs. bootstrapped data; classifier near random implies fail to reject CI.  
- Theory: provides bounds on total variation between NN-bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI}, and bounds on empirical risks for idealized classifiers and nearly independent samples.  
- Presentation: addresses an important problem and is clearly written.  
- Major 1 (modularity): asks whether each component can be replaced by existing methods (e.g., permutation-based approach [7] or kernel two-sample tests) and requests comparison of such hybrids.  
- Major 2 (sensitivity): questions method behavior when x and y are only weakly conditionally dependent and bootstrap approximation error is non-negligible.  
- Major 3 (symmetry/causal): asks if the method is symmetric in x and y and whether causal direction (x→y vs y→x) affects which variable to sample or model.  
- Major 4 (tuning): expresses serious concern about how to choose parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3.  
- Minor: empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should likely be normalized by dividing by the sample size.
