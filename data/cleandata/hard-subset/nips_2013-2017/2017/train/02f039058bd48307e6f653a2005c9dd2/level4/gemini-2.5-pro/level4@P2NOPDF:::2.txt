This paper proposes a new classifier-based conditional independence test.

### Strengths

1.  A key contribution of this work is the identification that for such a test to be theoretically sound, an assumption on the smoothness of the conditional density is required. The authors propose a formal smoothness assumption and, leveraging this, derive a novel bound on the total variation distance between the data-generating distribution under the null hypothesis and the distribution of the bootstrapped sample.

2.  This smoothness assumption is then used effectively to bound the error of the optimal classifier on the training set, as detailed in the inequality in section 1.1 (iii).

3.  Theorem 1 is a novel, insightful, and non-trivial theoretical result. It successfully integrates three critical components: the proposed smoothness assumption, the assumption that the empirical risk (`hat R`) can be optimized to within an error `eta`, and a bound on the generalization error (the difference in errors between the optimal and trained classifiers).

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its presentation. It is difficult to read and lacks sufficient interpretation of the theoretical results, which hinders comprehension to the extent that I am uncertain if I have correctly understood the central claims.

For example, I struggled to build an intuition for the result presented in section 1.1 (iii). To illustrate my confusion, consider the following scenario:
Assume the alternative hypothesis holds, and the misclassification rate of the optimal Bayes classifier is `r_0 = 0.1`. Let `G` be the class of classifiers used for the test, and suppose `G` is a relatively simple class (e.g., linear functions). Furthermore, assume `G` is not expressive enough to distinguish between the true joint distribution `f` and the factorized distribution under the null hypothesis, `f^CI`. In this case, any classifier from `G` would likely perform poorly.

The paper's result suggests that the empirical risk `hat R` should concentrate near `r_0`, such that `hat R < 0.1 + o_1` for some small `o_1` with high probability as `n` grows large. However, if the classifier class `G` cannot capture the underlying dependency, it is not clear how the trained classifier can achieve an empirical risk close to the optimal rate `r_0`. An intuitive explanation of how the theorem ensures this outcome, particularly in a challenging scenario like the one described, would significantly clarify the power of the proposed smoothness assumption and strengthen the paper.
