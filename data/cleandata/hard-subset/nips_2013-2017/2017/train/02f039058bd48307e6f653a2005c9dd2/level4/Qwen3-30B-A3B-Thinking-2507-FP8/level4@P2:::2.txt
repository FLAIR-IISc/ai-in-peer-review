

This paper introduces a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The authors propose a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI, which they theoretically show converges to f_CI in total variation distance under smoothness assumptions. They then develop generalization bounds for classifiers trained on these bootstrapped samples, translating these into error bounds for CI testing. The approach leverages powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks to achieve state-of-the-art performance in high-dimensional CI testing.

The work builds upon recent advances in model-driven approaches to statistical testing, particularly the use of binary classifiers for independence testing [19]. Unlike prior kernel-based methods [28, 32, 10], which rely on complex permutations or kernel methods for CI testing, the authors provide a simpler and theoretically grounded approach using nearest-neighbor bootstrapping. Their method also differs from [10] by providing theoretical guarantees on the distance between generated samples and f_CI, and using classifiers instead of kernel-based two-sample tests.

Strengths:
1. **Quality**: The theoretical contributions are strong. Theorem 1 establishes meaningful bounds on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped samples and f_CI under reasonable smoothness assumptions. The authors provide novel generalization bounds for classifiers trained on non-i.i.d samples (Theorem 2), which is particularly valuable as the bootstrapped samples are not perfectly i.i.d. The empirical validation on synthetic data and the flow-cytometry dataset demonstrates clear performance gains over state-of-the-art methods [28, 32, 10], especially as the dimension of Z increases.

2. **Originality**: The core idea of reducing CI testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis of the bootstrapping procedure and the subsequent generalization bounds under non-i.i.d conditions represent significant theoretical contributions that advance the understanding of model-powered statistical testing. The approach is conceptually simpler than existing kernel-based methods while providing comparable or better performance.

3. **Significance**: The paper addresses an important problem in statistics and machine learning with broad applications in causal inference, Bayesian network structure learning, and feature selection. By enabling the use of modern supervised learning tools for CI testing, the authors open up new possibilities for leveraging advances in classification to improve statistical testing. The demonstrated scalability to high-dimensional problems (up to dimension 70 in experiments) is particularly significant for real-world applications.

Weaknesses:
1. **Clarity**: The paper is technically dense and lacks sufficient interpretation of key results. For example, the explanation of how the classifier error rates translate to CI testing decisions in Section 1.1 (iii) is difficult to follow. The bound in Theorem 1 appears complex without clear intuition about the practical implications of the terms. The draft reviewer's question about how a small error probability can be achieved (with r0=0.1 and a limited classifier class) highlights this clarity issue. A more intuitive explanation of why the classifier can distinguish between H0 and H1 when r0=0.1 would greatly improve the paper's accessibility.

2. **Clarity**: The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2). While the assumptions are reasonable, the authors could better explain why these assumptions are necessary and what distributions satisfy them. A concrete example of a distribution that meets these conditions would help readers understand the scope of the theoretical results.

3. **Quality**: The experimental section, while comprehensive, could better address the practical implications of the theoretical bounds. The paper notes that "the performance with respect to dimension of Z scales much better than expected from our worst case theoretical analysis," but doesn't explore why this discrepancy occurs. A deeper analysis of this phenomenon would strengthen the empirical contribution.

Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:

PROS:
- Novel approach to CI testing that leverages modern machine learning tools
- Strong theoretical guarantees with meaningful bounds on the bootstrap procedure
- Clear empirical evidence of state-of-the-art performance, especially in high dimensions
- Well-motivated problem with significant applications in causal inference and statistical learning
- Theoretical contribution to generalization bounds under non-i.i.d conditions is valuable beyond CI testing

CONS:
- Paper is challenging to read due to insufficient interpretation of key results
- Smoothness assumptions require further explanation and justification
- Theoretical bounds are complex without clear intuition about practical implications
- The discrepancy between theoretical worst-case analysis and empirical performance needs more explanation

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that advances the state of the art in conditional independence testing. While the paper would benefit from improved clarity and more intuitive explanation of key results, the theoretical and empirical contributions are substantial and well-justified. The approach represents an important step toward model-powered statistical testing that leverages advances in machine learning. The paper makes a compelling case for the value of this paradigm shift, particularly for high-dimensional problems where existing methods struggle.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity, particularly regarding the interpretation of the theoretical results and the smoothness assumptions. The authors should provide more intuitive explanations of how the classifier error rates relate to the CI testing problem, and include a concrete example of a distribution satisfying their smoothness conditions. These improvements would make the paper more accessible to the NIPS community while preserving its strong technical contributions.
