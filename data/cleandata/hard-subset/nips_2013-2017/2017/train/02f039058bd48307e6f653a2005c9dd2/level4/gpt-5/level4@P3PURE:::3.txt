Summary
The paper proposes a general test for conditional independence among three continuous variables (X, Y, Z) without strong parametric assumptions. The method constructs samples from the joint distribution and from an approximation to the conditional product distribution p(x|z)p(y|z)p(z), with the approximation controlled in total variation distance via matching on Z. It then reduces conditional independence testing to a classifier-based two-sample test: a binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network) is trained to distinguish samples from the joint and approximate product distributions, and its predictive error is used to accept or reject the null of conditional independence. The authors compare their approach to existing methods, both conceptually and empirically, with experiments on synthetic and real data.

Strengths
- Conditional independence testing is central to modern machine learning and causal inference (e.g., assessing unconfoundedness), and the proposed method appears straightforward and powerful enough for many causal applications.
- The paper is generally well written. While parts of the theory and proofs are dense, the authors summarize the results effectively and make the methodology accessible to practitioners.
- The theoretical guarantees are the most impressive aspect. Although the approach is a natural extension of classifier two-sample tests (Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests, https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf)—here adapted to a third variable Z with neighbor matching—the strength of the guarantees is notable.

Questions and suggestions
- Discrete analog: Is there a version specifically for discrete Z (or more broadly for discrete variables)? If Z is discrete, resampling/bootstrapping may be particularly effective; clarifying this case and its potential advantages would be helpful.
- Classifier sensitivity and hyperparameters: How sensitive are the test results to the choice of classifier and tuning parameters? Could modest changes in architecture, regularization, or training lead to different accept/reject decisions? A robustness analysis alongside the simulations would strengthen the empirical section.
- Redundancy: Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) repeats the summary of the approach multiple times; this could be streamlined without loss of clarity.
- Algorithm 2: The algorithmic description appears longer than necessary and duplicates content already explained in the text; consider condensing or moving details to an appendix.

Minor issues
- Typos: “classifier” in line 157, and “graphs” in line 280.

Overall assessment
A clear, practically appealing contribution to conditional independence testing with strong theoretical backing and solid empirical comparisons. With added discussion on the discrete case, robustness to classifier choices, and some editorial tightening, the paper would be even stronger.
