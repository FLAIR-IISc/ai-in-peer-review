The authors propose a general test to determine conditional independence between three continuous variables. The method uses samples from the joint distribution to create samples that are close to the conditional product distribution in total variation distance. These samples are then fed to a binary classifier, such as a deep neural net, and the predictive error of the net is used to either accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors compare their proposed method to existing methods in the literature, both through descriptions and experimental results on synthetic and real data.

Conditional independence testing is an important tool for modern machine learning, particularly in the domain of causal inference, where claims of unconfoundedness are frequently assessed. The proposed method appears straightforward and powerful enough to be used for many causal applications. The paper is well-written, and the authors do a good job of summarizing the results and making the methodology clear to practitioners.

The theoretical results presented in the paper are impressive. Although the proposed method is an extension of the method for testing independence in the paper "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests," the guarantees of the theoretical results are notable. The authors demonstrate that their method can be used to test conditional independence between continuous variables, and they provide theoretical results regarding the generalization bounds for classification.

The paper has several strengths, including its clear and concise writing style, its thorough comparison to existing methods, and its impressive theoretical results. However, there are a few areas that could be improved. For example, the authors could provide more information about the sensitivity of the test to the classification method used. It would be useful to see how different hyperparameters affect the acceptance and rejection results. Additionally, some sections of the paper, such as Section 1.1 of Main Contributions, appear redundant and could be condensed or removed.
