Summary:
This paper proposes a model-powered approach for conditional independence testing for i.i.d. data. The key idea is to use a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap to generate samples distributed close to f_CI (the distribution under conditional independence), and then train a classifier to distinguish the observed data from the NN-bootstrapped data. If the classifier’s performance is close to random guessing, one fails to reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence; otherwise, one rejects the null. The authors provide bounds on the total variation distance between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and f_CI, as well as bounds on empirical risks under idealized classification settings and for nearly independent samples. Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is presented clearly.

Major comments:
1) Decoupling and replaceability of components:
The method naturally decomposes into two components: (i) generating samples that mimic f_CI and (ii) using a classifier to decide. Can either step be replaced by existing approaches from the literature? For example:
- Use the permutation-based method in [7] to generate samples and then apply the classification-based decision rule; or
- Use the NN bootstrap to generate samples but replace the classifier with a kernel two-sample test.
It would be useful to discuss, both theoretically and empirically, how these hybrid variants compare in terms of Type I error control, power under various alternatives (including weak dependence), computational cost, and robustness to model misspecification.

2) Sensitivity to weak conditional dependence:
The NN-bootstrapped distribution is close to f_CI in finite samples. If, in truth, X and Y are only weakly dependent given Z, how will the proposed test detect this? In other words, if the approximation error between the NN bootstrap and f_CI is of the same order as the effect size under weak dependence, the classifier may have limited power. Please clarify the regimes (in sample size, dimensionality, and smoothness) where the test retains power against weak conditional dependence and how the approximation error and classifier complexity interact in these regimes.

3) Symmetry with respect to X and Y and causal considerations:
Is the method fully symmetric in X and Y? From a causal perspective, X and Y can be dependent given Z due to X → Y or Y → X. In these scenarios, does the procedure require choosing which variable to sample or condition on at any stage of the NN bootstrap? The manuscript does not address whether such choices affect the test’s validity or power, or whether any asymmetry is introduced by the construction. Please clarify the role of X ↔ Y symmetry in both the algorithm and the theory.

4) Choice of the threshold parameter tau in Algorithms 2 and 3:
I am seriously concerned about how to choose the threshold tau in Algorithms 2 and 3. Practical guidance is needed: e.g., data-driven calibration procedures, dependence on sample size and class balance, or theoretically justified defaults that achieve nominal Type I error. Without a clear prescription, the test’s operating characteristics may be difficult to reproduce.

Minor comment:
- In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size.
