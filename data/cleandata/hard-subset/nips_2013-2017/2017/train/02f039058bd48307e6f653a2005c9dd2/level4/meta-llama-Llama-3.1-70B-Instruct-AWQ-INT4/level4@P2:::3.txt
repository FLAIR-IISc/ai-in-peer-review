The authors propose a general test to test conditional independence between three continuous variables. The authors do not have strong parametric assumptions; instead, they propose a method that uses samples from the joint distribution to create samples close to the conditional product distribution in total variation distance. Then, they feed these samples to a binary classifier, such as a deep neural net, and after training it on samples from each of the two distributions, use the predictive error of the net to either accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. They compare their proposed method to those existing in literature, both with descriptions and with experimental results on synthetic and real data.

Conditional independence testing is an important tool for modern machine learning, especially in the domain of causal inference, where we are frequently curious about claims of unconfoundedness. The proposed method appears straightforward and powerful enough to be used for many causal applications.

The paper is well-written, and the authors do a good job of summarizing the results and making the methodology clear to practitioners. While the details of the theoretical results and proofs can be hard to follow, they provide a good overview of the approach.

The theoretical results are impressive, and the proposed method is a somewhat straightforward extension of the method for testing independence in the paper "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as the authors acknowledge. However, the guarantees of the theoretical results are notable.

A few more comments are in order:

* Is there a discrete analog? The bootstrap becomes even more powerful when Z is discrete, right?
* How sensitive is the test on the classification method used? Is it possible that a few changed hyperparameters can lead to different acceptance and rejection results? This would be useful to see alongside the simulations.
* Section 1.1 of Main Contributions is redundant — the authors summarize their approach multiple times, and the extent appears unnecessary.
* Writing out Algorithm 2 seems unnecessary — this process can be described more succinctly, and has been described already in the paper.
* Slight typos — should be "classifier" in line 157, "graphs" in 280.

Strengths:

* The proposed method is straightforward and powerful enough to be used for many causal applications.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors do a good job of summarizing the results and making the methodology clear to practitioners.
* The theoretical results are impressive.

Weaknesses:

* The details of the theoretical results and proofs can be hard to follow.
* The sensitivity of the test on the classification method used is not clear.
* Some sections, such as Section 1.1 of Main Contributions, are redundant.

Acceptance:

* Yes, the paper is a good fit for NIPS.
* The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing.
* The results are well-supported by theoretical guarantees and experimental results.

Rejection:

* The paper does not provide a clear discrete analog.
* The sensitivity of the test on the classification method used is not clear.
* Some sections are redundant.

However, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe it is a good fit for NIPS.
