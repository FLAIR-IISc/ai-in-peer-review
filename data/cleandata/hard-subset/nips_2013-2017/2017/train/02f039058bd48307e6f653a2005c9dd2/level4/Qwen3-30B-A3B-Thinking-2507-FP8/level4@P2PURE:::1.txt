

This paper proposes a model-based approach for conducting conditional independence tests on i.i.d. data. The core method uses nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating the distribution $f^{CI}$, followed by training and testing a classifier to distinguish between the observed data distribution and the bootstrap-generated distribution. If the classifier's performance is near random chance, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is rejected.  

The authors establish theoretical bounds on the total variation distance between the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution and $f^{CI}$, as well as bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for nearly independent samples.  

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is presented clearly.  

**Detailed comments:**  
**Major:**  
1. The method appears to decompose into two distinct components: (1) generating samples mimicking $f^{CI}$, and (2) using a classifier for hypothesis testing. It is unclear whether either component could be substituted with established approaches from the literature. For instance, could a permutation-based method (e.g., [7]) replace the bootstrap step, or could the nearest neighbor bootstrap be combined with a kernel two-sample test? The performance implications of such substitutions require clarification.  
2. The nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution converges to $f^{CI}$ in finite samples. How does the method perform when the true relationship between $X$ and $Y$ given $Z$ is weakly dependent? The paper does not address this scenario.  
3. The method is not symmetric with respect to $X$ and $Y$. In causal contexts, dependence between $X$ and $Y$ given $Z$ may arise from either $X \to Y$ or $Y \to X$. The approach does not specify which variable should be sampled in such cases, nor does it account for this causal asymmetry.  
4. The selection of parameter $\tau$ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not justified. The authors provide no guidance on its choice, which is critical for practical application.  

**Minor:**  
In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size (not merely "devided").
