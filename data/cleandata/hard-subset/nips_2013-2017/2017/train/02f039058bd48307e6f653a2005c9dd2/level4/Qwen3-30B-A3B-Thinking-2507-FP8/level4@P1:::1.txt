

This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by converting it into a binary classification problem. The key idea is to use a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from a distribution close to f_CI (the conditional independent distribution), then train and test a classifier to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution and the bootstrapped samples. If the classifier's performance is close to random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence (X ⊥ Y | Z) is not rejected; otherwise, it is rejected.

The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the closeness of the nearest-neighbor bootstrapped samples to f_CI in terms of total variation distance and derive generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. This work builds upon and extends previous CI testing methods, particularly kernel-based approaches like KCIT and RCIT, as well as the permutation-based method for independence testing in [19]. Unlike previous approaches that rely on permutations (which don't generalize well to conditional independence) or kernel methods for two-sample testing, this paper provides a theoretically grounded bootstrap method for generating samples from f_CI.

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and addresses an important problem in statistics and machine learning. The authors make a strong case for their approach through both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. Their method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches on both synthetic and real-world datasets, particularly when the dimension of Z is high.

Major concerns:

1. The paper doesn't adequately address whether the method is symmetric with respect to X and Y. In causal inference, X ⊥ Y | Z could arise from two different causal structures (X → Y or Y → X), and the method's performance might depend on which variable is used as the "Y" in the bootstrap procedure. The authors should clarify whether the method is symmetric and how to handle asymmetric causal relationships.

2. The choice of threshold τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not well-justified. The paper sets τ = 1/√n, but this appears to be an ad-hoc choice without theoretical justification. The authors should provide a principled method for selecting τ based on the data or theoretical considerations.

3. The paper doesn't explore whether the nearest-neighbor bootstrap could be replaced with other techniques for generating f_CI samples. For example, could the permutation-based method from [10] be combined with their classifier approach? The authors should discuss alternative approaches to generating f_CI samples and how their nearest-neighbor bootstrap compares.

4. The paper doesn't address how the method behaves when variables are weakly dependent given Z. If the true dependence between X and Y given Z is very weak, will the classifier still be able to distinguish between the two distributions? This is particularly important for practical applications where weak dependencies might be common.

Minor concerns:

- In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size (as the authors define it), but this is not explicitly stated.

- The paper states "f^{CI} = f_X|Z(x|z)f_Y|Z(y|z)f_Z(z)" but later refers to "f_CI(x,y,z)" without defining the notation consistently.

- The phrase "weakly dependent" is used in the review but could be more precisely defined as "weakly dependent with small conditional mutual information" for clarity.

Strengths:
- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure provides a theoretically grounded method for generating samples from f_CI, which is a significant contribution.
- The theoretical analysis of generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples is novel and valuable.
- The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
- The modular design allows practitioners to leverage advances in classification methods without modifying the core CI testing procedure.

Weaknesses:
- The lack of symmetry discussion with respect to X and Y is a notable omission, especially given the causal inference applications.
- The threshold selection τ lacks theoretical justification, which could impact practical implementation.
- The paper doesn't fully explore how the method handles weak dependencies, which is an important consideration for real-world applications.
- The connection to causal inference could be strengthened by discussing how the method could be used in causal discovery pipelines.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to conditional independence testing. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling, and the model-powered approach is well-motivated. The main concerns relate to the symmetry of the method with respect to X and Y, the choice of threshold τ, and how the method handles weak dependencies. These issues should be addressed in the revision, but the paper's core contributions are solid and significant.

Pro:
- Novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure with theoretical guarantees
- Clear theoretical analysis of generalization bounds under near-independent samples
- Strong empirical results showing advantages over state-of-the-art methods
- Well-structured and clearly written paper
- Modular design that allows leveraging advances in classification

Con:
- Lack of discussion about symmetry with respect to X and Y
- Threshold τ selection lacks theoretical justification
- Insufficient analysis of behavior with weak dependencies
- Limited exploration of alternative methods for generating f_CI samples
