The authors propose a general test for conditional independence between three continuous variables. The method avoids strong parametric assumptions; instead, it uses samples from the joint distribution to generate a second set of samples that approximates the conditional product distribution. These two sets of samples—one from the original joint distribution and one from the generated conditional product distribution—are then used to train a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network. The classifier's predictive accuracy serves as the test statistic to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors compare their proposed method to existing approaches, providing both qualitative descriptions and empirical results on synthetic and real-world datasets.

Conditional independence testing is a vital tool in modern machine learning, particularly for causal inference, where assessing claims of unconfoundedness is crucial. The proposed method appears both straightforward and powerful, making it a promising tool for many applications in this domain.

Additionally, the paper is well-written. While the theoretical derivations and proofs can be intricate, the authors effectively summarize their findings and present the methodology in a way that is accessible to practitioners.

The most impressive aspect of this work is its theoretical results. As the authors acknowledge, the proposed method can be seen as a natural extension of the independence test from "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (Lopez-Paz and Oquab, 2016). The core modification involves introducing a third variable, Z, and performing nearest-neighbor matching on it, after which the procedure closely resembles the original two-sample test. Despite this conceptual simplicity, the accompanying theoretical guarantees are impressive.

A few further comments and questions:

*   Could the authors comment on a discrete analog of this method? It seems the bootstrap procedure would be particularly powerful when Z is discrete.
*   How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters? It would be valuable to see an analysis of whether small changes in the classifier's configuration could lead to different conclusions (i.e., accepting vs. rejecting the null hypothesis). Adding such a sensitivity analysis to the simulations would strengthen the paper.
*   The "Main Contributions" subsection (1.1) feels somewhat redundant, as the approach is summarized multiple times throughout the introduction. This section could be condensed for brevity.
*   Presenting the bootstrap procedure as Algorithm 2 seems unnecessary. This process is already described clearly in the text and could be stated more succinctly without the formal algorithm block.
*   I noted a few minor typos: "classifier" should be used in line 157, and "graphs" in line 280.
