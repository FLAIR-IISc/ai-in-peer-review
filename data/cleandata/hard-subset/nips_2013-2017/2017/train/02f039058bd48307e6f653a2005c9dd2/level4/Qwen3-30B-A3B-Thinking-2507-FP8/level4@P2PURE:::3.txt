

The authors propose a general test for conditional independence among three continuous variables. The method avoids strong parametric assumptions by using samples from the joint distribution to generate samples approximating the conditional product distribution in total variation distance. These samples are then fed to a binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network). After training the classifier on samples from both the joint and conditional product distributions, the test uses the classifier's predictive error to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors compare their approach to existing methods through both theoretical descriptions and empirical results on synthetic and real data.  

Conditional independence testing is crucial for modern machine learning, particularly in causal inference where unconfoundedness assumptions are frequently evaluated. The proposed method appears straightforward and sufficiently powerful for broad causal applications.  

The paper is well-written. While the technical details of the theoretical proofs may be challenging to follow, the authors effectively summarize the key results and clarify the methodology for practitioners.  

The most compelling aspect of the paper is its theoretical contribution. The method extends the independence test from "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (arXiv:1610.06545) by incorporating a third variable Z; after matching neighbors of Z, the approaches become similar, as acknowledged by the authors. The theoretical guarantees, however, are impressive.  

Additional comments:  
- Is a discrete analog feasible? The bootstrap procedure may become more powerful when Z is discrete.  
- How sensitive is the test to the choice of classification method? Could minor hyperparameter changes yield different acceptance/rejection outcomes? This sensitivity analysis would strengthen the simulations.  
- Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) contains redundant summaries of the approach; this level of repetition is unnecessary.  
- Explicitly writing out Algorithm 2 is superfluous, as the process is already adequately described in the paper.  
- Typos: "classifier" should replace "classifer" in line 157; "graphs" should replace "graph" in line 280.
