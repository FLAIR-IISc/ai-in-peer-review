Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables that reduces CI testing to a binary classification problem. Given i.i.d. samples from f(x, y, z), the authors generate pseudo-samples that are close (in total variation) to the conditionally independent product distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z) f(y|z) f(z) by using a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that swaps Y between nearest neighbors in Z. CI testing then becomes a two-sample problem between the original data and the NN-bootstrapped data, which can be tackled with off-the-shelf classifiers (e.g., boosted trees, deep nets). The authors also propose a bias-corrected variant that subtracts the baseline separability achievable using only (Y, Z), and they provide finite-sample generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the resulting “near-i.i.d.” samples. Experiments on synthetic post-nonlinear models and on the flow cytometry protein network benchmark show strong performance compared to KCIT and RCIT.

This work builds on several lines of research: kernel-based CI tests such as KCIT and RCIT; the permutation-based kernel conditional independence test (KCIPT), which also constructs a proxy for fCI but requires solving a linear program; and classifier two-sample tests for (unconditional) independence (e.g., Lopez-Paz and Oquab), where permutation of Y yields exact samples from fX fY. The main novelty here is a simple, computationally efficient NN bootstrap to approximate fCI for high-dimensional Z together with theoretical guarantees: (i) total variation closeness to fCI under smoothness assumptions; and (ii) classification generalization guarantees in a non-i.i.d., near-independent sampling regime.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper provides a clear statement of assumptions and results. The bound dTV(·, fCI) = O(n^{-1/dz}) (up to constants and terms depending on the mass of low-density regions) is plausible under smoothness and is carefully qualified. The generalization analysis under near-independence extends classical VC/Rademacher arguments to a dependent sampling scheme and is of independent interest. While the assumptions (curvature-like condition on f(y|z), smooth f(z)) may be stringent in practice and the rate suffers from the curse of dimensionality in z, the results are technically sound as presented.
- Method: The reduction is straightforward to implement, flexible (any classifier), and computationally practical due to fast NN search. The bias-corrected variant improves calibration in finite samples.
- Experiments: Synthetic studies are appropriate and show strong scaling in dz; the flow cytometry evaluation is useful, though ground truth is imperfect. It would be valuable to report type I error control under H0 across sample sizes and to show sensitivity to the choice of classifier and hyperparameters.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, with a clear high-level narrative and usable pseudocode. Some repetition can be trimmed (see below). Assumption 1 would benefit from more intuition (e.g., relate to Fisher information or Lipschitzness in z of the log-conditional density). Minor typos noted below.

Originality:
The overall reduction follows the classifier two-sample testing paradigm, but the key contributions—NN bootstrap for approximating fCI with TV guarantees and generalization bounds under near-independence—are novel and substantive relative to KCIT/RCIT and prior permutation approaches.

Significance:
CI testing is central to causal discovery, feature selection, and graphical models. A method that scales with modern classifiers and performs well in higher-dimensional Z is practically important. The theoretical treatment also pushes forward learning guarantees under dependent sampling.

Pros:
- Simple, modular method that leverages powerful classifiers.
- Theoretical guarantees: TV closeness to fCI and non-i.i.d. generalization bounds.
- Strong empirical performance, especially for larger dz.
- Public code and practical bias correction.

Cons and questions:
- Dependence on dz: The TV rate O(n^{-1/dz}) highlights the curse of dimensionality; more discussion on when high-dimensional Z remains tractable in practice would help.
- Classifier sensitivity: How robust are results to the choice of classifier, architecture, and hyperparameters? An ablation comparing XGBoost, random forests, and neural nets, and a sensitivity analysis over key hyperparameters would strengthen the empirical section.
- Calibration and size control: Beyond the bias-corrected variant, can the method guarantee nominal type I error (e.g., via cross-fitting, repeated NN bootstraps to estimate a null distribution, or conformal calibration)?
- Discrete/mixed Z: A discrete or mixed-type extension seems natural (exact matching on Z or hybrid NN for mixed data). A brief discussion or simple experiment would broaden applicability.
- Implementation details: Clarify tie-breaking in NN selection, the distance metric for Z (e.g., whitening or Mahalanobis distance), and the impact of approximate NN search in high dimensions.
- Sample splitting: The 3-way split is clean but reduces power. Could cross-fitting or re-using data without leakage improve efficiency?
- Computational complexity: kd-tree/ANN methods degrade in very high dz. Empirical timing vs. dz and n would be useful.

Writing and presentation suggestions:
- Section 1.1 repeats the high-level approach; condense to emphasize novel contributions (NN bootstrap and the non-i.i.d. generalization analysis).
- Algorithm 2 can be described more succinctly; the full pseudocode could be moved to the appendix.
- Provide more intuition for Assumption 1 and concrete examples where it holds.
- Minor typos: “classifier” (not “clasifier”) and “graphs” in the flow cytometry section; also fix “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems” and ensure consistent notation (e.g., fX,Y,Z vs. fX,Y,Z).

Overall assessment:
This is a strong and timely paper. The method is simple and effective, the theory is careful and nontrivial, and the empirical results are convincing. Addressing classifier sensitivity, size calibration, and the discrete/mixed Z case would further improve the work, but these are extensions rather than fundamental issues.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, novel theoretical contributions with practical implications.
- Method scales by leveraging modern classifiers and ANN search.
- Strong empirical gains over established kernel CI tests.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Finite-sample calibration and sensitivity to classifier/hyperparameters are not fully explored.
- Theoretical rates degrade with dz; more guidance on practical regimes would help.
- Minor repetition and presentation issues.

I lean toward acceptance, conditional on minor revisions that address the experimental sensitivity analysis, calibration under H0, and editing for brevity and clarity.
