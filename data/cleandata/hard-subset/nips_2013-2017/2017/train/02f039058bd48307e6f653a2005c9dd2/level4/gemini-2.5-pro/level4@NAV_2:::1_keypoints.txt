- Proposes reducing nonparametric conditional independence testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate f_CI and labeling real vs bootstrapped samples.  
- Decision rule: reject H0 if a classifier (e.g., gradient-boosted trees) can distinguish real and bootstrapped samples better than chance.  
- Extends ML-for-testing literature to conditional independence, directly leveraging powerful supervised models rather than kernels or permutations.  
- Nearest-neighbor bootstrap is an intuitive, computationally practical method to approximate f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) from joint samples.  
- Theoretical contributions: proof that bootstrapped samples are close to f_CI in total variation (Theorem 1).  
- Theoretical contributions: generalization risk bounds for classifiers trained on the non-i.i.d. bootstrapped data (Theorem 2) and Rademacher complexity analysis for non-i.i.d. samples.  
- Empirical results show CCIT can substantially outperform state-of-the-art CI tests, particularly with high-dimensional Z.  
- Strength: modular, elegant reduction allows leveraging advances in supervised learning for CI testing.  
- Strength: practical bootstrap algorithm and rigorous theoretical backing increase method credibility.  
- Weakness/Question 1: Needs ablation to isolate contributions of the bootstrap versus the classifier (e.g., bootstrap + kernel test or classifier + alternative sampling).  
- Weakness/Question 2: Potential loss of power when true distribution is very close to f_CI—can the classifier detect weak conditional dependence beyond bootstrap approximation error?  
- Weakness/Question 3: Bootstrap is asymmetric (swaps Y based on Z); unclear whether test is invariant to swapping X and Y and how that affects performance.  
- Weakness/Question 4: Selection of threshold parameter τ is critical; paper needs better justification, guidance, and practical selection strategy.  
- Minor point: Pseudocode for Algorithms 2 and 3 omits averaging (division by |D_r|) in the empirical risk definition.
