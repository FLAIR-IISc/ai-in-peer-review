The authors introduce a general nonparametric test of conditional independence for three continuous random variables. Rather than imposing strong parametric assumptions, they construct surrogate samples that approximate the conditional product distribution f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) in total variation distance using only draws from the joint. They then pose CI testing as a binary classification problem: train a high-capacity classifier (e.g., gradient-boosted trees or deep neural networks) to distinguish true joint samples from the surrogate ones, and decide to accept or reject the null based on predictive error. The method is compared to prior approaches both conceptually and empirically, with experiments on synthetic data and a real flow cytometry dataset.

Conditional independence testing is central to modern machine learning, particularly for causal inference where unconfoundedness assumptions are frequently scrutinized. The proposed approach is simple and powerful enough to be practical for a variety of causal analysis tasks.

The paper is clearly written. While some theoretical details and proofs are dense, the authors summarize their results effectively and present a methodology that practitioners can follow.

The theoretical contributions are the most compelling aspect. Although the approach is a fairly direct extension of classifier-based two-sample testing for (unconditional) independence in “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (the authors acknowledge this, adapting the test to include a conditioning variable Z; after matching on Z, the procedures align), the resulting guarantees are notably strong.

Additional comments:
- Is there a discrete analogue? If Z is discrete, the bootstrap seems even more effective—can the authors comment or provide a variant tailored to the discrete case?
- How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters? Could modest tuning changes lead to different accept/reject decisions? A robustness analysis alongside the simulations would be valuable.
- Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) is repetitive; the approach is summarized multiple times, and some condensation would improve flow.
- Presenting Algorithm 2 in full may be unnecessary—the process could be described more concisely, given prior explanations.
- Minor typos: “classifier” in line 157, “graphs” in line 280.
