- Proposes a novel conditional independence (CI) test by reframing CI testing as a binary classification problem and approximating f_CI via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for the classifier, yielding error bounds for the CI test.  
- Leverages powerful classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, deep nets) to scale CI testing to high-dimensional settings.  
- Distinguishes itself from prior work by directly approximating f_CI (vs. permutation methods) and using a simpler nearest-neighbor bootstrap instead of solving a linear program.  
- Theoretical contributions include bounds (Theorems 1 and 2) and analysis of Rademacher complexity in a non-i.i.d. setting.  
- Connects to kernel-based CI tests and prior model-driven inference literature, notably improving on related method [10] with stronger TV-distance guarantees.  
- Strength: genuine novelty in converting CI testing to classification and linking smoothness assumptions to bootstrap quality.  
- Strength: substantial theoretical rigor with concrete error bounds and non-i.i.d. generalization analysis.  
- Strength: practical potential demonstrated by empirical results suggesting scalability to higher dimensions.  
- Strength: clear motivation and positioning within model-powered statistical inference.  
- Weakness: paper is dense with heavy notation and weak logical flow, reducing readability and accessibility.  
- Weakness: theoretical results lack intuitive explanations connecting assumptions to final error bounds.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited and needs broader comparisons across datasets, dimensionalities, and SNRs.  
- Weakness: Algorithm 3’s bias-correction is insufficiently motivated and its practical impact is unclear.  
- Question: clarify how a limited function class G can still achieve small error under the alternative (example with r0 = 0.1).  
- Question: explain why performance scales better than expected in high dimensions—role of classifier choice, data properties, or other factors.  
- Question: discuss sensitivity to the Lipschitz constant assumption on f(z) and consequences of violations.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically sound approach with rigorous analysis and promising empirical performance.  
- Against acceptance: readability issues, limited experiments, and insufficient intuition for theoretical claims hinder full assessment.  
- Overall: significant contribution worthy of acceptance if revised for clarity and expanded empirical validation; recommendation — accept with minor revisions.
