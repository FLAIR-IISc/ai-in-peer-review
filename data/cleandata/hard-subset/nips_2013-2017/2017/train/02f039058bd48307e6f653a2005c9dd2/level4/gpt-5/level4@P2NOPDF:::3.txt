The paper proposes a general test for conditional independence among three continuous variables (X, Y, Z). Rather than imposing strong parametric assumptions, the authors construct synthetic samples that approximate the conditional product distribution P(X|Z)P(Y|Z)P(Z) in total variation distance using samples from the joint distribution. They then train a binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network) to distinguish between joint and synthetic samples and use the classifier’s predictive error to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The method is compared against existing approaches, both conceptually and through experiments on synthetic and real datasets.

Conditional independence testing is a key tool in modern machine learning, especially for causal inference where claims of unconfoundedness are central. The proposed approach appears straightforward and potentially powerful for many causal applications.

The paper is clearly written overall. While some of the theoretical details and proofs are dense, the authors provide helpful summaries that make the methodology accessible to practitioners.

The most impressive aspect, in my view, is the theory. Methodologically, the test is a natural extension of classifier two-sample tests (e.g., “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests”), adapted to the conditional setting by introducing a third variable Z and using nearest-neighbor matching or similar techniques on Z. Despite this conceptual continuity, the theoretical guarantees presented are strong and compelling.

Comments and suggestions:
- Is there a discrete analogue? If Z is discrete, one might expect the bootstrap or resampling step to be simpler and possibly more powerful. A discussion or experiment in this setting would be useful.
- How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters? Could modest hyperparameter changes flip accept/reject outcomes? Reporting sensitivity analyses alongside the simulations would strengthen the empirical section.
- Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) feels redundant; the approach is summarized multiple times, and the extent of repetition seems unnecessary.
- Writing out Algorithm 2 appears unnecessary; the procedure could be described more succinctly or consolidated with earlier descriptions.
- Minor typos: “classifier” (line 157) and “graphs” (line 280).

Overall, this is a well-executed and timely contribution. The approach is practical, the empirical results are promising, and the theoretical guarantees substantially enhance the paper’s impact.
