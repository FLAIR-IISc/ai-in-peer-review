This paper proposes a general, non-parametric method for testing the conditional independence (CI) of continuous random variables, X ⊥ Y | Z. Instead of relying on strong parametric assumptions, the authors present a "model-powered" approach that reframes the CI test as a binary classification problem. The core idea involves two main steps. First, given i.i.d. samples from the joint distribution f(x, y, z), the authors use a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate a new set of samples that approximate the conditional product distribution f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). The authors provide theoretical guarantees that these generated samples are close to the target distribution in total variation distance. Second, the original samples (labeled '1') and the bootstrapped samples (labeled '0') are used to train a powerful binary classifier, such as a gradient-boosted tree or a deep neural network. The test accuracy of this classifier is then used as a test statistic: an accuracy close to random guessing (0.5) suggests that the two distributions are indistinguishable and the null hypothesis of conditional independence cannot be rejected, whereas a high accuracy indicates dependence. The proposed method is evaluated against existing kernel-based methods on both synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating superior performance, especially as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Applicability:** Conditional independence testing is a fundamental problem in machine learning and statistics, with significant applications in areas like causal inference (e.g., assessing claims of unconfoundedness) and graphical model structure learning. The proposed method is straightforward, powerful, and appears readily applicable to many of these problems. By leveraging the power of modern, off-the-shelf classifiers, this approach has the potential to scale well to complex, high-dimensional data where traditional methods may falter.

2.  **Theoretical Contributions:** A key strength of the paper lies in its theoretical contributions. While the general idea of using a classifier for a two-sample test builds on prior work (e.g., Lopez-Paz et al., 2016, for unconditional independence), the extension to the conditional setting is non-trivial. The authors provide impressive theoretical guarantees for their nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure, formally bounding the distance between the generated and the true conditional product distribution. Furthermore, they develop novel generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the resulting "near-i.i.d." samples, which is a valuable contribution of independent interest.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem, clearly outlining their methodology, and summarizing their theoretical results, making the core ideas accessible to practitioners.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Sensitivity to Classifier Choice and Hyperparameters:** The test's performance relies on a chosen classifier. It would be valuable to understand the sensitivity of the results to the choice of classifier (e.g., XGBoost vs. a deep neural network) and its hyperparameters. The paper reports results using XGBoost, but it is unclear how much tuning was required or how robust the conclusions are to these choices. Could different hyperparameter settings for the same classifier lead to different hypothesis testing outcomes (i.e., accepting vs. rejecting the null)? A brief discussion or simulation exploring this sensitivity would strengthen the paper's practical guidance.

2.  **Applicability to Discrete Variables:** The paper focuses on continuous variables. Could the authors comment on how the proposed method might be adapted for discrete or mixed-type variables? The nearest-neighbor bootstrap seems particularly well-suited for discrete Z (where neighbors would be exact matches), which could make the method even more powerful and broadly applicable.

3.  **Presentation and Redundancy:** While the paper is clear, some parts could be more concise. The overall approach is summarized multiple times in the introduction and Section 1.1, with some degree of overlap. Similarly, the detailed pseudocode for Algorithm 2 largely repeats the procedure already described textually in the introduction. These sections could be condensed to improve readability.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 157: "classier" should be "classifier".
*   Line 280: "graph" should be "graphs".

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a novel, powerful, and well-supported method for a fundamental statistical problem. The combination of an intuitive methodology, strong theoretical backing, and impressive empirical results makes a compelling case for its acceptance. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed through small additions and revisions.
