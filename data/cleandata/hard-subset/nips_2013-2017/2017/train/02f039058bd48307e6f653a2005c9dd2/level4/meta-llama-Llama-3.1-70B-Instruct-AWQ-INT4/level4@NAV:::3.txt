The authors introduce a novel approach to test conditional independence among three continuous variables. This method eschews strong parametric assumptions in favor of utilizing samples from the joint distribution to generate samples approximating the conditional product distribution in terms of total variation distance. These samples are then used to train a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network, with the predictive error serving as the basis for accepting or rejecting the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The proposed method is benchmarked against existing techniques in the literature, with both qualitative descriptions and quantitative experimental results on synthetic and real-world data.

Conditional independence testing is a crucial tool in modern machine learning, particularly in causal inference, where assessing claims of unconfoundedness is often a priority. The proposed method appears to be a straightforward yet powerful approach suitable for various causal applications.

The paper is well-written, effectively summarizing theoretical results and methodology for practitioners, despite the intricate details of the theoretical results and proofs. The theoretical results are particularly noteworthy, representing a natural extension of the method for testing independence presented in "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests." The authors' modification of this test to incorporate a third variable Z and the subsequent guarantees are impressive.

Some additional comments and suggestions:

* Is there a discrete analog of this method? The bootstrap may become even more powerful when Z is discrete.
* It would be beneficial to investigate the sensitivity of the test to the classification method used. Can changes in hyperparameters lead to different acceptance and rejection results? Including these simulations would provide valuable insights.
* Section 1.1 of Main Contributions appears redundant, as the authors summarize their approach multiple times, rendering the extent unnecessary.
* Writing out Algorithm 2 in its entirety seems unnecessary, as this process can be succinctly described and has already been explained in the paper.
* Minor typos were found: "classifier" should be used in line 157, and "graphs" in line 280.
