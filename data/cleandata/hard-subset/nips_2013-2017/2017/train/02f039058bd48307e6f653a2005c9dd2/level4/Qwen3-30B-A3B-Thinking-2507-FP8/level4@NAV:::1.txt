

This paper proposes a model-based approach for conducting conditional independence tests on iid data. The core methodology involves using nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating the distribution \(f^{CI}\), followed by training and testing a classifier to distinguish between the observed data distribution and the bootstrapped distribution. If classification performance is near random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is rejected.  

The authors establish bounds on the closeness between the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and \(f^{CI}\) in terms of total variation distance, alongside bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for nearly independent samples.  

Overall, the paper addresses a significant problem and is clearly presented.  

**Detailed comments:**  
**Major:**  
1. The method appears decoupled into two components: (i) generating samples mimicking \(f^{CI}\), and (ii) using a classifier for inference. Could either component be substituted with established methods? For instance, could a permutation-based test (e.g., [7]) replace the bootstrap step while retaining the classifier? Alternatively, could the nearest neighbor bootstrap be combined with a kernel two-sample test? Performance comparisons with such alternatives would be valuable.  
2. The nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution approximates \(f^{CI}\) in finite samples. How does the method behave when the true relationship is weak conditional dependence (e.g., \(X \perp Y \mid Z\) only approximately holds)?  
3. Is the method symmetric in \(X\) and \(Y\)? In causal contexts, \(X \perp Y \mid Z\) may arise from \(X \rightarrow Y\) or \(Y \rightarrow X\), requiring different sampling strategies. The current approach does not address this asymmetry.  
4. The choice of parameter \(\tau\) in Algorithms 2 and 3 is critical but unexplained. How should \(\tau\) be selected in practice?  

**Minor:**  
In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size.
