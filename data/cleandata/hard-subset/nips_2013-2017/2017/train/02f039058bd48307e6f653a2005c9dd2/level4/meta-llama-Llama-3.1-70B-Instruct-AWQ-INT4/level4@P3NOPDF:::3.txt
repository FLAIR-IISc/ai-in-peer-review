The authors propose a general test to assess conditional independence between three continuous variables. Notably, their method does not rely on strong parametric assumptions, instead utilizing samples from the joint distribution to create samples that closely approximate the conditional product distribution in terms of total variation distance. These samples are then fed into a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network, which is trained on samples from both distributions. The predictive error of the classifier is subsequently used to either accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of their proposed method to existing approaches in the literature, both through descriptive analyses and experimental results on synthetic and real-world data.

Conditional independence testing is a crucial tool in modern machine learning, particularly in the domain of causal inference, where assessing claims of unconfoundedness is a common endeavor. The proposed method appears to be both straightforward and powerful, making it a viable option for numerous causal applications.

The paper is well-written, and the authors have done an excellent job of summarizing the theoretical results and making the methodology accessible to practitioners. While the details of the theoretical results and proofs can be challenging to follow, the authors provide a clear and concise overview of the key findings.

The theoretical results presented in the paper are particularly impressive. Although the proposed method can be seen as a natural extension of the approach for testing independence presented in "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), the authors have successfully adapted this method to incorporate a third variable Z. By matching neighbors of Z, the authors have developed a novel approach that offers robust guarantees.

A few additional comments and suggestions for improvement are offered below:

* It would be interesting to explore whether a discrete analog of the proposed method exists. As the authors note, the bootstrap method becomes even more powerful when Z is discrete. Investigating this further could provide valuable insights into the applicability of the method.
* The sensitivity of the test to the classification method used is an important consideration. It would be useful to examine whether small changes to hyperparameters could lead to different acceptance and rejection results. Including such an analysis alongside the simulations would strengthen the paper.
* Section 1.1 of the Main Contributions appears redundant, as the authors summarize their approach multiple times. Removing this section could help streamline the paper.
* Writing out Algorithm 2 in its entirety is unnecessary, as the process can be described more succinctly and has already been outlined in the paper.
* There are a few minor typos that should be corrected: "classifier" should be used in line 157, and "graphs" should be used in line 280.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing, offering a powerful and straightforward method that can be applied to a wide range of causal applications. With some minor revisions to address the comments above, the paper has the potential to make a substantial impact in the machine learning community.
