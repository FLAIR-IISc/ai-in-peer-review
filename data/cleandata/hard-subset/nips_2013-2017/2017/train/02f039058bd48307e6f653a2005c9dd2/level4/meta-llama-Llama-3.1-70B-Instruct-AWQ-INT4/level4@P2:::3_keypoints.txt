- Proposes a general test for conditional independence among three continuous variables using samples that approximate the conditional product distribution in total variation distance.  
- Creates synthetic samples near the conditional product distribution and uses a binary classifier (e.g., deep net) to distinguish joint vs. product distributions.  
- Uses classifier predictive error to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence.  
- Compares the proposed method to existing approaches with both descriptions and experiments on synthetic and real data.  
- Method is straightforward and appears powerful for causal applications, especially testing unconfoundedness.  
- Paper is well-written and clear for practitioners, with a good summary of results and methodology.  
- Theoretical results and guarantees are impressive, extending ideas from classifier two-sample tests.  
- Details of theoretical proofs can be hard to follow.  
- Acknowledges relation to prior work ("Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests") while claiming notable theoretical guarantees.  
- Raises question about a discrete analog and whether the bootstrap is more powerful when Z is discrete.  
- Questions sensitivity of the test to the choice of classifier and hyperparameters; suggests showing robustness analyses.  
- Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) is redundant and could be condensed.  
- Writing out Algorithm 2 is unnecessary and could be described more succinctly.  
- Minor typos noted ("classifier" at line 157, "graphs" at line 280).  
- Strengths: straightforward, broadly applicable to causal inference; well-written and practitioner-friendly; strong theoretical results.  
- Weaknesses: theoretical details are dense/hard to follow; unclear sensitivity to classifier/hyperparameters; some redundant sections.  
- Recommendation: accept for NIPS — significant contribution with good theoretical and experimental support; strengths outweigh weaknesses.
