

The paper introduces a novel conditional independence test for continuous variables, offering a nonparametric approach that avoids strong distributional assumptions. The method generates samples approximating the conditional product distribution (via neighbor matching on a third variable Z) and leverages a classifier (e.g., a deep neural network) trained on joint and conditional samples. The classifier's error rate under the null hypothesis of conditional independence provides a test statistic, enabling hypothesis testing without parametric constraints. The authors rigorously compare their method against state-of-the-art alternatives using both theoretical analysis and comprehensive experiments on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating strong performance. Given the centrality of conditional independence testing to causal inference (e.g., validating unconfoundedness), this work offers a practical, scalable tool for practitioners.

The theoretical contribution is particularly compelling. While the approach extends the classifier-based two-sample test framework from *Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests* (2016), the authors establish significant theoretical guarantees for the conditional case. The key innovation lies in proving finite-sample error bounds for the proposed test under minimal assumptions—this elevates the method beyond a simple extension to a robust, theoretically grounded solution. The paper’s clarity is commendable: despite the technical depth of the proofs, the authors provide an accessible summary of results and methodology, making the approach immediately usable for applied researchers. The writing is precise and well-structured, effectively bridging theory and practice.

A few minor revisions would further strengthen the paper. First, Section 1.1’s description of contributions contains redundant summaries; streamlining this would improve conciseness. Second, Algorithm 2’s explicit pseudocode is unnecessary given the clear verbal description in Section 3.1—condensing it would reduce redundancy. Third, two minor typos require correction: "classifer" (line 157) should be "classifier," and "graph" (line 280) should be "graphs."

Two technical points warrant discussion. The authors note the method’s extension to discrete Z is straightforward but do not elaborate. For discrete Z, neighbor matching could become more efficient (e.g., via exact matching), potentially enhancing power—addressing this in a brief discussion would strengthen the work. Second, the sensitivity of results to classifier choice and hyperparameters is not explored. Given that classifier performance can vary with architecture or tuning, a brief analysis (e.g., "How robust are p-values to classifier hyperparameters?") would bolster confidence in the method’s reliability. The current simulations focus on specific classifiers; including a sensitivity check would be valuable.

Overall, this is a strong contribution. The method is theoretically sound, practically motivated, and well-executed. The extensions to conditional independence are significant, and the empirical validation is thorough. The paper’s accessibility and clear presentation make it a valuable resource for causal inference and machine learning communities. With minor refinements to address the above points, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

**Summary**:  
*Strengths*: Novel nonparametric approach; strong theoretical guarantees; clear exposition; robust empirical validation.  
*Weaknesses*: Redundant section (1.1), unnecessary algorithm pseudocode, minor typos.  
*Recommendation*: Accept. The paper makes a meaningful advance in conditional independence testing with clear practical utility. Addressing the minor points would enhance its impact.
