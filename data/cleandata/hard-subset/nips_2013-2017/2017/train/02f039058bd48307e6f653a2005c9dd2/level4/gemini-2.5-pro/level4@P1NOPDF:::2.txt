This paper proposes a new test for conditional independence.

### Strengths

1.  A key contribution of this paper is the identification that for a conditional independence test to be effective, an assumption regarding the smoothness of the conditional density is necessary. The authors propose such an assumption and, based on it, derive a novel bound on the total variation distance between the density under the null hypothesis and the density obtained via the bootstrap.

2.  The authors then use this smoothness assumption to derive a bound on the error of the optimal classifier (as described in Section 1.1 (iii)).

3.  Theorem 1 is a significant contribution. It elegantly combines the smoothness assumption with further assumptions on the achievable error of the trained classifier (hat R) and the gap between the optimal and trained classifier errors. The resulting theorem is novel, insightful, and non-trivial.

### Weaknesses

The primary weakness of the paper is its presentation. The writing is dense and difficult to follow, and it provides limited intuition or interpretation of the main results. This makes it challenging for the reader to be confident in their understanding of the core claims.

For example, I would ask the authors to clarify the implications of the inequality in Section 1.1 (iii). Consider a scenario where the alternative hypothesis holds, and the optimal error is r0 = 0.1. Now, suppose the function class G used for the classifier is not expressive enough to distinguish between the true density f and the conditional independence density f^CI (e.g., if G consists only of linear functions).

In this case, can the authors provide an intuitive explanation for how the trained classifier can still achieve a small classification error (i.e., hat R < 0.1 + o1 < 0.2 for large n), as the theory suggests? A clear explanation for this scenario would significantly strengthen the paper and improve the reader's understanding of the result.
