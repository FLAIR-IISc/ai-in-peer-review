This paper proposes a model-powered approach for conditional independence testing for i.i.d. data. The main idea is to convert the testing problem into a binary classification task, utilizing powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The authors introduce a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples that mimic the conditionally independent distribution, and provide theoretical guarantees on the closeness of these samples to the true distribution in terms of total variational distance.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The introduction provides a comprehensive overview of the problem, and the related work section effectively places the paper's contributions within the broader context of conditional independence testing.

The authors' main contributions are threefold: (1) they propose a classification-based approach for conditional independence testing, (2) they develop a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples that mimic the conditionally independent distribution, and (3) they provide theoretical guarantees on the performance of their approach.

The paper's strengths include its ability to leverage powerful classifiers for conditional independence testing, its efficient nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure, and its theoretical guarantees on the performance of the approach. The authors also provide an extensive empirical evaluation of their method, demonstrating its effectiveness in various settings.

However, there are some concerns and questions that need to be addressed. Firstly, it is unclear whether the proposed method can be decoupled into two separate components, namely, generating samples that mimic the conditionally independent distribution and using a classifier for determination. Can existing solutions from the literature be used in place of either of these components? For instance, can the permutation-based method proposed in [7] be used in conjunction with the classification-based approach? Similarly, can the nearest-neighbor bootstrap be used with the kernel two-sample test approach?

Secondly, the nearest-neighbor bootstrap distribution is close to the conditionally independent distribution in the finite sample size setting. However, if the ground truth is that x and y are weakly dependent given z, how will the proposed method perform? Will it be able to detect the weak dependence, or will it incorrectly conclude that x and y are conditionally independent?

Thirdly, the method appears to be symmetric with respect to x and y. However, in a causal perspective, x and y can be dependent given z due to different causal models (e.g., x causes y or y causes x). Should the method be modified to account for these different scenarios, and if so, how?

Lastly, the choice of the parameter τ in algorithms 2 and 3 is unclear. How should this parameter be selected, and what are the implications of different choices of τ on the performance of the method?

In terms of minor comments, it appears that the empirical risk in algorithms 2 and 3 should be divided by the sample size.

Overall, the paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence testing, and its contributions are significant. However, addressing the above concerns and questions will strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method.
