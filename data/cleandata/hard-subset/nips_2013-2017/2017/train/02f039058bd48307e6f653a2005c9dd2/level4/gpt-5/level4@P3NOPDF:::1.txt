Summary
The paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test for i.i.d. data. The core idea is to construct a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that generates samples approximating the null distribution fCI (i.e., the distribution under X ⟂ Y | Z). A binary classifier is then trained to distinguish real observations from NN-bootstrapped pseudo-samples. If the classifier performs no better than chance, the test fails to reject CI; if it performs significantly better, the test rejects CI. The paper provides theoretical bounds on (i) the total variation distance between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and fCI, and (ii) the empirical risk gap under idealized classification settings and near-independent samples. The problem is important, the approach is appealingly modular, and the presentation is generally clear.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple reduction of CI testing to a two-sample classification problem, enabling the use of powerful off-the-shelf classifiers.
- The NN bootstrap is intuitive and avoids explicit density estimation.
- Theoretical results that quantify the approximation error of the NN bootstrap to fCI and relate it to classification error are valuable and help justify the method.
- The framework is flexible and can be adapted to different loss functions and classifiers.

Major comments
1) Modularity and alternatives for each component
- The method decomposes into: (a) constructing samples that mimic fCI; and (b) a decision rule via classification. It would strengthen the paper to discuss interchangeability:
  - Generation: Could the NN bootstrap be replaced by other conditional-null generators (e.g., permutation/stratified permutations within Z-bins, model-based residualization, or conditional randomization-like procedures if a conditional model is available)?
  - Testing: Could one replace the classifier with a kernel two-sample test (e.g., MMD/HSIC variants) without losing power? Conversely, how does the proposed classifier-based test compare to a permutation-based two-sample test on the same constructed datasets?
- Please include empirical comparisons that swap components (NN bootstrap + MMD; permutation-style null + classifier), and discuss trade-offs in power, runtime, and robustness across Z dimension, sample size, and classifier capacity.

2) Behavior under weak dependence
- When X and Y are only weakly dependent given Z, power hinges on both (i) how closely the NN bootstrap approximates fCI and (ii) the sensitivity of the classifier. The current exposition would benefit from:
  - A formal local alternatives analysis (e.g., rates at which the method detects vanishing dependence as n grows).
  - Sensitivity experiments showing power as a function of dependence strength, NN parameters (k or radius), and classifier capacity/regularization.
  - Discussion of finite-sample biases introduced by approximate null sampling and how they affect type I/II error trade-offs.

3) Symmetry in X and Y, and causal asymmetries
- Is the method symmetric in X and Y? In finite samples, resampling Y|Z vs. X|Z may induce different approximation errors and thus different decisions.
- Please clarify which variable is resampled in the NN bootstrap and why. Consider:
  - A symmetric variant that averages test statistics across both resampling directions or uses a max-combination with proper multiplicity correction.
  - A practical guideline: if one variable is low-dimensional or easier to model given Z, resample that variable to reduce approximation error.
- While causal direction is outside the CI testing goal, a brief discussion of how the choice of which variable to resample interacts with potential causal structures (X→Y vs. Y→X) would improve clarity.

4) Choice and calibration of the threshold τ (Algorithms 2 and 3)
- The paper should specify a principled way to set τ (the decision threshold on empirical risk/accuracy). Options include:
  - Label-permutation calibration within the constructed two-sample problem to obtain a null distribution for the statistic and corresponding p-values.
  - Sample-splitting or cross-fitting to prevent overfitting and to yield valid level-α tests by evaluating the classifier on an untouched test split; τ can then be set via the binomial tail under the null (chance-level accuracy).
  - If τ pertains to NN radius or acceptance criteria, provide data-driven selection (e.g., choose k or radius via cross-validation on the training split to maximize discrimination while maintaining type I error via an outer calibration loop).
- Please add a clear algorithmic description of calibration for τ and the resulting type I error guarantees.

5) Data reuse and dependence between samples
- If the same observations are used both to build NN bootstrapped samples and to train/test the classifier, dependencies can inflate type I error. To mitigate:
  - Use disjoint splits: one subset to build the NN bootstrap, another to train the classifier, and a third to evaluate the test statistic.
  - Alternatively, use cross-fitting with multiple splits and aggregate p-values (e.g., median or harmonic mean) to stabilize decisions.

6) High-dimensional Z and metric choice
- NN methods deteriorate with high-dimensional or heterogeneous Z. The paper should discuss:
  - Practical metric learning or dimension reduction for Z prior to NN search.
  - Sensitivity to Z’s dimension in theory (rates) and practice (experiments).
  - Computational complexity and approximate NN search choices.

Minor comments
- Algorithms 2 and 3: The empirical risk should be the average loss, i.e., divided by the sample size. Please correct the notation/implementation detail.
- Clarify statistical decision language: the test “rejects” or “fails to reject” H0; it does not “accept” H0.
- State assumptions explicitly: i.i.d. sampling, support conditions for Z to ensure meaningful NN matches, and any smoothness/Lipschitz conditions used in the TV bounds.
- Report variance across random splits/bootstraps in experiments; provide confidence intervals for type I error and power.

Suggested experiments
- Type I error control under known CI across varying Z dimension and sample sizes.
- Power under controlled alternatives with varying dependence strengths and forms (linear/nonlinear, heteroskedastic).
- Component swaps: NN bootstrap + classifier vs. NN bootstrap + MMD; permutation/stratification within Z-bins + classifier.
- Symmetry study: resample X|Z vs. Y|Z, and a symmetrized combination.
- Sensitivity to k/radius in NN and to classifier class/regularization.

Overall assessment
This is a timely and promising contribution that leverages modern classification tools for CI testing and provides useful theoretical guarantees for the NN bootstrap approximation. The paper would be significantly strengthened by clearer calibration procedures (especially for τ), careful handling of data reuse, discussion of symmetry and high-dimensional Z, and empirical comparisons that validate the modularity claims. With these additions, the method could be a compelling, practical alternative to existing CI tests.
