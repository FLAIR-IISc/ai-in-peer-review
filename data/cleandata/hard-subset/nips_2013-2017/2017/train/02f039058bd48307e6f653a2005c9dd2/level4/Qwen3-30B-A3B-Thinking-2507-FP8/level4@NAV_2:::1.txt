

This paper presents a novel model-powered approach for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing of continuous random variables. The authors convert the CI testing problem into a binary classification task by generating samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. The method then trains classifiers on the original samples (labeled 1) and the bootstrapped samples (labeled 0), with the classification accuracy serving as the test statistic for H0: X ⊥ Y |Z. The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing that the bootstrapped samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance (Theorem 1) and derive generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). Empirical results demonstrate superior performance over existing kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach for generating f_CI samples without requiring expensive linear programming as in KCIPT [10], (2) theoretical guarantees on the closeness of bootstrapped samples to f_CI, (3) generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d samples, and (4) empirical validation showing better scalability with dimensionality compared to state-of-the-art methods. This work relates to previous NIPS contributions on conditional independence testing, particularly the kernel-based methods KCIT [32] and RCIT [28], as well as the permutation-based approach for independence testing [19]. Unlike these methods, the proposed approach leverages powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks, enabling better performance in high-dimensional settings.

Strengths:
- The model-powered approach is well-motivated and addresses an important problem with significant applications in Bayesian networks, causal inference, and feature selection.
- The theoretical contributions are substantial, with novel analysis of Rademacher complexity for near-independent samples that extends beyond the standard i.i.d setting.
- The paper is clearly written with well-organized presentation of methods, theory, and experiments.
- Empirical results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method over state-of-the-art approaches, particularly for high-dimensional Z.

Weaknesses:
- The paper does not sufficiently address whether the method is symmetric with respect to X and Y. In causal contexts, X ⊥ Y |Z may hold for different causal mechanisms (X→Y or Y→X), and the method's performance might depend on which variable is used for the nearest-neighbor bootstrap. This is particularly concerning as the paper's flow-cytometry application involves causal relationships where directionality matters.
- The choice of threshold τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not well-justified. The paper sets τ = 1/√n, but provides no analysis of how this choice affects Type I and Type II error rates in practice, especially for weakly dependent variables.
- The paper does not thoroughly investigate how the method performs when X and Y are weakly dependent given Z. In such cases, the classifier's accuracy might be close to random guessing, making it difficult to distinguish between H0 and H1.
- The method's dependence on the dimension of Z is not fully explained. While the paper shows better empirical performance than expected theoretically, the analysis does not fully account for this gap, which is important for practical applications.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The theoretical results are appropriately developed and the empirical validation is comprehensive. However, the paper could benefit from more thorough analysis of parameter choices and edge cases.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The methodology is explained with sufficient detail, including pseudocode for the algorithms. The connection between the bootstrap procedure and the classification approach is particularly well-explained.

Originality: The paper's core idea of using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples from f_CI and leveraging powerful classifiers for CI testing is novel. The theoretical analysis of Rademacher complexity for non-i.i.d samples represents a significant contribution to the field. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly the permutation-based approach in [10] and kernel-based methods [32, 28].

Significance: Conditional independence testing is a fundamental problem with broad applications in machine learning, statistics, and causal inference. The proposed method addresses key limitations of existing approaches, particularly in high-dimensional settings, and demonstrates practical advantages on both synthetic and real-world data. The modular nature of the approach allows for easy incorporation of future advances in classification, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

Pro: Novel model-powered approach for CI testing that leverages powerful classifiers, strong theoretical guarantees for bootstrap procedure and classification generalization, superior empirical performance over state-of-the-art methods, clear presentation.

Con: Lack of symmetry analysis with respect to X and Y, insufficient justification for threshold parameter τ, limited analysis of weak dependence cases, theoretical analysis does not fully explain better-than-expected empirical performance.
