- Proposal: Use nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating f^{CI} and train a classifier to distinguish observed vs bootstrapped data; accept/reject CI based on classifier performance.  
- Theory: Provide bounds on total variation distance between NN-bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI}, and empirical risk bounds for ideal and near-independent cases.  
- Major concern 1: Unclear whether existing methods (e.g., permutation-based tests or kernel two-sample tests combined with NN bootstrap) could replace either component and how alternatives perform.  
- Major concern 2: Unclear performance in finite samples when x and y are weakly dependent given z.  
- Major concern 3: Potential asymmetry between x and y—unclear which variable to sample in different causal scenarios.  
- Major concern 4: The choice and tuning of parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not explained and may be problematic.  
- Minor comment: Empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by sample size.  
- Strengths: Addresses an important problem, offers a novel supervised-learning-based approach, and provides theoretical guarantees.  
- Weaknesses: Finite-sample behavior unclear for weak dependence, unclear τ selection, and possible asymmetry limits applicability.  
- Originality: Novel combination of nearest-neighbor bootstrap and classification with new theoretical results.  
- Significance: Relevant to many fields (statistics, ML, causal inference) and potentially more powerful in high-dimensional settings.  
- Quality and clarity: Well-written, well-organized, thorough related work, and supportive theoretical and empirical results.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—address τ choice, finite-sample performance, and limitations/future work discussion.
