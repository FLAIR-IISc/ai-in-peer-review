This paper proposes a general, non-parametric test for conditional independence between three continuous random variables. The core idea is to reframe the problem as a two-sample test. Instead of relying on strong parametric assumptions, the method uses samples from the joint distribution P(X, Y, Z) to generate a second set of samples from a distribution that is close in total variation distance to the conditional product distribution P(X|Z)P(Y|Z)P(Z). These two sets of samples are then used to train a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network. The classifier's accuracy in distinguishing between the two distributions serves as the test statistic for accepting or rejecting the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, comparing their method against existing approaches with both theoretical descriptions and experimental results on synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths:**
Conditional independence testing is a fundamental problem in modern machine learning, with significant applications in causal inference, particularly for verifying assumptions like unconfoundedness. The proposed method is straightforward to implement and appears powerful, making it a promising tool for a wide range of such applications.

The paper is well-written. Although the theoretical derivations and proofs are dense, the authors effectively summarize their key results and present the methodology in a manner that is accessible to practitioners.

The main strength of this paper lies in its theoretical contributions. While the proposed method can be seen as a natural extension of the classifier-based two-sample test from "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (Lopez-Paz and Oquab, 2016) to the conditional setting—a connection the authors rightly acknowledge—the theoretical guarantees provided for this extension are novel and impressive. The core adaptation involves a nearest-neighbor matching step on the conditioning variable Z, after which the procedure resembles the original two-sample test.

**Comments and Questions:**
*   Does a discrete analog of this method exist? When the conditioning variable Z is discrete, the nearest-neighbor matching step becomes exact. It seems the bootstrap procedure would be even more effective in this setting, and some discussion of this special case would be valuable.
*   How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters? It would be beneficial to include an analysis in the simulation section showing whether minor changes to the classifier's architecture or training parameters could lead to different conclusions (i.e., flipping from acceptance to rejection or vice-versa).
*   The "Main Contributions" subsection (1.1) feels redundant, as the approach is summarized multiple times throughout the introduction. This could be condensed for improved readability.
*   Algorithm 2, which details the training of the classifier, could likely be omitted. This procedure is standard and has already been described sufficiently in the text, so its formal inclusion seems unnecessary.
*   There are a few minor typos: "classifier" is misspelled in line 157, and "graphs" is misspelled in line 280.
