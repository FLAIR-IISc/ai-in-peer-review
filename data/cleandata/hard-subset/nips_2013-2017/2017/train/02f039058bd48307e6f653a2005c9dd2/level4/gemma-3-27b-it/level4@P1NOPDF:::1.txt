This paper proposes a model-powered approach to perform conditional independence tests for independent and identically distributed (iid) data. The core idea is to leverage nearest neighbor bootstrapping to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, f^{CI}. A classifier is then trained and evaluated to determine if it can distinguish between the observed data distribution and the distribution generated via nearest neighbor bootstrapping. If classification performance is close to random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is accepted.

The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution to f^{CI} in terms of total variational distance, and provide bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for near-independent samples. 

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is presented clearly.

Detailed Comments:

Major:

1. The proposed method appears to be decomposable into two main components: generating samples that approximate f_{CI}, and employing a classifier for decision-making. I question whether either of these steps could be replaced with existing solutions from the literature. For example, could one use the permutation-based method proposed in [7] in conjunction with a classification-based approach? Alternatively, could one combine the nearest neighbor bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test? I am curious about the performance implications of such substitutions.

2. The nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution is shown to approximate f_{CI} even in finite sample settings. However, if the ground truth is that x and y are *weakly* dependent given z, how would the proposed method perform? I’m concerned about its ability to discern such weak dependencies.

3. Is the method entirely symmetric with respect to x and y? From a causal perspective, dependence between x and y given z can arise from two distinct causal models: x causes y, or y causes x. In these two scenarios, does the method require determining which variable to sample from? The authors do not appear to address this issue.

4. I have a significant concern regarding the selection of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how to appropriately choose this value.

Minor:

In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculations appear to be missing a division by the sample size.
