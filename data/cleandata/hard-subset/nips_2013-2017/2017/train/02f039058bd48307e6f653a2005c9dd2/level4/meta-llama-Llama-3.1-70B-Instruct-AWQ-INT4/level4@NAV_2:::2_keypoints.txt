- Converts conditional independence testing into a binary classification problem using powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, deep nets) for high-dimensional settings.
- Proposes a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples that mimic the conditionally independent distribution.
- Provides theoretical guarantees bounding the total variation distance between generated samples and the true conditionally independent distribution.
- Develops generalization bounds for the classification problem that translate into error bounds for conditional independence testing.
- Reduces CI testing to supervised learning, enabling the use of modern classification tools.
- Introduces a novel smoothness assumption on the conditional density and uses it to bound classifier error.
- Theorem 1: a novel, non-trivial combination of smoothness, achievable optimal classifier error η, and gap between optimal and trained classifier errors.
- Empirical results claim the proposed algorithm outperforms state-of-the-art methods on synthetic and real datasets.
- Strength: identifies and formalizes a necessary smoothness assumption for CI testing.
- Strength: uses that assumption to derive training-set error bounds for the optimal classifier.
- Strength: provides an insightful and non-trivial theoretical result (Theorem 1).
- Weakness: paper is hard to read and lacks interpretation of theoretical results and their implications.
- Weakness: no intuitive explanation for how small error is achieved when the alternative holds but the function class is not expressive enough.
- Question: provide intuition on achieving small error when the classifier class cannot distinguish true vs conditionally independent distributions.
- Question: how do results scale when Z is high-dimensional and sample size is limited?
- Question: provide more empirical details — specific datasets used and hyperparameter tuning for each algorithm.
- Acceptance arguments: novel approach to CI testing, solid theoretical guarantees, and strong empirical performance.
- Rejection arguments: poor exposition, missing intuition in critical cases, and empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.
