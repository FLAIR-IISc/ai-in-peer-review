This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conducting conditional independence tests for iid data. The basic idea is to utilize nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that follow a distribution close to the conditional independence distribution, denoted as f^{CI}. A classifier is then trained and tested to determine if it can distinguish between the observation distribution and the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution. If the classification performance is close to a random guess, the null hypothesis that the data follows conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, the null hypothesis is accepted.

The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI} in terms of total variational distance. Additionally, they establish bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for near-independent samples. Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is presented in a clear manner.

Detailed comments:

Major:

1. The proposed method appears to be decoupled into two major components: generating samples that mimic f^{CI} and using a classifier for determination. A question arises as to whether either of these steps can be replaced with existing solutions from the literature. For instance, could the permutation-based method proposed in [7] be used in conjunction with the classification-based approach to solve the problem? Alternatively, could the nearest neighbor bootstrap be combined with the kernel two-sample test approach to achieve the same goal? The performance of these alternative methods is of interest.

2. In the finite sample size setting, the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution is close to f^{CI}. However, if the ground truth is that x and y are weakly dependent given z, it is unclear how the proposed method would distinguish between this scenario and one where x and y are conditionally independent.

3. The method appears to be symmetric with respect to x and y. However, from a causal perspective, x and y can be dependent given z due to different causal models (e.g., x causes y or y causes x). In such scenarios, it is unclear whether the method would need to determine which variable to sample. The authors do not consider this issue.

4. The choice of parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is concerning. It is unclear how this parameter should be selected, and its impact on the method's performance is not thoroughly discussed.

Minor:

In Algorithms 2 and 3, it appears that the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size to obtain a more accurate estimate of the risk.
