- Proposes reframing conditional independence testing X ⟂ Y | Z as a binary classification task using powerful supervised learners.  
- Generates approximate samples from the CI null f_CI via a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap that swaps Y from nearest Z neighbors.  
- Trains a binary classifier to distinguish original samples (label 1) from bootstrapped samples (label 0); near 50% accuracy indicates conditional independence.  
- Provides Theorem 1: a total variation bound showing the bootstrapped distribution is close to f_CI.  
- Provides Theorem 2: generalization bounds for classifier risk under the near-i.i.d. nature of bootstrapped samples.  
- Empirically, CCIT outperforms kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT), maintaining accuracy for conditioning dimension d_z up to 70 and on real flow cytometry data.  
- Strength — conceptual novelty: leverages advances in supervised learning to tackle a fundamental statistical problem.  
- Strength — strong empirical performance in high-dimensional settings compared to state-of-the-art.  
- Strength — solid theoretical foundation validating the bootstrap and classifier generalization.  
- Weakness — practical threshold selection: no principled, data-driven method for choosing τ (paper uses heuristic τ = 1/√n).  
- Weakness — potential loss of power when true dependency is weak and comparable to the bootstrap approximation error b(n).  
- Weakness — lacks ablation studies to disentangle contributions of the bootstrap vs. choice of classifier.  
- Weakness — algorithm is asymmetric in X and Y (swaps Y only); invariance or implications of swapping roles are not clarified.
