This paper presents a model-based framework for conducting conditional independence tests on i.i.d. data. The core principle involves using a nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate a synthetic dataset that approximates the distribution under conditional independence ($f^{CI}$). Subsequently, a classifier is trained to distinguish between the original observed data and this bootstrapped data. If the classifier's performance is indistinguishable from random chance, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is.

The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach, developing bounds on the total variation distance between the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and $f^{CI}$. They also establish bounds on the empirical risk, assuming ideal classification settings and nearly-independent samples.

Overall, the paper tackles a significant problem, and the manuscript is well-written and clearly presented.

**Detailed Comments:**

**Major:**

1.  The proposed methodology can be conceptualized as a two-stage process: first, generating samples to emulate the distribution under conditional independence, and second, using a classifier as a two-sample test. This modularity raises the question of whether each component could be substituted with alternative methods from the literature. For instance, what would the performance be if one were to use a permutation-based generation method (e.g., [7]) coupled with the proposed classification test? Conversely, how would a combination of the nearest neighbor bootstrap with a standard kernel two-sample test perform? A comparison with such alternative constructions would strengthen the paper.

2.  The theoretical guarantees suggest the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution approximates $f^{CI}$ in the finite sample regime. This raises a question about the method's statistical power in scenarios of weak conditional dependence. Specifically, if the true data-generating distribution is only weakly dependent, it will already be very close to the true conditional independence distribution. Given that the bootstrapped distribution is also an approximation, how would the proposed classifier reliably distinguish between these two distributions, which may be very similar?

3.  The paper does not explicitly discuss the symmetry of the method with respect to variables X and Y. From a causal discovery perspective, conditional dependence between X and Y given Z can arise from different underlying structures (e.g., X → Y or Y → X). Does the choice of which variable to resample in the nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure affect the outcome? It is unclear if the method is invariant to this choice or if one must pre-determine a "target" and "predictor" variable. This potential asymmetry is not addressed.

4.  A significant practical concern is the selection of the hyperparameter $\tau$ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The paper lacks guidance on how to set this parameter, which could critically influence the test's performance. A discussion on its sensitivity and a practical strategy for its selection would be necessary.

**Minor:**

*   In the presentation of Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculation appears to be a sum rather than an average. It should likely be normalized by the sample size.
