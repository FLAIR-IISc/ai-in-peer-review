

The authors introduce a general test for conditional independence among three continuous variables. The approach avoids strong parametric assumptions by generating samples approximating the conditional product distribution in total variation distance, using samples from the joint distribution. These samples are then fed into a binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network); after training on samples from both distributions, the classifier's predictive error determines acceptance or rejection of the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The method is rigorously compared to existing literature through both theoretical descriptions and empirical results on synthetic and real-world data.  

Conditional independence testing is vital for modern machine learning, particularly in causal inference where unconfoundedness assumptions are central. The proposed method is straightforward and sufficiently powerful for broad causal applications.  

The paper is well-written. While the theoretical proofs are technically dense, the authors effectively summarize key results and clarify the methodology for practitioners.  

The most compelling aspect is the theoretical contribution. The method extends the independence test from "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (arXiv:1610.06545) by incorporating a third variable Z (via neighbor matching), with the core procedure remaining similar. The theoretical guarantees, however, are notably robust.  

Minor concerns:  
- A discrete analog would be valuable; the bootstrap would be particularly effective for discrete Z.  
- Sensitivity to the choice of classifier and hyperparameters should be addressed—could minor hyperparameter changes alter test outcomes?  
- Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) contains redundant repetition of the approach; conciseness would improve clarity.  
- Algorithm 2 is unnecessary to write out explicitly, as the process is already fully described.  
- Typo corrections: "classifier" (line 157), "graphs" (line 280).
