

This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The authors convert CI testing into a classification problem where samples from the true joint distribution are labeled as 1 and samples from a conditionally independent distribution (f_CI) are labeled as 0. The key innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) that generates samples close to f_CI from i.i.d. samples of the true joint distribution. The authors theoretically demonstrate that these bootstrapped samples approximate f_CI in total variational distance, and provide generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d. samples. The method leverages powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees to achieve superior performance compared to prior kernel-based approaches (KCIT, RCIT) for high-dimensional CI testing.

The paper addresses an important problem in statistical learning with applications in causal inference, Bayesian network structure learning, and feature selection. It builds upon recent work using classifiers for independence testing (e.g., [19]) but extends it to the more challenging conditional independence setting. The authors' nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach differs significantly from [10] (which uses permutation-based sampling requiring expensive linear programming) and provides theoretical guarantees about the closeness of generated samples to f_CI.

**Strengths:**
1. **Significance:** The problem of high-dimensional CI testing is well-motivated, and the paper demonstrates clear empirical advantages over state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets (flow cytometry data). The method achieves near-perfect performance for dimensions up to 70, while alternatives degrade rapidly.
2. **Originality:** The paper's reduction of CI testing to binary classification with a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure represents a significant methodological contribution. The theoretical analysis of generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d. samples is particularly valuable.
3. **Quality:** The theoretical results are sound and well-justified, with appropriate smoothness assumptions. The empirical evaluation is thorough, including both controlled synthetic experiments and a real-world application to protein signaling networks.
4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise definitions and a helpful illustrative figure. The algorithmic details are presented in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Parameter Selection:** The choice of threshold parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not well-justified. While the authors set τ = 1/√n as an upper bound on variance under H0, this choice appears somewhat arbitrary, and the authors do not demonstrate how sensitivity to τ affects performance. A more principled approach to determining τ (e.g., via permutation testing) would strengthen the method.
2. **Weak Dependence Handling:** The paper does not address how the method performs when X and Y are weakly dependent given Z (i.e., d_TV(f, f_CI) is small but nonzero). In such cases, the classifier's accuracy may not differ substantially from random guessing, potentially leading to poor power for detecting weak conditional dependencies.
3. **Asymmetry in X/Y:** The method is not symmetric with respect to X and Y, which is problematic from a causal perspective. In causal discovery, X→Y and Y→X represent distinct causal structures, but the authors' approach does not distinguish between these cases. This could lead to misinterpretation of CI results in causal inference applications.
4. **Theoretical Assumptions:** The smoothness assumptions required for the theoretical guarantees (Assumptions 1-2) are quite strong. The authors should provide more intuition about whether these assumptions are reasonable for typical applications in causal inference.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:*
- The method provides a significant empirical improvement over state-of-the-art CI testing approaches
- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is novel and theoretically justified
- The reduction to binary classification leverages advances in machine learning
- The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to an important problem

*Con:*
- The parameter τ lacks principled justification
- The method's performance with weak conditional dependencies is not evaluated
- The lack of symmetry between X and Y could lead to misinterpretation in causal contexts
- Theoretical assumptions may be restrictive for real-world applications

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to conditional independence testing. The model-powered approach is innovative and well-executed, with compelling theoretical and empirical results. While the weaknesses noted above are important, they do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The method provides a valuable tool for practitioners working on high-dimensional CI testing problems, and the theoretical insights about classification under non-i.i.d. samples are of independent interest.

I recommend acceptance, as the paper advances the state of the art in CI testing and provides a useful new methodology for the NIPS community. The authors should address the parameter selection issue and consider the asymmetry in X/Y in future work.
