Summary
The paper proposes a general-purpose test for conditional independence among three continuous variables X, Y, and Z. The method constructs samples that approximate the conditional product distribution P(X|Z)P(Y|Z)P(Z) using resampling/matching over Z, and then trains a binary classifier to distinguish these from samples drawn from the joint P(X, Y, Z). The classifier’s error (or a related test statistic) is used to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The approach requires few parametric assumptions, is straightforward to implement, and is evaluated on synthetic and real datasets. The authors provide nontrivial theoretical guarantees that connect the resampling scheme and classifier performance to power and error control.

Strengths
- Relevance: Conditional independence testing is a core tool in causal inference and modern ML. The proposed approach is timely and practically valuable.
- Conceptual clarity: Casting the test as a classifier two-sample test in the conditional setting is intuitive and leverages strong recent advances in classification.
- Generality and practicality: The method accommodates flexible classifiers (including deep nets) and minimal modeling assumptions.
- Theoretical contributions: The guarantees are the most impressive aspect. Extending classifier-based tests to the conditional setting while quantifying approximation quality (e.g., via total variation bounds for the constructed samples) is nontrivial, and the provided results are compelling.
- Empirical evaluation: Comparisons to existing methods on synthetic and real data bolster the case for the approach.

Relation to prior work
The method is a natural extension of classifier two-sample tests (e.g., “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests”), adapted to conditional independence by constructing samples approximating P(X|Z)P(Y|Z)P(Z). While the high-level idea is related, the conditional setting introduces important complications (e.g., matching/weighting over Z and controlling the approximation error), and the paper’s theoretical analysis appears to go substantially beyond a straightforward reuse of prior results.

Main concerns and suggestions
- Discrete Z and bootstrap: Is there a discrete analog or specialized variant when Z is discrete or low-cardinality? In such cases, exact or nearly exact resampling by conditioning on strata of Z may strengthen finite-sample guarantees and power. Please clarify whether the proposed bootstrap or matching becomes simpler/more powerful in the discrete-Z regime, and whether separate guidance is provided for hybrid (mixed discrete/continuous) Z.
- Sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters: How robust is the test to the classifier architecture, training budget, and hyperparameters (regularization, learning rate, class weighting, early stopping)? Could two reasonable configurations yield different accept/reject outcomes? It would be helpful to:
  - Report variance across multiple classifiers and hyperparameter settings.
  - Include ablations (e.g., linear/logistic, boosted trees, shallow nets, deep nets) to illustrate stability of Type I error and power.
  - Clarify the hyperparameter selection protocol under the null to avoid test statistic inflation (e.g., nested cross-validation or a held-out validation set disjoint from the test statistic computation).
- Calibration and Type I error control: Please make explicit how the decision threshold is set. Is a permutation or sample-splitting scheme used to calibrate p-values and control Type I error in finite samples? If the theory is primarily asymptotic, some finite-sample calibration guidance would help practitioners.
- Construction of conditional product samples:
  - Detail how neighbors in Z are chosen (distance metric, kernel weighting, bandwidth/number of neighbors) and how these choices affect the TV approximation.
  - Discuss sensitivity to the dimensionality of Z and potential curse-of-dimensionality effects, both theoretically and empirically.
  - Provide computational complexity and scaling behavior for large n and high-dimensional Z.
- Data splitting and reuse: To avoid bias, clarify the data-splitting protocol for (i) constructing resampled data, (ii) training the classifier, and (iii) computing the final test statistic. Repeated splits or cross-fitting could stabilize results; consider reporting variability across splits.
- Comparisons and fairness: While comparisons are included, it would strengthen the case to:
  - Ensure strong baselines such as KCIT/RCIT and other kernel- or nearest-neighbor-based conditional independence tests are tuned fairly.
  - Report power at matched Type I error (e.g., 5%) across methods and include confidence intervals over multiple runs.
  - Provide guidance on sample sizes where the proposed method has clear advantages or disadvantages relative to baselines.
- Interpretability and reporting: Offer recommendations for reporting effect sizes alongside binary decisions (e.g., classifier AUC gap, calibrated p-values), to aid practitioners in causal workflows.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally well-written and accessible to practitioners. The methodological pipeline is clear and the high-level summaries are helpful.
- That said, Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) repeats material stated elsewhere. Consider tightening the exposition to reduce redundancy.
- Algorithm 2 can likely be described more succinctly, given that the procedure is already explained in text. A compact pseudo-code block or a reference back to Algorithm 1 with a short note on differences could suffice.

Minor issues and typos
- Line 157: “classifier” (spelling).
- Line 280: “graphs” (spelling).
- Consider a brief glossary for notation used in the theory sections to ease navigation for readers less familiar with measure-theoretic details.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and impactful paper. The idea of leveraging classifier-based two-sample testing in the conditional setting is natural yet technically challenging, and the authors provide meaningful theoretical guarantees and solid empirical evidence. With additional clarification on calibration, robustness to classifier choices, and streamlined presentation, the paper would be even stronger. I am leaning toward acceptance.
