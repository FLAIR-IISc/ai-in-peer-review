This paper introduces a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data. The core idea leverages nearest neighbor bootstrapping to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, f<sup>CI</sup>. A classifier is then trained to distinguish between the observed data distribution and this bootstrapped distribution. If the classifier performs no better than random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is accepted. 

The authors develop theoretical bounds quantifying the closeness between the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f<sup>CI</sup>, specifically in terms of total variational distance. They also provide bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification conditions and for nearly-independent samples. Overall, the paper tackles an important problem and is presented in a reasonably clear manner.

**Detailed Comments:**

**Major Concerns:**

1. The proposed method appears to be decomposable into two key components: sampling to approximate f<sup>CI</sup>, and classification to determine conditional independence. I question whether either of these components could be replaced with existing solutions from the literature, potentially with competitive performance. For instance, could one substitute the nearest neighbor bootstrap with a permutation-based approach (e.g., [7]) and then apply a classification-based test? Alternatively, could the nearest neighbor bootstrap be combined with a kernel two-sample test? A comparative analysis exploring such alternatives would be valuable.

2. The analysis demonstrates that the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution approximates f<sup>CI</sup> in finite sample settings. However, I am concerned about the method’s ability to discern conditional independence when the underlying relationship is weak – specifically, if x and y are only weakly dependent given z. How will the method perform in this scenario, and how will it avoid false positives?

3. The method’s symmetry (or lack thereof) with respect to x and y is unclear. From a causal inference perspective, dependence between x and y given z can arise from different causal structures (x causes y, or y causes x). These scenarios may require different sampling strategies. The paper does not address whether the order of variables influences the results or if a specific variable should be preferentially sampled, potentially based on domain knowledge or causal assumptions.

4. I have significant concerns regarding the selection of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The paper lacks a clear justification or guidance for choosing an appropriate value for τ, and its impact on performance is not sufficiently discussed. A sensitivity analysis or a more robust method for τ selection is needed.

**Minor Comments:**

In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculation appears to be missing a normalization factor; it should be divided by the sample size to provide a meaningful estimate of the risk.
