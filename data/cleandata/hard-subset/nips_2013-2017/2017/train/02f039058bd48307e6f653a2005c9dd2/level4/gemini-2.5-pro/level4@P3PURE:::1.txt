This paper proposes a classifier-based method for conducting conditional independence tests on i.i.d. data. The core idea is to frame the problem as a two-sample test. The first sample consists of the observed data. The second sample is generated via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure designed to approximate the null distribution where conditional independence holds (f^{CI}). A classifier is then trained to distinguish between these two samples. If the classifier's performance is close to random guessing, the two distributions are considered indistinguishable, and the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected. Conversely, if the classifier achieves high accuracy, the null hypothesis is rejected.

The authors provide theoretical support for this approach by developing bounds on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and the true null distribution f^{CI}. They also provide bounds on the empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for near-independent samples. In general, the paper addresses an important problem and is presented clearly.

**Major Comments:**

1.  The proposed method appears to consist of two distinct components: 1) a sample generation strategy (nearest-neighbor bootstrap) to approximate the null distribution, and 2) a testing mechanism (a classifier-based two-sample test). This modularity raises the question of whether these components can be interchanged with existing methods from the literature. For instance, what would the performance be if one were to use the permutation-based method from [7] for sample generation, followed by the authors' classification test? Alternatively, how would the nearest-neighbor bootstrap perform when paired with a different two-sample test, such as a kernel-based approach? A discussion of these alternatives would help to better contextualize the specific contributions of each component.

2.  The paper establishes that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap distribution is close to the true null distribution f_{CI} in the finite sample setting. A key question is how the method performs in the presence of weak conditional dependence. In this scenario, the true data-generating distribution is also very close to f_{CI}. It is unclear whether the proposed classifier-based test would have sufficient statistical power to distinguish the observed data from the bootstrapped samples. An analysis of the method's power against such weakly dependent alternatives would strengthen the paper.

3.  The property of conditional independence, X ⊥ Y | Z, is symmetric with respect to X and Y. However, it is not clear if the proposed algorithm is also symmetric in its treatment of X and Y. From a causal perspective, dependence between X and Y given Z can arise from different underlying causal models (e.g., X causes Y or Y causes X). It is not obvious whether the sampling procedure should differ depending on this structure. The authors do not appear to consider this potential asymmetry.

4.  A significant practical concern is the selection of the hyperparameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The paper provides little guidance on how this parameter should be chosen, which is critical for the method's performance and reproducibility.

**Minor Comment:**

*   In Algorithms 2 and 3, the definition of the empirical risk appears to be missing a normalization factor; it should likely be divided by the sample size.
