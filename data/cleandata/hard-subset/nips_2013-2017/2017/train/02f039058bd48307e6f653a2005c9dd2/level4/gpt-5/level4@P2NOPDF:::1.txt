This paper proposes a model-based approach for conditional independence testing on i.i.d. data. The core idea is to use a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples whose distribution approximates f^{CI}, then train a classifier to distinguish between observed samples and NN-bootstrapped samples. If the classifier performs at chance, one fails to reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence; if it significantly outperforms chance, one rejects the null.

The authors provide theoretical guarantees, including bounds on the total variation distance between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI}, as well as bounds on empirical risk under idealized classification settings and for nearly independent samples. Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is clearly written.

Detailed comments:

Major:
1. The procedure naturally decomposes into two components: (i) generating samples that mimic f^{CI} (via NN bootstrap), and (ii) deciding via a classifier. Could either component be swapped with existing methods? For example, could one pair the permutation-based approach of [7] with the proposed classification step, or combine the NN bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test (e.g., MMD)? A discussion and, if feasible, empirical comparisons would clarify the modularity and robustness of the approach.

2. The NN-bootstrapped distribution only approximates f^{CI} in finite samples. When the ground truth is that X and Y are weakly dependent given Z, how does the method behave? Specifically, what is the test’s power under weak conditional dependence, and how do finite-sample approximation errors interact with the classifier’s error to affect Type I/II errors?

3. Is the method symmetric in X and Y? From a causal perspective, conditional dependence given Z may result from X → Y or Y → X. In these two cases, do any steps (e.g., sampling or conditioning choices) introduce asymmetries or require choosing which variable to sample? The manuscript does not appear to address this; clarification would be helpful.

4. I am concerned about how to select the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3. Please provide practical guidance (e.g., heuristics, cross-validation, sensitivity analysis), theoretical criteria, or default settings with justification, and discuss how performance depends on τ.

Minor:
- In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk appears not to be normalized; it should be divided by the sample size.
