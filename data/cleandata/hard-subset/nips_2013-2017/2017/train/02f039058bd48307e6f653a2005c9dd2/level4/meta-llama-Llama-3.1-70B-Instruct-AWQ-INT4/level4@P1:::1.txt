This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conduct conditional independence tests for iid data. The basic idea is to use a nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that follow a distribution close to the conditionally independent distribution f^{CI} and train a classifier to distinguish the observed distribution from the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution. If the classification performance is close to random guessing, one fails to reject the null hypothesis that the data follows conditional independence; otherwise, one rejects the null hypothesis.

The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI} in terms of total variational distance and bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for near-independent samples.

In general, the paper addresses an important problem and is presented in a clear way.

Detailed comments:

Major:

1. The proposed method can be decoupled into two major components: generating samples that mimic f_{CI} and using a classifier for determination. It is unclear whether existing solutions from the literature can be used to replace either of these steps. For example, using the permutation-based method proposed in [7] with the classification-based approach or combining the nearest neighbor bootstrap with the kernel two-sample test approach. The performance of these alternative methods is unknown.

2. The nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution is close to f_{CI} in the finite sample size setting. However, if the ground truth is that x and y are weakly dependent given z, it is unclear how the proposed method will behave.

3. The method appears to be symmetric with respect to x and y. However, from a causal perspective, x and y can be dependent given z due to two different causal models: x causes y or y causes x. In these scenarios, it may be necessary to determine which variable to sample, but the authors do not address this issue.

4. The choice of the parameter τ in algorithms 2 and 3 is not clearly explained, and it is unclear how this parameter should be selected.

Minor:

In algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size to obtain the average loss.
