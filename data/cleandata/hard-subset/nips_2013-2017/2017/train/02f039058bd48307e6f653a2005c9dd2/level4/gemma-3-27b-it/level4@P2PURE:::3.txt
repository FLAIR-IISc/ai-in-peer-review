The authors present a general test for conditional independence between three continuous variables. Their approach minimizes parametric assumptions by leveraging samples from the joint distribution to approximate the conditional product distribution in total variation distance. This approximation is then used to train a binary classifier – such as a deep neural network – and the resulting classification error is used to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The method is evaluated through both descriptions and experimental results on synthetic and real datasets, comparing its performance to existing techniques.

Conditional independence testing is a crucial tool in modern machine learning, particularly within causal inference, where assessing unconfoundedness is frequently required. The proposed method appears both intuitive and sufficiently powerful for application in a range of causal settings.

The paper is well-written and, despite the complexity of the theoretical underpinnings, the authors effectively summarize their results and clarify the methodology for practitioners. The theoretical results are particularly noteworthy. While the method represents a natural extension of the independence test presented in “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf) – as the authors acknowledge, involving modifications to handle a third variable and neighbor matching – the strength of the theoretical guarantees is impressive. 

I have a few suggestions for improvement:
- Does a discrete version of this test exist? The bootstrap method often performs better when the conditioning variable Z is discrete.
- How robust is the test to the choice of classification method and its hyperparameters? Investigating the potential for differing acceptance/rejection outcomes with varying hyperparameters would be valuable and complement the existing simulations.
- Section 1.1, outlining the main contributions, feels redundant. The authors reiterate their approach multiple times, and the level of detail appears excessive.
- Including Algorithm 2 in full detail seems unnecessary; the process is adequately described elsewhere in the paper and could be presented more concisely.
- There are minor typographical errors: “classifier” should be used in line 157, and “graphs” in line 280.
