This paper introduces a general, non-parametric test for conditional independence among three continuous variables. The proposed approach avoids strong parametric assumptions by leveraging samples from the joint distribution to construct a second set of samples that approximates the conditional product distribution. The authors show this approximation is close in total variation distance. A binary classifier, such as a deep neural network, is then trained to distinguish between samples from the original joint distribution and the constructed conditional product distribution. The resulting classification error serves as the test statistic for accepting or rejecting the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors validate their method against existing techniques through both qualitative descriptions and empirical evaluations on synthetic and real-world datasets.

The problem of conditional independence testing is fundamental to modern machine learning, with significant applications in causal inference for verifying assumptions like unconfoundedness. The method presented here is both elegant in its simplicity and potent, making it a promising tool for a wide range of causal discovery and inference tasks. Furthermore, the paper is clearly written. Although the theoretical derivations and proofs are dense, the authors effectively distill the key takeaways and present the methodology in a manner that is accessible to practitioners.

The primary contribution of this work lies in its theoretical results. As the authors correctly note, the proposed methodology can be seen as a natural extension of the classifier-based two-sample test from Lopez-Paz and Oquab (2017), adapted for the conditional setting by introducing a third variable, Z, and performing nearest-neighbor matching on it. Despite this conceptual similarity to prior work, the theoretical guarantees the authors establish are novel and impressive.

A few suggestions for improvement:

*   Could the framework be extended to handle discrete variables? It seems the bootstrap procedure would be particularly effective when the conditioning variable Z is discrete.
*   How robust is the test to the choice of the binary classifier and its hyperparameters? An analysis of whether minor changes in the classifier's configuration could alter the test's outcome would be a valuable addition to the experimental section.
*   The "Main Contributions" in Section 1.1 feels repetitive. The summary of the approach is presented multiple times throughout the introduction, and this section could likely be condensed.
*   The inclusion of Algorithm 2 seems superfluous. The procedure it outlines has already been detailed in the text and could be summarized more concisely without a formal algorithm block.
*   Minor typographical errors were noted: "classifier" (line 157) and "graphs" (line 280).
