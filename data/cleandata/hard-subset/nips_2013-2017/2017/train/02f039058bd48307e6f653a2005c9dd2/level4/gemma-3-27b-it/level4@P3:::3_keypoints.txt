- Problem: Addresses non-parametric conditional independence testing for continuous variables.
- Method: Frames CI testing as a binary classification problem using a model-powered approach.
- Sample generation: Uses a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate the conditionally independent distribution.
- Classifiers: Leverages powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, deep neural nets) to distinguish joint vs. conditionally independent distributions.
- Theory: Provides guarantees on the generated samples (TV distance bounds) and generalization bounds for classification under near-independence.
- Empirics: Demonstrates empirical improvements over state-of-the-art methods on synthetic and real datasets.
- Scalability: Shows promising scaling behavior with respect to the dimensionality of Z.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on kernel-based CI tests and prior classification-based independence testing, offering a compelling alternative.
- Strength — Novelty: The reduction to classification is a fresh and promising perspective.
- Strength — Theoretical rigor: Significant theoretical contributions distinguish the work.
- Strength — Practicality: Public code repository improves reproducibility and usability.
- Weakness — Redundancy: Section 1.1 (“Main Contributions”) is repetitive with the introduction.
- Weakness — Presentation: Algorithm 2 is presented in excessive detail and could be streamlined.
- Weakness — Sensitivity: Lacks analysis of sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters.
- Weakness — Discrete Z: Does not discuss applying the bootstrap when Z is discrete.
- Weakness — Minor issues: A few typos (e.g., "classifier" line 157, "graphs" line 280).
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, theoretically strong, empirically superior, reproducible, and impactful.
- Rejection arguments: Repetitive presentation, missing sensitivity analysis, and reliance on smoothness assumptions that may not always hold.
- Recommendation: Accept, with suggested revisions to reduce redundancy, streamline Algorithm 2, discuss discrete Z, and add sensitivity analysis.
