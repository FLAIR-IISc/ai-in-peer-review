This paper proposes a model-based approach to perform conditional independence tests for independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) data. The core idea leverages nearest neighbor bootstrapping to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, f^{CI}. A classifier is then trained and evaluated to assess its ability to distinguish between the observed data distribution and the distribution generated by the nearest neighbor bootstrap. If the classifier's performance is comparable to random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is accepted.

The authors develop bounds quantifying the closeness between the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI} in terms of total variational distance, and provide bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification conditions and for near-independent samples.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is presented clearly. 

Detailed comments:

Major:

1. The proposed method appears to decompose into two main components: generating samples that approximate f_{CI}, and utilizing a classifier for decision-making. I question whether either of these components could be replaced with existing methods from the literature. For example, could one substitute the permutation-based method from [7] followed by a classification approach? Alternatively, could the nearest neighbor bootstrap be combined with a kernel two-sample test? I am interested in understanding the performance implications of such substitutions.

2. The method relies on the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution approximating f_{CI} even with finite sample sizes. However, if the true relationship between x and y is weak dependence given z, how would the proposed method discern this from conditional independence?

3. Is the method symmetric with respect to x and y? From a causal perspective, dependence between x and y given z can arise from either x causing y, or y causing x. These two scenarios may necessitate sampling from different variables. The authors do not address this consideration.

4. I have serious concerns regarding the selection of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3.

Minor:

In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculations appear to be missing division by the sample size.
