The paper proposes a new conditional independence test.

**Strengths**

1) The paper correctly identifies that a crucial assumption regarding the smoothness of the conditional density is necessary for any such test to function effectively. The authors propose such an assumption and provide a bound on the total variation distance between the density under the null hypothesis and the density obtained from bootstrapping under this assumption. This contribution is novel and significant.

2) The paper leverages this smoothness assumption to bound the error of the optimal classifier on the training set (1.1(iii)).

3) Theorem 1, which combines the smoothness assumption, the assumption that $\hat{R}$ can be achieved with error $\eta$, and the assumption regarding the difference in errors between the optimal and trained classifiers, is novel, insightful, and non-trivial.

**Weaknesses**

The paper is difficult to follow and lacks sufficient interpretation of its results. This makes it challenging to fully grasp the implications of the work; I am not entirely confident in my understanding. For example, consider the inequality in section 1.1(iii). Assume the alternative hypothesis holds and $r_0 = 0.1$. Suppose $\mathcal{G}$ is a small class – say, linear functions – and suppose these functions are insufficiently expressive to distinguish between $f$ and $f^{\text{CI}}$ at all. Could you explain, intuitively, how a small probability of error can be achieved (i.e., $\hat{R} < 0.1 + o_1 < 0.2$ for large *n*) in this scenario?
