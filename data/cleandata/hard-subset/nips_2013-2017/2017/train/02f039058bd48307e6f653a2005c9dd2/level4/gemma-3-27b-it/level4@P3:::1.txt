This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables. The core idea is to leverage the power of modern classifiers by reformulating the CI test as a binary classification problem. This is achieved by generating samples approximating the conditional independence distribution f<sup>CI</sup> using a novel nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure, and then training a classifier to distinguish between samples from the true joint distribution and the bootstrapped distribution. Theoretical guarantees are provided regarding the accuracy of the bootstrap approximation (in terms of total variational distance) and generalization bounds for the classifier in a non-i.i.d setting. The method is empirically validated on simulated and real datasets, demonstrating improvements over existing approaches.

This paper addresses an important problem in statistical inference with potential applications in causal discovery, Bayesian network structure learning, and feature selection. The approach of reducing CI testing to a classification problem is conceptually sound and leverages recent advances in machine learning. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the proposed method and theoretical results. The empirical evaluation provides initial evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem, combined with the nearest neighbor bootstrap, provides a novel perspective on this problem.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper includes a rigorous theoretical analysis, including bounds on the accuracy of the bootstrap approximation and generalization guarantees for the classifier. The analysis of Rademacher complexity in the presence of near-independence is a valuable contribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate the performance gains of the proposed algorithm over existing methods, both on synthetic and real datasets.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Decoupling and Baselines:** The method can be viewed as two separate components (bootstrap and classification). It's unclear why the proposed combination is superior to using existing methods for each component individually. Specifically, the authors should discuss and empirically compare their method against approaches that combine the permutation-based method from [19] with a classifier, or the nearest-neighbor bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test (e.g., as in [10]).
*   **Weak Dependency:** When the true relationship between X and Y given Z is weak, the proposed method might struggle to distinguish between the distributions. The paper does not sufficiently address this scenario and its potential impact on performance. More investigation into the method’s sensitivity to the strength of the conditional dependency is needed.
*   **Symmetry and Causal Direction:** The method doesn't explicitly address the asymmetry inherent in causal relationships. The proposed approach treats X and Y symmetrically, while in reality, the dependency could be due to X causing Y or vice versa. This limitation should be acknowledged and discussed. The paper would benefit from considering how to incorporate causal assumptions or identify the direction of the dependency.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the threshold parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is crucial for performance, but the paper lacks a clear guidance on how to set this parameter appropriately. A more detailed discussion of parameter tuning and its impact on the results is needed.
*   **Minor Technical Detail:** In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculation should be normalized by the sample size (i.e., divided by *n*).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to CI testing.
*   The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing techniques.
*   The analysis of generalization bounds in the non-i.i.d setting is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of comparison with natural baselines (combining existing methods) weakens the empirical evaluation.
*   The potential issues with weak dependencies and causal asymmetry raise concerns about the applicability of the method in certain scenarios.
*   The sensitivity to the parameter τ and the lack of guidance on its selection are concerning.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the weaknesses mentioned above are significant, they do not fundamentally invalidate the paper’s contributions. The novelty of the approach, the strong theoretical foundation, and the promising empirical results suggest that this work has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in CI testing. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding the choice of τ, provide a more detailed discussion of the method’s limitations (weak dependencies, causal asymmetry), and include a comparison with relevant baseline methods that combine existing techniques. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
