- Proposes a general test for conditional independence of three continuous variables by generating samples approximating the conditional product distribution and using a binary classifier to test independence.  
- Avoids strong parametric assumptions by using samples from the joint distribution and neighbor-matching to approximate total variation distance to the conditional product.  
- Trains a classifier (e.g., deep neural network) on original vs. generated samples and uses classification error to accept/reject the null hypothesis.  
- The method is compared descriptively and empirically to existing approaches on synthetic and real datasets.  
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous guarantees and impressive theoretical results.  
- Conceptually straightforward reduction to classification, extending classifier two-sample tests to include a third variable Z.  
- Empirical validation shows effectiveness across varied datasets, supporting practical utility for causal inference.  
- Paper is generally well-written and explains methodology accessibly despite complex proofs.  
- Suggest exploring a discrete Z case, where the bootstrap procedure might be more powerful.  
- Recommend more sensitivity analysis on choice of classifier and hyperparameter settings to guide practical use.  
- Sections contain redundancy: “Main Contributions” (1.1) repeats the approach unnecessarily.  
- Algorithm 2 is overly verbose and could be summarized since text already explains the procedure.  
- Minor typos to fix (e.g., "classifier" at line 157, "graphs" at line 280).  
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretical rigor, novel and flexible approach, strong empirical results, and significance for causal inference.  
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical complexity may deter some readers, paper verbosity/redundancy, and limited sensitivity analysis.  
- Overall recommendation: strong contribution and recommended for acceptance pending minor revisions and additional sensitivity experiments.
