The authors propose a general test for conditional independence between three continuous variables. They avoid strong parametric assumptions by developing a method that uses samples from the joint distribution to generate samples approximating the conditional product distribution in terms of total variation distance. These generated samples, along with original samples, are then used to train a binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network); the classifier’s predictive error is used to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The method is compared to existing approaches, both descriptively and empirically, on synthetic and real datasets.

Conditional independence testing is a crucial tool in modern machine learning, particularly in causal inference, where assessing unconfoundedness is frequently required. The proposed method appears both conceptually straightforward and potentially powerful for a range of causal applications.

The paper is generally well-written. While the theoretical details and proofs are complex, the authors effectively summarize the results and clearly explain the methodology for practitioners. The most compelling aspect of the work is the theoretical foundation. The proposed method represents a natural extension of approaches for testing independence, such as that in “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (Lopez-Paz et al., 2016), as the authors acknowledge; it adapts the core idea to include a third variable Z and employs a similar neighbor-matching strategy. However, the theoretical guarantees provided are impressive.

Here are some additional comments and suggestions:

*   **Discrete Analog:** Does the method benefit from a discrete variable Z? The bootstrap procedure might be more powerful in that scenario, and exploring this could be valuable.
*   **Classifier Sensitivity:** How sensitive is the test to the choice of classification method and hyperparameter settings? Investigating the impact of these choices through additional simulations would strengthen the findings and provide practical guidance.
*   **Redundancy in Section 1.1:** The “Main Contributions” section (1.1) feels repetitive. The authors summarize their approach multiple times, and this level of detail seems unnecessary.
*   **Algorithm 2:** Presenting Algorithm 2 in full detail feels somewhat verbose. The process is already adequately described in the text and could be summarized more succinctly.
*   **Minor Typos:** There are a few minor typos: "classifier" should be used in line 157, and "graphs" in line 280.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the proposed method, demonstrating its validity and performance bounds.
*   **Novel Approach:** The reduction to a classification problem offers a powerful and flexible way to leverage advances in supervised learning for conditional independence testing.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to existing approaches.
*   **Significant Contribution:** The work addresses an important problem in machine learning and provides a valuable tool for causal inference and other applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Complexity:** The theoretical details may be challenging for readers without a strong background in statistical learning theory.
*   **Redundancy:** The repeated summaries of the approach can make the paper feel somewhat verbose.
*   **Limited Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field. The theoretical rigor, novel approach, and promising empirical results make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. Addressing the minor points above (reducing redundancy, providing more sensitivity analysis) would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
