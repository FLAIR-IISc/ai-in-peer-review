The authors propose a general test to test conditional independence between three continuous variables. The authors do not have strong parametric assumptions – instead, they propose a method that uses a sample from a combined distribution to estimate a conditional product distribution in the total variance distance. Then, they feed these samples into a binary classifier such as a deep neural net, and after training on the samples from each of the two distributions, they use the net's predictive error to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. They compare their proposed method with existing methods in the literature, with details and experimental results on synthetic and real data.

Conditional independence testing is an important tool for modern machine learning, especially in the field of cause inference, where we are often curious about claims of unconfoundedness. The proposed method seems straightforward and powerful enough to be used for many cause applications.

Furthermore, the paper is well-written. Although it may be difficult to follow the theoretical results and explanations of the evidence, the authors do a good job of summarizing the results and clarifying the methodology for practitioners.

For me, the most impressive part of the paper is the theoretical result. The proposed method is a somewhat straightforward extension of the method for testing independence in the paper "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Test" (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as the authors acknowledge (they modify the test to include a third variable Z, and after matching Z with its neighbors, the methods are the same). However, the guarantee of the theoretical results is impressive.

A few more comments:
-Is there a different analogue? When Z is different, the bootstrap becomes even more powerful, right?
-How sensitive is the test on the classification method used? Is it possible that some changed hyperparameters could give different acceptance and rejection results? It would be useful to see this with simulations.
-Section 1.1 of the main contribution is unnecessary – the authors often summarize their approach, and the limit seems unnecessary.
-Writing algorithm 2 seems unnecessary – this process could be described more concisely, and it has already been described in the paper.
