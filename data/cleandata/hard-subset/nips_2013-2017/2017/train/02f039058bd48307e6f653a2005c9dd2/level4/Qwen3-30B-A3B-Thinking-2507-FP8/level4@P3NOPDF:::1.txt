

This paper proposes a novel model-based approach for conditional independence testing in i.i.d. data, leveraging nearest-neighbor bootstrap (NNB) and classifier-based hypothesis testing. The core idea involves generating samples from a distribution approximating the conditional independence reference distribution \(f_{CI}\) via NNB, then training a classifier to distinguish between the observed data distribution and the NNB-generated samples. If the classifier's performance is indistinguishable from random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is rejected. The authors establish theoretical guarantees, including bounds on the total variation distance between the NNB distribution and \(f_{CI}\), as well as empirical risk bounds under ideal classification and near-independent sampling scenarios.

The paper addresses a significant problem in causal inference and statistical testing, and the presentation is generally clear and well-structured. However, several critical concerns regarding methodology, theoretical scope, and practical implementation require clarification before acceptance.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Methodological Decoupling and Component Substitutability:** The approach comprises two key components: (a) generating samples mimicking \(f_{CI}\) (via NNB), and (b) using a classifier for hypothesis testing. The paper does not sufficiently justify why *both* components are necessary. Could one component be replaced with established alternatives without compromising performance? For instance, replacing the NNB with a permutation-based reference distribution (e.g., [7]) and using the classifier for testing, or combining NNB with a kernel two-sample test (e.g., [15]) for the hypothesis step? A comparative analysis of these alternatives—especially regarding power, Type I error control, and computational cost—would strengthen the contribution. The current work lacks such empirical or theoretical justification for the specific combination chosen.

2.  **Behavior under Weak Dependence:** The theoretical bounds assume a finite sample setting. Crucially, the paper does not address how the method behaves when \(X\) and \(Y\) are *weakly* dependent given \(Z\) (i.e., the true dependence is subtle). In such cases, the NNB distribution may not sufficiently approximate \(f_{CI}\), potentially leading to high Type II error (failing to reject a false null). The authors should provide analysis or simulations demonstrating the method's sensitivity to the *strength* of dependence, not just its presence/absence.

3.  **Asymmetry and Causal Interpretation:** The conditional independence test itself is symmetric in \(X\) and \(Y\). However, the reviewer notes a critical point: the *causal interpretation* of dependence given \(Z\) is not symmetric. If \(X \perp Y | Z\) fails, it could stem from \(X \rightarrow Y\) or \(Y \rightarrow X\). The test does not distinguish between these causal directions. While the paper focuses on statistical dependence, the discussion of causal models (e.g., "x causes y or y causes x") implies a need for directional inference. The authors should clarify whether the test is intended for causal discovery (where asymmetry matters) or purely for statistical dependence testing (where symmetry is expected). If the former, the method requires additional assumptions or modifications to handle directionality, which is currently unaddressed.

4.  **Parameter Sensitivity (\(\tau\)):** The choice of \(\tau\) in Algorithms 2 and 3 (defining the neighborhood size for NNB) is pivotal to the approximation quality and test performance. The paper provides no guidance on selecting \(\tau\) (e.g., via cross-validation, theoretical derivation, or sensitivity analysis). This parameter is likely highly data-dependent and could significantly impact results. The authors must demonstrate how \(\tau\) should be chosen in practice and quantify its influence on Type I/II errors across different datasets or dependence strengths.

**Minor Concerns:**

*   **Algorithm 2 & 3:** The empirical risk in these algorithms should be defined as the *average* loss over the sample (i.e., divided by the sample size \(n\)), not the total loss. This is a standard convention for empirical risk minimization and should be explicitly stated for clarity.
*   **Notation:** Use consistent subscript notation (e.g., \(f_{CI}\) instead of \(f^{CI}\)) throughout the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper tackles a fundamental problem with a theoretically grounded approach, and the core idea of using NNB for generating reference samples is novel. The clarity of presentation is commendable. However, the major concerns—particularly the lack of justification for the chosen methodology over alternatives, the gap in handling weak dependence, the unresolved issue of causal asymmetry in interpretation, and the critical dependency on \(\tau\) without guidance—significantly impact the paper's contribution and practical utility. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the work. As currently presented, the method's advantages over existing permutation-based or kernel-based approaches for conditional independence testing are not convincingly established, and key practical implementation details are missing.

**Recommendation:** The paper has potential but requires significant revision to address the major concerns above. I recommend **Rejection** in its current form, pending substantial revisions that provide:
1.  A clear justification for the NNB + classifier combination over established alternatives (with empirical comparisons).
2.  Analysis of performance under weak dependence.
3.  Clarification on the symmetry/asymmetry of the test and its intended scope (statistical vs. causal).
4.  Guidance on \(\tau\) selection and its impact on results.
5.  Correction of minor technical points (empirical risk normalization, notation).

The theoretical results are interesting but insufficient to overcome the methodological and practical gaps. The authors should focus on demonstrating the *unique value* of their specific approach before submission.
