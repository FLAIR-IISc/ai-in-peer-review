This paper introduces a novel, model-based approach to conduct conditional independence tests for i.i.d. data. The core idea is to reframe the hypothesis test as a two-sample problem. The first sample consists of the observed data, while the second is a synthetic dataset generated via nearest neighbor bootstrap. This bootstrapped distribution is designed to approximate the true data distribution under the null hypothesis of conditional independence (i.e., X ⊥ Y | Z).

A classifier is then trained to distinguish between the observed and the bootstrapped samples. If the classifier's performance is close to chance level, it suggests the two distributions are indistinguishable, and one fails to reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. Conversely, if the classifier can separate the two datasets with high accuracy, the null hypothesis is rejected.

The authors provide theoretical support for this methodology, developing bounds on the total variation distance between the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and the true conditional independence distribution. They also provide bounds on the empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for near-independent samples. In general, the paper addresses an important problem with an interesting approach and is presented clearly.

**Major Comments:**

1.  The proposed method appears to be modular, consisting of two primary components: a sample generation strategy (nearest neighbor bootstrap) and a testing mechanism (classification-based two-sample test). This modularity raises a natural question: can these components be interchanged with other existing techniques? For instance:
    *   Could the permutation-based sampling method proposed in [7] be used to generate the null distribution, followed by the proposed classification-based test?
    *   Alternatively, could the nearest neighbor bootstrap be paired with a different two-sample test, such as a kernel-based method, instead of a classifier?
    A discussion of these alternatives and a comparison of their potential performance would significantly strengthen the paper and clarify the unique contributions of each component.

2.  The theoretical analysis shows that the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution is a close approximation of the true conditional independence distribution in the finite sample regime. A key question is how the proposed method behaves in the presence of weak, but non-zero, conditional dependence. In such a scenario, the true data distribution is already very close to the null distribution. It is unclear whether the classifier would have sufficient power to distinguish the observed data from the bootstrapped samples, or if it would incorrectly fail to reject the null hypothesis. An analysis or empirical evaluation of the method's sensitivity to the strength of the conditional dependence would be valuable.

3.  The paper does not discuss the symmetry of the method with respect to the variables X and Y. From a causal perspective, conditional dependence between X and Y given Z can arise from different underlying causal structures (e.g., X causes Y, or Y causes X). Does the choice of which variable to resample during the nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure affect the test's outcome? If the procedure is not symmetric, the authors should provide guidance on how to make this choice. Clarification on this point is needed.

4.  A significant practical concern is the selection of the hyperparameter τ, the classification risk threshold used in Algorithms 2 and 3. The outcome of the test appears to be highly dependent on this value, yet the paper provides little guidance on how to set it in practice. A discussion of the method's sensitivity to τ and a principled, data-driven strategy for its selection would be crucial for the method's applicability and reproducibility.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk appears to be a sum over the samples. For it to be a risk (i.e., an expected loss), it should likely be normalized by the sample size. Please clarify if this is an omission.
