- Proposes reframing conditional independence testing X ⟂ Y | Z as a binary classification problem using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate counterfactual samples.  
- Bootstrapped samples approximate f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z); original samples are labeled positive and bootstrapped negative, classifier accuracy is the test statistic.  
- Significant contribution: leverages powerful modern classifiers for CI testing, showing strong empirical performance, especially in high-dimensional Z.  
- Theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1): nearest-neighbor bootstrap produces samples close to the target conditional product distribution in total variation distance.  
- Theoretical contribution (Theorem 2): novel Rademacher-style generalization analysis for classifiers trained on “near-independent” (not strictly i.i.d.) bootstrapped samples.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough: synthetic experiments show scalability with Z dimension and real flow-cytometry data demonstrates practical utility.  
- Novelty: the nearest-neighbor bootstrap for conditional product sampling and the near-i.i.d. theoretical analysis are new and clever.  
- Weakness: test performance depends on classifier choice and hyperparameters; sensitivity analysis/ablation is missing.  
- Weakness: clarity/organization could be improved; Main Contributions is repetitive and Algorithm 2 pseudocode is unnecessary.  
- Question: how would the method extend to discrete or mixed-type variables; discrete Z simplifies to exact matching—would guarantees tighten?  
- Question: how sensitive is performance to classifier expressiveness (e.g., XGBoost vs logistic regression), particularly in low-dimensional settings?  
- Minor comments: two typos noted (Line 157 “classifier”, Line 280 “graphs”).  
- Overall judgment: strong, well-motivated, theoretically rigorous, and empirically superior approach; minor weaknesses do not outweigh contributions—suitable for acceptance.
