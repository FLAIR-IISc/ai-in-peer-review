This paper proposes a new conditional independence test.

### Strengths

1.  A key contribution of this paper is the identification that a smoothness assumption on the conditional density is necessary for a test to be effective. The authors propose such an assumption and use it to bound the total variation distance between the distribution under the null hypothesis and the distribution obtained via a bootstrap procedure. This theoretical development is novel.

2.  The paper leverages this smoothness assumption to bound the error of the optimal classifier on the training set (as described in Section 1.1 (iii)).

3.  Theorem 1 represents a novel, insightful, and non-trivial result. It skillfully combines the smoothness assumption with further assumptions on the achievability of the empirical error rate (hat R) and the generalization gap (the difference in error between the optimal and trained classifiers).

### Weaknesses

The paper is difficult to read and lacks sufficient interpretation of the results, to the extent that I am not sure I understand them correctly.

For example, I would appreciate an intuitive explanation for the following scenario related to the inequality in Section 1.1 (iii). Assume the alternative hypothesis holds, and the optimal error rate is `r_0 = 0.1`. Now, suppose the classifier class `G` is simple (e.g., linear functions) and lacks the expressiveness to distinguish at all between the true density `f` and the density under conditional independence, `f^CI`. Can you explain, intuitively, how a small empirical error rate (i.e., `hat R < 0.1 + o(1) < 0.2` for large `n`) can be achieved? It seems that any classifier from this insufficiently expressive class `G` would be unable to capture the dependency, leading to an error rate far from the optimal `r_0`.
