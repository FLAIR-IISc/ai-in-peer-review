

This paper proposes a model-free approach for conditional independence testing in iid data. The core idea uses nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution $f^{CI}$, then trains a classifier to distinguish between the observed data and the bootstrapped samples. If classification performance approaches random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is rejected. The authors establish bounds on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and $f^{CI}$, as well as bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for nearly independent samples.

The paper addresses an important problem with a clear presentation. However, several critical concerns remain:

**Major Comments**  
1. **Methodological Decoupling**: The approach appears to decompose into two distinct components: (i) generating samples mimicking $f^{CI}$, and (ii) using a classifier for hypothesis testing. Could either component be replaced with established methods? For instance, could a permutation-based test (e.g., [7]) be combined with the classification framework? Alternatively, could nearest-neighbor bootstrap be paired with a kernel two-sample test? The authors should compare the performance of such hybrid approaches to justify their specific design.

2. **Weak Dependence Sensitivity**: The theoretical guarantees assume the bootstrapped distribution closely approximates $f^{CI}$ in finite samples. How does the method perform when the true dependence between $X$ and $Y$ given $Z$ is weak (e.g., low mutual information)? The paper lacks analysis for such cases, where distinguishing weak dependence from independence is particularly challenging.

3. **Causal Asymmetry**: The method treats $X$ and $Y$ symmetrically, but causal relationships are inherently asymmetric (e.g., $X \rightarrow Y$ vs. $Y \rightarrow X$). When $X$ and $Y$ are dependent given $Z$ due to a causal direction, the method does not account for which variable should be sampled. This is critical in causal inference contexts, yet the paper ignores this distinction entirely.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The choice of $\tau$ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is critical but unaddressed. How should $\tau$ be selected in practice? The authors must provide guidance on tuning $\tau$ or demonstrate robustness across reasonable values, as poor choices could invalidate results.

**Minor Comments**  
- In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size (not just computed as a sum). This is a standard normalization that ensures consistency with typical risk definitions.

Overall, the paper presents a novel testing framework with theoretical backing. However, the unresolved methodological gaps—particularly regarding weak dependence, causal asymmetry, and parameter selection—undermine its practical applicability. The authors should address these points before the work can be considered for publication.
