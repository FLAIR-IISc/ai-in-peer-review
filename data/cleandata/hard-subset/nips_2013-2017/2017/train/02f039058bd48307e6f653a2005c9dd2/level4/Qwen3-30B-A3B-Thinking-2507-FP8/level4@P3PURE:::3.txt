

The authors propose a general test for conditional independence among three continuous variables, requiring no strong parametric assumptions. Instead, they generate samples approximating the conditional product distribution (via matching on the conditioning variable Z) in total variation distance, then feed these samples into a binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network). After training the classifier on samples from the joint and conditional product distributions, the test uses the classifier's predictive error to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The paper compares the proposed method against existing approaches through both theoretical descriptions and empirical results on synthetic and real data.

Conditional independence testing is a critical tool in modern machine learning, particularly for causal inference where assumptions of unconfoundedness are frequently evaluated. The proposed method is straightforward and sufficiently powerful for widespread causal applications. The paper is well-written: while the theoretical proofs require careful reading, the authors effectively summarize results and clarify the methodology for practitioners.

The most significant contribution lies in the theoretical guarantees. The method extends the two-sample test from "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (arXiv:1610.06545) by incorporating a conditioning variable Z (with neighbor matching), as the authors acknowledge. The theoretical results provide strong, novel guarantees for this extension.

Specific comments:
- A discrete analog is not addressed. When Z is discrete, the bootstrap procedure may offer stronger theoretical guarantees—this merits discussion.
- Sensitivity to the choice of classifier and hyperparameters is unclear. It would be valuable to assess whether minor hyperparameter variations lead to inconsistent acceptance/rejection decisions, alongside the existing simulations.
- Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) is repetitive; the approach is summarized multiple times unnecessarily.
- Algorithm 2 is redundant; the described process is already sufficiently detailed in the text and need not be written out explicitly.
- Typos: "classifier" (line 157), "graphs" (line 280).
