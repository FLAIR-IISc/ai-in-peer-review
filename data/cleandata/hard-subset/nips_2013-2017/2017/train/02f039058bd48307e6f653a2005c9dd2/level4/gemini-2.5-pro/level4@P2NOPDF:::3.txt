This paper introduces a general, non-parametric test for conditional independence between three continuous random variables (X, Y, Z). The core idea is to reframe the problem as a two-sample test. The authors propose a method that uses samples from the joint distribution P(X,Y,Z) to construct samples from the conditional product distribution P(X|Z)P(Y|Z)P(Z), which corresponds to the null hypothesis of X and Y being independent given Z. This construction is guaranteed to produce a distribution that is close to the true conditional product in total variation distance. These two sets of samples—one from the original joint distribution and one from the constructed null distribution—are then fed to a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network. The classifier's accuracy in distinguishing between the two serves as the test statistic for accepting or rejecting the null hypothesis. The authors validate their method against existing approaches through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.

Conditional independence testing is a fundamental tool in modern machine learning, with critical applications in causal inference for verifying assumptions like unconfoundedness. The proposed method is both elegant in its simplicity and appears powerful enough for practical use in these domains. The paper is well-written and accessible; while the theoretical derivations are dense, the authors excel at summarizing the key results and presenting the methodology in a way that is clear for practitioners.

The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical contributions. The authors correctly acknowledge that their method is an extension of the classifier two-sample test framework proposed in "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests." Their novel contribution is the adaptation of this framework to the conditional setting by introducing a nearest-neighbor matching procedure on the conditioning variable Z. The theoretical guarantees that accompany this extension are impressive and form the most significant part of the paper.

I have a few minor comments and questions for the authors:

*   The paper focuses on continuous variables. Could the authors comment on extending this framework to discrete or mixed-type variables? For a discrete conditioning variable Z, the nearest-neighbor matching becomes exact, which would seem to simplify and strengthen the procedure.
*   A crucial practical question is the sensitivity of the test to the choice of the classifier and its hyperparameters. How robust are the results to changes in network architecture, optimization parameters, or other training details? It would be valuable to see an analysis of this alongside the simulations, as it could inform whether different hyperparameter settings might lead to different conclusions about independence.
*   The summary of contributions in Section 1.1 feels somewhat repetitive, as the core ideas are introduced multiple times throughout the introduction. This section could be condensed for improved conciseness.
*   Similarly, Algorithm 2, which outlines the training and testing procedure, could likely be omitted. The process is described clearly in the main text and is standard practice for classifier-based tests.
*   I noted a few minor typos: "classifier" should be used in line 157, and "graphs" in line 280.
