This paper introduces a novel, distribution-free test for conditional independence among three continuous variables. The core idea involves generating samples approximating the conditional product distribution using samples from the joint distribution, measured via total variation distance. A binary classifier – exemplified by a deep neural network – is then trained to discriminate between these distributions, with its resulting predictive error used to assess the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, comparing their method to existing approaches through both detailed descriptions and experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Conditional independence testing is a fundamental component of modern machine learning, particularly within causal inference where assessing unconfoundedness is crucial. The proposed method appears both conceptually simple and practically effective, holding promise for a wide range of causal applications. 

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the methodology to practitioners, despite some complexity in the theoretical details and proofs. The theoretical guarantees are particularly noteworthy. While building upon the framework of the “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” paper (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf) – as the authors acknowledge, through an extension to incorporate a third variable and neighbor matching – the resulting theoretical bounds are strong.

Several points could be further addressed:
- Does a discrete version of this test exist, and would the bootstrap procedure be enhanced with a discrete conditioning variable Z?
- How robust is the test’s performance to variations in the chosen classification method and its hyperparameters? Investigating the sensitivity of the results to these choices alongside the existing simulations would be valuable.
- Section 1.1, outlining the main contributions, feels somewhat repetitive, as the approach is summarized multiple times.
- The explicit presentation of Algorithm 2 appears unnecessary, given the process is already adequately described elsewhere in the paper.
- Minor typographical corrections are suggested: “classifier” in line 157 and “graphs” in line 280.
