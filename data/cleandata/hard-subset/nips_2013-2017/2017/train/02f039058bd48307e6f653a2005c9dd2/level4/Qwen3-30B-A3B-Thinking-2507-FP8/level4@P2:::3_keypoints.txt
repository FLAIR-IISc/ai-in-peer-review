- Proposes reducing conditional independence testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).

- Nearest-neighbor bootstrap generates samples close to f_CI in total variation distance, addressing the core sampling challenge.

- Uses powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, deep nets) to label original samples vs bootstrap samples; classifier error yields the CI test.

- Extends classifier-based two-sample/independence tests (Lopez-Paz & Oquab) to the Z-dependent CI setting with a more complex bootstrap.

- Provides theoretical guarantees missing from prior methods, with Theorem 1 proving bootstrap TV closeness.

- Establishes generalization bounds under near-independence for classification (Theorem 2), accounting for non-i.i.d. bootstrap samples.

- Empirical results show clear performance gains over state-of-the-art methods, especially when Z is high-dimensional, with ROC AUC and scaling analysis.

- Paper is well-written and accessible; methodology and motivation are clearly explained though some theoretical parts are dense.

- Algorithm 2 is presented redundantly and could be made more concise.

- Method is original in applying a theoretically justified nearest-neighbor bootstrap to enable classifier-based CI testing.

- Differs from permutation-based and kernel CI methods by providing theoretical bootstrap guarantees and avoiding linear programs.

- Significant potential impact for causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection, particularly in high-dimensional contexts.

- Strengths: strong theoretical foundation, clear empirical advantages, flexible classifier integration, and well-structured presentation.

- Weaknesses: redundant method description, overly detailed Algorithm 2, insufficient analysis of classifier hyperparameter sensitivity, and no discussion of discrete Z cases.

- Pros: rigorous proofs, empirical superiority in high dimensions, well-motivated and practical framework with theoretical backing.

- Cons: presentation redundancy, lack of hyperparameter sensitivity study, and omission of discrete Z discussion.

- Overall recommendation: accept — significant, theoretically sound, empirically validated contribution with only minor presentation and analysis gaps.
