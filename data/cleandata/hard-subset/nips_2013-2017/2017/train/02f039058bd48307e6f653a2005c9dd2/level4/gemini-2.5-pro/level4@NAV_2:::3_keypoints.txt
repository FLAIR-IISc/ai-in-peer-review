- Presents a novel classifier-based approach (CCIT) for nonparametric conditional independence testing by reducing X ⟂ Y | Z to a binary classification problem.  
- Core technical novelty: a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples approximating f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Uses classifier test error (≈50% under null) as the test statistic, enabling powerful models (GBTs, DNNs) to perform CI testing.  
- Theorem 1: proves the bootstrap samples converge in total variation to the target conditional product distribution.  
- Theorem 2: provides new generalization bounds for classification with the near-independent samples produced by the bootstrap.  
- Empirical evaluation is extensive and shows superior performance over kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially as dim(Z) grows.  
- Authors address finite-sample bootstrap bias and propose a practical bias-corrected Algorithm 3.  
- Paper quality is high: well-founded method, strong theoretical support, and comprehensive experiments.  
- Clarity is good and reproducible, with minor suggestions to condense repetitive sections and fix small typos.  
- Originality is strong: novel bootstrap approach and theoretical analysis of Rademacher complexity for near-independent samples.  
- Significance: important advance for CI testing with practical impact on causal inference, feature selection, and structure learning.  
- Suggestions: analyze sensitivity to classifier choice/hyperparameters and discuss extensions to discrete or mixed-type variables.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, practical, state-of-the-art empirical results, and solid theoretical guarantees.  
- Argument against acceptance: reliance on classifier/hyperparameter choices not fully explored (considered a minor issue).  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept.
