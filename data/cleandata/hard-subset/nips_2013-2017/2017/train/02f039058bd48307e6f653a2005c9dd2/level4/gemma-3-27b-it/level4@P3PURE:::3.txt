The authors propose a general test for conditional independence between three continuous variables. Their approach avoids strong parametric assumptions by leveraging samples from the joint distribution to generate samples approximating the conditional product distribution in total variation distance. These generated samples are then used to train a binary classifier – such as a deep neural network – and the classifier’s predictive error on samples from the two respective distributions is used to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The proposed method is compared to existing approaches through both descriptions and experimental results on synthetic and real datasets.

Conditional independence testing is a crucial tool in modern machine learning, particularly within causal inference, where assessing unconfoundedness is frequently required. The proposed method appears both conceptually straightforward and sufficiently powerful for a wide range of causal applications. 

The paper is well-written overall. While the theoretical details and proofs can be challenging to follow, the authors effectively summarize their findings and clearly articulate the methodology for practitioners. The most compelling aspect of this work lies in its theoretical results. The proposed method builds upon the independence testing framework presented in “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as the authors acknowledge; specifically, they extend the test to incorporate a third variable Z and, following neighbor matching with respect to Z, the methodologies exhibit significant similarity. However, the theoretical guarantees provided are remarkably strong.

Several points warrant further consideration:

- Does a discrete analog of this method exist? The bootstrap procedure often becomes more effective when Z is discrete.
- How sensitive is the test to the choice of classification method and its hyperparameters? It would be valuable to assess whether variations in hyperparameters could lead to differing acceptance or rejection decisions, and to present this analysis alongside the simulation results.
- Section 1.1, detailing the main contributions, is somewhat redundant. The authors reiterate their approach multiple times, and the extent of this repetition seems unnecessary.
- Presenting Algorithm 2 in full seems superfluous. The process could be described more concisely, as it has already been adequately described elsewhere in the paper.
- Minor typographical corrections are suggested: “classifier” in line 157 and “graphs” in line 280.
