The authors introduce a general-purpose test for conditional independence among three continuous variables. Rather than relying on strong parametric assumptions, they generate samples from the joint distribution that approximate the conditional product distribution in total variation distance. These samples are then supplied to a binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network), and after training on data from each distribution, the classifier’s predictive error is used to accept or reject the null of conditional independence. The paper compares the proposed method to prior work, both through conceptual discussion and experiments on synthetic and real datasets.

Conditional independence testing is a key tool in modern machine learning, especially in causal inference, where questions of unconfoundedness are common. The proposed approach appears both straightforward and sufficiently powerful for many causal applications.

The paper is well written. While some theoretical details and proofs are challenging to parse, the authors summarize the main results effectively and present the methodology clearly for practitioners.

The theoretical results are, to me, the most compelling part. The method is a relatively direct extension of the approach for testing (unconditional) independence in “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf)—as the authors note, they adapt the test to include a third variable Z, and after matching on neighbors of Z, the procedures are similar. Nevertheless, the theoretical guarantees they provide are impressive.

A few additional comments:
- Is there a discrete analog? If Z is discrete, does the bootstrap become even more effective?
- How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier? Could small hyperparameter changes lead to different accept/reject outcomes? It would be useful to include this alongside the simulations.
- Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) is redundant—the approach is summarized multiple times and could be tightened.
- Writing out Algorithm 2 seems unnecessary; the process can be described more concisely and is already explained elsewhere in the paper.
- Minor typos: “classifier” in line 157, “graphs” in line 280.
