Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a model-powered approach to nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method (i) constructs a proxy sample from a distribution close to fCI(x, y, z) = fX|Z(x|z) fY|Z(y|z) fZ(z) via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap in Z; (ii) labels original samples as class 1 and NN-bootstrapped samples as class 0; and (iii) trains a classifier (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, neural networks) to distinguish the two. Accuracy near chance implies failure to reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z; otherwise, reject H0. The authors provide theory showing that the NN bootstrap distribution is close to fCI in total variation (TV) distance under smoothness assumptions on f(z) and f(y|z), with TV convergence at a rate depending on dz. They also prove generalization bounds for classification with near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples produced by the bootstrap, adapting Rademacher/VC theory. Empirically, the method outperforms KCIT [32] and RCIT [28] on synthetic post-nonlinear settings and on CI verification tasks in a flow cytometry dataset [26].

This paper connects to prior NIPS and related work on kernel-based CI tests (KCIT [32], RCIT [28], KCIPT [10]), classifier-based two-sample and independence tests [19], nearest-neighbor entropy and mutual information estimation [12,13], and generalization bounds for non-i.i.d. processes [21]. The proposed NN bootstrap offers a computational alternative to the permutation scheme and linear program in [10], and the classification reduction is a natural extension of [19] from independence to conditional independence.

Strengths:
- Clear, modular reduction of CI testing to a two-sample classification problem, enabling use of strong classifiers in high dimensions.
- Novel NN bootstrap in Z with a total variation bound to fCI and an analysis that is intuitive and technically nontrivial.
- Generalization guarantees for classification under near-independence are of independent interest and extend classical VC/Rademacher arguments.
- Solid empirical results, including a real-data study, and publicly available code.
- The approach is flexible: improvements in classification or NN search can directly benefit the test.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Calibration and threshold selection: The test relies on a threshold τ (and a bias-correction via a Y,Z-only classifier in Algorithm 3). While τ ≈ 1/√n is used in experiments, a more principled calibration is desirable. For reproducible Type I error control and p-values, could the authors provide a null calibration via resampling (e.g., repeat the NN bootstrap with multiple random splits U1/U2/U3, or use permutation within Z-neighborhoods) to estimate the null distribution of the test statistic? Alternatively, can an asymptotic null distribution be derived?
- Power against weak dependence: When X and Y are weakly dependent given Z (local alternatives), the NN-bootstrapped distribution is close to fCI and the classification problem becomes hard. Can the authors provide power analyses (e.g., local alternatives with dependence strength shrinking with n) and empirical power curves versus effect size and dz? This would contextualize the dz-dependence in the TV bound.
- Asymmetry in X and Y: The bootstrap constructs Y′ by nearest neighbors in Z while keeping X fixed, and the bias correction also trains a classifier on (Y,Z). CI is symmetric in X and Y given Z; the procedure is not obviously symmetric. Does this asymmetry affect power or Type I error in scenarios where the conditional structure differs for X|Z versus Y|Z? Would a symmetrized version (run both “swap Y given Z” and “swap X given Z” and aggregate) improve robustness?
- Component interchangeability: The pipeline naturally decomposes into (A) generating samples approximating fCI and (B) performing a two-sample test. Have the authors explored hybrid variants, such as pairing their NN bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test (e.g., MMD) instead of a classifier, or pairing the permutation strategy in [10] with a classifier? Such ablations would clarify where the gains arise (generation vs. testing) and provide guidance for practitioners.
- Dependence on dz: The TV bound and generalization results incur terms scaling with dz and imply slow rates (e.g., n−1/dz). While the empirical results are encouraging, it would help to discuss when the NN bootstrap remains effective in high-dimensional Z (e.g., manifold structure, clustered Z, metric learning for Z) and to report sensitivity to dz with fixed n.
- Statistical guarantees and outputs: The paper mainly reports accuracies/ROC AUC. For adoption in structure learning pipelines, calibrated p-values or controlled false positive rates are crucial. Please add a procedure for p-value computation and provide Type I error plots across n and dz.
- Computational complexity: A more explicit complexity analysis (in n, dx, dy, dz) for the NN search and the overall test would be useful, along with wall-clock comparisons to KCIT/RCIT, especially as dz grows.

Evaluation by review criteria:
- Quality: The methodology is technically sound with reasonable assumptions. The TV bound and near-i.i.d. generalization results are carefully argued. Some practical aspects (τ calibration, p-values) need clarification for rigorous testing. Empirical validation is substantial.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well organized and readable. Suggestions: define constants (e.g., cdz) when introduced; ensure consistent notation for losses and datasets; clearly separate the bias term b(n) from τ and explain how τ is chosen in practice; proof sketches for main theorems in the main text would aid readability.
- Originality: The combination of an NN-based conditional product simulator with classification-based two-sample testing, plus non-i.i.d. generalization bounds, appears novel and distinct from KCIPT and kernel CI tests.
- Significance: The approach is promising for scalable CI testing and may influence practical causal discovery pipelines by leveraging modern classifiers. If accompanied by reliable calibration, it could become a widely used alternative to kernel-based CI tests.

Pros and cons for acceptance:
Pros:
- Simple, flexible reduction to classification with strong empirical gains.
- New NN bootstrap with TV guarantees to fCI.
- Generalization bounds under near-independence; theoretical contributions of independent interest.
- Open-source implementation; compelling real-data application.

Cons:
- Lack of principled calibration/p-values and guidance for τ selection.
- Potential asymmetry in X vs. Y and limited discussion of its impact.
- Theoretical rates degrade with dz; more guidance on when the method is effective in high-dimensional Z would help.
- Limited ablations to disentangle contributions of the bootstrap vs. classifier.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a calibration scheme (resampling-based null, or asymptotic) to report p-values and control Type I error; include corresponding empirical results.
- Analyze power under local alternatives and add sensitivity analyses for weak dependence and varying dz.
- Discuss and, if possible, evaluate a symmetrized variant (swap X given Z as well as Y given Z).
- Add ablations: NN bootstrap + MMD, KCIPT-style permutation + classifier, and multiple classifier choices.
- Expand on computational complexity and practical guidance for choosing the classifier class G and its VC/proxy capacity.

Minor comments:
- In Algorithms 2 and 3, ensure the empirical risk is normalized by sample size (it appears correct, but please double-check). Also fix minor typos (e.g., “developped” → “developed,” “variantional” → “variational,” “devided” → “divided,” spacing around symbols).
- Clarify the role and definition of G(ε), and state all constants upon first use.
- Consider reporting confidence intervals for ROC AUC and accuracy across bootstrap runs.
