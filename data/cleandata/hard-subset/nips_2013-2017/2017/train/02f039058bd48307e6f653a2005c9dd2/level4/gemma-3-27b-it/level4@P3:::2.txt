This paper proposes a novel model-powered conditional independence (CI) test, framing the problem as a binary classification task. The core idea is to leverage powerful classifiers (like gradient-boosted trees or deep neural networks) by generating samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, *f<sub>CI</sub>*, through a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of these bootstrapped samples and establish generalization bounds for the resulting classifier, ultimately translating into error bounds for the CI test.

The paper builds upon a growing body of work utilizing model-driven approaches for statistical inference, reminiscent of recent efforts in independence testing [19]. However, unlike prior work that often relies on permutation-based techniques, this paper tackles the more challenging problem of approximating *f<sub>CI</sub>* directly, especially in high-dimensional settings. It connects to kernel-based CI tests [32, 28, 10] by offering an alternative, classifier-based approach. The contribution most closely related is [10], however, this paper distinguishes itself by using a simpler nearest-neighbor bootstrap instead of solving a costly linear program and provides stronger theoretical guarantees based on total variational distance. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to CI testing, converting it into a classification problem and using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate the critical *f<sub>CI</sub>* distribution. The theoretical analysis relating smoothness assumptions to the quality of the bootstrap is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper includes a substantial theoretical analysis. Theorems 1 and 2 provide concrete bounds on the performance of the proposed method, considering both the accuracy of the bootstrap and the generalization ability of the classifier. The analysis of Rademacher complexity in a non-i.i.d setting is of independent interest.
*   **Practical Potential:** By leveraging the power of modern classification algorithms, the method has the potential to scale well to high-dimensional data, as demonstrated by the empirical results.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the importance of CI testing and positions the work within the broader context of model-powered statistical inference.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is dense and often difficult to follow. The notation is heavy, and the logical flow could be improved. A more intuitive explanation of the key steps and theoretical results would be beneficial. For example, the connection between the assumptions and the final error bounds isn't always immediately clear.
*   **Insufficient Intuition:** The discussion of the theoretical results often lacks sufficient intuition. For instance, the impact of the smoothness assumptions on the performance of the bootstrap procedure could be explained more concretely.
*   **Limited Empirical Analysis:** While the empirical results demonstrate promising performance, the experiments are relatively limited. A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods on a wider range of datasets, including those with varying dimensionality and signal-to-noise ratios, would strengthen the claims.
* **Algorithm 3 and Bias Correction:** The improvement in Algorithm 3 is not clearly motivated. The rationale for subtracting the bias estimated from the Y and Z coordinates is not sufficiently explained. More discussion on the practical impact of this correction would be helpful.



**Specific Questions/Suggestions:**

*   In section 1.1(iii), consider the alternative hypothesis holds and *r<sub>0</sub>* = 0.1. Suppose *G* is a limited class of functions (e.g., linear functions) that are insufficiently expressive to distinguish *f* and *f<sub>CI</sub>*. Could you intuitively explain how a small probability of error can still be achieved (*ĝ* < 0.1 + *o<sub>1</sub>* < 0.2 for large *n*)?
*   The paper mentions that the performance scales better than expected in high dimensions. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of this phenomenon? Is it due to the choice of classifier, the properties of the data, or some other factor?
*   The impact of the Lipschitz constant assumption on f(z) (Assumption 2) should be discussed further. How sensitive is the performance of the algorithm to violations of this assumption?




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to conditional independence testing.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the performance of the proposed method.
*   The empirical results demonstrate promising performance, suggesting the potential for practical impact.
*   The approach opens up new avenues for research in model-powered statistical inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper is difficult to read and understand, hindering its accessibility to a wider audience.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited and could be strengthened with more extensive experiments.
*   The lack of intuitive explanations for the theoretical results makes it challenging to assess the significance of the contributions fully.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses in clarity and presentation, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The novel approach, strong theoretical foundation, and promising empirical results warrant acceptance. However, I strongly encourage the authors to address the readability issues and expand the empirical evaluation in a revised version.

**Recommendation:** Accept with Minor Revisions. The revisions should focus on improving clarity, providing more intuitive explanations, and expanding the empirical analysis.
