This paper introduces a model-based approach for conducting conditional independence tests on independent and identically distributed (iid) data. The core idea leverages nearest neighbor bootstrapping to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, f^{CI}. A classifier is then trained and evaluated to discriminate between the observed data distribution and the distribution generated via bootstrapping.  Classification performance near random guessing leads to a failure to reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence; conversely, strong classification performance supports rejecting the null hypothesis.

The authors establish bounds quantifying the proximity of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution to f^{CI} using total variational distance, and provide bounds on empirical risks under both ideal classification conditions and for nearly independent samples. 

Overall, the paper tackles a significant problem and is presented with commendable clarity.

Detailed Comments:

Major:

1. The proposed method appears decomposable into two primary stages: generating samples approximating f_{CI}, and employing a classifier for decision-making. It is unclear whether either stage could be replaced with existing techniques from the literature. For instance, could the permutation-based method from [7] be used in conjunction with a classification-based approach? Alternatively, could the nearest neighbor bootstrap be combined with a kernel two-sample test?  A performance comparison against such alternatives would be valuable.

2. The analysis demonstrates the proximity of the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution to f_{CI} even with finite sample sizes. However, if the true relationship between variables x and y is weak dependence given z, how would the proposed method differentiate this from true conditional independence?

3. The method’s symmetry with respect to x and y is unclear. From a causal inference perspective, dependence between x and y given z can arise from x causing y, or y causing x.  These scenarios may necessitate different sampling strategies, a consideration absent in the current work.

4. I have significant concerns regarding the selection of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3.  The rationale and sensitivity of this parameter choice require further justification.

Minor:

In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculations appear to be missing division by the sample size.
