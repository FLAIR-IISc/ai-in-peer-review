

This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from a distribution close to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), which allows them to leverage powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks for CI testing. The main theoretical contributions include (1) proving that the generated samples are close to f_CI in total variational distance, and (2) establishing generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. Empirically, the authors demonstrate superior performance over state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper builds upon recent work in CI testing, particularly [10, 32, 28] which use kernel-based approaches. Unlike [10] that employs an expensive linear program for permutation-based sampling, the authors propose a simpler nearest-neighbor bootstrap with theoretical guarantees. The connection to [19] (which uses classification for independence testing) is appropriately noted, though the authors clarify the added complexity of conditional independence requiring Z-dependent permutation.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper presents technically sound work with rigorous theoretical analysis. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) provide meaningful guarantees about the bootstrap procedure and classification performance under non-i.i.d conditions. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic data with varying dimensions of Z and a real-world flow-cytometry dataset. The authors carefully address the challenges of high-dimensional CI testing, which is a significant practical problem.

*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The methodological contributions are presented logically, with clear pseudocode (Algorithms 1-3) and well-structured theoretical results. The relationship between the bootstrap procedure, classification, and CI testing is effectively explained. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results.

*Originality:* The conversion of CI testing to a classification problem represents a novel approach to this problem. While classification has been used for independence testing [19], extending it to conditional independence with a theoretically justified bootstrap procedure is a meaningful contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly [10] which uses permutation-based sampling without theoretical guarantees.

*Significance:* The problem of high-dimensional CI testing is important for applications in causal inference and Bayesian network learning. The authors demonstrate that their method scales better with dimension than existing approaches, which is particularly valuable for real-world applications where Z can be high-dimensional. The empirical results showing improved performance over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real data suggest practical significance.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* The theoretical analysis assumes smoothness conditions (Assumptions 1 and 2) that may not hold for all practical distributions. The authors should clarify the practical implications of these assumptions. The bias correction in Algorithm 3 is somewhat ad hoc, and the authors don't provide a principled way to select the threshold τ, which is critical for the method's performance.

*Clarity:* The connection between the theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) and the practical implementation (Algorithms 2 and 3) could be made more explicit. The relationship between the theoretical error bounds and the practical choice of τ needs further elaboration.

*Originality:* While the classification-based approach is novel, the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure bears some similarity to techniques used in density estimation [12, 13]. The paper should more thoroughly discuss how this differs from existing nearest-neighbor techniques for conditional independence.

*Significance:* The paper doesn't sufficiently address the computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap, which could be prohibitive for very large datasets. The authors should discuss the scalability of their approach beyond the experiments presented.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel model-powered approach to CI testing that converts it to classification
- Theoretical guarantees for bootstrap procedure and classification under near-independent samples
- Strong empirical results showing superior performance over state-of-the-art methods
- Clear, well-organized presentation of the method and results

*Con:*
- Some theoretical assumptions may be restrictive for practical applications
- Lack of principled method for selecting threshold τ
- Limited discussion of computational scalability for large datasets
- Insufficient differentiation from related nearest-neighbor techniques in density estimation

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the problem of conditional independence testing. The theoretical results are sound, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the approach is well-motivated. While there are some limitations in the theoretical assumptions and practical implementation details, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper represents a significant step forward in high-dimensional CI testing that could have substantial impact on causal inference and structure learning applications.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about theoretical assumptions, threshold selection, and computational complexity. The paper is technically strong, well-written, and makes an important contribution to the field.
