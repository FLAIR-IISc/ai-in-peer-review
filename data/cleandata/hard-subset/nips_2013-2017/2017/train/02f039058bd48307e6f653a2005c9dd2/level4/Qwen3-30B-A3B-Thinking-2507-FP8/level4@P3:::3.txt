

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables by reducing the problem to binary classification. The authors develop a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditional product distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), which is necessary for testing whether X ⊥ Y | Z. The generated samples are then used alongside original samples in a binary classification framework, where the classifier's accuracy determines whether to reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for their bootstrap procedure and generalization bounds for the classification problem under near-independent samples, and demonstrate superior performance over state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on conditional independence testing, which is crucial for applications in causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection. Previous work on non-parametric CI testing has primarily relied on kernel-based methods [10, 28, 32], while the authors extend the classifier-based approach from two-sample testing [19] to the more challenging conditional setting. This is an important extension, as the conditional case requires Z-dependent permutations that are computationally more challenging than the Z-independent permutations used in independence testing.

The main strengths of this paper are its theoretical contributions and empirical validation. The authors provide rigorous theoretical guarantees showing that their nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure generates samples close to f_CI in terms of total variational distance (Theorem 1), and establish generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). These results are particularly impressive as they extend Rademacher complexity analysis to non-i.i.d settings, which is of independent interest beyond the specific CI testing problem. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating significant performance gains over existing methods across multiple synthetic datasets with varying dimensions of Z, and on the flow cytometry protein signaling network data. The authors also provide a practical implementation on GitHub, enhancing reproducibility.

The paper is well-written and accessible to practitioners, though some sections could be clearer. The main contributions listed in Section 1.1 are somewhat redundant, as the core approach is repeatedly summarized. The presentation of Algorithm 2 could be more concise, as the process is already described in detail in the text. Additionally, the paper would benefit from addressing the sensitivity of the test to the choice of classifier and hyperparameters, which is a crucial consideration for practical applications. While the authors use XGBoost as the classifier, it would be valuable to see how different classifiers or hyperparameter settings affect the results.

The paper is technically sound, with appropriate theoretical analysis and thorough empirical validation. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations, noting that while their theoretical analysis suggests poor scaling with dimension of Z, their empirical results show better performance than expected. However, the paper could strengthen its connection to related work by addressing whether the method could be extended to discrete variables (the draft review raises this question), as the bootstrap procedure would likely be even more effective in that setting.

The significance of this work is high, as conditional independence testing is a fundamental problem in causal inference and machine learning. The proposed method offers a practical, scalable approach that leverages advances in classification to address a challenging statistical problem. By reducing CI testing to a classification problem, the authors create a framework where improvements in classification directly benefit CI testing, making the approach both flexible and forward-looking.

The paper meets NIPS criteria well. Quality: Strong theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs and comprehensive empirical validation. Clarity: Generally clear, though some sections could be more concise. Originality: Novel extension of classifier-based testing to the conditional setting with theoretical guarantees. Significance: Addresses an important problem with practical applications in causal inference.

The main weaknesses are the redundancy in Section 1.1, the unnecessary presentation of Algorithm 2, and the lack of discussion on classifier sensitivity. The paper would also benefit from a more thorough comparison with discrete-variable methods, though this is not strictly necessary for a paper focused on continuous variables.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of statistical testing. The authors present a novel, theoretically grounded approach to conditional independence testing that outperforms existing methods both theoretically and empirically. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and makes a valuable contribution to the literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the redundancy and classifier sensitivity points.
