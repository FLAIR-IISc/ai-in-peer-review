The authors propose a general test for conditional independence between three continuous variables. Their approach avoids strong parametric assumptions, instead leveraging samples from the joint distribution to generate samples approximating the conditional product distribution in total variation distance. These generated samples are then fed into a binary classifier – such as a deep neural network – and, after training on samples from each of two relevant distributions, the classifier’s predictive error is used to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors compare their method to existing techniques, providing both descriptive explanations and experimental results on synthetic and real datasets.

Conditional independence testing is a crucial tool in modern machine learning, particularly within causal inference, where assessing claims of unconfoundedness is frequently necessary. The proposed method appears both conceptually straightforward and powerful enough for a wide range of causal applications.

The paper is also well-written overall. While the details of the theoretical results and proofs can be challenging to follow, the authors effectively summarize their findings and clearly explain the methodology to practitioners. 

I found the theoretical results to be particularly impressive. The method is a natural extension of the approach for independence testing presented in “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as the authors acknowledge; they modify the test to incorporate a third variable, Z, and the methods become similar after matching neighbors of Z. However, the theoretical guarantees achieved are noteworthy.

Several minor points could be addressed:

*   Does a discrete analog of this test exist? The bootstrap method often becomes more powerful when Z is discrete.
*   How sensitive is the test to the choice of classification method? It would be beneficial to understand if variations in hyperparameters could lead to differing acceptance or rejection outcomes. Including a sensitivity analysis alongside the simulations would be valuable.
*   Section 1.1, outlining the main contributions, feels redundant. The authors reiterate their approach multiple times, and the length of this section seems excessive.
*   Presenting Algorithm 2 in full seems unnecessary. The process is already described succinctly elsewhere in the paper.
*   There are a few minor typos: “classifier” should be used in line 157, and “graphs” in line 280.
