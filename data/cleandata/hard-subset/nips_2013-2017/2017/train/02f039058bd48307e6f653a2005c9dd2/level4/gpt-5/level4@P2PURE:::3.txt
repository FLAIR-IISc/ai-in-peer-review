The authors propose a general-purpose test for conditional independence among three continuous variables. Rather than imposing strong parametric assumptions, they construct, from joint samples, surrogate samples that are close in total variation distance to the conditional product distribution. They then train a binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network) to distinguish between samples from the joint and from this proxy; using the classifier’s predictive error, they accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. They compare their method to prior approaches, both through methodological descriptions and experiments on synthetic and real data.

Conditional independence testing is important in modern machine learning, especially in causal inference, where we often evaluate unconfoundedness. The proposed method appears straightforward and sufficiently powerful for many causal applications.

The paper is generally well written. While some theoretical details and proofs are dense, the authors effectively summarize the results and make the methodology clear to practitioners.

The theoretical results are the most impressive aspect. The test is a fairly direct extension of the approach in “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests” (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as the authors acknowledge: they incorporate a third variable Z, and after matching neighbors in Z, the procedures are similar. Nevertheless, the resulting theoretical guarantees are strong.

A few more comments:
- Is there a discrete analogue? Would the bootstrap become even more powerful when Z is discrete?
- How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters? Could modest hyperparameter changes alter accept/reject outcomes? It would be useful to report this sensitivity alongside the simulations.
- Section 1.1 (Main Contributions) feels redundant; the approach is summarized multiple times, more than necessary.
- Listing Algorithm 2 seems unnecessary; the procedure is already described and could be conveyed more succinctly.
- Minor typos: “classifier” in line 157; “graphs” in line 280.
