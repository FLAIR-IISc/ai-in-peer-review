The authors propose a general test for conditional independence between three continuous variables, avoiding strong parametric assumptions. Instead, they develop a method that uses samples from the joint distribution to create samples close to the conditional product distribution in total variation distance. These samples are then fed into a binary classifier, such as a deep neural net, which is trained on samples from each of the two distributions. The predictive error of the net is used to either accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors compare their proposed method to existing methods in the literature, both through descriptions and experimental results on synthetic and real data.

Conditional independence testing is a crucial tool in modern machine learning, particularly in the domain of causal inference, where claims of unconfoundedness are frequently of interest. The proposed method appears straightforward and powerful enough to be applied to various causal applications.

The paper is well-written, and while the details of the theoretical results and proofs can be challenging to follow, the authors do an excellent job of summarizing the results and making the methodology clear to practitioners.

The most impressive aspect of the paper is the theoretical results. Although the proposed method is an extension of the approach for testing independence presented in "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as acknowledged by the authors, the guarantees provided by the theoretical results are impressive. The authors modify the test to include a third variable Z and, after matching neighbors of Z, the methods become similar.

A few additional comments are worth noting:

* Is there a discrete analog of the proposed method? The bootstrap becomes even more powerful when Z is discrete, correct?
* How sensitive is the test to the classification method used? It would be useful to investigate whether a few changed hyperparameters can lead to different acceptance and rejection results, alongside the simulations.
* Section 1.1 of Main Contributions appears redundant, as the authors summarize their approach multiple times, making the extent seem unnecessary.
* Writing out Algorithm 2 seems unnecessary, as this process can be described more succinctly and has already been described in the paper.
* There are a few minor typos: "classifier" should be used in line 157, and "graphs" in line 280.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of conditional independence testing, and with some minor revisions, it has the potential to be even more impactful.
