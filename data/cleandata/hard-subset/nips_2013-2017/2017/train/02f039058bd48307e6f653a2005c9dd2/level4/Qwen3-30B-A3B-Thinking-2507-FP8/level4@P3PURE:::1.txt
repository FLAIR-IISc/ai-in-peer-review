

This paper proposes a model-based approach for conditional independence testing on i.i.d. data. The core idea employs nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating the distribution \(f^{CI}\), followed by training a classifier to distinguish between the observed data distribution and the bootstrap distribution. If the classifier's performance is near random chance, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is rejected. The authors establish bounds on the total variation distance between the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution and \(f^{CI}\), as well as bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings for nearly independent samples.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is well-motivated and clearly presented. However, several critical concerns require clarification.

**Major Concerns:**
1.  **Decoupling and Alternative Methods:** The method appears decomposable into two key components: (i) generating samples mimicking \(f^{CI}\), and (ii) using a classifier for distinction. It is unclear whether either component could be replaced by established methods. For instance, could the permutation-based approach from [7] be combined with the classifier step? Or could nearest neighbor bootstrap be paired with a kernel two-sample test? The authors should compare performance against such alternatives.
2.  **Handling Weak Dependence:** The nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution converges to \(f^{CI}\) in finite samples. How does the method behave when the true dependence between \(X\) and \(Y\) given \(Z\) is weak (i.e., the null hypothesis is nearly true)? The paper does not address this scenario.
3.  **Symmetry and Causal Direction:** The method is not symmetric in \(X\) and \(Y\). In causal contexts, dependence given \(Z\) may arise from \(X \to Y\) or \(Y \to X\). The approach requires sampling from one variable (e.g., \(X\) or \(Y\)), but the authors do not specify how to choose the sampling direction in these distinct causal scenarios. This omission is problematic for causal inference applications.
4.  **Parameter Selection (\(\tau\)):** The choice of \(\tau\) in Algorithms 2 and 3 is critical but not justified. The authors provide no guidance on selecting \(\tau\) in practice, and its impact on performance is unclear. This raises a serious concern about the method's practical applicability.

**Minor Concerns:**
*   In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size \(n\) to ensure consistency with standard risk definitions.
