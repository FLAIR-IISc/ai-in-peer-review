This paper proposes a novel, model-powered approach for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing. The core idea is to reframe the statistical test as a binary classification problem. To achieve this, the authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples that approximate the conditional product distribution $f_{CI}(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)$, which represents the joint distribution under the null hypothesis ($H_0: X \perp Y | Z$). The original data samples are labeled as one class, and the bootstrapped samples as the other. A powerful classifier, such as a gradient-boosted tree, is then trained to distinguish between these two sets of samples. The test's outcome is determined by the classifier's performance: if it can distinguish the two distributions with high accuracy, the null hypothesis is rejected; if its performance is close to random guessing, the null is not rejected.

This work relates to a growing literature on leveraging machine learning for statistical testing, such as using classifiers for two-sample tests, but extends it to the more complex setting of conditional independence. Unlike prior kernel-based methods (e.g., KCIT, RCIT) or permutation-based tests, this approach can directly harness the power of modern, high-dimensional supervised learning models. The paper supports its methodology with theoretical contributions, including a proof that the bootstrapped samples are close to the target distribution $f_{CI}$ in total variation distance, and a novel generalization risk analysis for classifiers trained on the resulting non-i.i.d. data.

**Strengths:**
*   The conceptual reduction of CI testing to a binary classification problem is elegant and significant. This modular approach allows the method to directly benefit from ongoing advances in supervised learning, potentially offering a powerful and flexible tool for a fundamental statistical problem.
*   The proposed nearest-neighbor bootstrap is an intuitive and computationally practical solution to the challenging problem of sampling from the conditional product distribution $f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)$ when only given samples from the joint $f(x,y,z)$.
*   The theoretical analysis providing guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1) and the generalization bounds for the classifier under near-independence (Theorem 2) adds significant technical rigor to the proposed framework. The analysis of Rademacher complexity for non-i.i.d samples may be of independent interest.
*   The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates that the proposed method (CCIT) can substantially outperform state-of-the-art CI tests, especially in scenarios where the conditioning variable Z is high-dimensional.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**
1.  The method can be seen as having two key components: (i) the nearest-neighbor bootstrap for data generation and (ii) a classifier for the two-sample test. It would strengthen the paper to analyze the contribution of each component in isolation. For instance, what is the performance if the proposed bootstrap is paired with a different two-sample test, such as a kernel-based one? Conversely, how does a classifier-based test perform when using samples from an alternative procedure, like the permutation approach in Doran et al. (2014)? This would help clarify whether the performance gains stem primarily from the novel sampling scheme, the classifier, or the combination.

2.  The paper establishes that the distribution of bootstrapped samples is close to, but not identical to, $f_{CI}$ in the finite sample regime. A potential concern is the test's power in settings of weak conditional dependence, where the true distribution $f(x,y,z)$ is already very close to $f_{CI}$. In such cases, would the classifier be able to distinguish the subtle true dependence from the inherent approximation error of the bootstrap procedure?

3.  The bootstrap algorithm appears to be asymmetric with respect to X and Y, as it operates by swapping the Y-coordinates based on nearest neighbors in Z. From a causal discovery perspective, the dependence structure could be symmetric or directed (e.g., $X \to Y$ or $Y \to X$). Is the test's performance invariant to the choice of which variable's coordinates are swapped? A discussion on the potential impact of this choice would be valuable.

4.  The selection of the threshold parameter $\tau$ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is critical for practical application, as it governs the trade-off between Type I and Type II errors. The paper would be more complete with a more detailed justification for its choice in the experiments and guidance on how to set this hyperparameter in general practice.

**Minor Point:**
*   In the pseudocode for Algorithms 2 and 3, the definition of the empirical risk $\hat{L}(g, D_r)$ seems to be missing the averaging term (i.e., division by $|D_r|$).
