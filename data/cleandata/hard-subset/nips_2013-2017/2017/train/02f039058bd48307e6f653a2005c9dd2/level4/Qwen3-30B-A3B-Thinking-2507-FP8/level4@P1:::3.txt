

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables by converting the problem into a binary classification task. The authors develop a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples that approximate the conditional product distribution f_CI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), which characterizes conditional independence. These samples, combined with the original data, form a labeled dataset for training classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees or deep neural networks) to distinguish between the true joint distribution and the conditional product distribution. The paper provides theoretical guarantees showing that the bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance, and establishes generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d near-independent samples. Empirical results demonstrate significant performance gains over state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning, particularly for causal inference where conditional independence relations underpin many causal discovery methods. The proposed approach is both theoretically grounded and practically useful, as it leverages advances in classification to solve a challenging statistical problem. The authors make a clear connection to prior work on independence testing using classifiers (e.g., "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests"), noting how their method extends this approach to handle the conditional case.

The theoretical contributions are particularly strong. The paper provides rigorous guarantees showing that the bootstrap procedure generates samples close to f_CI in total variation distance, and establishes novel generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. These results are significant because they address the non-i.i.d nature of the generated samples, which is a critical technical challenge in this approach.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors demonstrate superior performance across various dimensions of the conditioning variable Z, with their method maintaining high accuracy even as dimensionality increases, whereas existing methods degrade rapidly. The application to protein signaling networks from flow cytometry data further validates the practical utility of the approach.

The paper is well-written and accessible to practitioners, though some sections contain unnecessary redundancy (particularly Section 1.1, which repeats the main methodological contribution multiple times). The description of Algorithm 2 could be more concise, as the procedure is already well-explained in the preceding text.

I have a few minor concerns:
1. The paper does not consider a discrete analog of their method, which would be valuable to explore. When Z is discrete, the nearest-neighbor bootstrap might be even more straightforward, potentially offering better performance or theoretical guarantees.
2. The sensitivity of the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters is not thoroughly explored. It would be useful to see how robust the results are to different classifier choices and parameter settings.
3. There are a few minor typographical errors (e.g., "classifier" should be used instead of "classifer" in line 157, and "graphs" should be used instead of "graph" in line 280).

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The method is novel, well-justified theoretically, and empirically validated. The authors have clearly demonstrated that their approach outperforms existing methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings where previous approaches struggle.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental statistical problem with a novel, technically sound approach that has clear practical applications in causal inference and machine learning. The theoretical results are rigorous, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the writing is clear (with the minor exceptions noted above).

Pro: Strong theoretical contributions with novel guarantees for non-i.i.d classification; Empirical results demonstrate significant performance gains over state-of-the-art methods, especially in high dimensions; Clear connection to prior work on classification-based independence testing; Practical utility in causal inference applications.

Con: Some redundancy in the main contributions section; Limited exploration of classifier sensitivity; No discussion of discrete analogs for the method.

I recommend acceptance.
