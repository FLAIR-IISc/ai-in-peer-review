Review- This paper introduces a model-based approach for conducting conditional independence tests for i.i.d. data. The core idea is to use a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that approximately follow the distribution f^{CI}, and then train and evaluate a classifier to determine whether it can distinguish between the observed data distribution and the nearest-neighbor–bootstrapped distribution. If the classifier’s performance is close to random guessing, one fails to reject the null hypothesis that the data are conditionally independent; otherwise, one rejects the null hypothesis.

The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest-neighbor bootstrapped distribution to f^{CI} in terms of total variation distance, along with empirical risk bounds under idealized classification settings and for nearly independent samples.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is clearly presented.

Detail comments:
Major:
1. The method seems to decouple into two main components: generating samples that mimic f_{CI}, and using a classifier for the decision. Can either step be replaced by existing methods? For example, one could use the permutation-based approach in [7] together with the classification-based decision rule, or combine the nearest-neighbor bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test. I am curious about the comparative performance of such hybrids.

2. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap distribution only approximates f_{CI} in finite samples. If the ground truth is that X and Y are weakly dependent given Z, how will the proposed method behave in this regime?

3. Is the method fully symmetric with respect to X and Y? From a causal perspective, dependence given Z can arise from either X causing Y or Y causing X. In these two scenarios, do we need to decide which variable to resample? The paper does not discuss this.

4. I am seriously concerned about how to choose the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3.

Minor:
In Algorithms 2 and 3, it appears that the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size.
