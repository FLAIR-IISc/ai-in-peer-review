This paper introduces a new conditional independence test with a focus on the theoretical underpinnings required for such tests to function reliably. A key strength lies in the authors’ recognition that existing conditional independence tests implicitly rely on assumptions about the smoothness of the conditional density. They explicitly propose such an assumption—a bound on the total variation distance between the null hypothesis density and the density estimated via bootstrapping—which represents a novel contribution.

Furthermore, the paper leverages this smoothness assumption to provide a bound on the error of the optimal classifier trained on the training data (specifically, claim 1.1(iii)). This connection between smoothness, conditional independence testing, and generalization error is noteworthy. 

The most significant contribution is arguably Theorem 1. This theorem elegantly combines the smoothness assumption, an assumption regarding the achievable error of an estimator (hat R, with error eta), and a condition on the difference in errors between the optimal and trained classifiers. The result is insightful and non-trivial, establishing a crucial link between theoretical guarantees and practical performance. 

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The writing is dense and lacks sufficient intuition, making it difficult to fully grasp the implications of the results. I found myself questioning my understanding of the core arguments, even after multiple readings. For example, consider the inequality presented in section 1.1(iii). If we assume the alternative hypothesis holds and *r<sub>0</sub>* = 0.1, and *G* represents a limited class of functions – such as linear functions – that are demonstrably unable to distinguish between *f* and *f<sup>CI</sup>*, it is unclear how a small error probability can be achieved. Specifically, how can  *hat R* < 0.1 + *o<sub>1</sub>* < 0.2 for large *n* if the function class is fundamentally incapable of representing the necessary complexity to detect the dependence? A more intuitive explanation of this scenario would greatly benefit the reader.

In summary, while the theoretical results presented are promising and novel, the lack of clarity and interpretability significantly hinder a complete assessment of the work. Addressing these issues would substantially improve the paper's impact and accessibility.
