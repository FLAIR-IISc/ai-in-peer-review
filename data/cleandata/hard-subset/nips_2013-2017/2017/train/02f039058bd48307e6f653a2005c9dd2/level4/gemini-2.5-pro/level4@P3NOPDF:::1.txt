This paper introduces a novel, model-powered approach for conditional independence testing on i.i.d. data. The core idea is to reframe the problem as a two-sample test. First, a synthetic dataset is generated using nearest neighbor bootstrap, with the goal of creating samples that approximate the distribution under the null hypothesis of conditional independence ($P(X, Y | Z) = P(X | Z)P(Y | Z)$). Second, a classifier is trained to distinguish between the original observed samples and the bootstrapped samples. If the classifier's accuracy is close to random chance, the null hypothesis is not rejected. Conversely, if the classifier can successfully distinguish the two distributions, the null hypothesis is rejected. The authors support their method with theoretical analysis, providing bounds on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and the true conditional independence distribution, as well as bounds on the empirical risks under specific settings.

Overall, the paper addresses a fundamental and important problem. The proposed method is innovative, and the presentation is clear. However, I have several major concerns regarding the method's justification, sensitivity, and practical implementation.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Modularity and Comparison to Alternatives:** The proposed method can be viewed as a two-part framework: (1) a sampling strategy (NN bootstrap) to generate data under the null hypothesis, and (2) a testing mechanism (a classifier-based two-sample test). This modularity raises important questions about the contribution and novelty of each component. It would significantly strengthen the paper to discuss how this framework relates to combinations of other existing methods. For example:
    *   What would be the performance if one used a different sampling method, such as the permutation-based approach from [7], followed by the proposed classification-based test?
    *   Conversely, what would be the performance if the nearest neighbor bootstrap samples were evaluated using a different two-sample test, such as a kernel-based method?
    An ablation study or discussion comparing these hybrid approaches would help isolate the specific benefits of the NN bootstrap and the classifier test, clarifying the paper's core contribution.

2.  **Power Against Weak Dependencies:** A key challenge for any conditional independence test is its power to detect weak dependencies. The method's validity rests on the assumption that the NN bootstrap distribution is a good approximation of the true conditional independence distribution, $f^{CI}$. However, in the finite sample regime, if the true dependency between X and Y given Z is weak, the NN bootstrap distribution might also be very close to the *true* data-generating distribution. In such cases, the classifier would naturally struggle to distinguish the two, leading to a failure to reject the null (a false negative). The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion and empirical evaluation of the method's statistical power under scenarios of varying degrees of weak conditional dependence.

3.  **Symmetry of the Test:** The procedure described appears to be asymmetric with respect to X and Y, as it involves resampling one variable (e.g., Y) based on its neighbors in the Z-space. It is not clear whether swapping the roles of X and Y would produce the same test statistic or conclusion. This is particularly relevant from a causal perspective, where a conditional dependence might arise from different underlying structures (e.g., $X \rightarrow Y$ or $Y \rightarrow X$). The authors should clarify if the method is symmetric and, if not, discuss the implications of this asymmetry. Does the choice of which variable to resample affect the test's validity or power?

4.  **Practicality and Hyperparameter Selection:** I have a significant concern regarding the practical application of the method, specifically the choice of the decision threshold $\tau$ in Algorithms 2 and 3. This parameter is crucial for making the final determination of independence, yet the paper offers no guidance on how to set it. Is the method's outcome highly sensitive to the value of $\tau$? Is there a principled, data-driven way to choose it, such as through cross-validation? Without a clear methodology for setting this hyperparameter, the reproducibility and reliability of the test are in question.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In the pseudocode for Algorithms 2 and 3, the calculation of the empirical risk appears to be missing a normalization factor (i.e., division by the sample size). Please clarify if this is a typo or an intentional formulation.
