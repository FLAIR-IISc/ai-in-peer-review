This paper introduces a general, non-parametric test for conditional independence between three continuous variables. The approach avoids strong parametric assumptions; instead, it uses samples from the joint distribution to generate a second set of samples that approximate the conditional product distribution. These two sets of samples—one from the original joint distribution and one from the generated approximation—are used to train a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network. The classifier's ability to distinguish between the two distributions, as measured by its prediction error, serves as the test statistic for accepting or rejecting the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors provide a thorough comparison to existing methods, including both theoretical discussion and empirical evaluations on synthetic and real datasets.

Conditional independence testing is a fundamental tool in modern machine learning, particularly for causal inference where it is essential for verifying assumptions like unconfoundedness. The proposed method is straightforward yet powerful, making it a promising tool for a wide range of causal applications.

The paper is well-written. While the theoretical derivations and proofs can be dense, the authors effectively summarize the key results and present the methodology in a manner that is clear and accessible to practitioners.

The primary strength of this work lies in its theoretical results. The proposed method is, as the authors acknowledge, a relatively straightforward extension of the classifier-based two-sample test from "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests," adapted here for the conditional setting by incorporating a neighbor-matching step on the conditioning variable Z. Despite this methodological similarity, the accompanying theoretical guarantees are impressive.

A few additional comments:
*   Could the method be adapted for discrete variables? It seems the bootstrap procedure would be particularly effective when the conditioning variable Z is discrete.
*   How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters? An analysis of whether different hyperparameter settings could lead to different conclusions would be a valuable addition to the simulation studies.
*   Section 1.1, "Main Contributions," is largely redundant, as the approach is summarized multiple times throughout the paper.
*   The inclusion of Algorithm 2 seems unnecessary, as the procedure it outlines is already well-described in the text and could be stated more succinctly.
*   Minor typos were noted: "classifier" should be used in line 157 and "graphs" in line 280.
