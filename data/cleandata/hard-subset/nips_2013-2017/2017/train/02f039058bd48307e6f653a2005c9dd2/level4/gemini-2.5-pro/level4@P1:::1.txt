This paper proposes a model-powered approach to non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing for i.i.d. data. The central idea is to reframe the CI test as a binary classification problem. The authors introduce a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from a distribution that approximates the joint distribution under the conditional independence hypothesis, f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). A classifier is then trained to distinguish between the original data samples (labeled '1') and these bootstrapped samples (labeled '0'). If the classifier performs near random chance, the null hypothesis (X ⊥ Y | Z) is not rejected. Conversely, if the classifier achieves high accuracy, it implies the original and bootstrapped distributions are distinguishable, leading to the rejection of the null hypothesis.

The authors provide significant theoretical support for this framework. They establish bounds on the total variation distance between the distribution of the bootstrapped samples and the true f_CI. Furthermore, they develop generalization bounds for the classification risk, adapting standard learning theory results to their setting of "near-independent" samples, which is a contribution of independent interest. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated empirically, where it outperforms prior state-of-the-art methods, especially in high-dimensional settings for the conditioning variable Z.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Powerful Framework:** The reduction of CI testing to a binary classification problem is an elegant and powerful idea. It allows the leveraging of sophisticated, high-performance supervised learning models (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, deep neural networks) for a fundamental statistical testing problem. This is particularly significant as these models are well-suited to handle high-dimensional data, a known challenge for many existing CI tests.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Support:** The paper is well-grounded in theory. The guarantee that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap generates samples provably close to the target distribution f_CI (Theorem 1) is a crucial justification for the entire procedure. The novel analysis of generalization bounds for classification with near-i.i.d samples (Theorem 2) further strengthens the paper's theoretical contribution.
3.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The experiments convincingly show that the proposed method (CCIT) outperforms established kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT. The performance gains are especially pronounced as the dimension of the conditioning variable Z increases, highlighting the practical utility of the approach in complex, high-dimensional scenarios.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The paper presents a very promising method, but several points could be clarified or explored further to strengthen the contribution.

1.  **Analysis of Method Components:** The proposed method can be seen as having two key components: (1) the nearest-neighbor bootstrap for generating samples from an approximate f_CI, and (2) the use of a classifier for the two-sample test. It would be valuable to understand the contribution of each component in isolation. For instance:
    *   How would the method perform if the sample generation step used an existing technique (e.g., the permutation-based approach from Doran et al., 2014) followed by the classification-based test?
    *   Conversely, how would a standard kernel-based two-sample test perform when applied to the samples generated by your novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap?
    This analysis would help disentangle the benefits of the new bootstrap procedure from those of using a classifier as the test statistic.

2.  **Sensitivity to Weak Dependence:** The test's decision relies on a classifier's ability to distinguish the true data distribution *f* from the bootstrapped approximation of *f_CI*. A critical question is how the proposed test performs when the conditional dependence is weak (i.e., when the total variation distance between *f* and *f_CI* is small but non-zero). How sensitive is the classifier to such subtle differences, and what is the statistical power of the test in these scenarios compared to other methods?

3.  **Asymmetry in the Bootstrap Procedure:** The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure described in Algorithm 1 appears to be asymmetric with respect to X and Y. Specifically, it resamples the Y-coordinates based on nearest neighbors in the Z-space. While conditional independence itself is a symmetric property, the procedure is not. Would swapping the roles of X and Y in the bootstrap algorithm affect the outcome of the test? Please clarify if this asymmetry has any theoretical or practical implications, particularly in contexts like causal discovery where the distinction between variables is meaningful.

4.  **Practical Guidance on Threshold Selection:** The choice of the threshold τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is critical for the test's practical application. The paper mentions setting τ = 1/√n in the experiments, justifying it as an upper bound on the test statistic's variance under the null hypothesis. However, the performance and reliability of any hypothesis test depend heavily on a principled way to set this threshold to control the Type I error rate. Could the authors provide a more detailed discussion or a more adaptive procedure for selecting τ, perhaps based on a permutation approach applied to the final test statistic?

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel and powerful approach to conditional independence testing. The combination of a clever reduction to classification, a new sampling procedure, and solid theoretical and empirical backing makes it a strong contribution to the field. The main areas for improvement lie in providing a deeper analysis of the method's components and practical aspects like parameter selection.
