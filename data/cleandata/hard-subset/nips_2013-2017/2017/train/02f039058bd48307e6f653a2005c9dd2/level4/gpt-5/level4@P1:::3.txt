Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables X, Y, Z. The authors reduce CI testing to binary classification by constructing a synthetic dataset that approximates the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z) f(y|z) f(z). Specifically, they introduce a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap: split the data, match each point in U1 to its nearest neighbor in U2 based on Z, swap Y coordinates, and form samples that mimic draws from fCI. They then train a classifier to distinguish original samples (from fX,Y,Z) from bootstrapped samples (approximately from fCI). If the classifier’s test error is near 0.5, they fail to reject CI; otherwise, they reject. A bias-corrected variant trains a classifier on (Y, Z) alone to subtract finite-sample bias.

The main theoretical contributions are: (i) proving that a single bootstrapped sample is close in total variation to fCI under smoothness assumptions, with an explicit rate that degrades with the dimension of Z; and (ii) deriving generalization bounds for classification when training data are only near-independent (because the NN bootstrap induces dependencies), with rates expressed in terms of VC dimension and small deviations from i.i.d. The empirical results compare favorably to kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) and a permutation-based approach (KCIPT), on both synthetic data and the flow cytometry dataset.

This builds on prior reductions of statistical testing to classification, notably the use of classifiers for two-sample testing and independence testing (e.g., Lopez-Paz and Oquab, “Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests”), and on RKHS-based CI testing (KCIT/RCIT) and permutation-based CI tests (KCIPT). The main novelty here is the NN bootstrap to emulate fCI without solving an expensive assignment or linear program, together with theoretically justified TV bounds and learning guarantees under near-independence.

Strengths:
- Clear, modular reduction that leverages modern classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, deep nets) and scales well in practice.
- Strong theoretical results: TV-distance control for the NN bootstrap under smoothness conditions and generalization bounds for ERM with near-i.i.d. samples. The analysis of Rademacher/VC-style guarantees under near-independence is particularly impressive and of independent interest.
- Solid empirical validation on synthetic and real data, with performance gains over KCIT and RCIT, and practical improvements over KCIPT by avoiding an LP permutation step.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Methodological novelty: the reduction is a fairly direct extension of classifier-based two-sample/independence tests to the CI setting by conditioning on Z and using nearest neighbors; the main conceptual advance lies in the bootstrap construction and its analysis.
- Sensitivity to classifier choice/hyperparameters: How robust are the conclusions to the choice of model class and training settings? Small changes in hyperparameters could alter accept/reject decisions. An ablation showing sensitivity across classifiers and hyperparameters would strengthen the empirical section.
- Discrete Z: Is there a discrete analogue? If Z is discrete (or mixed), nearest-neighbor matching becomes simpler and the bootstrap may be exact or near-exact. Clarifying this case would be useful.
- Redundancy: Section 1.1 repeats the approach already explained in the introduction; it could be compressed.
- Algorithm presentation: Writing out Algorithm 2 in full seems unnecessary given prior descriptions; a concise summary would suffice.
- Minor typos: “classifier” (line 157), “graphs” (line 280).

Assessment by criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound. The TV-distance bound for the NN bootstrap and the generalization bounds under near-independence are carefully argued, with assumptions stated (smoothness of f(y|z) and f(z), Lipschitzness). The rate O(n^{-1/dz}) highlights the curse of dimensionality in Z; the authors note that empirical performance is better than the worst case.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and accessible to practitioners despite technical proofs. Some repetition could be trimmed (e.g., Section 1.1, full pseudocode for Algorithm 2).
- Originality: The reduction to classification is known for two-sample and independence testing; here it is adapted to CI via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap with provable TV guarantees and non-i.i.d. generalization analysis. The theoretical treatment under near-independence is novel and valuable.
- Significance: CI testing is central to causal discovery, graphical models, and feature selection. The proposed approach is practical, flexible (plug-in classifiers), and shows strong empirical performance. The theoretical contributions on near-i.i.d. learning may influence related reductions.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include sensitivity analyses across classifiers (e.g., XGBoost vs. neural nets) and key hyperparameters (tree depth, learning rate, network architecture), reporting variability in Type I/II errors.
- Discuss guidance for selecting threshold τ and the number of bootstrap repetitions B in finite samples, possibly with calibration via permutation under H0.
- Comment on computational complexity and scalability of the NN bootstrap (with fast NN search) relative to KCIPT’s LP and kernel methods.
- Elaborate briefly on the discrete or mixed Z case and whether the bootstrap becomes exact by matching on equal Z.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical guarantees in a practically useful CI testing framework.
- Empirical improvements over established baselines on synthetic and real data.
- Modular method that leverages advances in classification.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Methodological novelty is incremental relative to classifier-based testing; the key contribution is theoretical.
- Limited analysis of robustness to classifier choice and hyperparameters.
