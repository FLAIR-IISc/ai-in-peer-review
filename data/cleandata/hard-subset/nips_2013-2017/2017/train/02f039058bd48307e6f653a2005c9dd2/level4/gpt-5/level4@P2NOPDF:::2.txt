The paper proposes a new conditional independence test.

Strengths
1) The paper correctly notes that, for the test to work, a smoothness assumption on the conditional density is required. The authors formalize such an assumption and, under it, bound the total variation distance between the distribution under the null hypothesis and the bootstrap distribution. This appears novel.
2) The paper leverages this assumption to bound the training error of the optimal classifier (Section 1.1(iii)).
3) Theorem 1—combining the smoothness assumption, the assumption that ĥR can be achieved within error η, and an assumption on the gap between optimal and trained classifier errors—is novel, insightful, and nontrivial.

Weaknesses
The paper is difficult to read and offers limited interpretation of the results; at times it is unclear whether my understanding is correct. For example, consider the inequality in Section 1.1(iii). Under the alternative with r0 = 0.1, suppose G is a small hypothesis class (e.g., linear functions) that lacks the expressive power to distinguish f from f^CI. Intuitively, how can the small-error probability event be achieved—i.e., why should we expect ĥR < 0.1 + o(1) < 0.2 for large n? Clarifying the mechanism by which the assumptions (smoothness, bootstrap approximation quality, and classifier capacity) jointly ensure this behavior would be helpful. In particular, it would be useful to explain whether the bound relies on properties of the Bayes classifier in the full function class versus the restricted class G, and how misspecification of G affects the inequality.
