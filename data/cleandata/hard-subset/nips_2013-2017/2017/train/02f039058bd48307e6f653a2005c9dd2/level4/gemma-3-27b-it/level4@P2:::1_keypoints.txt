- Proposes framing conditional independence (CI) testing as a classification problem using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate the CI distribution.  
- Introduces a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees on bootstrap sample quality (total variation bounds) and generalization bounds for the classifier under near-independence.  
- Demonstrates empirical gains on synthetic and real-world data, especially in high-dimensional settings.  
- Paper is generally well-written and situates itself clearly within related work (kernel CI tests, classifier two-sample tests).  
- Strength: novel, model-powered approach that leverages modern classifiers for CI testing.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis supporting the bootstrap and classification steps.  
- Strength: convincing empirical validation showing promising performance improvements.  
- Weakness: two-stage modular design raises questions about replacing components with existing methods; lacking comparative analysis with alternatives (e.g., permutation + classification, bootstrap + kernel tests).  
- Weakness: method may struggle when X–Y dependence given Z is weak; limited exploration of this sensitivity and remedies.  
- Weakness: symmetry between X and Y may obscure causal directionality; no discussion on distinguishing causal directions.  
- Weakness: critical threshold parameter τ in Algorithms 2/3 lacks principled justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- Minor technical issue: empirical risk in Algorithms 2/3 appears to miss normalization by sample size (division by n).  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded approach with strong empirical results and broad applicability.  
- Argument against acceptance: insufficient exploration of modular alternatives, weak-dependence behavior, causal directionality, and τ sensitivity.  
- Overall assessment: solid and promising contribution but current concerns warrant a weak reject; recommend revisions to address comparisons, weak dependencies, causal direction, and τ selection.
