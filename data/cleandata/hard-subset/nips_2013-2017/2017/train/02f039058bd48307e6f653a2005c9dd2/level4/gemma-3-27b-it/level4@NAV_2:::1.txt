This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous random variables. The core idea is to reformulate CI testing as a binary classification problem, leveraging the power of modern classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. To address the challenge of obtaining samples from the conditional independence distribution fCI(x, y, z), the authors introduce a novel nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure and provide theoretical guarantees on its accuracy, measured by total variational distance. They further develop theoretical results regarding generalization bounds for classification in this non-i.i.d setting, translating these into error bounds for CI testing. The method is empirically validated on both simulated and real datasets, demonstrating performance gains over existing methods.

The paper tackles an important problem in statistical inference and machine learning with broad applications in areas like causal discovery and feature selection. The proposed approach is well-motivated, clearly presented, and technically sound. The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem is a compelling strategy, and the nearest neighbor bootstrap provides a practical way to estimate the fCI distribution. The theoretical analysis, including the bounds on the variational distance and the generalization bounds, is rigorous and contributes to a deeper understanding of the method's properties. The empirical results provide convincing evidence of the approach's effectiveness. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of related work, properly referencing relevant publications and positioning their contribution within the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem is a novel and promising approach.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides strong theoretical foundations for the proposed method, including bounds on the accuracy of the bootstrap procedure and generalization bounds for the classifier.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the methodology and theoretical results.
*   **Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to a wide range of applications in statistics and machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the threshold parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not fully justified and could significantly impact performance. More guidance on how to set this parameter in practice would be valuable.
*   **Symmetry and Causal Direction:** The method, as presented, does not explicitly address the asymmetry between X and Y in terms of causal direction. It’s unclear how the method would perform when X causes Y versus Y causes X, and whether the algorithm needs to be adapted to account for this asymmetry.
*   **Weak Dependence Scenario:** The performance of the method when X and Y are only weakly dependent given Z is not thoroughly discussed. It is important to understand how the method behaves in such cases, as this is a common scenario in practice.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** While the paper compares to KCIT and RCIT, exploring the performance of combining the proposed nearest-neighbor bootstrap with existing kernel-based two-sample tests or using permutation-based methods (as suggested in the paper itself with reference to [7]) could offer a more complete comparison.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to CI testing.
*   The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees on the method's performance.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   The paper addresses an important problem with broad applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The sensitivity to the parameter τ and the lack of clear guidance on its selection are concerns.
*   The method does not explicitly address the issue of causal direction.
*   The performance in the weakly dependent scenario needs further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding parameter selection, causal direction, and the weakly dependent scenario. Specifically, the authors should provide more guidance on choosing the threshold parameter τ, discuss the implications of causal direction, and potentially include experiments or analysis on the performance of the method in the weakly dependent case.
