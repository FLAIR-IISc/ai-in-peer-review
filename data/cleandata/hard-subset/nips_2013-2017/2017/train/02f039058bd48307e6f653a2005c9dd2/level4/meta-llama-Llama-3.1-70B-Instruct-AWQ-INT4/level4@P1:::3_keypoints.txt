- Proposes a general, nonparametric test for conditional independence among three continuous variables.  
- Constructs samples approximating the conditional product distribution in total variation distance from observed joint samples.  
- Uses a binary classifier (e.g., deep neural net) trained to distinguish joint vs. conditional-product samples, and uses its error to accept/reject conditional independence.  
- Empirically compares the proposed method to existing approaches on synthetic and real data.  
- Method is straightforward and potentially powerful for causal inference tasks (e.g., testing unconfoundedness).  
- Paper is well-written and accessible to practitioners despite some technical proof complexity.  
- Theoretical guarantees are the most impressive contribution, extending classifier two-sample tests to the conditional setting.  
- Approach resembles "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" with neighbor-matching on Z but provides stronger theoretical analysis.  
- Reviewer asks whether a discrete analog exists and notes the bootstrap may be stronger when Z is discrete.  
- Reviewer requests analysis of sensitivity to classifier choice and hyperparameters, as this could affect test decisions.  
- Section 1.1 is redundant and repeats the approach excessively.  
- Algorithm 2 is unnecessary and can be described more succinctly.  
- Minor typos: "classifier" (line 157) and "graphs" (line 280).  
- Strengths: technically sound, thorough theoretical analysis, clear methodology, well-organized, original extension.  
- Weaknesses: limited discussion of method limitations, lack of sensitivity study to classifiers, and insufficient discussion of implications for causal inference.  
- Recommendation: overall positive—recommend acceptance conditional on addressing limitations and expanding discussion of robustness and causal implications.
