## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

This paper introduces a novel conditional independence test grounded in a smoothness assumption on the conditional density. The work identifies a crucial, often overlooked requirement for the validity of such tests – a reasonable assumption about the underlying data distribution – and provides a theoretical framework to analyze performance under this assumption. While the technical contributions are significant, the presentation hinders a complete understanding of the results.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its careful consideration of the assumptions underlying conditional independence testing. The authors correctly point out that a smoothness assumption on the conditional density is necessary for any practical test to function effectively. They then proceed to formulate such an assumption and provide a bound, in total variation distance, between the true conditional density under the null hypothesis and the density estimated via bootstrapping under their smoothness condition. This is a novel and valuable contribution to the field.

Furthermore, the authors leverage this smoothness assumption to derive a bound on the error of the optimal classifier trained on the training data (see 1.1(iii)). This connection between conditional independence and generalization error is insightful. 

Theorem 1 is particularly noteworthy. The combination of the smoothness assumption, an error bound on the estimated risk (represented by η), and a bound on the difference between the optimal and trained classifier’s errors represents a significant and non-trivial result. This theorem offers a potentially powerful framework for analyzing the performance of classifiers in situations where conditional independence holds approximately.

**Weaknesses:**

The most significant weakness of this paper is its lack of clarity and interpretability. The presentation is dense and the authors provide insufficient intuition for the derived results. This opacity makes it challenging to fully grasp the implications of the theorems and their practical applicability. 

For example, consider the inequality in section 1.1(iii). If the alternative hypothesis holds and *r<sub>0</sub>* = 0.1, a reader is left to wonder how a small probability of error can be achieved. Specifically, suppose *G* represents a small, limited class of functions – such as linear functions – that are demonstrably inadequate to distinguish between *f* and *f<sup>CI</sup>* (where *f<sup>CI</sup>* represents the density under conditional independence). Intuitively, how can *R̂* be less than 0.1 + *o<sub>1</sub>* < 0.2 for large *n* if the function class *G* cannot even represent the true underlying relationship?  A detailed explanation or illustrative example addressing this point would greatly enhance understanding. 

The paper would benefit significantly from more extensive discussion of the assumptions themselves. What types of data distributions are likely to satisfy the proposed smoothness condition? Are there practical guidelines for assessing whether this assumption holds in a given application? Providing concrete examples and/or discussing the limitations of the assumption would be extremely helpful.

**Questions/Suggestions for the Authors:**

*   Could the authors provide a more intuitive explanation for the result in section 1.1(iii), specifically addressing the scenario where the function class *G* is insufficient to distinguish between *f* and *f<sup>CI</sup>*?
*   What are the practical implications of the smoothness assumption? Are there methods for verifying or approximating the total variation distance bound in practice?
*   The paper heavily relies on theoretical analysis.  Could the authors discuss potential avenues for empirical validation of their results, even if a full-scale experimental evaluation is beyond the scope of the current work?
*   Consider adding a dedicated section outlining the key assumptions made throughout the paper and their potential limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the significant technical contributions, the lack of clarity and interpretability significantly detract from the paper's overall impact. The theoretical results are promising, but require further elaboration and contextualization to be fully appreciated.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would substantially improve the paper and make it more accessible to a wider audience.  As it stands, the paper presents a solid theoretical foundation but falls short in terms of practical insight and intuitive understanding. I would recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit with a focus on clarity, interpretability, and discussion of practical implications.
