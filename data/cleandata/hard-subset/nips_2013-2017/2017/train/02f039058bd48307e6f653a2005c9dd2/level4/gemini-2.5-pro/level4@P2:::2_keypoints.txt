- Introduces CCIT: reframes conditional independence testing as binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap that swaps y's to approximate the null f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Contribution: presents the reduction framework allowing supervised models to perform CI testing.  
- Contribution: proposes a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate null samples from observed joint data.  
- Contribution: Theorem 1 — bounds total variation distance between bootstrap distribution and true null under smoothness assumptions.  
- Contribution: Theorem 2 — provides Rademacher-complexity-style generalization bounds for “near-independent” (non-i.i.d.) bootstrap samples.  
- Contribution: Strong empirical results showing CCIT outperforms kernel methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially with high-dimensional Z.  
- Strength: Elegant, powerful and practical reduction leveraging modern supervised learning models.  
- Strength: Rigorous theoretical treatment of the bootstrap and clear identification of necessary smoothness conditions.  
- Strength: Novel, technically challenging generalization analysis for non-i.i.d. samples of independent interest.  
- Strength: Compelling empirical validation on synthetic and real (flow-cytometry) datasets.  
- Weakness: Paper is dense and clarity suffers; theoretical statements (e.g., Theorem 1) need more intuition and interpretation.  
- Weakness: Theory does not explicitly account for classifier capacity — if the hypothesis class G is insufficiently expressive, the test may fail in practice.  
- Recommendation: Overall positive — novelty, theory, and empirical strength justify acceptance.  
- For acceptance: novel reduction, rigorous bootstrap analysis, new non-i.i.d. generalization bounds, and state-of-the-art empirical performance.  
- For rejection: presentation difficulty and lack of explicit treatment of classifier expressiveness in theoretical guarantees.
