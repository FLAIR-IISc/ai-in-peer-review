This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conduct conditional independence tests for iid data. The core idea is to utilize nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that closely follow the conditional independence distribution, denoted as f^{CI}, and then train and test a classifier to determine if it can distinguish between the observation distribution and the bootstrapped distribution. If the classification performance is comparable to a random guess, the null hypothesis that the data follows conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, it is accepted.

The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI} in terms of total variational distance and bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for near-independent samples. Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is presented in a clear manner.

Detailed comments:

Major:

1. The proposed method appears to consist of two primary components: generating samples that mimic f_{CI} and using a classifier for determination. It is unclear whether these steps can be replaced with existing solutions from the literature. For instance, using the permutation-based method proposed in [7] in conjunction with the classification-based approach or combining nearest neighbor bootstrap with the kernel two-sample test. It would be interesting to investigate the performance of these alternative approaches.

2. In the finite sample size setting, the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution is close to f_{CI}. However, if the ground truth is that x and y are weakly dependent given z, it is unclear how the proposed method would perform in this scenario.

3. The method does not appear to be symmetric with respect to x and y. From a causal perspective, x and y can be dependent given z due to two different causal models: x causes y or y causes x. In these scenarios, it is unclear whether it is necessary to determine which variable to sample. The authors do not address this issue.

4. The choice of the parameter τ in algorithms 2 and 3 is a concern. It is unclear how this parameter should be selected, and its impact on the performance of the method is not discussed.

Minor:

In algorithms 2 and 3, it appears that the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size to obtain an unbiased estimate.
