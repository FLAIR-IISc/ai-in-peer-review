Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a model-powered approach to nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing CI testing to binary classification. Given i.i.d. samples from f(x, y, z), the authors generate a second sample set that approximates the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) using a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap in Z: for each (x, y, z) in one subset, they swap its Y coordinate with the Y of its nearest neighbor in Z from another subset. They then train a classifier to distinguish the original samples from the bootstrapped samples. If the classifier’s test accuracy is close to 0.5, they fail to reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z; otherwise they reject H0. The paper provides (i) a total variation (TV) bound that the NN-bootstrapped distribution is close to fCI under smoothness assumptions, with error O(n−1/dz); and (ii) generalization risk bounds for empirical risk minimization with near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples, expressed via VC/Rademacher-style terms. Empirically, the method (CCIT) outperforms kernel-based CI tests such as KCIT and RCIT on synthetic data and flow cytometry data.

This connects to several lines of work: kernel-based CI tests (KCIT/RCIT/KCIPT), the use of permutation-based procedures to emulate CI samples, classifier two-sample tests for independence, and nearest-neighbor methods used for density/entropy estimation. The main differences are the NN bootstrap (computationally simple compared to permutation schemes requiring optimization) and the use of flexible classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees) together with non-i.i.d. generalization analysis.

Strengths:
- Clear, modular reduction of CI testing to two-sample classification that can leverage state-of-the-art classifiers.
- Theoretical guarantees: a TV-distance bound establishing that the NN-bootstrapped samples approximate fCI, and generalization bounds under near-independence.
- Strong empirical performance, especially in higher-dimensional Z, with publicly available code.
- The bias-corrected variant (training on (Y, Z) alone) addresses finite-sample bias arising from non-i.i.d. bootstrapped samples.

Major comments:
1) Modularity and hybridization with existing methods. The approach decomposes into (a) constructing a surrogate sample from fCI (here via NN bootstrap), and (b) performing a two-sample test (here via classification). It would be helpful to evaluate cross-substitutions with existing tools. For example, can the permutation-based method proposed in [7] be used to generate fCI-like samples and then coupled with the classification-based decision rule? Conversely, can the NN bootstrap be paired with a kernel two-sample test (e.g., MMD) instead of a classifier? A comparative study of such hybrids would clarify the benefits of each module.

2) Power under weak conditional dependence. The NN bootstrap is only approximately fCI for finite n. If the ground truth exhibits weak dependence between X and Y given Z, how well can the proposed method distinguish H1 from H0? In other words, when the departure from CI is small and the bootstrap approximation error is non-negligible, what is the power, and how does it scale with n, dz, and classifier capacity? A discussion (or experiments) addressing sensitivity to weak alternatives would strengthen the contribution.

3) Symmetry in X and Y and causal interpretation. Is the method symmetric with respect to X and Y? From a causal perspective, X ⟂̸ Y | Z can arise from X → Y, Y → X, or other structures. In these scenarios, does the procedure’s performance depend on which variable’s coordinates are swapped in the NN bootstrap or used by the classifier? The paper does not explicitly address this, and some guidance (or a statement of symmetry/invariance) would be valuable.

4) Choice of τ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The performance depends on the threshold τ used to decide whether the classifier accuracy is significantly above 0.5. I am seriously concerned about how to choose τ in practice, especially as n, dz, and classifier complexity vary, and in the presence of finite-sample bias. More principled guidance (e.g., data-driven calibration, control of Type I error) would improve the practical utility of the method.

Minor:
- In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and timely problem; clear and modular approach that is easy to implement.
- Provides new theoretical analyses (TV closeness of NN bootstrap to fCI; generalization under near-independence).
- Strong empirical results, with scalability advantages in high-dimensional Z.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited guidance on setting τ and assessing power under weak dependence.
- The role of symmetry between X and Y is not discussed, which may matter in causal contexts.
- No empirical evaluation of hybrid variants that could isolate the contributions of the two modules.

Evaluation by review criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound overall, with solid theoretical bounds and careful empirical validation. The analysis under near-independence is a welcome addition. Practical calibration of τ and power under weak alternatives need further clarification.
- Clarity: Generally well written and well organized. Algorithms are clearly specified. Minor notational issues (empirical risk normalization) should be fixed.
- Originality: The reduction of CI testing to classification via an NN bootstrap and the accompanying non-i.i.d. generalization analysis are novel and distinct from kernel-based CI tests and prior permutation-based approaches.
- Significance: Potentially high impact, as the approach allows CI testing to inherit advances in supervised learning and appears to scale better with dz in practice. Clear guidance on τ and additional experiments on weak dependence and hybrid designs would further enhance significance.
