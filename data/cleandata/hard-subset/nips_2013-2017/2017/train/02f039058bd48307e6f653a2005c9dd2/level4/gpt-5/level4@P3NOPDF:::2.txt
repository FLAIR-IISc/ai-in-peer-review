This paper proposes a new conditional independence (CI) test built on a smoothness assumption for conditional densities and a bootstrap scheme, together with a classification-based analysis that yields finite-sample guarantees. The central technical contribution is a bound in total variation (TV) distance between the null density and the bootstrap density under the proposed smoothness condition, which is then leveraged to control the error of a learned classifier and to derive the main testing guarantee (Theorem 1).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The paper clearly identifies that some smoothness of the conditional density is necessary for a bootstrap-based CI test to be valid, formalizes such an assumption, and proves a TV bound between the true null and the bootstrap distribution under this assumption. To my knowledge, this is new and important.
- Methodological leverage: The TV bound is used to control the discrepancy between the empirical performance of a learned classifier and the idealized target under the null. The decomposition that combines (i) smoothness/TV control, (ii) an assumption that the optimal empirical risk within the chosen hypothesis class is achievable up to a small error η, and (iii) a bound on the gap between the optimal and learned classifiers, is insightful and non-trivial.
- Technical depth: Theorem 1 is carefully structured and, as far as I can tell, original. The way it integrates smoothness, bootstrap validity, and learning-theoretic error terms to produce a CI test with guarantees is elegant and of theoretical interest.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Clarity and interpretation: The paper is difficult to read and offers little intuition for how the pieces fit together. In particular, Section 1.1(iii) and Theorem 1 would benefit from a plain-language explanation of what each term (r0, η, empirical error, approximation error, etc.) represents, which are stochastic vs. deterministic, and how they scale with n and the complexity of the hypothesis class G.
- Role of approximation error: A key conceptual gap is the relationship between r0 (the target/”Bayes” risk under the alternative), the richness of G, and the empirical risk of the learned classifier. Consider the following scenario, which I believe exposes a potential inconsistency in the current presentation. Assume the alternative holds with r0 = 0.1. Suppose G is a small class (e.g., linear functions) that is not expressive enough to distinguish f from f^CI at all. Then the best classifier in G may have risk near 0.5, dominated by approximation error. In that case, how can the event {hat R < 0.1 + o1 < 0.2} have non-negligible probability for large n? Does r0 denote the unconstrained Bayes risk or the Bayes risk within G? If it is the former, it seems necessary to assume and make explicit that the approximation error of G relative to the target is at most η (or another specified term); otherwise, the test may have poor power regardless of sample size. Please clarify this point and adjust the statements so that the dependence on approximation capacity is explicit and checkable.
- Assumption verifiability and rates: The smoothness condition enabling the TV bound is central but currently abstract. It would help to specify the regularity class (e.g., Hölder/Lipschitz with order s and constant L), state the resulting TV rates in terms of (n, s, L, dimension), and indicate when these rates are sufficient to dominate the learning-theoretic errors. Similarly, specify how η depends on n and the capacity of G (e.g., via VC dimension, Rademacher complexity, or stability).
- Separation of concerns in Theorem 1: The theorem confounds three distinct phenomena—bootstrap validity (distributional), approximation capacity (bias), and estimation error (variance). The current statement makes it hard to see which regime controls power. A modular statement with separate lemmas for (i) TV control under smoothness, (ii) approximation error within G, and (iii) generalization from training to population would improve readability and interpretability.
- Guidance for practice: The paper lacks actionable guidance on choosing G, calibrating the bootstrap, and diagnosing when the assumptions plausibly hold. Without such guidance, applying the method may be challenging.

Requests for clarification
- Precisely define r0, hat R, and η in Section 1.1(iii): which are population vs. empirical, within G vs. unconstrained, and how they are estimated.
- In the small-G example above, explain how (or whether) the bound permits hat R < 0.2 with high probability. If it relies on r0 being defined within G, please say so; if it relies on η absorbing approximation error, please make that explicit and discuss how η scales.
- Provide intuition for the bootstrap TV bound: what smoothness property ensures that the bootstrap samples approximate the null distribution in TV? A short sketch or canonical example would help.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Add a brief high-level roadmap of how the test works: (1) define the bootstrap scheme under CI; (2) argue via smoothness that the bootstrap approximates the null in TV; (3) reduce testing to a classification problem; (4) control empirical risk via approximation and estimation errors; (5) combine to get a valid test.
- Consolidate and restate all assumptions just before Theorem 1, with clear notation and a one-line intuition for each. Provide at least one concrete setting where they hold with known rates.
- Include discussion of the choice of G: trade-offs between power and overfitting, and how capacity affects η and the final test threshold.
- If space allows, add a simple synthetic example illustrating the effect of G’s expressiveness: show that if G is too small, power collapses (as predicted), and that as G becomes sufficiently rich to make approximation error ≤ η, the test achieves the advertised performance.

Overall assessment
This is a technically strong and potentially impactful contribution: the smoothness-based TV control for bootstrap validity in CI testing, combined with a classification reduction, is novel and valuable. However, the paper’s presentation obscures the main ideas, and key quantities—especially the interplay between r0, η, and the approximation capacity of G—are insufficiently explained. With clearer statements, explicit rates and assumptions, and a more transparent discussion of approximation error, this work would be significantly strengthened. Pending these clarifications, I lean positive on the technical novelty but would like to see the authors address the interpretability and practicality concerns raised above.
