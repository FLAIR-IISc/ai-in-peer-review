The authors propose a general test for conditional independence between three continuous variables, eschewing strong parametric assumptions in favor of a method that leverages samples from the joint distribution to create samples close to the conditional product distribution in terms of total variation distance. These samples are then fed into a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network, which is trained on samples from both distributions and subsequently used to determine the predictive error. This error is then utilized to either accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of their proposed method with existing approaches in the literature, including both descriptive analyses and experimental results on synthetic and real-world data.

Conditional independence testing is a crucial tool in modern machine learning, particularly in the domain of causal inference, where assessing claims of unconfoundedness is a common endeavor. The proposed method appears to be a straightforward yet powerful approach that can be readily applied to various causal applications.

The paper is well-written, with the authors successfully summarizing theoretical results and making the methodology accessible to practitioners. While the details of the theoretical results and proofs can be challenging to follow, the authors have done a commendable job in presenting the material.

The theoretical results presented in the paper are particularly impressive. Although the proposed method is an extension of the approach outlined in "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as acknowledged by the authors, the guarantees provided by the theoretical results are noteworthy. The authors' modification of the test to incorporate a third variable Z, followed by neighbor matching, yields a method that is similar yet distinct.

Several additional comments and suggestions are offered:

* Is there a discrete analog to the proposed method? The bootstrap approach appears to become even more powerful when Z is discrete.
* How sensitive is the test to the choice of classification method used? It would be informative to investigate whether minor changes to hyperparameters can lead to different acceptance and rejection results, potentially alongside the simulations.
* Section 1.1 of Main Contributions appears redundant, as the authors provide multiple summaries of their approach, which seems unnecessary.
* Writing out Algorithm 2 in its entirety appears unnecessary, as this process can be described more succinctly and has already been outlined in the paper.
* There are minor typos that require correction, including "classifier" in line 157 and "graphs" in line 280.
