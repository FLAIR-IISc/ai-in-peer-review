The authors propose a general test for assessing conditional independence between three continuous variables, eschewing strong parametric assumptions in favor of a method that leverages samples from the joint distribution to create samples that closely approximate the conditional product distribution in terms of total variation distance. These samples are then fed into a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network, and the predictive error of the classifier is used to either accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of their proposed method to existing approaches in the literature, both through descriptive analyses and experimental results on synthetic and real data.

Conditional independence testing is a crucial tool in modern machine learning, particularly in the domain of causal inference, where assessing claims of unconfoundedness is a common endeavor. The proposed method appears to be a straightforward yet powerful approach that can be readily applied to various causal inference applications.

The paper is well-written, with the authors doing an excellent job of summarizing the theoretical results and making the methodology accessible to practitioners. While the details of the theoretical results and proofs can be challenging to follow, the authors provide a clear and concise overview of the key findings.

The theoretical results presented in the paper are particularly impressive. Although the proposed method can be seen as a natural extension of the approach for testing independence presented in "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), the authors' modification of the test to incorporate a third variable Z and their subsequent theoretical guarantees are noteworthy.

Several minor suggestions for improvement are offered below:

* It would be interesting to explore whether a discrete analog of the proposed method exists, as the bootstrap approach may become even more powerful when Z is discrete.
* An examination of the sensitivity of the test to the choice of classification method and its associated hyperparameters would be a valuable addition to the paper. In particular, it would be useful to investigate whether small changes to the hyperparameters can lead to different acceptance and rejection results.
* Section 1.1 of the Main Contributions appears to be redundant, as the authors provide multiple summaries of their approach throughout the paper.
* Writing out Algorithm 2 in its entirety may be unnecessary, as the process can be described more succinctly and has already been outlined in the paper.
* A few minor typos were noted: "classifier" should be used in line 157, and "graphs" should be used in line 280.
