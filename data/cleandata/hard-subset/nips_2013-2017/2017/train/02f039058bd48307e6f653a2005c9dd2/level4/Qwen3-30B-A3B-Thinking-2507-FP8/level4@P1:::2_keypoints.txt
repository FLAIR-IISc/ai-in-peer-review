- Proposes a novel reduction of conditional independence testing to binary classification using powerful classifiers (e.g., XGBoost, neural nets).  
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) to generate samples approximating f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Provides theoretical guarantee that the bootstrap sample is close to f_CI in total variation distance (Theorem 1).  
- Establishes generalization bounds for classification under near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples (Theorem 2).  
- Translates classifier generalization results into error guarantees for CI testing.  
- Empirically outperforms state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic benchmarks and a flow cytometry dataset.  
- Theoretical contributions are rigorous and non-trivial, improving on prior methods that lacked guarantees.  
- Leverages modern supervised learning tools, making the CI test scalable and modular to classifier choice.  
- Bias-corrected variant (CCITv2) addresses finite-sample issues and improves practical performance.  
- Main weakness: theoretical results lack practical interpretability—readers aren’t shown how bounds translate to realistic classifier performance.  
- Exposition issues: definition and role of G(ε) are buried and not clearly connected to algorithmic choices.  
- Exposition issues: the source of bias from the non-i.i.d. bootstrap and the intuition for the bias correction could be clearer.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to add practical examples, clarify G(ε) in Algorithm 3, and better explain the bootstrap’s non-i.i.d. effects.
