This paper proposes a novel, model-powered approach for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing. The core idea is to reframe the statistical test as a binary classification problem. To achieve this, the authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from a distribution that approximates the true conditionally independent distribution, $f_{CI}(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)$. A powerful classifier, such as a gradient-boosted tree, is then trained to distinguish between the original i.i.d. samples and these bootstrapped samples. If the classifier's performance is significantly better than random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence ($H_0$) is rejected.

The authors provide significant theoretical contributions to support this framework. They derive a bound on the total variation distance between their bootstrapped distribution and the ideal $f_{CI}$, demonstrating that the approximation improves with sample size. Furthermore, they provide novel generalization bounds for a classifier trained on the resulting "near-i.i.d." dataset, which is of independent interest. This analysis connects the classifier's test error to the total variation distance between the true joint distribution and $f_{CI}$, thereby grounding the hypothesis test in learning theory. The proposed method is evaluated on synthetic and real-world data, showing substantial performance gains over prior kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Framework:** The reduction of CI testing to binary classification is an elegant and powerful idea. It allows the vast and rapidly advancing toolkit of supervised learning to be directly applied to a fundamental statistical problem. This "model-powered" paradigm could have a significant impact on how such hypothesis tests are designed in the future.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing. The proposed method, CCIT, demonstrates a clear state-of-the-art performance, particularly in the challenging high-dimensional setting where the dimension of the conditioning set Z is large. The ability to maintain high AUC scores as $d_z$ scales up to 70 is a notable achievement.

3.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is not merely a heuristic application of classifiers. The authors provide rigorous theoretical justification for their approach, including convergence guarantees for their bootstrap sampler (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for the classifier in their specific non-i.i.d. setting (Theorem 2). This adds considerable depth and credibility to the work.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The problem, proposed algorithms, and theoretical results are presented in a logical and accessible manner.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Modularity and Ablation Study:** The proposed method has two main components: the nearest-neighbor bootstrap for sample generation and the classifier for the two-sample test. The experiments effectively show that the combination of these two is superior to previous end-to-end methods. However, the contribution of each component is not individually assessed. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study. For instance:
    *   How does the proposed nearest-neighbor bootstrap compare to other sampling methods (e.g., the permutation-based approach from Doran et al. 2014) when paired with the same classifier-based test?
    *   Conversely, how does the classifier-based test perform when paired with another sampling method, compared to a kernel-based two-sample test on the same data?
    This would help disentangle whether the performance gain is primarily from the novel sampler, the use of powerful classifiers, or their synergistic combination.

2.  **Sensitivity to Weak Dependence:** The nearest-neighbor bootstrap only generates samples that are *close* to the distribution $f_{CI}$, with an approximation error that depends on the sample size `n`. A key question is how the method distinguishes between the null hypothesis (where $f = f_{CI}$) and a case of weak dependence where the total variation distance $d_{TV}(f, f_{CI})$ is small and potentially of the same order as the bootstrap approximation error. Could the inherent bias in the bootstrapped samples mask a true, but weak, conditional dependence? A discussion on the statistical power of the test in this low-signal regime would be valuable.

3.  **Practical Guidance on Hyperparameter Selection:** A critical practical concern is the selection of the threshold parameter $\tau$ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The authors use $\tau = 1/\sqrt{n}$ in their experiments, but this choice lacks a thorough justification. The performance and Type I/II error rates of the test are directly dependent on this threshold. How sensitive is the method to the choice of $\tau$? Could the authors provide more principled guidance or a data-driven procedure for setting this parameter? Without this, the applicability of the method by practitioners is limited.

4.  **Symmetry of the Test:** The property of conditional independence, $X \perp Y | Z$, is symmetric with respect to X and Y. However, the proposed bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) is asymmetric, as it explicitly finds a nearest neighbor for a sample $(x, y, z)$ and swaps its y-coordinate to create $(x, y', z)$. Would swapping the x-coordinate instead yield an equivalent test statistic? Please clarify if the choice matters and if the theoretical results hold irrespective of which variable is resampled.

**Minor Point:**

*   In the pseudocode for Algorithm 2 (Line 6) and Algorithm 3 (Lines 4 and 5), the definition of Empirical Risk Minimization `L(g, D_r)` is missing the normalization factor `1/|D_r|`. The text describes it correctly, but the formulas in the algorithm block should be updated for consistency.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and effective method for conditional independence testing. The combination of a creative reduction to classification, strong empirical results, and solid theoretical backing makes it a strong contribution to the field. While there are some weaknesses regarding hyperparameter selection and the lack of an ablation study, these do not undermine the core contribution. I recommend acceptance. The authors are encouraged to address the weaknesses raised above in their rebuttal and final version of the paper to further improve its impact and practical utility.
