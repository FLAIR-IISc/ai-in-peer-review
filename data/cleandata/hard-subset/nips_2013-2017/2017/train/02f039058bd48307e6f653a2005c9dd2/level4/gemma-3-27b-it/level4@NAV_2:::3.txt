The authors propose a novel method for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing of continuous random variables. They formulate the CI test as a binary classification problem, leveraging powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks. The core challenge – generating samples from the conditional product distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) – is addressed with a nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure, for which theoretical guarantees regarding closeness to fCI in terms of total variational distance are provided. Furthermore, the paper develops generalization bounds for classification under near-independence, translating into error bounds for CI testing, and empirically validates the algorithm on both simulated and real datasets, demonstrating performance gains over existing methods.

This work addresses an important problem in statistical learning and causal inference. CI testing is a fundamental component of many algorithms, and a robust, scalable, and statistically sound method is highly valuable. The proposed approach is well-motivated, reducing CI testing to a classification problem allows harnessing recent advances in that field. The theoretical contributions, including the analysis of the bootstrap procedure and generalization bounds, are significant and strengthen the paper considerably. The empirical results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the method, showing improvements over state-of-the-art techniques.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the methodology. The authors effectively summarize the key ideas and results. While the mathematical details can be dense, the overall flow is logical and accessible. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world experiments adds to the credibility of the findings.

The strengths of this work lie in its novel approach, strong theoretical foundation, and promising empirical performance. The reduction to a classification problem is insightful, and the theoretical guarantees provide confidence in the method’s validity. The empirical results on both simulated and real data provide compelling evidence of its effectiveness. The algorithm’s performance appears particularly strong in high-dimensional settings, a common challenge in many applications.

However, there are a few areas for potential improvement. The discussion of the discrete analog of the bootstrap procedure could be expanded – a discrete Z may offer significant advantages in practice. Investigating the sensitivity of the test to the choice of classifier and hyperparameter settings would be valuable, possibly through a sensitivity analysis in the simulations. The repetition in Section 1.1, summarizing the contributions, could be streamlined. Algorithm 2’s detailed presentation feels redundant given prior explanations and might be omitted. There are also a few minor typos (e.g., “classifier” in line 157, “graphs” in 280) that should be addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to CI testing with strong theoretical guarantees.
*   Empirically demonstrates improved performance compared to existing methods.
*   Addresses a significant problem with broad applicability.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Minor areas for improvement in clarity and conciseness.
*   Sensitivity analysis to classifier choice and hyperparameters would strengthen the results.
*   The theoretical analysis, while strong, might be inaccessible to some readers.
