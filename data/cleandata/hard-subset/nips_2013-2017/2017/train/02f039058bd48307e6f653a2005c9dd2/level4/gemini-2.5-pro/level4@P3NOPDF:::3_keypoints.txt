- Introduces a non-parametric test for X ⟂ Y | Z that trains a binary classifier to distinguish samples from P(X,Y,Z) vs. constructed samples approximating P(X|Z)P(Y|Z)P(Z), using classifier accuracy as the test statistic.
- Method avoids strong parametric assumptions and uses nearest-neighbor matching on Z to construct the conditional product distribution.
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees, extending classifier two-sample test theory to the conditional independence setting with non-trivial analysis.
- Empirically compared against existing methods on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating competitive performance.
- Relevant and useful for causal inference tasks that require testing unconfoundedness.
- Paper is well-written; methodology is elegant and accessible to practitioners despite complex proofs.
- Primary contribution is the theoretical foundation and the clever nearest-neighbor adaptation for the conditional case.
- Request to comment on extensions to discrete or mixed-type variables, noting exact matching and simpler bootstrap when Z is discrete.
- Recommend a sensitivity analysis for classifier choice, architecture, and hyperparameters because results (p-values/decisions) may depend on these.
- Suggest condensing the redundant "Main Contributions" (Section 1.1) for conciseness.
