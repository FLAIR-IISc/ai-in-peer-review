This paper proposes a model-powered approach for conducting conditional independence tests on iid data. The core idea is to utilize nearest neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that follow a distribution close to the conditional independence distribution f^{CI}, and then train and test a classifier to determine if it can distinguish between the observation distribution and the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution. If the classification performance is close to random guessing, the null hypothesis that the data follows conditional independence is not rejected; otherwise, the null hypothesis is accepted.

The authors develop bounds on the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI} in terms of total variation distance and bounds on empirical risks under ideal classification settings and for nearly independent samples. Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is presented clearly.

Detailed comments:

Major:

1. The proposed method appears to consist of two primary components: generating samples that mimic f_{CI} and using a classifier for determination. A question arises as to whether either of these steps can be replaced with existing solutions from the literature. For instance, could the permutation-based method proposed in [7] be used in conjunction with the classification-based approach, or could the nearest neighbor bootstrap be combined with the kernel two-sample test? I am curious about the potential performance of these alternative approaches.

2. In the finite sample size setting, the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution is close to f_{CI}. However, if the ground truth is that x and y are weakly dependent given z, how will the proposed method distinguish between this scenario and true conditional independence?

3. Is the method symmetric with respect to x and y? From a causal perspective, x and y can be dependent given z due to different causal models (e.g., x causes y or y causes x). In these scenarios, is it necessary to determine which variable to sample? The authors do not consider this issue.

4. I am concerned about the choice of the parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The selection of this parameter is not clearly justified, and its impact on the method's performance is unclear.

Minor:

In Algorithms 2 and 3, it appears that the empirical risk should be divided by the sample size to obtain a more accurate estimate.
