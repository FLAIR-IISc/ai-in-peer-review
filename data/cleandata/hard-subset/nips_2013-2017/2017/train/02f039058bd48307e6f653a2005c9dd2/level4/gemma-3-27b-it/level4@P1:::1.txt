This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence testing for continuous random variables. The core idea is to convert the CI test into a binary classification problem by generating samples approximating the conditional independence distribution, *f*<sup>CI</sup>, using a novel nearest neighbor bootstrap procedure. A classifier is then trained to distinguish between samples from the original joint distribution and those generated by the bootstrap, allowing for a determination of conditional independence. The authors develop theoretical bounds on the closeness of the bootstrapped distribution to *f*<sup>CI</sup>, measured in terms of total variational distance, and provide generalization bounds for the classification task under near-independence assumptions.

The paper addresses an important problem in statistical learning with potential applications in causal inference, Bayesian network discovery, and feature selection. The approach is generally well-presented and clearly articulated. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature and position their work within the existing landscape of CI testing methods.

**Detailed Comments:**

**Major:**

1. The method can be conceptually divided into two main components: generating samples approximating *f*<sup>CI</sup> and using a classifier for decision-making. It would be valuable to understand whether substituting either component with existing methods from the literature impacts performance. For example, could using the permutation-based method from [19] in conjunction with a classifier yield comparable results? Alternatively, combining the nearest neighbor bootstrap with a kernel two-sample test might be a worthwhile comparison. Exploring these alternatives would strengthen the paper’s contribution.
2. The theoretical guarantees establish the closeness of the nearest neighbor bootstrap distribution to *f*<sup>CI</sup>, but this closeness is established in a finite sample size setting. If the true underlying relationship between *X* and *Y* given *Z* is weak dependence, how would the proposed method reliably distinguish this from conditional independence? Further discussion on the method’s sensitivity to weak dependencies would be beneficial.
3. The method appears symmetrical with respect to *X* and *Y*. However, from a causal perspective, dependence between *X* and *Y* given *Z* could arise from *X* causing *Y* or *Y* causing *X*. The current approach doesn’t explicitly address distinguishing these causal directions. Investigating whether sampling one variable versus the other affects performance, or if the method inherently favors identifying one direction, would be a valuable addition.
4. The selection of the threshold parameter *τ* in Algorithms 2 and 3 is a significant concern. The paper briefly mentions setting it to 1/sqrt(n), but a more thorough justification and sensitivity analysis are needed. How does the choice of *τ* impact the Type I and Type II error rates? Providing guidance on selecting an appropriate *τ* value in practice would enhance the usability of the method.

**Minor:**

In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculation should be normalized by dividing by the sample size. This is standard practice and ensures that the empirical risk is a valid estimate of the true risk.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to CI testing leveraging powerful classification techniques.
*   Theoretical guarantees provided for the bootstrapped samples and classification performance.
*   Empirical results demonstrate promising performance, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the method and theoretical results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of comparison with alternative approaches using existing methods as building blocks.
*   Limited discussion of the method’s sensitivity to weak dependencies.
*   Insufficient justification and sensitivity analysis of the threshold parameter *τ*.
*   Causal directionality is not addressed, which could limit applicability in certain contexts.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to conditional independence testing. While the theoretical and empirical results are encouraging, the concerns regarding the choice of the threshold parameter, sensitivity to weak dependencies, and comparison to alternative methods warrant careful consideration. I recommend a **weak accept** contingent upon the authors addressing the major concerns outlined above, particularly providing a more rigorous discussion of parameter selection and demonstrating robustness to varying degrees of dependence. The sensitivity analysis is critical for establishing confidence in the practical utility of the method.
