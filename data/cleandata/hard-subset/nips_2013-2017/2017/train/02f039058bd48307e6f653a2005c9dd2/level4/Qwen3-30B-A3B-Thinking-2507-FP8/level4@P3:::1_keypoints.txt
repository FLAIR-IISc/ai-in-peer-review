- Proposes a novel reduction of conditional independence (CI) testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).

- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure that generates samples provably close to the conditionally independent distribution in total variation distance.

- Provides theoretical generalization bounds for classification under near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples, formalized in Theorems 1 and 2.

- Leverages powerful classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, deep nets) for CI testing, enabling model-powered testing beyond kernel methods.

- Empirically outperforms state-of-the-art CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic datasets across varying Z dimensions and on a real-world flow-cytometry dataset.

- Well-organized, clearly written paper with reproducible details (Algorithms 1–3) and a clear explanation of the bootstrap–classification–CI testing pipeline.

- Novel extension of classification-based independence testing to the conditional case with a theoretically justified bootstrap; distinguishes from permutation-based [10].

- Builds on and differentiates from kernel-based CI work and prior classification-for-independence methods (e.g., [19]).

- Theoretical analysis relies on smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) that may not hold in all practical settings and need clearer practical implications.

- Bias correction in Algorithm 3 and the threshold τ selection are ad hoc; no principled method is provided for choosing τ, which is critical for performance.

- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap resembles existing nearest-neighbor techniques in density estimation; the paper should more fully differentiate its approach from prior NN methods.

- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap for very large datasets beyond the experiments shown.

- Strong overall contribution to high-dimensional CI testing with practical relevance to causal inference and structure learning.

- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address theoretical assumptions, principled threshold selection, and scalability/complexity discussion.
