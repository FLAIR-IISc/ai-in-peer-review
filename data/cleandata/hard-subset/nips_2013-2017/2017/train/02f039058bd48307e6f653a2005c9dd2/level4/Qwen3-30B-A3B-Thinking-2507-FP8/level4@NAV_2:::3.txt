

The authors present a model-powered approach to conditional independence testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. They propose a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples from the conditional product distribution (f_CI), which is theoretically shown to be close to the true conditional product distribution in total variation distance. This allows them to leverage powerful classifiers like gradient-boosted trees and deep neural networks for conditional independence testing, significantly outperforming prior state-of-the-art methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The paper builds on the framework of "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016), extending their approach to handle conditional independence with a third variable Z. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for their bootstrap procedure and derive generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples, which are of independent interest to the machine learning community.

The paper demonstrates several strengths. The core idea of converting conditional independence testing to a classification problem is elegant and well-motivated, with clear applications in causal inference and Bayesian network structure learning. The theoretical contributions are substantial, particularly the novel analysis of Rademacher complexity bounds under near-independence. The empirical evaluation is thorough, showing consistent performance gains over existing methods on both synthetic data (with varying dimensionality of Z) and real-world flow-cytometry data. The paper is well-written and accessible, with clear explanations of the methodology that would benefit practitioners.

Several weaknesses merit consideration. The main contributions section (Section 1.1) contains redundant descriptions of the approach, with the same core ideas repeated multiple times without adding new insights. The presentation of Algorithm 2 is unnecessarily detailed; the authors have already explained the procedure in the main text, so the pseudocode adds little value. There are minor typographical errors in the manuscript, including "classifier" missing in line 157 and "graphs" misspelled as "graps" in line 280. The paper would benefit from addressing these minor issues.

The authors might consider exploring whether their bootstrap method could be adapted for discrete Z variables, as the approach could potentially be even more powerful in that setting. Additionally, it would be valuable to include an analysis of how sensitive their method is to the choice of classification algorithm and hyperparameters, as this could impact the robustness of the test results.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing with a novel, theoretically grounded approach that leverages modern machine learning techniques. The method is both principled and practical, with clear advantages over existing approaches, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and will likely be of interest to researchers working on causal inference and statistical learning.
