

The authors propose a general test for conditional independence among three continuous variables. Unlike methods relying on strong parametric assumptions, their approach uses samples from the joint distribution to generate samples approximating the conditional product distribution in total variation distance. These samples are then fed into a binary classifier (e.g., a deep neural network). After training the classifier on samples from both the joint and conditional product distributions, the authors use its predictive error to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The method is rigorously compared to existing literature through both theoretical descriptions and empirical results on synthetic and real-world data.  

Conditional independence testing is crucial for causal inference, particularly in assessing unconfoundedness assumptions. The proposed method is straightforward and sufficiently powerful for practical application in causal analysis. The paper is well-written, with the authors effectively summarizing complex theoretical results and making the methodology accessible to practitioners, despite the technical depth of the proofs.  

The most compelling aspect of the paper is its theoretical guarantees. While the approach extends the two-sample test framework from "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (by adapting it for a third conditioning variable Z—after matching neighbors of Z), the resulting theoretical properties are impressive. The authors correctly acknowledge this connection, but the extension itself is non-trivial and well-justified.  

Minor concerns:  
1. **Discrete case**: Is there a discrete analog? When Z is discrete, the bootstrap procedure may become more straightforward, and the authors should address this.  
2. **Classifier sensitivity**: How sensitive is the test to the choice of classifier? A brief analysis of hyperparameter robustness (e.g., whether minor changes alter results) would strengthen the empirical validation.  
3. **Redundancy**: Section 1.1 redundantly reiterates the approach; the contributions could be stated concisely without repetition.  
4. **Algorithm 2**: The detailed presentation of Algorithm 2 is unnecessary, as the procedure is already clearly described in the main text.  
5. **Typos**: Line 157 should read "classifier" (not "classifer"); line 280 should read "graphs" (not a typo).  

Overall, this is a strong contribution with clear practical value and robust theoretical foundations. The minor suggestions above are easily addressed and would further enhance the paper’s impact.
