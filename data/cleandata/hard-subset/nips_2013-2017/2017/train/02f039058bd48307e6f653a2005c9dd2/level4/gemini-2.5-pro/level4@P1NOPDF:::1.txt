This paper proposes a model-powered approach for conducting conditional independence tests on i.i.d. data. The core idea is to use nearest-neighbor bootstrapping to generate samples from a distribution that approximates the true conditional independence distribution, denoted f^{CI}. A classifier is then trained to distinguish between the original, observed data distribution and this bootstrapped distribution. If the classifier's performance is close to random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence cannot be rejected. Conversely, if the classifier can distinguish the two distributions effectively, the null hypothesis is rejected. The authors develop theoretical bounds on the closeness of the nearest-neighbor bootstrapped distribution and f^{CI} in terms of total variation distance. They also provide bounds on the empirical risk for ideal classifiers and for near-independent samples.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem and is clearly presented.

**Major Comments:**

1.  The proposed method appears to consist of two distinct components: 1) a sample generation step to approximate f_{CI}, and 2) a classification step to perform the test. This raises the question of whether each component could be replaced by an existing alternative from the literature. For instance, could the permutation-based method from [7] be used for sample generation, followed by the proposed classification-based test? Alternatively, could the nearest-neighbor bootstrap be combined with a kernel two-sample test instead of a classifier? I am curious about the performance of these hybrid approaches.

2.  The paper shows that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap distribution is close to f_{CI} in the finite-sample setting. Given this, how would the proposed method perform when X and Y are weakly dependent conditional on Z? Since the bootstrapped distribution only approximates f_{CI}, it seems plausible that the classifier might fail to distinguish it from the observed distribution in a weak dependence scenario, potentially leading to a false negative.

3.  Is the method symmetric with respect to X and Y? From a causal perspective, conditional dependence between X and Y given Z can arise from different causal structures (e.g., X causes Y or Y causes X). In these different scenarios, does the choice of which variable to bootstrap matter? The authors do not appear to discuss this potential asymmetry.

4.  I have a significant concern regarding the practical selection of the threshold parameter $\tau$ in Algorithms 2 and 3. The paper provides little guidance on how to set this parameter, which could be a major hurdle for practitioners.

**Minor Comments:**

*   In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk calculation appears to be missing a division by the sample size.
