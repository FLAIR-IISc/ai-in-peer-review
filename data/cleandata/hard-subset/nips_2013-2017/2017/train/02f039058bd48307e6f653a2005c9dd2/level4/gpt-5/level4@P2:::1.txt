Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a model-powered approach to nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables by reducing CI testing to binary classification. The key idea is to generate synthetic samples approximating the conditionally independent product distribution fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap on Z: split the data, find for each (x, y, z) in one split its Z-nearest neighbor in the other split, swap the Y coordinate, and treat these modified samples as draws from fCI. The CI test is then implemented as a two-sample classification problem between original samples and NN-bootstrapped samples: if a classifier can separate them, reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z; otherwise, fail to reject H0.

The authors provide:
- A TV-distance bound showing the NN-bootstrapped distribution is close to fCI under smoothness assumptions, with error scaling roughly as O(n^{-1/dz}).
- Generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization when training on “near-independent” samples (due to dependence induced by the NN bootstrap), extending classical VC/Rademacher analyses to this setting.
- Empirical comparisons showing improved performance over kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and a related permutation-based approach (KCIPT), especially in higher-dimensional Z, by leveraging strong classifiers (e.g., XGBoost).

This work is closely related to prior reductions from statistical testing to classification (e.g., classifier two-sample tests for independence), to kernel-based CI testing (KCIT/RCIT) and to permutation-based constructions for fCI (e.g., the LP-based KCIPT). The proposed NN-bootstrap is markedly simpler than solving an LP and comes with TV-distance guarantees.

Strengths:
- Clear, modular reduction of CI testing to classification, enabling the use of powerful modern learners.
- Novel NN-bootstrap with total variation guarantees to emulate fCI using only samples from f(x, y, z).
- Generalization analysis for classification with near-independent samples; technically interesting beyond CI testing.
- Strong empirical performance, particularly in higher-dimensional Z, with an efficient and practical implementation.

Major questions and suggestions:
1) Interchangeability of modules:
- The method decomposes into (a) a generator for fCI-like samples and (b) a discriminator (classifier). It would be informative to explore hybrids:
  - Replace the NN bootstrap with other generators, e.g., permutation-based methods adapted to Z (KCIPT) or conditional resampling approaches, while keeping the classifier-based discriminator.
  - Replace the classifier with a kernel two-sample test (e.g., MMD) while keeping the NN bootstrap.
- A small empirical ablation would clarify how much each module contributes and whether certain pairings work better in specific regimes (e.g., small n, large dz).

2) Sensitivity to weak conditional dependence:
- When X and Y are only weakly dependent given Z, power hinges on the classifier’s ability to detect small deviations from 0.5 accuracy against the finite-sample bias from near-independence. Algorithm 3 mitigates bias by comparing a classifier on (X, Y, Z) to one on (Y, Z) only, but a more explicit treatment of detectability would help:
  - Provide power curves as a function of effect size and n, or characterize how the TV gap dTV(f, fCI) translates into expected excess accuracy using your bounds.
  - Discuss practical calibration (e.g., repeated splits/bootstraps, ensembling classifiers) to stabilize decisions near the boundary.

3) Symmetry in X and Y:
- The current bootstrap fixes (X, Z) and replaces Y via Z-NN, introducing an asymmetry in X and Y. Although the null is symmetric, the test statistic may not be. Consider:
  - A symmetric variant that also swaps X conditionally on Z (or averages the two directions).
  - Report whether results materially change when swapping X instead of Y, and whether combining both improves robustness or power.

4) Choice of the threshold τ (Algorithms 2 and 3):
- τ is critical for Type I error control. While the experiments use τ ≈ 1/√n, please provide concrete guidance:
  - Null calibration by repeated NN-bootstraps (or conditional permutations within Z-NN neighborhoods) to estimate the null distribution of the test statistic and select τ for a target α.
  - Cross-fitting or sample-splitting to reduce bias and variance in the estimated loss difference.
  - Sensitivity analysis of the decision to τ to help practitioners.

Additional comments:
- Dimensionality of Z: The TV bound scales as O(n^{-1/dz}), which is pessimistic. The empirical results suggest better scaling with modern classifiers. It would be helpful to discuss when the NN approximation is the bottleneck (e.g., large dz with limited n) and whether k-NN (k > 1), local regression, or learned Z-representations could improve the approximation to f(y|z).
- Computational aspects: Please comment on the cost of NN search in higher dimensions (e.g., approximate NN) and the overall runtime vs kernel methods, including hyperparameter tuning overhead for classifiers.
- Assumptions: Clarify the strength and practical testability of the smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z) (e.g., whether mild violations materially affect the NN approximation).
- Comparisons: Ensure fair bandwidth/parameter tuning for KCIT/RCIT and verify Type I error calibration across methods, possibly via synthetic nulls.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance:
- Quality: The technical development is largely sound, with clear statements of assumptions and theorems. Some proofs address non-i.i.d. challenges in a novel way.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. A short algorithmic schematic and a summary of parameter choices (k in k-NN, τ selection, classifier hyperparameters) would further aid reproducibility.
- Originality: The NN-bootstrap for approximating fCI and the generalization analysis under near-independence are original and meaningful contributions.
- Significance: The reduction to classification is practically impactful and likely to influence CI testing in high dimensions where kernel methods can struggle.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, modular method with strong empirical performance and available code.
- Theoretical guarantees for both the bootstrap approximation and the classifier generalization.
- Practical relevance for causal discovery and high-dimensional CI testing.

Cons against acceptance:
- Sensitivity and calibration near weak dependencies need clearer guidance and empirical characterization.
- Asymmetry between X and Y warrants further analysis or a symmetric variant.
- Choice of τ and its impact on error control are under-specified.

Minor comments:
- In Algorithms 2 and 3, the empirical risk should be explicitly normalized by the sample size (you already define averages; please ensure consistent notation).
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “variational,” “developed,” “divided,” “near-independent”).
- Specify the distance metric for NN in Z, tie-breaking, and whether features are standardized.
