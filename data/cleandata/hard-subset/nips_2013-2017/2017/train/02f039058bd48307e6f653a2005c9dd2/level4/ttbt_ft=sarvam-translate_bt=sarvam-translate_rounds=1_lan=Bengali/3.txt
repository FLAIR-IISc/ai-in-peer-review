The authors propose a general method for testing conditional independence among three continuous variables. The authors do not have strong parametric assumptions – instead, they propose a method that uses a sample from a joint distribution to create a sample close to the conditional product distribution in terms of total variance distance. Then, they feed these samples into a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network, and after training the network on samples from each of the two distributions, they use the network's predictive error to accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. They compare their proposed method with existing methods in the literature, both in description and in experimental results on synthetic and real data.

Testing conditional independence is an important tool for modern machine learning, especially in causal inference, where we are often curious about claims of non-mixability. The proposed method seems simple enough and powerful enough to be used for many causal applications.

Additionally, the paper is well-written. Although it is difficult to follow the theoretical results and evidence in detail, the authors have done a good job of summarizing the results and making the method clear to practitioners.

For me, the most impressive part of the paper is the theoretical result. The proposed method is a somewhat simple extension of the independence test method in the paper "Revisiting classifier two-sample tests" (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as the authors acknowledge (they modify the test to include a third variable Z and after matching Z's neighbors, the methods are similar). However, the certainty of the theoretical result is impressive.

A few more comments:
- Does the discrete equivalent exist? Bootstrap becomes more robust when Z is discrete, right?
- How sensitive is the test to the classification method used? Is it possible that a few modified hyperparameters could lead to different acceptances and rejections? It would be useful to see this in addition to simulations.
- The main contribution's section 1.1 is redundant – the authors have summarized their method multiple times and its scope seems unnecessary.
- Writing algorithm 2 seems unnecessary – this process could be described more concisely and it has already been described in the paper.
