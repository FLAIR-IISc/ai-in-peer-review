Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test for continuous variables that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given samples from f(x, y, z), the method constructs synthetic samples that approximate the conditionally independent product fCI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap on Z: for each (x, y, z) in a holdout set, it replaces y with the y of the nearest neighbor (in Z) from another split. The original and synthetic samples are then labeled and fed to a binary classifier (e.g., gradient-boosted trees or deep nets). If a classifier can reliably distinguish between the two sets, the null H0: X ⟂ Y | Z is rejected; otherwise, H0 is not rejected. A bias-corrected variant compares classifiers trained with and without X to mitigate finite-sample deviations introduced by the bootstrap.

The approach builds on classifier two-sample tests for (unconditional) independence (e.g., Lopez-Paz and Oquab, 2016), but addresses the harder CI setting by constructing Z-dependent pseudo-samples. It is complementary to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and KCIPT, which also simulate fCI but require solving a linear program. The paper provides theoretical guarantees that the bootstrap distribution is close to fCI in total variation, and derives generalization bounds for classification with near-independent samples. Empirical results on synthetic and flow cytometry datasets show improved performance over KCIT/RCIT.

Evaluation

Quality
- Theory: The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is shown to generate samples within O(n^(-1/dz)) total variation of fCI under smoothness assumptions, and the paper gives generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization with near-i.i.d. samples. These results are technically nontrivial and appear carefully argued.
- Methodology: The reduction is clear, and the bias-corrected variant addresses finite-sample bias. However, the paper does not fully describe how to obtain calibrated p-values or control type I error at a prescribed level; the choice of threshold τ is discussed but seems heuristic.
- Empirics: Results are strong on synthetic data with increasing dim(Z) and on real data. Runtime comparisons and hyperparameter sensitivity are not fully explored.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and accessible to practitioners. Some repetition exists (e.g., Section 1.1 restates the approach in detail), and Algorithm 2 could be summarized more concisely since it mirrors standard classifier training. Proofs are dense but summaries are helpful.

Originality
- The reduction to classification is known for independence testing, but the CI extension via a simple, efficient nearest-neighbor bootstrap and the analysis of classification under near-independence are, to my knowledge, novel and significant. The TV-distance guarantee to fCI is especially valuable.

Significance
- CI testing is central to causal discovery, graphical models, and feature selection. A modular approach that leverages advances in classification is practically useful and likely to be adopted. The theoretical guarantees increase trust in the method, although the rate suffers from the dimension of Z.

Pros
- Simple, modular reduction enabling the use of powerful classifiers.
- Clear theoretical guarantees: TV closeness of the bootstrap to fCI and generalization bounds with near-i.i.d. data.
- Strong empirical performance and publicly available code.
- Computationally practical compared to methods requiring optimization (e.g., KCIPT).

Cons
- Type I error control and p-value calibration are not fully specified; τ is chosen heuristically.
- Performance and decisions may depend on classifier choice and hyperparameters; sensitivity analysis is limited.
- The TV bound degrades with dim(Z), reflecting a potential curse of dimensionality in the bootstrap step.
- Assumptions (smoothness, bounded Hessian/Lipschitz density) may be restrictive in practice.
- Presentation is somewhat repetitive; some algorithmic details could be more concise.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Discrete or mixed Z: Is there a discrete/mixed-data analogue? When Z is discrete, one could resample Y within Z-cells exactly from f(y|z), potentially simplifying the bootstrap and improving guarantees. Discuss this case and how to handle mixed variables (e.g., distance metrics, encoding).
- Classifier sensitivity: Please report robustness across classifiers (e.g., XGBoost, random forests, shallow/deep nets) and across hyperparameters. Do acceptance/rejection decisions change with reasonable hyperparameter variations? A sensitivity plot would be informative.
- Calibration and level-α testing: Provide a principled way to set τ or, better, compute p-values (e.g., repeated label permutations between U1 and U'2, multiple random splits/bootstrap replications, or cross-fitting) to control type I error. Comparing empirical type I error vs α would strengthen the paper.
- Choice of k in nearest neighbors: You use 1-NN. Would a small k-NN swap (with random neighbor among the k nearest) reduce variance or improve TV rates? An ablation varying k and approximate-NN search would be useful.
- Runtime and scalability: Include wall-clock comparisons vs KCIT/RCIT/KCIPT across n and dim(Z), and discuss memory/ANN indexing choices.
- Additional baselines: Consider CI via conditional mutual information estimation with modern kNN estimators, to broaden comparisons beyond kernel baselines.
- Practical guidance: Provide guidance on preprocessing Z (scaling/whitening), distance metrics, train/test split strategy, and the recommended number of bootstrap replications.

Minor comments
- Some repetition in Section 1.1; consider condensing the summary to avoid restating the pipeline multiple times.
- Algorithm 2 could be described succinctly in text or as a compact pseudocode block; the bias-corrected variant (Algorithm 3) is the more distinctive contribution.
- Typos: “classifier” (line ~157) and “graphs” (line ~280).

Overall assessment
A strong and timely contribution: theoretically grounded, simple to implement, and empirically effective. Addressing calibration/type I error, classifier sensitivity, and discussing discrete/mixed Z would further improve the paper’s clarity and practical impact.
