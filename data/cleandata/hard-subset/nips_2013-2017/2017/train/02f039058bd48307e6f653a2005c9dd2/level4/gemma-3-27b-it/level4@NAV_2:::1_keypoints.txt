- Reformulates continuous conditional independence testing as a binary classification problem using modern classifiers (GBDT, DNN).
- Proposes a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples from the fCI distribution with theoretical total-variation distance guarantees.
- Derives generalization bounds for classification in the non-i.i.d. setting and translates these into CI testing error bounds.
- Empirically validates the method on simulated and real datasets, showing performance gains over existing methods.
- Paper is well-motivated, clearly presented, and technically sound.
- Novel reduction from CI testing to classification is a compelling strategy.
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees (bootstrap accuracy and classifier generalization bounds).
- Experimental results are convincing and demonstrate practical effectiveness.
- Addresses a highly relevant problem with broad applications in causal discovery and feature selection.
- Choice of threshold parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is not well justified and may strongly affect performance.
- Method does not explicitly address asymmetry or causal direction between X and Y, leaving applicability unclear in causal settings.
- Behavior under weak dependence between X and Y given Z is not thoroughly analyzed or evaluated.
- Comparison to baselines could be more complete (e.g., combining NN bootstrap with kernel two-sample tests or permutation methods).
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically solid approach with strong theory and empirical support.
- Arguments against acceptance: sensitivity to τ, lack of causal-direction treatment, insufficient analysis for weak dependence.
- Overall recommendation: strong paper worth acceptance with minor revisions to address τ selection guidance, causal-direction implications, and weak-dependence evaluation.
