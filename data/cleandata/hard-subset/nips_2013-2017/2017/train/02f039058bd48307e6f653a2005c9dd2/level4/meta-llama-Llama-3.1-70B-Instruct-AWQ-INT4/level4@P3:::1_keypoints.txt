- Proposes using nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating f^{CI} and training a classifier to distinguish observed vs bootstrapped data for conditional independence testing.

- Authors derive finite-sample bounds on the distance between the NN-bootstrap distribution and f^{CI} (total variation) and on empirical classification risks.

- Major concern: It's unclear whether existing alternatives (e.g., permutation-based methods or kernel two-sample tests) could replace either the sample-generation or classification component and how their performance compares.

- Major concern: Performance is unclear when X and Y are only weakly dependent given Z — sensitivity/power in weak-dependence regimes is not addressed.

- Major concern: Method may be asymmetric in X and Y; causal directions (X→Y vs Y→X) could affect which variable should be sampled, but this is not considered.

- Major concern: The choice and tuning of parameter τ in Algorithms 2 and 3 is unclear and needs further investigation.

- Minor issue: Empirical risk in Algorithms 2 and 3 should be normalized by sample size.

- Strength: Introduces a novel combination of nearest-neighbor bootstrap and classification for CI testing.

- Strength: Provides theoretical bounds on closeness to f^{CI} and on empirical risks.

- Strength: Paper is well-presented and easy to follow.

- Weakness: May lack power or correctness when X and Y exhibit weak conditional dependence.

- Weakness: Ambiguity around selecting the τ parameter reduces reproducibility and applicability.

- Weakness: Potential asymmetry with respect to X and Y could limit applicability in causal settings.

- Weakness: Does not address causal interpretation or the need to choose which variable to sample under different causal models.

- Originality: Novel methodological combination and new theoretical bounds on NN-bootstrap approximation to f^{CI}.

- Significance: Addresses an important CI testing problem with potential broad applicability.

- Clarity: Exposition and explanations are clear and accessible.

- Quality: Solid theoretical contribution but requires further clarification and empirical comparisons to address raised concerns.
