- Proposal: Reframe nonparametric conditional independence testing as a binary classification task using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).  
- Decision rule: Train a classifier to distinguish real vs. bootstrapped samples; near-chance accuracy accepts the null, high accuracy rejects it.  
- Theoretical support: Proved bounds on total variation between bootstrapped samples and true f_CI (Theorem 1).  
- Generalization theory: Derived classification risk/generalization bounds for “near-i.i.d.” samples (Theorem 2), a standalone contribution.  
- Empirical results: CCIT outperforms kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially with high-dimensional conditioning variable Z.  
- Strength — framework: Reduction to supervised classification is elegant and leverages powerful models suited to high-dimensional data.  
- Strength — theory: Strong, relevant theoretical guarantees justify the bootstrap and learning-based test.  
- Strength — empirical: Convincing experimental gains over state-of-the-art, notably as dim(Z) increases.  
- Weakness — component analysis: Needs ablation to separate contributions of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap vs. using a classifier (e.g., compare to permutation-based sample generation or applying kernel two-sample tests on bootstrap samples).  
- Weakness — sensitivity to weak dependence: Unclear power when conditional dependence is weak (small but nonzero TV distance); need quantification vs. other methods.  
- Weakness — asymmetry: Bootstrap resamples Y conditioned on Z (not symmetric in X and Y); unclear whether swapping roles affects results or has theoretical/practical implications.  
- Weakness — threshold selection: Choice of threshold τ (set to 1/√n in experiments) lacks a principled/adaptive procedure to control Type I error; suggest discussion or permutation-based selection.
