- Reframes conditional independence testing X ⟂ Y | Z as a binary classification problem distinguishing f(x,y,z) vs f_CI(x,y,z)=f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z).
- Constructs positive class from real joint samples and negative class from synthetic samples approximating f_CI.
- Generates synthetic f_CI samples via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap using only samples from f.
- Theorem 1: under smoothness of f(y|z), the bootstrap distribution is provably close to f_CI in total variation distance.
- Theorem 2: provides novel generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the near-i.i.d. bootstrapped data, linking classifier error to the distance between f and f_CI.
- Leverages powerful, flexible classifiers (gradient-boosted trees, neural nets), enabling a modular, model-powered CI test.
- Empirical results show strong performance gains over kernel-based CI tests, especially as conditioning dimension Z increases.
- Strength: identifies and rigorously justifies the smoothness requirement for the bootstrap approximation (novel theoretical contribution).
- Strength: develops a non-trivial generalization analysis for non-i.i.d. training data that is of independent interest.
- Weakness: paper presentation is dense and lacks intuitive explanations connecting assumptions and bounds to practice.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of how classifier expressiveness (function class G) affects excess risk η and thus the test’s power.
