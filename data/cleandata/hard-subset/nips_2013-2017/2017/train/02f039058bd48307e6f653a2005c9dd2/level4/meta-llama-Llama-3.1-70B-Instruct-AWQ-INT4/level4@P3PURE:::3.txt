The authors propose a general test for conditional independence between three continuous variables, without relying on strong parametric assumptions. Instead, they devise a method that leverages samples from the joint distribution to create samples that closely approximate the conditional product distribution in terms of total variation distance. These samples are then fed into a binary classifier, such as a deep neural network, which is trained on samples from both distributions. The predictive error of the classifier is subsequently used to either accept or reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of their proposed method to existing approaches in the literature, both through descriptive analysis and experimental results on synthetic and real data.

Conditional independence testing is a crucial tool in modern machine learning, particularly in the domain of causal inference, where assessing claims of unconfoundedness is a common endeavor. The proposed method appears to be both straightforward and powerful, making it a viable candidate for numerous causal applications.

The paper is well-written, with the authors doing an excellent job of summarizing theoretical results and making the methodology accessible to practitioners. While the details of the theoretical results and proofs can be challenging to follow, the authors provide a clear and concise overview of their approach.

The most impressive aspect of the paper is its theoretical results. Although the proposed method is an extension of the approach for testing independence presented in "Revisiting Classifier Two-Sample Tests" (https://arxiv.org/pdf/1610.06545.pdf), as acknowledged by the authors, the guarantees provided by the theoretical results are noteworthy. The authors modify the test to incorporate a third variable Z and, after matching neighbors of Z, the methods become similar.

A few additional comments are worth noting:

* Is there a discrete analog of the proposed method? The bootstrap becomes even more powerful when Z is discrete, suggesting potential benefits in this context.
* How sensitive is the test to the choice of classification method? It would be useful to investigate whether a few changes to hyperparameters can lead to different acceptance and rejection results, alongside the simulations provided.
* Section 1.1 of the Main Contributions appears redundant, as the authors summarize their approach multiple times, making the extent of the repetition unnecessary.
* Writing out Algorithm 2 seems unnecessary, as this process can be described more succinctly and has already been discussed in the paper.
* There are a few minor typos: "classifier" should be used in line 157, and "graphs" in line 280.
