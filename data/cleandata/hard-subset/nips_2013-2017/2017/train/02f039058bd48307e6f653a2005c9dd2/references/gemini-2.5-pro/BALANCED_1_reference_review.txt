### Summary

This paper introduces a novel, model-powered approach for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, termed Classifier CI Test (CCIT). The core idea is to reduce the statistical hypothesis test of H0: X ⊥ Y | Z to a binary classification problem. The authors propose a pipeline where, given samples from the joint distribution p(X,Y,Z), a second set of samples is generated to approximate the conditionally independent distribution p_CI(x,y,z) = p(x|z)p(y|z)p(z). This is achieved via a clever "nearest-neighbor bootstrap" procedure: for each sample (x, y, z), its nearest neighbor is found in a separate hold-out set based on the Z coordinate, and the corresponding Y-value is swapped. The original samples are then labeled as class 1, and the bootstrapped samples as class 0. A powerful classifier (e.g., XGBoost) is trained to distinguish between these two classes. If the classifier achieves an accuracy significantly greater than 0.5, the original and bootstrapped distributions are deemed distinguishable, and H0 is rejected. Otherwise, H0 is not rejected.

This work builds on the recent idea of using classifiers for two-sample testing, particularly for unconditional independence [19], and extends it to the more complex conditional case. The key challenge, generating samples from p_CI via a Z-dependent permutation, is elegantly addressed by the nearest-neighbor bootstrap. This contrasts with prior art like KCIPT [10], which requires solving an expensive linear program. The main contributions are: (1) the reduction framework itself, (2) the nearest-neighbor bootstrap algorithm, (3) a theoretical proof that the bootstrapped distribution converges to p_CI in total variation distance, and (4) novel generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the resulting "near-i.i.d." samples. The method is validated empirically, showing superior performance over state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially as the dimensionality of the conditioning set Z increases.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The central idea of reducing CI testing to a binary classification task via a simple and efficient bootstrap is highly original and significant. It provides a bridge between classical statistical testing and the modern supervised learning toolkit. This "model-powered" approach allows the CI test to directly leverage the power and scalability of state-of-the-art classifiers, which are well-suited for high-dimensional and complex data. Any future advances in classification technology could directly translate to improvements in this CI test, making the framework very impactful and forward-looking.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides solid theoretical justification for its approach. Theorem 1, which bounds the total variation distance between the distribution of the bootstrapped samples and the target conditionally independent distribution, is the theoretical cornerstone of the method. While the rate O(n^(-1/dz)) exhibits a curse of dimensionality, it formally establishes the validity of the bootstrap procedure. Furthermore, Theorem 2 provides a novel and important analysis of classifier generalization bounds in a non-i.i.d setting, which is of independent interest. This demonstrates a careful and thorough analysis of the proposed pipeline.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing. In synthetic experiments, CCIT significantly outperforms established kernel-based methods as the dimension of the conditioning variable Z grows. This is a crucial advantage, as high-dimensional conditioning sets are common in practice (e.g., in causal discovery). The application to the flow-cytometry dataset demonstrates the method's practical utility on real-world data, where it again shows a performance edge.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The pipeline is explained lucidly with the help of Figure 1 and detailed pseudocode for each component (Algorithms 1-3). The bias-corrected version of the algorithm (CCITv2) is a thoughtful addition that addresses a key practical challenge of the method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** There is a notable discrepancy between the theoretical convergence rate in Theorem 1 (O(n^(-1/dz))), which suggests poor performance in high dimensions of Z, and the excellent empirical results for dz up to 70. The paper acknowledges this but does not offer a deep discussion. It would be beneficial to explore why this gap exists. Is it because the data in the experiments has an intrinsically lower-dimensional manifold structure that the nearest-neighbor search effectively exploits? Or is it that the powerful classifiers are exceptionally robust to the noise introduced by the imperfect bootstrap, a phenomenon not captured by the worst-case theoretical bounds? A more detailed discussion on this point would strengthen the paper's conclusions.

2.  **Test Calibration and Practical Use:** The method relies on a threshold `τ` to make a decision. For a hypothesis test to be practically useful, it needs a principled way to control the Type I error rate at a desired significance level (e.g., α = 0.05). The paper uses `τ = 1/√n` in experiments, which is a heuristic based on variance. However, a more formal calibration procedure (e.g., via permutation of the class labels to generate a null distribution for the test statistic) is needed to allow users to specify a desired α-level. The paper reports ROC AUC scores, which is common for evaluating classifiers, but for a hypothesis test, presenting power analysis at a fixed Type I error rate would be more standard and informative.

3.  **Sensitivity to Classifier Choice:** The method's performance is intrinsically linked to the choice and tuning of the underlying classifier. This is both a strength (flexibility) and a potential weakness. A highly complex classifier could potentially "overfit" to artifacts of the bootstrap process rather than the true conditional dependence structure, possibly inflating the Type I error rate. While CCITv2 is designed to mitigate some of this, the paper would benefit from a sensitivity analysis regarding the choice of classifier (e.g., comparing XGBoost to a simpler model like logistic regression or a different one like a neural network) and its hyperparameters.

### Constructive Suggestions

*   To address the theory-practice gap, consider adding a discussion section conjecturing why the method works well for high dz. For instance, you could hypothesize about the role of manifold learning by the NN search or the robustness of tree-based ensembles.
*   To improve the practical utility of the test, I strongly recommend adding a section on how to calibrate the test to achieve a user-specified significance level α. A simple and effective method would be to generate a null distribution for the test statistic (`ˆL(ĝ', De') - ˆL(ĝ, De)`) by permuting the class labels (0/1) multiple times after the dataset D has been generated, re-training and re-evaluating the classifiers for each permutation. The (1-α) quantile of this empirical null distribution can then serve as the decision threshold.
*   The logic in Algorithm 3, Line 6 (`ˆL(ĝ, De) < ˆL(ĝ', De') - τ`), seems to imply rejecting H0 if the general classifier is *better* than the bias-correcting one by a margin `τ`. This should be written more clearly as `ˆL(ĝ', De') - ˆL(ĝ, De) > τ`. Please clarify this.
*   A brief discussion of the computational complexity of the NN-bootstrap step and how it compares to the baselines (e.g., KCIT's eigenvalue decomposition, RCIT's random features) would be a useful addition.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting an exciting and powerful new direction for conditional independence testing. The core idea is elegant, the theoretical and empirical support is strong, and the potential for impact is high. The weaknesses identified are primarily areas for further refinement rather than fundamental flaws.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and significant contribution connecting CI testing with modern supervised learning.
*   Strong theoretical analysis, including novel generalization bounds for a non-i.i.d. setting.
*   Empirically outperforms strong baselines, especially in the challenging high-dimensional conditioning setting.
*   The method is flexible and can benefit from future advances in classification models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Notable gap between the pessimistic theoretical rates and strong empirical performance is not fully explained.
*   The practical procedure for setting the decision threshold to control Type I error is not fully developed, which is a key aspect for a hypothesis test.

Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper are substantial. It introduces a valuable new tool and a promising research direction. I recommend acceptance.
