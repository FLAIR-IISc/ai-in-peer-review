- Summary: Proposes a model-powered CI test reducing CI testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate f_CI and a classifier to distinguish original vs. bootstrap samples.

- Novel approach: Extends classification-based independence testing to conditional independence with a theoretically grounded bootstrap.

- Theoretical contributions: Theorem 1 proves the bootstrap approximates f_CI in total variation; Theorem 2 gives generalization bounds for classification under near-independence.

- Empirical validation: Outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic and flow-cytometry data, notably in higher-dimensional settings.

- Practical implementation: Algorithm is simple and integrates with standard classification tools, facilitating adoption.

- Theoretical-practical gap: Empirical high-dimensional performance contradicts the weak 1/n^(1/d_z) bound in Theorem 1 and lacks a convincing theoretical explanation.

- Bias correction justification: Algorithm 3’s bias correction (subtracting loss of a classifier on Y,Z) appears ad hoc and lacks theoretical support.

- Limited comparison with recent work: Insufficient comparison to newer or more efficient methods (e.g., RCIT, kernel-based methods) and missing computational complexity analysis.

- Assumption strength: Guarantees rely on bounded Lipschitz f(z), a strong assumption whose practical restrictiveness is unclear.

- Critical praise: Nearest-neighbor bootstrap is clever and computationally efficient versus permutation-based KCIPT.

- Critical concern: Main weakness is failing to explain why the method works well in practice despite theoretical limitations, especially for d_z > 10.

- Bias correction concern: No clear rationale why a classifier on Y,Z provides a valid bias estimate, making that component unprincipled.

- Comparison critique: Correctly highlights KCIPT but underplays RCIT and efficient approximations; calls for deeper empirical and complexity comparisons.

- Overall assessment: Strong, novel, and non-trivial work with compelling experiments; recommend accept with minor revisions.

- Key revisions requested: (1) Theoretically explain strong high-dimensional performance, (2) justify bias correction in Algorithm 3, (3) add computational complexity analysis and compare thoroughly with RCIT, (4) clarify practical implications of the bounded Lipschitz assumption.
