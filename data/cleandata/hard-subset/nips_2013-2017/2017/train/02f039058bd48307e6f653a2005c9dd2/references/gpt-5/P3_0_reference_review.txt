Summary
The paper proposes a model-powered approach to nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. Given i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method constructs a “negative” sample approximating the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x, y, z) = fX|Z fY|Z fZ via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap: for each (x, z) from one split, replace y with the y of its 1-NN in Z from another split. A classifier is then trained to distinguish original samples from the bootstrapped ones; if it succeeds beyond a threshold, reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. A bias-corrected variant subtracts the performance of a classifier trained only on (Y, Z) to account for finite-sample artifacts in the bootstrap. Theoretical results include (i) a total variation (TV) bound showing the bootstrap sample distribution is O(n^{-1/dz})-close to fCI under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z); and (ii) generalization bounds for ERM under “near-i.i.d.” dependence induced by the bootstrap. Empirically, the method (with XGBoost) outperforms KCIT and RCIT on post-nonlinear synthetic data as dz increases and shows improved ROC-AUC on flow cytometry CI relations derived from several causal graphs.

Relation to prior work
- Kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT, KCI-permutation) are the main comparators; the paper cites and contrasts with these, especially with the permutation-based KCIPT, which uses a more expensive LP. The presented bootstrap is simpler and is analyzed in TV distance.
- Classifier two-sample tests are leveraged in the independence case (Lopez-Paz & Oquab), and this paper extends the idea to CI via a Z-conditional surrogate using nearest neighbors.
- Nearest-neighbor techniques for density/entropy estimation (Gao et al.) inform the analysis assumptions; non-i.i.d. generalization builds on Rademacher complexity under dependence (Mohri & Rostamizadeh).

Strengths
- Conceptually clean reduction: CI testing becomes a two-sample test with off-the-shelf classifiers. This is attractive in practice and potentially benefits from rapid ML advances.
- Simple, efficient bootstrap: 1-NN in Z is computationally light and easy to implement. The three-way sample split avoids data re-use across classes.
- Nontrivial theory: The TV bound giving proximity to fCI is a substantial and relevant guarantee; the “near-independence” generalization analysis is thoughtful and of independent interest.
- Empirical performance: Strong ROC-AUC on synthetic PNL models as dz grows, and competitive performance on real data (flow cytometry), with an implemented package.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Curse of dimensionality: The O(n^{-1/dz}) TV rate and dependence of the theory on dz suggest severe degradation as dim(Z) grows. Although the authors claim better practical behavior with strong classifiers, the generation step is fundamentally local in Z and susceptible to high-dimensional nearest-neighbor issues (distance concentration, ineffective kd-trees).
- Lack of calibrated Type I error control: The test is presented as thresholding accuracy (or the bias-corrected difference), with τ heuristics (e.g., 1/√n). There is no formal level-α guarantee nor a way to compute p-values. This is a key limitation compared to permutation-based or asymptotically valid kernel tests.
- Bounds may be loose or vacuous in practice: The generalization bounds depend on the VC dimension V; for deep nets/boosted trees V can be very large, yielding vacuous bounds. The constants and G(ε) terms are opaque and leave little actionable guidance for finite samples.
- Assumptions and clarity: Assumption 1 (generalized curvature matrix) is nonstandard and hard to parse; intuition and examples of distributions satisfying it would help. The dependence on unknown constants (e.g., cdz) and on G(ε) complicates practical interpretation.
- Evaluation breadth: Synthetic data focuses on a single PNL family; additional regimes (linear-Gaussian, heteroskedastic/noisy, additive noise with varying strength, non-smooth conditionals) would strengthen claims. No study of discrete/mixed Z, which is common in practice. An ablation on classifier choice, k in k-NN (k>1), and the effect of B bootstraps is missing.
- Calibration and fairness: Comparisons are done via ROC-AUC; while useful, most practitioners need level-controlled tests. Hyperparameter tuning fairness (KCIT/RCIT vs. XGBoost) could be discussed. The flow cytometry “ground truth” is acknowledged as imperfect; more datasets would be welcome.

Quality
Technically competent, with a meaningful theoretical contribution and a solid empirical demonstration. However, the statistical testing perspective (control of false positives) is underdeveloped; the paper presents a powerful score but not a calibrated test.

Clarity
Generally well organized and readable, but several equations contain formatting issues and the key assumptions require clearer intuition and examples. The algorithmic data splits and bootstrap should be described more consistently (Algorithm 1 vs. its use in Algorithm 2/3). Explicit pseudocode for the bias-corrected testing decision and its intended τ calibration would help.

Originality
The reduction of CI testing to classification via a novel nearest-neighbor conditional bootstrap and the associated TV and generalization analyses are original and timely. The approach differs substantively from kernel CI tests and from existing permutation-based CI bootstraps.

Significance
High potential impact in practice due to flexibility and ease of use; bridges modern ML with classical CI testing. If issues of calibration and high-dimensional Z can be mitigated, this could be widely adopted.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a level-α version: e.g., compute a permutation distribution of the accuracy statistic by repeatedly shuffling the labels between U1 and U02, or use cross-fitting to build a studentized statistic, yielding p-values and FWER control.
- Analyze and/or use k-NN with k>1 and local weighting; report sensitivity to k. Consider adaptive neighborhood selection or metric learning in Z to mitigate high-dimensional issues.
- Add experiments covering broader data-generating mechanisms (including discrete/mixed Z) and stress-test as dz and n vary.
- Offer intuition and examples for Assumption 1 and bounds in Theorem 1; discuss constants and practical regimes where the bounds are informative.
- Include ablations: classifier choice, depth/trees for XGBoost, B, and train/test splitting strategies; report runtime scaling for the bootstrap in higher dz.
- Consider cross-fitting to decouple bootstrap generation from both training and testing folds, reducing dependence and bias.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, powerful idea with promising empirical gains and a usable package.
- Theoretical guarantees tailored to the proposed bootstrap and near-i.i.d. setting.
- Potentially influential in bringing ML reductions to CI testing.

Arguments against acceptance
- No calibrated test with controlled Type I error; thresholding accuracy is ad hoc.
- Strong dz dependence in theory; nearest-neighbor step may be fragile in high-dimensional Z.
- Limited experimental breadth and ablations; assumptions are opaque.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
