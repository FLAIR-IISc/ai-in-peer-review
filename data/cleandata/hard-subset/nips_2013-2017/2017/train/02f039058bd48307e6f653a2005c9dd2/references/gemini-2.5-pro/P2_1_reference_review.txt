### Summary

This paper introduces a novel method for non-parametric Conditional Independence (CI) testing, termed CCIT (Classifier CI Test). The core idea is to reduce the statistical hypothesis testing problem of whether X is independent of Y given Z (X ⊥ Y | Z) to a binary classification task. The authors propose a "nearest-neighbor bootstrap" procedure to generate a synthetic dataset that approximates the distribution under the null hypothesis (i.e., the distribution where X and Y are conditionally independent given Z). Specifically, for each sample (x, y, z) in a hold-out set, they find a nearest neighbor (x', y', z') based on the Z-coordinate from another set and swap the y-coordinates to create a new sample (x, y', z). The original data is labeled '1' and the synthetic data is labeled '0'. A powerful binary classifier (e.g., XGBoost) is then trained to distinguish between these two datasets. If the classifier achieves an accuracy significantly better than random guessing (0.5), the null hypothesis is rejected.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) The reduction methodology itself, which allows leveraging advances in supervised learning for statistical testing. (2) A theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) showing that the distribution of the bootstrapped samples converges to the true conditionally independent distribution in total variation distance. (3) Novel generalization bounds for the classifier (Theorem 2) in the challenging non-i.i.d. setting created by the bootstrap procedure. (4) Strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating superior performance over existing kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially when the conditioning variable Z is high-dimensional.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of reducing CI testing to classification is highly original and significant. It connects a fundamental problem in statistics and causal inference to the powerful and rapidly evolving machinery of supervised learning. This approach allows practitioners to directly use state-of-the-art, off-the-shelf classifiers, potentially leading to immediate performance gains. This "model-powered" philosophy is a promising research direction.

2.  **Technical Quality (Theoretical Soundness):** The paper is not merely a heuristic proposal; it is well-supported by theory.
    *   **Justification of Bootstrap:** Theorem 1 provides a formal guarantee that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure generates samples that are asymptotically close to the target null distribution. This is a crucial and non-trivial result that forms the foundation of the method.
    *   **Generalization under Near-Independence:** The analysis leading to Theorem 2 is a key technical contribution. Standard learning theory results rely on i.i.d. data, a condition violated by the bootstrap process. The authors provide a novel analysis of Rademacher complexity under a "near-independence" condition, which is of independent interest and strengthens the paper's theoretical contribution.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing. The method (CCIT) demonstrates a clear and substantial advantage over established kernel-based methods, particularly as the dimensionality of the conditioning set Z increases (Figure 2a). This is a critical scenario in many applications, such as causal discovery, where conditioning sets can become large. The application to the flow cytometry dataset further showcases its utility on real-world problems.

4.  **Practicality and Thoughtfulness:** The authors propose a bias-corrected version of their algorithm (CCITv2). This shows careful consideration of the practical limitations of the method (i.e., the classifier might learn biases from the bootstrap procedure itself, even under the null). This refined algorithm, which compares the performance of a classifier with and without access to X, is clever and makes the method more robust.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** There is a significant disconnect between the theoretical convergence rates and the observed empirical performance. The bound on the total variation distance in Theorem 1 scales as O(n⁻¹/ᵈᶻ), which suffers severely from the curse of dimensionality. For a Z of dimension 70 (as in the experiments), this theoretical rate is extremely slow. Yet, empirically, the method works exceptionally well. The authors acknowledge this as a "worst case" analysis but do not provide sufficient intuition for why the practical performance is so much better. A deeper discussion on whether the classifiers are implicitly exploiting some low-dimensional manifold structure in Z, or if the smoothness assumptions are overly pessimistic, would greatly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Clarity on Assumptions:** The smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) are quite technical. While necessary for the proofs, the paper would benefit from a more intuitive discussion of what these assumptions mean and how restrictive they are in practice. For a practitioner, it is unclear when these conditions might be violated and what the implications would be.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The paper highlights the efficiency of the bootstrap due to fast nearest-neighbor algorithms. However, a formal discussion of the computational complexity is missing. The nearest-neighbor search for all n samples in a dataset of size n takes O(n² d_z) time naively, or roughly O(n log(n) d_z) using k-d trees. This should be explicitly stated and compared to the complexity of competing methods like KCIT (O(n³)) and RCIT (O(n²)). While likely favorable, this analysis is an important part of the overall evaluation.

4.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces at least one key hyperparameter: the threshold `τ`. The bias-corrected CCITv2 cleverly mitigates the need to set a bias-related threshold, but the final decision still depends on `τ`. The paper mentions setting `τ = 1/√n` in experiments, but a more thorough discussion or sensitivity analysis regarding this choice, and the hyperparameters of the underlying classifier (e.g., the depth or number of trees in XGBoost), would improve the paper's practical guidance and reproducibility.

### Conclusion

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, elegant, and powerful approach to conditional independence testing. The reduction to binary classification is a significant conceptual contribution that opens up new avenues for research. The method is supported by solid theoretical analysis and backed by impressive empirical results that demonstrate a clear advance over the state of the art, especially in challenging high-dimensional settings.

While there is a notable gap between the pessimistic theoretical rates and the strong empirical performance, and some practical aspects like computational complexity could be elaborated upon, these are not fatal flaws. The strengths—originality, technical depth, and empirical superiority—far outweigh the weaknesses. This work is likely to be highly influential and widely used by both researchers and practitioners.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
