This paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing, framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea – leveraging the power of modern classifiers to discriminate between the joint distribution and a conditionally independent surrogate – is a compelling and potentially impactful shift. The authors propose a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution and provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of this approximation. This is a bold and interesting direction, and I find the approach promising despite some areas needing further development.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem is a genuinely new perspective. While classification *has* been used for independence testing (as the authors acknowledge), extending this to the *conditional* case with a principled bootstrap method is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide theoretical analysis, including bounds on the total variational distance between the bootstrapped samples and the target distribution, and generalization bounds for the classifier. This adds credibility to the method.
*   **Potential for Scalability:** By leveraging existing, highly optimized classification algorithms, the proposed method has the potential to scale well to high-dimensional data, a common challenge in CI testing.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate promising performance, outperforming state-of-the-art methods, especially in high-dimensional settings. The analysis on the flow cytometry data provides a real-world validation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Bootstrap Assumptions:** The smoothness assumptions required for the theoretical guarantees (Assumption 1 & 2) may be restrictive. The sensitivity of the method to violations of these assumptions needs further investigation.
*   **Bias Correction Complexity:** Algorithm 3, the bias-corrected version, adds complexity. The practical benefit of this correction, and its robustness to parameter tuning (⌧), is not fully clear from the presentation.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The synthetic experiments, while helpful, rely on specific data generation processes. More diverse and challenging synthetic datasets would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Theoretical Tightness:** While the theoretical results are valuable, the constants and rates involved (e.g., 1/n<sup>1/dz</sup>) are potentially loose and might obscure the practical behavior of the method. A deeper discussion of these aspects would be useful.
*   **Implementation Details:** While the code is available, a more detailed explanation of key implementation choices (e.g., the specific XGBoost parameters used) would improve reproducibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a conceptually novel and potentially impactful approach to CI testing.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the method.
*   The empirical results demonstrate promising performance.
*   The potential for scalability is a significant advantage.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong theoretical assumptions might limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The bias correction algorithm adds complexity without a clear demonstration of significant benefit.
*   The experiments could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of CI testing. The novelty of the approach, combined with the theoretical guarantees and promising empirical results, outweighs the existing weaknesses. While further refinement and more extensive evaluation would be beneficial, the paper presents a compelling and potentially transformative idea. I lean strongly towards acceptance. The authors are tackling a core problem in statistical inference with a fresh, model-powered perspective and I am excited to see this line of work developed further.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I encourage the authors to address the reviewers’ comments regarding the assumptions and robustness of the bias correction in a revised version. A more detailed discussion of the theoretical constants and rates would also be beneficial.
