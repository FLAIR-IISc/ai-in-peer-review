

# Peer Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) that generates samples close to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI, which they then use to create a binary classification problem. Under the null hypothesis (X ⊥ Y | Z), the original samples (labeled 1) should be indistinguishable from the bootstrap samples (labeled 0), while under the alternative hypothesis, the classifier should achieve high accuracy. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of their bootstrap samples (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), along with extensive empirical evaluations showing superior performance compared to state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT).

The paper relates well to recent work in the field, particularly the classification-based independence testing approach of [19] and kernel-based CI methods like KCIT [32] and RCIT [28]. The key innovation is addressing the conditional case (vs. unconditional independence testing) with a practical, theoretically grounded bootstrap approach, rather than relying on computationally expensive permutations as in KCIPT [10].

## Strengths

1. **Novel Approach to a Challenging Problem**: The paper successfully addresses the challenging problem of CI testing by reducing it to binary classification, a powerful framework that leverages advances in supervised learning. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field.

2. **Strong Theoretical Guarantees**: The authors provide rigorous theoretical results (Theorem 1 and Theorem 2) that justify their approach. The characterization of the total variation distance between the bootstrap samples and f_CI is particularly valuable. The generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2) are novel and of independent interest.

3. **Effective Empirical Validation**: The empirical results are thorough, including both synthetic experiments (Fig. 2a) and a real-world application to protein signaling networks (Fig. 2b, Table 2c). The method consistently outperforms state-of-the-art competitors, especially as the dimension of Z increases, which aligns with the theoretical motivation.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a clear, efficient algorithm (CCITv2) with bias correction that is computationally feasible, leveraging fast nearest-neighbor algorithms and existing classification tools like XGBoost.

5. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and effectively uses figures and pseudocode to explain the approach. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical implementation is well-maintained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical-Practical Discrepancy**: The theoretical results (Theorem 1) suggest that the performance should degrade with increasing dimension of Z (d_z), but the empirical results show excellent performance even at high dimensions (Fig. 2a). While the authors acknowledge this in the introduction, it would be beneficial to provide a more detailed discussion of why the empirical results outperform the theoretical bounds.

2. **Bias Correction Justification**: The bias correction in Algorithm 3 (using ĝ0 trained on Y,Z coordinates) is somewhat ad-hoc. The authors claim that "the classifier is expected to distinguish between the two data-sets only based on the Y,Z coordinates," but the justification for why this particular approach works well is not fully developed. A more rigorous analysis of the bias correction mechanism would strengthen the paper.

3. **Comparison with Recent Methods**: The paper compares with KCIT [32] and RCIT [28], but does not include the most recent methods like the permutation-based kernel conditional independence test [10] (which the authors cite) or the more recent causal discovery methods [22, 26]. While KCIPT is mentioned, it would be helpful to have a direct comparison with this method.

4. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper mentions the efficiency of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap, it doesn't provide a detailed computational complexity analysis of the proposed method compared to existing approaches. This would be valuable for practitioners.

5. **Assumptions in Theoretical Guarantees**: The smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) are reasonable but somewhat restrictive. It would be useful to discuss the practical implications of these assumptions and whether they are commonly satisfied in real-world applications.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- Novel approach that successfully reduces CI testing to binary classification
- Strong theoretical guarantees with well-motivated assumptions
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing superior performance
- Practical, efficient implementation that leverages existing classification tools
- Well-written and clearly organized paper

**Against Acceptance:**
- Theoretical results have a dimensionality dependence that isn't fully reflected in empirical results
- Bias correction mechanism is somewhat heuristic without rigorous justification
- Limited comparison with the most recent methods in the field
- Some assumptions may be restrictive for real-world applications

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The core idea of using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to convert CI testing into binary classification is novel and well-executed. The theoretical results provide solid justification for the approach, and the empirical evaluation convincingly demonstrates its superiority over existing methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with practical relevance to causal inference, Bayesian networks, and feature selection. While there are some minor weaknesses (particularly regarding the theoretical-practical discrepancy and the bias correction mechanism), these do not substantially undermine the paper's contributions.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a meaningful advancement in the field of conditional independence testing, with both theoretical and empirical contributions that are well-supported and significant. The approach is practical, generalizable, and has clear potential for impact in causal inference and machine learning applications.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 3 (Theoretical Results), include a more detailed discussion of why the empirical results outperform the theoretical bounds, particularly regarding the dimensionality dependence. This would strengthen the connection between theory and practice.

2. Provide a more rigorous justification for the bias correction mechanism in Algorithm 3. Consider adding a small theoretical analysis of the bias correction step.

3. Include a comparison with the KCIPT method [10] in the empirical evaluation, as it's the most directly comparable method in the literature.

4. Add a brief discussion of the practical implications of Assumptions 1 and 2 in real-world applications, and perhaps some discussion of how to check these assumptions in practice.

5. Provide a computational complexity analysis of the proposed method compared to existing approaches.

These suggestions are minor and would enhance an already strong paper, but they are not essential for acceptance. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and would be of interest to a broad audience in machine learning and statistics.
