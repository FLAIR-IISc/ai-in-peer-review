

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The method generates samples close to the conditionally independent distribution using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) and then uses classifiers to distinguish between these generated samples and the original data. The authors claim theoretical guarantees for their bootstrap procedure and empirical superiority over existing methods.

## Summary and Context

The paper builds on recent work using classification for independence testing (e.g., [19]), extending it to conditional independence. The key innovation is the nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution. The authors theoretically show that the bootstrap samples are close to the target distribution in total variation distance (Theorem 1), and provide generalization bounds for the classification procedure (Theorem 2).

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear connection between supervised learning and statistical testing, which is an interesting conceptual bridge. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach is novel in the context of CI testing, and the theoretical analysis provides some formal justification for the method. The empirical results on synthetic data and the flow-cytometry dataset show promise compared to KCIT and RCIT.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of rigorous validation against established baselines**: The paper claims CCIT outperforms KCIT and RCIT, but the experimental comparison is limited. The synthetic experiments only test under a specific post-nonlinear noise model, and the real-world dataset is a single application. The paper fails to compare with other state-of-the-art methods like KCIPT [10], which uses a similar permutation approach. The ROC AUC results shown in Figure 2a are not statistically significant (no p-values reported) and the sample size (300 datasets) is insufficient to draw strong conclusions.

2. **Theoretical guarantees are impractical**: Theorem 1 shows d_TV(, f_CI) = O(1/n^(1/dz)), which is extremely slow to converge as dimension dz increases. The authors acknowledge this in Section 1.1 but claim "in practice the performance with respect to dimension of Z scales much better than expected." However, they provide no theoretical justification or empirical evidence for this claim beyond a single experiment. For realistic dimensions (e.g., dz=10), this rate would require enormous sample sizes to achieve meaningful accuracy.

3. **The method is highly dependent on classifier choice**: The paper states "any improvements in the field of binary classification imply an advancement in our CI test," but fails to demonstrate robustness to classifier choice. The empirical results use XGBoost without comparing to other classifiers (e.g., random forests, SVMs) or showing how performance varies with different model architectures. This undermines the claim of methodological independence from classifier choice.

4. **Ad hoc bias correction**: Algorithm 3 introduces a bias correction that subtracts the loss of a classifier trained only on Y and Z coordinates. This is not theoretically justified and appears to be a workaround for the lack of strong theoretical guarantees under H0. The paper provides no justification for why this correction should work or how it affects Type I error control.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper makes no mention of how the method would handle discrete variables (the problem setting assumes continuous variables), nor does it address how the method scales to very high-dimensional Z (e.g., dz > 100), despite acknowledging the dimensionality problem in Theorem 1.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel approach to CI testing, but the claims of superiority over established methods are not sufficiently supported by rigorous theoretical analysis or thorough empirical validation. The theoretical guarantees are too weak to be practically useful, and the empirical results do not provide convincing evidence that the method reliably outperforms existing approaches across diverse data-generating processes.

The authors' reliance on classifier performance is a significant weakness. The method essentially "hides" the CI testing problem within a classification problem, but the performance of the overall method is entirely dependent on the classifier's ability to distinguish between the original and bootstrap samples. This is not a fundamental advance in CI testing but rather a clever application of existing classification techniques.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound in its specific implementation, the lack of rigorous validation against established baselines and the impractical theoretical guarantees prevent it from making a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their empirical evaluation (with more diverse datasets, statistical significance testing, and comparisons with more methods) and provide better theoretical justification for their claims before the paper would be suitable for NIPS. The method's dependence on classifier choice also makes it less appealing than more principled statistical approaches to CI testing.
