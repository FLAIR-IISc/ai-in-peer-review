Summary
The paper proposes a “model-powered” reduction of nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing X ⟂ Y | Z to binary classification. Given 3n samples, the authors: (i) hold out n originals (U1), (ii) apply a 1-NN “Y-swap” bootstrap on the remaining 2n (U2∪U3) to synthesize n samples intended to approximate fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z(x|z)fY|Z(y|z)fZ(z), and (iii) train a classifier to distinguish U1 (label 1) from the bootstrapped set (label 0). Good accuracy implies f ≠ fCI (reject H0), while chance-level accuracy implies failure to reject. A bias-corrected variant subtracts off a classifier trained on (Y,Z) only. Theoretical contributions: a TV-distance bound between the bootstrap distribution and fCI of order O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions; and generalization bounds for classification under “near-independent” samples. Empirically, the method (with XGBoost) outperforms KCIT/RCIT on synthetic datasets and on CI checks derived from flow cytometry networks.

Quality
Strengths
- Conceptually clean reduction leveraging modern classifiers; modular and potentially practical.
- Sensible sample-splitting to avoid trivial leakage via (X,Z) marginals; bias-corrected variant addresses residual YZ artifacts.
- Nontrivial analysis: a TV bound between the generated and CI distributions, and generalization bounds with dependent training data.
- Open-source implementation; empirical gains on selected benchmarks.

Weaknesses
- Lack of statistical calibration: No valid level-α test or p-values are provided. The threshold τ is heuristic (set to 1/√n in experiments), while the bias term scales as O(n^{-1/dz}), which decays much more slowly than 1/√n when dz>2. The bias-corrected variant compares test errors of two different classifiers trained on different feature spaces, which does not yield a well-calibrated test statistic or error control.
- Theory is fragile and pessimistic: the TV bound depends on strong smoothness (a nonstandard “generalized curvature matrix” for f(y|z), Hessian bounds for f(z), and a low-density mass function G(·)), and suffers the curse of dimensionality in dz. It guarantees closeness for a single generated sample, while the joint dependence structure across U′02 is only loosely handled, leading to very weak generalization rates with extraneous terms (e.g., (log n / n)^{1/3}).
- Algorithmic details insufficient: 1-NN in high-dimensional Z is unstable; no guidance on k>1, metric choice or normalization, or ANN indexing. The claim of efficiency via kd-trees is unrealistic in high dimensions. Tie-breaking, duplicate Zs, and sensitivity to Z scaling are not addressed.
- Experiments lack critical information and calibration: sample sizes n, precise hyperparameters (for XGBoost and baselines), and runtime/memory are missing. AUC-centric evaluation does not assess type-I error control. The flow cytometry evaluation constructs negative examples by asserting “edges are never CI given any conditioning set,” which is generally false in causal graphs with colliders/mediators; conditioning on arbitrary sets can induce or destroy dependence. It’s unclear which subsets (observational vs interventional) are used and whether pooling is appropriate. No sensitivity analyses (e.g., k in NN, choice of classifier class, τ, B) are provided.
- Clarity/formatting: Many notational inconsistencies and typos hinder readability (e.g., garbled math, undefined symbols, inconsistent use of dz/d z, “b(n)” undefined when first referenced, “KCIPT” naming, mismatched equation numbering). Several references are incomplete (missing years/pages) or imprecise. Algorithms omit practical details (distance metric, normalization, tie-breaking).

Clarity
The high-level idea is clear, but the paper needs substantial polishing to be reproducible: precise assumptions, complete statements with constants/quantifiers, rigorous definition of the “generalized curvature matrix,” and a step-by-step testing protocol with τ selection and p-value computation. Fix typos, unify notation, and complete references.

Originality
The reduction of CI testing to a labeled two-sample test via Z-conditional resampling is interesting, and the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a simple alternative to the LP-based permutation in prior work. The generalization analysis under near-i.i.d. samples is a useful angle, though the setting is close to existing dependencies/mixing analyses. Overall, a novel combination with some new technical lemmas.

Significance
If statistically calibrated and robust, a classifier-based CI test would be valuable. However, the current method lacks a principled way to control false positives and depends heavily on classifier choice and high-dimensional nearest-neighbor behavior. Theoretical rates are too pessimistic to explain empirical success, and the empirical section does not convincingly establish reliability across diverse regimes.

Pros
- Simple, modular algorithm; broadly applicable with off-the-shelf classifiers.
- Theoretical attempt to justify the bootstrap and learning under dependence.
- Promising empirical performance; open-source code.

Cons
- No valid level-α testing procedure or p-values; τ is heuristic and likely too small relative to bias for large dz.
- Strong, nonstandard, and insufficiently justified smoothness assumptions; curse-of-dimensionality rates.
- Limited and potentially flawed evaluation design; missing details, calibration, and sensitivity analyses.
- Numerous presentation/notation issues; incomplete references.
- Efficiency claims for NN search in high dimension are not substantiated.

Questions for authors
1) How do you control type-I error at a prescribed α? Can you provide a permutation/wild bootstrap calibration of the final statistic (e.g., cross-fitted, label-swapped within Z-neighborhoods) and report empirical size? 2) What are the exact n, B, and classifier hyperparameters? 3) How sensitive is power/type-I error to k in kNN, scaling of Z, and classifier class? 4) Can you compare to kernel MMD-based two-sample testing on your generated datasets to decouple the resampling from the classifier? 5) Please justify the negative examples in the flow-cytometry experiment and clarify data splits and intervention handling.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is appealing and potentially impactful, but the paper in its current form has too many methodological, theoretical, experimental, and presentation issues. A future version with rigorous calibration of the test, clearer theory with interpretable constants/assumptions, stronger and better-documented experiments, and improved clarity would be much more compelling.

Confidence
High (I am familiar with CI testing, resampling, and learning-theory generalization bounds).
