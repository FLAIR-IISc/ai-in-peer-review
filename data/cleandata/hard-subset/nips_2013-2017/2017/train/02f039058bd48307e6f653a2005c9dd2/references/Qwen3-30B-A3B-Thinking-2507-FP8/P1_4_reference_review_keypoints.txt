- Introduces a novel reduction of conditional independence testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate the conditionally independent distribution.

- Uses classifiers to distinguish original samples (label 1) from bootstrap samples (label 0), forming the core testing procedure.

- Theorem 1 provides a bound on total variation distance between bootstrap samples and f_CI, scaling as O(1/n^(1/dz)).

- Theorem 2 gives generalization bounds for classification under near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples.

- Empirical results show superiority over KCIT and RCIT on synthetic benchmarks and a real-world flow-cytometry dataset.

- Builds on classifier-based independence testing but extends it to the more challenging conditional case with theoretical guarantees.

- Technical contributions are sound and well-justified, with novel analysis of generalization in the non-i.i.d. setting.

- Empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating scalability with Z dimensionality and a real-world application to protein signaling networks.

- Paper is clearly written and well-organized, with easy-to-follow pseudocode for all algorithms.

- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap algorithm (Algorithm 1) is particularly clearly explained.

- The connection between theoretical bounds and empirical performance could be made more explicit.

- A simple illustrative example for why the bootstrap works in high-dimensional Z would improve clarity.

- The bootstrap approach is fundamentally different and simpler than permutation or linear-program-based methods like KCIPT.

- The work is significant for causal inference and Bayesian network learning, offering a scalable solution for high-dimensional Z.

- Strengths include the novel reduction, theoretical guarantees, strong empirical scaling, practical application, and clear exposition.

- Weaknesses include a possibly loose theoretical bound in practice, lack of treatment for discrete variables, and an ad-hoc bias correction in Algorithm 3.

- Additional weaknesses: limited discussion of computational complexity vs. kernel methods and limited connection to broader model-powered statistical trends.

- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address discrete-variable applicability and provide more detailed computational complexity analysis.

- Suggested future work: extend to discrete variables, compare complexity with kernel-based methods, and emphasize links between theoretical bounds and empirical performance.
