Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test (CCIT) that reduces CI testing to binary classification by generating a synthetic dataset that approximates samples from the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x, y, z) = fX|Z(x|z) fY|Z(y|z) fZ(z). Given 3n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method splits data, constructs a “nearest-neighbor bootstrap” by replacing Y of each point in one subset with the Y from its 1-NN in Z from the other subset, labels original vs. bootstrapped samples (1 vs. 0), trains a classifier, and uses held-out accuracy to decide whether to reject CI. A bias-corrected variant subtracts a classifier trained on (Y,Z) only to account for finite-sample dependence introduced by the bootstrap.

Theoretical contributions include: (i) a total variation (TV) bound showing that the distribution of a bootstrapped sample is close to fCI at rate O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions on f(z) and a generalized curvature condition on f(y|z), and (ii) generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization with “near-independent” samples arising from the bootstrap, expressed in terms of VC dimension and small deviations from i.i.d. The method is empirically compared to KCIT [Zhang et al., 2012; Sen et al., 2017] and RCIT [Strobl et al., 2017], and applied to the flow cytometry protein signaling dataset [Sachs et al., 2005]. The approach is related to classifier two-sample tests for independence [Lopez-Paz and Oquab, 2016] and to KCIPT [Doran et al., 2014], which uses a permutation found by solving a linear program; here, the NN bootstrap is simpler and accompanied by TV-distance guarantees.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and flexibility: reducing CI testing to standard supervised learning allows leveraging strong classifiers (e.g., XGBoost, DNNs) and efficient nearest-neighbor search.
- Theoretical guarantees: a nontrivial TV-distance guarantee for the bootstrapped distribution and generalization bounds under dependence induced by the bootstrap. The analysis of near-i.i.d samples for classification is of independent interest.
- Practical algorithmic design: bias-corrected variant (comparing classifiers with and without X) addresses finite-sample bias.
- Empirical performance: on synthetic post-nonlinear noise models with increasing dz, the method shows strong ROC AUC compared to KCIT/RCIT; the application to biological data demonstrates applicability.
- Relation to prior work is appropriately drawn, especially contrasting to KCIPT’s more expensive permutation scheme.

Weaknesses
- Control of Type I error and calibration: The procedure is framed as a classification-based decision using accuracy thresholds (τ) and ROC evaluation. There is no finite-sample or asymptotic control of test level (e.g., p-values or guaranteed size-α test). The choice of τ (e.g., 1/√n) is heuristic; the bias-correction helps but does not guarantee level control.
- Dependence on classifier and tuning: Power and even size can depend on the function class G, training protocol, and hyperparameters. There is limited guidance on how to select G and how sensitive results are to these choices. The theoretical bounds are in terms of VC dimension and may not reflect practical modern classifiers (ensembles, DNNs).
- High-dimensional conditioning: The TV bound scales as O(n^{-1/dz}), reflecting a curse of dimensionality in Z. While empirical results suggest robustness beyond the worst-case, there is no theoretical mitigation (e.g., manifold or low-intrinsic-dimension assumptions).
- Assumptions: The “generalized curvature” condition on f(y|z) and smoothness/integrability conditions on f(z) are relatively strong and somewhat nonstandard; intuition and examples are limited in the main text. The bound also assumes a bounded Lipschitz constant for f(z) in later results.
- Non-i.i.d. samples: Although the paper presents near-independence generalization bounds, the bootstrapped samples are dependent in a way that may vary with data geometry. The practical effect of dependence on test variability and calibration is not quantified.
- Experimental scope: X and Y are 1D in synthetic studies; there are no experiments with higher-dimensional X, Y or mixed discrete–continuous settings. Comparisons do not include KCIPT (even small-scale), and details about hyperparameter tuning for baselines are limited. The flow-cytometry evaluation relies on uncertain ground truth; negative examples are constructed heuristically.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction and bootstrap are well-motivated. Theorem 1 provides a clear asymptotic closeness result under explicit assumptions; Theorem 2 extends generalization theory to the near-i.i.d. setting. Proof details are deferred, but the statements are precise. The main gap is statistical calibration: the work does not provide a test with controlled size, nor a principled way to set τ, which is important for CI testing.
- Completeness: Algorithms (including bias correction) and assumptions are specified, and empirical evaluation is reasonably comprehensive for the chosen setting, though broader scenarios would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized with clear algorithms and a helpful narrative. Some notation (e.g., the generalized curvature matrix) could use more intuition and examples. Guidance on choosing τ, the classifier class G, k in k-NN (beyond 1-NN), and practical hyperparameter tuning is limited. A discussion of computational complexity and the effect of approximate nearest neighbors would help.

Originality
- The NN bootstrap to approximate fCI and the analysis of near-i.i.d classification are novel contributions. The reduction to classification itself is a natural extension of classifier two-sample tests; the closest prior (KCIPT) uses an optimization-based permutation, whereas this work provides a simple, analyzable alternative.

Significance
- The approach is promising in practice due to flexibility and performance, and the theoretical insights on near-i.i.d classification may be useful more broadly. The lack of level control limits immediate adoption as a statistical test in settings requiring calibrated p-values, but the method can serve as a powerful heuristic or be combined with calibration procedures.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide a calibration procedure to control Type I error (e.g., a permutation or conditional randomization scheme within Z-neighborhoods, or a data-splitting approach that estimates the null distribution of the accuracy gap).
- Analyze or at least empirically study the effect of k in k-NN (k>1) for the bootstrap; this might improve variance–bias trade-offs and dependence structure.
- Add sensitivity analyses: classifier choice (XGBoost vs. DNNs), hyperparameters, τ selection, number of bootstrap repetitions B, and approximate NN search.
- Expand experiments to higher-dimensional X and Y, mixed discrete–continuous variables, and scenarios with structured Z (e.g., manifold data).
- Discuss computational complexity and scalability explicitly, including the impact of dz on nearest-neighbor search and training.
- Clarify the generalized curvature assumption with examples where it holds, and compare to more standard smoothness conditions.
- If possible, include small-scale comparisons to KCIPT to contextualize the practical improvement over optimization-based permutations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, flexible reduction with strong empirical performance.
- Novel and technically interesting analysis (TV closeness; near-i.i.d generalization).
- Practical bias-corrected variant and public code.

Arguments against acceptance
- No formal level control; reliance on heuristic thresholds.
- Strong assumptions and unfavorable asymptotic rate in dz.
- Limited experimental scope (1D X,Y; no calibrated testing; few baselines in complex settings).
