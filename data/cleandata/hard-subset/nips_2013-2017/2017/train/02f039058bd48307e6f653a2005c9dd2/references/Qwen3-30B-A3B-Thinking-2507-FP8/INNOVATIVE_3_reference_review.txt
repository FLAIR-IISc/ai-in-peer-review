

# Review: Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem, leveraging supervised learning models to solve a statistical testing problem. The central innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap method that generates samples close to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x,y,z), which are then used in a binary classification task to distinguish between the original data and the bootstrap samples. This "model-powered" approach represents a significant conceptual shift from traditional kernel-based methods in CI testing.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) a novel reduction of CI testing to binary classification, (2) theoretical guarantees on the nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure showing d_TV(·, f_CI) = O(1/n^(1/dz)), (3) generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples, and (4) empirical evidence demonstrating superior performance over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a strong theoretical foundation supported by theorems and proofs. The approach is particularly compelling because it leverages advances in classification (e.g., gradient boosted trees, neural networks) to improve CI testing, creating a natural pipeline where improvements in classification directly benefit CI testing. This is a bold conceptual shift from traditional non-parametric CI testing methods like KCIT and RCIT, which rely on kernel-based approaches.

The novelty of this work is substantial. While binary classification has been used for independence testing (e.g., [19]), extending it to conditional independence with a proper bootstrap mechanism for generating f_CI samples is a significant advancement. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap technique provides a simple yet theoretically grounded way to simulate f_CI samples, avoiding the computational complexity of methods like KCIPT [10] that require solving linear programs. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, providing precise bounds on the variational distance between the bootstrap samples and f_CI, and extending generalization bounds to non-i.i.d settings.

The empirical results are convincing, with the proposed CCIT algorithm outperforming competitors across both synthetic experiments (with dimensionality of Z scaling up to 70) and the flow-cytometry dataset. The paper's observation that performance scales better in practice than the worst-case theoretical analysis suggests the method may be particularly valuable in real-world applications.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that the classifier's test error may have a small bias even under H0, which is addressed through a bias-corrected version (Algorithm 3). The theoretical results are properly contextualized against related work, including kernel-based methods [10, 28, 32] and recent classification-based approaches [19].

This paper represents a significant conceptual contribution to a well-studied problem. While the theoretical results involve some technical complexity, the core idea is elegant and the approach has clear potential for broader application in statistical testing. The authors successfully demonstrate that model-powered approaches can yield substantial improvements over traditional methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings where kernel-based methods struggle.

The paper's main weakness is that the theoretical analysis contains many technical details that might be challenging for some readers to follow. However, as a reviewer who values novelty over methodological perfection, I believe this minor issue is outweighed by the paper's significant contribution. The work provides a new direction for CI testing that could influence future research, and the empirical results validate the theoretical claims.

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS as it introduces a novel approach with strong theoretical and empirical results, addressing a fundamental problem with broad applications in causal inference, Bayesian networks, and feature selection. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a new framework for CI testing that leverages advances in supervised learning.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a model-powered approach to CI testing that reduces the problem to binary classification. The theoretical guarantees, empirical results, and conceptual novelty make it an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
