

# Peer Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing, reducing the problem to binary classification. The core insight is to generate samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution f_CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1), then train a classifier to distinguish between original samples and bootstrapped samples. The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing that the bootstrapped samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance (Theorem 1) and establish generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). The method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches (KCIT, RCIT) in both synthetic experiments and real-world flow cytometry data.

This work builds on recent advances in using classification for statistical testing (e.g., [19] for independence testing) but addresses the more challenging CI setting. Unlike permutation-based approaches like KCIPT [10], which require solving expensive linear programs, their nearest-neighbor bootstrap is computationally efficient and provides theoretical guarantees about the quality of the generated samples.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contributions (Quality & Originality):** The paper makes two significant theoretical contributions. First, Theorem 1 rigorously establishes that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap produces samples close to f_CI in total variation distance, which is a substantial advance over previous methods that lacked such guarantees. Second, Theorem 2 provides novel generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples, extending standard learning theory results to a non-i.i.d setting. These contributions are well-motivated and properly contextualized against related work.

**Empirical Results (Quality & Significance):** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The synthetic experiments demonstrate superior performance, especially as dimensionality increases (Fig. 2a), with CCIT maintaining high accuracy up to dimension 70 while competitors degrade rapidly. The real-world application to flow cytometry data (Fig. 2b, Table 2c) shows clear practical advantages, with CCIT outperforming KCIT and RCIT across three different causal graphs. The authors demonstrate that their method scales better than expected from theoretical worst-case analysis, which is a valuable practical insight.

**Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The core idea is explained with a figure (Fig. 1), pseudocode (Algorithms 1-3), and precise theoretical statements. The contributions are clearly enumerated, and the relation to prior work is carefully discussed. The writing is accessible to both statisticians and machine learning researchers.

## Weaknesses

**Bias Correction Justification (Quality):** While Algorithm 3 (CCITv2) with bias correction is effective, the justification for why using only Y,Z coordinates for the bias-correcting classifier (ĝ₀) works is somewhat superficial. The paper states that "the joint distribution of X and Z remains the same in the nearest-neighbor bootstrap" but doesn't elaborate on why this implies the bias-correcting classifier would have error close to 0.5 under H₀. A more rigorous explanation or additional empirical evidence would strengthen this important component.

**Comparison with Recent Work (Originality & Significance):** The paper references work up to 2017 (NIPS 2017), but several important papers on CI testing have been published since then, such as the recent work by Zhang et al. [33] on kernel-based methods. While the paper acknowledges KCIPT and RCIT, a comparison with more recent methods would better establish the paper's position in the current literature and strengthen the significance claim.

**Computational Complexity (Quality):** The paper mentions computational efficiency due to fast nearest-neighbor algorithms, but doesn't provide explicit complexity analysis or runtime comparisons with competitors. For a method targeting high-dimensional settings, understanding the computational cost as dimensionality increases would be valuable for practitioners.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes significant contributions to the field of statistical testing by bridging the gap between machine learning and statistics. The model-powered approach is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated with clear advantages over existing methods. The work exemplifies how to leverage advances in supervised learning for fundamental statistical problems.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), presents novel methodology (Originality), and addresses a significant problem with practical impact (Significance). The minor weaknesses regarding bias correction justification and comparison with more recent work don't detract substantially from the paper's overall contribution.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution that should interest both the statistics and machine learning communities at NIPS. The authors have clearly demonstrated that model-powered approaches can advance fundamental statistical testing problems, and their method provides a practical, scalable solution to a challenging problem.

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel approach converting CI testing to binary classification
- Strong theoretical guarantees with clear proofs
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing clear advantages
- Well-written and well-structured paper
- Practical significance for causal inference and Bayesian networks

**Con Acceptance:**
- Bias correction justification could be strengthened
- Could include comparison with more recent CI testing methods
- Computational complexity analysis would be beneficial

This paper represents a significant step forward in CI testing and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
