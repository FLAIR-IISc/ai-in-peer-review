This paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test, framing the problem as a binary classification task. The core idea involves generating samples mimicking the conditionally independent distribution using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, labeling original samples as positive and bootstrapped samples as negative, and then training a classifier to distinguish between the two. Theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for the classifier are provided. Empirical evaluation demonstrates performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world data.

The paper builds upon existing work in CI testing, notably kernel-based methods [32, 28, 10] and recent approaches utilizing classification for independence testing [19].  It distinguishes itself by offering a theoretically grounded nearest-neighbor bootstrap for approximating the conditional independence distribution and leveraging the power of modern classification algorithms. The reduction to classification is a compelling approach, potentially benefiting from advances in that field.  The work also addresses the challenge of non-i.i.d samples generated by the bootstrap, providing generalization bounds under near-independence assumptions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a binary classification problem, combined with the specific nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach, represents a novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for the classifier (Theorem 2), lending credibility to the proposed method.
* **Empirical Performance:** The results demonstrate competitive or superior performance compared to existing CI testing algorithms on both synthetic and real datasets. The scaling behavior shown in Fig. 2a is particularly promising.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithmic steps are clearly presented, and the main ideas are explained in a concise manner.
* **Modularity:** The model-powered approach offers flexibility, allowing for the use of different classifiers based on the data characteristics.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantees rely on smoothness assumptions on the underlying distributions (Assumptions 1 & 2). The practicality and validity of these assumptions in real-world scenarios should be further discussed. While acknowledged, the sensitivity of performance to violations of these assumptions is not fully explored.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The choice of parameters like the threshold ⌧ in Algorithm 3 could significantly impact performance.  The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of parameter selection strategies.
* **Computational Cost:** The nearest-neighbor bootstrap can be computationally expensive, especially in high dimensions. A more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of the method would be valuable.
* **VC Dimension:** While the VC dimension (V) is used in the generalization bounds, its specific value for the chosen classifiers (e.g., XGBoost) isn't explicitly stated or justified. This limits the practical interpretation of the bounds.
* **Presentation of Empirical Results:** While the empirical results are positive, the comparison to existing methods is somewhat limited. A more extensive evaluation across a wider range of datasets and conditions would strengthen the findings. The discussion of the Flow-Cytometry dataset and its limitations (potential ground truth discrepancies) is good, but could be more detailed.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to CI testing.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The reduction to classification offers a flexible and potentially scalable framework.
* The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the properties of the algorithm.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The theoretical guarantees rely on potentially strong assumptions.
* The computational cost of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap might limit scalability.
* The parameter sensitivity and lack of detailed parameter tuning guidelines could hinder practical application.
* Further validation with more extensive empirical evaluations is desirable.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of CI testing. The proposed model-powered approach is novel, theoretically well-grounded, and empirically promising. While there are some weaknesses regarding assumptions, computational cost, and parameter tuning, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, specifically providing a more thorough discussion of the assumptions and their potential impact, and exploring strategies for mitigating the computational cost.  The authors should also clarify the choice of VC dimension and potentially provide more detailed results from the empirical evaluation.
