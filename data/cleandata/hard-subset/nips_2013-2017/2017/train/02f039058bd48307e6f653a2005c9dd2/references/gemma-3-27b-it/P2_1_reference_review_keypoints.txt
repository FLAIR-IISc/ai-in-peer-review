- Novel reduction of CI testing to a binary classification problem that leverages modern classifiers.
- Theorem 1 gives guarantees on closeness of nearest-neighbor bootstrapped samples to the target CI distribution.
- Theorem 2 provides generalization bounds for the classifier used in the test.
- Empirically outperforms baselines on synthetic data and the flow cytometry protein signaling dataset, especially in high dimensions.
- Algorithm 3 includes bias correction, showing attention to practical performance issues.
- Overall paper structure and algorithm descriptions are clear, but theoretical sections are dense and notation can be cumbersome.
- Theoretical derivations need more intuitive explanations of assumptions and implications.
- Computational cost of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is under-addressed; scaling with Z dimensionality and sample size requires further analysis.
- Sensitivity to parameters (e.g., threshold τ = 1/√n) is insufficiently justified and needs deeper investigation.
- Empirical comparisons are limited (primarily KCIT and RCIT); more recent CI tests should be included.
- Practical limitations and behavior on specific data distributions are not thoroughly discussed despite theoretical caveats.
- Overall evaluation: technically sound, original, and significant with promising empirical results, but clarity and computational analysis need improvement.
- Pros: novel approach with strong theory and empirical gains; potential to benefit from advances in classification.
- Cons: dense theory, computational and parameter-tuning concerns, limited empirical baselines.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to improve theoretical clarity, computational analysis, and empirical comparisons.
