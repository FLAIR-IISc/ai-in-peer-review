This paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test, reducing the problem to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating the conditional independence distribution. The core idea is sound, building on recent work leveraging supervised learning for statistical inference. The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for the classifier, and demonstrates empirical performance competitive with state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of reducing CI testing to a classification problem, coupled with the specific nearest-neighbor bootstrap, is a novel contribution. While model-powered approaches exist (e.g., [19, 10]), this work offers a different instantiation and a theoretically grounded method for generating appropriate training data.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides theoretical results, including bounds on the total variational distance between the generated samples and the target conditional independence distribution (Theorem 1), and generalization bounds for the classifier (Theorem 2). This rigor is valuable and differentiates this work from purely empirical approaches.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The empirical results demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed method, showing competitive or superior performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets (flow cytometry data). The comparison with established methods (KCIT, RCIT) is well-conducted.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithmic details are clearly presented, and the steps are logically connected.
*   **Efficiency:** The authors mention that their bootstrap is efficient owing to fast algorithms for nearest neighbors, which is a relevant practical consideration.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantees rely on smoothness assumptions (Assumption 1 and 2) that may not always hold in practice. The sensitivity of the method to these assumptions is not thoroughly explored in the empirical section. The justification for the chosen smoothness assumptions is limited.
*   **Threshold Selection:**  The choice of the threshold ⌧ in Algorithm 3 (CCITv2) is based on an upper bound on the variance.  A more adaptive or data-driven approach to threshold selection could potentially improve performance.  The practical impact of varying this threshold isn't discussed.
*   **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** While the flow cytometry dataset provides a real-world application, the results are largely framed around existing causal structures. A more comprehensive evaluation with diverse datasets and varying levels of noise would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The related work section is comprehensive but could benefit from a more focused discussion on how the proposed method differs from existing model-powered CI tests, specifically highlighting the trade-offs.  For example, the computational cost of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap versus the linear programming step in [10] could be explicitly compared.
*   **Mathematical Notation:**  The notation, particularly in the theoretical section, is dense. While mathematically correct, it could be made more accessible with clearer explanations and more frequent referencing of definitions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to CI testing.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate competitive performance.
*   The paper addresses a challenging problem with potential applications in various fields.
*   The technical contributions (bootstrap method, theoretical guarantees) are significant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The strong assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis may limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The empirical evaluation, while solid, could be more extensive and diverse.
*   The notation and mathematical presentation could be more accessible.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The theoretical analysis and empirical results are compelling. While the assumptions and scope of evaluation are limitations, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above in a future version or during the presentation. Specifically, addressing the sensitivity to the smoothness assumptions and providing a more in-depth discussion of practical threshold selection would be valuable.
