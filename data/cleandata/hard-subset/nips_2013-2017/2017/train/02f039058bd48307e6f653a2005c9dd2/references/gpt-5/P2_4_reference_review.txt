Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “model-powered” nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test (CCIT) that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method: (i) constructs a synthetic dataset by swapping Y values across samples that are nearest neighbors in Z (1-NN bootstrap), which aims to emulate samples from the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z(x|z)fY|Z(y|z)fZ(z); (ii) labels original samples as class 1 and bootstrapped samples as class 0; (iii) trains a supervised classifier to distinguish the two. If the classifier performs better than chance, reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. A bias-corrected variant subtracts the performance of a classifier that only sees (Y,Z) to account for finite-sample discrepancies induced by the bootstrap.

Theoretically, the authors show that a single bootstrapped sample’s distribution is close in total variation (TV) to fCI under smoothness assumptions, with TV distance O(n^{-1/dz}). They also derive generalization bounds for ERM classifiers under the near-i.i.d. dependence induced by the bootstrap. Empirically, they compare against kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic post-nonlinear models and on protein signaling flow-cytometry data, reporting superior ROC AUC in several regimes. The approach relates to classifier two-sample tests (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab) and to kernel CI tests (Fukumizu et al.; Zhang et al.); closest prior work on simulating fCI uses a permutation found via a linear program (KCIPT), which the authors seek to replace with a simpler 1-NN bootstrap.

Quality
Strengths:
- Solid technical development: the TV closeness of the 1-NN bootstrap to fCI (Theorem 1) is nontrivial and clearly stated. The dependence O(n^{-1/dz}) matches intuition from NN-based density/entropy estimation. The generalization analysis under near-independence (Theorem 2) is also valuable, and extends beyond the immediate application.
- Sound reduction: using classification between original and “CI-simulated” data leverages modern classifiers’ strengths; the design ensures the (X,Z) marginals are matched across classes to focus the test on conditional structure.
- Practicality and efficiency: the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is simple, scalable, and avoids the expensive optimization in KCIPT. Code availability is a plus.

Weaknesses:
- Statistical calibration is underdeveloped. The paper does not provide a principled asymptotic level-α test or finite-sample Type I error control. The threshold τ is set heuristically (1/√n) and ROC AUC is emphasized, but for CI testing one typically expects control of false positives at a specified α. Under H0, U1 and U′2 are asymptotically identically distributed, so a permutation/randomization test on labels could deliver valid p-values; this should be integrated and evaluated.
- Assumptions and rates: Assumption 1 (curvature matrix bound for f(y|z)) is nonstandard and not easy to verify in practice; Assumption 2 and Lipschitz conditions on f(z) may be restrictive. The TV rate O(n^{-1/dz}) is slow in dz and highlights a curse of dimensionality; while the empirical results are encouraging, some discussion of sample sizes required in higher dz would help.
- Analysis-to-test gap: Theorem 2 bounds excess risk under dependence but does not yield clear test guarantees (size/power) for CCITv1 or the bias-corrected CCITv2. The bias-correction idea is sensible, but a formal justification that the difference in losses concentrates at 0 under H0 with quantifiable deviations is missing.
- Experimental evaluation: (i) Comparisons omit KCIPT (cited as closest related) and other recent CI procedures (e.g., conditional MMD variants), which would better position the contribution. (ii) Evaluation relies on ROC AUC; plots of empirical Type I error vs power at fixed α across n and dz would more directly assess test quality. (iii) Implementation details (classifier family, hyperparameter tuning protocol, data splitting to avoid test leakage) are not fully specified; since performance hinges on classification, more clarity is needed. (iv) The flow-cytometry evaluation uses proxy ground truth from learned graphs; while common, it limits interpretability. Reporting runtime would also strengthen claims of scalability.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful pseudocode. Some notation is heavy (e.g., the generalized curvature matrix Ia(z)); an intuitive explanation and concrete examples of when Assumption 1 holds would aid readers. A few minor typos persist. The description of CCITv2 is clear, but its statistical properties deserve more explicit discussion.

Originality
The core idea—using an NN-based bootstrap to approximate fCI and then reducing CI testing to classification—is, to my knowledge, novel and distinct from prior kernel-based CI tests and from previous permutation/LP-based CI sample construction. The generalization analysis under near-independence is also a nice theoretical contribution. The work is a meaningful extension of classifier-based two-sample testing to the CI setting.

Significance
Conditional independence testing is central to causal discovery and graphical modeling. A simple, scalable approach that leverages strong classifiers is practically valuable. The theoretical results improve our understanding of when NN-based resampling can emulate conditional structure. If the authors address calibration and evaluation concerns, this method could see real uptake.

Pros
- Conceptually simple and practically attractive reduction from CI testing to classification.
- Nontrivial theory: TV closeness of the NN bootstrap to fCI and generalization bounds under dependence.
- Good empirical performance in several regimes; open-source implementation.
- Flexible: can plug in domain-appropriate classifiers and fast NN search.

Cons
- No principled calibration/p-values; Type I error control at fixed α is not demonstrated.
- Strong/opaque assumptions and slow rates in dz.
- Missing comparisons to closest alternative (KCIPT) and to more recent CI tests; limited analysis of runtime and ablations.
- CCITv2 bias correction lacks theoretical guarantees.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you add a permutation/randomization scheme over labels (under H0 symmetry) to obtain valid p-values and demonstrate size control at target α? This would address the main practical weakness.
- How sensitive is performance to using k-NN (k>1) or to approximate NN search? Any guidance on choosing k?
- Please clarify hyperparameter tuning and data splits to avoid test leakage; include ablations over classifiers.
- Consider adding experiments with fixed-α error/power curves, varying n and dz, and including KCIPT and conditional MMD baselines. Report runtimes.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a compelling and useful idea with solid theoretical underpinnings and promising empirical results. The lack of calibration and some experimental omissions are notable but, in my view, fixable. With attention to test-level guarantees and more comprehensive evaluation, this work would be a strong addition to the NeurIPS program.
