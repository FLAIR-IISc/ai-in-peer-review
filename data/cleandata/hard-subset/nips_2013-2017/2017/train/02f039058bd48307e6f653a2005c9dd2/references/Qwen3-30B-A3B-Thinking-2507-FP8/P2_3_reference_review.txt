

# Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel model-driven approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The key innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap method (Algorithm 1) that generates samples approximately distributed according to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI, followed by a classifier that distinguishes between original samples and these bootstrap samples. The paper makes four main contributions: (1) reducing CI testing to binary classification, (2) providing theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap samples (Theorem 1), (3) establishing generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), and (4) demonstrating superior empirical performance over state-of-the-art methods.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated. Theorem 1 provides a meaningful bound on the total variation distance between the bootstrap samples and f_CI (d_TV(, f_CI) = O(1/n^(1/dz))), which is a significant contribution to the literature. The proof, while deferred to the appendix, appears sound based on the presented assumptions. Theorem 2 extends classification theory to handle the non-i.i.d nature of the bootstrapped samples, which is a substantial theoretical advance. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive: synthetic experiments demonstrate scalability with dimension (Z up to dimension 70), and the flow-cytometry application shows practical utility. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the classifier bias under H0, and provide a bias-corrected algorithm (CCITv2) to address this. The experimental design is robust, with multiple bootstrap runs and careful comparison against relevant baselines (KCIT, RCIT).

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and the proposed approach, with Figure 1 providing a helpful visual summary. The algorithms (Algorithms 1-3) are presented with clear pseudocode, and the theoretical results are precisely stated with appropriate definitions. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout. One minor weakness is that some theoretical details (e.g., proof of Theorem 1) are deferred to the appendix, which could make verification challenging without consulting supplementary material. However, this is common practice in machine learning papers and does not significantly detract from the main contributions.

## Originality

This paper makes substantial original contributions. The reduction of CI testing to binary classification is novel, particularly the nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach to generate samples from f_CI, which differs fundamentally from existing permutation-based methods (e.g., KCIPT). The theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap method (Theorem 1) and the generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2) represent significant advances beyond prior work. The paper properly situates itself against related work, particularly KCIPT, KCIT, and RCIT, and clearly articulates how it differs from these approaches. The novelty is substantial and well-supported by both theory and experiments.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high. CI testing is a fundamental problem in causal inference, Bayesian networks, and feature selection, with wide-ranging applications. The proposed method addresses a key challenge in CI testing: scalability with the dimension of the conditioning set Z. The empirical results show CCIT outperforms state-of-the-art methods, especially when Z has high dimension (up to 70 in experiments), where existing methods fail. By leveraging powerful classification tools (XGBoost, neural networks), the approach also benefits from future advances in classification, creating a natural pipeline for CI testing. The application to the flow-cytometry dataset demonstrates practical impact in a real-world causal discovery scenario.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes significant contributions to a fundamental statistical problem. The theoretical results are novel and rigorous, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing, and the approach has clear practical value. The paper meets all four NeurIPS criteria with excellence.

**Strengths:**
- Novel approach to CI testing by reducing to binary classification
- Rigorous theoretical guarantees for bootstrap method and classification generalization
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing superior performance, especially in high dimensions
- Practical application to real-world causal discovery

**Weaknesses:**
- Some theoretical details deferred to appendix (though common practice)
- Limited discussion on why nearest-neighbor bootstrap works better than other methods for high-dimensional Z
- Bias correction in CCITv2 is somewhat ad hoc, though the paper acknowledges this limitation

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper makes a substantial contribution to statistical testing and causal inference. The model-powered approach is elegant, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. It represents a meaningful advance over existing CI testing methods and has the potential to impact both theoretical research and practical applications in causal discovery. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a compelling case for its contributions. I believe this would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.
