- Presents a novel reduction of conditional independence (CI) testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate conditionally independent-like samples.  
- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a clever mechanism to approximate the CI distribution for training the classifier.  
- Strong theoretical contribution: bounds on total variation distance of bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds in the near-independent setting.  
- Empirical results show promising performance, outperforming state-of-the-art on synthetic data and one real-world dataset.  
- Approach is potentially scalable to high-dimensional settings by leveraging modern classifiers.  
- Clear motivation and articulation of advantages over traditional CI methods.  
- Unclear connection between theoretical guarantees and practical algorithm choices (e.g., how TV bounds inform classifier choice or accuracy interpretation).  
- Insufficient guidance on setting the threshold (τ) in Algorithm 3 and limited explanation of practical implications of theory.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited: synthetic experiments are solid but real-world testing uses only one dataset with ambiguous ground truth.  
- Algorithm 3 (bias correction) adds complexity without a clear demonstration that its benefits justify the added complexity.  
- Presentation is dense; theoretical sections could be streamlined for readability.  
- Computational cost discussion is lacking—need detailed analysis of bootstrap and classification costs as Z dimensionality grows.  
- Originality: extends classification-based independence testing to the harder CI problem and addresses sample generation via nearest-neighbor bootstrap.  
- Significance: high potential impact for causal discovery and related fields if validated broadly, but practical impact depends on further validation and refinement.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—improve clarity linking theory to practice, expand empirical validation, justify Algorithm 3, and streamline presentation.
