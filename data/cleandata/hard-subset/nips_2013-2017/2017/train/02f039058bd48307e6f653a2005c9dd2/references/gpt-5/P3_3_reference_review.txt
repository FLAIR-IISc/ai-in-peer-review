Summary
The paper proposes a “model-powered” approach to nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n iid samples of (X, Y, Z), the method splits data into U1, U2, U3. It creates a synthetic set U02 by replacing the Y in each point u ∈ U2 with the Y from its 1-NN in Z among U3 (nearest-neighbor bootstrap). The original U1 (label 1) and synthetic U02 (label 0) constitute a two-sample classification problem: if a classifier can separate them on a held-out test set, one rejects H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. A bias-corrected variant (CCITv2) subtracts a classifier trained on (Y, Z) alone from one trained on (X, Y, Z). Theoretical contributions include (i) an upper bound on the total variation distance between the distribution induced by the bootstrap and fCI(x, y, z) = fX|Z fY|Z fZ under smoothness assumptions, scaling as O(n−1/dz) (up to constants/low-density terms), and (ii) generalization error bounds for ERM under the “near-independent” dependence structure introduced by the bootstrap. Empirical comparisons on synthetic data and a flow-cytometry dataset indicate better empirical performance than KCIT and RCIT, particularly as dim(Z) grows. The approach is computationally simple and leverages modern classifiers (XGBoost).

Relation to prior work
- Kernel-based CI tests (KCIT/RCIT) and permutation-based KCIPT are the closest baselines. KCIPT also constructs proxy samples for fCI but via a matching/LP; the proposed nearest-neighbor bootstrap is simpler and is analyzed in TV distance.
- Classifier two-sample tests (C2ST) for independence testing motivate the reduction; here the challenge is conditional resampling, which is addressed by the NN bootstrap.
- The non-iid generalization analysis relates to Rademacher bounds for dependent processes (e.g., Mohri & Rostamizadeh), adapted to the geometric dependence induced by nearest-neighbor reuse.
Overall, the paper is a natural and interesting continuation of C2ST ideas to CI testing, with a simpler alternative to KCIPT’s matching.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple reduction allowing users to leverage strong classifiers, feature engineering, and domain knowledge.
- Efficient and easy to implement; nearest-neighbor search is standard and scales reasonably with approximate methods.
- Theoretical analysis of the bootstrap in TV distance is valuable and connects conditional resampling to measurable closeness to fCI; the low-density trimming term G(·) is sensible.
- Non-iid generalization bounds are technically interesting and relevant beyond this application.
- Empirical results on synthetic and real data are encouraging; performance appears to degrade slowly with dz in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Statistical validity/calibration: The paper does not provide a procedure to control type I error at a user-specified level α, nor a way to compute p-values. The use of a fixed threshold τ ≈ 1/√n is ad hoc and not tied to a null distribution of the statistic for a given classifier. For CI testing, level control is primary.
- Assumptions and rates: Theorem 1 uses strong smoothness/curvature assumptions (Assumption 1 is unusual and hard to verify) and yields rates O(n−1/dz) plus low-density terms—implying severe curse of dimensionality and no guarantees in high-dz regimes that drive the empirical selling point. It would help to clarify regimes in which the bound is tight vs. pessimistic.
- Dependence structure: While the near-independence analysis is appreciated, it remains unclear whether the constants/rates make the generalization bounds informative at practical n, and how often Y reuse in U3 impacts classifier training/testing. The current results bound risk but do not translate to level/power guarantees.
- Experimental design and fairness:
  - Results are presented via ROC/AUC; the paper does not report achieved type I error vs. power at fixed α, nor sensitivity to τ. This makes it hard to compare to KCIT/RCIT as hypothesis tests.
  - Hyperparameters for XGBoost and the kernel baselines are not discussed in depth; different kernel choices/approximations can materially affect KCIT/RCIT, especially with growing dz.
  - The synthetic data class is limited (X, Y 1D; relatively simple post-nonlinear structure). More heterogeneous and challenging generative processes (e.g., heteroskedastic or non-smooth Y|Z; heavy tails) would stress-test the bootstrap assumptions.
- Scope: The method currently targets continuous variables; applicability to discrete/mixed data is not discussed.
- Clarity issues: Assumption 1 (curvature matrix) is opaque; more intuition is needed. Algorithm 1/2 descriptions flip between which split is “held out” vs. “used for bootstrap” and can confuse the reader. The choice and role of B (number of bootstraps) and its computational cost are not fully specified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a calibration scheme to control type I error (and compute p-values), e.g.:
  - Cross-fitting: repeat the 3-way split K times and aggregate a test statistic across folds.
  - Null calibration by repeated conditional resampling: under H0-like surrogates, recompute the bias-corrected statistic to form an empirical null.
  - Label permutation within the classifier two-sample framework, combined with the CCITv2 bias correction, to estimate the null of indistinguishability.
- Report type I error and power at fixed α across n and dz, comparing to KCIT/RCIT.
- Expand experiments to include tougher settings (non-smooth/heteroskedastic conditionals, multimodal Y|Z, higher dy, dx), and ablations across classifier families and hyperparameters.
- Clarify assumptions and provide practical diagnostics (e.g., NN distance vs. n, G(·) estimates) to inform when the bootstrap is reliable.
- Discuss approximate NN search and its effect on theory/empirics.
- Improve clarity of Algorithm 1/2, define τ concretely, and detail computational complexity.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, broadly useful reduction with strong empirical showing and an accessible implementation.
- Novel and interesting theoretical analysis of a practical conditional resampling scheme.
- Potentially impactful for practitioners who prefer flexible model-based CI tests.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of level-α control/p-values; current procedure is not a proper hypothesis test as presented.
- Strong/opaque assumptions and pessimistic rates raise questions about theoretical guarantees in the regimes emphasized empirically.
- Experimental evaluation does not directly assess type I error control and may not be fully fair across baselines.

Overall assessment
This is a promising and well-motivated approach with solid technical and empirical components. However, for a paper about hypothesis testing, the absence of a calibrated level-α procedure is a significant limitation. I lean toward acceptance if the authors can add a principled calibration (with level control) and strengthen the experimental evaluation accordingly; otherwise, I would be a weak reject.
