Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes CCIT, a “model-powered” conditional independence (CI) test that reduces CI testing of X ⊥ Y | Z to binary classification. The key idea is a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap: split 2n samples into U1 and U2, for each u ∈ U1 find its 1-NN in Z among U2, and swap the Y coordinate with its NN’s Y to create a new sample u′. Under smoothness assumptions, the distribution of u′ is shown to be close in total variation to fCI(x, y, z) = fX|Z(x|z) fY|Z(y|z) fZ(z). With an additional held-out set, CCIT labels original samples as class 1 and NN-bootstrapped samples as class 0, and trains a classifier; accuracy above chance indicates deviation from CI. A bias-corrected variant subtracts the accuracy of a classifier trained only on (Y, Z), aiming to remove finite-sample bias introduced by the bootstrap.

Theoretical contributions include: (i) a bound on dTV between the law of a NN-bootstrapped sample and fCI that scales as O(n−1/dz) plus small terms under regularity on f(z) and a smoothness condition on f(y|z); (ii) generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization with “near-independent” samples arising from the NN bootstrap, including VC-based rates with additional terms capturing dependence and low-density regions; and (iii) a connection between achievable classifier error and dTV(f, fCI). Empirically, CCIT with XGBoost appears to outperform kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on post-nonlinear synthetic data as dz grows and on the flow cytometry protein network data.

This work relates to kernel CI tests (KCIT, RCIT), permutation-based CI tests (KCIPT), and classifier two-sample tests for independence (Lopez-Paz & Oquab). It advances the classifier-based paradigm to the CI case by proposing an efficient, analyzable Z-conditional resampling scheme via nearest neighbors.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean reduction of CI testing to binary classification, enabling use of modern, scalable classifiers and domain knowledge.
- Simple, efficient resampling procedure (1-NN in Z) with nontrivial theory showing marginal closeness to the CI distribution.
- Generalization analysis for non-i.i.d. training sets produced by the NN bootstrap is novel and of independent interest.
- Practical bias-correction idea (subtract accuracy of classifier on (Y, Z) only).
- Strong empirical performance, especially as dz grows; open-source implementation.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Finite-sample validity: Unlike permutation-based tests, the NN bootstrap only approximates fCI. The paper does not provide level control (type I error at a target α) or calibrated p-values; ROC/AUC summaries do not substitute for a test with guaranteed size. τ = 1/√n is heuristic and depends on classifier and data distribution.
- Assumptions: The generalized curvature (Assumption 1) and smoothness/integrability conditions on f(z) are strong and somewhat opaque; guidance about when they hold in practice is limited. Results hinge on dz; rates suffer from the curse of dimensionality (n−1/dz), which can be severe.
- Dependence structure: While the paper bounds generalization error under near-independence, the practical impact of heavy reuse of the same neighbor (hub points in Z) is not fully explored; this can introduce strong dependence among label-0 samples.
- Evaluation: No systematic assessment of size/power trade-offs at fixed α across settings, sensitivity to classifier choice/hyperparameters, or runtime scaling with n and dz. Comparisons omit some recent conditional randomization–style or LP-based CI tests beyond a brief mention of KCIPT (and do not include it experimentally).
- Scope: Only continuous variables are considered; handling of mixed or discrete Z,X,Y is not addressed.
- Some theoretical statements are intricate with many constants and low-density (G(·)) terms, making practical implications hard to parse.

Quality
Overall technically sound with interesting proofs. The marginal dTV bound for a single bootstrapped point and the non-i.i.d. generalization analysis are meaningful. However, as a hypothesis testing contribution, the lack of finite-sample level guarantees or a calibration procedure is a notable gap. Empirics are promising but would benefit from size-controlled evaluations and broader stress tests.

Clarity
The high-level algorithm and motivation are clear. Some notation and assumptions (e.g., the curvature matrix, the role of G(·), constants) are dense and could be simplified or complemented with intuitive special cases (e.g., Lipschitz f(y|z) in z). Provide more detail on experimental settings (n, dz, classifier hyperparameters, runtime) and on the bias-correction step.

Originality
The idea to approximate fCI via a simple NN swap in Z and reduce CI testing to classification is novel relative to prior kernel and LP/permutation approaches, and the non-i.i.d. generalization analysis is original. The work builds on classifier two-sample testing but extends it nontrivially to the conditional setting.

Significance
CI testing is central to causal discovery and graphical models. A flexible, scalable approach that leverages powerful classifiers can have real impact. Theoretical guarantees, even if conservative, enhance credibility. The absence of level control tempers immediate applicability in rigorous pipeline stages, but the method is likely to influence practice and follow-up work.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a practical calibration scheme to control type I error (e.g., sample-splitting with repeated NN bootstraps to estimate the null distribution of the accuracy gap, or conformal-style calibration)?
- How sensitive is performance to classifier choice/hyperparameters? Any guidance on selecting G?
- How often are Y values reused as nearest neighbors, and how does this affect dependence and performance? Any benefit from k-NN with random neighbor selection?
- Can you handle discrete or mixed variables? What modifications are needed?
- Please report n, dz, runtime, and size/power at α across synthetic settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a level-controlled test and p-value computation with empirical null calibration.
- Provide ablations: with/without bias correction, different classifiers, k-NN variants, approximate NN.
- Expand experiments to include size/power curves, computational scaling, and robustness to low-density Z regions.
- Simplify/clarify assumptions (e.g., state a Lipschitz-in-z version of Theorem 1).

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, elegant reduction with strong empirical gains in challenging regimes.
- Nontrivial theory for both the bootstrap approximation and non-i.i.d. generalization.
- Practical method with code; likely to be used and extended.

Arguments against acceptance
- No finite-sample level guarantees or calibration procedure; testing claims rest primarily on AUC.
- Strong/opaque assumptions and dimension-dependent rates; unclear practical regime of theoretical guarantees.
- Limited evaluation breadth (size/power, runtime, sensitivity).

Recommendation: Weak accept
Confidence: Medium
