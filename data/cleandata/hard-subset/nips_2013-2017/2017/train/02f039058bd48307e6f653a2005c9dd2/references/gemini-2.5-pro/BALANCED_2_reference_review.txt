### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel method for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning. The core idea is to reduce the CI test of H0: X ⊥ Y | Z to a binary classification problem. The authors introduce a "nearest-neighbor bootstrap" procedure to generate a synthetic dataset. This procedure takes a set of i.i.d. samples from the true joint distribution p(X, Y, Z) and, for each sample (x, y, z), swaps its y-coordinate with the y'-coordinate of its nearest neighbor in Z-space, resulting in a new sample (x, y', z). The key insight is that the distribution of these generated samples is proven to be close in total variation distance to the conditionally independent distribution p_CI(x, y, z) = p(x|z)p(y|z)p(z).

The CI test is then framed as a two-sample test: a powerful binary classifier (e.g., XGBoost) is trained to distinguish the original samples (labeled '1') from the bootstrapped samples (labeled '0'). If the classifier achieves an accuracy significantly better than random guessing on a held-out test set, the null hypothesis H0 is rejected. The paper provides two main theoretical contributions: (1) a proof that the bootstrapped samples converge to the target CI distribution, and (2) generalization bounds for the classifier trained on these "near-independent" (non-i.i.d.) samples. Empirically, the proposed method, named CCIT, is shown to outperform state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic data, especially as the dimension of Z increases, and a real-world flow cytometry dataset. This work builds upon the recent trend of using classifiers for two-sample and independence testing, offering a novel and effective extension to the more challenging conditional independence setting.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The proposed reduction of CI testing to binary classification via the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a highly novel and powerful idea. It provides a new paradigm for tackling this fundamental problem, distinct from the dominant kernel-based approaches. This "model-powered" approach allows the CI test to directly leverage the immense progress in supervised learning, particularly in high-dimensional classification. The potential impact is significant, as it could make powerful CI testing more accessible and scalable.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper does not just present a heuristic but provides solid theoretical justification for its approach. Theorem 1, which bounds the total variation distance between the generated sample distribution and the true CI distribution, is the cornerstone that validates the entire framework. Furthermore, the analysis of generalization risk under the non-i.i.d. setting of the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 2) is a non-trivial and valuable contribution, which could be of independent interest.

3.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate a clear advantage over strong, widely-used baselines. The superior scalability of CCIT with respect to the dimension of the conditioning set Z (Figure 2a) is a particularly strong result, addressing a key bottleneck in many existing CI tests. The successful application to the flow cytometry dataset further showcases its practical utility.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The central idea is communicated clearly, supported by an intuitive figure (Fig. 1) and well-defined algorithms. The motivation for the bias-corrected version (CCITv2) is logical and demonstrates a thoughtful approach to potential practical issues.

**Arguments against Acceptance / Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** There is a notable gap between the theoretical rate of convergence and the observed empirical performance. The bound in Theorem 1 scales as O(n^(-1/d_z)), which suggests a severe curse of dimensionality. However, the experiments show excellent performance even for d_z up to 70. While the authors briefly acknowledge this, the paper would be stronger if it included a more detailed discussion hypothesizing why modern classifiers seem to mitigate this curse in practice (e.g., by exploiting lower intrinsic dimensionality of the data manifold).

2.  **Practical Aspects of Hypothesis Testing:** The method relies on a threshold `τ` to make a decision. The choice of `τ = 1/√n` is motivated but lacks a formal procedure for calibration to control the Type I error rate at a specific significance level (e.g., α = 0.05). For a statistical test, providing a p-value is standard practice and often more useful than a binary outcome. The paper would be significantly more practical if it discussed how to obtain calibrated p-values, for example, through permutation testing on the labels of the classifier's test set.

3.  **Missing Complexity Analysis:** The paper claims the method is efficient but omits a formal analysis of its computational complexity. The nearest-neighbor search is the main computational cost, which is O(n^2 * d_z) naively or O(n log n * d_z) with structures like k-d trees. A direct comparison of this complexity with that of KCIT and RCIT would provide a more complete picture of the practical trade-offs.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

This is an excellent paper introducing a promising new direction for conditional independence testing. The suggestions below are intended to further strengthen the work for the final version.

*   **On the theoretical rate:** In the conclusion or discussion, I encourage the authors to elaborate on the gap between the worst-case theoretical bounds and the strong empirical results. Could the analysis be tightened under additional assumptions, or is this a testament to the adaptive nature of the classifiers used?

*   **From Classification Error to p-value:** To enhance the method's practical utility as a formal statistical test, I strongly suggest adding a section or paragraph on computing p-values. A straightforward approach would be to use the test statistic (e.g., `ˆL(ĝ0, D'e) - ˆL(ĝ, De)`) and build its null distribution by repeatedly permuting the labels {0, 1} in the test set `De` and re-evaluating the classifier's error difference. The p-value would then be the proportion of permutations yielding a statistic as extreme as the one observed on the original test set.

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance of CCIT depends on the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters. A brief comment on the sensitivity to these choices, and the protocol used in the experiments (e.g., was cross-validation used for the classifier's hyperparameters?), would be helpful for reproducibility and practical guidance.

*   **Dimensionality of X and Y:** The synthetic experiments focused on scaling the dimension of Z while keeping X and Y one-dimensional. How is the method expected to perform when X and Y are also high-dimensional? A brief discussion on this would be welcome.

Despite these minor points for improvement, the paper's strengths in novelty, theoretical support, and empirical performance are compelling. It presents a complete and significant contribution to the field.
