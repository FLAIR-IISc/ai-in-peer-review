- Summary: Proposes nearest-neighbor bootstrap to reduce conditional independence testing to binary classification, with theoretical guarantees and strong empirical performance versus kernel methods.

- Strength 1: Novel reduction of CI testing to supervised classification leveraging modern classifiers.

- Strength 2: Rigorous theoretical results (TV bound for bootstrap, generalization under near-independence).

- Strength 3: Thorough empirical validation on synthetic and real protein-signaling data, outperforming KCIT/RCIT, especially as dim(Z) grows.

- Strength 4: Practical, efficient algorithm (CCITv2) with bias correction using fast nearest neighbors and off-the-shelf classifiers.

- Strength 5: Clear, well-structured presentation linking theory, algorithm, and experiments.

- Weakness 1: Theory predicts degradation with increasing dim(Z) but experiments show strong performance—gap not fully explained.

- Weakness 2: Bias correction in Algorithm 3 is heuristic and lacks rigorous justification.

- Weakness 3: Empirical comparisons omit some recent/related methods (e.g., permutation-based KCIPT and newer causal discovery methods).

- Weakness 4: No detailed computational-complexity analysis comparing the proposed method to alternatives.

- Weakness 5: Smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1–2) may be restrictive; practical implications are not fully discussed.

- Arguments for acceptance: Innovative practical approach with solid theory, strong empirical results, efficient implementation, and clear writing.

- Arguments against acceptance: Theoretical-practical mismatch, heuristic bias correction, limited method comparisons, and potentially restrictive assumptions.

- Overall assessment: Strong, well-executed contribution to CI testing with minor but addressable weaknesses.

- Recommendation: Accept.

- Suggestion 1: Discuss why empirical results outperform theoretical bounds, especially dimensionality effects.

- Suggestion 2: Provide a more rigorous analysis or justification of the bias-correction step.

- Suggestion 3: Add direct empirical comparison to KCIPT (and other recent relevant methods).

- Suggestion 4: Include a computational complexity analysis versus existing approaches.

- Suggestion 5: Discuss practical implications and checks for the smoothness assumptions used in theory.
