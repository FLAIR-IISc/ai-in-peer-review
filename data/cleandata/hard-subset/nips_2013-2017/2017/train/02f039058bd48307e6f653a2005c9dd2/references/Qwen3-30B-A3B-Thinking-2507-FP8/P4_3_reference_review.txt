

# Review: Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test

This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The core idea involves using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) to generate samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution fCI, then training a classifier to distinguish between the original samples and the bootstrapped samples. The authors prove theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1) and provide generalization bounds for the classification problem under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). The method is evaluated on synthetic data and a flow cytometry dataset, showing improved scalability with dimension of Z compared to state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT).

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound work with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide non-trivial theoretical guarantees (Theorem 1) showing that the distribution of samples generated by their nearest-neighbor bootstrap is close to fCI in total variation distance (with bound O(1/n^(1/dz))). Theorem 2 establishes generalization bounds for the classification problem under near-independent samples, which is a significant contribution. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with synthetic experiments demonstrating better scaling with dimension of Z compared to existing methods, and real-world experiments on the flow cytometry dataset showing consistent improvements. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., bias in classifier under H0) and provide a bias-corrected algorithm (Algorithm 3). The paper is a complete contribution, not merely a position paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The main idea is effectively explained with a figure (Fig. 1) and detailed algorithm descriptions. The theoretical results are presented with appropriate definitions and context. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results, including the algorithm pseudocode and experimental setup. The writing is precise and accessible to readers familiar with machine learning and statistics.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by reducing CI testing to binary classification via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, which is novel compared to existing kernel-based approaches (KCIT, RCIT, KCIPT). The authors properly differentiate their work from related literature: KCIPT uses an expensive linear program to simulate fCI, while their method is simpler and provides theoretical guarantees. The theoretical analysis of generalization under near-independent samples (Theorem 2) is particularly novel and of independent interest. The approach aligns with the growing trend of "model-powered" statistical testing that leverages advances in supervised learning.

**Significance:** CI testing is a fundamental problem with applications in causal inference, Bayesian networks, and feature selection. The authors demonstrate that their method scales better with dimension of Z compared to state-of-the-art methods, addressing a key limitation in existing approaches. By reducing CI testing to classification, the method leverages advances in classification (e.g., gradient boosted trees, deep neural networks), providing a flexible and powerful approach. The empirical results show consistent improvements across synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating practical significance.

**Strengths:** The theoretical contributions are strong and well-justified. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is elegant and simple to implement, yet provides the necessary theoretical guarantees. The paper clearly articulates how their approach differs from and improves upon related work. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincingly demonstrates the advantages of their method.

**Weaknesses:** The theoretical bound in Theorem 1 has a complex dependence on the dimension of Z (dz), which might limit practical applicability when Z is very high-dimensional. The paper does not address how the method would perform with categorical Z variables, which are common in practice. The bias correction in Algorithm 3 is somewhat ad hoc and could benefit from stronger theoretical justification. The comparison with KCIPT is somewhat superficial, as the paper states KCIPT requires solving an expensive linear program but doesn't quantify the computational cost difference.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel reduction of CI testing to binary classification with theoretical guarantees
- Strong theoretical contributions (Theorems 1 and 2) not present in prior work
- Clear empirical superiority over state-of-the-art methods in both synthetic and real-world experiments
- Addresses a significant limitation in existing CI testing methods (scaling with dimension of Z)

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Theoretical bound dependence on dz might be problematic for very high-dimensional Z
- Limited discussion of categorical Z variables, which are common in practice
- Bias correction method lacks strong theoretical foundation
- Comparison with KCIPT could be more thorough

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of statistical testing by introducing a model-powered approach that leverages advances in classification. The theoretical results are sound, the empirical evaluation is convincing, and the method addresses a practical limitation of existing approaches. While the dependence on dz in the theoretical bounds and limited discussion of categorical variables are noteworthy limitations, the paper's strengths outweigh these concerns. The work is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical innovation with practical significance in a fundamental statistical problem.
