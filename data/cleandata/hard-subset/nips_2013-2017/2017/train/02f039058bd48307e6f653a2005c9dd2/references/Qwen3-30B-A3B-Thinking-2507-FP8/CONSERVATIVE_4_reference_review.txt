

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors claim to generate samples close to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1), then use classifiers to distinguish between original and generated samples. While the idea of leveraging powerful classifiers for statistical testing is appealing, the paper falls short of providing sufficient evidence to support its claims.

**Strengths:** The paper builds on recent work using classification for statistical testing [19] and presents a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap method. The theoretical guarantees (Theorem 1) claim that the bootstrap samples approximate f_CI with d_TV = O(1/n^(1/d_z)), and Theorem 2 provides generalization bounds for classification under "near-independent" samples. The empirical results on synthetic data and flow-cytometry data show CCIT outperforming KCIT and RCIT, particularly as dimension of Z increases.

**Major Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical claims lack rigor and clarity:** Theorem 1's bound is extremely complex (involving G(ε) = P(f(Z) < ε), Hessian terms, and exponential decay), making it difficult to interpret or verify. The bound depends on d_z in a way that suggests performance should degrade with dimension, yet the paper claims empirical performance scales "much better than expected." This contradiction isn't adequately explained. The paper also refers to "Appendix A" for proofs without providing sufficient detail in the main text, which is unacceptable for a NIPS submission.

2. **Empirical validation is insufficient:** The paper compares against two baselines (KCIT, RCIT) but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the ROC AUC differences. The synthetic experiments use 300 datasets per point but don't report error bars or confidence intervals. The claim that CCIT "outperforms" baselines is not properly substantiated. The paper also fails to demonstrate how the method behaves when the smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) are violated, which is crucial for real-world applications.

3. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper doesn't isolate the contribution of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap versus the classifier choice. It's unclear whether the performance gains come from the bootstrap method or simply from using more powerful classifiers. Without this, it's impossible to determine the actual novelty of the proposed method.

4. **Theoretical guarantees don't match empirical results:** The theoretical bound suggests performance should deteriorate with dimension, yet the empirical results show improved performance as dimension increases. The paper acknowledges this discrepancy but provides no meaningful explanation or analysis of why the theoretical bound is pessimistic compared to practice.

5. **Comparison with relevant work is superficial:** The paper mentions KCIPT [10] but doesn't adequately compare to it. KCIPT also uses a permutation-based approach for CI testing, which is conceptually similar to the proposed nearest-neighbor bootstrap. The paper doesn't explain why the nearest-neighbor approach is superior to KCIPT's permutation method, especially given KCIPT's theoretical guarantees.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting approach but fails to provide rigorous theoretical and empirical validation to support its claims. The theoretical results are too complex to verify and don't align with the empirical findings. The empirical validation is insufficient for a NIPS submission, lacking statistical significance tests and proper error analysis. The paper would benefit from a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of the proposed bootstrap method, and a more detailed analysis of how the method behaves under realistic conditions where the smoothness assumptions may not hold.

While the idea of using supervised learning for statistical testing is promising, this paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support its claims. The authors have made a novel methodological contribution but haven't properly validated it against the standards expected at NIPS. The paper would need substantially more theoretical analysis and empirical validation to be considered for acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by evidence, and the theoretical results are not adequately justified or explained. The empirical results do not provide convincing evidence of the method's superiority over established approaches. The authors should provide more rigorous theoretical analysis and more thorough empirical validation before resubmitting.
