- Proposes reframing conditional independence (CI) testing as a binary classification problem using nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate fCI samples.  
- Uses a classifier to distinguish original from generated samples; success indicates dependence, failure implies conditional independence.  
- Provides theoretical bounds on bootstrap sample quality (Theorem 1) and classifier generalization (Theorem 2), including analysis under “near-independence.”  
- Empirical results show the proposed CCIT outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic and real datasets and scales favorably with Z dimension.  
- Novel reduction of CI testing to classification leverages advances in supervised learning.  
- Solid theoretical contributions give the method a firm foundation and insight into behavior.  
- Clear, well-organized presentation with detailed algorithmic descriptions and pseudocode.  
- Practical implementation uses common tools (gradient-boosted trees, neural nets), easing adoption.  
- Nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) may be computationally expensive for high-dimensional or large datasets; runtime analysis is lacking.  
- Method performance likely sensitive to classifier choice, threshold τ, and nearest-neighbor bandwidth; sensitivity analysis is missing.  
- Theoretical guarantees depend on smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 & 2) whose real-world applicability is not fully discussed.  
- Experimental scope is limited and may be biased toward scenarios favoring the method; broader datasets and comparisons are needed.  
- Bias-correction (Algorithm 3) requires training an additional classifier, adding complexity and unclear benefit quantification.  
- Overall assessment: compelling, novel approach with strong theory and promising results, but computational and validation limitations remain.  
- Quality: strong technical contributions; Clarity: good; Originality: high; Significance: potentially high if computational issues are solved.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel idea, strong theory, promising empirical performance, potential to leverage ML advances for CI testing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability concerns due to bootstrap cost, parameter sensitivity, and limited experimental validation.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, asking for complexity discussion, parameter sensitivity analysis, expanded experiments, and ablation study on bias correction.
