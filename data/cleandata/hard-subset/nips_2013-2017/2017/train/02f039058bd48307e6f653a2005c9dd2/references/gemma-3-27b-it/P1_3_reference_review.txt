## Peer Review for "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea involves generating samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap method, and then training a classifier to distinguish these samples from the original data. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of the generated samples (total variational distance) and generalization bounds for the classifier. Empirical evaluations demonstrate competitive performance against state-of-the-art CI tests, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions its contribution within the existing landscape of non-parametric CI testing, referencing kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT, KCIPT) and recent work leveraging classification for independence testing. It effectively highlights how their approach differs by avoiding expensive linear programming (as in KCIPT) and offering theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap sampling process.  The connection to recent work using classification for independence testing [19] is clearly articulated, emphasizing the challenge of Z-dependent permutation and how their approach addresses it.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a binary classification problem is a conceptually appealing and potentially powerful idea. This allows leveraging advancements in classification algorithms directly for CI testing.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** Providing theoretical bounds on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1) and generalization performance of the classifier (Theorem 2) is a significant strength.  These bounds, although potentially loose, provide a foundation for understanding the method’s behavior.
* **Empirical Performance:** The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method, CCIT, outperforms existing CI tests, particularly as the dimensionality of Z increases. The results on the flow cytometry dataset are also encouraging.
* **Efficiency:** The use of a nearest-neighbor bootstrap appears computationally efficient, especially compared to methods relying on expensive optimization procedures.
* **Modularity:** The modular design allows for flexibility in choosing the underlying classifier, enabling adaptation to specific data characteristics.
* **Bias Correction:** The introduction of Algorithm 3, with bias correction, shows a careful consideration of the practical implications of the theoretical results and is a significant improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantees rely on specific smoothness assumptions on the underlying distributions.  The practical impact of these assumptions isn't thoroughly discussed.  How sensitive is the method to violations of these assumptions?
* **Constant Factors:** The theoretical bounds (Theorem 1 and 2) contain constant factors (c, C, etc.) whose values are unspecified. This makes it difficult to assess the tightness of the bounds and their practical relevance.  The dependence on `d_z` (dimension of Z) in Theorem 1 seems potentially problematic for very high-dimensional Z.
* **VC Dimension:** The VC dimension (V) of the classifier class plays a crucial role in the generalization bounds.  Choosing an appropriate classifier with a well-understood VC dimension is important, and the paper doesn't offer much guidance on this.
* **Bootstrap Stability:** The performance of the method may be sensitive to the random partitioning of data in the bootstrap procedure. While the authors report averaging over multiple bootstraps, a more detailed analysis of the stability of the results would be beneficial.
* **Empirical Setup:** The synthetic experiments, while demonstrating promising results, are somewhat limited. More diverse data-generating processes and a wider range of parameter settings could strengthen the conclusions. It's not clear if the performance advantage holds across different data distributions.
* **Practical Threshold Selection:** Choosing the threshold `⌧` in Algorithm 2 and 3 is crucial. The authors suggest 1/sqrt(n), but a more principled method for threshold selection, perhaps based on cross-validation, could improve robustness.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to CI testing.
* The empirical results are promising, demonstrating competitive performance against existing methods.
* The modular design and efficiency of the proposed method make it a potentially valuable tool for researchers and practitioners.
* The authors have addressed limitations of the initial approach with Algorithm 3, providing a bias-corrected version.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The theoretical guarantees rely on strong assumptions, and the practical impact of these assumptions is not fully explored.
* The theoretical bounds contain unspecified constant factors, limiting their practical interpretability.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of CI testing. The novel idea of reducing the problem to binary classification, combined with the theoretical guarantees and empirical validation, makes it a strong candidate for acceptance. However, the weaknesses regarding the assumptions, constant factors in the bounds, and the limited empirical evaluation warrant some caution.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  **Discuss the sensitivity of the method to the smoothness assumptions** and provide guidance on how to assess their validity in practice.
2.  **Attempt to provide more interpretable bounds** by either estimating the constant factors or discussing the implications of the asymptotic behavior.
3.  **Expand the empirical evaluation** to include more diverse data-generating processes and a wider range of parameter settings.
4.  **Provide more guidance on the selection of the threshold** `⌧` in Algorithm 3, potentially exploring cross-validation-based approaches.
5.  **Briefly discuss the computational complexity** of the algorithm, particularly the nearest-neighbor search.
