### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel, model-powered approach for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning. The core idea is to reduce the CI test H₀: X ⊥ Y | Z to a binary classification problem. The authors' method, named Classifier CI Test (CCIT), involves three key steps: (1) Given 3n i.i.d. samples, n samples are set aside. (2) A novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is used on the remaining 2n samples to generate n new samples that provably mimic the conditionally independent distribution f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). This bootstrap, for each sample (x, y, z), finds a nearest neighbor (x', y', z') based on the z-coordinate and creates a new sample (x, y', z). (3) The original n samples are labeled '1' and the n bootstrapped samples are labeled '0'. A binary classifier is then trained to distinguish between these two sets. The test accuracy serves as the test statistic: if accuracy is significantly above 0.5, the original and bootstrapped distributions are distinguishable, leading to a rejection of H₀. The paper provides theoretical guarantees, bounding the Total Variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and the true conditionally independent distribution. It also derives generalization bounds for the classifier under the non-i.i.d. setting induced by the bootstrap. Empirically, the method, particularly a bias-corrected version, is shown to outperform state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially as the dimension of the conditioning variable Z increases.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Contribution:** The reduction of CI testing to binary classification is an elegant and powerful idea. It bridges a fundamental statistical problem with the rich and rapidly advancing field of supervised learning. This allows practitioners to leverage powerful, off-the-shelf classifiers like gradient boosted trees and deep neural networks for CI testing, which is a significant departure from traditional kernel or information-theoretic methods. This "model-powered" philosophy is a valuable contribution to the field.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are impressive. The proposed CCIT method demonstrates substantially better performance (in terms of ROC AUC) than established methods like KCIT and RCIT, particularly in the challenging setting where the dimensionality of the conditioning set Z is high. This practical advantage addresses a major limitation of many existing CI tests and makes the proposed method highly relevant for applications like causal discovery in complex systems.

3.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides non-trivial theoretical analysis to support its methodology. Theorem 1, which bounds the TV distance between the generated samples and the target CI distribution, is the cornerstone that justifies the entire classification-based framework. Furthermore, Theorem 2 provides a novel analysis of generalization bounds using Rademacher complexity for the "near-independent" samples produced by the bootstrap, a result of potential independent interest. This theoretical rigor lends significant credibility to the proposed approach.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The proposed algorithms are presented cleanly, and the overall pipeline is explained intuitively with the aid of a figure. The authors do a good job of situating their work within the existing literature on CI testing and machine learning reductions.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** There is a notable gap between the theoretical guarantees and the observed empirical performance. The rate of convergence in Theorem 1, O(1/n^(1/dz)), suffers from the curse of dimensionality with respect to the dimension of Z (dz). While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be stronger if it discussed potential reasons for the excellent practical performance despite this pessimistic bound. It might be that the classifiers are exploiting some underlying low-dimensional manifold structure in Z, or that the smoothness assumptions are overly conservative for the data distributions encountered in practice.

2.  **Lack of Formal p-value Calibration:** The method as presented in Algorithm 3 outputs a test statistic (the difference in test errors) which is then compared to a threshold τ. While this is sufficient for producing an ROC curve, it falls short of being a formal statistical hypothesis test, which typically provides a p-value to control the Type I error rate at a pre-specified level (e.g., α = 0.05). The paper does not provide a procedure for generating a null distribution for the test statistic to compute such p-values. This makes it harder to apply in scientific settings where rigorous control over false positives is paramount.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The nearest-neighbor search required for the bootstrap procedure can be computationally intensive, scaling as O(n² * dz) with a naive search or O(n log n * dz) with k-d trees for n samples in each partition. While the paper mentions fast NN algorithms, k-d trees themselves degrade to linear scans in high dimensions. A more thorough discussion of the computational complexity, especially in comparison to competitors like RCIT (which uses random features to scale), would be beneficial.

4.  **Choice of Hyperparameters:** The method relies on the choice of a powerful classifier (e.g., XGBoost), which comes with its own set of hyperparameters that require tuning. More critically, the performance depends on the threshold τ. The paper suggests τ = 1/√n, but a sensitivity analysis or a more principled method for setting this threshold would strengthen the practical applicability of the algorithm.

### **Constructive Suggestions**

1.  To address the gap between theory and practice, the authors could add a discussion on why their method appears to overcome the theoretical curse of dimensionality. Are there specific properties of the classifiers (e.g., feature selection in tree-based models) that might explain this?

2.  For formal hypothesis testing, the authors should discuss how their framework could be extended to produce p-values. A natural approach would be to use a permutation-based scheme. For instance, one could permute the labels of the test set Dₑ multiple times to generate an empirical null distribution for the test statistic (e.g., `ˆL(ĝ, Dₑ)`) under the assumption that the two samples are indistinguishable. This would make the method much more versatile.

3.  The bias-correction in Algorithm 3 is a clever and important practical contribution. It would be illuminating to include an experiment that visualizes the magnitude of the bias `b` (i.e., `0.5 - ˆL(ĝ₀, D₀ₑ)`) under the null hypothesis across different sample sizes or dimensions to better motivate the necessity of this correction.

4.  It would be helpful to provide more intuition regarding the smoothness assumptions (1 and 2). For instance, what are some simple examples of distributions that would violate these assumptions, and how might the method perform in those cases?

### **Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper. The core contribution—reducing conditional independence testing to a classification problem via a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap—is original, significant, and opens up a promising new direction for research in this area. This conceptual novelty is backed by strong empirical results that show a clear advantage over existing methods in high-dimensional settings, as well as a solid, non-trivial theoretical analysis. While there are weaknesses, particularly concerning the theoretical rates and the lack of a formal p-value calibration procedure, these represent avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws. The paper's strengths in novelty, practical performance, and theoretical grounding make it a high-quality contribution to the NeurIPS community.
