- Overall: Novel reduction of CI testing to binary classification; promising but hard-to-read and under-detailed empirics — recommend weak accept with major revisions.

- Core idea: Generate pseudo-samples approximating the fCI distribution via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, label them vs real samples, train a classifier, and use its performance to test X ⟂ Y | Z.

- Prior work relation: Builds on ML reductions for inference, addresses sample-generation gap left by prior methods (e.g., [10]), and contrasts with kernel-based CI tests while tying to two-sample testing and Rademacher bounds.

- Strength — novelty: Clever reduction enabling use of modern classifiers for CI testing.

- Strength — theory: Provides bounds on distance to fCI (Thm.1) and classifier generalization (Thm.2), giving theoretical grounding.

- Strength — scalability: Nearest-neighbor bootstrap can be made efficient with fast NN search, promising better scaling than kernel methods.

- Strength — empirical: Shows improved performance in high-dimensional settings and a real protein signaling network; includes Algorithm 3 to correct bias from non-i.i.d. samples.

- Weakness — clarity: Dense notation and lack of intuitive explanation (esp. of NN bootstrap and fCI connection) reduce accessibility.

- Weakness — empirical detail: Missing specifics on classifiers, hyperparameter tuning, sensitivity analyses, choice of threshold τ, and low number of bootstraps (B=50); lacks error bars and robustness checks.

- Weakness — theoretical complexity: Bounds are hard to interpret; smoothness assumptions need justification and discussion of practical implications.

- Weakness — comparisons & limitations: Limited benchmarking against state-of-the-art across varied datasets and limited exploration of sensitivity to assumption violations and NN algorithm effects.

- For acceptance: Novel, theoretically grounded, scalable approach with promising empirical results and rigorous analysis.

- Against acceptance: Poor clarity, insufficient empirical reporting, complex theory without intuition, and limited comparative evaluation.

- Recommendations: Improve exposition (intuitive explanations, diagram), expand experimental details and sensitivity analyses (more bootstraps, error bars), clarify and justify theoretical assumptions, report NN computational cost, and investigate sensitivity to assumption violations.

- Question for authors: How does CCIT perform relative to kernel-based methods on datasets that clearly violate the smoothness assumptions?
