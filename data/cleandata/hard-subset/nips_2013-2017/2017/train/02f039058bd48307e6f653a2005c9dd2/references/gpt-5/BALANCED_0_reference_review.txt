Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a model-powered approach to nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing for continuous variables by reducing CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n samples from fX,Y,Z, the method splits the data, uses a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap to create “negative” samples intended to mimic fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z(x|z)fY|Z(y|z)fZ(z) by swapping Y between 1-NN matches in Z-space, and then trains a classifier to distinguish original samples (label 1) from NN-bootstrapped samples (label 0). High classification accuracy implies fX,Y,Z differs from fCI, hence reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. The paper provides: (i) total variation (TV) distance bounds showing that the distribution of a single NN-bootstrapped sample is close to fCI under smoothness assumptions, with rate O(n−1/dz), (ii) generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization with “near-i.i.d.” samples induced by the bootstrap, and (iii) empirical results on synthetic data and the flow cytometry dataset, comparing against KCIT and RCIT.

This work connects to classifier two-sample tests (C2ST; Lopez-Paz & Oquab) and prior kernel-based CI tests (KCI/KCIT; Zhang et al.; RCIT; Strobl et al.), and to permutation-based KCIPT (Doran et al.), which also reduces to a two-sample test but requires solving an LP to construct permutations. The NN bootstrap is conceptually similar to conditional resampling schemes in conditional testing, but here specialized to a simple 1-NN swap. The generalization analysis under dependence relates to Rademacher bounds for non-i.i.d. processes (Mohri & Rostamizadeh).

Strengths
- Simple, modular reduction that leverages advances in classification; easily adoptable with off-the-shelf learners (e.g., XGBoost).
- Novel and efficient NN-bootstrap to emulate fCI without solving an LP, with a TV-distance guarantee under explicit smoothness assumptions.
- Generalization bounds for ERM with near-independent samples are of independent interest.
- Empirical performance is strong on the reported synthetic benchmarks and the flow cytometry case study; code is provided.

Weaknesses
- Statistical validity: No finite-sample or even asymptotic level control is established. The test threshold τ is heuristic (e.g., 1/√n), and experiments are reported via ROC/AUC, not at fixed type-I error. This is a significant concern for CI testing in scientific applications.
- Curse of dimensionality: The TV bound scales as O(n−1/dz), which is very slow for large dz. While experiments up to dz≈70 look good, the theory suggests the bootstrap may be poor unless n is huge. A gap remains between worst-case theory and practice.
- Assumptions/clarity: The “generalized curvature matrix” (Assumption 1) is nonstandard and hard to interpret; concrete examples (e.g., Gaussian conditionals) are not worked out to show when the conditions hold. Assumption 2 on fZ includes integrability and Hessian bounds that may be restrictive.
- Dependence and calibration: The dependence induced by the NN bootstrap complicates using standard holdout loss as a test statistic. The bias-corrected variant (subtract Y,Z-only classifier loss) is ad hoc; there is no principled calibration or p-value.
- Baselines and evaluation: Comparisons exclude KCIPT due to computational cost, and there is no evaluation at a fixed α. Details on classifier tuning, computational cost, and sensitivity to B, k, and choice of classifier are limited.

Assessment by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The reduction is correct in spirit, and the proofs (sketched) for TV closeness and generalization are plausible under the stated assumptions. However, the current procedure lacks guarantees on false positive control. The dependency structure and threshold selection require stronger theoretical and empirical support.
- Clarity: Overall well organized; algorithms are clearly stated. However, Assumption 1 is difficult to parse and would benefit from intuition and worked examples. The role and choice of τ and the magnitude of the finite-sample bias b(n) need clearer guidance.
- Originality: Using a simple NN swap to approximate fCI and analyzing its TV distance is novel relative to KCIPT’s LP-based scheme. The generalization analysis under near-independence is a nice contribution.
- Significance: If properly calibrated, the approach could be impactful due to its modularity and scalability. As is, the lack of level control limits adoption for causal discovery and scientific inference, where CI tests are often used inside larger pipelines.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, practical reduction to classification with strong empirical results.
- New theoretical angle on approximating fCI via NN bootstrap and on learning with near-i.i.d. samples.
- Potentially broad impact by enabling flexible classifiers in CI testing.

Cons for acceptance
- No control of type-I error; reliance on AUC rather than fixed-α testing.
- Theory degrades with dz; assumptions are strong and not well illustrated.
- Calibration and stability details (choice of τ, k, B, classifier tuning) are heuristic.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a calibrated test with nominal level α. Options include:
  - Use a null calibration via repeated re-generation of U′2 (and/or label permutations conditional on Z) to estimate the null distribution of the test statistic, with sample-splitting to avoid overfitting.
  - Cross-fitting: create multiple disjoint (U1,U2,U3) splits so train/test statistics are independent across splits; aggregate p-values across splits.
  - Replace the 0/1 loss with a proper scoring rule (e.g., log-loss) and calibrate the null via resampling to reduce variance.
- Explore k-NN (k>1) swaps or kernel-weighted resampling in Z to mitigate variance and reduce dependence; analyze the bias–variance trade-off and update Theorem 1 accordingly.
- Clarify and exemplify Assumption 1: provide families (e.g., exponential families, Gaussian conditionals with Lipschitz parameters) where the assumptions hold; quantify constants.
- Report level and power at fixed α (e.g., 0.05) on synthetic data; include size-control plots under H0 across n and dz.
- Add runtime and scalability analysis, including performance of NN search in high dz (kd-trees vs approximate NN), and sensitivity to classifier choice/hyperparameters.
- Compare against an additional strong baseline such as HSIC-based tests with randomized features or KCIPT (even on small subsets) to contextualize trade-offs.
- Discuss handling of discrete/categorical Z and mixed data types; clarify how nearest neighbors are defined there.
- Minor: fix notation/typos; define all constants; provide full details for synthetic data generation and classifier tuning for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and the empirical results are encouraging, but the current version lacks principled calibration and level control, and the theoretical assumptions/limitations are not fully reconciled with practice. Addressing these issues would make the contribution significantly stronger.
