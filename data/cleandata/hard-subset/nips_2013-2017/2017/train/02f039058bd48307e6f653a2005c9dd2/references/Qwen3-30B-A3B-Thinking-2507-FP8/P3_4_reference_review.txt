

# Peer Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The key innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) that generates samples close to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI, followed by training a classifier to distinguish between original samples and bootstrapped samples. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for the classifier (Theorem 2), and demonstrate empirical superiority over state-of-the-art methods on synthetic and flow-cytometry data.

## Strengths

**Originality:** The model-powered approach to CI testing is conceptually novel. While classification-based independence testing has been explored [19], extending it to conditional independence with a Z-dependent bootstrap is a significant contribution. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach offers a computationally efficient alternative to the permutation-based method in [10], which requires solving an expensive linear program.

**Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides substantial theoretical analysis. Theorem 1 establishes a non-trivial bound on the total variation distance between bootstrapped samples and f_CI, which is particularly valuable given the lack of such guarantees in prior work. The generalization bounds under near-independent samples (Theorem 2) are noteworthy, as they address a gap in the literature on non-i.i.d learning.

**Empirical Results:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, showing significant performance gains over KCIT and RCIT on synthetic data with high-dimensional Z (up to dimension 70). The flow-cytometry dataset results demonstrate practical utility for causal inference, where the method outperforms competitors even with low-dimensional Z.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Assumptions:** The smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1-2) are quite strong. Assumption 1 requires bounded generalized curvature of f(y|z), which may not hold for many real-world distributions. The paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the method is to violations of these assumptions, which is critical for practical adoption.

**Dimensional Scaling:** The theoretical guarantee d_TV = O(1/n^(1/dz)) suggests poor scaling with dimension (dz), yet the empirical results show good performance even at dz=70. The authors acknowledge this discrepancy but don't provide a theoretical explanation. Without this, the practical utility for high-dimensional problems remains questionable.

**Comparison with [10]:** The paper claims superiority over [10] but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity. The nearest-neighbor approach requires O(n log n) for k-NN search, which is comparable to the linear program solution in [10]. The paper should clarify why their approach is more efficient in practice.

**Bias Correction Justification:** The bias correction in Algorithm 3 (using classifiers trained on Y,Z only) is somewhat ad hoc. The authors provide intuitive justification but lack a rigorous theoretical basis for why this specific correction works better than alternatives.

**Empirical Scope:** The synthetic experiments use a specific post-nonlinear noise model, which may not represent the full range of CI testing scenarios. The paper would benefit from testing on more diverse data-generating processes.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of CI testing by introducing a model-powered approach that leverages modern classification tools. The theoretical guarantees are a strong point, though the assumptions require careful consideration. The empirical results are promising, but the disconnect between the theoretical worst-case analysis and empirical performance needs better explanation.

The most significant limitation is the high-dimensional dependence in the theoretical guarantee. While the paper acknowledges this, it doesn't provide a compelling theoretical or empirical justification for why the method works well in practice despite the theoretical bound. This is a critical gap for a paper claiming to address high-dimensional problems.

The comparison with [10] is somewhat superficial. The paper states that [10] "requires an expensive linear program" but doesn't quantify the computational advantage of their method. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the argument for their approach.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to CI testing with strong theoretical and empirical results. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the empirical results demonstrate practical utility. The main weaknesses (theoretical assumptions and dimensional scaling) are acknowledged by the authors, and the empirical performance suggests these limitations may not be as severe as the theory indicates.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. Providing a more detailed comparison with [10] including computational complexity analysis
2. Adding a discussion of how sensitive the method is to violations of the smoothness assumptions
3. Exploring the theoretical basis for the empirical gap between the worst-case bound and observed performance

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of statistical testing. It advances the state of the art in CI testing and demonstrates how model-powered approaches can leverage advances in supervised learning for statistical problems. The work is highly relevant to NIPS and would be of interest to the machine learning and statistics communities.

## Overall Assessment

*Quality: 8/10* - Theoretical claims are well-supported, but assumptions are strong.
*Clarity: 8/10* - Well-organized, but some technical details could be clearer.
*Originality: 9/10* - Novel approach to CI testing.
*Significance: 8/10* - Practical contribution with demonstrated advantages, though theoretical limitations remain.

The paper represents a solid contribution to the field and deserves acceptance at NIPS.
