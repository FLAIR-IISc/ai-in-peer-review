This paper introduces a novel method for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, named Classifier CI Test (CCIT). The central idea is to reduce the statistical hypothesis testing problem of H0: X ⊥ Y | Z versus H1: X not ⊥ Y | Z to a binary classification task. This reduction allows the method to leverage the power of modern, high-performance classifiers like gradient boosted trees or deep neural networks.

The proposed procedure operates in three main steps. First, given a dataset of i.i.d. samples from the joint distribution f(X,Y,Z), the authors use a novel "nearest-neighbor bootstrap" algorithm to generate a second dataset. For each sample (x, y, z), this bootstrap finds a nearest neighbor (x', y', z') based on the Z coordinate in a separate part of the data, and creates a new synthetic sample (x, y', z). The paper provides theoretical justification that the distribution of these synthetic samples approximates the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). Second, the original samples are labeled as class '1' and the synthetic samples as class '0'. These are combined to form a classification dataset. Third, a binary classifier is trained to distinguish between the two classes. The test accuracy of this classifier serves as the test statistic: if the accuracy is close to random guessing (0.5), the original and synthetic distributions are indistinguishable, and H0 is not rejected. If the accuracy is significantly better than random, H0 is rejected. The authors also propose a bias-corrected version of the algorithm (CCITv2) to account for small deviations in the bootstrap process.

This work relates to several lines of research. It provides an alternative to the popular kernel-based CI tests like KCIT and RCIT, which characterize CI through covariance operators in an RKHS. It is also related to permutation-based tests like KCIPT, but replaces an expensive linear programming step for finding permutations with a computationally simpler nearest-neighbor search. The paper's core idea is an extension of recent work using classifiers for two-sample testing, particularly for unconditional independence testing [19]. The main novelty lies in devising a method (the nearest-neighbor bootstrap) to generate samples from the conditional product distribution f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z), which is a significantly harder problem than sampling from the unconditional product f(x)f(y).

### Strengths

*   **Originality:** The proposed reduction of CI testing to binary classification via the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a novel and elegant contribution. It provides a new paradigm for this fundamental problem, moving beyond the dominant kernel-based approaches. The method for generating samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution is the key technical innovation that enables this reduction.
*   **Significance:** The method is of high practical significance. By converting CI testing into a classification problem, it allows practitioners to directly apply powerful, off-the-shelf supervised learning models. These models are well-suited for high-dimensional data and complex relationships, potentially overcoming some limitations of existing methods. As classification algorithms continue to improve, so too will the performance of this CI test.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides two key theoretical results. First, Theorem 1 offers a non-asymptotic bound on the total variation distance between the distribution of the bootstrapped samples and the true conditionally independent distribution, formally justifying the core of the algorithm. Second, Theorem 2 provides generalization bounds for the classifier trained on the resulting dataset, which is non-trivial as the generated samples are not i.i.d. This analysis of Rademacher complexity for "near-independent" samples is a valuable contribution in its own right.
*   **Clarity and Empirical Support:** The paper is clearly written and the proposed algorithms are well-described. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing CCIT against strong baselines (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real-world data. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art competitors, particularly as the dimension of the conditioning variable Z increases, which is a common challenge for CI tests.

### Weaknesses

*   **Theoretical Rates vs. Practical Performance:** The convergence rate derived in Theorem 1, which scales as O(n^(-1/dz)), indicates a severe curse of dimensionality with respect to the dimension of Z (dz). While the authors correctly note that the empirical performance is much better than this worst-case bound suggests, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion on the reasons for this gap. It is unclear if the loose bound is an artifact of the proof technique or if the strong performance of modern classifiers on high-dimensional data is the primary reason for the empirical success.
*   **Guidance on Hyperparameters:** The method relies on a choice of classifier (e.g., XGBoost) and its associated hyperparameters, as well as a threshold parameter τ for the hypothesis test. The paper provides limited guidance on setting τ, using τ = 1/√n in experiments. A discussion on the sensitivity of the test's power and Type I error rate to the choice of τ and the classifier's complexity would strengthen the paper's practical utility.
*   **Justification for Bias Correction:** The bias correction mechanism in Algorithm 3 is presented intuitively. It proposes training a second classifier on only the (Y, Z) coordinates to estimate and subtract a bias term. While the idea is plausible, a more formal justification for why this specific procedure correctly isolates the bias would be beneficial. The assumption that the bias is independent of the X coordinates is implicit and could be discussed further.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The nearest-neighbor search required by the bootstrap algorithm can be computationally intensive, scaling poorly with the number of samples n and the dimension dz. While faster than the LP in KCIPT, a brief analysis of its computational complexity and comparison with the runtime of KCIT and RCIT would provide a more complete picture of the method's trade-offs.

In summary, this paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective method for conditional independence testing. The core idea of reducing the problem to binary classification is powerful and opens up new avenues for research. The work is supported by sound theoretical analysis and strong empirical results. The weaknesses identified are primarily areas for clarification and future work rather than fundamental flaws in the approach. The paper represents a significant contribution to the field and is well-suited for the conference.
