- Proposes CCIT: reduce conditional independence testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to simulate the null by swapping Y from Z-nearest neighbors.
- Labels original samples as 1 and bootstrapped samples as 0, then trains classifier; reject H0 if accuracy exceeds chance.
- Introduces a bias-corrected statistic that subtracts performance of a (Y,Z)-only classifier from the (X,Y,Z) classifier to mitigate finite-sample bias.
- Novelty: simple NN-based bootstrap for approximating fCI and leveraging powerful classifiers (XGBoost/DNNs) for CI testing.
- Provides TV-distance guarantees showing bootstrap distribution is within O(n^{-1/dz}) of fCI under smoothness assumptions.
- Proves generalization bounds for ERM under the paper’s near-i.i.d. dependency structure, extending VC/Rademacher theory.
- The generalized curvature assumption (Assumption 1) is unusual and needs more intuition.
- Theoretical results are nontrivial and carefully stated but suffer the curse of dimensionality in Z (n^{-1/dz} dependence).
- Lacks a provably level-α test or p-value calibration; TV closeness and generalization bounds do not give finite-n Type I error guarantees.
- Practical thresholds (e.g., τ≈1/√n) and bias-correction are proposed, but no principled calibration procedure is provided.
- Empirically shows strong scaling vs KCIT/RCIT on synthetic data and promising flow cytometry results; code is available.
- Experiments are limited: mostly 1D X,Y, few baselines (no kNN-CMI), report AUC but not fixed-α Type I/II error or runtimes.
- Would be strengthened by higher-dimensional X,Y experiments, additional baselines (kNN-CMI, Gaussian partial correlation), and runtime comparisons.
- Suggests sensitivity analyses: k in k-NN, metric choice for Z, classifier/hyperparameter effects, number of bootstraps B.
- Clarity: paper is well-organized with clear algorithms but dense notation, minor typos, and assumptions needing more intuition.
- Original contributions: NN-swap reduction, TV-distance guarantees for the bootstrap, and learning-theoretic bounds under near-i.i.d. sampling.
- Significance: promising and practical if calibrated, but immediate scientific use limited by lack of Type I control and dz-dependent theory.
- Strengths: simple efficient method, solid theoretical guarantees, strong empirical performance, flexible use of modern classifiers, released code.
- Weaknesses: no calibrated hypothesis test, strong dependence on dim(Z) in theory, limited experimental scope.
- Suggested fixes: add calibration (permutation/resampling or cross-fitting asymptotic control), broaden baselines and metrics, provide intuition/diagnostics for assumptions and robustness to mixed Z.
- Pros for acceptance: novel reduction with theoretical guarantees, practical simplicity, good empirical results and code release.
- Cons for acceptance: heuristic thresholding lacking formal Type I control, dimensional dependence in theory, narrower experiments.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — advances the field and is promising but needs calibration and broader experiments to be a clear accept.
