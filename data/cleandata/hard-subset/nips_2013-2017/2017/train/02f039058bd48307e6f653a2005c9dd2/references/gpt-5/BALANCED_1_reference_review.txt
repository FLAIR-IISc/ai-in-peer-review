Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes CCIT, a “model-powered” conditional independence (CI) test that reduces CI testing to binary classification via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method: (i) uses a 1-NN match in Z-space to swap Y-coordinates between partitions, producing pseudo-samples close (in total variation) to fCI(x, y, z) = fX|Z fY|Z fZ under both H0 and H1; (ii) labels original samples as 1 and bootstrapped ones as 0; (iii) trains a classifier to distinguish the two. High test accuracy implies deviation from CI. A bias-corrected variant trains a second classifier on (Y, Z) only to subtract finite-sample bias. Theoretical results bound dTV between the bootstrap distribution and fCI at O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness conditions, and provide generalization guarantees for ERM in a near-i.i.d. setting. Empirically, CCIT outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear models and on flow cytometry CI evaluations.

This work sits at the intersection of two-sample testing via classifiers (e.g., C2ST/Lopez-Paz & Oquab) and kernel-based CI tests (KCIT/RCIT). It is close to KCIPT (Doran et al.), which constructs an approximate fCI via constrained permutation (requiring solving an LP); CCIT offers a simpler NN-based bootstrap and analyzes its closeness in TV. The paper also connects to learning reductions and to generalization under non-i.i.d. processes (e.g., Mohri & Rostamizadeh).

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and modularity: a clear reduction of CI testing to two-sample classification, enabling use of strong off-the-shelf classifiers and fast NN search.
- Theoretical contributions: (a) a TV-distance bound between the NN-bootstrap and fCI; (b) generalization bounds for ERM under near-independence with explicit dependence on VC dimension and dz.
- Practical variant (bias correction via (Y, Z)-only classifier) that addresses finite-sample bias from the non-i.i.d. bootstrap.
- Empirical results show strong ROC-AUC, especially as dim(Z) grows, and a real-data application on flow cytometry.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Level control/calibration: The proposed tests use a fixed threshold τ (set to 1/√n in experiments) but do not provide a level-α guarantee or valid p-values. Without null calibration, Type I error control is unclear, especially across datasets and classifiers.
- Curse of dimensionality in theory: The TV bound scales as O(n^{-1/dz}). While practice appears better, the worst-case rate is prohibitive and the paper does not give conditions (e.g., manifold or low intrinsic dimension, margin assumptions) under which better rates hold.
- Assumptions may be strong: Assumption 1 (generalized curvature of f(y|z)) and Assumption 2 (smoothness/regularity of f(z)) could exclude important heavy-tailed or multimodal settings. The paper could better justify their prevalence and provide robustness diagnostics.
- Dependence induced by 1-NN bootstrap: Although addressed in the generalization analysis, the construction creates structured dependencies; the analysis remains high-level and may not tightly characterize practical regimes (e.g., many near-ties in high-dimensional Z).
- Sensitivity to design choices: No ablation on the choice of classifier, Z-metric/normalization, number of neighbors (k>1), or the effect of varying B bootstraps. These choices could materially affect both power and false positives.
- Empirics: (i) Comparisons are limited to KCIT/RCIT (and KCIPT is discussed but not compared due to computational cost). (ii) The flow-cytometry evaluation reports ROC without addressing multiple testing or ground-truth uncertainty; no control of FPR at fixed α. (iii) Runtime comparisons are missing, despite claims of efficiency.
- Sample efficiency: The method uses 3n samples and splits again for training/testing, which could reduce power at small n. Cross-fitting or repeated subsampling strategies might help but are not analyzed.

Quality
The methodology is sound and well-motivated. The proofs are sketched at a reasonable level (details in appendix), but the rates’ dependence on dz and constants may limit interpretability. The non-i.i.d. generalization result is valuable, though assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz f(z), near-independence via spatial separation) deserve more discussion on when they hold in practice.

Clarity
Generally clear, with helpful algorithms and discussion. Some notation (e.g., the curvature matrix Ia(z)) could be contextualized with intuition. Please specify tie-breaking in 1-NN, normalization of Z, and practical guidance for τ and B. It would help to state computational complexity and memory costs explicitly.

Originality
Moderately high. The idea of reduction to classification and of constructing an approximate fCI is known, but the 1-NN bootstrap construction plus TV analysis and the near-i.i.d. generalization bounds are novel and interesting.

Significance
Promising practical impact: if strong classifiers can be leveraged, CI testing could become more scalable and powerful in complex settings. The lack of calibrated Type I error currently limits adoption in scientific workflows, but the framework is a useful step.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, modular, and potentially impactful idea with solid theoretical underpinnings.
- Novel analysis of 1-NN bootstrap closeness to fCI and ERM generalization under near-i.i.d.
- Strong empirical performance, especially as dim(Z) grows.

Cons for acceptance
- No level-α control or p-values; threshold choice is ad hoc.
- Theory degrades severely with dz; limited guidance on when practical gains occur.
- Empirical comparisons and ablations are incomplete; no runtime analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a calibrated test: e.g., null calibration via repeated bootstrap to form an empirical null for the test statistic (accuracy gap), or a permutation over labels within the constructed two-sample setting to compute p-values, with cross-fitting to avoid reuse bias.
- Study k-NN (k>1), distance metrics, and Z preprocessing; include ablations and guidance.
- Explore conditions (low intrinsic dimension of Z, Lipschitz regression functions, margin conditions) yielding improved rates, and/or derive manifold-dependent bounds.
- Add runtime and memory benchmarks vs. KCIT/RCIT and, if feasible, a smaller-scale comparison to KCIPT.
- Report fixed-α power/FPR alongside ROC; address multiple testing in the flow-cytometry study.
- Discuss robustness to misspecification of smoothness; include stress tests (heavy tails, multimodality, covariate shift in Z).

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a fresh, well-argued reduction with nontrivial theory and promising empirical results. However, the absence of calibrated error control and limited ablations weaken the case. With added calibration and more thorough empirical validation, this would be a strong accept.
