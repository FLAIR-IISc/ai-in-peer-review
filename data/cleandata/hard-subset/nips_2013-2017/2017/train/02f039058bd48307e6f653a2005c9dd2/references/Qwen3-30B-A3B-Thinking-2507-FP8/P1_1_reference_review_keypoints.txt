- Proposes reducing conditional independence (CI) testing to a binary classification problem by distinguishing original samples from samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution.

- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) to generate samples close to f_CI and provides a theoretical bound on their total variation distance (Theorem 1).

- Reduces CI testing to training a classifier on labeled real vs. bootstrap samples and uses two-sample testing via classifiers.

- Provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), extending prior classifier-based testing theory.

- Empirical evaluation on synthetic and flow-cytometry data shows the method outperforms KCIT and RCIT across settings and dimensions of Z.

- Practical contributions include clear pseudocode, public implementation, and a well-structured presentation.

- Novelty: model-powered perspective and nearest-neighbor bootstrap are original and distinguish the paper from kernel-based CI methods.

- Technical strength: rigorous theoretical analysis with proofs and comprehensive experiments supporting claims.

- Weakness: smoothness assumptions in Theorem 1 (Assumption 1) may be restrictive and are not sufficiently discussed in practical contexts.

- Weakness: lacks a detailed computational complexity comparison with kernel-based methods, which is important for high-dimensional data.

- Weakness: bias correction in Algorithm 3 appears ad hoc and lacks a rigorous theoretical justification beyond empirical support.

- Weakness: limited demonstration of downstream real-world impact (e.g., improved causal graph discovery) beyond CI testing accuracy.

- Weakness: missing empirical comparison to some recent related methods (e.g., [22]) despite citing them.

- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized, though technical assumptions may require specialist background to fully assess.

- Significance: results are important for causal inference and feature selection, with potential to generalize supervised-learning approaches to other statistical tests.

- Recommendation: reviewer recommends acceptance at NIPS.

- Suggested improvements: discuss prevalence/violation effects of smoothness assumptions, add computational complexity analysis, demonstrate causal discovery benefits, provide theoretical bias-correction justification, and compare to the most recent methods.
