

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification through a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. The authors claim their method generates samples close to the conditionally independent distribution fCI, which can then be distinguished from the original data using classifiers. The paper presents theoretical guarantees on the bootstrap procedure and generalization bounds for the classification step.

## Technical Issues

The paper contains several significant technical inconsistencies that undermine its theoretical claims:

1. **Fundamental Notational Confusion**: In the ideal classification setting, the authors define q̃(x,y,z|` = 0) = fCI(x,y,z), but fCI is defined as a factorization fX|Z(x|z)fY|Z(y|z)fZ(z), which is not a proper joint distribution. This fundamental confusion between a distribution and its factorization invalidates much of the theoretical analysis.

2. **Theoretical Bound Misrepresentation**: The paper claims "dTV(, fCI) = O(1/n^(1/dz))" in the introduction, but Theorem 1 provides a much more complex bound with multiple terms including G(ε), exp(-1/(n^dz c_dz ε^(dz+2))), and other components. The paper fails to explain why the O(1/n^(1/dz)) simplification is valid, and the actual bound contains terms that don't decay with n for fixed dz.

3. **Algorithmic Ambiguity**: Algorithm 1 states "z0 is the 1-Nearest Neighbor (1-NN) of z (in `2 norm) in the whole data-set U2" - but z is a coordinate, not a sample, making this description nonsensical. The correct description should reference the Z-coordinate of the nearest neighbor in U2.

4. **Proof Sketch Deficiencies**: The smoothness assumption (Equation 1) introduces the "generalized curvature matrix" Ia(z) without proper definition or explanation of what this matrix represents or why it's relevant to the problem. This is a critical gap in the theoretical development.

5. **Empirical Implementation Details Missing**: The paper mentions using XGBoost in experiments but provides no hyperparameters (learning rate, number of trees, etc.), making reproducibility impossible. The threshold τ = 1/√n is used without justification for its specific value.

## Clarity and Presentation Issues

1. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses both "U 02" (with space) and "U'2" to denote the bootstrapped dataset, creating confusion throughout the paper.

2. **Typos and Formatting Errors**: The paper contains numerous typos: "w.p at least 1 8" (should be 1-8ε), "dTV (f, fCI) = O(1/n1/dz)" (should be O(1/n^(1/dz))), and inconsistent citation formatting (e.g., missing page numbers in references).

3. **Algorithm 3 Definition Error**: In Algorithm 3, the authors define "D0_r = {((y, z), `)}(u=(x,y,z),`)2D_r" but the notation is inconsistent - they're using u=(x,y,z) but then referring to (y,z) as a separate entity without proper transformation.

4. **Lack of Clarity on Test Statistic**: The paper fails to properly define the test statistic and its relationship to the classifier error rate, making it unclear how the threshold τ is chosen to control type I error.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting approach to CI testing and the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is conceptually novel, the technical shortcomings are substantial. The fundamental confusion between distributions and their factorizations invalidates the theoretical framework. The theoretical claims are not properly supported by the presented analysis, and the empirical results lack sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The paper's most significant weakness is the disconnect between the claimed theoretical guarantees and the actual bounds provided in Theorem 1. The paper oversimplifies a complex bound as "O(1/n^(1/dz))" without justifying why other terms in the bound can be ignored, and the empirical results contradict this bound (showing good performance up to dimension 70 despite the theoretical decay with dimension).

The paper also fails to adequately compare with related work beyond superficially stating that their bootstrap is "simpler" than KCIPT's permutation method. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison of computational complexity and practical performance.

## Recommendation

**Reject**. While the idea of reducing CI testing to classification is potentially interesting, the paper's technical flaws are too severe to recommend acceptance. The fundamental confusion between distributions and their factorizations, the misleading theoretical claims, and the lack of sufficient detail for reproducibility undermine the paper's credibility. The authors would need to substantially revise the theoretical foundations and clarify the notation before this work could be considered for publication.
