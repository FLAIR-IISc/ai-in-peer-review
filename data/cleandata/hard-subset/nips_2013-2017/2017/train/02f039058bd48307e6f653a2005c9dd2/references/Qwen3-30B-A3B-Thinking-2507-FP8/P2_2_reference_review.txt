

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification, which they call "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test" (CCIT). The key insight is to generate samples close to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure, then train a classifier to distinguish between these generated samples and the original samples. The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing that the bootstrapped samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance and derive generalization bounds for the classification problem under non-i.i.d samples. Empirical results demonstrate that their method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches (KCIT and RCIT) on both synthetic data and a real-world flow-cytometry dataset, particularly when the dimension of the conditioning variable Z is high.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation. The theoretical contributions are substantial, with Theorem 1 providing a rigorous bound on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped samples and f_CI, and Theorem 2 establishing generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. The authors carefully address the technical challenges of non-i.i.d samples in the bootstrap procedure, which is a significant improvement over previous permutation-based approaches [10].

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The synthetic experiments clearly demonstrate the scalability of CCIT with increasing dimension of Z, where other methods fail. The flow-cytometry dataset application provides real-world relevance and demonstrates practical utility. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their theoretical bounds (e.g., dependence on dimension d_z) while noting that empirical performance exceeds theoretical expectations, showing good scientific honesty.

One minor limitation is that the paper doesn't provide a detailed computational complexity analysis of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap, though they mention it's efficient due to fast nearest-neighbor algorithms. The authors also could have provided more intuition about why the nearest-neighbor approach works for high-dimensional Z.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement, methodology, and theoretical results are presented in a logical, step-by-step manner. The algorithms (Algorithms 1-3) are clearly described with pseudocode, making them reproducible. The figures (Fig. 1, 2a, 2b, 2c) effectively illustrate the key concepts and results.

The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results, though the theoretical proofs are deferred to the appendix. The notation is consistent throughout, and the authors clearly distinguish between their contributions and related work. The only minor improvement I'd suggest is to provide a more intuitive explanation of why the nearest-neighbor bootstrap works for high-dimensional Z, beyond the technical smoothness assumptions.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by reducing CI testing to binary classification, which is a novel approach for conditional independence testing (previous work [19] applied binary classification only to independence testing, not conditional independence). The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure is a clever technical innovation that provides theoretical guarantees about the quality of the generated samples, unlike previous permutation-based methods [10] which require solving expensive linear programs.

The paper properly situates its contribution in the context of related work, clearly differentiating their approach from KCIT [32], RCIT [28], and KCIPT [10]. The key distinction is that their method provides theoretical guarantees about the closeness of the bootstrapped samples to f_CI, whereas previous methods either lacked such guarantees or required more complex procedures.

## Significance
Conditional independence testing is a fundamental problem in causal inference, structure learning, and Bayesian networks, making this work highly significant. The paper demonstrates that their method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches, especially in high dimensions where existing methods struggle. This is particularly important because many real-world applications involve high-dimensional conditioning variables.

The model-powered approach is significant because it leverages advances in classification (which are constantly improving) to improve CI testing. As the authors note, "any improvements in the field of binary classification imply an advancement in our CI test." This creates a natural pathway for the method to improve over time as better classifiers are developed.

The empirical results on the flow-cytometry dataset provide practical validation of the method's utility in a real-world biological context. The authors demonstrate that their method can recover CI relations in a protein signaling network, which has important implications for understanding biological systems.

## Overall Assessment

### Strengths:
- Novel reduction of CI testing to binary classification
- Theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap procedure and classification generalization
- Strong empirical results showing scalability with dimension
- Practical application to a real-world biological dataset
- Careful analysis of both strengths and limitations

### Weaknesses:
- Limited discussion of computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap
- Theoretical bounds depend on dimension d_z, though empirical results exceed theoretical expectations
- Some technical details of the theoretical analysis could be more intuitive

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of causal inference and statistical testing. The combination of theoretical rigor, empirical validation, and practical relevance meets all the criteria for acceptance at NeurIPS. The work is well-positioned to be impactful, as it provides a method that not only advances the state of the art in CI testing but also creates a natural bridge between advances in classification and statistical testing.

I recommend **acceptance**. This paper is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in a fundamental problem, and it has the potential to influence future research in causal inference and statistical testing.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. Consider adding a brief explanation of why the nearest-neighbor approach works for high-dimensional Z (beyond the smoothness assumptions), as this is a critical insight for the method's success.

2. While the paper mentions computational efficiency, providing a more detailed complexity analysis (e.g., O(n log n) for the bootstrap) would strengthen the paper.

3. In the empirical section, it would be helpful to include more details about the hyperparameters used for XGBoost (e.g., number of trees, learning rate) to aid reproducibility.

4. The paper could briefly discuss how the method might extend to discrete variables, as the current focus is on continuous variables.

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear contribution to the field, and I believe it would be valuable to the NeurIPS community.
