Summary
The paper proposes a “model-powered” nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n samples, the method (i) constructs a synthetic dataset intended to approximate the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z fY|Z fZ via a simple nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that swaps Y across 1-NN matches in Z; (ii) forms a two-sample classification problem between original samples and the synthetic samples; and (iii) uses a classifier’s test accuracy to accept/reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. A bias-corrected variant subtracts the accuracy of a classifier trained only on (Y,Z). Theoretical results bound, in total variation (TV), the distance between the bootstrap distribution and fCI as O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z), and provide generalization bounds for ERM under “near-independence.” Experiments on synthetic data and the flow cytometry dataset compare against KCIT and RCIT, claiming improved ROC-AUC, especially for larger dim(Z).

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, modular reduction to classification that can leverage advances in off-the-shelf classifiers.
- Practical, easy-to-implement NN bootstrap without solving a matching LP (cf. KCIPT).
- Theoretical effort: a TV bound for the NN bootstrap’s closeness to fCI and learning-theoretic generalization under dependence.
- Open-source implementation and empirical comparisons to common CI baselines.

Weaknesses
- No finite-sample Type I error control or valid p-values. The decision threshold τ is set heuristically (e.g., 1/√n), and the “bias correction” via the (Y,Z)-only classifier does not provide guarantees on test size. For a CI test, rigorous calibration (e.g., via permutation/resampling) is crucial.
- The TV bound scales as O(n^{-1/dz}) with a collection of strong smoothness assumptions (curvature/Fisher-like bound for f(y|z), Lipschitz/Hessian and integrability conditions on f(z)). This rate is extremely slow in moderate/large dim(Z), raising concerns about practical sample complexity and the claimed high-dimensional robustness.
- The classification step is a black box; power and size will depend sensitively on model class G, hyperparameters, and tuning protocols. The paper does not systematically study this dependence or guard against overfitting beyond a simple train/test split, and it sacrifices 2/3 of the data to generate the synthetic set.
- Empirical evaluation emphasizes ROC-AUC rather than power at a fixed size. For hypothesis testing, demonstrating size control and reporting controlled-power curves is more appropriate. The use of averaged bootstrap repetitions without a calibrated null distribution can inflate apparent AUC.
- Synthetic experiments are limited (univariate X,Y; specific post-nonlinear model; unclear n vs. dz scaling; no assessments under model misspecification, heavy tails, multimodality, or non-smooth densities). Claims of scaling to dz≈70 are hard to interpret without explicit n and size control.
- Flow-cytometry evaluation lacks ground truth and uses graphs as proxies; discrepancies and cycles/feedback complicate interpretation. This does not substitute for calibrated Type I/II error analyses.
- Computational aspects are under-discussed: 1-NN in high-d Z is known to degrade; choice of exact/approximate NN, metric sensitivity, and reuse of neighbors (inducing dependence) are not analyzed empirically.

Quality
The methodological idea is plausible and the theory is nontrivial, but key pieces are missing for a testing paper: size control, null calibration, and power at fixed α. The assumptions behind Theorem 1 are strong, the rates are pessimistic, and the practical guidance for τ is ad hoc. The dependence of results on classifier choice and hyperparameters is not quantified. Experimental design does not match standard hypothesis-testing evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well organized. However, some assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1’s generalized curvature) are nonstandard and need more intuition. Experimental details (sample sizes, hyperparameters, NN search specifics, cross-validation protocols, number of bootstraps B and its effect) should be reported more thoroughly.

Originality
Reducing testing to classification is known (e.g., C2ST for two-sample testing, permutation-based KCIPT for CI). The main novelty is the specific NN bootstrap to emulate fCI and a generalization bound for ERM with near-independent samples. While interesting, the reduction is incremental relative to prior reductions and permutation/matching-based CI methods.

Significance
A robust, scalable CI test that leverages supervised learning could be impactful. However, without valid size control and p-values, it is hard to recommend the method for scientific use. The slow dz-dependent rates and reliance on strong smoothness also limit theoretical significance. The empirical gains reported via AUC, absent calibration, are not convincing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a calibrated testing procedure with finite-sample Type I error control (e.g., null-label permutations within the constructed two-sample setting, cross-fitting, or studentized test statistics with resampling). Report power at fixed α.
- Study sensitivity to classifier class G, capacity, and tuning; include simpler baselines (logistic regression, RF) and show robustness.
- Explore k-NN or local averaging in the bootstrap, or optimal transport/matching variants, to improve the bias-variance trade-off; analyze and compare.
- Expand experiments: vary n, dz systematically; include non-smooth/heavy-tailed settings; report runtime and NN search method; compare size/power to KCIPT with p-values.
- Clarify and justify assumptions; provide diagnostic conditions practitioners can check.

Pros
- Simple, modular method; easy to implement.
- Theoretical analysis of NN bootstrap closeness and ERM under dependence.
- Promising empirical indications on some datasets.
- Public code.

Cons
- No valid calibration or Type I error control; threshold is heuristic.
- Strong assumptions and very slow rates in dz.
- Results depend on black-box classifiers; lack of robustness analysis.
- Empirical evaluation focuses on AUC rather than size-controlled power; limited synthetic diversity.
- Limited discussion of computational behavior in high-dimensional Z.

Recommendation: Reject
