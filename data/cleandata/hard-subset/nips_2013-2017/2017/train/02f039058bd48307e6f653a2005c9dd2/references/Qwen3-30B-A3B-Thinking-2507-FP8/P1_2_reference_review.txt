

# Peer Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by converting the problem into a binary classification task. The authors introduce a "nearest-neighbor bootstrap" algorithm that generates samples close to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI. The key insight is that if X and Y are conditionally independent given Z (H0), then the original samples (labeled 1) and the bootstrapped samples (labeled 0) should be indistinguishable by a classifier. If they are distinguishable, H0 is rejected.

The paper builds on recent work in binary classification for independence testing [19], but extends it to the more complex conditional independence setting. Unlike previous kernel-based methods [32, 28, 10] that rely on expensive permutations or kernel methods, the authors use a simple nearest-neighbor approach with theoretical guarantees on the closeness of the generated samples to f_CI. They provide theoretical guarantees about the total variation distance between the bootstrapped samples and f_CI (Theorem 1), and generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). Empirically, their method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches, especially as the dimension of Z increases.

## Strengths

**Quality:**
- The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for their nearest-neighbor bootstrap method, showing that the generated samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance (Theorem 1).
- The generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2) are a significant contribution, as they extend standard learning theory to the non-i.i.d setting that arises from their bootstrap procedure.
- The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, including both synthetic experiments with varying dimensionality of Z and a real-world application to protein signaling networks from flow cytometry data.
- The paper is technically sound with well-structured proofs and a clear connection between theory and practice.

**Originality:**
- The conversion of CI testing to binary classification is a novel approach that differs significantly from previous kernel-based methods.
- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap technique for generating samples from f_CI is a clever and simple solution to a challenging problem (the Z-dependent permutation required for CI testing).
- The theoretical analysis of classification under near-independent samples is original and addresses a gap in the literature.

**Significance:**
- The method shows substantial improvements over state-of-the-art approaches, particularly in high-dimensional settings where previous methods fail.
- The approach is flexible and can leverage advances in binary classification (e.g., gradient boosted trees, neural networks), meaning that improvements in classification directly benefit CI testing.
- The application to protein signaling networks demonstrates practical utility in a challenging real-world setting.

**Clarity:**
- The paper is well-organized with clear sections and logical flow.
- The main idea is well-explained with a figure (Fig. 1) illustrating the approach.
- The algorithms are presented in pseudocode, making the method easy to understand.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:**
- The theoretical guarantees have a strong dependence on the dimension of Z (d_z), with the error term O(1/n^(1/d_z)). While the empirical results show better performance than expected, the theoretical analysis doesn't fully address how this scaling affects practical performance in very high-dimensional settings.
- The bias correction in Algorithm 3 seems somewhat ad-hoc. The authors don't provide a rigorous theoretical justification for why subtracting the loss from the classifier trained on Y,Z coordinates would correct the bias, though they note it's empirically effective.
- The smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) might be restrictive for some real-world applications, though the authors acknowledge this limitation.

**Clarity:**
- Some of the theoretical results are quite complex and might be difficult for readers to follow without significant background in learning theory.
- The notation could be clearer in some places (e.g., the use of f_CI vs. f_CI(x,y,z)).
- The connection between the theoretical results and the practical implementation could be better explained, particularly how the threshold τ is chosen in practice.

**Significance:**
- The paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison of computational efficiency with existing methods, which is important for practical adoption.
- While the method shows better performance on the flow cytometry dataset, the paper doesn't establish how much better the method is in terms of practical utility for causal discovery tasks.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel approach that converts CI testing to binary classification, which is a significant departure from previous kernel-based methods.
- Strong theoretical guarantees with the first analysis of classification under near-independent samples.
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing substantial improvements over state-of-the-art methods, especially in high-dimensional settings.
- Practical utility demonstrated on a real-world biological dataset.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Theoretical guarantees have strong dependence on dimension of Z, which might limit applicability in very high-dimensional settings.
- The bias correction in Algorithm 3 lacks rigorous theoretical justification.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address computational efficiency compared to existing methods.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors should provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the d_z dependence in Theorem 1. How does this scaling affect performance in real-world applications where d_z might be large? Can they provide any intuition for why the empirical results scale better than the theoretical analysis suggests?

2. **Bias Correction:** The authors should either provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the bias correction in Algorithm 3 or consider a different approach to address the bias issue.

3. **Computational Efficiency:** The paper should include a comparison of computational efficiency with existing methods, as this is crucial for practical adoption, especially in high-dimensional settings.

4. **Notation Clarity:** The paper could improve clarity by using consistent notation throughout, particularly regarding the use of f_CI vs. f_CI(x,y,z).

5. **Comparison to Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, it could more explicitly contrast their approach with the permutation-based method in [10], especially regarding the computational complexity of the two approaches.

## Final Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The novel approach of converting CI testing to binary classification is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The theoretical guarantees, particularly the generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples, are a major contribution to the literature.

While there are some limitations in the theoretical analysis (particularly the dependence on d_z) and the bias correction method, these do not undermine the overall quality of the work. The empirical results are compelling, showing substantial improvements over state-of-the-art methods, especially in high-dimensional settings where previous methods fail.

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. It represents a significant advance in the field of CI testing with clear theoretical contributions and practical utility. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in statistical learning and causal inference.

**Overall Rating: 8 (Strong Accept)**
