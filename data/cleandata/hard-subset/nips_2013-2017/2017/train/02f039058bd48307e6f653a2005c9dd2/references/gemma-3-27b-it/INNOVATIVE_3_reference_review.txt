This paper presents a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea – leveraging the power of modern classifiers to discriminate between jointly distributed data and conditionally independent samples generated via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap – is compelling and represents a significant departure from traditional kernel-based methods. The theoretical analysis, providing bounds on the variational distance between bootstrapped samples and the true conditional independence distribution, and subsequent generalization guarantees, are ambitious and valuable. I particularly appreciate the attempt to address the non-i.i.d nature of the bootstrapped samples in the generalization analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem is a genuinely new and potentially impactful perspective. It opens the door to readily incorporating advancements in classification algorithms directly into CI testing.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper attempts to provide theoretical justification for the method, with bounds on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and generalization error analysis. While complex, this is a strength compared to purely empirical approaches.
*   **Potential for Scalability:** By relying on classifiers, the method implicitly inherits their ability to handle high-dimensional data, a known bottleneck for many CI tests. The empirical results support this claim.
*   **Modularity:** The framework is modular. Different classifiers can be plugged in, offering flexibility.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The core ideas are explained in a relatively accessible manner, aided by Fig. 1.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Complexity:** The theoretical results, while ambitious, are difficult to fully grasp and rely on numerous assumptions (smoothness conditions, bounded Lipschitz constant, etc.). The practical implications of the bounds (e.g., how to choose appropriate parameters) are not always clear. The interplay between ‘d_z’ and ‘n’ in the bounds requires further clarification on the sample complexity needed for reliable performance.
*   **Bootstrap Assumptions:** The success of the method heavily relies on the nearest-neighbor bootstrap accurately mimicking the conditional independence distribution. The smoothness assumptions are fairly strong, and the sensitivity of the method to violations of these assumptions is not thoroughly explored.
*   **Empirical Evaluation Limitations:** The synthetic experiments demonstrate promising results, but the setup feels somewhat contrived (post-nonlinear noise) and the comparison with other methods is not comprehensive enough. The flow cytometry results are encouraging, but the claim that existing methods 'fail' requires stronger justification. The discussion regarding discrepancies with known causal relationships is important, but should be more clearly elaborated.
*   **Algorithm 3’s Practicality:** While theoretically sound, Algorithm 3 (with bias correction) adds complexity. The practical benefit of correcting for a potentially small bias needs to be weighed against the added computational cost.
*   **Missing Details:**  The hyperparameters used for the XGBoost classifier in the experiments are not fully specified. This hinders reproducibility.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   High novelty and potential impact.
*   Ambitious theoretical framework.
*   Promising empirical results suggesting scalability and accuracy.
*   Addresses a fundamental problem in statistical inference with a fresh perspective.

**Con:**

*   Theoretical results are complex and rely on strong assumptions.
*   Empirical evaluation could be more robust and comprehensive.
*   Practical considerations regarding the bootstrap and bias correction are not fully addressed.
*   Lack of complete experimental details for reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a bold and potentially groundbreaking paper. While the theoretical framework is complex and the empirical evaluation could be strengthened, the novelty of the approach and its potential to significantly impact the field of CI testing outweigh the current limitations. I believe the paper introduces a valuable new direction for research and warrants acceptance, despite some remaining open questions and potential flaws. The authors successfully demonstrate a creative and innovative way to apply modern machine learning techniques to a classical statistical problem. The focus on a classification based approach to CI testing is likely to inspire further research in this area. I would rate this as a strong accept.  I suggest the authors focus on clarifying the assumptions, providing more thorough experimental validation, and discussing the practical implications of their theoretical results in the final version.
