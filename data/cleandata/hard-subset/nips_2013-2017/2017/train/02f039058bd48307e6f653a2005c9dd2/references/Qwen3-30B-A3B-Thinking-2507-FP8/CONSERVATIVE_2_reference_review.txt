

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The key idea is to generate samples that mimic the conditionally independent distribution fCI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, then train a classifier to distinguish between original samples (labeled 1) and bootstrap samples (labeled 0). The paper claims theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap procedure and generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples. The empirical results show competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on synthetic and flow-cytometry datasets.

## Strengths
1. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical results to empirical validation.
2. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach is novel and addresses a challenging aspect of CI testing (generating samples from fCI under Z-dependent permutations).
3. The empirical results on the flow-cytometry dataset demonstrate potential practical utility.

## Weaknesses
The paper suffers from several critical weaknesses that undermine its claims of significant contribution and theoretical soundness:

1. **Theoretical guarantees are too weak to be practically meaningful**: Theorem 1 shows dTV(, fCI) = O(1/n^(1/dz)), which implies that the approximation error decreases extremely slowly with sample size n, especially when the dimension of Z (dz) is large. For example, with dz=10, the error decreases as n^(-0.1), which is practically negligible for reasonable sample sizes. This contradicts the paper's claim that the method "scales much better than expected" in practice. The authors provide no theoretical justification for this discrepancy between their worst-case analysis and empirical results.

2. **Insufficient empirical validation**: The paper only compares against two baselines (KCIT and RCIT) from 2016-2017, omitting more recent methods like KCIPT (cited in the paper) and other recent advances in CI testing. The synthetic experiments use a single noise model (post-nonlinear) without exploring more challenging settings. The real-world dataset analysis is limited to three causal graphs without proper statistical validation of the results.

3. **Inadequate comparison to related work**: The paper cites [10] (a permutation-based CI test) but fails to adequately contrast their nearest-neighbor bootstrap with the permutation approach. The paper claims their method is more efficient, but provides no runtime comparisons or analysis of computational complexity. The paper also doesn't explain why their bootstrap method would be superior to the permutation approach in general settings.

4. **Lack of rigorous statistical validation**: The paper claims "our algorithm outperforms the state of the art" based on ROC AUC scores, but provides no statistical significance tests to confirm that the improvements are meaningful. The claimed performance gains (e.g., "accuracy close to 1 for dimensions up to 70") are presented without confidence intervals or error bars.

5. **Misleading theoretical claims**: The paper claims that "any improvements in the field of binary classification imply an advancement in our CI test," but this ignores the fact that the performance of the CI test depends critically on the quality of the bootstrap procedure, which is the paper's main contribution. The bootstrap method itself may not benefit from advances in classification.

## Major Concerns
The most significant concern is that the theoretical guarantees provided in the paper are so weak that they don't justify the claimed practical advantages. The O(1/n^(1/dz)) bound suggests that the method would perform poorly in high-dimensional settings (which is precisely where CI testing is most challenging), yet the paper claims empirical performance that contradicts this theoretical analysis. The authors fail to provide a proper explanation for this discrepancy, which is a serious theoretical weakness.

The paper also makes an unwarranted leap from "we use classifiers" to "our method automatically benefits from advances in classification." This is a common pitfall in model-powered approaches that don't properly account for the interaction between the bootstrap procedure and the classifier.

## Minor Concerns
- The paper uses the term "model-powered" without clearly defining what this means in the context of CI testing.
- The bias correction in Algorithm 3 is not rigorously justified.
- The theoretical analysis assumes smoothness conditions that may not hold for many real-world datasets.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a novel bootstrap approach, the theoretical guarantees are too weak to support the empirical claims, and the validation is insufficient to demonstrate a meaningful advance over existing methods. The paper's central claim about scaling with dimension contradicts its own theoretical analysis without proper justification. For a method to be accepted at NIPS, it must demonstrate both theoretical soundness and significant practical advantages over established baselines, which this paper fails to do.

The authors would need to substantially strengthen their theoretical analysis, provide more comprehensive empirical validation against a wider range of baselines, and better explain the discrepancy between their theoretical bounds and empirical results to make a compelling case for acceptance.
