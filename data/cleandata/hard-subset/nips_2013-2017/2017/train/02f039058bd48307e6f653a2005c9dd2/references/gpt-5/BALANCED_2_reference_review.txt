Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “model-powered” conditional independence (CI) test that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n samples from fX,Y,Z, the method (i) sets aside n samples (U1), (ii) uses a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap on the remaining 2n samples (U2, U3) to create n “pseudo-null” samples U’2 by replacing Y in U2 with the Y of the 1-NN in U3 found using Z as the metric key, and (iii) trains a classifier to distinguish U1 (label 1) from U’2 (label 0). If accuracy exceeds chance, reject H0: X ⫫ Y | Z. A bias-corrected variant subtracts the accuracy of a classifier trained only on (Y,Z) from the accuracy using (X,Y,Z), aiming to remove residual label leakage caused by the bootstrap.

Theoretically, the authors show that the marginal distribution of a single bootstrap sample is close in total variation to fCI = fX|Z fY|Z fZ under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z), with a rate O(n^(-1/dz)) up to constants and low-density terms. They also derive generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization under “near-i.i.d.” dependence induced by the NN bootstrap, yielding rates involving the VC dimension and terms depending on dz and the tail mass G(ε) where fZ is small.

Empirically, the method (CCIT) is compared against KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear models (varying dim(Z)) and on the flow cytometry dataset; CCIT shows strong AUC, especially as dim(Z) increases. The procedure is computationally practical due to fast NN search and can leverage strong classifiers (e.g., XGBoost).

This work connects to: classifier-based two-sample/independence tests (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab), kernel CI tests (KCIT, RCIT), and permutation-based conditional tests (KCIPT by Doran et al.), as well as NN-based estimation for entropy/CMI and generalization theory under dependence (e.g., Rademacher bounds for mixing processes). The paper’s NN bootstrap offers a simple alternative to KCIPT’s LP-based permutation.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing reduction: turns CI testing into a flexible, modular two-sample problem where advances in classification directly translate to testing power.
- Simple, scalable NN bootstrap with intuitive Z-local Y-replacement; practical and easy to implement.
- Theoretical analysis that (a) quantifies closeness to fCI in total variation and (b) gives generalization bounds under near-independence; the latter is of independent interest.
- Strong empirical performance, especially in higher-dimensional Z, where kernel tests often struggle; code is provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Lack of size control/p-values: The procedure is presented with a heuristic threshold τ (and bias subtraction in CCITv2), but there is no formal guarantee that Type I error is controlled at a chosen level α. ROC/AUC summaries are useful but do not establish valid hypothesis testing under H0.
- Assumptions and rates: The TV bound’s dependence O(n^(-1/dz)) is very pessimistic in dz and relies on nontrivial smoothness (Assumption 1 with a “generalized curvature matrix” that is unusual and hard to interpret) and density regularity (Assumption 2). The role and verifiability of G(ε) and Lipschitzness of f(z) are not discussed in practice.
- Dependence structure: Although the paper analyzes “near-i.i.d.” samples due to shared U2/U3 in the NN bootstrap, the finite-sample consequences for classifier behavior (e.g., small systematic differences between classes) are not accompanied by rigorous calibration. The bias-corrected variant helps but remains heuristic without guarantees on size.
- Comparisons and baselines: KCIPT is acknowledged as most related, but not evaluated empirically. There is no comparison to CMI-based tests (e.g., KSG-style conditional MI + permutation) or recent nonparametric CR-type tests. Sensitivity to the choice of classifier, k in k-NN (k=1 is used), and distance metric is only briefly addressed.
- Experimental design details: The flow cytometry analysis uses “pseudo-ground-truth” and mixes many CI/non-CI tests without multiple-testing control. For synthetic data, power/size curves at fixed α are not reported (only AUC). τ is set as 1/√n without justification that this yields nominal size.

Quality
Technically interesting and mostly sound within the stated assumptions. The bootstrap-to-fCI TV analysis is valuable, and the generalization result for near-i.i.d. samples is carefully motivated. However, as a CI test, the lack of a calibrated null distribution or Type I control is a significant gap.

Clarity
The high-level idea is well explained, but parts of the theory are hard to parse:
- Assumption 1 (Ia(z)) is nonstandard; more intuition and simpler sufficient conditions (e.g., Lipschitzness of log f(y|z) in z under TV or Wasserstein) would help.
- Theorems include several constants and terms (e.g., G(ε)) whose practical implications are unclear.
- Notation has typos and inconsistencies (U′2 vs U0 2, etc.). A brief complexity discussion would help.

Originality
- The reduction of CI testing to classification is not entirely new in spirit, but the specific NN bootstrap to emulate fCI and the near-i.i.d. generalization analysis appear novel relative to KCIT/RCIT/KCIPT and classifier-based independence tests.

Significance
- If accompanied by valid calibration to control size, this could be a practical, powerful alternative to kernel CI tests, especially when dz is moderate-to-large and strong classifiers are available. As is, the methodological promise is high, but the testing aspect is incomplete.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a calibrated test with provable size control. Options include:
  - A label-permutation test between U1 and U′2 to obtain a null distribution of the chosen statistic (e.g., test accuracy or margin), taking into account near-dependence via sample splitting or repeated independent bootstraps.
  - Cross-fitting: generate multiple U′2 from disjoint U2/U3 splits and aggregate to reduce dependence.
  - Use k-NN with k→∞, k/n→0, and analyze the trade-offs; report sensitivity to k and metric.
- Report size/power at fixed α on synthetic data; show robustness to classifier choice and hyperparameters.
- Compare against KCIPT and at least one CMI-based test; include settings with mixed/discrete variables or clarify scope.
- Clarify Assumption 1 with interpretable sufficient conditions; tighten or simplify the TV rate statement and discuss practical regimes where it is meaningful.
- Minor: clean up notation/typos; describe computational complexity and NN choices; release scripts to reproduce all figures.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant, practical reduction with strong empirical promise.
- Novel and useful theoretical analysis of NN bootstrap closeness to fCI and generalization under near-i.i.d. data.
- Scales well and is easy to implement; code is available.

Arguments against acceptance
- No calibrated testing procedure or Type I error control; τ selection is ad hoc.
- Theoretical rates are very pessimistic in dz and rely on opaque assumptions.
- Missing key baselines (KCIPT, CMI-based tests) and size/power evaluations at fixed α.

Recommendation: Weak reject
This is a promising line of work with solid ideas and interesting theory, but the current version does not yet deliver a statistically valid CI test with controlled size, and empirical evaluation lacks key comparisons and calibration. Addressing these points would make a strong case for acceptance.
