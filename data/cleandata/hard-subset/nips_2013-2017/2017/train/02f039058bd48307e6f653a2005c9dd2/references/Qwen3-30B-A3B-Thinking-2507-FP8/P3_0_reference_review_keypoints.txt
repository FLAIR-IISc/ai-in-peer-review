- Summary: Reduce conditional independence testing to binary classification by generating f_CI samples via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap and training a classifier to distinguish real vs. bootstrapped samples, rejecting H0 if accuracy is above random.

- Theoretical contributions: Provide a TV-distance bound between bootstrapped samples and f_CI (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for near-i.i.d. samples (Theorem 2) with Rademacher complexity analysis.

- Empirical validation: Synthetic and flow-cytometry experiments show better ROC AUC than KCIT and RCIT, especially as the dimension of Z increases.

- Practical implementation: Method readily uses modern classifiers (e.g., XGBoost), making it applicable without specialized tooling.

- Weakness — Theorem 1 clarity: Bound contains complex unexplained terms (e.g., G(·)) and suggests poor scaling O(1/n^{1/d_z}) that conflicts with empirical results.

- Weakness — Bound behavior: The presence of terms like exp(-1/(n^{d_z} c_dz ε^{d_z+2})) is concerning and appears to loosen as n increases for fixed ε.

- Weakness — Computational cost: No complexity analysis for the nearest-neighbor bootstrap; brute-force NN can be O(n^2 d_z), raising scalability concerns.

- Weakness — Statistical testing: Empirical comparisons lack statistical significance testing, so reported improvements may not be meaningful.

- Weakness — Smoothness assumption: Theoretical smoothness assumption on f(y|z) is not discussed in practical settings or its robustness to violations.

- Clarity issue — Notation: Inconsistent notation for conditional densities (e.g., f(y|z) vs. f_{Y|Z}(y|z)) causes confusion.

- Clarity issue — Algorithm 1: The rationale for using 1-NN in Z and swapping Y-coordinates to approximate f_CI is not clearly explained.

- Clarity issue — Bias correction: Algorithm 3’s bias-correction via classifiers on different feature subsets lacks rigorous justification.

- Clarity issue — Theoretical intuition: Theorems are technical without sufficient intuitive explanation for why bounds hold.

- Originality: Combining nearest-neighbor bootstrap with classification for CI testing is a novel contribution.

- Originality caveat — Prior related work: Reduction to classification for independence was explored previously [19]; the paper doesn’t clearly distinguish the conditional case from that prior work.

- Originality caveat — NN usage: Nearest-neighbor methods for generating samples have been used in other contexts; comparisons to such approaches are insufficient.

- Originality caveat — KCIPT comparison: Inadequate discussion of how the NN bootstrap differs from KCIPT’s permutation/LP approach in theory and practice.

- Significance: CI testing is important for causal inference and related fields; claimed performance gains could be impactful if validated.

- Significance caveat — Dimensionality: Theoretical curse-of-dimensionality limits applicability (O(1/n^{1/d_z})), and the mismatch with empirical scaling is unexplained.

- Significance caveat — Limited real-data evaluation: Only one small real-world dataset (flow-cytometry) was used; broader evaluations are needed.

- Significance caveat — Comparisons: Insufficient benchmarking against other model-based CI approaches beyond mentions.

- Overall assessment: Promising and interesting approach with solid ideas and results but important theoretical, computational, and empirical gaps; recommend rejection pending major revisions.

- Pro arguments for acceptance: Novel reduction, theoretical guarantees for bootstrap, strong empirical results on synthetic data, practical implementation using standard classifiers.

- Con arguments for rejection: Questionable theoretical dimensionality behavior, missing computational complexity analysis, lack of statistical testing, unclear bias-correction, and overstated novelty.

- Suggested revisions: Explain and tighten Theorem 1 and its discrepancy with experiments, add complexity analysis for NN bootstrap, include statistical significance testing, rigorously justify bias correction, and expand related-work comparisons.
