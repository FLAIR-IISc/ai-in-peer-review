

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The key insight is to generate samples close to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, then train a classifier to distinguish between these simulated samples and the original data. The authors claim theoretical guarantees on the bootstrap samples and generalization bounds for the classification step.

## Strengths

The paper presents an interesting and potentially useful approach to CI testing. The reduction to binary classification is conceptually clear, and the nearest-neighbor bootstrap technique appears novel. The empirical results on synthetic data and the flow-cytometry dataset demonstrate competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods, which is promising. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of related work, particularly distinguishing their method from permutation-based approaches.

## Weaknesses

Despite these strengths, the paper contains several significant technical flaws that undermine its theoretical contributions and limit its scientific impact.

### Theoretical Inconsistencies

The most serious issue is the incorrect treatment of the total variation distance bounds. In the main text, the authors claim "d_TV(·, f_CI) = O(1/n^{1/dz})", but Theorem 1 presents a complex bound with inconsistent notation and unclear terms. For example:

1. The bound includes "n^d_z" which should be written as "n^{d_z}" for clarity and correctness.
2. The term "c3 ⇤ 21/dz (1/dz) (n^d_z)^(-1/d_z d_z)" is mathematically nonsensical and contains unclear notation.
3. The bound contains "G(2c_dz ε²)" where G(ε) is defined as P(f(Z) ≤ ε), but the argument 2c_dz ε² doesn't make sense dimensionally for a probability.

These notational inconsistencies suggest a lack of mathematical precision that undermines the theoretical claims.

### Algorithmic Flaws

Algorithm 1 contains a critical notational error: "Let v = (x0, y0, z0) ∈ U2 be the sample such that z0 is the 1-Nearest Neighbor (1-NN) of z (in `2 norm) in the whole data-set U2". This is confusing because z0 should be a coordinate value, not a sample. The algorithm should state that z0 is the Z-coordinate of the nearest neighbor, not the entire sample.

The theoretical justification for the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is also problematic. The authors claim that "if f_Z(z) is not too small, then z0 (the 1-NN sample from U2) will not be far from z." However, for continuous distributions, the probability that f_Z(z) is "too small" (i.e., near zero) is non-negligible, especially in high dimensions, which would make the bootstrap unreliable in practice.

### Experimental Design

The experimental evaluation has several issues:
1. The synthetic experiments don't clearly specify the dimensions of X, Y, and Z beyond "X and Y are dimension 1, and the dimension of Z scales."
2. The ROC AUC calculation method is unclear: "For generating each point in the plot, 300 data-sets were generated... Half of them are according to H0 and the other half are from H1. Then each of the algorithms are run on these data-sets, and the ROC AUC score is calculated from the true labels (CI or not CI) for each data-set and the predicted scores."

This description suggests the authors are averaging ROC AUC scores across data sets, which is not standard practice. ROC AUC is typically calculated per data set and then averaged, but the description here is ambiguous.

### Theoretical Claims

The paper's claim that "the samples generated by the bootstrap (in U'2) are close to f_CI under both hypotheses" is misleading. The bootstrap generates samples close to f_CI only under H0 (where f = f_CI), not under H1. This is a critical misunderstanding of the theoretical setting.

The generalization bound in Theorem 2 (ii) contains the term "d_TV(f, f_CI) b(n) 2 R_q(g_S) 1/2 1 d_TV(f, f_CI) + b(n) 2 + ⌘ + n", which appears to have a formatting error. The left-hand side should likely be a bound on the difference between the true risk and the Bayes risk, not a single expression.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents an interesting approach to CI testing, the technical flaws in the theoretical analysis are substantial. The inconsistent notation, unclear assumptions, and incorrect claims about the bootstrap's behavior under H1 significantly undermine the paper's scientific contribution. The experimental evaluation is also not sufficiently rigorous.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite of the theoretical section, with careful attention to mathematical precision and clarity. The authors should also clarify their experimental methodology and provide more detailed specifications of the experimental setup.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial technical flaws in the theoretical analysis that prevent a proper assessment of the method's validity. The paper's contribution is potentially valuable, but the current presentation fails to deliver on its theoretical promises.
