This paper proposes a novel model-powered conditional independence (CI) test, reducing the problem to a binary classification task. The core idea is to generate samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, label original samples as positive and bootstrapped samples as negative, and then train a classifier to distinguish between them. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (total variational distance) and generalization bounds for the classifier in a near-independent setting. Empirical evaluation on synthetic and real-world data demonstrates competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental problem in statistical inference with broad applications. The reduction to binary classification is a clever idea, allowing leveraging advances in supervised learning. The theoretical analysis is a significant contribution, providing guarantees on both the bootstrap procedure and the classification performance. The empirical results are encouraging, showing the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world experiments strengthens the evaluation. The detailed related work section is also commendable.

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. First, while the theoretical analysis is impressive, the assumptions required for the bounds (smoothness of f(y|z) and f(z)) might be restrictive in practice. It would be valuable to discuss the sensitivity of the method to violations of these assumptions and potentially investigate alternative assumptions.  Second, the performance gains over existing methods (KCIT, RCIT) are not always substantial in the empirical results, and sometimes the improvement is marginal. A more thorough comparison with stronger baselines, including more recent CI tests, could solidify the claim of superior performance. Third, the implementation details are not fully reproducible. While the authors provide a link to a GitHub repository, there isn’t enough information on specific versions of libraries, hyperparameter settings, or data preprocessing steps to guarantee reproducibility. Fourth, the discussion of the bias correction in Algorithm 3, while theoretically sound, could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why simply subtracting the bias of the Z/Y classifier is sufficient. A connection to the theoretical guarantees would be useful. Fifth, the choice of threshold ⌧ = 1/ p n seems arbitrary and lacks clear justification beyond being an upper bound on the variance. A more principled approach for setting this threshold, potentially based on a Bonferroni correction or similar, would be beneficial. Finally, the paper is very dense and could benefit from improved readability through clearer formatting, more concise phrasing, and additional illustrative examples.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to CI testing.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and classification performance.
*   Competitive empirical results demonstrating practical effectiveness.
*   Detailed related work and clear presentation of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Restrictive assumptions for the theoretical analysis.
*   Modest performance gains over existing methods in some experiments.
*   Lack of complete reproducibility details.
*   Limited discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical assumptions.
*   Threshold selection is not well justified.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The core idea is innovative, the theoretical analysis is strong, and the empirical results are promising. However, the restrictive assumptions, modest performance gains, and lack of reproducibility details prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. I believe that addressing these weaknesses would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a valuable addition to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the sensitivity of the method to violations of the smoothness assumptions and explore alternatives.
2.  Compare against a wider range of state-of-the-art CI tests.
3.  Provide a comprehensive description of the implementation details, including library versions, hyperparameter settings, and data preprocessing steps to ensure reproducibility.
4.  Provide a more intuitive explanation of the bias correction in Algorithm 3.
5.  Justify the choice of the threshold ⌧ with a more principled approach.
6.  Improve the readability of the paper through clearer formatting, concise phrasing, and illustrative examples.
