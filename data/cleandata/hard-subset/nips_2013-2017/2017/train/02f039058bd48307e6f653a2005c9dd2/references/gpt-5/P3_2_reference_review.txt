Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “model-powered” conditional independence (CI) test by reducing CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method splits data, uses a nearest-neighbor bootstrap on Z to construct synthetic samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z(x|z)fY|Z(y|z)fZ(z), labels original vs. bootstrapped samples, and trains a classifier to distinguish them. Under H0 (X ⫫ Y|Z), the distributions are indistinguishable and the classifier should be at chance; under H1, it should perform better. A bias-corrected variant compares a classifier using all features (X,Y,Z) with one using only (Y,Z) to remove the finite-sample bias induced by the bootstrap.

Theoretical contributions include: (i) a total-variation bound showing that a sample produced by the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is O(n^{-1/dz})-close to fCI under smoothness assumptions on fY|Z and fZ; (ii) generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization when one class (bootstrapped) is only “near-i.i.d.”; and (iii) a link between classifier risk and dTV(f, fCI). Empirically, the method (with XGBoost) outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear data and on CI checks derived from flow cytometry protein networks.

This work connects to classifier two-sample tests [Lopez-Paz & Oquab ‘16], but extends them to CI by generating an approximate fCI distribution via nearest-neighbor matching on Z. It resembles KCIPT [Doran et al. ‘14] in spirit (simulate fCI and run a two-sample test) but replaces an optimal transport/permutation step with a simpler 1-NN bootstrap and leverages modern classifiers instead of kernel tests.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and modularity: reduces CI testing to well-understood classification, allowing one to leverage strong learners and domain knowledge.
- Novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap construction with total-variation guarantees to approximate fCI; proof techniques and curvature assumptions are interesting and potentially reusable.
- Generalization analysis under near-independence is nontrivial and useful; connects learning-theoretic bounds to this setting.
- Strong empirical performance, especially in moderately high-dimensional Z where kernel tests can struggle; practical implementation with XGBoost; code released.
- Bias-corrected variant addresses a key finite-sample artifact of the bootstrap.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Curse of dimensionality: The TV closeness rate O(n^{-1/dz}) (and terms involving G(ε)) scales poorly with dz. This makes the theoretical guarantees weak in the high-dimensional Z regimes, which are common in CI testing for causal discovery. While empirical results are encouraging, the theory does not explain the observed robustness beyond favorable structure implicitly exploited by the classifier.
- Assumptions and interpretability: Assumption 1 (generalized curvature matrix for fY|Z) is nonstandard and difficult to verify in practice. The clarity and intuition of this assumption could be improved; e.g., can it be implied by Lipschitz/smoothness of log fY|Z in z uniformly in y? Similarly, the bounded Lipschitz assumption on fZ is plausible, but the role and magnitude of G(ε)=P(f(Z)≤ε) in the bounds makes them hard to interpret.
- Dependence across bootstrapped samples: Only the marginal of an individual bootstrapped sample is shown to be close to fCI; the samples in U′ are dependent. The generalization bounds address this, but the rates are loose (terms like (log n / n)^{1/3}) and include opaque o(n(1/ε))/n terms. It would help to state a clean corollary with explicit constants/choices that yields a readable finite-sample bound.
- Statistical testing vs. scoring: The paper uses ROC/AUC and a fixed threshold τ=1/√n, but does not provide a procedure with finite-sample type I error control at level α. Under H0, U1 and U′ are exchangeable; a permutation test on labels would give an exact (or at least asymptotically valid) test. As is, the method is a powerful scoring procedure but lacks calibrated p-values or level control.
- Sensitivity to nearest-neighbor quality: 1-NN in high-dimensional Z is fragile and sensitive to scale/irrelevant features; no ablations on distance metric, feature scaling, or k-NN variants. Also, multiple U1 points can share the same neighbor in U2, potentially inducing duplicates of y0 that classifiers can exploit even under H0. The bias-corrected variant mitigates this, but further analysis or constraints (e.g., matching without replacement or k-NN randomized draws) would strengthen the approach.
- Experimental details: Comparisons against KCIT/RCIT need more clarity on hyperparameter selection (e.g., kernel choices, random features in RCIT) and computational time/memory. Since classifier choice is central, ablations across learners/hyperparameters and cross-validation protocols would improve reproducibility. Reporting type I error and power at fixed α (not only ROC/AUC) would better reflect a testing objective.

Quality
Technically sound overall with carefully stated assumptions and nontrivial proofs. The main theoretical limitation is the dimensional dependence and the opacity of some terms in Theorem 2. The empirical section demonstrates strong performance but should assess level control and sensitivity to design choices to fully establish reliability as a test.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, but some notation and assumptions need tightening. Suggestions:
- Provide intuition for Assumption 1 and concrete sufficient conditions (e.g., bounded Hessian of log fY|Z). Clarify the relationship to Fisher information.
- Move key steps of Theorem 1’s proof sketch to the main text to explain where the dz-exponent arises and how G(ε) enters.
- Explicitly state a level-α testing procedure (e.g., permutation on labels within D under H0-exchangeability), and clarify how τ is selected in practice.
- Minor: streamline notation for distributions (e.g., ϕ for bootstrapped distribution), fix typos/symbols (e.g., 6? for not independent), and unify Algorithm 1/2 naming of partitions.

Originality
The reduction of CI testing to classification via an approximate fCI sampler is novel relative to kernel CI tests and classifier-based independence tests. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap for conditional decoupling and the generalization analysis under near-i.i.d. samples are original and interesting. KCIPT is the closest, but this work proposes a simpler, scalable construction with TV guarantees.

Significance
CI testing is central in causal discovery and graphical models; a method that scales by leveraging strong classifiers is impactful. If the testing aspect is calibrated (level control) and robustness to high-dimensional Z is better understood, this could become a widely used tool.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide a permutation-based calibration to obtain p-values and finite-sample level control? Under H0, are the labels in D exchangeable?
- How sensitive is CCIT to the choice of metric on Z, feature scaling, and presence of irrelevant Z coordinates? Any gains from k-NN (k>1) with randomized draws?
- Can you present ablations over classifier families and hyperparameters and report type I error/power at fixed α?
- Are there practical strategies to reduce dependence in U′ (e.g., matching without replacement or enforcing uniqueness constraints) and do they help?
- Could you add runtime comparisons versus KCIT/RCIT and report complexity of nearest-neighbor search for varying dz?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and appealing reduction; practical method with strong empirical results.
- New NN bootstrap with TV guarantees; nontrivial theory for near-i.i.d. generalization.
- Simple, scalable implementation leveraging modern classifiers; code released.

Cons for acceptance
- No calibrated hypothesis test with type I error control as presented.
- Theoretical rates degrade sharply with dz; assumptions are somewhat opaque.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity and fairness in empirical comparisons.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is compelling and empirically strong; adding a calibrated testing procedure (permutation-based) and strengthening clarity around assumptions and sensitivity would make it a solid NIPS contribution.
