

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach for Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The key insight is to generate samples close to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1), then train a classifier to distinguish between original samples (label 1) and bootstrapped samples (label 0). The method theoretically guarantees that the generated samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance (Theorem 1) and provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). The authors demonstrate empirical superiority over state-of-the-art kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic data and the flow-cytometry dataset.

The work relates to recent NIPS papers on CI testing [10, 28, 32] and builds on the idea of using classifiers for independence testing [19], but makes a significant conceptual shift by addressing the more challenging conditional case through a novel bootstrap method rather than permutation approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Approach**: The reduction of CI testing to binary classification via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap is conceptually elegant and represents a significant shift from kernel-based approaches. The method leverages advances in classification to solve a fundamental statistical problem.

2. **Theoretical Contributions**: The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) that justify the bootstrap procedure and classification approach. The characterization of the total variation distance between generated samples and f_CI is particularly valuable.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The empirical evaluation is thorough, including both synthetic experiments (with varying dimensionality of Z) and a real-world application on protein signaling network data. The results consistently show CCIT outperforming state-of-the-art methods.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with detailed pseudocode and sufficient technical depth for experts to understand the method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Dimensionality Limitation**: The theoretical guarantee d_TV(·, f_CI) = O(1/n^(1/dz)) implies poor scaling with high-dimensional Z (d_z), which is a significant limitation for practical applications. While the paper notes that empirical performance scales better than theory predicts, it lacks a convincing explanation for this discrepancy. The authors should investigate why this gap exists and provide either improved theoretical guarantees or practical recommendations for high-dimensional settings.

2. **Lack of Statistical Significance in Experiments**: The paper claims CCIT "outperforms the state of the art" but doesn't provide statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to confirm that the ROC AUC differences are significant. This is particularly important given the relatively small number of bootstrap runs (B=50) mentioned.

3. **Bias Correction Justification**: The bias correction in Algorithm 3 appears ad hoc. The paper states "the classifier ĝ0 is expected to have test loss roughly 0.5 - b" but doesn't provide theoretical justification for this claim or for the specific correction procedure. A more rigorous treatment would strengthen this aspect.

4. **Comparison with Recent Methods**: While the paper cites [10, 28, 32], it doesn't compare with more recent methods like [22] or [32] that may have addressed some of the limitations of earlier kernel-based approaches. A more comprehensive comparison would better establish the method's position in the current landscape.

5. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't analyze the computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap, which could be prohibitive for large datasets or high-dimensional Z. The authors should provide more detail on the computational requirements.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper with a novel approach to CI testing that addresses an important problem in causal inference. The theoretical contributions are significant and the empirical results are promising. However, the dimensionality limitation and lack of statistical significance testing in the experiments are notable weaknesses that need to be addressed.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of statistical testing by introducing a model-powered approach that leverages advances in classification. The method has potential for practical adoption in causal discovery applications.

## Recommendations

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper's novelty, theoretical contributions, and empirical results are strong, and the weaknesses can be addressed with relatively minor changes.

Specifically, the authors should:
1. Provide statistical significance testing for the empirical results
2. Investigate and explain the discrepancy between theoretical guarantees and empirical performance with respect to dimensionality
3. Strengthen the theoretical justification for the bias correction procedure
4. Include a more comprehensive comparison with recent methods in the literature

These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a valuable contribution to the field of causal inference and statistical testing.
