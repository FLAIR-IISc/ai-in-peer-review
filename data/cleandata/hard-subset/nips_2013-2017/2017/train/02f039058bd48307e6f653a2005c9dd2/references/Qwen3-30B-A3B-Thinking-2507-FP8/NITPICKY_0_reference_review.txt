

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors use a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution, then train classifiers to distinguish between the original samples and the bootstrapped samples. The main contributions include a novel bootstrap algorithm with theoretical guarantees, generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples, and empirical results showing improved performance over state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel reduction from CI testing to classification, which is an interesting approach that leverages advances in supervised learning. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a clever way to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution, and the theoretical guarantees in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 are valuable contributions. The empirical results on synthetic and real data demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Typographical Errors
The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies that undermine its professionalism. In the abstract, "f X,Y,Z (x, y, z) factorizes as f X,Y,Z (x, y, z) = f X|Z(x|z)fY |Z(y|z)fZ(z)" should use proper LaTeX notation (f_{X,Y,Z} instead of f X,Y,Z). Similarly, "f CI(x, y, z) , f X|Z(x|z)fY |Z(y|z)fZ(z)" should be "f_{CI}(x, y, z) = f_{X|Z}(x|z)f_{Y|Z}(y|z)f_Z(z)". The repeated "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" appears to be a typo for "The 31st Conference".

### Methodological Issues
Algorithm 1 contains a critical error: "Let u0 = (x, y0, z)" should be "u0 = (x, y0, z0)" to maintain the z-coordinate from the nearest neighbor sample. This would make the algorithm consistent with the description in the text.

Theorem 1's bound is extremely messy and hard to interpret: "d TV ( , fCI) b(n), 1 2 s c3 ⇤ 21/dz (1/dz) (n dz) 1/dz dz + ...". The notation is inconsistent and the expression appears to be missing parentheses. The bound should be presented in a clear mathematical form.

Theoretical analysis in Theorem 2 contains a typo: "d TV (f, fCI) b(n) 2 R q (g S ) 1 2 1 d TV (f, fCI) + b(n) 2 + ⌘ + n" which should be properly formatted as "d_{TV}(f, fCI) \leq b(n) \implies \frac{1}{2} - \frac{d_{TV}(f, fCI) + b(n)}{2} \leq R_q(g_S) \leq \frac{1}{2} - \frac{d_{TV}(f, fCI) - b(n)}{2} + \delta + \epsilon_n".

### Theoretical Concerns
The paper claims "novel analysis of Rademacher complexity bounds [4] under near-independence" but doesn't clearly explain how this differs from Mohri and Rostamizadeh's [21] work on non-i.i.d generalization bounds. The connection to existing literature is not sufficiently detailed.

The smoothness assumptions in Theorem 1 are not well-motivated. The paper states "analogous assumptions have been made on the Hessian of the density in the context of entropy estimation [12]" but doesn't explain why these assumptions are reasonable for CI testing.

### Empirical Evaluation
The synthetic experiments use a specific post-nonlinear noise model but don't justify why this is representative of real-world data. The paper claims "our algorithm outperforms the state of the art" without providing statistical significance tests for the comparisons.

The flow-cytometry dataset analysis mentions "X $ Y" which should be "X → Y" for directed edges. The paper also notes that "all three CI testers CCIT, KCIT and RCIT are fairly confident that these edges should be absent" but doesn't explain why this might be problematic for the ground truth.

### Missing Comparisons
The paper doesn't compare the computational complexity of their method to existing approaches, which is crucial for practical adoption. The time complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap (O(n log n) for kd-trees) isn't discussed relative to kernel-based methods.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents an interesting approach to CI testing that leverages classification. However, the numerous formatting errors, critical methodological issues in the algorithm description, and unclear theoretical bounds significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The theoretical contributions are potentially valuable but are obscured by poor presentation and unclear notation.

While the empirical results are promising, the lack of statistical significance testing for these comparisons and the insufficient theoretical justification for the assumptions limit the paper's impact. The paper would be substantially improved with a careful proofreading pass to fix all the formatting issues, clarifying the theoretical results, and providing more complete comparisons.

Given the number of minor but significant flaws in the paper, I recommend rejection. While the core idea is potentially interesting, the paper as written does not meet NIPS's standards for technical precision and clarity. The authors should address these issues before resubmitting.
