

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The key insight is to generate samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution fCI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, then train a classifier to distinguish between original samples (labeled 1) and bootstrapped samples (labeled 0). The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the closeness of the bootstrapped samples to fCI in total variation distance (Theorem 1), and establish generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). The approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic data and a flow-cytometry dataset. This work builds on recent advances in using classification for independence testing (e.g., [19]) but extends them to the more challenging conditional case.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Reduction to Classification**: The paper successfully reduces CI testing to a binary classification problem, which is a significant conceptual advance. This leverages the power of modern classifiers in high-dimensional settings, making CI testing more scalable than previous kernel-based methods.

2. **Theoretical Contributions**: The authors provide strong theoretical guarantees (Theorem 1) showing that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap produces samples close to fCI in total variation distance. The generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2) are particularly valuable, as they address a non-trivial technical challenge in the problem.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The empirical results are thorough, comparing with multiple state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic data (with varying dimensionality of Z) and a real-world biological dataset. The flow-cytometry experiments demonstrate practical utility for causal discovery.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-structured, with clear algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-3) and effective use of figures to illustrate results. The contributions are explicitly stated in Section 1.1.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical vs. Empirical Gap**: The theoretical bound in Theorem 1 shows dTV = O(1/n^(1/dz)), suggesting poor scaling with dimension. However, the empirical results show good scaling up to dimension 70 (Fig. 2a). The authors note this discrepancy but don't adequately explain why the theory underperforms compared to practice. A more detailed discussion of this gap would strengthen the paper.

2. **Bias Correction Justification**: The bias correction in Algorithm 3 (subtracting the loss of the classifier trained on (Y,Z)) is somewhat ad hoc. While the authors provide intuition, a stronger theoretical justification for why this correction works would be valuable. The paper should better explain why the bias is approximately equal to the loss of the (Y,Z) classifier.

3. **Classifier Selection**: The paper uses XGBoost as the classifier without discussing how to choose the best classifier for a given problem or how classifier choice affects performance. This is a notable gap in the methodology section, especially since the authors emphasize the flexibility of their approach.

4. **Limited Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper compares with KCIT, RCIT, and KCIPT but doesn't mention more recent methods like the approximate kernel-based conditional independence test by Strobl et al. (2017), which is directly relevant to the problem. A comparison with this work would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

5. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper mentions efficiency due to fast nearest-neighbor algorithms, it doesn't provide a detailed computational complexity analysis compared to existing methods. This is important for assessing practical utility.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of CI testing by proposing a novel model-powered approach that leverages advances in classification. The theoretical guarantees are strong, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods. The main weaknesses relate to the theoretical vs. empirical gap and the lack of detailed justification for the bias correction method.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors should address the following points in their revision:

1. Provide a more detailed explanation of the gap between theoretical guarantees and empirical performance (Section 3.2).
2. Strengthen the justification for the bias correction method in Algorithm 3.
3. Include a comparison with Strobl et al.'s (2017) approximate kernel-based CI test.
4. Add a brief discussion of how to select the best classifier for a given problem.

The paper aligns well with NIPS' focus on novel, theoretically grounded machine learning approaches with practical significance. The model-powered approach has potential for broader application beyond CI testing, making it an interesting contribution to the literature.
