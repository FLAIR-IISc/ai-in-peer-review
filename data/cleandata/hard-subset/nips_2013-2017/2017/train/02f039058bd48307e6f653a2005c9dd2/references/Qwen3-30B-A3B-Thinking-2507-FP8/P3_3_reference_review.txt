

# Peer Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing, reducing the problem to binary classification. The key insight is to generate samples that mimic the conditionally independent distribution f_CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, then train a classifier to distinguish between original samples and these bootstrap samples. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap method (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for the classification step under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), with empirical results showing superior performance over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Approach**: The reduction of CI testing to binary classification is a fresh perspective that leverages advances in supervised learning. While binary classification has been used for independence testing [19], this work extends it to conditional independence with a theoretically grounded bootstrap method.

2. **Theoretical Contributions**: The authors provide non-trivial theoretical guarantees (Theorem 1) showing that their bootstrap method produces samples close to f_CI in total variational distance. The generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2) are particularly valuable, addressing a gap in the literature.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on synthetic data show clear advantages over existing methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially as dimensionality increases. The flow-cytometry dataset application demonstrates practical relevance in causal discovery.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The algorithm is straightforward to implement and integrates well with existing classification tools, making it accessible to practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical-Practical Gap**: The paper acknowledges that "the performance with respect to dimension of Z scales much better than expected from our worst case theoretical analysis," but fails to provide a compelling theoretical explanation. The theoretical bound in Theorem 1 has a 1/n^(1/d_z) dependence that becomes extremely weak as dimensionality increases, yet the empirical results show strong performance even for high d_z. This gap undermines the theoretical contribution.

2. **Bias Correction Justification**: The bias correction in Algorithm 3 (subtracting the loss of a classifier trained on Y,Z coordinates) appears ad hoc. While empirically effective, it lacks theoretical justification, making it difficult to assess when this correction is appropriate.

3. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites [10] as most relevant but doesn't adequately compare with more recent approaches like the kernel-based methods in [28] or the more efficient implementations of RCIT. The computational complexity analysis is notably absent.

4. **Assumption Strength**: The theoretical guarantees require f(z) to have a bounded Lipschitz constant (Theorem 2), which is a strong assumption. It's unclear how restrictive this is in practical applications.

## Critical Analysis

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to a fundamental problem in causal inference. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a clever and efficient way to generate samples from f_CI, avoiding the computational complexity of permutation-based methods like KCIPT. The theoretical results are technically sound, but the disconnect between the worst-case theoretical guarantees and empirical performance is concerning.

The most significant weakness is the failure to address why the method works so well in practice despite the theoretical limitations. In high-dimensional settings (d_z > 10), the 1/n^(1/d_z) term becomes prohibitively large, yet the experiments show excellent performance. The authors should investigate whether their method implicitly exploits structure in the data that isn't captured by their theoretical assumptions.

The bias correction mechanism in Algorithm 3 is particularly problematic. It's not clear why training a classifier on Y,Z coordinates would provide a good estimate of the bias, and the paper doesn't provide theoretical justification for this approach. This makes the method less principled than the rest of the paper.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper correctly identifies KCIPT [10] as the most directly comparable work, noting that it uses a more expensive permutation method requiring linear programming. However, it underplays the relevance of RCIT [28], which is an approximate version of KCIT designed for efficiency with large sample sizes. A more thorough comparison with RCIT would strengthen the empirical claims.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with a novel approach to a fundamental problem. The theoretical contributions are non-trivial, and the empirical results are compelling. The main weakness is the lack of theoretical justification for the observed empirical performance, which is a critical gap in a theoretically-oriented venue like NeurIPS.

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, but the authors should address the theoretical-practical gap and provide better justification for the bias correction mechanism. A more thorough comparison with RCIT and a computational complexity analysis would also strengthen the paper.

**Key Revisions Needed**:
1. Provide theoretical explanation for why the method performs well in high dimensions despite the theoretical bounds
2. Justify the bias correction mechanism in Algorithm 3
3. Add computational complexity analysis and comparison with RCIT
4. Clarify the practical implications of the bounded Lipschitz constant assumption

This work has significant potential to advance CI testing and causal discovery, and with these revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NeurIPS.
