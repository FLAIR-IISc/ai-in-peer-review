## Peer Review for "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea – using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution and then leveraging powerful classifiers – is clever and potentially impactful. The paper’s strengths lie in its theoretical justifications (total variational distance bounds, generalization bounds) and initial empirical results demonstrating competitive performance, particularly in higher dimensions. However, the presentation is dense, some theoretical assumptions feel strong, and the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive. While the paper isn't a revolutionary breakthrough, it represents a solid contribution and, with revisions, could be a valuable addition to NIPS.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The authors propose a model-powered CI test that transforms the problem into a binary classification task. They introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap algorithm to generate samples approximating the distribution of X and Y independent given Z. Original samples are labeled ‘1’ and bootstrapped samples ‘0’, forming a dataset for classifier training. The performance of the classifier on a held-out test set then determines whether to reject the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (via total variational distance) and analyzes the generalization properties of the classifier in this near-independence setting.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of non-parametric CI testing, contrasting it with kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT, KCIPT) and entropy estimation approaches. It highlights the key advantage of their method – leveraging the power of modern classification techniques and avoiding the computationally expensive linear programming step required by KCIPT.  The connection to independence testing via permutation (as in [19]) is also well explained, emphasizing the difficulty of Z-dependent permutations and how their bootstrap addresses this.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem is a conceptually appealing and novel approach.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides theoretical guarantees in the form of total variational distance bounds and generalization bounds. While potentially restrictive, these bounds offer valuable insight into the behavior of the proposed method.
* **Empirical Performance:**  The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method (CCIT) outperforms existing state-of-the-art algorithms, especially as the dimension of Z increases. This suggests a potential advantage in higher-dimensional settings.
* **Modular Design:** The modularity of the approach—separating the bootstrap sampling from the classifier selection—allows for flexibility and potential integration of future advancements in classification.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The problem is well-defined and the authors clearly articulate their goals.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper is densely written and can be difficult to follow. The notation is heavy, and the presentation of the theoretical results could be streamlined. More intuitive explanations would be beneficial.
* **Strong Assumptions:** The smoothness assumptions on the underlying distributions (Assumptions 1 & 2) feel quite strong and may not hold in real-world scenarios. The sensitivity of the method to these assumptions isn't thoroughly investigated.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments show promising results, they are somewhat limited. The synthetic experiments primarily focus on a specific post-nonlinear noise model. A broader range of data distributions and noise levels would strengthen the conclusions.  The flow cytometry dataset results are promising but rely on ground truth derived from other causal discovery algorithms, which themselves have limitations.
* **Bias Correction Algorithm (Algorithm 3):** The motivation for Algorithm 3 is sound, but it adds complexity. The practical impact of this bias correction is not adequately demonstrated in the experiments. A clearer explanation of why the bias is present, and how the Y and Z classifier helps is needed.
* **Computational Cost:** While boasting efficiency, a detailed comparison of computational costs (training time, bootstrap sampling time) with existing methods is missing.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The approach is novel and offers a promising alternative to existing CI testing methods.
* The theoretical analysis, while potentially restrictive, provides valuable insight.
* The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance, particularly in higher dimensions.
* The modular design allows for future improvements and extensions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper's density and lack of intuitive explanations hinder understanding.
* The strong assumptions on the underlying distributions raise concerns about real-world applicability.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and rigorously demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method.



**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap algorithm and its connection to generating samples from the conditionally independent distribution?
* How sensitive is the performance of your method to violations of the smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 & 2)?
* Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of your method with existing CI testing algorithms?
*  Can you provide more details on the selection of the threshold ⌧ in Algorithm 2 and 3?
* What is the impact of different classifier choices (beyond XGBoost) on the performance of your method?
* In Algorithm 3, why is training a classifier only on Y and Z sufficient to estimate the bias?

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, addressing the concerns regarding the smoothness assumptions, and expanding the empirical evaluation. Specifically, they should provide more intuition for the theoretical results, explore the sensitivity to the assumptions, conduct more comprehensive experiments, and provide a more detailed cost analysis. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
