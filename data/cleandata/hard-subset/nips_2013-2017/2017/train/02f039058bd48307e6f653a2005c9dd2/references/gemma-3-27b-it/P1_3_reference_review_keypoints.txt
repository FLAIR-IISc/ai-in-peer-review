- Proposes framing conditional independence testing as a binary classification problem using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate conditionally independent samples.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees on bootstrap sample quality (total variation) and classifier generalization (Theorems 1 and 2).  
- Empirical results show competitive or superior CI test performance, especially as Z dimensionality increases, and promising results on flow cytometry data.  
- Positions contribution relative to kernel-based CI tests and recent classification-based independence tests, highlighting avoidance of expensive linear programming.  
- Claims novelty in combining nearest-neighbor bootstrap with classification and offering theoretical guarantees for the sampling process.  
- Method is computationally efficient compared to optimization-heavy alternatives.  
- Modular design allows plugging in different classifiers to adapt to data.  
- Introduces Algorithm 3 with bias correction to improve practical performance.  
- Theoretical guarantees rely on smoothness assumptions whose practical sensitivity is not thoroughly discussed.  
- Bounds contain unspecified constant factors (c, C), reducing interpretability and raising concerns about tightness.  
- Dependence on Z’s dimension in Theorem 1 may be problematic for very high-dimensional Z.  
- Generalization bounds depend on classifier VC dimension, but the paper gives little guidance on classifier choice or V estimation.  
- Bootstrap performance may be sensitive to random data partitioning; stability analysis is limited.  
- Synthetic experiments are somewhat limited; more diverse data-generating processes and parameter sweeps are needed.  
- Threshold selection (τ) in Algorithms 2/3 is ad hoc (suggested 1/√n); a more principled selection method is lacking.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, efficient and modular, with promising empirical results and bias-corrected algorithm.  
- Arguments for rejection: reliance on strong assumptions, unspecified constants in bounds, and insufficient empirical evaluation.  
- Overall assessment: significant contribution recommending acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: discuss sensitivity to smoothness assumptions, provide more interpretable/estimated constants, expand empirical evaluation, give guidance on τ selection (e.g., cross-validation), and discuss computational complexity of nearest-neighbor search.
