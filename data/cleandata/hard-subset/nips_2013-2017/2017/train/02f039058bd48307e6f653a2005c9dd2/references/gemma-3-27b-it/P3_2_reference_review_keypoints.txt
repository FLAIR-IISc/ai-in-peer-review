- Reduces conditional independence testing to binary classification by generating fCI samples via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap.
- Provides theoretical guarantees: total variation bounds for bootstrap samples and generalization bounds under a near-independence assumption (Theorems 1 & 2).
- Analyzes Rademacher complexity under near-independence, a contribution of independent interest.
- Empirically outperforms kernel-based CI tests (e.g., KCIT, RCIT), especially with high-dimensional Z, and is applied to a protein signaling dataset.
- Positions itself relative to prior work: builds on ML-for-inference trends, contrasts with kernel methods, and avoids KCIPT’s costly linear programming.
- Novelty: the reduction to supervised learning is conceptually appealing and leverages advances in classification.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis provides a foundation for method performance.
- Strength: strong empirical evidence of improved performance in relevant scenarios.
- Strength: computational efficiency from nearest-neighbor bootstrap and scalable classifiers.
- Strength: clear presentation and helpful figures (notably Fig. 1).
- Weakness: theoretical bounds depend on terms like 1/n^(1/dz), implying slow convergence with increasing Z-dimension and a theory–practice gap.
- Weakness: sensitivity to the threshold τ in Algorithms 2 and 3; current heuristic is insufficiently studied.
- Weakness: bootstrap bias correction in Algorithm 3 appears ad-hoc and needs more principled justification or alternatives.
- Weakness: limited comparison set — missing simpler CI methods (e.g., mutual information estimators) to clarify trade-offs.
- Weakness: reproducibility concerns — experimental details (library versions, exact parameters like XGBoost settings) are lacking despite a GitHub link.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, useful theoretical insights, promising empirical results, and potential interdisciplinary impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: theoretical bounds may not reflect empirical behavior, parameter/bias-correction issues need further work, and baseline comparisons are limited.
- Overall assessment: strong paper meriting acceptance with minor revisions.
- Suggested revisions: discuss theoretical limitations vs. practical scaling, analyze τ sensitivity, rigorously justify or redesign bias correction, include simpler-test comparisons, and improve reproducibility documentation.
