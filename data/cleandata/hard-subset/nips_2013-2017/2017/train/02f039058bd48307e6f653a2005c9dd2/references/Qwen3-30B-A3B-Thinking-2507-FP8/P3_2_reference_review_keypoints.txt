- Proposes reducing conditional independence testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate fCI-like samples.  
- Theorem 1: bootstrap samples are close to fCI in total variation with a bound O(1/n^{1/dz}).  
- Theorem 2: provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples.  
- Empirical results show the method outperforms kernel-based CI tests on synthetic and flow-cytometry data.  
- Practical contribution: a usable, model-powered CI test that leverages modern supervised learners.  
- Novel bootstrap: nearest-neighbor bootstrap is simple, avoids costly permutations, and has theoretical guarantees.  
- Theoretical guarantees: meaningful link between total variation distance and classification error, plus non-i.i.d. generalization analysis.  
- Empirical validation: well-designed experiments demonstrating advantages over KCIT/RCIT.  
- Clear motivation: effectively argues for model-powered approaches using supervised learning advances.  
- Theorem 1's bound is complex and lacks intuitive discussion for realistic n and dz values.  
- Omits empirical comparison to KCIPT, a direct competitor that uses permutations to simulate fCI.  
- Bias correction in Algorithm 3 is ad-hoc and lacks principled justification or estimation methods.  
- Smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1–2) are not discussed in terms of practical restrictiveness or real-data relevance.  
- Paper does not explain why empirical performance scales better with dim(Z) than worst-case theory predicts.  
- Terminology ambiguity: "generalized curvature matrix" is not clearly defined or referenced in standard literature.  
- Theorem 1 may contain practically vacuous terms (e.g., extreme exponentials) that are not addressed.  
- Claims about bootstrap efficiency are misleading without discussing nearest-neighbor search complexity (potentially O(n^2) naive).  
- Lacks sensitivity analysis to classifier choice and threshold τ in Algorithm 3.  
- Related work: coverage adequate but fails to concretely differentiate from KCIPT or justify performance gains without direct comparison.  
- Suggested improvements: simplify/clarify theoretical bounds, include KCIPT in experiments, better justify bias correction, discuss assumptions, and explain theory-practice gap.  
- Overall: technically sound and well-written with practical promise; theoretical parts need clearer exposition and empirical comparisons.  
- Scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 7/10, Significance 8/10; recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
