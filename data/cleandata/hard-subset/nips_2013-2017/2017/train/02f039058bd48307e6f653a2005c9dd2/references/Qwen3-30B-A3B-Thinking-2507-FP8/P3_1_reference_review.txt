

# Peer Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors introduce a "nearest-neighbor bootstrap" method to generate samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x,y,z) = f_X|Z(x|z)f_Y|Z(y|z)f_Z(z). These generated samples are then combined with original samples to train a classifier that distinguishes between the two distributions. If the classifier performs significantly better than random guessing, the null hypothesis of conditional independence is rejected.

The paper makes several theoretical contributions: (1) a proof that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap generates samples close to f_CI in terms of total variation distance (Theorem 1), (2) generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), and (3) empirical validation showing superior performance to state-of-the-art methods, particularly for high-dimensional Z.

## Quality Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound approach to CI testing with strong empirical validation. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) form the core of the paper's contribution, and while the proofs are deferred to the appendix, the main results appear to be well-motivated.

Theoretical strength: The bound in Theorem 1 (d_TV(, f_CI) = O(1/n^(1/dz))) correctly captures the curse of dimensionality, which is a fundamental challenge for non-parametric methods. This is a reasonable bound for nearest-neighbor approaches. However, the authors' empirical results show better scaling than this bound would suggest, which is an interesting tension between theory and practice that deserves more discussion.

Theoretical weakness: The smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) are not well-motivated. Assumption 1 defines a generalized curvature matrix with a complex definition that isn't clearly connected to standard smoothness conditions for densities. The authors cite entropy estimation work [12, 13] as motivation, but it's unclear why these specific assumptions are necessary for CI testing. A more detailed explanation of these assumptions' necessity and plausibility would strengthen the paper.

Empirical validation: The synthetic experiments (Fig. 2a) are well-designed, showing that CCIT outperforms KCIT and RCIT as the dimension of Z increases, which is the main theoretical claim. The flow-cytometry dataset results (Fig. 2b, Table 2c) provide a meaningful real-world application. However, the paper doesn't adequately separate the contribution of the bootstrap method from the choice of classifier (XGBoost). It's unclear whether the performance gain is due to the bootstrap method or simply because XGBoost is a powerful classifier.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written, with clear figures (Fig. 1) and pseudocode (Algorithms 1-3). The problem statement is precise, and the authors do a good job of explaining the intuition behind their approach.

Clarity issues:
1. The paper uses "f_CI" to denote both the conditionally independent distribution and the distribution of bootstrapped samples, which creates confusion.
2. The bias correction in Algorithm 3 is not well-motivated. The authors mention a small bias b > 0 in the test loss under H0, but don't clearly explain its origin or how it's estimated.
3. The definition of G(ε) in Definition 2 is not adequately explained in the main text, making Theorem 1 difficult to interpret.
4. The connection between the nearest-neighbor bootstrap and the conditionally independent distribution isn't fully explained in the main text (the intuition is good, but the theoretical justification could be clearer).

## Originality Assessment

The core idea of reducing CI testing to classification is not entirely novel—[19] previously used a similar approach for independence testing (X ⊥ Y). The key novelty here is the nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach for generating samples from f_CI, which differs from previous methods like KCIPT [10] that require solving an expensive linear program for permutation.

However, the nearest-neighbor technique is commonly used in non-parametric statistics, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish this approach from other nearest-neighbor based methods in the literature. The authors cite [10] as using a permutation of samples, but don't clearly explain how their nearest-neighbor bootstrap differs from other nearest-neighbor based approaches to CI testing.

I recommend a more thorough comparison with related work, particularly with [10] and other nearest-neighbor-based methods in the literature. The claim that their method is simpler and provides theoretical guarantees about the closeness to f_CI is valid, but needs stronger justification against existing approaches.

## Significance Assessment

The problem of CI testing is important in causal inference and Bayesian network learning, and the paper's claim that their method scales better with dimension than previous methods has significant practical implications. The empirical results support this claim, particularly for high-dimensional Z.

The significance is somewhat limited by the fact that the approach is specific to CI testing, though the "model-powered" philosophy of using supervised learning tools for statistical testing is promising for broader applications. However, the paper doesn't fully explore this broader implication.

The most significant contribution is the practical scalability of the method for high-dimensional Z, which is a major limitation of existing kernel-based methods [28, 32]. The authors correctly identify that high-dimensional Z is a key challenge in many causal inference applications.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of CI testing. The method is novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem.

However, there are some concerns about the theoretical motivation and clarity that need to be addressed. The connection between the theoretical guarantees and the empirical results could be stronger, particularly regarding the scaling behavior with dimension.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel approach to CI testing that reduces it to binary classification
- Theoretical guarantees for the nearest-neighbor bootstrap method
- Strong empirical results showing better scalability with dimension than state-of-the-art methods
- Practical application to protein signaling network data
- The "model-powered" approach could inspire similar methods for other statistical testing problems

**Weaknesses:**
- Unclear motivation for smoothness assumptions
- Theoretical analysis lacks sufficient connection to practical performance
- Insufficient separation of contributions between the bootstrap method and classifier choice
- Bias correction in Algorithm 3 is not well-motivated
- The paper doesn't fully explain why empirical performance exceeds theoretical bounds

## Recommendations

1. **Clarify the theoretical assumptions**: Provide more motivation for Assumptions 1 and 2, and explain why these are reasonable for CI testing applications.

2. **Improve the connection between theory and practice**: Discuss why the empirical results scale better than the theoretical bound (O(1/n^(1/dz))). This is an important point that the authors observe but don't explain.

3. **Clarify the bias correction**: Explain the origin of the bias b in Algorithm 3 and provide justification for the bias correction method.

4. **Separate the contributions**: Conduct additional experiments to show that the performance gain is due to the bootstrap method rather than just the choice of classifier (e.g., test with other classifiers like neural networks or SVMs).

5. **Strengthen the related work**: Provide a more thorough comparison with nearest-neighbor based methods in the literature, particularly [10] and other similar approaches.

## Final Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to CI testing with a novel method that is theoretically grounded and empirically validated. The concerns I've raised are important but can be addressed with relatively minor revisions to the paper. The practical significance of the method, particularly for high-dimensional Z, is substantial and aligns well with the NIPS focus on practical machine learning applications.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
