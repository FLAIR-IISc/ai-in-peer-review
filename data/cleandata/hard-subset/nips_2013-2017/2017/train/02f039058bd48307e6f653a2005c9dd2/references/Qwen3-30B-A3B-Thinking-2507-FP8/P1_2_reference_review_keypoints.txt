- Proposes converting conditional independence testing into a binary classification problem using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate f_CI-like samples.
- Key insight: if X and Y are conditionally independent given Z, original and bootstrapped samples should be indistinguishable to a classifier.
- Introduces a nearest-neighbor bootstrap with theoretical guarantees on closeness to f_CI (Theorem 1, total variation bounds).
- Provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples (Theorem 2).
- Empirical evaluation shows the method outperforms state-of-the-art, especially as Z’s dimensionality increases.
- Demonstrates practical utility on a real-world protein signaling (flow cytometry) dataset.
- Quality: rigorous theoretical analysis and well-structured proofs connecting theory to practice.
- Originality: novel CI→classification reduction and a simple, clever nearest-neighbor bootstrap for Z-dependent permutations.
- Significance: method scales better empirically in high dimensions and can leverage advances in binary classification models.
- Clarity: paper is well-organized, with illustrative figures and pseudocode for algorithms.
- Weakness: theoretical bounds depend strongly on dim(Z) (error term O(1/n^(1/d_z))), limiting theoretical guarantees in very high dimensions.
- Weakness: bias correction in Algorithm 3 appears ad-hoc and lacks rigorous justification.
- Weakness: smoothness assumptions may be restrictive for some real-world applications.
- Weakness: some theoretical results and notation are complex or unclear, making parts hard to follow and implementation choices (e.g., threshold τ) insufficiently explained.
- Weakness: lacks a thorough comparison of computational efficiency with existing methods.
- Pro-accept: novel approach, strong theoretical contributions (first analysis for near-independent classification), and comprehensive empirical gains.
- Con-accept: dimensional dependence, unproven bias correction, and insufficient computational complexity analysis could limit applicability.
- Suggestion: provide deeper discussion/intuition on practical impact of d_z scaling and why empirical scaling is better than theory predicts.
- Suggestion: give rigorous justification or alternative for the bias correction in Algorithm 3.
- Suggestion: add computational efficiency comparisons to existing methods, especially for high-dimensional settings.
- Suggestion: improve notation consistency (e.g., f_CI vs f_CI(x,y,z) ) and clarify practical choices like threshold τ.
- Suggestion: explicitly contrast computational complexity with permutation-based approaches (e.g., [10]).
- Final recommendation: strong accept (Overall rating 8) — significant advance with solid theory and empirical validation despite some limitations.
