This paper proposes a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to a binary classification task. The core idea is to generate samples that mimic the conditionally independent distribution using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap and then train a classifier to distinguish between original samples and the generated ones. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for the classifier. Empirical results on synthetic data and a flow cytometry dataset demonstrate promising performance compared to existing methods.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning with wide-ranging applications. The reduction to a binary classification problem is a clever idea, allowing the leveraging of recent advances in classification algorithms. The theoretical analysis, including the total variational distance bound and the generalization error bound, is a significant contribution, and the empirical validation supports the proposed approach.  The presentation is mostly clear and well-organized, with Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 clearly outlining the key steps.

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. First, the theoretical analysis, while substantial, is quite complex and relies on several assumptions (smoothness of f(y|z) and f(z)). The practical implications of these assumptions and their sensitivity to violations are not thoroughly discussed. For example, the authors mention that the performance scales better than expected in practice, but don't deeply analyze *why* the worst-case theoretical bounds aren't tight. A more intuitive explanation or discussion of the limitations of the theoretical guarantees would be helpful.  

Second, the empirical evaluation, while demonstrating good performance, could be more comprehensive. The comparison with existing methods (KCIT and RCIT) is limited, and the parameter tuning details for each algorithm are not fully provided.  The performance gains on the flow cytometry dataset are not dramatically superior, and the discussion of potential discrepancies in the ground truth could be expanded. More thorough ablation studies, for example, examining the impact of different classifiers or nearest neighbor search algorithms, would strengthen the empirical results. 

Third, while the paper introduces Algorithm 3 as a bias-corrected version, the motivation for its effectiveness could be more clearly articulated. The explanation regarding subtracting the bias from the test loss feels somewhat hand-wavy and would benefit from a more rigorous justification. The effect of the threshold ⌧ on performance is not explored in detail.

Fourth, the related work section, although comprehensive, could benefit from discussing more recent advancements in non-parametric CI testing, particularly those utilizing deep learning techniques beyond just the references provided.  Specifically, newer methods that directly estimate conditional mutual information using neural networks (e.g., those based on Neural Information Estimation) might be relevant.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a binary classification problem is a novel and promising approach.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper provides valuable theoretical guarantees, including bounds on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and generalization error bounds.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* **Potential Impact:** The approach has the potential to benefit a wide range of applications, including causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* **Complexity of Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical analysis is complex and relies on strong assumptions.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and include more detailed comparisons with existing methods.
* **Bias Correction Explanation:** The justification for Algorithm 3's bias correction is not entirely clear.
* **Lack of Discussion of Assumption Limitations:** The paper does not adequately discuss the practical implications and sensitivity of the smoothness assumptions.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of CI testing. The core idea is innovative, and the theoretical and empirical results are promising. However, the complexity of the theoretical analysis, the limited scope of the empirical evaluation, and the lack of detailed discussion regarding the assumptions warrant some concerns.  

**Recommendation:**

Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit.  

Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical results and discuss the limitations of the assumptions.
2.  Conduct a more comprehensive empirical evaluation with detailed parameter tuning and ablation studies.
3.  Provide a more rigorous justification for the bias correction in Algorithm 3.
4.  Expand the related work section to include more recent advancements in non-parametric CI testing.
5.  Consider including a discussion of computational cost and scalability of the proposed algorithm.
