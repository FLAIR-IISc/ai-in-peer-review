

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper introduces a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification through a model-powered framework. The authors propose a nearest-neighbor bootstrap method (Algorithm 1) to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI, which are then used to train a classifier to distinguish between original samples and the bootstrapped samples. The key innovation is recognizing that CI testing can be transformed into a two-sample test between original data and data generated under conditional independence, which can be effectively handled by modern classifiers.

The paper's main contributions are: (i) a novel reduction of CI testing to binary classification, (ii) theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1), (iii) generalization bounds for classifiers under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), and (iv) empirical validation showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods. The authors demonstrate that their CCIT algorithm (Algorithm 3) outperforms kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT, particularly in high-dimensional settings where Z has large dimensionality.

This work stands out for its bold conceptual shift from the dominant kernel-based approaches to a model-powered framework. While prior work has used classification for independence testing (e.g., [19]), extending this to conditional independence is non-trivial, especially since the permutation required for CI is Z-dependent. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap provides a clever solution to this problem, and the theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) showing d_TV(, f_CI) = O(1/n^{1/d_z}) under smoothness assumptions is a significant contribution. The authors further provide the first generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), which is of independent interest.

The empirical results are compelling. In synthetic experiments (Figure 2a), CCIT maintains high AUC up to dimension 70 for Z, while other methods degrade significantly. On the flow-cytometry dataset (Figure 2b, Table 2c), CCIT consistently outperforms KCIT and RCIT across three different causal graphs. The authors also note that the performance scales better in practice than the theoretical worst-case analysis suggests, which is a valuable practical observation.

The paper's greatest strength is its conceptual novelty and the elegant reduction of CI testing to a well-studied classification problem. This approach leverages the rapid advances in classification directly, making CI testing more accessible to practitioners and opening new research directions. The connection to current trends in machine learning (using powerful classifiers to solve statistical problems) is particularly timely.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The smoothness assumptions in Theorem 1 may be restrictive in some applications, though the authors acknowledge this limitation.
2. The method requires 3n samples (as opposed to 2n for some competing methods), which could be a limitation in small-sample settings.
3. The theoretical analysis is quite complex, which might make it challenging for some readers to follow the exact guarantees.

However, these are minor concerns given the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The authors provide thorough experimental validation, and the methodological shift from kernel-based approaches to model-powered classification is substantial enough to warrant attention.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field. It introduces a fundamentally new approach to CI testing that is both theoretically grounded and empirically effective. The model-powered framework represents a paradigm shift in how we think about statistical testing problems, and the authors successfully demonstrate its advantages through both theory and experiments.

The paper aligns well with NeurIPS' interest in novel machine learning approaches to statistical problems. It's particularly relevant to recent trends in "learning to test" and leveraging supervised learning for statistical inference. The work is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on CI testing, which have primarily focused on kernel methods ([10, 28, 32]).

In conclusion, this paper presents a bold and novel approach to conditional independence testing with strong theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. The conceptual shift from kernel-based methods to model-powered classification is significant and represents a meaningful advance in the field. While there are minor technical limitations, the overall contribution is substantial and would be valuable to the NeurIPS community.

**Recommendation: Accept**
