

# Review: Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The core idea involves generating samples that mimic the conditionally independent distribution f_CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1), then using classifiers to distinguish between original samples (from the true joint distribution) and the bootstrap samples (close to f_CI). The paper provides theoretical guarantees showing that the bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance (Theorem 1), generalization bounds for the classifier under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), and demonstrates empirical superiority over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic and flow-cytometry data.

## Quality Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical contribution (Theorem 1) is significant, providing a concrete guarantee that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap produces samples close to f_CI with rate O(1/n^(1/dz)), which is a meaningful theoretical result for a practical method.
- The paper addresses an important limitation of existing kernel-based CI tests (like KCIT and RCIT) by providing a more efficient way to generate samples from f_CI without expensive linear programming.
- The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed method, particularly in high-dimensional settings where other methods fail to scale (Fig. 2a shows CCIT maintaining high accuracy up to dimension 70, while competitors degrade significantly).

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical analysis assumes smoothness conditions (Assumptions 1-2) that may not hold for many real-world datasets, particularly those with discontinuous densities or heavy-tailed distributions.
- The bias correction in Algorithm 3 is not rigorously justified - the paper states that "the classifier is expected to distinguish between the two data-sets only based on the Y, Z coordinates," but doesn't prove that the bias b(n) is correctly estimated by the difference in losses.
- The empirical evaluation lacks comparisons with other model-based approaches (e.g., deep learning-based CI tests) and doesn't thoroughly analyze computational complexity, which is crucial for practical adoption.

The paper is technically sound and provides well-supported claims, but the theoretical guarantees have specific assumptions that limit their applicability. The experimental validation is comprehensive for the problem domain but could be strengthened with more extensive benchmarking.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written with a clear structure. The main ideas are effectively communicated through Figure 1 and Algorithm 1. The theoretical contributions are presented in a logical sequence, building from the bootstrap method to the classification approach to the theoretical guarantees.

However, some sections could be improved for clarity:
- The relationship between the theoretical results (Theorems 1-2) and the practical implementation (Algorithms 2-3) could be made more explicit.
- The notation for the total variation distance (d_TV) is used consistently but could benefit from a brief intuitive explanation for non-specialists.
- The flow-cytometry experiment (Section 4.2) would be clearer if it explicitly stated why the "discrepancies" in edges (pkc-raf, pkc-mek, pka-p38) might exist in the ground truth graphs.

## Originality

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by introducing a novel bootstrap method (Algorithm 1) for CI testing that differs fundamentally from existing approaches. While classification-based approaches to independence testing have been explored (citing [19]), this is the first to extend the approach to conditional independence with a theoretically grounded bootstrap.

The paper effectively distinguishes its contribution from related work, particularly KCIPT [10], by showing that their nearest-neighbor bootstrap is simpler and provides theoretical guarantees on the quality of the generated samples. The paper also properly cites relevant prior work in kernel-based CI tests (KCIT [32], RCIT [28]).

The approach represents a novel combination of statistical testing and machine learning, leveraging advances in classification to address a challenging statistical problem. This "model-powered" perspective is both novel and promising for future work in statistical testing.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in causal inference and graphical models where CI testing is fundamental. The proposed method has clear practical significance as it outperforms state-of-the-art methods, especially in high-dimensional settings where existing methods fail.

The significance is enhanced by the paper's demonstration that the method scales better in practice than theoretical bounds suggest (Section 1.1), which is valuable for practitioners. The "model-powered" approach also opens a new paradigm for statistical testing that could be applied to other problems beyond CI testing.

However, the paper doesn't fully demonstrate how much more scalable the method is compared to competitors in terms of computational complexity. While the empirical results show better performance, a more detailed analysis of time complexity would strengthen the significance claim.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of causal inference and statistical testing. It presents a novel, well-motivated approach with theoretical guarantees and empirical validation that demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The quality is high, the ideas are well-justified, and the paper is clearly written.

The main weaknesses are the specific theoretical assumptions (which could be more thoroughly discussed) and the limited analysis of computational complexity. These are important but not critical flaws for a NeurIPS submission.

**Recommendation: Accept** (Strong Accept)

The paper advances the state of the art in CI testing in a meaningful way, and the "model-powered" approach has the potential to influence future work in statistical testing. The empirical results are compelling, and the theoretical contributions are non-trivial. While the theoretical guarantees have specific assumptions, the paper acknowledges these limitations and demonstrates strong practical performance.

The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS as it bridges statistical testing and machine learning, which is a key focus area for the conference. The approach of leveraging powerful supervised learning tools for statistical testing is particularly relevant to NeurIPS' interests in the intersection of machine learning and statistics.
