Summary
The paper proposes CCIT, a “model-powered” conditional independence (CI) test that converts CI testing into a two-sample classification problem. Given 3n i.i.d. samples of (X,Y,Z), the authors construct an approximate sample from the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z(x|z)fY|Z(y|z)fZ(z) via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap: split data into U1,U2,U3; for each u∈U2, find its 1-NN in U3 in Z-space and replace Y with the neighbor’s Y, producing U02. They then perform binary classification between U1 (label 1, drawn from fX,Y,Z) and U02 (label 0, close to fCI). If a classifier can discriminate, reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. A bias-corrected variant trains a classifier on (Y,Z) alone to estimate residual bias and subtract it. Theoretical contributions include: (i) a total variation (TV) bound showing a single NN-bootstrapped sample’s distribution is O(n^{-1/dz})-close to fCI under smoothness assumptions; (ii) generalization bounds for ERM classifiers trained on “near-i.i.d.” samples induced by the NN bootstrap; (iii) a connection between classification error and dTV(f,fCI). Experiments on synthetic post-nonlinear models (varying dim(Z) up to 70) and the flow cytometry protein data compare CCIT against KCIT and RCIT, reporting superior ROC-AUC and good empirical scaling.

Relation to prior work
The paper is related to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and to permutation-based CI testing (KCIPT), as well as to classifier two-sample tests and NN-based estimators for entropy/CMI. The main difference from KCIPT is replacing an expensive Z-dependent permutation (solved by LP) with a simple NN bootstrap, and replacing MMD-style two-sample testing with supervised classifiers. The approach is philosophically close to “reductions to classification” and to conditional resampling methods, but does not rely on parametric regression.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: turning CI testing into a supervised two-sample classification task enables leveraging strong off-the-shelf classifiers (e.g., gradient boosting, DNNs) with good high-dimensional performance.
- Technical novelty: a clean nearest-neighbor conditional resampling scheme with a nontrivial TV-distance guarantee to fCI, and generalization bounds under near-independence that quantify deviations from i.i.d. due to NN reuse.
- Modularity: any advances in classification can translate to improved CI testing power.
- Empirical results suggest strong performance and scalability in dim(Z), and the code is released.
- The bias-correction idea (subtracting the (Y,Z)-only classifier’s loss) is intuitive and addresses finite-sample artifacts of the bootstrap.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Statistical calibration and Type I control: The procedure as presented does not yield calibrated p-values or explicit finite-sample control of the false positive rate at a target level α. The threshold τ is set heuristically (e.g., 1/√n) and depends on an unspecified bias b(n). For CI testing in structure learning pipelines, calibrated tests are crucial. The theory bounds risk but does not map to level-α testing with guaranteed size.
- Strong and somewhat opaque assumptions: Assumption 1 (bounded “generalized curvature” of f(y|z)) and the smoothness/integrability conditions on f(z) are heavy. The exposition of Assumption 1 is hard to parse, and the constants and regimes under which the bounds are meaningful are not clarified. The guarantees depend on G(ε)=P(f(Z)≤ε), which can be large in high dimensions and low-density regions; practical implications are not discussed.
- Rates dominated by dim(Z): The core TV error is O(n^{-1/dz}), which is very slow for large dz. While experiments are encouraging, the theory suggests a curse of dimensionality that could bite in more complex settings or for moderate n. There is little guidance on choosing k>1 NN or other smoothing to improve bias-variance tradeoffs.
- Dependence structure and reuse of neighbors: The NN bootstrap induces complex dependencies (e.g., multiple U2 points choosing the same Y from U3). The generalization bound is interesting but the result has unusual terms (e.g., n^{-1/3} rates), and the constants/conditions are not made explicit enough to judge when the learning-theoretic guarantees are tight.
- Experimental evaluation details: 
  - Comparisons to KCIT/RCIT appear to use default hyperparameters; kernel tests are sensitive to bandwidths. It is unclear whether careful tuning was performed, which could bias results.
  - No runtime comparisons, despite claims of efficiency via fast NN search.
  - The flow-cytometry evaluation uses CI/non-CI pairs derived from learned graphs; while common, the “non-CI” construction assumes faithfulness and may still include ambiguous cases. Ground truth is acknowledged as uncertain, but this complicates interpretation of AUC.
- Lack of guidance for mixed/categorical Z or metric choice: The approach hinges on Euclidean NN in Z; extensions to mixed data are not discussed.

Quality
The main ideas are sound and the reduction is well-motivated. The theoretical results are nontrivial, but assumptions are strong and explanations could be clearer. Empirical results are promising but would benefit from stronger baselines, tuning, runtime, and calibration analyses.

Clarity
Overall clear, but several notational and editorial issues: the “generalized curvature” assumption needs intuitive explanation; the constants in Theorem 1 are hard to interpret; “d” is overloaded; figures/tables are referenced but not included here. A section on practical choices (distance metrics, k-NN, classifier class, cross-fitting) would help.

Originality
Moderately high. While reductions to classification and conditional resampling are known, the specific NN-bootstrap construction for CI with TV guarantees and the generalization analysis under near-independence are novel.

Significance
Potentially high practical impact: a simple, flexible CI tester that leverages modern classifiers can be very useful. The lack of calibrated testing and strong assumptions temper the immediate impact for structure learning workflows that need p-values.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a calibrated test with level-α control, e.g., via Monte Carlo calibration using repeated NN bootstraps to estimate the null distribution of the test statistic, cross-fitting, or a label-flip test on the held-out set.
- Explore k-NN (k>1) or local regression to reduce bias; analyze its effect theoretically and empirically.
- Include thorough hyperparameter tuning for KCIT/RCIT, and report runtimes.
- Add ablations: classifier choice, k, metric choice, effect of dz and n, sensitivity to low-density Z regions.
- Clarify and motivate Assumption 1; simplify Theorem 1’s statement with interpretable constants and regimes.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The idea is elegant and practical, with novel analysis and strong empirical promise. However, the lack of calibrated Type I error control and heavy assumptions are concerns. With added calibration and stronger empirical baselines, this could be a solid contribution.

Reviewer confidence: High in methodology and related work; moderate on the technical proof details due to dense assumptions.
