### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel, model-powered approach for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning. The core idea is to reduce the CI test `X ⊥ Y | Z` to a binary classification task. This is achieved by creating two datasets: one with original i.i.d. samples from the joint distribution `f(x, y, z)` (labeled '1'), and another with synthetic samples designed to approximate the conditionally independent distribution `f_CI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)` (labeled '0'). A powerful classifier is then trained to distinguish between these two sets. If the classifier achieves an accuracy significantly better than random guessing, the null hypothesis (H0) of conditional independence is rejected.

The key technical innovation is the method for generating the synthetic samples: a "nearest-neighbor bootstrap." For each sample `(x_i, y_i, z_i)`, the algorithm finds another sample `(x_j, y_j, z_j)` whose `z_j` is the nearest neighbor to `z_i`, and then creates a new synthetic sample by swapping the y-coordinates: `(x_i, y_j, z_i)`. The paper provides theoretical justification for this procedure, proving that the distribution of these synthetic samples is close in total variation (TV) distance to the true CI distribution. Furthermore, it derives generalization bounds for the classifier's performance, carefully handling the fact that the synthetic samples are not strictly i.i.d. Empirical results on synthetic and real-world flow cytometry data show that the proposed method, named CCIT, outperforms state-of-the-art kernel-based methods like KCIT and RCIT, particularly as the dimension of the conditioning set Z increases.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The reduction of CI testing to binary classification is an elegant and powerful idea. It falls into the highly valuable category of "machine learning reductions," allowing the vast and rapidly advancing toolkit of supervised learning (e.g., gradient boosted trees, deep neural networks) to be brought to bear on a classical statistical problem. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could have a broad impact, as CI testing is a cornerstone of causal discovery, graphical modeling, and feature selection.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is substantial and addresses the two most critical aspects of the proposed method.
    *   **Theorem 1:** Justifying that the nearest-neighbor bootstrap actually works by bounding the TV distance between the generated distribution and the target `f_CI` is crucial and non-trivial. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the entire approach.
    *   **Theorem 2:** The analysis of generalization bounds under "near-independence" is particularly impressive. Recognizing that the bootstrapped samples are not i.i.d. and then adapting Rademacher complexity analysis to this setting is a sophisticated step. This result is of independent interest for scenarios where machine learning models are trained on correlated data.

3.  **Practical Performance:** The empirical results are compelling. The method's strong performance as the dimensionality of Z increases (Fig. 2a) is a key advantage, as this is a known weakness of many non-parametric, especially kernel-based, methods which often suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The superior performance on the flow cytometry dataset further validates its practical utility.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The core idea is explained intuitively in the introduction and with a helpful diagram (Fig. 1). The algorithms (CCITv1 and the bias-corrected CCITv2) are presented in clear pseudocode, aiding reproducibility.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** There is a significant disconnect between the theoretical convergence rates and the excellent empirical performance. The rate of convergence in Theorem 1, which scales as `O(n^(-1/d_z))`, is extremely slow and suggests a severe curse of dimensionality. Yet, the experiments show the method scaling well up to `d_z = 70`. The authors briefly acknowledge this but do not offer a satisfying explanation. Is the theoretical bound loose? Or, more likely, are the powerful classifiers implicitly discovering a lower-dimensional manifold or structure in Z that the worst-case analysis does not capture? A more detailed discussion of this gap would greatly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** The smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) are quite technical and may not be intuitive to a general audience. Assumption 1, concerning the "generalized curvature matrix," and Assumption 2, on the Hessian of `f(z)`, are central to the main theoretical result. It would be very helpful to include a brief discussion on what classes of common distributions satisfy these assumptions, and which might violate them. This would help practitioners gauge the applicability of the theoretical guarantees to their specific problems.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method introduces several hyperparameters: the choice of classifier, its internal parameters, and the threshold `τ` in Algorithm 3. The paper sets `τ = 1/√n` with a brief justification, but the method's sensitivity to this choice is not explored. A more robust approach might involve setting the threshold via a permutation test on the final test statistic (`ˆL(ĝ', D'_e) - ˆL(ĝ, D_e)`), which would provide a data-driven p-value. A discussion on the robustness of the method to the choice of classifier and `τ` would be valuable.

4.  **Scope of Experiments:** The synthetic experiments are conducted with `d_x = 1` and `d_y = 1`. While scaling with `d_z` is critical, the performance when X and Y are also high-dimensional is an important and unaddressed question. The effectiveness of the classifier depends on its ability to handle high-dimensional inputs `(x, y, z)`. It is plausible that performance could degrade, and exploring this regime would make the evaluation more comprehensive.

5.  **Comparison with Related Work:** The paper positions itself against kernel methods like KCIT and RCIT. However, the most conceptually similar work appears to be KCIPT [10], which also uses a permutation scheme to generate samples from `f_CI`. The authors argue their nearest-neighbor approach is simpler than the expensive linear program in KCIPT. While plausible, a direct empirical comparison of runtime and statistical power against KCIPT would be a much stronger and more direct way to demonstrate superiority.

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a highly original and significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The core idea of reducing the problem to classification is both elegant and practically powerful, allowing practitioners to leverage state-of-the-art supervised learning models. The theoretical analysis is deep and rigorously addresses the key challenges of the proposed framework. Despite some weaknesses, primarily the gap between the theoretical rates and practical performance and the need for a more thorough experimental evaluation (high-dim X/Y, comparison to KCIPT), the strengths of this work are overwhelming. The paper is well-executed, tackles an important problem, and opens up a promising new direction for statistical testing. It is a strong candidate for acceptance.
