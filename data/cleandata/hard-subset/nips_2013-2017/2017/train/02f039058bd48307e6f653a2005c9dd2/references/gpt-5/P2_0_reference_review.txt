Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes CCIT, a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test that reduces CI testing to binary classification via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap. Given 3n i.i.d. samples of (X,Y,Z), the method: (i) splits data, constructs a “null-like” dataset by replacing Y in each point of one split with the Y of the 1-NN in Z from another split; (ii) labels original samples as 1 and bootstrapped samples as 0; (iii) trains a classifier to distinguish the two. If accuracy exceeds chance, reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. A bias-corrected variant subtracts the performance of a classifier trained only on (Y,Z) from that of one trained on (X,Y,Z), aiming to remove finite-sample bias from imperfect null simulation.

Relative to prior work: classifier-based two-sample tests and independence testing have been explored (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab ’16), and kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and permutation-based KCIPT exist. The novelty here is a simple NN-based bootstrap to simulate samples close to fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z fY|Z fZ and a reduction to classification that leverages modern learners (XGBoost/DNNs). The authors provide total variation (TV) closeness guarantees for the bootstrap and generalization bounds for classification under near-i.i.d. samples, contrasting with KCIPT’s more expensive permutation via LP.

Quality
- Theory: Theorem 1 establishes that a single bootstrapped sample’s distribution is within TV distance O(n^{-1/dz}) (up to constants and tail terms) of fCI under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z). This is technically nontrivial and clearly stated, though the generalized curvature assumption (Assumption 1) is unusual and could use more intuition. Theorem 2 provides generalization bounds for ERM under near-independence, extending classical VC/Rademacher results to their dependency structure. This is valuable and appears careful.
- Practical testing: The paper proposes thresholds (e.g., τ≈1/√n) and a bias-corrected test statistic, and reports ROC AUC across synthetic and flow cytometry datasets. However, the work does not provide a provably level-α test or a p-value calibration procedure under H0. For CI testing as a hypothesis test, Type I error control is critical; here, the TV closeness and generalization bounds do not translate into concrete level guarantees for finite n. This is a significant gap compared to kernel tests that at least offer asymptotic level control or permutation-based calibration.
- Empirics: Synthetic results show strong scaling with increasing dim(Z) versus KCIT/RCIT; real-data results on flow cytometry are encouraging and code is provided. That said, the experiments focus on X,Y being 1D; it would strengthen the case to assess higher-dimensional X,Y and include additional baselines (e.g., conditional mutual information estimators based on kNN). Reporting fixed-α Type I/II error, not just AUC, and runtime comparisons would improve completeness.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized with clear algorithms and a coherent narrative. Some notation is dense and assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1) would benefit from more intuition. There are minor typos and formatting issues, but the method and theory are understandable. The bias-correction rationale is intuitive but the statistical properties (consistency, variance) of the difference-of-losses statistic could be better explained.

Originality
- The reduction of CI testing to classification via a nearest-neighbor Z-conditional Y-swap appears novel and simpler than prior permutation/transport approaches (e.g., KCIPT).
- Providing TV-distance guarantees for the NN bootstrap toward fCI and generalization bounds under near-i.i.d. sampling are original contributions.
- The idea of “model-powered” testing that leverages progress in classification is timely and relevant.

Significance
- If robustly calibrated, this approach could be impactful for high-dimensional CI testing by leveraging off-the-shelf classifiers. The empirical gains over KCIT/RCIT are promising.
- However, the curse of dimensionality in Z is explicit in the theory (n^{-1/dz}) and may limit formal guarantees in high-dz regimes; practice seems better than worst-case, likely due to strong learners, but this mismatch should be discussed more.
- The lack of a principled, level-controlled test or p-value limits immediate applicability in scientific inference. Providing a permutation/wild bootstrap that is valid under the approximate-null, or a cross-fitting scheme with asymptotic level control, would substantially raise impact.

Specific strengths
- Simple, computationally efficient NN bootstrap with a clear pipeline.
- Nontrivial TV-distance guarantee and learning-theoretic bounds under near-i.i.d. dependence.
- Strong empirical performance and available code.
- Flexible use of modern classifiers adapts to complex relationships.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions
- No formal level-α test. Suggest adding a calibration procedure (e.g., repeated sample splitting with null resampling and conformal-style calibration; or an approximate permutation leveraging the two-sample framework with justification of asymptotic validity).
- Limited baselines (no CMI estimators) and limited settings (mostly 1D X,Y). Include kNN-CMI, Gaussian partial correlation, and report Type I/II at fixed α, plus runtimes.
- Sensitivity analyses: effect of k in k-NN (k>1 may reduce variance), metric choice for Z, classifier choice/hyperparameters, and number of bootstraps B.
- Assumptions: provide more intuition and practical diagnostics for Assumption 1 and the f(z) smoothness/tail assumptions; discuss robustness to mixed/categorical Z.

Pro/con list for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel reduction of CI testing to classification via an NN bootstrap with theoretical TV guarantees.
- Generalization bounds under near-i.i.d. sampling.
- Strong empirical performance and practical simplicity; leverages modern ML tools; released code.

Cons:
- No calibrated hypothesis test with Type I error control; threshold choice is heuristic.
- Theory exhibits strong dependence on dim(Z); practical behavior outpaces guarantees but is not characterized.
- Experimental scope could be broader (higher-dim X,Y, more baselines, fixed-α metrics, runtimes).

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper advances CI testing by a simple, well-motivated reduction, with solid theory and promising results. Its main shortcoming is the lack of formal calibration for hypothesis testing; addressing this (even asymptotically) and broadening experiments would make it a clear accept. Even so, the ideas are likely to spur follow-up work and be useful in practice. Confidence: medium.
