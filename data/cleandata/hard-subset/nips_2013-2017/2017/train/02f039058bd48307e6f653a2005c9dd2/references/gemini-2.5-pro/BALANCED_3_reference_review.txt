### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel, "model-powered" approach for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, called CCIT. The core idea is to reduce the statistical problem of testing H0: X ⊥ Y | Z to a supervised binary classification task. The method proceeds in two main stages. First, given an original dataset of i.i.d. samples from the joint distribution f(x, y, z), it generates a second dataset designed to approximate the null distribution f_CI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z). This is achieved through a clever "nearest-neighbor bootstrap" procedure: for each sample (x, y, z), it finds another sample whose conditioning variable z' is the nearest neighbor to z, and swaps its y' value to create a new sample (x, y', z). Second, the original samples are labeled '1' and the generated samples are labeled '0'. A powerful binary classifier (e.g., XGBoost) is then trained to distinguish between these two classes. The final test decision is based on the classifier's accuracy on a held-out test set; if the accuracy is significantly better than random guessing (0.5), the null hypothesis is rejected.

This work builds upon the emerging theme of using classifiers for two-sample tests, notably extending the idea from marginal independence testing [19] to the more complex conditional setting. Unlike prior work on marginal independence which can use simple random permutations, this paper's key contribution is the Z-dependent resampling scheme via nearest neighbors. The proposed method stands in contrast to the dominant kernel-based approaches for CI testing (e.g., KCIT, RCIT), offering a flexible framework that can leverage any state-of-the-art classification model. The authors provide theoretical justification, proving that the distribution of bootstrapped samples converges to the true null distribution in total variation distance (Theorem 1) and deriving generalization bounds for the classifier trained on the resulting "near-i.i.d." data (Theorem 2). Empirical results on synthetic and real-world data show the method outperforming existing kernel-based tests, particularly as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of reducing CI testing to classification via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap is both novel and elegant. It provides a practical and effective way to extend classifier-based testing to the conditional setting, which is a non-trivial step. This "model-powered" paradigm is significant as it opens the door to applying the full power of modern, high-capacity supervised learning models to a fundamental problem in statistics and causal inference. The ability to simply plug in powerful off-the-shelf models like Gradient Boosted Trees or Neural Networks is a major practical advantage.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The theoretical contributions are solid: Theorem 1 formally justifies the core nearest-neighbor bootstrap mechanism, and Theorem 2 provides a valuable analysis of Rademacher complexity for the resulting non-i.i.d. samples, which is of independent interest. The proposed bias-correction technique (Algorithm 3) is a thoughtful addition that addresses a potential practical pitfall of the method, demonstrating a careful treatment of the problem.

3.  **Empirical Strength:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The method shows a clear advantage over established baselines (KCIT, RCIT) on synthetic data, especially in the challenging and highly relevant scenario where the dimensionality of the conditioning set Z is high. The application to the flow-cytometry dataset demonstrates the method's utility on a real-world scientific problem, further strengthening the paper's claims.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the core idea is explained intuitively (aided by Figure 1), and the algorithms are presented cleanly. The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work within the existing literature and highlighting their specific contributions.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The theoretical rate of convergence in Theorem 1, O(1/n^(1/d_z)), suffers from the curse of dimensionality with respect to Z. While the authors rightly note this and argue that the practical performance is much better due to the power of modern classifiers, the paper would be stronger if it discussed this gap more deeply. For instance, are there implicit assumptions (e.g., data lying on a low-dimensional manifold) that explain the strong empirical performance? Acknowledging and providing some intuition for why the empirical results defy the pessimistic worst-case bound would be beneficial.

2.  **Lack of a Principled p-value Calculation:** The proposed algorithms rely on a user-defined threshold `τ` to make a decision. This is a significant limitation for a statistical hypothesis test, where a p-value is the standard and expected output. While the authors suggest a reasonable heuristic for `τ` in the experiments, this ad-hoc nature makes the test's statistical properties (e.g., Type I error control) unclear. The paper would be a much more complete and practical contribution if it included a method for computing p-values, perhaps via a permutation scheme on the classifier labels.

3.  **Data Splitting and Sample Efficiency:** The proposed procedure requires splitting the data into three separate sets, which reduces the effective sample size used for generating null samples and for training/testing the classifier. For datasets with a limited number of samples, this could significantly reduce the power of the test. It would be valuable to discuss potential remedies, such as cross-fitting, to use the data more efficiently.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

Even though this is a strong paper, the following suggestions could further improve it:

*   **Primary Suggestion: Introduce a P-value Calculation:** The most critical addition would be a mechanism for computing p-values. A natural approach would be to use a permutation test. After generating the classification dataset D, the test statistic (e.g., `L(ĝ', D'e) - L(ĝ, De)` from Algorithm 3) can be calculated. Then, by repeatedly permuting the {0, 1} labels in D, re-training the classifiers, and re-computing the test statistic, one can generate an empirical null distribution for the statistic. The p-value would then be the proportion of permuted statistics that are more extreme than the observed one. This would eliminate the need for the threshold `τ` and provide a rigorous foundation for statistical inference.

*   **Discuss Theory vs. Practice:** Please add a brief discussion in Section 4 or 5 that addresses the gap between the theoretical `d_z`-dependent bounds and the excellent high-`d_z` empirical performance. This would add nuance and insight for the reader.

*   **Classifier Sensitivity:** While space is limited, a brief note in the appendix regarding the sensitivity of the results to the choice of classifier and its hyperparameters would be helpful for practitioners. Was any hyperparameter tuning performed for XGBoost?

*   **Data Usage:** Briefly mentioning the possibility of using cross-fitting or sample-swapping techniques to improve data efficiency would be a valuable addition for readers concerned about applying this method to smaller datasets.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a highly novel, intuitive, and powerful framework for conditional independence testing. It successfully bridges a fundamental statistical problem with the advanced machinery of modern supervised learning. The method is backed by both non-trivial theory and strong empirical results that demonstrate a clear state-of-the-art performance in important high-dimensional settings.
*   **Con:** The method in its current form is not a complete statistical test, as it lacks a principled procedure for calculating p-values and relies on a user-set threshold. The theoretical guarantees exhibit a curse of dimensionality that is not fully reconciled with the empirical success.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is an excellent paper presenting a creative and significant contribution. The strengths in novelty, technical quality, and empirical results far outweigh the weaknesses. While the lack of a p-value calculation is a notable limitation, it is an addressable one. The proposed framework is likely to inspire follow-up work and be a valuable tool for practitioners. I recommend acceptance.
