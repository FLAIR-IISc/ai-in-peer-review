This paper presents a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea – leveraging the power of modern classifiers to discern between the joint distribution and a conditionally independent distribution constructed via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap – is genuinely intriguing and represents a significant departure from traditional CI testing methods like kernel-based approaches. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and analyze generalization bounds in this non-i.i.d setting, demonstrating a commendable level of rigor. I particularly appreciate the attempt to address the bias introduced by the bootstrap procedure in Algorithm 3.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to a classification problem is a fresh perspective. This opens the door to exploiting advancements in classification, potentially leading to more scalable and accurate CI tests, particularly in high dimensions.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper doesn’t simply propose an empirical method; it backs it up with theoretical analysis, including bounds on the total variational distance between the generated distribution and the target CI distribution, as well as generalization error bounds.
* **Potential for Scalability:** By leveraging classifiers, the method promises better scaling compared to methods like KCIPT, which involve solving linear programs.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The core idea is explained relatively clearly, and the algorithmic steps are well-defined. The inclusion of pseudocode is helpful.
* **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrating improved performance over existing methods on synthetic data and a real-world dataset is a positive sign.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on smoothness assumptions on the underlying distributions, which might be strong in practice. While acknowledged, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Algorithm 3:** Algorithm 3, while aiming to correct bias, adds complexity. The justification for the threshold selection (⌧ = 1/ p n) feels somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from further theoretical grounding.
* **Empirical Evaluation Depth:** The empirical evaluation, while present, lacks extensive comparison across diverse datasets and a wider range of classifier choices. More detailed ablation studies exploring the impact of different classifiers and parameter settings would strengthen the findings.  The statement about "performance with respect to dimension of Z" scaling better than expected from theory is intriguing, but needs more quantitative support – showing the scaling empirically would be very convincing.
* **Clarity in Certain Sections:** While the core idea is clear, sections describing the theoretical results (e.g., the definitions and theorems) are dense and challenging to follow without a strong background in statistical learning theory. Simplifying the notation and providing more intuitive explanations would improve readability.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as potential issues with high-dimensional Z, or the impact of the nearest-neighbor search on performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to CI testing.
* The theoretical analysis, while complex, demonstrates a commitment to rigor and provides some guarantees on the performance of the method.
* The empirical results, while not exhaustive, show promising performance compared to existing methods.
* The potential for scalability and adaptability to advancements in classification makes this a worthwhile contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The strong assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis may limit the applicability of the method in practice.
* The empirical evaluation lacks depth and breadth.
* Certain sections of the paper are difficult to follow due to dense notation and complex theoretical arguments.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a bold and promising direction for CI testing. While the paper isn't perfect – the theoretical details are challenging, and the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive – the novelty of the approach and the theoretical grounding justify acceptance *despite* the flaws. This paper introduces a fundamentally new way to think about CI testing and has the potential to significantly advance the field. I am willing to overlook the minor imperfections given the significant conceptual leap. I strongly encourage the authors to address the clarity issues and expand the empirical evaluation in future work.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
