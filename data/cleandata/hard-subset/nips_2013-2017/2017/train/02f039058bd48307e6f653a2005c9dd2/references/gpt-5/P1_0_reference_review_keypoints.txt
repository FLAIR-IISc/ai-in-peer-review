- Proposes CCIT: reduce conditional independence testing X ⟂ Y | Z to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor (1-NN) bootstrap that swaps Y among Z-neighbors.  
- Shows under smoothness assumptions the NN-bootstrapped distribution is close in total variation to the CI product fX|Z fY|Z fZ.  
- Uses a held-out set to train a classifier distinguishing original vs NN-bootstrapped samples; accuracy above chance indicates rejection of CI.  
- Introduces a bias-corrected variant that subtracts classifier accuracy on (Y,Z) to reduce finite-sample bootstrap bias.  
- Theoretical result: dTV between NN-bootstrapped law and fCI scales as O(n−1/dz) plus small terms under regularity and smoothness conditions.  
- Provides generalization bounds for ERM with “near-independent” NN-bootstrapped samples, including VC-based rates with extra dependence and low-density terms.  
- Establishes a connection between achievable classifier error and dTV(f, fCI).  
- Empirically, CCIT with XGBoost outperforms kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) on post-nonlinear synthetic data as dz grows and on flow cytometry data.  
- Advances classifier-based two-sample testing methods to the conditional setting via an analyzable Z-conditional NN resampling scheme.  
- Strength: clean, scalable reduction enabling use of modern classifiers and domain knowledge.  
- Strength: simple, efficient 1-NN resampling with nontrivial theoretical marginal closeness guarantee.  
- Strength: novel non-i.i.d. generalization analysis of NN-bootstrapped training sets.  
- Strength: practical bias-correction idea and open-source implementation.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance in higher-dimensional Z regimes.  
- Weakness: lacks finite-sample level control or calibrated p-values; ROC/AUC summaries do not guarantee test size.  
- Weakness: τ = 1/√n choice is heuristic and depends on classifier/data distribution.  
- Weakness: strong, opaque assumptions (generalized curvature, smoothness/integrability) and rates suffer from curse of dimensionality (n−1/dz).  
- Weakness: reuse of neighbors (hub points) may induce strong dependence among label-0 samples; practical impact not fully explored.  
- Weakness: evaluation missing size/power curves at fixed α, sensitivity to classifier/hyperparameters, and runtime scaling; omitted comparisons to some recent tests.  
- Weakness: only continuous variables considered; mixed/discrete handling not addressed.  
- Weakness: theoretical statements involve many constants and low-density terms, making practical implications hard to parse.  
- Quality: technically sound with meaningful proofs, but missing finite-sample testing guarantees limits hypothesis-testing contribution.  
- Clarity: high-level algorithm and motivation clear, but notation and assumptions are dense and could use intuitive special cases and more experimental detail.  
- Originality: novel NN-swap idea and non-i.i.d. generalization analysis extending classifier two-sample testing to CI.  
- Significance: potentially impactful scalable approach for CI testing, though lack of level control tempers immediate rigorous use.  
- Questions: request practical calibration scheme to control type I error, sensitivity to classifier/hyperparameters, neighbor reuse statistics and k-NN variants, discrete/mixed variable handling, and reporting of n, dz, runtime, and size/power.  
- Suggestions: add level-controlled test and p-value calibration, ablations (bias correction, classifiers, k-NN), expand experiments (size/power, scaling, robustness), and simplify/clarify assumptions (e.g., Lipschitz case).  
- Arguments for acceptance: elegant reduction, nontrivial theory, practical code, and promising empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: no finite-sample level guarantees, strong/opaque assumptions with dimension-dependent rates, and limited evaluation breadth.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept (Confidence: Medium).
