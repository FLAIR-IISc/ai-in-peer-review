Summary
The paper proposes a “model-powered” conditional independence (CI) test (CCIT) that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n iid samples of (X,Y,Z), the method: (i) uses a nearest-neighbor “bootstrap” to create a synthetic sample set whose distribution is close (in TV distance) to fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z(x|z) fY|Z(y|z) fZ(z); (ii) labels original data as class 1 and bootstrapped data as class 0; (iii) trains a binary classifier (e.g., XGBoost) and interprets accuracy > 0.5 as evidence against H0: X ⟂ Y | Z. A bias-corrected variant subtracts the accuracy of a classifier trained only on (Y,Z) to mitigate finite-sample biases from the bootstrap. Theoretical results bound the TV distance between the bootstrap distribution and fCI under smoothness assumptions, and provide generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization under “near-independent” samples. Experiments on synthetic post-nonlinear models (1D X,Y; high-dim Z) and flow cytometry protein data compare against KCIT and RCIT, reporting ROC/AUC gains.

Relation to prior work: The reduction to classification mirrors the classifier two-sample testing idea (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016), and the surrogate sample construction is akin to KCIPT (Doran et al., 2014), which uses a more expensive LP-based permutation. The use of kNN-style constructions relates to recent kNN entropy/CMI estimators. The paper aims to provide cleaner algorithmics (1-NN swap on Y conditional on Z) and some theory on closeness to fCI and classifier generalization under dependence.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and modular: turns CI testing into a two-sample problem and leverages strong off-the-shelf classifiers.
- The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is computationally simple and does not require solving a global matching/LP.
- Theoretical effort: (i) TV-distance bounds to fCI under smoothness, (ii) generalization bounds for ERM with near-independent samples. This is a valuable attempt to justify a pragmatic pipeline.
- Code is provided; empirical results suggest improved ROC/AUC over KCIT/RCIT in certain regimes (notably large dim(Z)).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Statistical calibration is unclear. A CI test should control Type I error at a target α. The paper reports ROC/AUC across datasets but does not provide a procedure with guaranteed size control or p-values. The proposed thresholds (e.g., τ=1/√n) and bias correction via subtracting the (Y,Z)-only classifier loss are heuristic; no finite-sample or asymptotic level guarantees are established. Without a calibrated null distribution, practitioners cannot set α reliably.
- Strong and somewhat opaque assumptions. The curvature assumption on f(y|z) (Assumption 1) is nonstandard; no examples are given demonstrating it holds for common families, nor diagnostic/checkable conditions. Assumption 2 and the proof strategy lead to rates that degrade as O(n^{-1/d_z}), which become vacuous in high d_z. The paper then appeals to “powerful classifiers” empirically, but that does not remedy the theoretical curse of dimensionality.
- Near-independence generalization bounds are technically interesting but yield loose rates with heavy dependence on d_z and V (VC dimension), and the constants/terms (e.g., (log n / n)^{1/3}) are not very informative for practice. It is unclear how these translate to error control in the proposed test.
- Limited empirical scope and fairness:
  - Only X,Y are 1D in synthetic experiments; no evaluation with higher-dimensional X or Y.
  - Baselines are limited to KCIT/RCIT. Missing comparisons to CMI-based tests (e.g., kNN-CMI or recent conditional MMD-based tests beyond those cited), or newer randomized kernel CI tests that include null calibration.
  - No reporting of Type I error and power curves at fixed α, nor p-value calibration (e.g., via permutation/resampling). ROC/AUC over mixed CI/non-CI tasks can hide inflated false positives.
  - No ablations on choice of classifier, hyperparameter tuning (potentially favoring CCIT), number of nearest neighbors (k=1 is potentially unstable), or the number of bootstrap replications B. Runtime comparisons are also missing.
- The dependence introduced by the bootstrap is not fully addressed in the test design. Although near-independence is analyzed, the test statistic (difference in test losses) still lacks a known null distribution. A straightforward label-permutation within D under H0 is nontrivial because labels encode the constructed versus original sample origin; the paper does not offer a robust calibration scheme.
- Applicability is restricted to continuous variables with smooth densities. Mixed/continuous-discrete settings, or manifold-supported Z, are not addressed.
- The real-data study relies on imperfect graph “ground truths” and again uses ROC/AUC; conclusions about edges being absent contradict all graphs, but without calibrated testing it is hard to interpret.

Quality
- Some proofs and theorems are nontrivial, but the key theoretical guarantees (TV bound and generalization) come with heavy assumptions and do not translate into a statistically valid test with controlled level. The empirical evaluation, while positive, does not address size control or broader baselines.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and well-organized. However, Assumption 1 needs more intuition/examples; the choice of τ and the bias-correction rationale need clearer justification; and a discussion on how to obtain p-values or control Type I error is missing.

Originality
- The reduction to classification for CI via a NN-based conditional resampling is a reasonable and somewhat novel twist relative to KCIPT. The near-independence generalization analysis is also a worthwhile angle. However, the overall approach builds on known ideas (classifier two-sample, NN resampling), and the novelty lies in the specific bootstrap and analysis.

Significance
- If properly calibrated, a simple, classifier-based CI test would be impactful. As presented, the lack of level control and the limited empirical validation reduce the practical significance.

Questions and suggestions
- How to calibrate the test to guarantee size α? Can you propose a permutation/bootstrap scheme that yields valid p-values under H0, perhaps via cross-fitting and resampling the NN bootstrap multiple times to approximate the null?
- Provide Type I error and power at fixed α across settings, not just ROC/AUC.
- Evaluate sensitivity to k in k-NN swaps, choice of classifier and hyperparameters, and report runtimes.
- Include baselines based on conditional mutual information (e.g., kNN-CMI) and more recent conditional kernel tests; expand to higher-dimensional X and Y.
- Give concrete examples where Assumption 1 holds; discuss robustness to violations and mixed data types.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, potentially scalable idea; code available.
- Nontrivial theoretical attempts to justify a practical pipeline.
- Promising empirical AUC in certain regimes.

Cons for acceptance
- No statistically calibrated test; Type I error uncontrolled.
- Strong assumptions and theory that do not yield practical guarantees in high d_z.
- Limited and potentially favorable empirical setting; missing baselines and ablations.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is interesting, but I would need a statistically valid, calibrated testing procedure with demonstrated size control, broader empirical validation (including higher-dimensional X/Y, stronger baselines, ablations), and clearer practical guidance on assumptions before recommending acceptance.

Confidence: High.
