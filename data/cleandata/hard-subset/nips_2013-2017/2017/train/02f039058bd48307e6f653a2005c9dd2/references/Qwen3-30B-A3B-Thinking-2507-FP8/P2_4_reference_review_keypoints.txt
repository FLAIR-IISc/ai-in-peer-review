- Proposes reducing conditional independence testing to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate f_CI-like samples.  
- Provides theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) that bootstrap samples approximate f_CI in total variation distance with rate O(1/n^(1/d_z)).  
- Gives generalization bounds for classifiers trained on near-independent samples (Theorem 2).  
- Empirically outperforms kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially in high-dimensional settings (maintains accuracy up to dim 70).  
- Strength: significant theoretical contribution providing concrete rates for a practical nearest-neighbor bootstrap.  
- Strength: avoids expensive linear programming and offers a more efficient way to generate f_CI samples than kernel methods.  
- Strength: empirical results demonstrate clear advantages and scalability in challenging settings.  
- Weakness: theoretical analysis relies on smoothness assumptions that may fail for discontinuous or heavy-tailed real-world data.  
- Weakness: bias correction in Algorithm 3 lacks rigorous justification and the estimator for b(n) is not proven correct.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation omits comparisons to other model-based/deep learning CI tests and lacks detailed computational complexity analysis.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and structured, with helpful figures and algorithms.  
- Clarity issues: relationship between theorems and practical algorithms could be made more explicit; TV distance notation needs intuitive explanation.  
- Clarity issue: flow-cytometry experiment should explain possible reasons for discrepancies in specific edges.  
- Originality: introduces a novel, theoretically grounded nearest-neighbor bootstrap for CI testing and distinguishes itself from prior kernel/linear-program approaches.  
- Significance: addresses a foundational problem in causal inference, offers practical scalability, and introduces a promising "model-powered" paradigm for statistical testing.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — solid technical contribution with compelling empirical validation, despite assumptions and limited complexity analysis.
