

# Review for "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The core idea is to generate samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution f_CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1), then train a classifier to distinguish between the original samples (labeled 1) and the bootstrapped samples (labeled 0). If the classifier achieves significantly better than random accuracy on the test set, the null hypothesis of conditional independence (H0) is rejected; otherwise, it is not rejected.

The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing that the bootstrapped samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance (Theorem 1) and derive generalization bounds for the classifier under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). They also introduce a bias-corrected version of their algorithm (Algorithm 3) to address a small bias that exists even under H0. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that their method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic data (with increasing dimension of Z) and real-world flow-cytometry data.

This work extends the recent use of binary classification for independence testing [19] to the more challenging conditional independence setting, while providing theoretical guarantees that previous permutation-based approaches [10] lack.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough empirical validation. The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are rigorously derived with appropriate assumptions, and the authors carefully address the non-i.i.d nature of the bootstrapped samples. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, including both synthetic experiments with varying dimensions of Z and a real-world application to protein signaling networks.

The authors demonstrate careful evaluation by acknowledging limitations (e.g., the dimension dependence in their theoretical guarantees) and providing a bias-corrected algorithm to address them. The paper also properly compares with relevant prior work, particularly KCIT, RCIT, and KCIPT.

One minor weakness is that some technical terms (like the "generalized curvature matrix" in Assumption 1) could be better explained for readers without a strong background in statistical theory. However, this is a common challenge in theoretical papers and doesn't significantly undermine the quality.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written with logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical evaluation. The algorithms (Algorithms 1-3) are well-described with pseudocode, making them reproducible. The theoretical results are presented with proper definitions and assumptions.

The main clarity issue is the inconsistent notation for dimension (dz, d_z, dz) throughout the paper. Additionally, some technical terms like "generalized curvature matrix" in Assumption 1 could be better motivated for a broader audience. The paper would benefit from a brief intuitive explanation of these concepts before the formal definitions.

## Originality

The core idea of reducing CI testing to binary classification is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach (Algorithm 1) is a key technical innovation that provides theoretical guarantees about the closeness of generated samples to f_CI, which previous approaches [10] lack. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related methods, particularly explaining why permutation-based approaches for CI testing are difficult (due to Z-dependence) and how their bootstrap overcomes this.

While binary classification has been used for independence testing [19], extending this to conditional independence with theoretical guarantees represents a meaningful advance. The paper also provides new theoretical results on generalization under near-independent samples, which is of independent interest to the machine learning community.

## Significance

CI testing is a fundamental problem with wide applications in causal inference, Bayesian networks, and feature selection. The proposed method has significant practical advantages, particularly in high-dimensional settings where existing methods struggle (as demonstrated in Figure 2a). The modular approach allows leveraging advances in classification, making it a flexible and powerful tool for practitioners.

The paper's significance is further enhanced by its connection to broader trends in machine learning where model-powered approaches (using supervised learning to solve statistical problems) are gaining traction. The real-world application to protein signaling networks demonstrates practical relevance, and the paper's performance on the flow-cytometry dataset (Table 2c) shows clear advantages over existing methods.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pros:**
1. Novel approach that significantly advances CI testing by reducing it to binary classification.
2. Strong theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap method (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds (Theorem 2).
3. Superior empirical performance, especially in high-dimensional settings where other methods fail.
4. Practical bias-corrected algorithm (Algorithm 3) addressing a key limitation of the initial approach.
5. Clear connection to related work with proper attribution to prior methods.

**Cons:**
1. Some technical concepts (e.g., generalized curvature matrix) could be better explained for broader accessibility.
2. The theoretical guarantees have a dimension dependence (d_z) that might limit applicability in extremely high dimensions.
3. Limited discussion of computational complexity compared to other methods.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a high-quality, novel, and significant contribution to the field of statistical testing. The proposed method is theoretically sound, well-validated empirically, and addresses an important practical problem in a way that leverages advances in machine learning. The paper meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS submission.

While there are minor weaknesses in clarity regarding some technical terms, these do not detract from the overall quality and contribution of the work. The paper makes a clear and meaningful advance in CI testing, with both theoretical and practical significance.

I recommend acceptance to NIPS. This work would be valuable to both the machine learning community (for its model-powered approach) and the statistics/causal inference community (for its practical and theoretically grounded CI testing method).

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Provide a brief intuitive explanation of the "generalized curvature matrix" before presenting Assumption 1 to help readers without a strong statistical background.
2. Consistently use d_z notation throughout the paper to avoid confusion.
3. Add a brief discussion of computational complexity (e.g., time complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap) to help practitioners evaluate the method.
4. Consider adding a short discussion of the limitations of the dimension dependence in Theorem 1 and potential future directions to address it.
