This paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing, framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea is to generate samples mimicking the conditional independence distribution using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, and then train a classifier to distinguish these generated samples from the original data. The paper provides theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for the classifier, and demonstrates promising empirical results on synthetic and real-world data.

The strength of this work lies in its clever reduction of a statistical testing problem to a binary classification task, enabling the utilization of powerful modern machine learning techniques. The theoretical analysis, particularly the bounds on the total variational distance between the generated samples and the true conditional independence distribution (Theorem 1), provides a strong foundation for the method.  Furthermore, the generalization bounds (Theorem 2) are notable for extending existing results to a near-independent sampling setting. The empirical evaluation demonstrates the method’s competitive performance, particularly its scalability to higher dimensions compared to existing kernel-based methods. The inclusion of experiments on the flow cytometry dataset adds practical relevance. The modularity of the approach is also a strength, allowing flexibility in choosing the underlying classifier.  The related work section is comprehensive, accurately placing the contribution within the existing literature on CI testing and highlighting the advantages of their approach (e.g., avoiding the expensive linear program in KCIPT [10]).

However, there are several weaknesses that should be addressed. First, while the theoretical analysis provides valuable insights, the assumptions required for the theorems (smoothness of the distribution, bounded Lipschitz constant) may be restrictive in practice. A discussion of the robustness of the method to violations of these assumptions would be beneficial. The constant factors hidden within the big-O notation are likely significant and could affect practical performance; a sensitivity analysis would be valuable.

Second, the practical implementation details could be elaborated further. The choice of the threshold  ⌧  in Algorithm 3 is mentioned, but a more detailed justification and guidance on setting this parameter in practice would be helpful. The performance sensitivity to hyperparameters of the chosen classifier (XGBoost) should be addressed. 

Third, the empirical evaluation, while promising, could be more extensive. Comparing against a wider range of CI tests, including those based on entropy estimation [13] as mentioned in the related work, would provide a more complete benchmark.  A more detailed ablation study, investigating the impact of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap parameters (e.g., the number of neighbors used) on the performance of the CI test, is also needed.  The statement that the performance "scales much better than expected from our worst-case theoretical analysis" is vague; providing quantitative evidence to support this claim would strengthen the empirical analysis. While the flow cytometry results are positive, it is difficult to assess their significance without a more rigorous comparison to established causal discovery methods.

Finally, the paper's writing could be improved. While generally clear, certain sections are dense and require careful reading. Improving the flow and providing more intuitive explanations of the theoretical concepts would enhance readability. The notation could be made more consistent throughout the paper.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to CI testing leveraging the power of modern classification techniques.
*   Strong theoretical foundations with bounds on sample quality and generalization error.
*   Empirically competitive performance, especially in high-dimensional settings.
*   Modularity and flexibility of the approach.
*   Comprehensive related work section.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Restrictive assumptions in the theoretical analysis.
*   Limited discussion of practical implementation details and hyperparameter tuning.
*   Empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and rigorous.
*   Writing could be clearer and more accessible.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable and promising contribution to the field of conditional independence testing.  The core idea is innovative, and the theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the method. However, the weaknesses identified above warrant careful consideration.  While not currently suitable for acceptance in its current form, I believe that addressing the concerns outlined above could significantly strengthen the paper and make it a worthy addition to NIPS. I recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The potential impact of this work is considerable, and I would be happy to review a revised version.
