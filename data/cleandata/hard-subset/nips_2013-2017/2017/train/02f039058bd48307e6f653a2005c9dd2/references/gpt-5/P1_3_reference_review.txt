Summary
The paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test, CCIT, that reduces nonparametric CI testing of X ⟂ Y | Z to binary classification. The key idea is to approximate draws from the CI factorized distribution fCI(x, y, z) = fX|Z(x|z) fY|Z(y|z) fZ(z) using a simple nearest-neighbor bootstrap: split the data, for each (x, y, z) in one half find the 1-NN in Z-space in the other half and swap the Y coordinates. The resulting samples U′ are shown to be close in total variation (TV) to fCI. The CI test becomes a two-sample test between original samples and the synthesized samples (labeled 1 vs 0) using a flexible classifier. A bias-corrected version subtracts the accuracy of a classifier that only sees (Y, Z) from the accuracy of a classifier that sees (X, Y, Z), to account for finite-sample deviations introduced by the bootstrap. Theoretical contributions include (i) a TV bound dTV(·, fCI) = O(n−1/dz) under smoothness assumptions, and (ii) generalization bounds for ERM classifiers trained on the non-i.i.d “near-independent” bootstrapped samples, extending classical VC/Rademacher analyses. Empirically, the method with XGBoost outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear models (scaling to Z-dimension ~70) and on CI checks derived from flow cytometry protein networks. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
The approach is a natural extension of classifier two-sample tests and independence testing via permutation (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016) to the CI setting. Unlike independence, where permuting Y yields exact samples from fX fY, CI requires Z-dependent resampling. The most related prior is the permutation-based KCIPT (Doran et al., 2014), which computes a Z-aware permutation via a linear program; here, the authors propose a much simpler nearest-neighbor bootstrap and provide TV guarantees, which is novel and attractive. The paper also situates itself relative to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and kNN-based information estimators. The generalization analysis under near-independence complements work on generalization under mixing processes.

Strengths
- Simple and practical reduction: The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is easy to implement, fast with standard NN data structures, and compatible with any off-the-shelf classifier.
- Theoretical grounding: A nontrivial TV closeness bound to fCI and generalization guarantees under non-i.i.d, near-independent sampling. This is new and potentially useful beyond this specific test.
- Strong empirical performance: The method scales well with Z-dimension in synthetic tests and performs best on flow cytometry benchmarks; open-source implementation enhances impact.
- Modular design: Advances in classification can directly improve test power; domain knowledge can inform the choice of classifier.

Weaknesses
- Size control and calibration: The test is presented as a classifier-based decision with a threshold τ (and a bias correction via a (Y, Z)-only classifier), but there is no principled way to set τ to guarantee Type I error at a user-specified α. Results are reported via ROC/AUC rather than size-power tradeoffs at fixed α, unlike KCIT/RCIT which provide level control and p-values.
- Asymptotic rates and assumptions: The TV bound scales as O(n−1/dz), reflecting a severe curse of dimensionality in Z. While the empirical scaling is better, the theoretical rate suggests weak finite-sample guarantees in high-dimensional Z. Assumptions (curvature of f(y|z), smoothness and tail conditions on f(z), Lipschitzness) are strong and may not hold in common settings.
- Dependence structure: While a per-sample TV bound and near-independence arguments are provided, the dependence among bootstrapped samples is nontrivial. The generalization bound has many constants and logarithmic factors, and it’s not clear how it translates to a practically calibrated test statistic.
- Experimental reporting: Missing details that affect reproducibility and interpretation—exact n for the synthetic experiments, hyperparameter tuning protocols for all methods, runtime comparisons, sensitivity to the classifier choice, number of neighbors (k=1 vs k>1), and number of bootstrap splits B. Power and Type I error at fixed α are not reported.
- Scope: The method assumes continuous variables; handling of discrete or mixed Z (common in causal discovery) is not discussed. Robustness to low-density regions (where G(·) appears in the bounds) is not evaluated.
- Clarity and presentation: There are several typesetting glitches and notational inconsistencies (e.g., acceptance condition inequality directions, repeated symbols, broken math), and some references are incomplete.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a novel bootstrap construction supported by a careful analysis. However, the paper falls short on statistical calibration: without a null distribution or p-value computation, practitioners cannot control Type I error, and comparisons to calibrated baselines are less informative.

Clarity
Readable and well organized at a high level, but notation is heavy and some definitions (e.g., the generalized curvature matrix) are hard to parse; several typos and formatting issues detract. Algorithmic steps and intuition are clear.

Originality
The nearest-neighbor conditional bootstrap to approximate fCI and the generalization analysis under near-independence appear novel. The reduction to classification builds on known ideas but is adapted thoughtfully to CI.

Significance
Potentially impactful: the method is simple, scales with modern classifiers, and shows strong empirical performance. If calibration is addressed, it could be widely adopted in causal discovery and feature selection workflows.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a calibrated testing procedure: e.g., construct a null distribution of the accuracy gap via repeated resampling (multiple random splits and NN bootstraps), or a label-swapping scheme that preserves the constructed dependence, to obtain p-values and control size at level α. Formalize τ selection or propose a permutation-based calibration.
- Report Type I error and power at fixed α alongside AUC; include runtime comparisons.
- Explore k-NN (k>1) bootstraps and cross-fitting/multisplitting to reduce bias and dependence; study sensitivity to B and k.
- Evaluate robustness to discrete/mixed Z and low-density regions; include ablations across classifiers and hyperparameters.
- Clarify acceptance rules and fix notation; provide more guidance on choosing τ in practice; complete and standardize references.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a compelling and practical idea with solid theory and promising empirical results. The lack of statistical calibration is the main limitation; adding a principled size-control mechanism would significantly strengthen the contribution.

Confidence
Medium.
