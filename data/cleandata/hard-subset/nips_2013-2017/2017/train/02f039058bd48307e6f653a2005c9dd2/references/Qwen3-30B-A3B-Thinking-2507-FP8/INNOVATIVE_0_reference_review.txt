

# Review: Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification, leveraging supervised learning models to detect dependencies. The core insight is that CI testing can be converted to a two-sample test between original data and samples generated from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) to generate samples close to f_CI, then train classifiers to distinguish between the original samples (labeled 1) and the generated samples (labeled 0). The method is theoretically grounded with guarantees on the bootstrap quality and classifier generalization, and empirically outperforms state-of-the-art kernel-based methods.

**Novelty:** This work stands out for its bold conceptual shift in CI testing. While binary classification has been used for independence testing ([19]), extending it to conditional independence is challenging due to the need to emulate f_CI, which requires Z-dependent permutations. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach is a clever technical innovation that avoids the computational complexity of previous permutation-based methods ([10]). The paper's model-powered perspective—treating statistical testing as a pipeline that leverages advances in supervised learning—is a fresh and valuable viewpoint for the field.

**Theoretical Contributions:** The paper makes two key theoretical contributions. Theorem 1 establishes that the bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance under smoothness assumptions, which is a significant advance over prior work. Theorem 2 provides generalization bounds for classification under non-i.i.d samples, which is a technically sophisticated contribution with independent interest in learning theory. The authors demonstrate that the method scales better with dimensionality than theoretical worst-case analysis suggests, which is a compelling practical observation.

**Empirical Results:** The synthetic experiments (Figure 2a) show CCIT significantly outperforms KCIT and RCIT as the dimension of Z increases, with near-perfect performance up to dimension 70. On the flow-cytometry dataset (Figure 2b, Table 2c), CCIT consistently outperforms competitors across all three causal graphs, even with low-dimensional Z. The empirical results strongly support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical utility.

**Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, clearly structured, and presents a genuinely novel perspective on CI testing. The authors successfully bridge statistical testing and machine learning in a way that's both theoretically sound and practically effective. The model-powered approach is elegant and opens new avenues for statistical testing. The theoretical analysis, while complex, appears rigorous, and the empirical validation is thorough.

**Weaknesses:** The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2), which could be restrictive in real-world applications. While the authors claim the method scales better than theoretical analysis suggests, they don't fully explain why this occurs. The paper also doesn't compare to more recent CI testing methods like KCIPT ([10]) beyond stating it's "more expensive." The connection to "model-powered" approaches could be clarified—it's not clear how this differs from other model-based statistical methods.

**Comparison to Related Work:** The paper correctly positions itself against kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) and permutation-based methods (KCIPT), highlighting the advantages of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap. It builds on the classification-based independence testing framework ([19]) but extends it to the more challenging conditional independence setting, which is a significant step forward.

**Significance:** This work has substantial significance for the field. It provides a new, principled approach to CI testing that leverages advances in machine learning, with clear empirical advantages. The method is flexible, scalable, and can incorporate future improvements in classification. This represents a paradigm shift in how CI testing might be approached, moving beyond traditional statistical tests to model-powered pipelines.

**Recommendation:** This paper introduces a genuinely novel and impactful approach to conditional independence testing. While the theoretical analysis is complex, the core idea is bold, the empirical results are compelling, and the method offers clear practical advantages. The paper meets the high bar for NIPS with its strong novelty and potential to influence future work in causal inference and statistical testing. I recommend acceptance.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel reduction of CI testing to binary classification with theoretical guarantees
- Nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a clever technical innovation that avoids prior limitations
- Strong empirical results showing clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods
- Model-powered approach opens new avenues for statistical testing
- Theoretical contributions (Theorems 1 and 2) have independent value

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Theoretical analysis relies on smoothness assumptions that may not hold in all applications
- Paper could better explain why the method scales better than theoretical analysis suggests
- Limited discussion of practical limitations and when the method might fail

Overall, this is a high-quality, novel contribution that aligns with NIPS' emphasis on bold, innovative approaches. The paper's conceptual shift and strong empirical validation make it a compelling candidate for acceptance.
