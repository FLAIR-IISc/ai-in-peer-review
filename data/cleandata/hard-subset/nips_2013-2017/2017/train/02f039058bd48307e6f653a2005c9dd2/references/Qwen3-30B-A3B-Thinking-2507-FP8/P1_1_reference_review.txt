

# Peer Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The key insight is that CI testing can be converted into a two-sample test between original data samples (labeled 1) and samples generated from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI (labeled 0). The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) that efficiently generates samples close to f_CI, which they theoretically justify in Theorem 1. The CI testing problem then reduces to training a classifier on the labeled data and checking whether it can distinguish between the two classes.

This work builds upon recent advances in using binary classifiers for statistical testing, particularly the independence testing approach in [19], but extends it to the more challenging conditional independence setting. It differs significantly from kernel-based approaches like KCIT [32] and RCIT [28] by using a simple nearest-neighbor bootstrap instead of complex kernel methods, and from KCIPT [10] by providing theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrap samples (in terms of total variational distance) and using classifiers rather than kernel methods for the two-sample test.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Approach**: The model-powered approach to CI testing is genuinely novel and well-motivated. Converting a statistical testing problem into a machine learning problem that leverages powerful classifiers is a fresh perspective that could inspire similar approaches in other statistical testing contexts.

2. **Strong Theoretical Contributions**: The paper makes significant theoretical contributions. Theorem 1 provides a rigorous bound on the total variational distance between the bootstrap samples and f_CI, which is a critical component of the method. Theorem 2 extends generalization bounds to near-independent samples, which is of independent interest to the machine learning community.

3. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation**: The paper includes thorough empirical results on both synthetic data (with varying dimensions of Z) and a real-world flow-cytometry dataset. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art alternatives (KCIT, RCIT) across various settings.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a clear description of their algorithms (with pseudocode) and make their implementation publicly available, which is valuable for reproducibility and adoption.

5. **Well-Structured Paper**: The paper is well-organized, with clear problem definition, detailed algorithm descriptions, thorough theoretical analysis, and comprehensive experiments.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The smoothness assumptions in Theorem 1 (particularly Assumption 1) might be restrictive in practice. The paper doesn't provide much discussion on how common these assumptions are in real-world applications or how violations might affect performance.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational complexity between their approach and existing kernel-based methods. For high-dimensional data, this could be a critical factor in practical adoption.

3. **Bias Correction Justification**: The bias correction in Algorithm 3 (Section 2) seems somewhat ad hoc. While the authors provide empirical justification, a more rigorous theoretical foundation for this correction would strengthen the paper.

4. **Limited Real-World Impact Demonstration**: While the flow-cytometry dataset evaluation is good, the paper doesn't demonstrate how the method leads to better causal discovery or practical insights beyond the CI testing task itself. It would be valuable to see how the CI testing results translate to improved causal graph learning.

5. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper references [32, 28] but doesn't compare with the most recent approaches like [22] (which is cited in the paper but not directly compared to).

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical results are rigorously proven (with proofs deferred to the appendix), and the empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for bias correction in Algorithm 3) and provide a clear comparison to related work. This is a complete piece of work that makes a significant contribution to the field.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The main contributions are explicitly stated in Section 1.1, and the algorithms are presented with pseudocode. The theoretical results are well-explained with appropriate definitions. The empirical results are presented clearly with informative figures and tables. Some technical details (e.g., the smoothness assumptions) might require some background knowledge, but the paper provides sufficient context for readers familiar with machine learning and statistics.

## Originality Assessment

The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to CI testing. The model-powered perspective is a fresh take on a well-studied problem, and the nearest-neighbor bootstrap technique is original. The theoretical analysis of generalization bounds under near-independent samples is also a significant contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work, particularly KCIPT [10] and kernel-based methods, and demonstrates how their approach is both theoretically justified and practically effective.

## Significance Assessment

The results are highly significant. CI testing is a fundamental problem with wide applications in causal inference, Bayesian networks, and feature selection. The proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches, especially in high-dimensional settings, which is a major practical advantage. The framework is generalizable to other statistical testing problems, potentially enabling the field to leverage advances in supervised learning for a broader range of statistical challenges.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of statistical testing and machine learning. It presents a novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically effective approach to a fundamental problem. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the state of the art.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 3, consider adding a brief discussion of how common the smoothness assumptions are in real-world applications, and what happens when they're violated.

2. Include a computational complexity analysis comparing your method with KCIT/RCIT, particularly as dimensionality increases.

3. In the flow-cytometry evaluation (Section 4.2), consider showing how the CI testing results translate into improved causal graph discovery, rather than just verifying existing graphs.

4. In Algorithm 3, provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the bias correction, rather than just empirical justification.

5. Add a comparison with the most recent causal discovery methods (e.g., [22]) in the empirical evaluation.

## Summary of Arguments for Acceptance

- Novel model-powered approach that reduces CI testing to binary classification
- Theoretical guarantees on the nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Theorem 1)
- Generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2)
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods
- Well-written, well-organized paper with clear contributions

## Summary of Arguments Against Acceptance

- Some theoretical assumptions might be restrictive in practice
- Limited discussion of computational complexity
- Bias correction in Algorithm 3 could be better justified theoretically
- Flow-cytometry evaluation doesn't fully demonstrate practical impact

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and it makes a valuable contribution to the field.
