Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes CCIT, a “model-powered” conditional independence (CI) test that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method: (i) partitions data, (ii) generates a synthetic dataset intended to approximate the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x,y,z) = fX|Z(x|z)fY|Z(y|z)fZ(z) by swapping Y across 1-NN matches in Z (nearest-neighbor bootstrap), (iii) trains a binary classifier to distinguish real samples (label 1) from bootstrapped samples (label 0), and rejects H0: X ⟂ Y | Z when the classifier performs better than chance. A bias-corrected version subtracts the test loss of a Y,Z-only classifier from that of an X,Y,Z classifier. Theoretical contributions include (a) bounds showing the bootstrap distribution is close to fCI in total variation under smoothness assumptions, with a rate O(n^{-1/dz}); and (b) generalization bounds for ERM under “near-i.i.d.” samples produced by the bootstrap, extending VC/Rademacher-style guarantees to this setting. Empirically, CCIT (using XGBoost) reportedly outperforms KCIT [32], RCIT [28], and a permutation-based kernel CI test [10] on synthetic post-nonlinear models and on flow cytometry data [26].

This approach sits at the intersection of classifier two-sample tests (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab [19]) and nonparametric CI testing (KCIT/RCIT [32,28], KCIPT [10]). The nearest-neighbor resampling echoes k-NN methods used in density/entropy estimation [12,13]. The learning-theoretic analysis under dependence relates to Rademacher bounds for non-i.i.d. processes (e.g., Mohri & Rostamizadeh [21]).

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and flexible reduction: once the bootstrap is defined, the problem becomes a two-sample test solvable with off-the-shelf classifiers, benefiting from advances in ML and domain-specific modeling.
- Novelty in the bootstrap: an efficient Z-conditional “Y-swap” via 1-NN to approximate fCI without solving a global matching/LP (contrast with [10]).
- Theory: nontrivial proof that a bootstrapped sample is close to fCI in TV distance, and generalization bounds for ERM with near-independent samples. These analyses are of independent interest.
- Empirics: strong ROC AUC in synthetic settings up to large dz, and a real-data application; open-source implementation.
- Practical scalability: leverages fast NN search and scalable classifiers.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Size control/calibration is not established. The test threshold τ is heuristic (e.g., 1/√n), and there is no finite-sample or asymptotic Type I error guarantee (no p-values or exact α-level control). For CI testing in structure learning and causal discovery, controlled false positive rates are essential.
- The bootstrap closeness rate O(n^{-1/dz}) is very slow in dz and depends on strong smoothness/low-density mass conditions. The constants and the G(·) terms make guarantees potentially vacuous in high dimensions.
- The 1-NN bootstrap induces complex dependencies; while “near-independence” is quantified for learning bounds, the impact on the null distribution of the classifier’s test statistic is not characterized.
- Hyperparameter tuning and repeated bootstrapping could inflate Type I error if not nested within the train/test split; protocol details are insufficient to rule out subtle leakage.
- No analysis of approximate NN or of using k-NN (k>1) for bias/variance trade-offs; nearest neighbors degrade in high dz.

Clarity
- Assumption 1 (generalized curvature matrix) is hard to parse; intuition and concrete examples (e.g., Gaussian location/scale families) would help. Several notational slips and typos remain. Theorems are stated with heavy constants and functions (e.g., G(·)) without sufficient interpretation of regimes where bounds are meaningful.
- Statistical testing protocol is underspecified: how τ is set in practice, whether cross-fitting is used, how multiple runs are aggregated, whether hyperparameters are tuned on a validation split disjoint from the test split, etc.

Originality
- The reduction to classification for CI, enabled by a local resampling scheme approximating fCI, is original relative to KCIT/RCIT and to prior classifier-based independence testing [19]. The non-i.i.d. generalization analysis is also a novel angle. Relation to [10] (global permutation via LP) is well-drawn.

Significance
- If properly calibrated, the approach could be impactful: it decouples CI testing from specific kernels and adapts to high-dimensional, nonlinear settings via flexible classifiers. However, the lack of size control limits immediate applicability in pipelines (e.g., PC/FCI algorithms). The worst-case dz dependence in theory is discouraging, though empirical results are promising.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a testing procedure with controlled Type I error (e.g., permutation or cross-fitting-based null calibration that respects the data-dependent bootstrap; label-exchange within folds; or split-conformal style calibration). Report power at fixed α (e.g., 5%) in addition to ROC AUC.
- Clarify and justify Assumption 1 with examples; compare to standard smoothness/Lipschitz conditions on log f(y|z); discuss when G(·) terms are small.
- Explore k-NN resampling and analyze its effect on TV distance and dependencies; discuss approximate NN.
- Add ablations: classifier choice, hyperparameters, number of bootstraps B, sample size n; runtime comparisons vs. KCIT/RCIT; robustness to dz, dy, dx.
- Strengthen experimental baselines: include kernel permutation test [10], partial distance covariance/HSIC variants if applicable; evaluate on mixed discrete/continuous settings.
- For the flow cytometry case, report power/size with assumed ground truth edges and discuss sensitivity to graph uncertainty; add a synthetic DAG benchmark with known CI structure.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a finite-sample calibration scheme yielding valid p-values under H0 despite the dependencies induced by the bootstrap?
- How sensitive is CCIT to the choice of classifier and its tuning? Did you use a validation split disjoint from the test set for hyperparameter selection?
- Have you tried k>1 nearest neighbors or approximate NN? Any empirical gains?
- Can the theoretical rate be improved under stronger (but common) models (e.g., Lipschitz conditionals, bounded curvature) or with k-NN averaging?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, general, and potentially powerful framework.
- Novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate fCI with supporting theory.
- Non-i.i.d. generalization bounds of independent interest.
- Strong empirical AUC in challenging settings; open-source code.

Cons:
- No Type I error control; thresholding is heuristic.
- Theoretical rates scale poorly with dz and rely on strong assumptions.
- Some clarity issues in assumptions and protocol details.
- Limited ablations and baseline breadth; real-data evaluation has uncertain ground truth.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The core idea is promising and the theory is interesting, but the absence of a calibrated testing procedure with size control and the heavy assumptions/weak rates make the current submission fall short of NeurIPS standards for a hypothesis test. With calibration, clearer assumptions, and expanded experiments, I would be inclined to support acceptance.
