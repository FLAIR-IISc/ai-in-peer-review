Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a “model-powered” nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test (CCIT) that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method:
1) uses a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap over Z to construct synthetic samples whose distribution is close (in total variation) to fCI = fX|Z fY|Z fZ;
2) labels original versus synthetic samples and trains a classifier to distinguish them;
3) rejects H0: X ⟂ Y | Z when the classifier achieves accuracy significantly above chance.
A bias-corrected variant subtracts the accuracy of a classifier trained on (Y,Z) only, aiming to cancel the finite-sample bias introduced by the near-i.i.d. bootstrap.

The paper provides theory: (i) a TV-distance guarantee between the NN-bootstrap distribution and fCI with rate O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions; (ii) generalization bounds for ERM classifiers trained on “near-independent” samples generated by the bootstrap; (iii) a risk sandwich showing classifier error is near 0.5 under H0, and strictly lower under H1 when dTV(f, fCI) is bounded away from 0. Empirically, CCIT outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic datasets (Z up to 70 dims) and on a flow cytometry dataset.

This work relates to: classifier-based two-sample tests (Lopez-Paz & Oquab 2016) and to permutation-based CI tests (e.g., KCIPT), but replaces a costly matching/permutation optimization by a simple 1-NN over Z; and to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT/RCIT). The nearest-neighbor construction is also related in spirit to kNN-based entropy/CMI estimation.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The reduction is well-motivated, and the NN bootstrap is simple and plausible. Theorem 1 gives a clear and useful TV bound to fCI that depends only on dz (not dx,dy), which matches the intuition that only conditioning quality in Z matters. The non-i.i.d. generalization analysis is thoughtful and addresses a key subtlety of their pipeline. However:
  - The TV rate O(n^{-1/dz}) reflects a curse of dimensionality in Z; the paper acknowledges this but empirics suggest better behavior. It would help to clarify regimes or structural conditions (e.g., low intrinsic dimension/manifold, Lipschitz link functions) under which the practical performance departs from worst-case rates.
  - Statistical testing properties are underdeveloped: there is no finite-sample control of Type I error at a prespecified α. The choice of threshold τ (e.g., 1/√n) in experiments is heuristic; no calibration or p-value procedure is provided, and the bias term b(n) is unknown. ROC/AUC is useful, but for CI testing a valid level-α test is often desired. A simple calibration via repeated null bootstraps or label-exchangeability arguments (if available) would strengthen the paper.
  - Assumptions: The smoothness/curvature Assumption 1 on f(y|z) and the conditions on f(z) (bounded Hessian, integrability, Lipschitz) are nontrivial; a short discussion of their practical plausibility (and failure modes) would help. The term G(ε) requires fZ to not put too much mass in low-density regions; what happens for heavy tails or concentrated manifolds?
  - Computational aspects: 1-NN search over Z is efficient in low/moderate dimensions but degrades in high dz; approximate NN is mentioned but not analyzed empirically. Complexity and runtime comparisons to KCIT/RCIT are missing.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. Algorithms are clearly stated; the pipeline is easy to follow. Some typos and formatting glitches remain (e.g., stray symbols “?”, “st Conference”, equation formatting), and a few definitions (e.g., Assumption 1) could be more intuitively explained.
- Experimental details are sparse: sample sizes, classifier hyperparameters, and number of trees/layers should be specified; sensitivity to the choice of classifier G and to k in kNN (k=1 is used) would be informative. Reporting standard errors would help.

Originality:
- The reduction of CI testing to classification via a NN-based conditional resampling is, to my knowledge, novel relative to contemporaneous work that uses permutations/matching via optimization (KCIPT) or kernel criteria. The theoretical TV guarantee for the conditional-resampling scheme and the near-i.i.d. generalization analysis are original and valuable.
- The generalization results build on VC/Rademacher tools adapted to weak dependence; related to mixing-process bounds, but tailored to the geometric “near-independence” induced by NN over Z. This seems like a useful idea beyond CI testing.

Significance:
- Practically significant: framing CI as classification lets practitioners leverage modern ML advances. The method is flexible (plug-in any classifier), simple to implement, and seems to scale better than some kernel methods in practice.
- Theoretically, giving explicit TV guarantees to fCI is a solid contribution that clarifies when conditional resampling works. If the authors can provide calibrated tests (Type I control) and runtime comparisons, CCIT could become a go-to baseline.

Pros:
- Simple, modular algorithm with compelling empirical gains.
- Clear theoretical connection between NN bootstrap and fCI in TV.
- Generalization bounds for near-independent data; addresses a real subtlety.
- Public code; application to a real biological dataset.

Cons/concerns:
- No formal level-α testing procedure; reliance on ROC/AUC and heuristic τ.
- Worst-case rates suffer from the curse of dimensionality in Z.
- Empirical details and baselines are limited (e.g., conditional mutual information estimators, modern regression-residual-based HSIC/dCor variants).
- Dependence on NN in high-dimensional Z and on classifier choice not explored; no ablations.
- Some assumptions are strong; practical guidance on when they hold is limited.

Questions for authors:
- Can you provide a calibrated test with provable asymptotic level (e.g., using repeated null resampling to estimate b(n) and the variance, or a permutation within the constructed D that is valid under H0)?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of k in kNN, and to the classifier G? Any ablations?
- What are typical runtimes vs KCIT/RCIT for your synthetic setups?
- How does CCIT behave when Z has mixed discrete/continuous components or lies on a low-dimensional manifold?

Recommendation:
Promising contribution with a simple idea, solid theory, and strong empirical results. The main gap is the lack of a calibrated testing procedure with Type I control and limited experimental detail. With clarifications and a basic calibration, this would meet NeurIPS standards well.
