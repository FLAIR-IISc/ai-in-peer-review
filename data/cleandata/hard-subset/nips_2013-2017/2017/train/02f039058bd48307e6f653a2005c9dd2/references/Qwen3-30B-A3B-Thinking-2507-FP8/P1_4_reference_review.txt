

# Peer Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel model-powered approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The core idea is to use a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x,y,z), then train classifiers to distinguish between original samples (labeled 1) and bootstrap samples (labeled 0). The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the bootstrap method (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2), demonstrating empirical superiority over state-of-the-art methods like KCIT and RCIT on synthetic data and a real-world flow-cytometry dataset. This work builds on recent classifier-based independence testing [19] but extends it to the more challenging conditional case, offering a significant methodological advance over kernel-based approaches [28, 32] and permutation-based methods [10].

## Quality
The paper presents technically sound contributions with well-justified theoretical results. Theorem 1 provides a concrete bound on the total variation distance between bootstrap samples and f_CI (O(1/n^(1/dz))), which is a significant theoretical contribution. Theorem 2 extends generalization bounds to the non-i.i.d setting, a novel and important aspect of the work. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with both synthetic experiments demonstrating scalability with dimensionality of Z (Fig. 2a) and a real-world application to protein signaling networks. The authors properly acknowledge limitations, such as the bias correction in Algorithm 3 and the theoretical bound's dependence on dimensionality.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The main ideas are presented logically, with pseudocode for all algorithms (Algorithms 1-3) that are easy to follow. The exposition of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) is particularly clear. The theoretical results are well-motivated, though the connection between the theoretical bounds and empirical performance could be made more explicit. The paper would benefit from a simple illustrative example showing why the nearest-neighbor bootstrap works for high-dimensional Z, but this is a minor point given the overall clarity.

## Originality
This work makes a strong original contribution. The reduction of CI testing to binary classification is novel, and the nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach differs fundamentally from previous methods. Unlike KCIPT [10] which requires solving a linear program to compute permutations, this paper proposes a simple, efficient bootstrap method with theoretical guarantees. The theoretical analysis of generalization under near-independent samples is also novel and of independent interest. The paper properly differentiates from related work, making it clear what's new. The approach represents a meaningful advance over the state of the art in CI testing.

## Significance
CI testing is a fundamental problem in causal inference and Bayesian network learning with significant practical implications. The paper demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings where other approaches struggle (Fig. 2a shows CCIT maintaining high accuracy up to dimension 70 while others decline). By leveraging modern classifiers, the approach benefits from ongoing advances in machine learning. The application to the protein signaling network data (Fig. 2b, Table 2c) demonstrates practical relevance and provides a meaningful real-world validation. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, offering a scalable solution to an important problem.

## Strengths
- Novel reduction of CI testing to binary classification
- Theoretical guarantees for bootstrap method and classification generalization
- Strong empirical results showing better scaling with dimensionality of Z
- Practical application to a real-world causal inference problem
- Clear exposition of algorithms and main ideas

## Weaknesses
- The theoretical bound in Theorem 1 (O(1/n^(1/dz))) might be loose in practice, though the empirical results show better performance
- The paper assumes continuous variables but doesn't address discrete cases (common in real applications)
- The bias correction in Algorithm 3 is somewhat ad-hoc without theoretical justification
- Limited discussion of computational complexity compared to kernel-based methods
- The connection to broader trends in model-powered statistical methods could be emphasized more

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong, technically sound contribution to the field of causal inference and conditional independence testing. The theoretical results are well-justified, the empirical evaluation is thorough, and the work has clear significance for both theoretical and applied researchers. While there are some minor weaknesses to address (particularly regarding discrete variables and computational complexity), these do not undermine the core contribution. The work represents a meaningful advance over existing approaches and aligns well with NIPS' interest in novel statistical methods leveraging machine learning.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by discussing discrete variables and providing more detailed computational complexity analysis.

## Additional Comments
1. The authors might consider extending their approach to discrete variables in future work, as this would broaden the applicability of their method.
2. A more detailed comparison of computational complexity with kernel-based methods (KCIT/RCIT) would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
3. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of how their approach fits into the broader trend of "model-powered" statistical methods.
4. The connection between their theoretical bound and the observed empirical performance (which scales better than expected) is interesting and deserves more emphasis.
