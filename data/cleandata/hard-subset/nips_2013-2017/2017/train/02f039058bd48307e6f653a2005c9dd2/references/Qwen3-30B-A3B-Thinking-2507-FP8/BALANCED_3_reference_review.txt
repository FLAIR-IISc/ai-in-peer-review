

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The key insight is to use a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution f_CI, then train a classifier to distinguish between original samples (labeled 1) and bootstrapped samples (labeled 0). The authors provide theoretical guarantees for their bootstrap method and classifier generalization, and demonstrate empirical superiority over state-of-the-art kernel-based methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Original Contribution:** The paper makes a significant contribution by reducing CI testing to binary classification, which is a novel approach compared to existing kernel-based methods. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap technique (Algorithm 1) provides a simple yet theoretically justified way to generate f_CI-approximate samples, addressing a key limitation in prior work (e.g., KCIPT requires expensive linear programming for permutations).

2. **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) showing the bootstrap produces samples close to f_CI (in total variation distance) and that classifiers trained on these samples generalize well. The analysis of generalization under near-independent samples is particularly valuable and addresses a non-trivial technical challenge.

3. **Empirical Performance:** The experiments demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT), especially as the dimension of Z increases. The results on the flow-cytometry dataset show practical utility in a real-world causal discovery context.

4. **Practical Impact:** The approach leverages advances in classification (e.g., XGBoost, deep learning), meaning improvements in those fields directly benefit CI testing. The bias-corrected version (Algorithm 3) effectively addresses a key practical limitation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Complexity:** The theoretical guarantees (Theorem 1) contain complex dependencies on dimension d_z and sample size n (e.g., O(1/n^{1/d_z})). While theoretically sound, these dependencies make it difficult to translate guarantees into practical guidelines for practitioners. The paper could better explain how these theoretical bounds relate to the observed empirical performance.

2. **Computational Considerations:** The paper claims efficiency but doesn't provide concrete computational complexity analysis of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap step (Algorithm 1). For large datasets, the nearest-neighbor search could become a bottleneck, especially as dimension d_z increases. The paper should address this limitation more explicitly.

3. **Bias Correction Mechanism:** While Algorithm 3 improves performance, the bias correction is somewhat ad-hoc (subtracting test loss on Y,Z coordinates). A more principled approach to bias correction would strengthen the method and its theoretical justification.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper cites recent work on kernel-based CI testing but could better position itself against more recent methods like the permutation-based KCIPT (2019) and other neural approaches. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a strong, novel approach to conditional independence testing with clear theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. The reduction to binary classification is elegant and leverages advances in machine learning to address a fundamental statistical problem. While the theoretical guarantees could be made more accessible to practitioners, and the computational complexity needs more explicit discussion, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), offers a genuinely novel approach (Originality), and addresses an important problem with demonstrable significance (Significance). The empirical results convincingly demonstrate advantages over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Simplify the presentation of theoretical guarantees to better highlight practical implications for practitioners.
2. Provide a more detailed analysis of computational complexity, especially for the nearest-neighbor bootstrap step.
3. Consider a more principled approach to bias correction than the ad-hoc method in Algorithm 3.
4. Include a brief discussion comparing the method to the most recent neural approaches to CI testing (e.g., from NeurIPS 2021-2023).

This is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in conditional independence testing. I recommend acceptance.
