

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The authors introduce a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure to generate samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution (f_CI), then use binary classifiers to distinguish between original samples and the bootstrapped samples. The method differs from prior kernel-based approaches (e.g., KCIT, RCIT) by leveraging powerful supervised learning models and providing theoretical guarantees about the quality of the bootstrapped samples. This work builds on recent classification-based two-sample testing methods [19] but extends them to the more challenging conditional independence setting.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper provides strong theoretical foundations. Theorem 1 establishes meaningful bounds on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped samples and f_CI, while Theorem 2 extends generalization bounds to non-i.i.d settings, which is a significant contribution. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods (KCIT, RCIT) across both synthetic and real-world datasets, particularly in higher dimensions.

**Originality:** The core idea of reducing CI testing to binary classification is novel in the context of non-parametric CI testing. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure with theoretical guarantees represents a significant technical contribution that distinguishes this work from prior kernel-based methods. The paper clearly articulates how it differs from related work, particularly [10] which uses a more complex permutation approach.

**Significance:** The method addresses a fundamental problem with wide applications in causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection. The empirical results show that the approach scales better with the dimension of Z than existing methods, which is particularly valuable in high-dimensional settings where many existing methods struggle. The model-powered framework could inspire similar approaches for other statistical testing problems.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The pseudocode for the algorithms is precise, and the theoretical results are presented with appropriate definitions and careful justification. The authors effectively explain the intuition behind their approach and its advantages.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Limitations:** The theoretical guarantees (Theorem 1) contain a term O(1/n^(1/dz)) that could be problematic for high-dimensional Z. While the authors note that empirical performance is better than theoretical predictions, they don't thoroughly explain why this discrepancy occurs, which weakens the theoretical contribution.

**Bias Correction Justification:** The bias correction in Algorithm 3 (subtracting the loss from a classifier trained on Y,Z coordinates) appears somewhat ad hoc. The paper lacks a rigorous theoretical justification for why this specific correction works, which makes the method feel less principled.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper doesn't compare against more recent CI testing methods that use entropy estimation [13] or other approaches. This omission makes it difficult to fully assess the relative standing of the proposed method in the current literature.

**Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap, which could be a significant concern for large-scale applications. While the authors note the efficiency of nearest-neighbor algorithms, a more detailed analysis would be valuable.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify the theoretical-practical discrepancy:** In Section 3, provide a more detailed explanation for why the empirical results outperform the theoretical guarantees, particularly regarding the dimensionality dependence. This could involve discussing the practical effectiveness of modern classifiers in high dimensions.

2. **Strengthen the bias correction argument:** Provide a theoretical justification for why the bias correction in Algorithm 3 works, perhaps by showing that the difference between the two classifiers' losses converges to zero under H0.

3. **Expand comparison to recent methods:** Include comparisons with entropy-based approaches [13] and other recent CI testing methods to better position the work in the current literature.

4. **Add computational analysis:** Provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap, especially in high dimensions, and discuss potential optimizations.

5. **Improve exposition of theoretical results:** For readers less familiar with learning theory, consider adding a brief intuitive explanation of the key theoretical results before presenting the formal theorems.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of statistical testing by introducing a novel model-powered approach to conditional independence testing with strong empirical performance and meaningful theoretical guarantees. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened to better explain the empirical results, and the bias correction could be more rigorously justified, the core ideas are sound and well-executed.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and effective solution that advances the state of the art in CI testing. The method's ability to scale better with dimensionality than existing approaches is particularly noteworthy. With the suggested improvements, the paper would be even stronger, but as it stands, it represents a significant contribution to the field that would be valuable to the NIPS community.
