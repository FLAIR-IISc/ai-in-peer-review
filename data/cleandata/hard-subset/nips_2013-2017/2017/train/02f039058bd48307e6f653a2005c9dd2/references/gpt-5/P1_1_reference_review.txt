Summary
The paper proposes a model-powered, reduction-based approach to nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing. Given i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the authors (i) construct “conditionally independent-like” samples via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that, for each (x,y,z) in one split, replaces y with y′ from the 1-NN in z-space found in another split; (ii) label original samples as 1 and bootstrapped samples as 0; and (iii) reduce CI testing to a two-sample classification problem solved by off-the-shelf classifiers (e.g., XGBoost, DNNs). They provide theory showing the bootstrapped distribution is close in total variation (TV) to fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z fY|Z fZ at rate O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness conditions, and generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization with “near-independent” samples. Empirically, the method (CCIT) outperforms kernel-based baselines KCIT and RCIT on synthetic data with growing dim(Z) and on the flow-cytometry dataset.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on the line of “reduce statistical testing to classification,” echoing recent classifier two-sample tests and independence testing (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016). For CI testing, KCIT/RCIT use RKHS embeddings/approximations, and KCIPT (Doran et al., 2014) constructs a Z-aware permutation via an expensive matching/LP; this work proposes a simpler NN-based bootstrap with TV guarantees. It also connects to k-NN based information/entropy estimation (Gao et al.) and learning-theory generalization bounds in non-i.i.d. settings (Mohri & Rostamizadeh).

Strengths
- Conceptually simple reduction. Turning CI testing into a two-sample classification problem is elegant and practically attractive, enabling the use of strong classifiers and domain knowledge.
- Novel bootstrap and theory. The NN-based “swap Y by Z-NN” construction is intuitive and appears new versus previous permutation/matching strategies. Theorem 1’s TV bound formalizes its validity.
- Generalization under near-independence. The classification risk bounds for dependent-but-near-i.i.d. samples are interesting beyond this application.
- Empirical performance. Strong ROC-AUC, especially as dim(Z) increases, and a real-data case study with code provided.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Calibration and Type I error control. The method produces a classifier accuracy/statistic and uses a heuristic threshold τ=1/√n. There is no formal null distribution or finite-sample/type-I error calibration (e.g., via permutation/cross-fitting). Without p-values or controlled significance levels, the procedure is not a proper hypothesis test in the classical sense.
- Curse of dimensionality in theory. The main TV rate O(n^{-1/dz}) and dependence on dim(Z) are very slow, reflecting 1-NN distances. While practice may be better with strong classifiers, theory provides limited guidance for high-dz regimes.
- Dependence and model complexity. The generalization bounds are stated in terms of VC dimension and are likely vacuous for boosted trees/deep nets; practical guarantees for modern classifiers remain unclear.
- Assumptions and scope. The smoothness conditions (curvature of f(y|z), Lipschitz f(z), Hessian bounds, integrability) and the requirement of continuous densities exclude mixed discrete-continuous settings common in practice.
- Baselines and ablations. KCIPT (permutation-based CI test) is not compared empirically, despite being the closest conceptual baseline. No ablation on classifier choice, k in k-NN (only 1-NN used), metric choice in Z, or sensitivity to B (number of bootstraps).
- Sample splitting inefficiency. Using 3n samples to form n training pairs (and n test pairs) may be statistically suboptimal. Cross-fitting or repeated sample-splitting could improve power without inflating Type I error, but this is not analyzed.
- Practical guidance. Details such as selecting τ, choosing the Z-metric, and handling high-dimensional Z (where k-d trees degrade) are not deeply discussed.

Quality
- Theoretical claims are carefully stated and supported under stated assumptions; proofs are sketched to appendix. The main limitation is that the theory does not deliver a level-α test or power analysis against specific alternatives, nor does it cover modern classifiers’ complexities.
- Experiments are encouraging but somewhat narrow (1D X,Y; particular nonlinear model family; no stress tests on confounding structure or heteroskedasticity). Real-data evaluation uses proxy ground-truth with acknowledged uncertainty.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well organized, with helpful algorithms. Some notation is heavy and assumptions (e.g., generalized curvature matrix) could be better motivated and interpreted. The presentation of b(n), G(·), constants, and the final rates could be streamlined. Minor typos/formatting issues in equations.

Originality
- The NN bootstrap for emulating fCI and the near-independence learning bounds are original to my knowledge. The reduction-to-classification framing is known, but the conditional version here is nontrivial.

Significance
- Conditional independence testing is central in causal discovery and graphical models. A flexible, scalable approach that leverages modern classifiers is valuable. With stronger calibration and broader validation, this could become a practical tool.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a calibrated testing procedure (e.g., permutation over labels within Z-local neighborhoods; wild bootstrap; or cross-fitting with repeated splits) to control Type I error and yield p-values.
- Explore k-NN (k>1) with randomized neighbor selection; study the tradeoff bias/variance and impact on TV bounds.
- Add empirical comparisons to KCIPT and to CMI-based tests (e.g., KSG-based conditional mutual information estimators with permutation calibration).
- Broaden experiments: higher-dimensional X,Y; mixed discrete/continuous; alternative Z metrics; sensitivity to classifier choice; ablations on B and τ.
- Discuss computational complexity in high-dimensional Z and alternative ANN methods.

Overall evaluation
- Pros: Simple and practical idea; novel bootstrap with theory; compelling empirical gains in some regimes; code available.
- Cons: Lack of formal calibration/p-values; theory suffers from dz curse and may be loose for chosen classifiers; limited baselines and robustness analysis.

Recommendation: Weak accept (score: 7/10). This is a solid, well-motivated contribution that advances CI testing by leveraging modern classification. Addressing calibration and expanding empirical validation would further strengthen the work.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
