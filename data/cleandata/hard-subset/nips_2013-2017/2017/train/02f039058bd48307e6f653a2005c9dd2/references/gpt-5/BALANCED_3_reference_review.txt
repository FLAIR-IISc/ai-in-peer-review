Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a “model-powered” conditional independence (CI) test (CCIT) that reduces CI testing to binary classification by synthetically generating samples approximating the factorized null fCI(x,y,z) = fX|Z(x|z) fY|Z(y|z) fZ(z). The key step is a nearest-neighbor (1-NN) bootstrap in Z: given two disjoint subsets U2 and U3 from the original data, for each point (x,y,z)∈U2, replace y with y’ from the 1-NN of z in U3, producing U’2. Label the held-out original samples U1 as 1 and the synthetic samples U’2 as 0, and train a classifier to distinguish them. If the classifier performs no better than chance, fail to reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z; otherwise, reject. A bias-corrected variant (CCITv2) subtracts the test loss of a classifier trained only on (Y,Z) from that of a classifier trained on (X,Y,Z). The authors provide: (i) a TV-distance guarantee that the distribution of a synthetic sample is close to fCI at rate O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions on f(y|z) and f(z); (ii) generalization bounds for ERM under “near-independent” samples induced by the NN bootstrap, including a bound relating misclassification risk to dTV(f,fCI); and (iii) empirical results showing strong ROC-AUC relative to KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear data and the flow cytometry dataset.

This work connects to several threads: kernel CI tests (KCIT/RCIT; Zhang et al. 2012; Strobl et al. 2017), the permutation-based KCIPT (Doran et al. 2014), classifier-based two-sample tests for independence (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016), nearest-neighbor based information/entropy estimators (Gao–Oh–Viswanath), and learning-theoretic generalization bounds for non-i.i.d processes (Mohri & Rostamizadeh).

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple, modular reduction from CI testing to binary classification that leverages modern classifiers (e.g., XGBoost).
- Nearest-neighbor bootstrap is computationally attractive relative to permutation-by-LP approaches (KCIPT) and requires no kernel choice.
- Theoretical contribution: a total variation bound between the bootstrap distribution and fCI, and generalization guarantees for ERM with dependent but “near-independent” samples. The bias-corrected variant is intuitive and addresses finite-sample artifacts.
- Empirically promising: on synthetic post-nonlinear data, CCIT scales well with dim(Z) (ROC-AUC ≈ 1 up to dz ≈ 70 in the reported setting) and outperforms KCIT/RCIT on the flow cytometry case study. Open-source implementation provided.

Weaknesses:
- Calibration/size control: The procedure is presented as a test but does not provide a way to choose τ to guarantee a prescribed Type I error level. Results are reported as ROC/ROC-AUC rather than fixed-size tests; no p-values or null calibration (e.g., via additional resampling) are provided. This is a major gap relative to kernel tests that offer asymptotic calibration or permutation-based p-values.
- Dimensional dependence: The TV bound decays as O(n^{-1/dz}), unfavorable in high-dimensional Z, and generalization bounds include terms that worsen with dz. While practice may be better with strong classifiers, the worst-case theory is pessimistic.
- Assumptions: The smoothness/curvature assumption on f(y|z) (Ia(z) bounded) is nonstandard and difficult to interpret; the G(ε) term and ε choice in Theorem 2 are left unspecified for practice.
- Dependence structure: Theorem 1 controls the marginal distribution of a single synthetic sample, but U’2 samples are not i.i.d.; the “near-independence” argument is high level, and constants/conditions (e.g., metric entropy of Z, low-density regions) are not transparent. More intuition and finite-sample diagnostics would help.
- Practical considerations not fully addressed: choice of metric and scaling in Z for NN search; sensitivity to k (k-NN vs 1-NN); robustness to mixed/discrete Z and heavy tails; hyperparameter tuning and fairness in comparisons (e.g., how XGBoost parameters were selected vs kernel bandwidths in KCIT/RCIT); computational scaling of NN search in high dz; potential leakage/overfitting with flexible classifiers and repeated bootstraps.
- Empirical baselines: No comparison to CMI-based tests (e.g., KSG-style conditional mutual information estimators) or to simple Gaussian partial correlation tests where appropriate.

Quality:
- The approach is technically plausible and well-motivated. Proof sketches (as described) rely on smoothness and NN approximations, consistent with literature on kNN density/entropy estimation. However, rigor and clarity could be strengthened (Assumption 1, bounds’ constants, guidance for ε and τ).
- The experimental design is broadly reasonable but focused on ROC-AUC; absence of size-controlled comparisons limits interpretability.

Clarity:
- Generally well organized with clear algorithms. Some notation inconsistencies between Algorithm 1 and the description (which subsets are used where). Assumption 1 needs more intuition, examples, and discussion. Provide a simple diagram illustrating why the bootstrap approximates fCI.
- Specify how τ is chosen in practice and how B bootstraps are aggregated (averaging scores vs majority vote), and ensure the split strategy prevents leakage.

Originality:
- The NN bootstrap to emulate fCI in continuous Z and the reduction to classification with theory are, to my knowledge, novel relative to KCIT/RCIT and KCIPT.

Significance:
- If calibrated, the approach could be impactful: it opens CI testing to rapid advances in classification. Current lack of size control limits immediate applicability in scientific workflows needing controlled false positives.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a calibration procedure to control Type I error (e.g., nested resampling/permutation that preserves Z while re-running the NN bootstrap under H0 to estimate the null of the classifier score; or cross-fitting strategies).
- Explore k-NN (k>1) bootstrap and metric learning/standardization for Z; analyze trade-offs and provide robustness experiments.
- Add comparisons to conditional mutual information tests (e.g., KSG-CMI variants) and Gaussian partial correlation where applicable.
- Clarify Assumption 1 and Theorem 2 (choice of ε, interpretation of G(ε)), and give a concrete consistency statement (power→1 for fixed dz).
- Report sensitivity to classifier choice/hyperparameters and ensure fair tuning for all baselines. Provide computational timings and NN method (exact vs approximate).
- Discuss handling of discrete/mixed Z, scalability when dz is large, and potential use of dimensionality reduction for Z.

Minor comments:
- Clean up notation mismatches between U1/U2/U3 across algorithms; define all constants (c_dz, d) clearly once.
- A few typos and formatting issues (e.g., missing equation references).

Recommendation: Weak accept (6/10). Novel idea with promising empirical results and some theory; however, test calibration and clarity of assumptions should be strengthened. Confidence: 3.5/5.
