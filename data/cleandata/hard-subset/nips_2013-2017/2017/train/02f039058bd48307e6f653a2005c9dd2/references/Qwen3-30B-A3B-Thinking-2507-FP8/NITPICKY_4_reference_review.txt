

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The key idea involves generating samples from the conditionally independent distribution fCI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1), then training classifiers to distinguish between original samples and the bootstrapped samples. The paper claims theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples' closeness to fCI and provides generalization bounds for the classification procedure.

**Strengths:** The paper presents an interesting approach that leverages modern classification techniques for CI testing, which is a novel angle compared to existing kernel-based methods. The empirical results on synthetic data and flow-cytometry show promising improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in high dimensions. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is computationally efficient, and the theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the non-i.i.d nature of the bootstrapped samples.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Critical Theoretical Inconsistency:** The paper claims "the samples generated by the bootstrap (in U'2) are close to fCI(x, y, z) under both hypotheses" (Section 1), but Theorem 1 only provides guarantees under H0 (the null hypothesis). The theoretical analysis does not address how the bootstrap behaves under H1, which is crucial for the validity of the method.

2. **Major Formatting and Notation Errors:** 
   - "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" (p. 1) is clearly a typo that should be "2017 Conference".
   - The term "b(n)" appears in Theorem 1 without prior definition.
   - In Definition 1, the TV distance formula contains a typo: "sup p2B|Ef [p(X)] Eg[p(X)]|" should be "sup p∈B |Ef[p(X)] - Eg[p(X)]|".
   - The paper uses inconsistent notation for the ideal distribution (q̃ vs q).

3. **Algorithmic Inconsistency:** Algorithm 3 (step 6) states "If ˆL(ĝ, D e ) < ˆL(ĝ0, D0 e ) ⌧, then conclude X 6? Y |Z" which appears to be missing a minus sign: it should be "If ˆL(ĝ, D e ) < ˆL(ĝ0, D0 e ) - ⌧, then conclude X 6? Y |Z".

4. **Missing Reference:** The paper cites [19] for using classifiers for independence testing but [19] does not appear in the reference list.

5. **Citation Mismatch:** The paper cites [28] as "RCIT" but the reference list has [28] as "Approximate kernel-based conditional independence tests" by Strobl et al., while the paper describes RCIT as "an approximate version of KCIT" (citing [28] as RCIT). This is a significant citation inconsistency.

6. **Methodological Ambiguity:** The paper states "the samples generated from the nearest-neighbor bootstrap do not remain i.i.d but they are close to i.i.d" but provides no quantitative measure of "closeness" beyond the TV distance. The theoretical analysis in Theorem 2 relies on this "closeness" without clearly defining it.

7. **Experimental Details:** The threshold ⌧ = 1/√n is used without justification in Section 4.1, and the choice of B = 50 for bootstraps in Section 4.1 is arbitrary without explanation.

8. **Terminology Inconsistency:** The paper uses "fCI" and "fCI(x, y, z)" interchangeably without clear distinction, which could confuse readers.

**Recommendation:** While the paper presents an interesting approach with promising empirical results, the theoretical inconsistencies and numerous formatting errors undermine the paper's credibility. The critical theoretical gap (lack of guarantees under H1) is particularly concerning, as it directly impacts the validity of the method. The numerous minor errors and inconsistencies (typographical, notational, and citation-related) suggest a lack of careful proofreading that would be unacceptable in a NIPS submission.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues, particularly the theoretical gap under H1 and the numerous formatting errors. The core idea is interesting, but the current presentation fails to meet NIPS's high standards for technical rigor and presentation quality.

**Pro/Con Summary:**
- Pro: Novel approach combining CI testing with modern classification techniques; promising empirical results on high-dimensional data.
- Con: Critical theoretical gap (no guarantees under H1); numerous formatting errors; inconsistent notation; missing reference; citation mismatch.

Given the severity of the theoretical issues and the number of formatting inconsistencies, I recommend rejection. The paper's contribution is interesting but the current version is not technically sound enough for NIPS.
