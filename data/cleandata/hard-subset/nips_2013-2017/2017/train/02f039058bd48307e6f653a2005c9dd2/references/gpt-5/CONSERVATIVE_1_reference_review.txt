Summary
The paper proposes a “model-powered” nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n samples from fX,Y,Z, the method splits the data, constructs an approximate sample from the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x,y,z)=fX|ZfY|ZfZ via a nearest-neighbor (NN) “conditional permutation” of Y conditioned on Z, labels original samples as 1 and NN-bootstrapped ones as 0, and trains a classifier to distinguish them. If the classifier performs significantly above chance on a held-out test set, the null X ⫫ Y | Z is rejected. The authors prove a bound on the total variation distance between the distribution of a single NN-bootstrapped sample and fCI that decays as O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions, and provide generalization bounds for ERM with “near-i.i.d.” samples. Empirically, the method (using XGBoost) reportedly outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear examples and a flow cytometry dataset. A bias-corrected variant subtracts a classifier trained only on (Y,Z) to account for dependence introduced by the bootstrap.

Relation to prior work: The approach is analogous to classifier two-sample tests and independence testing via permutation and classification, extending Doran et al. (KCIPT) by replacing a costly permutation via linear programming with a 1-NN scheme. Theoretical analysis leverages ideas from VC/Rademacher bounds and kNN-based density/entropy estimation.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and modular reduction that can leverage advances in classification.
- Practical algorithm (kNN + standard classifier), with code provided.
- Theoretical attempt to justify the NN bootstrap’s closeness to fCI and to handle non-i.i.d. effects in the classifier training.
- Promising empirical results, including a real-data case study, and a bias-correction idea to mitigate bootstrap-induced bias.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Statistical calibration is unclear. The paper does not provide valid p-values or level-α control. Reporting ROC AUC is not sufficient for hypothesis testing; CI tests are typically evaluated on Type I error at fixed α (e.g., 0.05) and power. The proposed threshold τ=1/√n is heuristic and not justified for level control, and the bias-correction via “(Y,Z)-only” classifier does not guarantee correct null calibration.
- The theoretical TV bound scales as O(n^{-1/dz}), which is extremely slow in dz; for dz up to 70 (as shown in experiments), the bound is vacuous. The empirical claim that “classifiers perform well in high-dimensions” does not reconcile with the bootstrap’s asymptotics; the gap between theory and practice is large and not analyzed.
- The NN bootstrap induces dependency among generated samples. While the authors present generalization bounds for near-independence, the conditions (e.g., G(ε), Lipschitz constants) are strong and hard to verify; constants are opaque; and the results do not translate into practical Type I error guarantees for the proposed decision rules.
- Sensitivity to classifier choice, hyperparameters, and sample splitting is not explored. There is a substantial risk of overfitting or distribution-shift exploitation, especially with high-capacity models and limited data. No ablation on k in kNN (beyond 1-NN), distance metrics, or alternative classifiers is provided.
- Comparisons and metrics: only KCIT and RCIT are compared; no comparisons to conditional mutual information–based kNN tests, partial correlation in Gaussian settings, or more recent kernel-based CI tests. Reporting AUC masks calibration issues; Type I/II error curves at fixed α are needed.
- Real-data evaluation relies on uncertain “ground-truth” graphs and includes contrived negative examples; without interventional ground truth, the conclusions are tentative. The discussion notes discrepancies but does not probe whether the method is miscalibrated.
- Practical details and efficiency: Running time and scalability are not analyzed. The method repeats the bootstrap B times and trains classifiers repeatedly; nearest-neighbor search is only part of the cost. Hyperparameter selection and prevention of data leakage are not clearly described.
- The analysis of the bias-corrected test is heuristic; subtracting test errors is a noisy operation and does not come with a null distribution or variance estimate.

Quality
The paper is technically ambitious and provides nontrivial analysis, but it falls short on the most important statistical criterion for CI testing: level control and practical calibration. The asymptotic results hinge on strong smoothness assumptions and are dimensionally pessimistic. Empirical methodology does not evaluate the test in the conventional manner (fixed α), and sensitivity analyses are lacking. Proof sketches and constants are hard to interpret and to map to practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-organized. However, key practical aspects are underspecified: how τ is set in practice beyond a heuristic; how p-values (if any) are computed; hyperparameter tuning; number of trees/depth for XGBoost; aggregation over B bootstraps; and computational complexity. The theorems’ conditions (e.g., Assumption 1’s curvature matrix) could be better motivated with examples.

Originality
The reduction to classification and the use of a conditional NN permutation are interesting and incremental to prior classifier-based two-sample/independence tests. The non-i.i.d generalization analysis is a novel angle, though related to mixing-process bounds. The idea overlaps in spirit with KCIPT but simplifies the permutation step.

Significance
Conditional independence testing is important, and a robust, scalable alternative would be valuable. However, without calibration and thorough validation against strong baselines at fixed α, it is hard to recommend adoption. The theoretical results do not convincingly justify performance in high-dimensional Z.

Questions and requests for the authors
- How do you obtain p-values and control Type I error at a target α? Can you provide null calibration (e.g., via cross-fitting, permutation within Z-neighborhoods) and report false positive rates?
- How sensitive is power/Type I error to classifier choice and hyperparameters? Provide ablations (XGBoost depth/trees, regularization) and k in kNN (>1).
- Can you compare against CMI-based kNN CI tests and additional kernel CI tests? Include Gaussian sanity checks with partial correlation.
- Provide running times and scalability vs KCIT/RCIT, and the effect of B on both runtime and performance.
- Analyze why the method appears to work when dz is large despite the O(n^{-1/dz}) rate. Are there structural assumptions (e.g., manifold Z) that explain this?
- Clarify whether your decision rule is fold-specific or aggregated, and how you prevent tuning on the test set.

Pros
- Simple and flexible framework that leverages classification.
- Code availability and empirical promise.
- Nontrivial theoretical effort addressing bootstrap closeness and near-i.i.d learning.

Cons
- No demonstrated level control; reliance on AUC rather than hypothesis-testing metrics.
- Theory conflicts with claimed high-dimension performance; strong assumptions.
- Limited baselines/ablations and unclear sensitivity.
- Real-data evaluation not conclusive; negative examples contrived.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is appealing and could be impactful with proper calibration and more rigorous empirical evaluation, but in its current form the method lacks the statistical guarantees and validation expected for a CI test.
