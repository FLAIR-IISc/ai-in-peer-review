

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a model-powered approach to conditional independence (CI) testing that reduces the problem to binary classification using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure. The authors claim to convert CI testing into a two-sample test between original samples and bootstrap samples generated to mimic the conditionally independent distribution.

## Summary
The paper introduces a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap algorithm (Algorithm 1) to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution fCI. It then reduces CI testing to a binary classification problem where the classifier distinguishes between original samples (labeled 1) and bootstrap samples (labeled 0). Theoretical guarantees are provided for the bootstrap samples' closeness to fCI and for the classification error under near-independent samples. Empirical results claim superior performance over state-of-the-art methods.

## Strengths
The core idea of reducing CI testing to binary classification is conceptually interesting and aligns with recent trends in leveraging machine learning for statistical testing. The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of the bootstrap procedure and generalization bounds for non-i.i.d samples, which is valuable for the field. The empirical results on synthetic data and the flow-cytometry dataset demonstrate practical utility.

## Weaknesses
Despite the promising concept, the paper contains numerous technical and presentation issues that undermine its credibility:

1. **Notation and definition inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for fCI (sometimes as fCI(x,y,z), sometimes as fCI(x|z,y|z)), and fails to properly define key terms like "b(n)" in Theorem 1. The definition of dTV in Definition 1 has a typo ("p2B" should be "p∈B").

2. **Algorithmic errors**: Algorithm 1 contains a syntax error ("U 0 = ;" should be "U' = ∅" or similar). The paper states "Let U'2 = DataGen(U2, U3, 2n)" in Algorithm 2, but Algorithm 1 requires two partitions U1 and U2 as inputs, creating a logical inconsistency.

3. **Theoretical claims**: Theorem 1 claims "dTV(, fCI) = O(1/n1/dz)" but the actual bound is far more complex and is misstated. The paper oversimplifies the bound without explaining its practical implications. Theorem 2's inequality is poorly formatted with missing inequality signs.

4. **Missing details**: The paper claims to defer proofs to Appendix A but provides no appendix. The paper references "Fig. 2" multiple times but no figures are included in the text. The paper claims to have "extensive numerical experiments" but lacks statistical significance tests for performance differences.

5. **Theoretical justification gaps**: The paper states "the samples generated from the nearest-neighbor bootstrap do not remain i.i.d but they are close to i.i.d" without properly defining "close to i.i.d" or explaining how the total variation bound implies this property. The relationship between the theoretical bound and empirical performance is not adequately explained.

6. **Experimental details**: The paper sets "B = 50" for bootstraps without justification, and uses "τ = 1/√n" as a threshold without proper statistical motivation. The synthetic experiments don't specify what the x-axis represents in "Fig. 2a."

7. **Misreferences**: The paper cites [19] as using classifiers for independence testing but [19] is a two-sample test paper, not specifically for independence testing. The paper also mischaracterizes KCIPT [10] as requiring "an expensive linear program" without providing evidence.

8. **Formatting issues**: The paper has inconsistent spacing ("fCI(x, y, z) , fX|Z(x|z)..."), inconsistent notation for sets ("U1" vs "U'2"), and numerous punctuation errors throughout.

## Recommendation
This paper presents an interesting conceptual approach but suffers from numerous technical and presentation flaws that prevent it from being considered for publication at NIPS. The theoretical claims are misstated, the algorithmic descriptions contain errors, and key details are missing or poorly explained. While the core idea has merit, the execution is sloppy and undermines the paper's credibility.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues. Without significant revisions to correct the technical errors, clarify the theoretical claims, and provide complete experimental details, I cannot recommend acceptance.

The strengths of the paper (novel reduction to binary classification, theoretical analysis) are overshadowed by the numerous minor but critical flaws that make the paper difficult to evaluate and reproduce. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot overlook these inconsistencies in a paper that claims to provide rigorous theoretical guarantees.

I recommend rejection.
