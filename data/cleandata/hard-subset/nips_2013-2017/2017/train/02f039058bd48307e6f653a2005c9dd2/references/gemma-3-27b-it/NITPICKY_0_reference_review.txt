This paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test, reducing the problem to binary classification via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution. While the core idea of leveraging powerful classifiers for CI testing is potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to significant concerns regarding theoretical justification and empirical evaluation.

**Summary & Related Work:** The authors correctly identify the importance of CI testing in various fields. Their approach, building a classifier to distinguish between joint and conditionally independent distributions, is a logical extension of recent work utilizing classifiers for independence testing (e.g., [19]). They appropriately position their work relative to kernel-based CI tests [32, 28, 10], highlighting the potential advantages of a more flexible, model-driven approach. However, the related work section, while mentioning relevant papers, lacks depth in discussing their limitations and how this work *specifically* addresses them beyond simply stating it uses a different technique.

**Strengths:**
* **Novelty of Approach:** The reduction of CI testing to a binary classification problem is a conceptually interesting approach.
* **Potential for Scalability:** Leveraging the advancements in binary classification could indeed lead to more scalable CI tests, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The attempt to provide theoretical guarantees on the bootstrapped samples (Theorem 1) and generalization bounds (Theorem 2) is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**
* **Notational Inconsistencies & Clarity:** The paper is riddled with minor notational inconsistencies. For example, fX,Y,Z is sometimes written as f(x, y, z) and sometimes as f(X, Y, Z).  The variable naming is also inconsistent (e.g., U02 vs U’02). This hinders readability. The description of Algorithm 3 (bias correction) is confusing, and the rationale for training a classifier *only* on Y and Z coordinates is not sufficiently explained.
* **Theoretical Concerns:** Theorem 1’s assumptions (1) and (2) regarding smoothness of f(z) and f(y|z) are strong and rarely met in practice. The term “generalized curvature matrix” is introduced without sufficient context or explanation.  The complexity of the bound in Theorem 1 (O(1/n1/dz)) suggests limited practical benefit, particularly given the dependence on dz. Furthermore, the connection between the total variational distance and the classifier's error rate (Theorem 2) feels tenuous without a more rigorous justification of why the classifier effectively learns to distinguish these distributions. The "o(n)" term is undefined.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is weak. The synthetic experiments lack detail regarding parameter settings (e.g., the choice of 'a' and 'b' in X and Y generation). The claim that CCIT "scales much better" than other algorithms is not convincingly demonstrated; the performance differences are marginal in the presented figures. The use of flow cytometry data is superficial.  Simply stating that the algorithm "outperforms" others without statistical significance testing or a thorough ablation study is insufficient. The discussion of discrepancies with known causal structures is hand-wavy and doesn't provide concrete insights. The code availability is a positive, but the lack of detailed experimental setup information limits reproducibility.
* **Algorithm Redundancy:** Algorithm 3, the bias-corrected version, introduces unnecessary complexity. The motivation for this bias correction isn't clearly established, and the empirical impact is not demonstrated. Why is training a classifier on only Y and Z expected to provide a good estimate of the bias?
* **Missing Details:** The paper omits crucial implementation details. What specific gradient boosted trees or deep neural network architectures were used? What hyperparameter tuning strategies were employed? These omissions impede reproducibility and make it difficult to assess the robustness of the results.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Novel reduction of CI testing to a classification problem.
* Attempt to provide theoretical justification (although flawed).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
* Numerous notational inconsistencies and lack of clarity.
* Strong, potentially unrealistic assumptions in the theoretical analysis.
* Weak and insufficiently detailed empirical evaluation.
* Limited practical benefit of the proposed algorithm given the theoretical complexity.
* Redundant algorithm (Algorithm 3).
* Lack of crucial implementation details.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea but is ultimately unconvincing in its current form. The numerous flaws in presentation, theoretical justification, and empirical evaluation outweigh the novelty of the approach. While the concept of leveraging classifiers for CI testing is promising, this paper does not deliver a robust and well-supported implementation. I recommend **rejection**.  The authors should address the clarity and notational issues, strengthen the theoretical foundations with more realistic assumptions, provide a much more thorough empirical evaluation, and justify the need for Algorithm 3. A significant rewrite is required to address these concerns.
