### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper introduces a novel, "model-powered" approach for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning. The proposed method, named Classifier CI Test (CCIT), cleverly reduces the CI testing problem (distinguishing H0: X ⫫ Y | Z from H1: X ⫫̸ Y | Z) to a standard binary classification task.

The core idea is as follows:
1.  Generate samples that emulate the conditionally independent distribution f_CI(x, y, z) = f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z) from the original data drawn from f(x, y, z). This is achieved via a novel "nearest-neighbor bootstrap" procedure. For each sample (x, y, z), it finds a nearest neighbor in a separate hold-out set based on the z-coordinate, and swaps their y-coordinates.
2.  Label the original samples as class '1' and the bootstrapped samples as class '0'.
3.  Train a powerful binary classifier (e.g., XGBoost) to distinguish between these two classes.

The intuition is that if H0 is true, the original and bootstrapped distributions are nearly identical, and the classifier's accuracy will be close to 50% (random guessing). If H1 is true, the distributions are distinguishable, and a good classifier will achieve significantly better accuracy.

This work fits into the growing literature on leveraging machine learning models for statistical testing. It is closely related to [19], which used classifiers for two-sample independence testing (the Z-unconditional case), but the key innovation here is the nearest-neighbor bootstrap to handle the conditioning variable Z, which is non-trivial. The approach provides a flexible alternative to existing kernel-based methods like KCIT [32] and RCIT [28], and a more computationally efficient alternative to permutation-based methods like KCIPT [10] which require solving a linear program. The paper also provides a solid theoretical foundation, including guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and novel generalization bounds for classifiers trained on the resulting non-i.i.d data.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The theoretical contributions are significant. Theorem 1, which bounds the total variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and the true conditionally independent distribution, provides a firm theoretical justification for the core mechanism.
    *   Theorem 2 is particularly impressive, as it provides generalization bounds for the classifier in a non-i.i.d setting. Analyzing the Rademacher complexity under the "near-independence" structure induced by the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a novel and valuable contribution, potentially of independent interest.
    *   The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The method is benchmarked against strong, relevant baselines (KCIT, RCIT) on both synthetic and real-world data. The results, particularly the superior scaling with the dimension of the conditioning set Z, strongly support the paper's claims.
    *   The introduction of a bias-corrected version (CCITv2) demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of the practical challenges of the method, improving its robustness.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   There is a notable gap between the theoretical guarantees and the empirical performance regarding the dimensionality of Z. Theorem 1 suggests a rate of O(1/n^(1/d_z)), implying a severe curse of dimensionality. However, the experiments show excellent performance for d_z up to 70. While the authors acknowledge this is a worst-case analysis, a deeper discussion of why practical performance is so much better (perhaps due to manifold assumptions or the power of the classifier) would strengthen the paper.
    *   The smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) required for the theoretical results are quite strong. It would be helpful to discuss their practical implications and how restrictive they might be for certain real-world distributions.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The main idea is presented intuitively at the beginning and supported by a clear figure (Fig. 1).
    *   The algorithms are described with clear pseudocode, which would aid in reproducibility.
    *   The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, making the comparisons easy to follow.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   Section 3 (Theoretical Results) is dense and could be challenging for non-experts. While the main theorems are stated clearly, more intuition behind the proof techniques (especially for the non-i.i.d bounds) in the main text could improve accessibility. For instance, a brief explanation of how "spatial near-independence" is formalized would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The central contribution—the reduction of CI testing to classification via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap—is a novel and elegant idea.
    *   While its components (classifiers, nearest-neighbor search) are standard, their composition into this specific pipeline for CI testing is new.
    *   The theoretical analysis for the non-i.i.d samples generated by the procedure is a novel contribution to learning theory.

**Significance:** The paper has the potential for high impact.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   CI testing is a cornerstone of many fields, including causal discovery and feature selection. A method that is computationally efficient and scales well to high-dimensional conditioning sets is a major advance.
    *   The "model-powered" philosophy is very appealing. It allows the CI test to directly leverage ongoing, rapid progress in supervised learning. As classifiers improve, so will this test. This modularity makes the contribution long-lasting.
    *   The provided open-source implementation is a significant plus, encouraging adoption by practitioners.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Novel and Elegant Reduction:** The paper presents a new, intuitive, and powerful way to frame the fundamental problem of CI testing.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art competitors, especially in the challenging and important setting where the conditioning set Z is high-dimensional.
3.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The claims are backed by rigorous theoretical analysis, including novel generalization bounds for a non-i.i.d setting created by the method itself.
4.  **High Potential Impact:** Given its performance, flexibility, and the foundational nature of the problem it solves, the method is likely to be widely used and built upon by both researchers and practitioners.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

1.  **Theory-Practice Gap:** The theoretical bounds on sample complexity suffer from a curse of dimensionality that is not reflected in the experiments. This gap, while not invalidating the work, could be addressed more directly.
2.  **Strong Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on strong smoothness and density assumptions, which may limit their applicability in some domains.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, effective, and theoretically-grounded method for conditional independence testing. The core idea of reducing the problem to binary classification via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap is both clever and powerful. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating a significant improvement over existing methods, particularly in high dimensions. While there is a gap between the worst-case theoretical bounds and practical performance, the overall contribution is strong, original, and highly significant. I strongly recommend acceptance.
