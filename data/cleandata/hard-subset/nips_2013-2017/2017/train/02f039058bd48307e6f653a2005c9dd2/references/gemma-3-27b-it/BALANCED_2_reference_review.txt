This paper presents a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing, framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea is to generate samples mimicking the conditionally independent distribution via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap, and then train a classifier to distinguish between original and generated samples. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples and generalization bounds for the classifier. Empirical results demonstrate competitive performance against existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly in higher dimensions and on a flow cytometry dataset.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning with broad applications. The reduction to a classification problem is a clever idea, allowing leveraging advances in supervised learning. The theoretical analysis is substantial, providing bounds on the total variational distance between generated and true conditionally independent distributions, as well as generalization guarantees. The empirical evaluation is thorough, encompassing synthetic datasets and a real-world application, and convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method. The connection to existing work, particularly kernel-based CI tests and recent work utilizing classification for independence testing, is clearly articulated. The provision of code is a valuable contribution.

However, several weaknesses and areas for improvement exist. The notation, while generally clear, can be dense and could benefit from more careful use of subscripts and superscripts for clarity, particularly in the theoretical sections. The presentation of the theoretical results, specifically Theorems 1 and 2, is quite technical and relies on several assumptions. While the authors state these assumptions, a more intuitive explanation of their implications and limitations would be helpful. For instance, explaining what constitutes a "smoothness assumption" in the context of the results would make them more accessible. 

The discussion of the threshold ⌧ in Algorithm 3 feels slightly superficial. More justification for its selection (beyond being an upper bound on variance) and a sensitivity analysis regarding its impact on performance would be valuable. Furthermore, while the empirical results are strong, a comparison to simpler, non-kernel-based CI tests (e.g., based on mutual information estimation) would provide additional context for the performance gains.  The impact of the classifier choice is briefly mentioned, but a more systematic exploration of different classifier types and their influence on the results could further strengthen the paper.  The conclusion focuses heavily on the model-powered approach; a brief discussion of potential limitations and future directions specifically related to the bootstrap method (e.g., computational cost for very high-dimensional Z) would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The reduction of CI testing to binary classification is a novel and promising approach.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the proposed method.
* **Empirical Performance:**  The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods.
* **Clarity (Overall):** Despite some density in the theory, the overall presentation is clear and well-organized.
* **Code Availability:** The provision of code promotes reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Technical Density:**  The theoretical analysis is challenging to follow for readers not deeply familiar with the relevant mathematical tools.
* **Assumptions:**  The theoretical results rely on several assumptions that may not always hold in practice.
* **Limited Comparison:** The empirical comparison could be broadened to include simpler CI tests.
* **Superficial Discussion of Threshold:** The justification and sensitivity of the ⌧ parameter are not fully explored.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. While the technical details can be demanding, the core idea is innovative, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are compelling. Addressing the suggested weaknesses would further enhance the quality of the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I recommend the authors focus on improving the accessibility of the theoretical results, providing a more thorough discussion of the threshold parameter, and expanding the empirical comparison to include a wider range of baseline methods.
