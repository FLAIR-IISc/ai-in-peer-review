

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The key innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) that generates samples close to the conditionally independent distribution f_CI, enabling the use of powerful classifiers for CI testing. The authors theoretically prove that the generated samples are close to f_CI in total variational distance (Theorem 1) and provide generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). The approach builds on recent work using classifiers for independence testing [19] but extends it to the more challenging conditional independence setting by solving the Z-dependent permutation problem.

The paper relates to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT [32], RCIT [28], KCIPT [10]), with the authors distinguishing their work by providing theoretical guarantees on the bootstrap quality (unlike KCIPT which uses an expensive linear program) and using classifiers rather than kernel methods for the two-sample test. The paper also connects to recent work on generalization bounds for non-i.i.d settings [21].

## Strengths

**Quality:** The theoretical contributions are sound and well-motivated. The authors provide rigorous guarantees (Theorems 1 and 2) on the bootstrap procedure and classification generalization, with appropriate smoothness assumptions. The empirical evaluation is thorough, showing significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods across both synthetic experiments (Fig. 2a) and a real-world flow-cytometry dataset (Fig. 2b, Table 2c). The bias-corrected version (Algorithm 3) addresses a practical limitation in the initial approach, demonstrating careful consideration of implementation details.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, algorithm descriptions, theoretical results, and empirical evaluation follow a logical progression. The figures (particularly Fig. 1) effectively illustrate the core methodology, and the algorithms (1-3) are precisely specified. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the main results.

**Originality:** The nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach to generate samples from f_CI is novel and addresses a key limitation of previous permutation-based methods [10]. The reduction of CI testing to binary classification (using a different approach than [19] for independence testing) is a significant conceptual contribution. The theoretical analysis of generalization bounds under near-independent samples (Theorem 2) is also novel and of independent interest.

**Significance:** The paper solves an important problem with broad applications in Bayesian networks, causal inference, and feature selection. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings where previous approaches struggle. The model-powered approach enables leveraging advances in classification, making the method more adaptable to specific data characteristics.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Complexity:** While theoretically sound, Theorem 1's bound (d_TV(·, f_CI) ≤ b(n)) involves a complex expression with multiple terms depending on dimensionality. This makes it difficult to interpret the practical implications of the bound, particularly for readers not deeply familiar with the technical details. The authors could provide more intuitive discussion of how the bound scales with n and d_z.

**Practical Implementation:** The paper doesn't adequately address how to choose the threshold τ in Algorithm 3 for real-world applications. While they set τ = 1/√n in experiments, this is an ad-hoc choice without theoretical justification. A more principled approach to threshold selection would strengthen the practical applicability.

**Comparison with Related Work:** The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze why the proposed method outperforms alternatives in high dimensions. The empirical results show CCIT scales better with d_z, but the authors don't provide detailed insights into the specific mechanisms (e.g., how the nearest-neighbor bootstrap better captures dependencies than kernel-based methods).

**Computational Considerations:** While the paper mentions "fast algorithms for nearest-neighbor search" [24], it doesn't discuss the computational complexity of Algorithm 1, particularly for high-dimensional Z. This is a significant omission for a method that relies on nearest-neighbor searches.

## Conclusion

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The nearest-neighbor bootstrap approach is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically validated as superior to state-of-the-art methods. The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem with clear practical relevance.

The main limitations are the complexity of the theoretical bounds and insufficient discussion of practical implementation details (particularly threshold selection). However, these do not undermine the paper's core contributions, which are substantial and well-supported.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:** Novel approach to CI testing; strong theoretical guarantees; comprehensive empirical validation showing clear advantages over state-of-the-art; well-written and clear presentation.

**Con-acceptance arguments:** Theoretical bounds are complex and difficult to interpret; insufficient practical guidance on threshold selection; limited discussion of computational complexity.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field with sound technical content, clear presentation, and meaningful empirical results. It advances the state of the art in CI testing and provides a new framework that could be applied to other statistical testing problems. I recommend acceptance.
