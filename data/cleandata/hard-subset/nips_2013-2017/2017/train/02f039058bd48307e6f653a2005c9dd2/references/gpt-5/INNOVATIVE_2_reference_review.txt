Summary
The paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test (CCIT) that reduces CI testing of X ⟂ Y | Z to a binary classification problem. The key idea is a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap in Z-space: split the data, and for each (x,y,z) in one partition, replace y with the y of its 1-NN in Z from the other partition, yielding samples approximately from fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z(x|z) fY|Z(y|z) fZ(z). Then perform a classifier two-sample test between original samples and NN-bootstrapped samples. A bias-corrected variant subtracts the accuracy of a classifier trained only on (Y,Z) from that trained on (X,Y,Z). Theoretical contributions include: (i) a total variation bound between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and fCI with rate O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions, and (ii) generalization bounds for ERM classifiers trained on “near-i.i.d.” samples arising from the bootstrap. Empirically, CCIT outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear data and on CI relations derived from protein signaling networks.

Relation to prior work
This approach complements kernel-based CI tests (KCIT/RCIT) and the permutation-based KCIPT, offering a simple, scalable bootstrap rather than solving a global matching/LP. It also aligns with the classifier two-sample testing paradigm (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab) and with the reduction philosophy prominent at NeurIPS. Compared to independence testing via random permutation, the contribution is to design a Z-aware bootstrap and analyze its deviation from fCI and the implications for classification. While the paper mentions entropy/CMI estimation via NN estimators, it would be useful to further position CCIT relative to conditional randomization-style tests (e.g., modeling X|Z, as in CRT) and MI-based approaches.

Strengths
- Conceptually bold and simple reduction: CI testing → classification via a nearest-neighbor conditional bootstrap. This modularity allows immediate leverage of advances in supervised learning.
- Nontrivial theory: a TV-distance guarantee that the NN-bootstrapped distribution approximates fCI, and generalization guarantees for ERM under near-independence. The “spatial near-independence” perspective is interesting beyond this application.
- Practicality and scalability: efficient to implement with standard NN search; uses off-the-shelf classifiers (XGBoost). Code is provided.
- Empirical results are strong, especially scaling to high dz where kernel methods often struggle. The bias-corrected variant is a sensible practical improvement.

Weaknesses and questions
- Level control and p-values: The paper reports ROC/AUC and uses a heuristic threshold τ≈1/√n. There is no provable type-I error control at a user-specified α. Can the authors provide a calibration procedure (e.g., label-permutation between U1 and U’2 under H0, repeated splits/bootstraps to form an empirical null) and show size control?
- Sensitivity to classifier choice and tuning: Performance depends on the classifier, its capacity, and hyperparameters. The theory is stated in terms of VC dimension, which is vacuous for complex models (boosted trees/deep nets). Some ablations (e.g., RF, linear, DNN) and guidance for practitioners would help.
- High-dimensional Z: The TV bound scales as O(n^{-1/dz}), which is pessimistic. While experiments are encouraging up to dz=70, more systematic study is needed (sample size vs dz vs effect size; NN distance concentration; approximate NN). Also, 1-NN may be suboptimal; k-NN with k>1 or local smoothing might improve bias–variance trade-offs.
- Dependence structure: The generated samples are not i.i.d. Although the paper provides a near-i.i.d. analysis, assumptions (e.g., bounded Lipschitzness of f(z)) and constants could be discussed more explicitly. A cross-fitting scheme (multiple disjoint partitions for generation/training/testing) could further reduce dependence; consider recommending it.
- Practical bias correction: Subtracting the (Y,Z)-only classifier loss is clever but lacks a finite-sample guarantee that this exactly cancels the bias. Some theoretical or empirical justification of its calibration impact would strengthen the contribution.
- Comparisons: KCIT/RCIT are included; please also report computational cost vs. n and dz, and sensitivity to kernel choices for baselines. Clarify whether baselines were tuned fairly.
- Related work: Consider discussing conditional randomization tests (CRT; modeling X|Z to test X ⟂ Y | Z) and conditional permutation/matching ideas; and classic MI estimators (e.g., KSG) as alternative CI tests via CMI, to better triangulate the landscape.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The NN bootstrap analysis and near-i.i.d. generalization results are interesting and, to my knowledge, new in this context. Empirics are solid, though the lack of level control is a notable gap for a testing paper. Clarity of assumptions (Assumptions 1–2) could be improved; adding intuition and examples for when they hold would help.

Clarity
Well organized and mostly clear. Some notation is heavy and could be streamlined. The algorithms are easy to follow. Please polish typos and minor formatting issues, and more explicitly state the full testing protocol (data splits, number of bootstrap replicates, classifier tuning). Provide runtime plots.

Originality
High. The nearest-neighbor conditional bootstrap to emulate fCI and the reduction to classification with accompanying non-i.i.d. generalization bounds is a fresh direction relative to kernel CI tests and CMI estimation. The idea is simple yet powerful.

Significance
High practical relevance: CI testing is central to causal discovery and graphical models. The method is likely to be adopted given its modularity and performance. The theoretical tools may be reusable in other model-powered tests.

Pros
- Simple, modular, and novel reduction of CI testing to classification via NN bootstrap
- Theoretical guarantees: TV closeness to fCI and near-i.i.d. generalization
- Strong empirical performance and scalability; code available

Cons
- No rigorous level-α test or p-values; τ selection is heuristic
- Theoretical rates degrade with dz; guidance to mitigate in practice is limited
- Sensitivity to classifier choice/hyperparameters; limited ablations
- Dependence introduced by bootstrap not fully neutralized in practice

Suggestions
- Add a calibrated testing procedure with empirical null (e.g., repeated label permutations between U1 and U’2 under H0) to report p-values and control type-I error.
- Explore k-NN or local-kernel variants of the bootstrap; include ablations on k and approximate NN.
- Provide runtime comparisons and sensitivity analyses (classifier choice, hyperparameters, dz, n).
- Consider cross-fitting partitions to further reduce dependence between generated and original samples.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). The paper introduces a compelling and novel direction with solid theory and promising results. While level control and calibration deserve further work, the conceptual contribution and empirical scalability merit acceptance at NeurIPS.

Confidence
High.
