Summary
The paper proposes a “model-powered” nonparametric conditional independence (CI) test that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples of (X, Y, Z), the method splits the data, constructs pseudo-samples via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that swaps Y between points with similar Z, and then trains a classifier to distinguish original data (label 1) from NN-bootstrapped data (label 0). If the classifier cannot distinguish the two, the data are deemed consistent with CI. Theoretical contributions include (i) a total variation (TV) bound showing that the NN-bootstrapped distribution is close to fCI(x, y, z) = fX|Z fY|Z fZ at rate O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions, and (ii) generalization bounds for classification with “near-i.i.d.” samples produced by the bootstrap. Empirical comparisons on synthetic data and on flow cytometry CI relations report improved ROC-AUC over KCIT and RCIT. A bias-corrected variant subtracts a classifier trained on (Y, Z) alone.

Relation to prior work
The reduction to classification aligns with classifier two-sample testing (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2016) and the permutation-based approach to independence testing. KCIT/RCIT and KCIPT are closest; KCIPT also simulates fCI via permutation but requires solving a linear program, whereas this paper proposes a simple 1-NN swap in Z. Theoretical elements (TV closeness and generalization under dependence) connect to kNN-based estimation (Gao et al.) and Rademacher complexity for non-i.i.d. processes (Mohri & Rostamizadeh).

Strengths
- Clear high-level idea: use a simple NN-based resampling to approximate fCI and leverage modern classifiers.
- Theoretical attempt to justify the reduction: TV closeness of a bootstrapped sample to fCI and generalization bounds under near-independence.
- Practical appeal: leverages advances in classification, can adapt to domain classifiers, and is easy to implement.
- Empirical results suggest favorable ROC-AUC vs. KCIT/RCIT, including in moderate-to-high dz settings.
- Bias-correction idea to mitigate finite-n artifacts is sensible in spirit.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Methodology and statistical validity
- No finite-sample control of type-I error or clear procedure to produce p-values; decisions hinge on ad hoc thresholds (τ ≈ 1/√n) without rigorous calibration. ROC-AUC demonstrations are not a substitute for a valid level-α test.
- Bias-correction via subtracting a (Y,Z)-only classifier’s loss is not theoretically justified. The two losses are learned with different input spaces and different induced function classes/comparators, so subtracting them does not consistently remove the bias induced by the bootstrap. A principled calibration (e.g., permutation-based null distribution of the test statistic, cross-fitting, or conformal calibration) is missing.
- The classifier may exploit artifacts of the NN resampling beyond the intended Y|Z shift (e.g., subtle sample-dependent correlations); the bias correction assumes the only bias resides in the (Y,Z) marginals, which need not hold.
- The 1-NN swap in Z can be unstable in high dimensions; kd-trees deteriorate and nearest neighbors become distant. The method depends critically on a metric and scaling of Z; there is no discussion of standardization/metric choice or sensitivity.

Theory
- Theorem 1 (TV closeness) depends on strong and nonstandard smoothness assumptions (Assumption 1’s “generalized curvature” matrix Ia(z)), whose definition is unclear, uses unusual notation (mixing z, z′, a), and lacks intuition. It is difficult to verify in practice.
- Rates O(n^{-1/dz}) are extremely slow and reflect the curse of dimensionality in Z; while the authors claim empirical performance is better, there is no theory bridging the worst-case bounds with practical regimes (e.g., manifold, sparsity, or margin assumptions).
- The samples in U′ are dependent; Theorem 2 attempts to give generalization bounds under “near-independence,” but the final bound is complex, with many opaque constants, a log(n)/n^{1/3} term appearing without clear derivation, and an o_n(1/ε) term that is hard to interpret or tune. The reliance on G(ε) = P(f(Z)<ε) puts the burden on tail regions without giving guidance on selecting ε.
- The decision rules in Algorithms 2–3 are ambiguous: lines “> 0.5 τ” and “< … − τ” appear to miss a minus sign and are inconsistent with the text that compares to random guessing (0.5). This is a correctness issue that affects reproducibility.

Empirical evaluation
- No type-I error/size control plots at fixed α across n and dz. ROC-AUC alone is insufficient. Power-size tradeoffs vs. KCIT/RCIT are not reported.
- Fairness of baselines is unclear: kernel bandwidths, parameter tuning, and computational budgets are not described; RCIT/KCIT often require careful parameterization in high dimensions.
- For the flow cytometry study, the ground-truth is uncertain; selecting 50 negative examples via random conditioning sets may not reliably represent non-CI. Discrepancies with common edges are reported but not analyzed. No ablation on classifier choice, hyperparameters, or the effect of B (number of bootstraps) on stability.
- Computational complexity and wall-clock time are not reported. In high dz, 1-NN search can be costly and unstable; B=50 multiplies the cost.

Clarity, presentation, and formatting
- Numerous formatting/notation issues impair readability: “psuedo-code,” “st Conference…” header, broken symbols (e.g., “X ? Y |Z”), inconsistent superscripts (U 02), ambiguous inequalities in decision rules, missing bars/parentheses, and undefined constants (e.g., “d is the volume of the unit ball”). Many equations contain typos.
- Algorithm 1: tie-breaking in NN not specified; union operation U′ = U′ ∪ {u′} suggests sets (duplicates ignored) rather than multiset; the input “2n” to DataGen is unused. Metric choice and normalization for Z are unspecified.
- References are incomplete (missing years/pages), several appear as placeholders, and important related work (e.g., modern conditional MMD-based tests, cross-fitting CI tests, recent classifier two-sample advances) is not adequately discussed.
- The paper mixes v1/v2 algorithm labels, uses nonstandard notation for TV and risk bounds, and includes undefined symbols (e.g., r0 vs. r_0).

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and simple idea to approximate fCI with NN resampling and leverage strong classifiers.
- Attempted theoretical justification for both approximation error (TV) and learning with dependent samples.
- Promising empirical AUC in synthetic and real-world settings.

Cons for acceptance
- No valid, calibrated test with controlled type-I error; reliance on ad hoc thresholds and ROC curves.
- Theoretical assumptions are strong/unclear; rates are pessimistic and constants opaque; decision rule ambiguities.
- Empirical evaluation lacks rigor in baseline tuning, size/power analysis, runtime, and sensitivity studies.
- Numerous clarity/formatting/notation issues and incomplete references.

Questions for the authors
1. How would you calibrate the test to control type-I error at a chosen α? Could you provide a permutation/wild bootstrap under H0 that preserves exchangeability?
2. Can you justify bias correction in Algorithm 3 theoretically, or propose a matched-pairs or cross-fitting scheme that avoids subtracting incomparable losses?
3. How sensitive is performance to the choice of metric/standardization in Z and to k in k-NN (k>1)? Any robustness results?
4. Can you report size and power across n, dz at fixed α with KCIT/RCIT tuned competitively, and provide runtime comparisons?
5. Please clarify Assumption 1 rigorously and correct the decision inequalities in Algorithms 2–3.

Recommendation: Reject
While the idea is appealing and could be impactful, the paper in its current form suffers from significant methodological, theoretical, and presentation issues. A revised version addressing calibration, clearer and lighter assumptions, rigorous empirical evaluation (size/power and runtime), and substantial editing could be much stronger.

Confidence: High
