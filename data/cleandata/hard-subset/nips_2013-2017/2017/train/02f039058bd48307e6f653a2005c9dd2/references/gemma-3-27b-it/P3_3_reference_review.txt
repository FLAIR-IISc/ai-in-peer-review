## NIPS 2017 Review: Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and promising approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by framing it as a binary classification problem. The core idea of using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples approximating the conditionally independent distribution is clever, and the theoretical analysis provides some guarantees on the quality of these samples.  The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance, particularly in higher dimensions. However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in certain sections, a somewhat incremental nature given the existing literature, and some theoretical results that feel conditional and require further scrutiny. I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**, contingent on addressing the clarity issues and providing more convincing empirical evidence for the scalability and robustness of the method.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The authors tackle the problem of CI testing, a fundamental task in causal inference and statistical modeling.  They propose a "model-powered" approach that leverages the power of binary classifiers. The central innovation is a nearest-neighbor bootstrap algorithm designed to generate samples approximating the fCI distribution (the joint distribution under conditional independence). These generated samples, combined with original data, form a labeled dataset for training a classifier to distinguish between the CI and dependent scenarios.  The authors correctly position their work within the existing landscape of CI testing, acknowledging kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) and permutation-based approaches (KCIPT). They highlight the computational expense of KCIPT’s linear programming step as a key motivation for their work.  The connection to recent work using classifiers for independence testing [19] is also well-made.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:**  Reducing CI testing to a classification problem is not entirely new (as acknowledged in relation to [19]), but the specific strategy of generating samples via nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate fCI is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The paper provides theoretical results on the total variational distance between the bootstrapped samples and fCI, along with generalization bounds for the classifier. This is a valuable addition and sets this work apart from purely empirical approaches.  
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method (CCIT) outperforms state-of-the-art methods, especially as the dimension of Z increases. This is a strong indicator of the method’s potential.
* **Modularity and Flexibility:** The model-powered framework is inherently flexible, allowing for the incorporation of different classifiers and potentially benefiting from advances in the classification field.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for their work - overcoming the computational cost of existing methods.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Presentation:** The presentation can be dense and difficult to follow in places. The notation is heavy, and the flow between sections could be improved.  Algorithm 3, while conceptually interesting, feels tacked on and its motivation isn't sufficiently explained.  The discussion surrounding "bias correction" is somewhat hand-wavy and requires more detailed justification.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on strong smoothness assumptions on the underlying distributions (Assumptions 1 & 2). While not unreasonable, the practical implications of these assumptions are not thoroughly discussed.  How sensitive is the method to violations of these assumptions?
* **Incremental Improvement:**  While the approach is novel, the performance gains over existing methods in the synthetic experiments are not always dramatic.  The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to baselines, including simpler methods.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is primarily focused on synthetic data and one flow cytometry dataset. It would be beneficial to see the performance of CCIT on a wider range of real-world datasets with varying characteristics.
* **VC Dimension:** The use of VC dimension in the generalization bounds feels somewhat limiting. In practice, other measures of model complexity might be more relevant and provide tighter bounds.
* **Computational Cost:** While positioned as a faster alternative, the paper doesn’t provide a clear analysis of the computational cost of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap algorithm, especially in high dimensions.

**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Algorithm 1:**  The choice of using the 1-Nearest Neighbor is somewhat arbitrary. Has the sensitivity of the approach to the 'k' parameter in k-NN been explored?
* **Theorem 1:** The bound on the total variational distance contains a term proportional to `1/n^(1/dz)`. This suggests that the convergence rate to fCI is slow in high dimensions (large `dz`).  This needs to be addressed – the empirical results suggest better scaling, which needs to be explained.
* **Algorithm 3:** The rationale behind training a classifier solely on (Y, Z) and subtracting its loss is not immediately clear. A more detailed explanation and justification are needed.  How is the threshold ⌧ determined in practice?
* **Synthetic Experiments:** What are the specific parameters used in the synthetic experiments (e.g., noise level, dimensionality of Z)? Providing more details would make the results more reproducible.
* **Flow Cytometry Dataset:** Why were the specific graphs selected as "ground truth"?  Acknowledging the uncertainties inherent in causal discovery from observational data is important.



**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

* Novel and promising approach to CI testing.
* Theoretical guarantees on the quality of the bootstrapped samples.
* Competitive empirical performance, particularly in higher dimensions.
* Potential for scalability and flexibility.

**Con Acceptance:**

* Clarity issues in the presentation and notation.
* Strong assumptions underlying the theoretical results.
* Incremental improvement over existing methods.
* Limited empirical scope.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I believe this paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field of CI testing. However, addressing the clarity issues, providing a more thorough discussion of the theoretical assumptions, and expanding the empirical evaluation are crucial for strengthening the paper and justifying its acceptance. Specifically, I recommend:

* **Improving Clarity:**  Rewriting sections 2 and 3 for better readability. Clearly explain the motivation behind Algorithm 3.
* **Addressing Theoretical Concerns:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to violations of the smoothness assumptions.  Investigate the scaling of the bootstrap algorithm in high dimensions.
* **Expanding Empirical Evaluation:**  Testing the method on a wider range of real-world datasets.
* **Providing a computational cost analysis:** Compare computational time of Algorithm 1 to other CI testing algorithms.
