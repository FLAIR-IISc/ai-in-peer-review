Summary
This paper proposes a reduction of nonparametric conditional independence (CI) testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method (CCIT) constructs a “conditionally independent” proxy sample via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap in Z: it partitions the data, replaces Y in one partition with the Y of its 1-NN in Z from another partition, thereby preserving the (X,Z) marginal while approximately breaking any X–Y dependence given Z. A classifier is then trained to distinguish original samples (label 1) from NN-bootstrapped samples (label 0). Poor classification performance supports H0: X ⟂ Y | Z; strong performance supports H1. The paper provides: (i) a TV-distance bound showing the bootstrapped distribution approaches fCI at rate O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions; (ii) generalization bounds for ERM under “near-independent” samples; (iii) a bias-corrected variant subtracting a classifier that uses only (Y,Z); and (iv) experiments showing improved ROC-AUC over KCIT/RCIT on synthetic data and a flow cytometry dataset. The approach relates to previous classifier two-sample tests and permutation-based CI tests (particularly [10], [19], [32], [28]), but differs in the neighbor-based resampling scheme and the accompanying theoretical analysis.

Strengths
- Simple, modular reduction leveraging modern classifiers (e.g., XGBoost), with flexible applicability.
- Conceptually appealing NN bootstrap that preserves (X,Z) while approximating Y ⟂ X | Z.
- Nontrivial theory: a TV bound quantifying closeness to fCI, and generalization guarantees under non-i.i.d. sampling—potentially of independent interest.
- Empirically promising ROC-AUC, especially as dim(Z) grows in the synthetic post-nonlinear setup.
- Public code release.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness:
- Lack of calibrated hypothesis testing: The paper reports ROC curves but does not provide a procedure with controlled Type I error (p-values/critical values). The proposed thresholds (e.g., τ=1/√n) are heuristic; no null distribution, permutation/calibration scheme, or size control is established. KCIT/RCIT provide p-values; CCIT as presented is primarily a scoring method, not a valid CI test.
- Asymptotic rate O(n^{-1/dz}) in Theorem 1 is extremely slow. While the authors argue classifiers perform well empirically at high dz, the theory is pessimistic and there is no bridging analysis explaining why practice outperforms the worst case.
- Assumptions are opaque and nonstandard. Assumption 1 defines a “generalized curvature” matrix via second derivatives of a log-likelihood ratio integral; the quantity, its interpretability, and verifiability are unclear. Additional assumptions (bounded Lipschitz f(z)) appear later without being integrated cleanly with Assumption 2.
- The non-i.i.d. generalization analysis (Theorem 2) is interesting but the bounds are complex, involve multiple overlapping constants, and give limited actionable guidance (e.g., dependence on k-NN neighborhood size, dz, and classifier capacity). Moreover, the analysis does not cover common high-capacity learners (deep nets, boosted trees) in a meaningful way given huge/unknown VC dimension.
- Potential distributional artifacts: Since U’2 is built from U2 and U3 while U1 is left intact, finite-sample imbalances in Z-density or edge effects may be detectable by a high-capacity classifier; the bias-correction via (Y,Z)-only classifier is a heuristic without guarantees on exact bias cancellation.
- No discussion of computational complexity and scalability of NN search in high-dimensional Z (kd-trees degrade). Practical runtime vs KCIT/RCIT is not reported.

Clarity/presentation:
- Numerous notation overloads/inconsistencies: G denotes both the classifier class and the function G(ε)=P(f(Z)≤ε); d denotes both a dimension-related constant and volume of a unit ball; “V” is the VC dimension while “dz” is the Z dimension. Several symbols are undefined or inconsistently used (e.g., b(n) in Theorem 1 appears without a clean definition;  denotes what?).
- Many typographical/formatting issues: mis-rendered symbols (⊥ shown as “?”), inconsistent subscripts/superscripts (U 02, U 0 vs U’2), missing operators and spacing in inequalities, “psuedo-code,” etc. Several references are incomplete (years, venues, pages missing).
- Algorithm descriptions occasionally conflict (Algorithm 1 uses U1, U2; Algorithm 2 passes U2, U3) and should more clearly state that (X,Z) marginals are preserved for the label-0 class.

Originality:
- Relation to permutation-based CI testing (e.g., [10]) is acknowledged; the novelty is the simple NN-based resampling and TV guarantees. However, the conceptual reduction to classifier two-sample testing is well established; the main originality rests on the specific bootstrap and its analysis.

Significance:
- If accompanied by a valid calibration method with Type I control and clean empirical evaluation, this could be impactful. As is, the absence of formal testing calibration and extensive sensitivity analyses limits practical adoption.

Empirical evaluation:
- ROC-only evaluation is insufficient for a CI test. Please report Type I error at fixed α across settings and power vs α curves.
- Baseline tuning and fairness are unclear (KCIT/RCIT hyperparameters). Runtime comparisons are missing.
- Flow cytometry evaluation relies on assumed ground-truth graphs; the claim “edges are never CI given any other conditioning set” is not universally valid in general causal structures. Negative example generation may mislabel some cases, confounding evaluation.
- Limited ablations: no study of k-NN choice (k=1 only), classifier choice/hyperparameters, effect of sample size n vs dz, or sensitivity to Z-density tails (G(·)).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a calibrated testing procedure (e.g., label permutation between U1 and U’2, cross-fitting, or conditional randomization tests) with finite-sample or asymptotic size control; report Type I error and power.
- Clarify and simplify assumptions; replace Assumption 1 with more standard smoothness/Lipschitz conditions if possible, or carefully motivate and interpret Ia(z).
- Improve theory/practice bridge: analyze how k>1 NN or kernel resampling affects bias/variance and the TV rate; discuss why high-capacity classifiers help despite poor worst-case rates.
- Clean up notation, symbols, and proofs; fix typos and references; clearly define all constants and functions; avoid symbol overloading.
- Add runtime analysis and NN-search details; consider approximate NN for high dz.
- Expand experiments: ablate k, classifier families, hyperparameters; ensure fair baseline tuning; report calibration quality; include additional modern CI tests if feasible.

Pros
- Elegant, practical reduction; simple NN bootstrap.
- Theoretical effort to bound closeness to fCI and to handle non-i.i.d. samples.
- Strong-looking ROC on synthetic/high-dz settings; code available.

Cons
- No valid size-controlled hypothesis test; heavy reliance on ROC.
- Opaque assumptions; messy notation and several presentation errors.
- Weak practical guidance from theory; computational aspects under-discussed.
- Evaluation design issues on real data; limited ablations.

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the paper in its current form suffers from significant clarity issues and, more importantly, lacks a calibrated testing procedure with controlled Type I error. Strengthening the theoretical assumptions/presentation, adding proper calibration and Type I error results, and expanding/clarifying experiments would substantially improve the submission.
