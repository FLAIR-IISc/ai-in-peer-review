

# Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by converting it into a binary classification problem. The authors use a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) to generate samples that approximate the conditionally independent distribution f_CI, then train classifiers to distinguish between the original samples and the bootstrapped samples. The key theoretical contribution is Theorem 1, which claims the bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance (d_TV = O(1/n^(1/dz))), and Theorem 2, which provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The reduction of CI testing to binary classification is conceptually interesting, and the authors provide reasonable theoretical justification for their bootstrap procedure. The empirical results on synthetic data show that the proposed method (CCIT) outperforms KCIT and RCIT for moderate dimensions of Z (up to 70 in their experiments), which is a notable finding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical guarantees are practically useless for high-dimensional Z:** Theorem 1's bound d_TV = O(1/n^(1/dz)) is extremely weak for high-dimensional Z, which is precisely where CI testing is most needed. For example, if dz = 10 and n = 1000, the bound is O(1/10) = 0.1, which is not meaningful for practical CI testing. The paper fails to acknowledge this fundamental limitation, despite claiming "in practice we observe that the performance with respect to dimension of Z scales much better than expected from our worst case theoretical analysis."

2. **Insufficient comparison with relevant baselines:** The paper cites KCIPT [10] as a related work but fails to compare with it directly. KCIPT also uses a permutation-based approach to simulate f_CI, but the paper doesn't explain how their nearest-neighbor bootstrap differs from KCIPT's method or why their approach should outperform it. The comparison is limited to KCIT and RCIT, which are not the most relevant baselines for their approach.

3. **Lack of practical justification for classifier use:** The paper claims "any improvements in the field of binary classification imply an advancement in our CI test," but fails to provide evidence that this actually happens in practice. The authors use XGBoost as their classifier, but don't demonstrate that the method benefits from the specific strengths of modern classifiers beyond what simpler methods could achieve.

4. **Theoretical analysis has gaps:** Theorem 2 claims generalization bounds for non-i.i.d samples, but the proof relies on assumptions (bounded Lipschitz constant for f(z)) that are not justified as commonly satisfied in practice. The paper also doesn't adequately address how the non-i.i.d nature of the bootstrapped samples affects classifier performance in real-world scenarios.

5. **Empirical evaluation is limited:** The flow cytometry dataset analysis is superficial - they only evaluate one graph (Fig. 2b) and provide only ROC AUC scores (Table 2c) without detailed analysis of specific CI relations. The paper doesn't explain why the method performs well on this real-world data, especially given the high-dimensional nature of the protein signaling data.

6. **Computational inefficiency:** The method requires 3n samples (n for U1, n for U2, n for U3), while many existing methods can work with fewer samples. The nearest-neighbor search in Algorithm 1 is computationally expensive for high-dimensional Z, which the paper doesn't address.

**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting but fundamentally flawed approach to CI testing. The theoretical guarantees are not practically meaningful for the high-dimensional settings where CI testing is most valuable. The empirical results are limited and fail to demonstrate a clear advantage over the most relevant baselines. While the idea of using classifiers for CI testing is conceptually appealing, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims or justify the complexity of the proposed method over established kernel-based approaches.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more thorough comparison with KCIPT [10], which is the most similar method
2. A detailed analysis of the method's performance in high-dimensional settings (dz > 20)
3. More convincing empirical evidence showing why this approach is better than existing methods in practice
4. A clearer discussion of the theoretical limitations, particularly regarding high-dimensional Z

While the paper has some interesting ideas, it falls short of the rigorous validation required for a NIPS submission. The method appears to be an incremental improvement at best, and the theoretical guarantees are insufficient to support the claims of practical utility.

**Recommendation: Reject**
