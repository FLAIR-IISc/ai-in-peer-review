- Proposed method (CCIT): reduce conditional independence testing to binary classification via a 1-NN Z-dependent bootstrap that creates pseudo-samples by swapping Y from Z-nearest neighbors, then train a classifier on original vs pseudo and use accuracy to test H0: X ⟂ Y | Z; bias-corrected variant subtracts a (Y,Z)-only classifier.

- Theoretical result: single-sample NN-bootstrap marginal is close in TV to fCI with dTV = O(n−1/dz) under smoothness and low-density mass conditions.

- Generalization theory: derive ERM/VC-style bounds when negative samples are only near-independent due to neighbor reuse, extending classical guarantees.

- Empirical findings: using gradient-boosted trees, CCIT outperforms KCIT/RCIT on post-nonlinear synthetic data (dz up to 70) and improves ROC/AUC on flow cytometry data.

- Relation to prior work: builds on classifier two-sample tests and KNN MI estimators; simpler and cheaper alternative to KCIPT (assignment-based) and complementary to kernel-based CI tests.

- Strength — simplicity/modularity: straightforward reduction allows leveraging off-the-shelf supervised models and advances in classification.

- Strength — technical contribution: provides TV-distance control for NN-bootstrap and nontrivial generalization bounds under near-independence.

- Strength — practicality/scalability: 1-NN with standard data structures is computationally cheap; empirical robustness in moderate-high dz with strong classifiers.

- Strength — empirical evaluation: demonstrates competitive performance on both synthetic and real datasets.

- Weakness — no principled calibration: testing procedure lacks p-values or finite-sample type-I error control; τ=1/√n is ad hoc and permutation calibration is not developed.

- Weakness — curse of dimensionality in theory: TV bound scales as n−1/dz, making theoretical guarantees pessimistic and potentially vacuous in low-density/heavy-tailed regions.

- Weakness — unclear/strong assumptions: nonstandard “generalized curvature matrix,” constants, and admissible families need clearer intuition and verifiability.

- Weakness — complex dependence bounds: Theorem 2’s bound has multiple terms (e.g., n−1/3, G(ε)), lacks interpretation of dominating terms and practical regimes.

- Weakness — experimental gaps: missing sample sizes, hyperparameter tuning for baselines, runtime comparisons, classifier sensitivity analyses, and no size/power at fixed α.

- Weakness — scope limits: only continuous variables considered; sample-splitting into three equal parts may be sample-inefficient.

- Quality assessment: theoretical arguments seem careful but the testing procedure’s lack of level control is a substantive gap; empirical results are promising but need stronger calibration and baselines.

- Clarity assessment: paper is generally well-written with helpful figures/algorithms, but assumptions and generalization bounds need better motivation and more explicit experimental details.

- Originality: NN Z-conditional bootstrap reduction to classification is novel and the near-independence generalization analysis is a meaningful contribution.

- Significance: method could be practically valuable for CI testing and causal discovery if accompanied by valid calibration.

- Suggested improvements — calibration: add permutation-based calibration or regenerate U′ per permutation to obtain valid p-values and finite-sample type-I error control.

- Suggested improvements — assumptions: clarify, simplify, and give intuition/examples for key assumptions (e.g., Assumption 1).

- Suggested improvements — experiments: report empirical size/power at fixed α, runtime/stability, add ablations (different classifiers, k>1, B bootstraps), and include additional baselines (e.g., KNN-CMI).

- Pros (summary): simple, modular, theoretical guarantees, and strong empirical performance.

- Cons (summary): lacks valid level control, theory limited by dz curse and heavy assumptions, and experimental reporting incomplete.

- Recommendation: Weak accept contingent on adding valid calibration (permutation testing) and strengthening empirical evaluation (size/power and runtime analyses).
