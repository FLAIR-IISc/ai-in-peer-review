### Review of "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel method for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, termed the Classifier CI Test (CCIT). The core idea is to reduce the statistical problem of testing H0: X ⊥ Y | Z to a supervised learning problem of binary classification. The procedure involves generating a synthetic dataset designed to approximate the conditionally independent distribution f(X|Z)f(Y|Z)f(Z). This is achieved via a "nearest-neighbor bootstrap" where for each sample (x, y, z), its y-value is swapped with the y-value of its nearest neighbor in the Z-space. The original samples are labeled as class 1, and the synthetic samples are labeled as class 0. A binary classifier is then trained to distinguish between these two classes. If the classifier achieves an accuracy significantly greater than 0.5, the null hypothesis is rejected. The authors provide theoretical justification for this approach, including a proof that the bootstrapped distribution is close to the true conditionally independent distribution in total variation distance, and generalization bounds for the classifier trained on the resulting non-i.i.d. data. Empirical results on synthetic and real-world data suggest the method outperforms existing kernel-based CI tests, particularly as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The reduction of CI testing to binary classification is an elegant and creative idea. It provides a framework to leverage the impressive recent advancements in high-dimensional classification models (like gradient boosted trees and deep neural networks) for a fundamental statistical problem. This is a significant departure from traditional kernel or permutation-based methods.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling. In the synthetic data experiments, CCIT demonstrates substantially better scaling with the dimension of the conditioning set Z compared to established methods like KCIT and RCIT. This is a critical advantage, as the "curse of dimensionality" in Z is a primary challenge for most CI tests. The performance on the flow cytometry dataset further supports its practical utility.

3.  **Theoretical Support:** The authors have made a commendable effort to provide theoretical underpinnings for their method. Theorem 1, which bounds the total variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and the target CI distribution, is a key result that provides justification for the entire framework. The subsequent analysis of generalization bounds (Theorem 2) in a non-i.i.d. setting is also a non-trivial contribution.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Statistical Rigor:** My main concern lies with the statistical foundation of the proposed test. A hypothesis test requires a clear test statistic and a well-characterized null distribution to control for Type I errors. The proposed method is not a conventional statistical test in this regard.
    *   The test outcome depends on comparing a classifier's test accuracy against a threshold `τ`. The procedure for selecting `τ` to achieve a desired significance level (e.g., α = 0.05) is not rigorously established. The paper suggests `τ = 1/√n` in experiments, which is justified as an "upper-bound on the expected variance," but this is an ad-hoc choice. Established methods like KCIT provide principled ways to compute p-values, which this method lacks. Without a proper p-value calculation, it is difficult to assess the test's statistical properties or compare it fairly to other tests in terms of error control.
    *   The bias-correction version (CCITv2) is an intelligent heuristic to account for artifacts from the bootstrap procedure, but the distribution of the final statistic (`ˆL(ĝ', D'e) - ˆL(ĝ, De)`) under the null hypothesis remains unknown.

2.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** There is a significant disconnect between the provided theoretical rates and the observed empirical performance. The convergence rate of the bootstrapped distribution to the target CI distribution in Theorem 1 is `O(n^(-1/d_z))`. This rate is exponentially slow in the dimension of Z, suggesting the method should perform very poorly in the high-dimensional Z regime where it is shown to excel. The authors acknowledge this as a "worst case" analysis, but this large gap undermines the theory's explanatory power. It raises the question of whether the impressive empirical results are due to the specific (and perhaps favorable) data-generating processes used in the experiments rather than the general robustness of the method.

3.  **Dependence on Black-Box Classifiers:** While leveraging powerful classifiers is presented as a strength, it also introduces a major vulnerability. The performance of the entire CI test is now critically dependent on the choice of classifier, its architecture, and the tuning of its hyperparameters. The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the hyperparameter tuning process for the XGBoost classifier used in the experiments. This makes the results difficult to reproduce and raises concerns that the performance may be the result of extensive, data-dependent tuning, which would not be feasible for a user applying this as a general-purpose test. This dependency complicates the interpretation of the test outcome: is a rejection of H0 due to true conditional dependence, or an artifact of the classifier's inductive biases and a fortunate hyperparameter choice?

4.  **Scope of Evaluation:** The synthetic experiments are confined to a post-nonlinear noise model. It would be beneficial to evaluate the method on a wider variety of data-generating processes, including those with dependencies in higher-order moments or more complex, non-additive structures, to better understand its limitations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a highly original and promising framework for CI testing.
*   The empirical results are very strong and address a known major limitation (high-dimensional Z) of existing state-of-the-art methods.
*   The core idea could inspire a new line of research into model-powered statistical testing.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The method lacks a rigorous statistical hypothesis testing procedure for controlling Type I error, a fundamental requirement for a statistical test.
*   The theoretical guarantees are extremely weak in terms of their dependence on the dimension of Z, contradicting the method's main empirical selling point.
*   The test's performance is heavily confounded by the choice and tuning of the underlying classifier, making it less of a standalone, reliable statistical tool.

**Conclusion**

The paper presents a very interesting and creative idea that achieves impressive empirical results. However, I am skeptical of endorsing this approach in its current form due to significant concerns about its methodological and statistical rigor. The "test" is more of a discriminative heuristic whose statistical properties are not well understood. The large gap between the slow theoretical convergence rates and the strong high-dimensional performance is a major red flag that requires further investigation. Before this method can be considered a reliable replacement for established CI tests, the authors must develop a principled way to calibrate the test and compute p-values to allow for formal control of Type I error. While the engineering is clever, the statistical foundation is currently too fragile for a NIPS paper.
