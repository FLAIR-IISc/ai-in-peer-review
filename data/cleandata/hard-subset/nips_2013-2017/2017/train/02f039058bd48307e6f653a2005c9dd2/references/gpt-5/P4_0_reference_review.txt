Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a model-powered conditional independence (CI) test (CCIT) that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples of (X,Y,Z), the method splits the data, constructs a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap that replaces Y in U1 with the Y of the 1-NN in U2 in terms of Z (Algorithm 1), and uses the original samples (label 1) vs. NN-bootstrapped samples (label 0) to train a classifier to distinguish the two. If the classifier cannot distinguish them (accuracy ~ 0.5), the method fails to reject H0: X ⟂ Y | Z; otherwise it rejects. A bias-corrected variant (Algorithm 3) subtracts off a baseline classifier trained on (Y,Z) alone to correct finite-sample bias. Theoretical contributions include: (i) a bound in total variation distance between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and the CI distribution fCI; (ii) generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization under “near-independent” samples induced by the NN bootstrap; and (iii) conditions implying that classifier accuracy approaches 0.5 under H0 and is lower under H1 at a rate tied to dTV(f, fCI). Experiments on synthetic post-nonlinear models and the Sachs flow-cytometry dataset show improved ROC AUC vs. KCIT [32] and RCIT [28]; KCIPT [10] is discussed but not evaluated.

The work builds on classifier two-sample testing [19], replacing permutation with a Z-dependent NN bootstrap to emulate fCI, in contrast to KCIPT [10] which uses an optimal matching via a linear program. It also relates to nearest-neighbor-based information/entropy estimation [12,13], and to generalization bounds for non-i.i.d processes [21].

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The NN bootstrap is a simple, computationally efficient mechanism to approximate fCI by locally matching in Z. Theorem 1 provides a TV-distance bound between the bootstrapped distribution and fCI with rate O(n^{-1/dz}) up to terms depending on low-density regions G(·) and smoothness constants. The generalization analysis (Theorem 2) derives risk bounds under dependence induced by the shared U2 neighbors, leveraging a spatial near-independence argument to recover VC-type bounds with extra terms. These are plausible given the setup and align with related non-i.i.d generalization literature, though details (e.g., handling ties/multiplicity of NN matches and exact dependence control) are deferred to the appendix.
- Assumptions: Assumption 1 (a “generalized curvature matrix” for f(y|z)) is nonstandard and not intuitively motivated; practitioners may find it hard to verify. Assumption 2 on f(z) is closer to standard smoothness conditions used in kNN density/entropy analyses. The generalization bound further assumes Lipschitz f(z). The reliance on dTV is strong and the rate inherits a curse of dimensionality in dz; this is acknowledged.
- Testing procedure and calibration: The method defines a threshold τ (set as 1/√n in experiments) but does not provide a formal way to control Type I error at a user-specified α nor p-values. This is a significant limitation for a hypothesis test. A permutation or label-exchangeability argument under H0 is complicated by the near-i.i.d nature of U02, but some form of calibration (e.g., repeated re-labeling, cross-fitting, or conformal-style calibration) would strengthen the testing claims. The bias-correction via (Y,Z)-only classifier is sensible, but its statistical properties (consistency of bias removal, variance inflation) are not analyzed.
- Experiments: The synthetic experiments vary dz up to 70 for dy=dx=1 and report ROC AUC; CCIT shows strong performance vs. KCIT/RCIT. Runtime and sensitivity to classifier choice/hyperparameters are not reported. The real-data evaluation on Sachs is reasonable given the field conventions, though underlying “ground truth” uncertainty is noted. KCIPT—arguably the closest prior—was not included in the comparison; a runtime vs. accuracy trade-off would be informative. Type I error control and power at nominal α are not reported.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written with clear algorithms and motivation. Some notation is heavy and Assumption 1 needs more intuition and examples. The statements of Theorems 1–2 include multiple constants and G(·) terms; a simplified corollary highlighting the main rates would aid readability. The decision thresholds and bias term b(n) deserve a more explicit discussion, especially for practice. Minor typos and reference formatting issues are present.

Originality:
- Using a simple 1-NN Z-matching to emulate fCI is a novel and practical alternative to LP-based matching in KCIPT [10], with theoretical control in TV distance.
- Adapting classifier two-sample testing to CI via this bootstrap and analyzing generalization under near-independence is original in this context.
- Theoretical rates (O(n^{-1/dz})) are aligned with known nonparametric limits; the generalization bound under near-independence is a useful technical contribution, though related in spirit to mixing-process results [21].

Significance:
The approach is attractive because it leverages advances in supervised learning for CI testing and scales to higher-dimensional Z in practice. If coupled with proper calibration, it could become a widely used nonparametric CI test. The current lack of level control limits immediate applicability in domains requiring formal inference, but the empirical gains and simplicity are promising.

Pros:
- Simple, modular method reducing CI testing to classification with an efficient NN bootstrap.
- Theoretical guarantees: TV distance to fCI and generalization bounds under near-independence.
- Strong empirical performance, especially as dz increases.
- Public code and practical classifier choices (e.g., XGBoost).

Cons:
- No formal control of Type I error or p-values; threshold selection is heuristic.
- Strong and somewhat opaque smoothness assumptions (Assumption 1); practical verifiability unclear.
- Curse of dimensionality in dz in the main bound; limited guidance on when theory predicts success.
- Missing empirical comparison to KCIPT [10]; limited runtime/sensitivity analyses.
- Scope of experiments limited to dx=dy=1; no study of dependence on dx, dy or k-NN variants.

Suggestions and questions:
- Provide a calibration strategy for significance: e.g., label permutations within folds under H0 using additional sample-splitting to ensure exchangeability; or a wild bootstrap leveraging Z-locality; report empirical Type I error at nominal α.
- Explore k-NN variants (k>1) and their effect on the TV bound and empirical power/robustness.
- Include KCIPT [10] in experiments or justify omission; add runtime comparisons and sensitivity to classifier/hyperparameters.
- Clarify Assumption 1 with intuition, examples, and relationship to Fisher information or Lipschitz/smoothness of log f(y|z).
- Analyze dx, dy effects and provide guidance on sample sizes needed as functions of (dx,dy,dz).
- Consider cross-fitting to reduce dependence between training and test labels and tighten bias control.

Overall, this is a technically interesting and practically promising contribution. Strengthening the inferential calibration and clarifying assumptions would significantly increase its impact.
