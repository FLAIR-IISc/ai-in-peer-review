

# NeurIPS 2017 Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to conditional independence (CI) testing by reducing the problem to binary classification. The key insight is to generate samples from the conditionally independent distribution f_CI using a nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1), then train a classifier to distinguish between the original samples (labeled 1) and the bootstrapped samples (labeled 0). If the classifier achieves significantly better than random accuracy, the null hypothesis of conditional independence (H0) is rejected.

The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing that the bootstrapped samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance (Theorem 1), and they derive generalization bounds for the classification problem under near-independent samples (Theorem 2). Empirical results on synthetic data and the flow-cytometry dataset demonstrate that their method outperforms state-of-the-art approaches (KCIT, RCIT) in terms of ROC AUC, particularly when the dimension of the conditioning variable Z increases.

## Quality

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contributions:** The paper makes a non-trivial theoretical contribution by providing a bound on the total variation distance between the bootstrapped samples and f_CI (Theorem 1), and by extending generalization bounds to the non-i.i.d setting (Theorem 2). The analysis of Rademacher complexity under near-independence is of independent interest.

2. **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results on synthetic data with increasing dimensionality of Z demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed method over existing approaches. The flow-cytometry dataset results further validate the practical utility of the method.

3. **Practical Implementation:** The method leverages modern classification algorithms (like XGBoost), making it immediately applicable to real-world problems without requiring specialized implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Bound Analysis:** The bound in Theorem 1 is complex and contains terms like G(2c_dz ε^2) without clear explanation of their meaning or behavior. The bound d_TV(, f_CI) = O(1/n^(1/dz)) suggests a poor scaling with dimension, but the empirical results show better performance than expected. The paper doesn't adequately explain this discrepancy. The term exp(-1/(n^d_z c_dz ε^(d_z+2))) in the bound is particularly concerning as it suggests the bound becomes looser as n increases for fixed ε.

2. **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't provide any analysis of the computational complexity of the nearest-neighbor bootstrap step. For large n and high-dimensional Z, this step could become prohibitively expensive (O(n^2 d_z) for brute-force nearest-neighbor search).

3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The empirical results don't include statistical significance testing for the observed performance differences, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements over KCIT and RCIT are meaningful.

4. **Dimensionality Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis assumes a smoothness condition on the conditional density f(y|z), but doesn't discuss how this assumption holds in practice or how violations might affect performance.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and organized, but suffers from several clarity issues:

1. **Notation Inconsistency:** The paper uses inconsistent notation for conditional densities (e.g., f(y|z) vs. f_{Y|Z}(y|z)), which can be confusing for readers.

2. **Algorithm Explanation:** Algorithm 1 (nearest-neighbor bootstrap) is somewhat unclear. It's not explained why 1-NN in Z coordinates specifically is sufficient to generate samples close to f_CI, or why the Y-coordinates are swapped with the nearest neighbor's Y-coordinates.

3. **Bias Correction Explanation:** Algorithm 3's bias correction mechanism (using classifiers trained on different feature subsets) is not well-explained. The paper claims "the classifier is expected to distinguish between the two data-sets only based on the Y, Z coordinates," but doesn't provide a rigorous justification for this claim.

4. **Theoretical Results:** The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are quite technical, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient intuition for why the bounds hold. A more intuitive explanation would greatly improve the paper.

## Originality

The paper's main contribution is the reduction of CI testing to binary classification with the use of a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to generate samples from f_CI. This is a novel approach compared to existing methods.

However, the paper's claim of novelty is somewhat overstated:

1. The reduction to binary classification for independence testing was previously explored in [19], and the paper doesn't sufficiently explain how the conditional independence case differs from the independence case.

2. The use of nearest-neighbor techniques for generating samples from a different distribution has been explored in other contexts (e.g., in density estimation or generative modeling). The paper doesn't adequately compare to these related approaches.

3. The paper cites KCIPT [10] as the most relevant work, noting that KCIPT uses a permutation to simulate f_CI but requires solving a linear program. However, it doesn't thoroughly discuss how their nearest-neighbor bootstrap differs from the permutation approach in terms of both theoretical guarantees and practical implementation.

While the specific combination of nearest-neighbor bootstrap with classification for CI testing is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently establish how this differs from previous work in the field.

## Significance

The problem of CI testing is highly significant for causal inference, Bayesian network learning, and feature selection. The paper's claim that their method "outperforms the state of the art" is potentially significant if true.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by:

1. **Dimensionality Scaling:** The theoretical bound suggests poor scaling with dimension (O(1/n^(1/dz))), which is a well-known curse of dimensionality in nearest-neighbor methods. While the empirical results show better scaling than expected, this remains a fundamental limitation.

2. **Limited Real-World Applications:** The paper only evaluates on one real-world dataset (flow-cytometry), which is relatively small (11 variables). It would be more convincing to demonstrate performance on larger, more diverse datasets.

3. **Lack of Comparison to Other Model-Based Approaches:** The paper doesn't compare to other model-based CI testing approaches like RCIT [28] or KCIPT [10] beyond mentioning them in the related work.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting approach to CI testing by reducing it to binary classification with a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are promising, but the paper has several important weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be accepted.

The most significant issues are the theoretical bound's questionable behavior with respect to dimensionality, the lack of computational complexity analysis, and the insufficient explanation of the bias correction mechanism. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the method, particularly regarding high-dimensional settings.

## Recommendations

**Pro Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel reduction of CI testing to binary classification
- Theoretical guarantees on bootstrapped samples
- Strong empirical results showing advantage over state-of-the-art methods for increasing dimensionality of Z
- Practical implementation leveraging modern classification algorithms

**Con Arguments for Rejection:**
- Theoretical bound has questionable behavior with respect to dimensionality
- No analysis of computational complexity
- Lack of statistical significance testing for empirical results
- Insufficient explanation of bias correction mechanism
- Overstated claims of novelty compared to related work

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection with the following suggestions for revision:

1. Provide a more thorough analysis of the theoretical bound in Theorem 1, particularly explaining the discrepancy between the theoretical scaling and empirical results.
2. Include a detailed computational complexity analysis for the nearest-neighbor bootstrap step.
3. Add statistical significance testing for the empirical results.
4. Clarify the bias correction mechanism in Algorithm 3 with a more rigorous justification.
5. Expand the discussion of related work to better distinguish the paper from previous model-based approaches to CI testing.

Without these revisions, the paper's theoretical claims and empirical results are difficult to fully evaluate and trust. The paper has potential but requires significant strengthening before it can be considered for publication at NeurIPS.
