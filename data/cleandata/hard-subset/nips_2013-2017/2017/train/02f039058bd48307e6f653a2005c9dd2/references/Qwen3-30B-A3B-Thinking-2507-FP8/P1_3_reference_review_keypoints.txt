- Proposes a model-powered CI test that reduces CI testing to binary classification via a nearest-neighbor bootstrap to approximate the conditionally independent distribution.

- Provides theoretical guarantee (Theorem 1) that bootstrap samples are close to f_CI in total variation distance.

- Extends learning theory (Theorem 2) to give generalization bounds for classification under near-independent (non-i.i.d.) samples.

- Empirical results: outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic data, maintaining accuracy up to 70 dimensions where competitors degrade.

- Real-world validation: superior performance on flow cytometry causal graphs across multiple settings.

- Method is computationally efficient in practice due to nearest-neighbor bootstrap and scales better than worst-case theory suggests.

- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides helpful figures, pseudocode, and precise theoretical statements.

- Weakness: justification for bias correction (using only Y,Z for ĝ₀) is superficial and needs more rigorous explanation or empirical evidence.

- Weakness: lacks comparison to several recent CI testing methods (post-2017), which would better situate contributions in current literature.

- Weakness: no explicit computational complexity analysis or runtime comparisons as dimensionality increases.

- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, empirically convincing, and suitable for acceptance at NIPS.

- Pro-accept points summarized: novel conversion to classification, strong theory, comprehensive experiments, clear presentation, practical significance.

- Con-accept points summarized: strengthen bias-correction justification, compare to newer CI methods, add computational complexity/runtime analysis.
