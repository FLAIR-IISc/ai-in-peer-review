- Novel framing: Reduces conditional independence (CI) testing to a binary classification problem using nearest-neighbor bootstrapping to approximate the conditionally independent distribution.  
- Theoretical guarantees: Theorem 1 bounds the total variation distance between bootstrap samples and the fCI distribution; Theorem 2 gives generalization bounds linking classifier performance to the CI test.  
- Empirical performance: Outperforms KCIT and RCIT, especially as dimensionality of Z increases; demonstrated on a flow cytometry dataset.  
- Flexibility: Method can leverage different classifiers depending on domain knowledge and compute resources.  
- Efficiency: Nearest-neighbor bootstrap is efficient in practice due to fast nearest-neighbor search algorithms.  
- Presentation clarity: Paper uses dense notation and sometimes fails to clearly connect theoretical results with the practical algorithm; Algorithm 3’s motivation is buried.  
- Theoretical complexity vs. practicality: Generalization bounds are mathematically complex and provide limited practical guidance for parameter tuning.  
- Threshold selection: The chosen threshold (τ = 1/(p n)) appears arbitrary and needs a more principled justification.  
- Limited baselines: Comparisons are limited to KCIT/RCIT; more recent CI methods and detailed runtime/cost analyses are missing.  
- Bootstrap variance: Paper does not discuss variability across bootstrap runs or how that affects test power and reliability.  
- Algorithm 3 question: Unclear why subtracting bias from a Y|Z-only classifier specifically addresses non-independence.  
- Smoothness assumptions: Assumptions in Theorem 1 may be restrictive; sensitivity to violations and applicable data types are not discussed.  
- "Near-independence" unclear: The term used in Theorem 2 is not well-defined and its role in the bounds is ambiguous.  
- Synthetic experiments: Experimental design lacks detail (e.g., ranges for parameters 'a' and 'b') and robustness checks.  
- Flow cytometry claims: Statements about algorithmic “confidence” need quantification (p-values, confidence intervals).  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—improve clarity, streamline/move complex theory to appendix, provide principled threshold selection, and address the detailed questions above.
