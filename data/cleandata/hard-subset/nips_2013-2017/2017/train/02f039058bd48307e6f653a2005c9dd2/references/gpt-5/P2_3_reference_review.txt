Summary
The paper proposes a “model-powered” conditional independence (CI) test (CCIT) that reduces CI testing to a two-sample classification problem via a nearest-neighbor (NN) bootstrap. Given i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method: (i) uses a 1-NN match in Z across two data splits to swap Y-values and generate samples approximately from fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z(x|z)fY|Z(y|z)fZ(z); (ii) labels original samples as class 1 and NN-bootstrapped samples as class 0; (iii) trains a binary classifier to distinguish them. High accuracy implies fX,Y,Z differs from fCI, prompting rejection of H0: X ⫫ Y | Z. A bias-corrected variant compares a classifier using (X,Y,Z) with one using only (Y,Z) to subtract the residual distinguishability introduced by the bootstrap under H0. Theoretically, the authors bound the total variation (TV) distance between the NN-bootstrapped distribution and fCI at O(n^{-1/dz}) under smoothness assumptions, and provide generalization bounds for ERM under “near-independent” samples. Empirically, CCIT reportedly outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear data (scaling to dz≈70) and on the Sachs flow-cytometry dataset across three graph assumptions.

Relation to prior work
- Closely related to classifier two-sample tests (C2ST) and the use of classifiers for independence testing (Lopez-Paz & Oquab ’16). The key difference is a Z-dependent resampling to emulate conditional factorization versus global permutation for marginal independence.
- Compared to kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT) and KCIPT (Doran et al., permutation + LP), the contribution is a simple NN-based conditional bootstrap with TV guarantees, coupled with flexible classifiers instead of fixed kernels.
- Also related to nearest-neighbor–based entropy/MI estimators, though here NN matching is used to construct a surrogate null distribution rather than estimate information directly.

Quality
- Theoretical analysis: The central TV bound (Theorem 1) is a meaningful step: it formalizes that the 1-NN swap in Z produces samples close to fCI, with an explicit (though dimension-dependent) rate. The generalization analysis (Theorem 2) under near-independence extends standard VC/Rademacher arguments to the dependent construction and is of independent interest.
- Assumptions: Smoothness/curvature assumptions on f(y|z) and Hessian/Lipschitz conditions on f(z) are nontrivial and somewhat uncommon; they should be better motivated. The rate depends exponentially on dz via the curse of dimensionality (n^{-1/dz}), which is expected for NN methods but limits worst-case power in high-dimensional Z.
- Testing calibration: While the reduction to classification is sound in principle, the paper does not provide a finite-sample type-I error control procedure. The threshold τ is chosen heuristically (∼1/√n), and the bias-correction (difference of losses with and without X) is intuitive but lacks a formal size guarantee. The theory explains closeness of distributions and classifier risk but stops short of a complete test with controlled size and power against broad alternatives.
- Experiments: Synthetic studies are reasonable and show strong ROC AUC relative to KCIT/RCIT. The real-data evaluation is informative but uses approximate ground truth and ROC rather than size/power at a fixed level. Practical guidance on hyperparameters (k in k-NN, classifier class G, τ, number of bootstraps B) is limited.

Clarity
- The high-level idea and algorithms are well communicated and easy to follow. Pseudocode helps.
- Some notation and assumptions (e.g., generalized curvature matrix Ia(z)) are dense and could be clarified with intuition and examples. The dependence of constants and the roles of dx, dy are not fully discussed.
- The test decision rule and thresholding strategy merit a clearer, principled description (e.g., how to obtain p-values).

Originality
- The reduction of CI testing to two-sample classification via a simple NN-based conditional bootstrap and the accompanying TV and generalization bounds are novel in this specific combination. Prior work either used kernels or more complex permutations/optimizations; bringing modern classifiers into CI testing with theoretical support is a fresh, useful angle.
- The bias-correction via comparing (X,Y,Z) vs (Y,Z) classifiers is simple and clever, though not fully formalized statistically.

Significance
- CI testing is central to causal discovery and graphical models. A method that flexibly leverages strong classifiers is practically compelling and could be broadly adopted, especially given the provided code.
- The theoretical bounds increase confidence in the surrogate data generation and the learning-theoretic step. Even with a pessimistic dz-dependence, empirical performance suggests utility in moderate to high dimensions in practice.
- Lack of formal calibration may limit adoption in statistical testing settings where level control is required, but the approach is promising and likely to stimulate follow-on work.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, modular reduction to classification; can plug in any strong classifier.
- Novel NN conditional bootstrap with TV guarantees.
- Generalization bounds under near-independence; technically interesting.
- Strong empirical results, especially scaling in dz, and real-data application; open-source implementation.

Weaknesses and concerns
- No finite-sample size control or p-values; τ is heuristic, and bias correction lacks a formal guarantee.
- Strong smoothness assumptions and poor worst-case rate n^{-1/dz}; limited discussion of when assumptions hold.
- Dependence induced by 1-NN bootstrap; limited analysis of k>1, metric choice, and practical sensitivity.
- Evaluation based on ROC rather than size/power at fixed α; limited baselines (e.g., regression-based residual tests).
- Data inefficiency from multiple splits (3n samples) and training/test split; impact on power not quantified.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you propose a permutation/bootstrap procedure to empirically calibrate the null distribution of the accuracy gap (XYZ vs YZ) to obtain p-values and control type-I error?
- What is the effect of using k-NN (k>1) or distance-weighted matching in Z? Any empirical sensitivity analyses?
- Guidance on choosing τ, classifier class G, and B; ablations across different classifiers and hyperparameters would help.
- Report size/power at fixed α, and computational time comparisons to KCIT/RCIT.
- Discuss robustness to high-dimensional Z beyond the synthetic manifold setting; any theory under manifold or margin conditions that mitigates the n^{-1/dz} rate?

Overall recommendation
- Leaning to accept (weak accept). The paper presents a simple and potentially impactful approach with nontrivial theory and strong empirical performance. The main limitation is the lack of a fully calibrated hypothesis test. With added discussion and, if possible, a practical calibration procedure, this would be a strong contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Moderate.
