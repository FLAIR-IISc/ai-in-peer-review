### Summary

This paper introduces a novel method for non-parametric conditional independence (CI) testing, termed CCIT (Classifier CI Test). The core idea is to reduce the statistical hypothesis testing problem of `X ⟂ Y | Z` to a binary classification task. The procedure involves generating a "null" dataset that mimics the conditionally independent distribution `f(x|z)f(y|z)f(z)` using a novel nearest-neighbor bootstrap technique. These generated samples are labeled as class 0, while original samples from the true joint distribution `f(x,y,z)` are labeled as class 1. A powerful binary classifier (e.g., gradient boosted trees) is then trained to distinguish between these two sets of samples. The test accuracy of this classifier serves as the test statistic: an accuracy significantly greater than 0.5 suggests that the original and bootstrapped distributions are distinguishable, leading to the rejection of the null hypothesis of conditional independence. The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach, including a proof that the bootstrapped distribution is close to the target conditionally independent distribution in total variation distance, and generalization bounds for the classifier trained on the resulting non-i.i.d. data. Empirically, the proposed method is shown to outperform established kernel-based CI tests (KCIT, RCIT), particularly as the dimensionality of the conditioning variable Z increases.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of the Reduction:** The central contribution of reducing CI testing to binary classification is both creative and compelling. This "model-powered" philosophy allows the difficult problem of non-parametric density estimation to be implicitly handled by powerful, off-the-shelf supervised learning models. This is an elegant way to leverage the remarkable progress in classification methods for a fundamental statistical problem.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are impressive. In synthetic experiments, CCIT demonstrates a clear advantage over established kernel-based methods, especially in the challenging high-dimensional regime where the dimension of Z is large. This suggests the method has significant practical potential in areas like causal discovery where high-dimensional conditioning sets are common. The application to the flow cytometry dataset further supports its utility on real-world problems.

3.  **Theoretical Grounding:** The authors provide non-trivial theoretical analysis to support their method. Theorem 1, which bounds the total variation distance between the bootstrapped distribution and the true conditionally independent distribution, provides a formal justification for the core data generation step. Furthermore, Theorem 2 provides a novel analysis of Rademacher complexity for the non-i.i.d. samples produced by the bootstrap, connecting the classifier's performance back to the statistical properties of the original distribution. This adds a layer of rigor that is often missing in purely heuristic approaches.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Questionable Soundness of the Bootstrap Procedure:** The entire method hinges on the nearest-neighbor bootstrap (Algorithm 1) successfully generating samples from the conditionally independent distribution `f_CI`. The theoretical guarantee provided in Theorem 1 shows a convergence rate of `O(n^(-1/d_z))`, where `d_z` is the dimension of Z. This rate is extremely slow and suffers severely from the curse of dimensionality. For even moderate `d_z` (e.g., `d_z` > 5), this bound suggests that an astronomical number of samples would be needed for the bootstrapped distribution to be close to `f_CI`. This theoretical result is in stark contrast to the excellent empirical performance reported for `d_z` up to 70. This significant gap between the pessimistic theory and optimistic results is a major concern and is not adequately addressed. It raises questions about whether the method's success is due to the bootstrap procedure itself or is an artifact of the specific power of the chosen classifier on the test data.

2.  **Lack of a Rigorous Statistical Test Framework:** The paper transforms a hypothesis test into a classification problem, but it fails to fully translate the outcome back into a rigorous statistical framework.
    *   **Undefined Null Distribution:** The test statistic is the classifier's test accuracy. The distribution of this statistic under the null hypothesis is unknown and not derived. Without a null distribution, it is impossible to formally control the Type I error rate or calculate a p-value.
    *   **Heuristic Thresholding:** The decision rule relies on a threshold `τ`. The paper provides a heuristic justification for setting `τ = 1/√n`, but this is not derived from the properties of the test statistic. Established methods like KCIT provide procedures for approximating the null distribution (e.g., via permutation or gamma approximation), allowing for principled p-value computation. The proposed method lacks this statistical rigor, making it feel more like a well-performing heuristic than a formal statistical test.

3.  **Introduction of Nuisance Hyperparameters:** By relying on a complex classifier like XGBoost, the method introduces a large number of "researcher degrees of freedom". The performance of the CI test is now critically dependent on the choice of the classifier and the tuning of its many hyperparameters (e.g., tree depth, learning rate, regularization). This makes the method difficult to standardize and poses a risk to reproducibility. An under-tuned classifier might fail to reject `H0` when it is false (low power), while an over-tuned one might find spurious differences, potentially inflating Type I error. The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the test to these choices. This contrasts poorly with established methods where the choice of a kernel and its bandwidth, while important, represents a far more constrained and better-understood hyperparameter space.

4.  **Limited Scope of Baselines:** The comparison is primarily against kernel-based methods. The paper mentions KCIPT [10], which also uses a permutation-based strategy to generate null samples, but dismisses it as computationally expensive without an empirical comparison. Given the conceptual similarity, a direct comparison with KCIPT would have been highly relevant and would have strengthened the evaluation of the proposed bootstrap method.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

The paper presents an innovative and potentially powerful approach to conditional independence testing. The idea of leveraging modern classification machinery is highly appealing, and the empirical results are very promising. However, the work in its current form has significant weaknesses that temper my enthusiasm. The methodological foundation—the nearest-neighbor bootstrap—has theoretical guarantees that suggest it should fail in the very high-dimensional settings where it is empirically shown to excel. This disconnect is concerning. More importantly, the method is not presented as a complete, rigorous statistical test; the lack of a defined null distribution and the reliance on heuristic thresholding make it difficult to assess and control its statistical properties, such as the Type I error rate. While the approach is clever, I am skeptical of endorsing a method that replaces a well-defined statistical problem with a complex machine learning pipeline without providing a clear and rigorous framework for interpreting its output as a formal hypothesis test. Therefore, despite the strong empirical results, I lean towards rejecting the paper in its current form. I would be willing to reconsider if the authors could (a) better reconcile the theory and practice of their bootstrap method and (b) provide a more principled way to calibrate the test to control Type I error, for instance, by developing a procedure to estimate the null distribution of the classifier's accuracy.
