

# Peer Review: "Model-Powered Conditional Independence Test"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel model-powered approach to Conditional Independence (CI) testing by reducing it to a binary classification problem. The key insight is to use a nearest-neighbor bootstrap procedure (Algorithm 1) to generate samples close to the conditionally independent distribution fCI(x,y,z) = fX|Z(x|z)fY|Z(y|z)fZ(z). The authors then train a classifier to distinguish between original samples (labeled 1) and the bootstrapped samples (labeled 0), with good classification performance indicating rejection of the null hypothesis H0: X ⊥ Y | Z.

The paper makes several theoretical contributions: (1) Theorem 1 proves that the generated samples are close to fCI in total variation distance with a bound of O(1/n^{1/dz}), (2) Theorem 2 provides generalization bounds for classification under near-independent samples (not exactly i.i.d.). Empirically, the authors demonstrate their method outperforms state-of-the-art kernel-based approaches on synthetic data and a flow-cytometry dataset.

## Strengths

1. **Practical Contribution**: The paper addresses an important problem in causal inference and graphical models with a practical, implementable approach that leverages modern machine learning tools (gradient boosted trees, neural networks).

2. **Novel Bootstrap Method**: The nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a simple yet effective way to generate samples from fCI, which is a significant contribution. It avoids the computationally expensive permutation approach in KCIPT [10] while providing theoretical guarantees.

3. **Theoretical Guarantees**: The authors provide meaningful theoretical analysis of their approach, particularly the connection between the total variation distance and classification error (Theorem 2). The analysis of generalization under non-i.i.d. samples is particularly valuable.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on synthetic data and the flow-cytometry dataset are well-designed and demonstrate clear advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches.

5. **Clear Motivation**: The paper effectively explains the motivation for model-powered approaches in statistical testing, highlighting the advantages of leveraging advances in supervised learning.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Complexity**: The bound in Theorem 1 is extremely complex with multiple terms (c3, G(ε), etc.) that aren't well explained in the main text. The bound is O(1/n^{1/dz}), but the paper doesn't provide clear intuition about how this bound behaves for realistic values of n and dz. The practical implications of the bound are not sufficiently discussed.

2. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison**: The paper mentions KCIPT [10] as a relevant work but doesn't include it in the empirical evaluation. Since KCIPT is a direct competitor that also uses a permutation approach to simulate fCI, this omission is significant. The comparison to KCIT and RCIT is insufficient without KCIPT.

3. **Ad-hoc Bias Correction**: The bias correction in Algorithm 3 is not well justified. The paper states that "the optimal classifier may be able to achieve a loss slightly less than 0.5 in the case of finite n, even when H0 is true" but doesn't provide a principled way to estimate or correct for this bias.

4. **Limited Discussion of Assumptions**: The smoothness assumptions (Assumptions 1 and 2) are critical to the theoretical results, but the paper doesn't discuss how restrictive these assumptions are in practice. For example, how common is it for real-world data to satisfy the generalized curvature matrix condition?

5. **Theoretical-Practical Disconnect**: The paper claims "the performance with respect to dimension of Z scales much better than expected from our worst case theoretical analysis" but doesn't provide a clear explanation for this discrepancy. This is a significant gap between theory and practice.

## Technical Concerns

1. **Ambiguous Terminology**: The paper uses "generalized curvature matrix" (Assumption 1) without clear definition or reference to standard statistical literature. This makes it difficult to assess the reasonableness of the assumption.

2. **Theorem 1's Practical Relevance**: The bound in Theorem 1 includes terms like exp(-1/(n^{dz}c_{dz}ε^{dz+2})) which decay extremely rapidly for small ε and moderate dz. This suggests the bound may be vacuous for practical purposes, but the paper doesn't address this.

3. **Misleading Claim in Section 1.1**: The paper claims "our bootstrap is also efficient owing to fast algorithms for identifying nearest-neighbors [24]." However, Algorithm 1 requires a nearest-neighbor search for each point in U1 with respect to U2, which is O(n^2) for n points without specialized data structures. The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the bootstrap procedure.

4. **Lack of Sensitivity Analysis**: The paper doesn't analyze how sensitive their method is to the choice of classifier (e.g., XGBoost vs. neural networks) or to the threshold τ used in Algorithm 3.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper's related work section adequately covers key approaches to CI testing, including kernel-based methods (KCIT, RCIT) and KCIPT. However, the paper doesn't fully articulate how their approach differs from KCIPT in a way that justifies the performance improvements. KCIPT uses a linear program to compute an optimal permutation, while the paper uses a simple nearest-neighbor approach. The paper claims that the nearest-neighbor approach is simpler and provides theoretical guarantees, but doesn't demonstrate this in practice with a direct comparison to KCIPT.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Simplify and Clarify Theoretical Results**: The bound in Theorem 1 should be simplified and better explained. The paper should provide more intuition about how the bound behaves for realistic values of n and dz.

2. **Include KCIPT in Empirical Evaluation**: The paper should compare against KCIPT to provide a more complete picture of the relative merits of their approach.

3. **Justify Bias Correction**: The bias correction in Algorithm 3 needs a more principled justification or alternative approach.

4. **Expand Discussion of Assumptions**: The paper should discuss how restrictive the smoothness assumptions are in practice, perhaps with examples from the flow-cytometry dataset.

5. **Address Theoretical-Practical Disconnect**: The paper should provide a more thorough explanation for why the method performs better than theoretically expected with respect to dimension.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of conditional independence testing. The model-powered approach is conceptually sound and the empirical results are promising. The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights, though it could be made more accessible and practical.

**Quality**: 7/10 - The paper is technically sound but the theoretical analysis could be more accessible and the practical implications of the bounds need clarification.

**Clarity**: 8/10 - Well-written, but some technical sections could be clearer, particularly the theoretical results.

**Originality**: 7/10 - The core idea of reducing CI testing to classification is not entirely novel (citing [19]), but the nearest-neighbor bootstrap is a novel contribution.

**Significance**: 8/10 - The paper addresses an important problem with practical implications for causal inference and graphical models. The approach has clear potential for wider adoption.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, but needs to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly the incomplete theoretical explanation and the lack of comparison with KCIPT. The authors should clarify the theoretical bounds, provide more intuition about their practical implications, and include KCIPT in their empirical evaluation.

The paper is in scope for NeurIPS as it combines statistical theory with practical machine learning approaches to solve an important problem in causal inference. The work is relevant to the broader NeurIPS community interested in statistical learning and causal discovery.
