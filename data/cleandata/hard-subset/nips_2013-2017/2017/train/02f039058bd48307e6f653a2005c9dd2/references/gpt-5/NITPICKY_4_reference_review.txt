Summary
The paper proposes a “model-powered” conditional independence (CI) test (CCIT) that reduces CI testing to binary classification. Given 3n i.i.d. samples from fX,Y,Z, the method: (i) uses a nearest-neighbor bootstrap in Z to construct a synthetic dataset that approximates fCI(x,y,z)=fX|Z(x|z) fY|Z(y|z) fZ(z); (ii) labels original samples as class 1 and bootstrapped samples as class 0; (iii) trains a classifier (e.g., XGBoost) and rejects H0: X ⟂ Y | Z when the classifier separates the two sets. Theoretically, the authors bound the TV distance between the bootstrap and fCI under smoothness conditions, and derive generalization bounds for classification with “near-i.i.d.” samples produced by the bootstrap. Empirically, CCIT outperforms KCIT and RCIT on synthetic post-nonlinear models (with large dz) and on flow cytometry data; code is provided.

Relation to prior work: The use of classifiers for two-sample and independence testing is well known (e.g., Lopez-Paz & Oquab). For CI, permutation-based emulation of fCI has been explored (Doran et al.), but requires solving an LP; nearest-neighbor resampling is a computationally simpler surrogate. The paper also connects to kNN density/entropy estimation analyses.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and modular reduction that can leverage strong off-the-shelf classifiers.
- Computationally attractive compared to LP-based conditional permutations; uses standard NN search.
- Nontrivial theory: (a) a bound on dTV between the bootstrap distribution and fCI that depends on dz and low-density mass; (b) a generalization analysis under near-independence (non-i.i.d.) sampling.
- Strong empirical AUCs in synthetic experiments with large dz; competitive performance on a real dataset.
- Open-source implementation.

Weaknesses (technical and methodological)
- Lack of a calibrated statistical test: The method is evaluated via ROC AUC, but the paper does not provide a valid level-α test with guaranteed Type I error. The chosen threshold τ=1/√n is heuristic; no finite-sample or asymptotic size control is established. Without a p-value or critical value construction under H0, the procedure is not yet a principled CI test.
- Theorem statements and notation are hard to parse and contain errors/ambiguities. In Theorem 1, b(n) is undefined in the main text, the bound is written inconsistently (“b(n), 1/2 sqrt{…}”), and constants/terms (c3, c_dz, d, G(·)) are insufficiently explained and mixed with formatting issues. Assumption 1’s “generalized curvature matrix” I_a(z) is unusual and its role is not clearly justified.
- Dependence on dz is pessimistic (O(n^(-1/dz))): the core guarantee degrades sharply with the conditioning dimension. The paper’s main empirical claim (“scales much better than theory”) is not investigated via ablations (e.g., varying k in kNN, different classifiers, sensitivity to Z’s intrinsic dimension or manifold structure).
- Near-i.i.d. generalization results are weakly connected to practice. The VC-dimension–based bounds do not reflect modern boosted trees or deep networks used in experiments; the resulting guarantees may be vacuous. The “spatial near-independence” argument is only sketched; formal dependence coefficients are not provided in the main text.
- Bias correction (Algorithm 3) is ad hoc. Using a second classifier on (Y,Z) to estimate bias introduces additional variance and model mismatch. No analysis shows that L(g,De) − L(g0,D0e) is centered at 0 under H0 with controlled dispersion. No ablation on its necessity/effect is provided.
- Experimental evaluation lacks critical details: No runtime comparisons; no report on classifier hyperparameters, tuning, or model capacity; no sensitivity to B (number of bootstraps) or to the choice of 1-NN versus k-NN; no calibration curves or Type I error rates under H0; fairness of baselines (KCIT/RCIT bandwidths and settings) is unclear.
- Real-data construction of CI/non-CI pairs is weakly justified. The assumption that edges imply non-CI “given any conditioning set” is not carefully argued with respect to d-separation; random conditioning sets of size 3 may occasionally block paths or open colliders, potentially mislabeling ground truth. This undermines conclusions from ROC on the real dataset.

Clarity and presentation issues
- Numerous typos, formatting and notation inconsistencies hinder readability: missing math symbols (⊥ replaced by ?), inconsistent subscripts (dz vs d z), “psuedo-code,” “upto,” undefined symbols, broken equations, and incomplete theorem statements. Several references are incomplete (missing venues/years/pages) and some lines (“st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems…”) look corrupted.
- The paper promises details “in Appendix,” but as submitted, crucial definitions/lemmas are absent, and proofs are not summarized sufficiently for an 8-page venue.

Significance and originality
- The idea of Z-conditional nearest-neighbor resampling to approximate fCI is sensible and likely useful; reducing CI testing to classification remains appealing. However, without a calibrated testing procedure and clearer theory, the contribution is not yet a reliable CI test practitioners can use, and the novelty over prior permutation/OT-based approaches is incremental rather than transformative.

Questions for the authors
- How to obtain a valid level-α test? Can you propose a sample-splitting scheme with a conditional permutation/NN bootstrap to estimate the null distribution of the accuracy (or a likelihood ratio proxy) and derive p-values?
- Why 1-NN instead of k-NN or kernel resampling? Any analysis for k > 1 or adaptive k?
- How sensitive is performance to classifier choice and capacity? Please provide hyperparameters, tuning protocol, and ablations.
- Can you report Type I error and power curves instead of only ROC AUC?
- Can you supply runtime and memory benchmarks versus KCIT/RCIT and KCIPT?

Pros
- Simple, modular idea with practical potential.
- Nontrivial theoretical effort; open-source code.
- Promising empirical AUCs on synthetic and one real dataset.

Cons
- No calibrated test or Type I error control.
- Theoretical results are hard to follow, with unclear assumptions and dz-curse.
- Ad hoc bias correction; lack of ablations and fairness/runtime details.
- Significant clarity/formatting/reference issues.

Recommendation
Reject. While the core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, the paper in its current form has substantial methodological and presentation shortcomings. I encourage the authors to (i) provide a level-α test with provable size, (ii) clean up and clarify the theory and assumptions, (iii) add ablations, sensitivity and runtime studies, and (iv) thoroughly revise writing/notation and references. With these improvements, the work could be much stronger in a future submission.
