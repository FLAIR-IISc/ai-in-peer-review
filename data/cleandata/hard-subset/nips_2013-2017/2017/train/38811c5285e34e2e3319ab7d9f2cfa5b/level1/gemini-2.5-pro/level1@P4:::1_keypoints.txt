- Introduces the "Radon machine": partition data, train base learners in parallel, and hierarchically aggregate hypotheses using Radon points.  
- Aggregation tree uses Radon number r (r = d+2 in d-dimensional Euclidean space) to compute Radon points at internal nodes.  
- Main theoretical result: failure probability improves doubly-exponentially with tree height; ε-bad probability bounded by (rδ)^(2^h).  
- Shows a broad class of regularised risk minimisers can be parallelised to polylogarithmic time given quasi-polynomial processors and larger sample sizes.  
- Contrasts with parameter averaging and distributed SGD, claiming stronger guarantees and faster asymptotic runtime.  
- Empirical evaluation shows large speedups (80–700x) and competitive or superior predictive performance versus Spark MLlib.  
- Technical soundness: proofs are rigorous (Theorem 3) and complexity analysis correctly derives the claimed runtime under assumptions.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized; algorithms and theoretical development are presented clearly.  
- Suggestion for clarity: discuss practical implications of the δ ≤ 1/(2r) assumption and how required subset sizes scale with dimension d.  
- Originality: novel use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation, connecting convex geometry with parallel learning theory.  
- Theoretical significance: advances a long-standing open problem on parallel complexity of learning, extending NC-style results to regularised risk minimisers.  
- Practical significance: black-box applicability to many learners and strong empirical speedups make the method practically appealing.  
- Major limitation: exponential dependence on feature dimension d (r^h = (d+2)^h) causes infeasibility for high-dimensional problems.  
- Additional concern: main theoretical claims require a quasi-polynomial number of processors, which is unrealistic in practice.  
- Overall conclusion: novel, rigorous, and empirically effective work but constrained by the curse of dimensionality and strong processor assumptions.
