The paper "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning" presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, which can be applied to a broad class of learning algorithms without further mathematical derivations and without writing dedicated code. The scheme is able to reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units.

The main theoretical contribution of this paper is to show that algorithms satisfying certain conditions can be parallelised effectively. The authors consider a parallelisation to be effective if the (ε,∆)-guarantees are achieved in time polylogarithmic in N0(ε,∆). The cost for achieving this reduction in runtime comes in the form of an increased data size and in the number of processing units used. For the parallelisation scheme presented in this paper, the authors are able to bound this cost by a quasi-polynomial in 1/ε and log 1/∆.

The main practical contribution of this paper is an effective parallelisation scheme that treats the underlying learning algorithm as a black-box, i.e., it can be parallelised without further mathematical derivations and without writing dedicated code. The authors apply the underlying learning algorithm in parallel to random subsets of the data. Each resulting hypothesis is assigned to a leaf of an aggregation tree which is then traversed bottom-up. Each inner node computes a new hypothesis that is a Radon point of its children’s hypotheses.

The empirical evaluation of the Radon machine in Section 4 confirms its potential in practical settings. Given the same amount of data as the underlying learning algorithm, the Radon machine achieves a substantial reduction of computation time in realistic applications. Using 150 processors, the Radon machine is between 80 and around 700-times faster than the underlying learning algorithm on a single processing unit.

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their parallelisation scheme and its theoretical and practical contributions. The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides a good understanding of the performance of the Radon machine in practice.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes that data is abundant and that the hypothesis space can be parametrised in a fixed, finite dimensional Euclidean space.
* The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the communication complexity of the scheme.
* The paper does not discuss the applicability of the scheme to non-linear models.
