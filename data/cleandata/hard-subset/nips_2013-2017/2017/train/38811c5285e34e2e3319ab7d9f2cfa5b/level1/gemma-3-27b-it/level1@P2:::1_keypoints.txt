- Introduces the "Radon machine": parallelize regularized risk minimization by training on random data subsets and aggregating hypotheses via Radon points, claiming polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomial processors with comparable error/confidence at higher sample complexity.

- Well-motivated and clearly stated problem, tied to an open complexity question (Nick’s Class).

- Conceptually interesting use of Radon points and a black-box aggregation scheme applicable to many learning algorithms.

- Provides theoretical runtime and sample-complexity analysis.

- Empirical results show substantial speedups versus the sequential base learner and competitive performance against Spark MLlib.

- Theoretical claims hinge on the Radon number being polylogarithmic in sample complexity — a potentially restrictive condition that needs justification for typical ML models.

- Empirical evaluation lacks depth: mostly small datasets, limited base learners (WEKA, scikit-learn), no modern models (e.g., deep learning), and incomplete Spark comparison details.

- Figures are hard to interpret with limited error bars/statistical testing; ablation study on the choice of h is missing.

- Communication overhead of computing Radon points as processor count grows is under-analyzed and needs quantitative evaluation.

- Related work section is incomplete; should discuss federated learning, distributed optimization, and give a more nuanced comparison to averaging-based methods.

- Writing can be dense; numerous theorems interrupt flow and could be moved to an appendix for readability.

- Pros for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, black-box applicability, empirical speedups, and connection to a fundamental parallel-computation problem.

- Cons for acceptance: restrictive theoretical condition, limited empirical scope, missing communication analysis, incomplete related work, and dense presentation.

- Overall recommendation: Weak Reject; encourage authors to address theoretical justification, strengthen empirical evaluation, quantify communication costs, and expand related work.
