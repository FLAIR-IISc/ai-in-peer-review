- Proposes the Radon machine: train base learners on many i.i.d. data subsets in parallel and aggregate hypotheses via iterated Radon points along a tree.  
- Theoretical core: if base learner has (ε, δ) guarantee, iterated Radon aggregation yields Δ ≤ (r δ)^(2^h), i.e., double-exponential reduction in bad-event probability with tree height h.  
- With h ≈ log M − log log M and r^h processors, runtime to reach (ε, Δ) becomes polylogarithmic in the sequential sample complexity.  
- Aggregation cost is O(h r^3) and is negligible when TL(n) dominates runtime.  
- The (r δ)^2 union-bound step is conservative; binomial factors could tighten constants.  
- The requirement δ ≤ 1/2^r is stringent in high dimensions (r = d+2), so dimensionality reduction or feature mapping is needed.  
- “iid subsets” phrasing is misleading—partition without replacement is not iid; either state “disjoint subsets of an i.i.d. sample” or use bootstrap to match theory.  
- Theoretical assumptions use NL0(ε,δ) and TL(n)=O(n^κ); clarifying which loss/regularizer/optimizers satisfy these would strengthen the paper.  
- Empirically demonstrates large wall-clock speedups with 150 processors and accuracy competitive with MLlib, outperforming simple averaging.  
- Experiments do not directly probe the (ε, Δ) high-confidence guarantees (AUC is not aligned); need calibration/coverage or PAC-style empirical estimates.  
- Sensitivity analysis for h, r, and per-leaf sample size is only partial; a controlled study of accuracy/runtime trade-offs is needed.  
- Comparisons omit some strong modern distributed ERM/SGD solvers (e.g., CoCoA variants, approximate Newton methods) and require thorough tuning for fairness.  
- Communication costs are discussed qualitatively; the paper should report actual bytes/time per round and aggregation time.  
- Paper is generally well written; Radon point definitions and main results are clear and precise.  
- Minor clarity fixes: replace “iid subsets”, define ld as log2, unify log notation, and state when independence holds across tree levels.  
- Using iterated Radon points as aggregation is novel, provides a clean geometric insight, and yields explicit concentration improvements for convex measures.  
- NC framing is interesting but relies on quasi-polynomial processors, limiting scalability claims in practice for large d.  
- Pros: simple, broadly applicable black-box parallel primitive; provable double-exponential confidence amplification; clear NC connection; strong empirical speedups; minimal code changes.  
- Cons: dependence on Radon number limits high-dimensional/kernel models; iid/independence wording needs tightening; experiments don’t validate (ε,Δ) guarantees; quasi-polynomial processor needs; limited baseline/communication evaluation.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify independence/data splitting or adopt bootstrap; add experiments varying h and per-leaf n; report communication volume and aggregation time; demonstrate nonlinear models via random features/Nyström and show how r is controlled; tighten or discuss constants in Proposition 2.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — valuable geometric aggregation idea with solid theory and empirical support, requiring manageable revisions to improve rigor and scope.
