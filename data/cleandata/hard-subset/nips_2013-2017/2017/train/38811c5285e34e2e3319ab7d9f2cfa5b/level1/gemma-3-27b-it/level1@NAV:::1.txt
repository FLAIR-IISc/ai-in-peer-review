## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms aiming to reduce runtime to polylogarithmic time, albeit at the cost of increased sample complexity. The approach is broadly applicable, treating the base learning algorithm as a black-box and aggregating hypotheses via a Radon point-based method. The authors relate their work to the open question of efficient parallelization within Nick’s Class (NC).

The paper clearly outlines the motivation for parallelizing ML algorithms, particularly for applications requiring high confidence. The introduction establishes the regularized risk minimization framework and defines consistency and efficiency. The core contribution, the Radon machine, is presented with a clear algorithmic description and theoretical analysis demonstrating the promised runtime reduction. The empirical evaluation, comparing the Radon machine to standard algorithms and Spark’s MLlib, provides encouraging results showing significant speedups with comparable performance.  

**Strengths:** The novelty lies in the general applicability of the scheme – it doesn't require algorithm-specific derivations. The theoretical analysis providing guarantees on runtime and sample complexity is strong. The empirical results are well-presented and demonstrate substantial speedups, especially compared to Spark implementations. The connection to Nick’s Class and discussion of its implications are valuable. The writing is generally clear and well-organized. 

**Weaknesses:** The discussion of communication complexity is brief, a critical factor in real-world distributed systems. While the authors acknowledge limitations regarding high-dimensional data, the proposed solutions (random projections, low-rank approximations) feel somewhat hand-wavy and could benefit from more detailed discussion. The empirical evaluation focuses heavily on relatively small datasets, and scaling to truly massive datasets isn't fully demonstrated. The practical overhead of computing Radon points isn't thoroughly explored. The discussion could be made more accessible to readers less familiar with Radon geometry.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Novel and generally applicable parallelization scheme.
* Strong theoretical guarantees on runtime.
* Compelling empirical results demonstrating significant speedups.
* Connection to a fundamental open problem in parallel computation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* Limited exploration of communication complexity.
* Scalability to very high-dimensional data not fully addressed.
* Relatively small datasets used in empirical evaluation.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The theoretical and empirical results are promising, and the approach has the potential to significantly improve the scalability of many ML algorithms. While some areas could benefit from further exploration, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.
