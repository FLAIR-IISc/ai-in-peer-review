**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel parallelization scheme, called the Radon machine, for machine learning algorithms. The Radon machine is designed to simplify the adaptation of learning algorithms to growing amounts of data and to improve the accuracy and confidence of predictions in critical applications. The scheme is applicable to a broad class of learning algorithms without requiring further mathematical derivations or dedicated code. The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the Radon machine, showing that it can reduce the runtime of learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units. The cost of this parallelization is an increased sample complexity. The paper also presents an empirical evaluation of the Radon machine, demonstrating its potential in practical application scenarios.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work on parallel learning algorithms, including averaging-based parallelizations and stochastic gradient descent. However, the Radon machine differs from these approaches in its use of Radon points to aggregate hypotheses, which allows for a more efficient and effective parallelization scheme. The paper also relates to the concept of Nick's Class (NC) and the question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization is possible in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors.
