- Introduces the Radon Machine: train base learners on many small disjoint subsets in parallel and hierarchically aggregate hypotheses using Radon points.
- Theoretical claim: aggregation doubly-exponentially boosts confidence, yielding polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial number of processors, relating to NC.
- Originality: novel use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation and a black-box parallelization scheme for off-the-shelf learners.
- Practical advantage: can speed up existing algorithms without modifying base learners.
- Technical quality: sound theoretical analysis (Theorems 3 and 4) with supporting proofs.
- Empirical quality: extensive experiments showing up to 700x speed-ups and favorable runtime–performance trade-offs versus averaging baselines and Spark MLlib.
- Clarity: well-written paper with a clear Algorithm 1 and well-presented motivation and contributions.
- Weakness: strong dependence on hypothesis-space dimensionality (Radon number r = d+2), causing r^h scaling that may be infeasible in high dimensions.
- Weakness: polylogarithmic runtime requires a quasi-polynomial number of processors, which is impractical and needs clearer connection to fixed-processor speed-ups.
- Argument for acceptance: offers a novel, theoretically grounded method for parallelizing a broad class of ML algorithms.
- Argument for acceptance: provides compelling empirical evidence of substantial speed-ups over strong baselines.
- Argument for acceptance: advances understanding of parallel complexity in learning and addresses a fundamental theoretical question.
- Argument against acceptance: dimensionality dependence may limit practical applicability and deserves deeper evaluation.
