Summary: The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization (RRM) that aggregates hypotheses via iterated Radon points rather than averaging. Under convexity and finite Radon number (r = d+2 in Rd), the scheme reduces the probability of ε-bad hypotheses doubly exponentially with tree height h, enabling polylogarithmic runtime in the target sample size when using quasi-polynomially many processors. The authors relate this to NC-style parallel efficiency and prior work on parallel learning, including averaging-based schemes, distributed SGD/ERM (e.g., ADMM), and foundational results on NC-learnability and large-margin learning. Empirically, the method attains 80–700× speedups over base learners and favorable runtime-quality trade-offs versus Spark MLlib, with code released.

Quality: The theory is soundly stated for convex RRM with clear assumptions (iid partitions, fixed finite-dimensional parameterization, δ ≤ 1/2r for base learners). The reduction TR,h = TL(n) + h r^3 and the choice h ≈ log M − log log M are plausible, and the doubly-exponential confidence amplification from Radon aggregation is well supported. However, practical constraints when r = d+2 can be severe in high dimensions; the cost and numerical stability of solving O(r^3) linear systems and the necessity of δ ≤ 1/2r deserve deeper discussion and ablation. Experiments are solid but focus primarily on AUC; given the emphasis on confidence, calibration or conformal coverage would strengthen claims.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. Algorithm 1 and the Radon-point definition are clear. A concise reminder of how r^3 arises and a brief complexity table contrasting averaging vs. Radon aggregation would help.

Originality: Using Radon points to aggregate parallel hypotheses for confidence amplification is novel relative to averaging-based ensembles and distributed optimization. The NC connection is thoughtfully framed and extends prior NeurIPS work on parallel learnability.

Significance: The approach is broadly applicable to convex RRM with a black-box interface and yields substantial speedups. The quasi-polynomial processor requirement and dependence on r limit immediate applicability to high-dimensional/nonlinear models, though the authors discuss embeddings/random features.

Strengths:
- Novel, general aggregation principle with theoretical guarantees.
- Clear path to polylog runtime (with many processors).
- Strong empirical speedups; open-source implementation.
- Good positioning within NC and distributed learning literature.

Weaknesses:
- Sensitivity to dimensionality via r = d+2; r^3 aggregation cost.
- Assumption δ ≤ 1/2r may be stringent for challenging tasks.
- Limited evaluation of calibration/confidence; mostly AUC.
- Fairness of Spark comparisons could be elaborated (tuning, communication).

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually fresh and well-grounded method with promising results.
- Bridges theory and practice; likely to inspire follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical scalability in very high d and confidence claims need deeper empirical validation.
- Processor scaling is quasi-polynomial, not polynomial as in NC.
