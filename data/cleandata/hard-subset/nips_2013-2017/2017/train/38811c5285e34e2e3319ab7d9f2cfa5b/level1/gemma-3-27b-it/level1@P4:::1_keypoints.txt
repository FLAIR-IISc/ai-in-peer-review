- Proposes a novel parallelisation scheme using iterative Radon points to achieve polylogarithmic runtime with quasi-polynomial processors at the cost of increased sample complexity.
- Clear, well-structured paper with detailed mathematical foundations and definitions (Radon points) and theoretical performance guarantees.
- Novelty: using Radon points for parallelisation offers a stronger confidence boost per iteration than averaging-based methods.
- Theoretical guarantees: analysis shows potential for polylogarithmic runtime and provides bounds on the increased sample complexity.
- Black-box approach: method applies broadly to many learning algorithms without algorithm-specific modifications.
- Empirical validation: experiments show substantial speedups over sequential learners and Spark MLlib.
- Presentation: writing, organization, and notation are clear and logical.
- Weakness: performance tightly depends on the Radon number of the hypothesis space; high Radon numbers and the r^3 runtime factor need deeper examination.
- Weakness: practical overheads and constant factors for computing Radon points may be significant and require more analysis.
- Weakness: empirical evaluation focuses on low-dimensional feature sets; needs extension to high-dimensional datasets.
- Weakness: connection to Nick’s Class (NC) is mentioned but implications and limitations are not fully explored.
- Weakness: communication complexity is only briefly discussed with no quantitative analysis, despite its importance in distributed systems.
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically solid, empirically promising, widely applicable “black-box” solution addressing fundamental scalability.
- Against acceptance: practical limitations from Radon dependency and computational overhead, limited empirical scope, and underdeveloped NC connection.
- Overall: strong paper, recommended for acceptance with minor revisions addressing Radon number dependency, communication costs, and NC implications.
