- Proposes the "Radon machine": run base learners on random data subsets in parallel and iteratively aggregate hypotheses via Radon points to obtain stronger confidence bounds.  
- Claims runtime can be reduced to polylogarithmic in sample complexity at the cost of more data and processing units.  
- Novelty: using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation in parallel learning is a new idea with compelling theoretical justification.  
- Generality: black-box approach applicable to a broad class of regularised risk minimisation algorithms without algorithm-specific changes.  
- Theoretical grounding: provides runtime and sample complexity bounds and connects to complexity classes like Nick’s Class.  
- Empirical validation: shows promising speedups and comparable performance to existing methods, including insightful comparisons to Spark’s MLlib.  
- Presentation: paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and theory.  
- Practical limitation: requirement of a finite Radon number may limit applicability, especially in high-dimensional hypothesis spaces.  
- Dimensionality reduction: suggested as a remedy but practical impact and trade-offs are not fully explored.  
- Scalability concerns: empirical evaluation is limited in dataset size and may not demonstrate performance on truly massive datasets.  
- Communication costs: aggregating Radon points across many processors could become a bottleneck in large-scale distributed settings.  
- Constant factors: asymptotic polylogarithmic runtime may hide large constants that reduce practical speedups.  
- Computational cost: computing Radon points has r^3 complexity and may be prohibitive in high dimensions.  
- Sensitivity to hyperparameter h: performance depends on the number of iterations h, with no robust guidance for selecting it.  
- Baseline comparisons: lacks extensive benchmarking against more recent, highly optimized distributed frameworks (e.g., Horovod, Ray).  
- Online learning: adaptation to online learning is mentioned but not developed or evaluated.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, broadly applicable, and empirically promising with valuable theoretical connections.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical applicability concerns (Radon number, communication/computation costs), scalability uncertainties, sensitivity to h, and incomplete comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: significant contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: discuss practical implications of the Radon number and dimensionality reduction; expand empirical evaluation to larger datasets and modern frameworks; provide strategies for tuning h; address Radon point computational costs; and elaborate on online-learning adaptations.
