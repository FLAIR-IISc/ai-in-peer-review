- Summary: Proposes the Radon machine, a novel parallelization scheme using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses, enabling polylogarithmic runtime on many processors at the cost of increased sample complexity.  
- Applicability: Designed for a broad class of learning algorithms without extra derivations or dedicated code, with empirical evaluation showing practical potential.  
- Theoretical contribution: Provides rigorous analysis proving consistency and bounding sample complexity, and shows runtime reductions under quasi-polynomial processors.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on averaging-based parallelizations and SGD but differs by using Radon points; relates to NC and polylog-time risk minimization questions.  
- Strength: Novel parallelization scheme applicable to many algorithms.  
- Strength: Thorough theoretical analysis including consistency proof and sample complexity bound.  
- Strength: Empirical results demonstrating runtime and predictive improvements over state-of-the-art.  
- Weakness: Increased sample complexity may limit usefulness in data-scarce settings.  
- Weakness: Limited direct applicability to high-dimensional or non-linear models, though possible remedies are suggested.  
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on properties of Radon points, which may be insufficiently understood in some contexts.  
- Originality: Innovative use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation and parallel learning.  
- Significance: Potentially important contribution for efficient parallel learning with demonstrated theoretical and empirical gains.  
- Quality: Technically sound with reproducible theoretical and experimental results.  
- Clarity: Well-written and clearly organized for expert readers.  
- Recommendation: Accept, subject to addressing limitations around sample complexity and applicability to high-dimensional/non-linear models.
