- Introduces the Radon Machine, a novel black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization that achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors with theoretical guarantees.
- Aggregates hypotheses using Radon points instead of averaging; Theorem 3 shows error probability decreases doubly exponentially with aggregation-tree height.
- Formally relates sample complexity and runtime of the Radon Machine to the base learning algorithm, enabling black-box applicability without code changes or derivations.
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear definitions and structured proofs (Proposition 2, Theorem 3); connects to Nick’s Class and the question of polylogarithmic-time consistent learning.
- Novelty: doubly-exponential confidence improvement over averaging-based aggregation methods.
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing to averaging baselines and Spark implementations across tasks (classification, regression, multi-class), showing 80–700× speedups with comparable predictive performance.
- Paper is well-organized and clear, with a reproducible algorithm description (Algorithm 1) and logical flow from theory to experiments.
- Practical scalability concern: required number of processors may be prohibitively large; discussion of handling fewer processors (Section 5) is superficial.
- Applicability to high-dimensional data is limited because dataset sizes must be multiples of the Radon number; proposed workarounds (random projections, low-rank approximations) are not thoroughly demonstrated.
- Theoretical assumptions restrict the approach to Euclidean hypothesis spaces with finite Radon number, excluding some important settings (e.g., non-linear kernel methods without low-rank approximations).
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important open problem, provides rigorous and novel theoretical guarantees, yields significant practical speedups, and is broadly applicable as a black-box method.
- Arguments against acceptance: impractical processor requirements, limited treatment of high-dimensional data, and theoretical restrictions that may limit general applicability.
- Overall: strong contribution combining theoretical advance and empirical validation, suitable for NIPS despite noted practical and applicability limitations.
