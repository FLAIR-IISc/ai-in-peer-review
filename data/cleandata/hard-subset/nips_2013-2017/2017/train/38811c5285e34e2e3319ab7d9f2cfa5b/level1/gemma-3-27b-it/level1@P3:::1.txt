This paper proposes a novel parallelisation scheme, dubbed the "Radon machine," for regularised risk minimisation algorithms. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm to random subsets of the data in parallel and then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, which provide stronger confidence bounds than simple averaging. The authors claim this scheme can reduce runtime to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity, at the cost of increased data usage and processing units. 

The work builds upon existing parallelisation techniques like averaging-based methods [32, 45, 46] but distinguishes itself by avoiding algorithm-specific derivations and offering theoretical performance guarantees. It connects to the theoretical computer science literature on parallel computation (Nick’s Class [9]) and attempts to bridge the gap between theoretical parallelisability and practical applicability in machine learning. The authors explicitly address the challenge of efficiently parallelising polynomial-time learning algorithms, a problem recognised as difficult in the field.  The provided empirical evaluation compares the Radon machine against standard Spark implementations and averaging, demonstrating promising speedups, particularly with a substantial number of processors.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Radon points for aggregating hypotheses in a parallel learning setting is a genuinely novel idea. The theoretical justification for the improved confidence bounds is compelling.
* **Generality:** The "black-box" nature of the approach – applicability to a broad class of learning algorithms without modification – is a significant strength. This lowers the barrier to adoption and makes the method broadly useful.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, including runtime and sample complexity bounds, and connect their work to established complexity classes like Nick’s Class.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results, while limited by dataset size constraints, demonstrate substantial speedups and comparable performance to existing parallel learning methods.  The comparison to Spark’s MLlib is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the Radon machine algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical limitations of Radon number:** The requirement of a finite Radon number for the hypothesis space, while addressed with the mention of dimensionality reduction techniques, could limit applicability to certain high-dimensional problems. The practical impact of applying these dimensionality reduction techniques isn't fully explored.
* **Scalability to very large datasets:** While the empirical evaluation shows promise, it's limited by the scalability of the base learners used.  The results on truly massive datasets (beyond the SUSY and HIGGS examples) are lacking.  The communication costs associated with calculating and aggregating Radon points across a very large number of processors could become a bottleneck.
* **Constant Factors & Hidden Costs:** The theoretical analysis focuses on asymptotic complexity. The constant factors hidden within the polylogarithmic runtime could be significant, potentially negating some of the speedup in practice. The computational cost of computing Radon points (r^3 complexity mentioned in section 3) might become prohibitive for very high-dimensional spaces.
* **Sensitivity to Parameter h:** The performance of the Radon machine seems sensitive to the choice of the parameter 'h' (number of iterations). The paper doesn’t offer a robust guideline for selecting 'h' in different scenarios, requiring potentially extensive tuning.
* **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While compared to Spark’s MLlib, the paper doesn’t extensively benchmark against more recent and highly optimised distributed learning frameworks (e.g., Horovod, Ray) which have emerged since the publication of some cited works.
* **Limited Exploration of Online Learning:** The mention of adapting the scheme to online learning is brief and lacks detail. This is a promising avenue for future work that could benefit from further elaboration.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound parallelisation scheme for machine learning.
* The "black-box" nature of the approach makes it potentially applicable to a wide range of algorithms.
* The empirical results are encouraging and demonstrate significant speedups.
* The connection to the theoretical computer science literature (Nick’s Class) is valuable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Practical limitations related to the Radon number and potential communication costs could restrict the scheme’s applicability.
* The scalability to extremely large datasets needs further investigation.
* The sensitivity to the parameter 'h' requires a more detailed analysis.
* The comparison with the state-of-the-art could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The novel application of Radon points provides a theoretically grounded and potentially practical approach to accelerating learning algorithms. While there are limitations and potential challenges, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the Radon number requirement and the potential benefits/drawbacks of dimensionality reduction techniques.
2.  Expand the empirical evaluation to include larger datasets and comparisons with more recent distributed learning frameworks.
3.  Investigate strategies for automatically tuning the parameter 'h' and provide guidance for its selection.
4.  Address the computational cost of the Radon point calculation, particularly for high-dimensional spaces.
5.  Elaborate on the application of the scheme to online learning scenarios.
