

# Peer Review: "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that aims to achieve polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The authors build on the regularized risk minimization framework, proposing to parallelize learning algorithms by applying them to random subsets of data, then aggregating the resulting hypotheses using Radon points (a concept from convex geometry) rather than averaging. The key theoretical contribution is Theorem 3, which shows that the error probability decreases doubly exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. The empirical results demonstrate significant speedups (80-700x) on moderate-sized datasets while maintaining comparable predictive performance to base learning algorithms and Spark MLlib implementations.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning, particularly in relation to Nick's Class (NC) of efficiently parallelizable problems. It builds upon earlier theoretical work on parallel learning (e.g., Vitter and Lin [42], Long and Servedio [21]), but distinguishes itself by providing a black-box parallelization scheme that works for a broad class of learning algorithms without requiring mathematical derivations or code modifications. This addresses a key limitation of previous approaches that often required problem-specific parallelization.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous proofs for Proposition 2 and Theorem 3, establishing the doubly exponential error reduction property of the Radon aggregation. The connection to Radon's theorem and the careful treatment of convexity and quasi-convexity are particularly strong.

2. **Practical Relevance:** The empirical results on real-world datasets demonstrate significant speedups (721x on SUSY with 150 processors) while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The comparison to Spark MLlib and the averaging-at-the-end baseline is well-executed.

3. **Clear Black-Box Approach:** The scheme's ability to treat the underlying learning algorithm as a black box is a major practical advantage, making it broadly applicable to various machine learning algorithms without modification.

4. **Well-Motivated Theoretical Question:** The paper correctly identifies and addresses the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be done in polylogarithmic time with polynomial processors, a question previously posed by Long and Servedio [21].

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Misleading "Polylogarithmic Time" Claim:** The paper claims "polylogarithmic time" but the number of processors required is quasi-polynomial, not polynomial. Theorem 4 correctly states "quasi-polynomially many processing units," but the main text and abstract overstate the result by implying polynomial processors, which would be required for a true NC-class solution. This misalignment between the claim and the actual result is potentially misleading.

2. **Limited Scope of Applicability:** The paper assumes a hypothesis space with finite Radon number, which for Euclidean spaces means the dimension d must be finite. This excludes many practical applications, particularly non-linear models (e.g., kernel methods) and high-dimensional data without dimensionality reduction. While the paper discusses potential workarounds (low-rank approximations, random projections), it doesn't demonstrate how well these work in practice for large-scale non-linear models.

3. **Empirical Limitations:** The empirical evaluation is restricted to moderate-sized datasets (up to 5 million instances), which doesn't fully demonstrate scalability to truly massive datasets. The comparison with Spark MLlib is limited to a few datasets and algorithms, and it's unclear how the Radon machine would perform with more complex models or larger scale.

4. **Comparison to Other Parallelization Schemes:** The paper doesn't adequately compare with the most recent parallel learning approaches, such as the distributed stochastic gradient descent methods by Zhang et al. [40] or the communication-efficient algorithms by Shamir et al. [33]. These approaches have been shown to achieve good performance with much lower communication costs than the Radon machine's aggregation phase.

**Critical Evaluation:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the parallelizability of machine learning algorithms, but the practical implications are somewhat limited by the quasi-polynomial processor requirement. The Radon machine's theoretical guarantees are compelling, but the "black-box" nature comes with a cost: the need for quasi-polynomial processors to achieve polylogarithmic runtime. For real-world applications with limited computational resources, this might not be feasible.

The empirical results are promising, but the speedups reported (721x on SUSY with 150 processors) seem to rely on the specific dataset characteristics (18 features) and the fact that the Radon machine with h=3 still uses small subsets (100 instances per subset). For datasets with more features or larger subsets, the speedup might be less dramatic. The paper also doesn't provide sufficient analysis of the communication complexity, which is a critical factor in distributed learning.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution to the understanding of parallel machine learning. However, the claim of "polylogarithmic time" is misleading without the quasi-polynomial processor requirement, and the practical applicability is limited by the dimensionality constraints. The empirical results are promising but insufficient to demonstrate scalability to truly massive datasets.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and addresses a fundamental open question in parallel learning.
- The Radon machine's black-box nature and empirical speedups are compelling for practical applications.
- The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous averaging-based approaches with stronger theoretical guarantees.

*Con:*
- The quasi-polynomial processor requirement undermines the practical significance of the polylogarithmic runtime claim.
- The limited empirical evaluation on moderate-sized datasets doesn't fully validate the approach for real-world large-scale applications.
- The paper doesn't adequately address how the scheme would work with the most common modern machine learning models (non-linear, high-dimensional).

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field, but the practical significance is somewhat limited by the quasi-polynomial processor requirement. The claims about "polylogarithmic time" need to be more precisely qualified. The paper would benefit from a more thorough empirical evaluation on larger datasets and with more diverse learning algorithms, as well as a clearer discussion of the trade-offs between processor requirements and practical scalability. I recommend acceptance, but with the understanding that the practical implications are more limited than the title might suggest. The authors should clarify the processor requirements throughout the paper to avoid misleading readers.
