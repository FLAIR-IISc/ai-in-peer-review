- Proposal: Introduces the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for convex regularized risk minimization using Radon-point aggregation on an r-ary tree.
- Method: Train base learner independently on r^h iid subsamples, arrange hypotheses in a balanced r-ary tree, aggregate bottom-up by computing Radon points at internal nodes.
- Main theoretical claim: Confidence amplification — if base learner fails with prob δ, after h aggregation levels failure probability contracts to at most (rδ)2^h (Proposition 2, Theorem 3).
- Complexity results: Sample requirement NR0 = r^h NL0(ε,δ); parallel runtime TL(n)+h r^3 with Radon computation O(r^3); polylogarithmic runtime in M under explicit assumptions (Theorem 4).
- Assumptions: Convex hypothesis space, convex loss and regularizer, finite Radon number (r=d+2), iid subsamples, and a specific form for NL0(ε,δ).
- Empirics: Demonstrates large wall-clock speedups and competitive accuracy versus single-node base learners and Spark MLlib; code released.
- Relation to prior work: Differs from averaging-based aggregation with stronger amplification, complements PAC/NC literature, and contrasts with distributed SGD that needs many synchronizations.
- Theoretical quality: Bounds follow from quasi-convexity and union bounds; analysis coherent but constants are loose and the δ ≤ 1/(2r) requirement can be demanding.
- Experimental quality: Practical implementation and speedups shown, but limited reporting on variance/statistical significance, overhead breakdowns, and fairness of baselines.
- Comparison baseline gap: Should add robust aggregators (geometric median, median-of-means) beyond averaging-at-the-end to strengthen empirical claims.
- Clarity: Writing generally clear; odd use of "ld" for log and important sample-complexity specializations and r-polylog conditions should be moved into the main text.
- Originality: Novel use of Radon points for generic convex learning aggregation and a simple compositional analysis yielding double-exponential confidence amplification.
- Significance: Useful when data is abundant and dimensionality moderate; limited applicability when d is large due to r=d+2 and quasi-polynomial processor needs.
- Strength: Black-box, broadly applicable parallelization for convex ERM/RRM learners.
- Strength: Clean, compositional theoretical confidence amplification via Radon aggregation.
- Strength: Low communication approach (aggregation-only) and polylogarithmic runtime under assumptions.
- Strength: Strong empirical speedups and open-source implementation.
- Weakness: Dependence on Radon number r=d+2 increases data/processor needs and forces δ ≤ 1/(2r), limiting high-dimensional applicability.
- Weakness: Polylog-time guarantee requires quasi-polynomial processors; not strictly NC.
- Weakness: Probability bounds hide combinatorial constants; Proposition 2 could be tighter/more explicit.
- Weakness: Experimental reporting lacks variance/significance and robust aggregator baselines.
- Suggestions: Provide tighter bounds with explicit constants and discuss empirical relevance of constants.
- Suggestions: Move αε,βε specializations (VC/Rademacher) into main text and give concrete numbers for common models.
- Suggestions: Add robust aggregation baselines, report statistical significance, and provide breakdowns of training vs aggregation overhead.
- Suggestions: Evaluate impact of dimensionality reduction (random projections, Nyström) and include high-dimensional experiments with/without projection.
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, broadly applicable aggregation mechanism with provable guarantees, practical speedups, and available code.
- Rejection arguments: Practical limits in high dimensions, reliance on strong dimensionality/processor assumptions, and incomplete empirical evaluation.
