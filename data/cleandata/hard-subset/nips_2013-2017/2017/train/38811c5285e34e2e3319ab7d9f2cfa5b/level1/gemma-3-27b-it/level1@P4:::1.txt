This paper introduces a novel parallelisation scheme for learning algorithms based on iteratively computing Radon points of hypotheses obtained from parallel execution on data subsets. The core idea is to achieve polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial number of processors, at the cost of increased sample complexity. The authors position this as a step toward addressing the efficient parallelisation of machine learning, a challenging problem linked to Nick’s Class (NC).

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction of the problem and the proposed solution. The mathematical foundations are presented with sufficient detail, including the definition of Radon points and theoretical guarantees on the parallelisation scheme’s performance. The empirical evaluation provides evidence of speed-ups compared to baseline learners and existing parallel implementations like Spark MLlib.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The use of Radon points for parallelising learning algorithms is a novel approach. While averaging-based parallelisation is common, the authors convincingly argue that using Radon points provides a stronger confidence boost with each iteration.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical analysis demonstrating the potential for polylogarithmic runtime, a significant improvement over polynomial runtime for many algorithms. The analysis also provides bounds on the increased sample complexity.
*   **Black-box Approach:**  The scheme is presented as a “black-box” method, meaning it can be applied to a broad class of learning algorithms without requiring algorithm-specific modifications. This is a significant advantage for practical adoption.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups in realistic application scenarios, both compared to sequential execution and alternative parallel implementations.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organised.  The notation is clear, and the main ideas are presented in a logical sequence.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Radon Number Dependency:** The performance of the scheme is tightly coupled to the Radon number of the hypothesis space. The paper acknowledges this and mentions potential workarounds (e.g., low-rank approximations) for high-dimensional spaces, but a more thorough discussion of this dependency, including its practical implications, would be beneficial.  The impact of a large Radon number on computation time (r^3 in the runtime analysis) needs further examination.
*   **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical analysis shows polylogarithmic runtime, the constant factors and overhead associated with computing Radon points could be significant in practice. This is somewhat addressed in the empirical section, but a more detailed analysis of the overhead would be valuable.
*   **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is primarily focused on datasets where the number of features is relatively small.  Extending the evaluation to higher-dimensional datasets would strengthen the claims about the scheme’s practicality.
*   **Connection to NC:** The link to Nick’s Class is mentioned frequently, but the implications of achieving polylogarithmic time in this context are not fully explored. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the result in relation to NC would be helpful.
*   **Communication Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions communication complexity but doesn’t provide a quantitative analysis.  For distributed systems, communication costs can be a major bottleneck, and a more thorough analysis would be important.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallelising machine learning algorithms.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant speedups in realistic scenarios.
*   The "black-box" nature of the scheme makes it widely applicable.
*   The work addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with potential implications for scalability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical performance of the scheme could be limited by the dependency on the Radon number and the overhead of computing Radon points.
*   The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
*   The connection to Nick’s Class is not fully exploited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically well-supported approach to parallelising machine learning algorithms. The empirical results are promising, and the "black-box" nature of the scheme is a significant advantage. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly regarding the Radon number dependency, communication complexity, and a more in-depth discussion of the implications of achieving polylogarithmic runtime in relation to Nick's Class.
