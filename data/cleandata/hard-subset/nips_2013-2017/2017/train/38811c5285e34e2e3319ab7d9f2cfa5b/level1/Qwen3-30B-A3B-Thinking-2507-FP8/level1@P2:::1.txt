

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that leverages Radon points from convex geometry to achieve significant speedups while maintaining theoretical error guarantees. The authors address an important open question in parallel computing: whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be performed in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors (Nick's Class NC). The Radon Machine operates by first training weak hypotheses on random data subsets in parallel, then iteratively aggregating them using Radon points (a point in the intersection of convex hulls of partitions of the set of hypotheses), which provides doubly exponential confidence improvements with each aggregation level compared to simple averaging.

The paper relates to previous work on parallel learning in several ways. It builds upon averaging-based approaches [32, 45, 46] but demonstrates a key theoretical advantage: while averaging provides only linear improvements in confidence, Radon points provide doubly exponential improvements. The work also connects to theoretical treatments of parallel learning in NC [5, 39, 42] and complements recent results on learning half-spaces [21]. Unlike stochastic gradient descent approaches [34] that still require linear time in the number of iterations, the Radon Machine achieves polylogarithmic runtime under the stated conditions.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by proving that the Radon Machine achieves doubly exponential confidence improvements (Theorem 3) and provides a step toward answering the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be performed in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors. The proof of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 is elegant and well-structured.

2. **Empirical validation:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups (80-700x) compared to base learning algorithms on 150 processors while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The comparison with Spark's MLlib and averaging-at-the-end baseline is thorough and shows clear advantages of the Radon Machine.

3. **Practical applicability:** The approach is presented as a "black-box" parallelization scheme, applicable to a broad class of learning algorithms without requiring mathematical derivations or dedicated code. The discussion of practical considerations (high-dimensional data, non-linear models, limited processors) shows good understanding of real-world constraints.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Quasi-polynomial processor requirement:** The theoretical results require a quasi-polynomial number of processors (r^h), which may not be practical for many applications. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, the practical relevance of the results is somewhat diminished for cases where such a large number of processors is unavailable.

2. **Radon number dependence:** The approach depends on the Radon number of the hypothesis space (r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces), which grows with dimensionality. For high-dimensional problems (common in modern machine learning), this would require an impractically large number of processors, limiting the method's applicability.

3. **Communication complexity:** The paper mentions that communication is "low compared to periodically communicating schemes" but doesn't provide concrete analysis or measurements of communication complexity, which is a critical factor in distributed systems.

4. **Limited empirical scope:** The experiments focus on moderately sized datasets due to scalability limitations of the base learners. While the paper discusses the potential for larger datasets, the empirical evaluation doesn't fully demonstrate the method's performance on truly large-scale problems.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to an important open question in machine learning parallelization.
- The Radon Machine provides a novel approach that achieves theoretically proven doubly exponential confidence improvements over simple averaging.
- The empirical results are strong, demonstrating substantial speedups while maintaining comparable predictive performance.
- The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides thorough theoretical justification for the proposed approach.

*Con:*
- The requirement for quasi-polynomially many processors limits the practical applicability for many real-world scenarios.
- The dependence on the Radon number (which grows with dimensionality) makes the method less suitable for high-dimensional problems without additional techniques (like random projections).
- The communication complexity analysis is insufficient for a distributed systems paper.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a theoretically sound and practically promising approach to parallel machine learning. The theoretical contribution is significant, addressing a fundamental question about the parallelizability of learning algorithms. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate real-world utility. While the quasi-polynomial processor requirement is a limitation, the authors provide thoughtful discussion of practical workarounds (random projections, low-rank approximations) and show that the approach can be applied with fewer processors with a modest performance penalty.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria well. It's of high technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis and strong empirical validation. It's clearly written and well-organized. The approach is original, representing a novel application of Radon points to machine learning parallelization. The significance is high, as it addresses an important open question in parallel learning with both theoretical and practical contributions.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a genuine contribution to the field, and while it has some practical limitations, these are acknowledged and addressed by the authors. The work is relevant to the NIPS community and advances the state of the art in parallel machine learning.
