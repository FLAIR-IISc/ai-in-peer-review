Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization over convex hypothesis spaces in finite-dimensional Euclidean spaces. The algorithm partitions the dataset into r^h iid subsets, trains the base learner independently on each subset, and then aggregates the resulting hypotheses up a tree by repeatedly computing Radon points of groups of size r = d + 2 (the Radon number of Rd). The key theoretical claim is that, if the base learner produces an ε-good hypothesis with probability at least 1 − δ, then one level of Radon aggregation drives the failure probability to at most (r δ)^2, and h levels produce (r δ)^(2^h). With an appropriate choice of h and assuming r is polylogarithmic in the sample complexity, the scheme achieves polylogarithmic runtime in the target sample complexity using quasi-polynomially many processors, at the cost of increased sample complexity. Empirically, using Weka/Scikit base learners and Spark execution, the Radon machine attains 80–700× speedups over single-processor baselines (with comparable AUC) and is substantially faster than several Spark MLlib implementations, while outperforming averaging-at-the-end in AUC.

The approach connects geometric median/centerpoint ideas (iterated Radon points) to ensemble aggregation and builds on prior NIPS/theory work on NC-learnability and distributed learning: averaging-based parallelization [e.g., Zinkevich et al., Rosenblatt & Nadler], distributed SGD and mini-batch methods [Dekel et al., Shamir & Srebro], ADMM-based ERM solvers, and classic PAC/NC-learnability results (Vitter & Lin; Long & Servedio).

Quality:
- Theory: The probabilistic improvement from Radon aggregation (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) is clear and relies on quasi-convexity of the regret and Radon’s theorem. The runtime analysis TR,h(N) = TL(n) + h r^3, with n the per-worker sample size, is reasonable given an r×r linear system per node and parallel solve across nodes. The polylog-time result (Theorem 4) is contingent on r being polylog in the sample complexity and on access to quasi-polynomially many processors; the paper is careful to state these conditions. A notable assumption is δ ≤ 1/2^r for the base learner, which becomes stringent as r = d + 2 grows. This is acknowledged but its practical feasibility is not deeply analyzed.
- Empirics: The experiments show strong wall-clock gains on moderate hardware, with AUC comparable to single-node baselines and Spark methods. The benefit over averaging is consistent and aligns with the theory. However, the empirical section could better control for implementation differences (e.g., highly optimized distributed linear models vs. Weka/Scikit on Spark wrappers), and does not quantify the sample complexity inflation or the effect of non-iid partitions. Confidence intervals or statistical tests for AUC are missing.
- Limitations: The method requires convex parameter spaces and finite Radon number; r scales linearly with dimension, making the δ requirement and r^3 per-aggregation cost problematic in high dimensions unless a projection/low-rank trick is used. The theoretical NC connection hinges on r = polylog(N0), which is restrictive.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The algorithm is stated clearly. The progression from Radon properties to aggregation guarantees is easy to follow. Some notation is dense (switching between δ and ∆), and the necessity/impact of δ ≤ 1/2^r and of iid partitions could be emphasized earlier. The discussion of NC and processor counts is clear but could better separate asymptotic claims from practical regimes.

Originality:
Using iterated Radon points for hypothesis aggregation with explicit high-confidence guarantees appears novel in ML parallelization. The double-exponential reduction in failure probability with tree height is a distinctive contribution beyond averaging-based schemes. The black-box applicability to a range of convex learners is appealing.

Significance:
Conceptually, the paper offers an interesting bridge between computational geometry and parallel learning, and highlights a path toward NC-style parallelization under convexity. Practically, the speedups on fixed hardware and improved accuracy over averaging suggest immediate utility in low-to-moderate dimensional settings and high-confidence applications. The strong dependence on dimension and required processor/data growth limits universality, but the idea is likely to spur follow-on work and adaptations (e.g., random features, projections).

Strengths:
- Clean, general aggregation scheme with simple implementation and strong intuition.
- Provable confidence amplification via Radon aggregation; doubly-exponential in tree height.
- Clear conditions under which polylog-runtime is achievable (and honest about processor/sample costs).
- Empirical speedups with comparable accuracy; consistent outperformance of averaging-at-the-end.

Weaknesses:
- Theoretical guarantees hinge on r = d + 2; δ ≤ 1/2^r can be very demanding in high dimensions, and r^3 aggregation cost grows quickly.
- Polylog-time claim requires quasi-polynomial processors and r polylog in sample complexity; restrictive for many practical models.
- Empirical comparisons conflate algorithmic advances with implementation differences; limited ablations on h, r, and partition assumptions.
- No analysis of numerical stability/conditioning of Radon linear systems; no report on communication overheads beyond big-O.
- Limited treatment of non-iid partitions, label imbalance, and calibration—important in distributed settings.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Quantify sample complexity inflation and wall-clock cost of aggregation vs. learning, including communication bytes transferred.
- Provide sensitivity analyses over h, subset sizes, and deviations from iid partitioning; report confidence intervals for AUC.
- Include strong, optimized baselines that match the same model class (e.g., LIBLINEAR/Hogwild!/Vowpal distributed) and measure end-to-end resource usage.
- Discuss numerical stability and regularization when solving r×r systems; provide empirical evidence in higher-d regimes or with random projections/random features.
- Clarify and foreground the δ ≤ 1/2^r assumption and when it is realistic.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and well-justified aggregation method with clear theoretical guarantees.
- Bridges NC-style reasoning with practical ML and shows real speedups.
- Black-box applicability across convex learners is attractive to practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical impact constrained by dependence on dimension and processor/sample requirements.
- Empirical evaluation, while promising, lacks rigorous controls and uncertainty quantification.
- Polylog-time result applies under restrictive assumptions; NC framing may oversell practical generality.

Overall recommendation:
Borderline accept. The core idea is original and theoretically interesting, and the empirical gains are encouraging. Strengthening the empirical methodology and clarifying the scope of the theoretical claims would significantly improve the paper.
