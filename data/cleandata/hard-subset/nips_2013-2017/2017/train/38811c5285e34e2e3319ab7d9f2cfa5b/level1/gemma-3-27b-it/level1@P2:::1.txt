This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme, the "Radon machine," designed to reduce the runtime of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm in parallel to random subsets of the data and then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points. The authors claim polylogarithmic runtime on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units, achieving comparable confidence and error bounds to sequential execution, albeit at the cost of increased sample complexity.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial challenge in modern machine learning – efficiently scaling algorithms to large datasets. The problem is clearly stated, and the connection to the open question regarding efficient parallelization (Nick’s Class) is explicitly made. The approach is conceptually interesting, drawing upon Radon points (a somewhat niche geometric concept) to achieve a novel aggregation strategy. The authors rightly emphasize the black-box nature of the scheme, highlighting its potential applicability to a broad range of learning algorithms without requiring modifications to their underlying implementations. The empirical evaluation provides evidence of substantial speedups compared to the sequential base learner and competitive performance against existing parallel implementations (Spark MLlib).  The inclusion of theoretical analysis bounding the runtime and sample complexity is also a significant strength.

However, there are several weaknesses that prevent this paper from being a strong accept. First, while the theoretical analysis is present, its connection to practical implications feels somewhat loose. The claim of polylogarithmic runtime relies on the Radon number being polylogarithmic in the sample complexity—a condition that isn’t always guaranteed and requires further justification in the context of typical machine learning models.  The practical impact of this condition is not thoroughly discussed. 

Second, the empirical evaluation, while demonstrating speedups, lacks depth. The datasets used are relatively small (except for SUSY and HIGGS in the comparison with Spark). The choice of base learners (WEKA and scikit-learn algorithms) feels somewhat limited; it would be beneficial to include more diverse and modern machine learning models (e.g., deep learning).  The performance gains against Spark MLlib are encouraging, but the comparison isn’t fully fleshed out – details about the Spark configurations and specific algorithms used are needed for a more robust evaluation. The figures are also somewhat difficult to interpret, with limited error bars or statistical significance testing. The ablation study regarding the choice of *h* is lacking.

Third, the paper doesn't adequately address the communication overhead associated with the Radon point calculations, particularly as the number of processing units increases. While the authors mention the low communication cost compared to other schemes, a more detailed analysis quantifying this overhead would strengthen the argument. 

Fourth, the related work section, while referencing relevant papers, could be more comprehensive.  Recent advancements in federated learning and distributed optimization techniques warrant further discussion, especially concerning the trade-offs between communication efficiency, computational cost, and convergence rates.  The discussion of averaging-based methods is also somewhat dismissive without a more nuanced comparison.

Finally, the writing quality, while generally clear, could be improved.  Some sections are dense and require careful reading.  The numerous theorems and propositions, while mathematically sound, interrupt the flow of the paper and could be moved to an appendix.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded parallelization scheme.
*   Black-box nature applicable to a wide range of algorithms.
*   Demonstrated speedups in empirical evaluation.
*   Connection to a fundamental open problem in parallel computation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical guarantees rely on a potentially restrictive condition on the Radon number.
*   Limited scope and depth of the empirical evaluation.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of communication overhead.
*   Insufficient discussion of related work and comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Dense writing style.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **Weak Reject**.  While the ideas presented are interesting and potentially significant, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that prevent it from meeting the NIPS quality standards.  The theoretical claims need further justification, the empirical evaluation needs to be more thorough, and the discussion of related work needs to be expanded. I encourage the authors to address these concerns and resubmit a revised version in the future.
