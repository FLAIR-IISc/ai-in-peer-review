- Introduces the "Radon Machine," a parallelization scheme using Radon points to aggregate weak hypotheses and achieve large speedups with theoretical error guarantees.  
- Addresses whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be done in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors (NC).  
- Radon Machine trains weak models on random subsets in parallel and iteratively aggregates them with Radon points for doubly exponential confidence gains versus simple averaging.  
- Builds on averaging-based parallel learning but claims a theoretical advantage: doubly exponential vs. linear confidence improvements.  
- Relates to prior NC treatments and complements recent results on learning half-spaces; contrasts with SGD which needs linear iteration time.  
- Theoretical contribution: proves doubly exponential confidence improvement (Theorem 3) with elegant proofs for Proposition 2 and Theorem 3.  
- Empirical validation: shows substantial speedups (80–700x) on 150 processors while keeping comparable predictive performance and outperforming Spark MLlib and averaging baselines.  
- Practical applicability: black-box scheme usable for many learning algorithms; discusses handling high-dimensional data, non-linear models, and limited processors.  
- Weakness: requires quasi-polynomially many processors (r^h), limiting practical applicability.  
- Weakness: dependence on the Radon number (r = d+2) makes the approach problematic for high-dimensional problems without dimensionality reduction.  
- Weakness: communication complexity lacks concrete analysis or measurements, which is important in distributed systems.  
- Weakness: empirical scope limited to moderately sized datasets due to base learner scalability, not fully demonstrating large-scale performance.  
- Pro acceptance: significant theoretical advance on an important open problem, novel aggregation method with strong empirical speedups, well-written and rigorous.  
- Con acceptance: impractical processor requirements and Radon-number dependence for high dimensions, plus insufficient communication analysis.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically sound and practically promising with acknowledged limitations; recommended for acceptance.
