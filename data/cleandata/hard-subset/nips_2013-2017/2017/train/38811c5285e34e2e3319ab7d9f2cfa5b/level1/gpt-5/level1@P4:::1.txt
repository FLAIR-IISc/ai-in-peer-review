Summary
The paper proposes a generic, black-box parallelization scheme, the Radon machine, for convex regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean hypothesis spaces. The method trains the base learner independently on rh iid data subsamples, organizes the resulting hypotheses on a balanced r-ary tree of height h, and aggregates bottom-up by computing Radon points at each internal node. Using basic properties of Radon points and quasi-convexity, the paper proves a confidence amplification result: if the base learner yields an ε-good hypothesis with failure probability δ, then after h aggregation levels the failure probability contracts to at most (rδ)2^h (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3). Assuming base sample complexity NL0(ε,δ) of the form (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k and polynomial-time training TL(n)=O(n^κ), the total sample requirement becomes NR0(ε,∆)=r^h NL0(ε,δ) and the parallel runtime is TR,h = TL(n) + h r^3 with n ≥ NL0(ε,δ). Choosing h ≈ log M − log log M (with M the sample size needed for the base learner to reach (ε,∆)), the Radon machine runs in polylogarithmic time in M provided a quasi-polynomial number of processors and r bounded polylogarithmically in M (Theorem 4). The empirical study on classification, regression, and multiclass tasks shows large wall-clock speedups over single-node base learners and competitive accuracy/runtimes vs Spark MLlib baselines; code is provided.

Relation to prior work: The approach differs from averaging-based “train on chunks and aggregate” schemes by using Radon points, which yield stronger confidence amplification than averaging and avoid some of the limitations in prior bounds for averaging. It complements results on parallel PAC learning and the NC-learnability literature (e.g., Vitter & Lin; Long & Servedio) by providing a general aggregation mechanism with theoretical guarantees and discussing its relevance to Nick’s Class. It also contrasts with distributed optimization/SGD approaches that require many synchronization rounds.

Quality
- Theory: The confidence amplification bound is clear and follows from quasi-convexity plus a union bound over pairs, propagating through the aggregation tree. Assumptions (convex hypothesis space, convex loss and regularizer, finite Radon number, iid subsamples) are explicit. The runtime/sample complexity analysis is coherent; computing Radon points via r×r linear systems (O(r^3)) is reasonable. A potential weakness is the looseness of constants in Proposition 2 (combinatorial factors are suppressed), and the requirement δ ≤ 1/(2r) can be demanding in high dimensions (since r=d+2), increasing the per-leaf sample size.
- Experiments: The implementation is practical and shows substantial speedups with similar predictive performance vs both base learners and Spark baselines. However, details about statistical significance (variance across folds), overhead breakdowns (training vs aggregation), and fairness of baselines (e.g., hyperparameter tuning budgets, threading, communication settings) are limited in the main text. The comparison to averaging-at-the-end is appropriate; including a geometric-median or median-of-means aggregation baseline would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The writing is generally clear and well organized. The use of “ld” for logarithm is unconventional; standard log notation would improve readability. The sample complexity template NL0(ε,δ)=(αε+βε ld(1/δ))^k is introduced without much intuition; moving key specializations (e.g., VC/Rademacher-based αε,βε) from the appendix into the main text would help. The conditions for r to be polylogarithmic in sample size (a key premise in Theorem 4’s consequence) deserve more prominent discussion.

Originality
Using Radon points for parallel hypothesis aggregation in generic convex learning, together with a simple composition analysis yielding double-exponential confidence amplification, is novel and interesting. The black-box nature (no algorithm-specific derivations) differentiates it from many distributed optimization methods. The connection to NC is properly framed as a step rather than a resolution.

Significance
The work offers a generally applicable aggregation scheme with provable guarantees and strong practical speedups when data is abundant and d is moderate. Its main limitations stem from the dependence on the Radon number (r=d+2), which leads to quasi-polynomial processor counts and larger sample complexity; for high-dimensional models (e.g., kernels without approximations, or very wide linear models), applicability requires dimensionality reduction or feature embeddings. The NC angle is thus informative but conditional.

Strengths
- Generic, black-box parallelization applicable to many convex ERM/RRM learners.
- Clean theoretical confidence amplification via Radon aggregation; simple and compositional.
- Polylogarithmic runtime under explicit assumptions; low communication at aggregation-only scheme.
- Empirical results show large speedups and competitive accuracy; open-source implementation.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on Radon number r=d+2 induces large data/processor requirements when d is large; δ ≤ 1/(2r) assumption can be onerous.
- Polylog-time guarantee needs quasi-polynomial processors; not NC in the strict sense.
- Probability bounds hide constants; Proposition 2 could state exact combinatorial factors.
- Experimental reporting lacks variance/significance and more diverse robust aggregation baselines.
- Clarity issues: log notation, explicit constants/parameters for common hypothesis classes relegated to appendices.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide tighter versions of Proposition 2 with explicit constants, and discuss their empirical relevance.
- Elaborate (in main text) αε,βε for VC/Rademacher settings and give concrete numbers for common models.
- Add comparisons to robust aggregators (e.g., geometric median, coordinate-wise median, median-of-means) and report statistical significance.
- Quantify the impact of dimensionality reduction (random projections, Nyström/features) on accuracy and the Radon machine’s runtime; include high-d experiments with and without projection.
- Report runtime breakdown (training vs aggregation) and communication/memory overhead; include results for c < r^h processors to validate the scheduling factor.
- Clarify the scope of the NC discussion and explicitly state when r is polylogarithmic in sample complexity (e.g., sparse/low-rank regimes).

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, broadly applicable aggregation mechanism with clear theory and practical benefits.
- Strong empirical speedups with competitive accuracy; code available.
- Provides a useful perspective on parallel learning beyond averaging and distributed optimization.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical applicability limited when d is large; theoretical guarantees rely on strong dimensionality assumptions and quasi-polynomial processors.
- Empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive with robust baselines and statistical analysis.
- Some clarity and constants could be improved in the theoretical presentation.
