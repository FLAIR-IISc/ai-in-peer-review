## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, aiming to reduce runtime to polylogarithmic time on a quasi-polynomial number of processors. The core idea revolves around a “Radon machine” which applies the base learning algorithm in parallel to random subsets of the data and aggregates the results using Radon points, offering a doubly-exponential increase in confidence compared to simple averaging. The authors claim this approach is broadly applicable, requiring no algorithm-specific derivations, and provide empirical validation demonstrating significant speedups compared to standard implementations and Spark’s MLlib.

This work builds upon existing parallelisation techniques like averaging-based methods [32, 45, 46] but distinguishes itself by leveraging Radon points for aggregation, leading to stronger confidence guarantees. It also connects to the theoretical question of efficient parallelisation within Nick’s Class (NC) [9], aiming to address whether consistent regularised risk minimisation can be achieved in polylogarithmic time. The paper appropriately references relevant work in parallel computing [7, 13] and parallel learning [21, 34].

**Strengths:** The paper's primary strength lies in its theoretical contribution – a provably effective parallelisation scheme with a clear runtime analysis. The claim of a black-box approach, applicable to a broad class of algorithms without modification, is compelling. The empirical results, while limited in scope, demonstrate substantial speedups and comparable performance to existing solutions. The connection to the NC complexity class provides a valuable theoretical framing. The writing is generally clear and well-structured, and the inclusion of proofs in the appendix is commendable.

**Weaknesses:** The paper’s focus on theoretical guarantees comes at the expense of detailed practical considerations. The empirical evaluation, while positive, is somewhat limited. The datasets used are not extensively described, and the comparison to Spark MLlib lacks detail regarding specific configurations and parameter tuning. The discussion of communication complexity is brief. The assumption of abundant data and a finite-dimensional hypothesis space might limit the applicability of the scheme in certain scenarios, and the authors acknowledge this but offer only high-level solutions like random projections. The dependence on the Radon number 'r' could become a bottleneck for high-dimensional data. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Novel and theoretically sound parallelisation scheme.
* Provable guarantees on runtime and confidence.
* Potential for significant speedups in practical applications.
* Connection to a fundamental question in parallel computation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* Limited empirical evaluation and dataset description.
* Practical limitations related to high dimensionality and communication costs.
* The theoretical benefits might not translate directly to all real-world scenarios.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened, the theoretical results and the potential for practical speedups make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to expand the empirical evaluation and provide more detailed discussion of practical considerations.
