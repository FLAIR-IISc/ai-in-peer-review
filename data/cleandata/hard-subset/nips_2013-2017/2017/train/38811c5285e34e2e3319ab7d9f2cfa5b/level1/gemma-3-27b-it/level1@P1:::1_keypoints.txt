- Proposes the Radon Machine: parallelise regularised risk minimisation by training on random subsets and aggregating hypotheses via Radon points to boost confidence doubly-exponentially with tree height.  
- Claims polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors, trading increased sample complexity for runtime.  
- Empirical evaluation shows substantial speedups over the base learner and Spark’s MLlib on several datasets.  
- Strong theoretical grounding connecting to Nick’s Class (NC) parallelisation and proving the polylogarithmic runtime guarantee.  
- Novel use of Radon points for aggregation in parallel machine learning, providing theoretical confidence-boosting guarantees.  
- Empirical results support theory, with notable speedups and comparable accuracy to existing parallel frameworks.  
- Includes discussion of communication complexity and consideration of distributed data streams.  
- Mathematical derivations and appendices are clear and well-presented.  
- Does not analyze constant factors hidden in the polylogarithmic runtime, particularly dependence on the Radon number r.  
- Potentially prohibitive dependence on r (linked to hypothesis-space dimensionality) limits applicability to high-dimensional problems.  
- Experimental evaluation focuses on low-dimensional features; performance on truly high-dimensional datasets is underexplored.  
- Comparison with Spark’s MLlib is limited in scope and lacks detailed analysis of performance differences.  
- Ablation study on aggregation tree height h is limited; needs a more systematic exploration of h’s impact on performance and runtime.  
- Practical “deparallelisation” scenario (c < r^h processors) lacks concrete performance analysis and discussion.  
- Connection to averaging-based parallelisation techniques (e.g., bagging) is insufficiently explored and differentiated.  
- Recommendation: accept, contingent on revisions addressing dimensionality impact, broader empirical comparisons, and analysis of hidden constants in runtime.
