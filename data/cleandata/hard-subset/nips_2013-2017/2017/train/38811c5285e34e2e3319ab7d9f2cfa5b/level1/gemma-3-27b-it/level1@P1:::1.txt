This paper proposes a novel parallelisation scheme, dubbed the Radon Machine, for regularised risk minimisation algorithms. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm to random subsets of the data and iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, a technique that provably increases confidence doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. The authors claim this achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processing units, a significant step towards efficient parallelisation of machine learning algorithms, at the cost of increased sample complexity. The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups compared to the base learner and Spark’s MLlib on several datasets.

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical grounding. The authors connect their approach to the challenging problem of achieving Nick’s Class (NC) parallelisation for learning algorithms. The theoretical analysis, establishing the polylogarithmic runtime guarantee, is a key contribution. The use of Radon points, a concept seemingly unexplored in the context of parallel machine learning, appears novel and provides a strong theoretical basis for confidence boosting during aggregation. The empirical results support the theoretical claims, showcasing significant speedups and comparable performance to existing parallel frameworks like Spark’s MLlib. The inclusion of communication complexity discussion and consideration of distributed data streams are forward-looking. The clarity of the mathematical derivations, particularly in the appendices, is commendable.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the constant factors hidden within the polylogarithmic runtime are not discussed. Specifically, the dependence on the Radon number 'r' which is related to the dimensionality of the hypothesis space could be prohibitive for high-dimensional problems. The paper acknowledges this in the discussion, but a more detailed analysis of this limitation would be valuable. The experimental setup, while comprehensive, focuses heavily on datasets where the number of features is much smaller than the number of instances. The performance on truly high-dimensional datasets requires more investigation. The comparison with Spark's MLlib is somewhat limited – only a few algorithms are considered, and a more detailed analysis of the specific reasons for performance differences would be beneficial. The ablation study regarding the choice of 'h' (aggregation tree height) is limited; a more systematic exploration of its impact on performance and runtime would strengthen the empirical evaluation. The practical considerations regarding the “deparallelisation” scenario (c < rh processors) could be expanded, including a more concrete analysis of the performance degradation. Finally, while the paper references related work extensively, the connection to existing averaging-based parallelisation techniques (e.g., bagging) isn't fully explored. A clearer differentiation and a more detailed comparison beyond just runtime and accuracy would be useful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The Radon Machine and the application of Radon points to parallel learning are novel ideas.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The polylogarithmic runtime guarantee is a significant theoretical result.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate substantial speedups.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental challenge in scaling machine learning algorithms to large datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practical Limitations:** The dependence on the Radon number may limit applicability to high-dimensional problems.
*   **Limited High-Dimensional Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation doesn’t sufficiently explore performance on high-dimensional datasets.
*   **Incomplete Comparison with Existing Methods:** The comparison with Spark’s MLlib could be more detailed.
*   **Hidden Constants:** The impact of constant factors in the polylogarithmic runtime is not thoroughly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The theoretical results are strong, and the empirical evaluation is convincing. While the practical limitations and areas for further investigation exist, the novelty and potential impact of the Radon Machine outweigh these concerns.  I recommend **acceptance**, with a request for the authors to address the aforementioned weaknesses in a revised version. Specifically, a more in-depth discussion of the impact of dimensionality on the performance, a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods on a wider range of datasets, and a more detailed analysis of the hidden constants in the runtime complexity would significantly strengthen the paper.
