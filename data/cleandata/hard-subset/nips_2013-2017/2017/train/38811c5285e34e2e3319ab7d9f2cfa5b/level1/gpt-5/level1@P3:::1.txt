Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a generic parallelization scheme, the Radon machine, for a broad class of convex regularized risk minimization algorithms (e.g., linear SVM, ridge regression, logistic regression). The key idea is to (i) train base learners on many i.i.d. data subsets in parallel and (ii) aggregate the resulting hypotheses via iterated Radon points along a tree. Theoretical results show that if the base learner achieves an (ε, δ) guarantee, the iterated Radon aggregation reduces the bad-event probability doubly exponentially in the tree height h: Δ ≤ (r δ)^(2^h), where r is the Radon number (r = d + 2 in Rd). With appropriate choice of h and enough processors (quasi-polynomial in 1/ε and log 1/Δ), the overall runtime to reach a given (ε, Δ) becomes polylogarithmic in the sample complexity that the base learner would need to achieve the same guarantee. Empirically, the method yields substantial wall-clock speedups versus single-node baselines and competitive accuracy against Spark MLlib methods, with runtime comparable to an averaging-at-the-end baseline but better accuracy.

The approach is distinct from model-averaging strategies (e.g., Zinkevich et al. 2010; Ma et al. 2015) and distributed ERM/SGD (e.g., Boyd et al. 2011; Shamir et al. 2014; Zhang et al. 2013), which typically require many communication rounds and cannot achieve polylog time in the NC sense. It also differs from centerpoint/median-of-means-type robust aggregations by leveraging Radon points to obtain explicit concentration improvements for convex performance measures.

Quality:
- Theory: The core bounds (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) are simple but sound, relying on quasi/convexity and Radon’s theorem; the union-bound-based (r δ)^2 step is conservative (binomial factors could tighten constants) but adequate. The argument that the Radon machine attains polylog runtime relative to the sequential sample complexity hinges on choosing h ≈ log M − log log M and on access to r^h processors. The dependency on the Radon number is explicit, and the cost of aggregation is O(h r^3), which is negligible when TL(n) dominates.
- Assumptions and caveats: 
  - The requirement δ ≤ 1/2^r can be satisfied by increasing the per-leaf sample size, but in high dimensions (r = d + 2) this becomes stringent; the paper acknowledges the need for dimensionality reduction/feature mapping.
  - The algorithm description says “divide D into r^h iid subsets” though a partition without replacement is not iid; this should be restated as disjoint subsets drawn from an i.i.d. sample (or use bootstrap to ensure independence).
  - The theoretical section assumes a specific functional form NL0(ε, δ) for sample complexity and TL(n) = O(n^κ). This is reasonable for many ERM learners, but clarifying what classes of loss/regularizer and optimizers satisfy these bounds would strengthen the claims.
- Empirics: The experiments substantiate large speedups with 150 processors and show accuracy at least on par with MLlib and better than averaging. However:
  - The evaluation does not directly probe the (ε, Δ) confidence narrative (AUC is not aligned with the high-confidence guarantees). Including calibration/coverage or PAC-style estimates (e.g., empirical Chernoff bounds) would better connect theory and practice.
  - Sensitivity to h, r, and per-leaf sample size is only partially explored; a controlled study of the accuracy/runtime trade-off as a function of these parameters would be valuable.
  - Comparisons against strong modern distributed ERM/SGD solvers (e.g., CoCoA variants, approximate Newton methods) with thorough tuning would solidify the practical case.
  - Communication costs are discussed qualitatively; reporting actual bytes/time per round would help.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. Definitions of Radon points and the algorithm are clear, and the main results are stated precisely. Minor clarity issues:
- Replace “iid subsets” by “disjoint subsets of an i.i.d. sample” or explicitly sample with replacement.
- Define ld as log2 explicitly; unify log notation.
- Be explicit about when independence assumptions hold across tree levels (disjoint data per leaf ensures independence of leaf hypotheses; higher-level nodes aggregate deterministic functions of disjoint subsets).

Originality:
Using iterated Radon points as an aggregation mechanism for parallel learning appears novel and is a nice geometric insight. The explicit double-exponential reduction in bad-event probability tied to convexity and Radon’s theorem provides a clean alternative to averaging or geometric median approaches. The NC framing is also interesting, though it does require quasi-polynomial processors.

Significance:
The work advances our understanding of when and how ERM can be parallelized with strong guarantees. Practically, the method is attractive as a black-box wrapper with low communication and simple implementation. The main limitation is scalability in high dimensions (r = d + 2) and the need for many processors to achieve the strongest asymptotic claims. Nonetheless, for moderate d and abundant data, the approach is compelling.

Pros:
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable parallelization primitive.
- Provable double-exponential confidence amplification under convexity.
- Clear NC connection and conditions for polylog runtime.
- Strong empirical speedups and accuracy competitive with widely used distributed baselines.
- Black-box applicability with minimal code changes.

Cons:
- Dependence on Radon number makes the method less suitable for very high-dimensional or kernelized models without embeddings.
- Theoretical independence and “iid subset” phrasing need tightening.
- Empirics do not directly validate the (ε, Δ) guarantees; reliance on AUC only.
- Quasi-polynomial number of processors may be large in practice; scalability with c < r^h is briefly treated but not deeply evaluated.
- Comparison set could include more recent/strong distributed ERM/SGD baselines and communication metrics.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify independence assumptions and data splitting; consider bootstrap sampling to adhere to the theory.
- Add experiments varying h and per-leaf n to quantify the trade-off between runtime, accuracy, and implied Δ.
- Report communication volume and aggregation time separately; include sensitivity to d (thereby r).
- Demonstrate applicability to nonlinear models via random features or Nyström and report how r is controlled.
- Tighten constants in Proposition 2 or discuss them to show robustness of the guarantee.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The geometric aggregation idea and its theoretical and empirical support make this a valuable contribution, with manageable revisions to improve rigor and scope.
