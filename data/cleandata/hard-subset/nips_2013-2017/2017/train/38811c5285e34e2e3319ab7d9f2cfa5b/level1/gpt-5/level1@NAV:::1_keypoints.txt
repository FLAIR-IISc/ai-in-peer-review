- Proposal: Radon machine — black-box parallelization for regularized risk minimization that aggregates hypotheses via iterated Radon points instead of averaging.  
- Theoretical claim: Under convexity and finite Radon number (r = d+2), aggregation yields doubly-exponential reduction in ε-bad hypothesis probability with tree height, enabling polylog runtime using quasi-polynomial processors.  
- Assumptions: iid data partitions, fixed finite-dimensional parameterization, δ ≤ 1/(2r), and r = d+2 in Rd.  
- Complexity: Reduction TR,h = TL(n) + h·r^3 with suggested h ≈ log M − log log M; r^3 cost drives aggregation overhead.  
- Soundness: The doubly-exponential confidence amplification and theoretical statements are well supported.  
- Practical limitation: r = d+2 makes the method sensitive to high dimensionality; solving O(r^3) linear systems raises cost and numerical-stability concerns.  
- Stringent requirement: The δ ≤ 1/(2r) condition for base learners may be hard to meet on difficult tasks and deserves empirical analysis.  
- Experiments: Strong empirical speedups reported (80–700×) and favorable runtime-quality trade-offs vs Spark MLlib; code is released.  
- Evaluation gaps: Experiments focus mainly on AUC; calibration/conformal coverage and ablations on δ/r effects are missing.  
- Comparisons: Fairness of Spark MLlib baselines (tuning, communication) should be clarified.  
- Clarity: Paper is well written; Algorithm 1 and Radon-point definition are clear; add a brief reminder of r^3 origin and a complexity table contrasting averaging vs Radon.  
- Originality: Novel use of Radon points for ensemble aggregation and a thoughtful NC-style framing extending prior parallel-learning work.  
- Significance: Broad applicability to convex RRM with substantial speedups, but quasi-polynomial processor needs and r-dependence limit immediate use in very high-dimensional/nonlinear settings.  
- Acceptance pros: Conceptually fresh, theoretically grounded, bridges theory and practice, likely to inspire follow-ups.  
- Acceptance cons: Practical scalability and confidence claims need deeper empirical validation; processor scaling is quasi-polynomial rather than polynomial.
