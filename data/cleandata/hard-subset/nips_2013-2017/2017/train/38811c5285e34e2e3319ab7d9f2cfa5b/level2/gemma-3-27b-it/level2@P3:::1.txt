This paper introduces a novel parallelisation scheme, termed the “Radon machine,” aiming to reduce the runtime of learning algorithms while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The core idea involves applying the underlying learning algorithm to random subsets of the data and aggregating the resulting hypotheses using Radon points. The work claims to achieve polylogarithmic runtime on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units, and demonstrates empirically significant speedups compared to standard and Spark-based implementations on several datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical challenge in modern machine learning – scaling algorithms to large datasets. The theoretical framework, grounding the parallelisation scheme in regularised risk minimisation and leveraging Radon points, is a significant strength. The analysis suggesting a reduction in runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic is compelling, and the connection to Nick’s Class (NC) provides a broader context. The empirical evaluation appears thorough, comparing the Radon machine against both the base learners and Spark’s MLlib. The reported speedups are substantial and the preservation of predictive performance is encouraging. 

However, several areas could benefit from clarification and further exploration. The notion of “effectiveness” – achieving the same confidence and error bounds in shorter time – is central, but the trade-off in sample complexity isn’t always clearly articulated in the empirical results. While the paper mentions a quasi-polynomial increase in data size, it would be valuable to visualise this increase alongside the runtime reduction, demonstrating the overall resource implications.  

The discussion of the Radon number 'r' and its impact on applicability is somewhat limited. While the paper mentions low-dimensional embeddings or block coordinate descent as potential solutions for high-dimensional data, a more detailed discussion of their practical overhead and limitations would be beneficial. The claim that the scheme is “black-box” could be nuanced; while it avoids re-deriving learning algorithm mathematics, the choice of the base learner and parameter tuning still requires expertise. 

Section 3's derivation of sample and runtime complexity relies on assumptions about the base learning algorithm (e.g., sample complexity of the form NL0). While reasonable, it would be strengthened by explicitly stating the implications if the base learner doesn’t conform to this structure. Similarly, the analysis of communication complexity in Section 5 is brief and could be expanded.

The writing is generally clear, but some sections are dense with mathematical notation.  For instance, the proof of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 in the appendix could benefit from more explanatory text. The detailed explanation of the system of linear equations for calculating Radon points in Appendix C.1, while complete, might be more accessible with a more intuitive explanation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The Radon machine presents a new approach to parallelising learning algorithms, distinct from averaging-based methods.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The work is grounded in solid theoretical analysis, providing guarantees on runtime reduction and sample complexity.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments demonstrate significant speedups and comparable performance to existing methods.
*   **Significance:** Addressing the scalability of machine learning algorithms is a crucial challenge, and this work offers a promising approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practical Overhead:** The increased data size and potential overhead of techniques like low-rank approximations need more thorough investigation.
*   **Complexity Assumptions:** The analysis relies on specific assumptions about the base learning algorithm.
*   **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The discussion of applicability to high-dimensional or non-linear models is somewhat superficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper represents a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning.  The theoretical results are compelling, and the empirical validation is encouraging. While some aspects require further clarification and exploration, the potential impact of this work is substantial.  A strong recommendation for acceptance, contingent on addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding the practical implications of increased data size and the robustness of the approach to different base learners. Specifically, requesting that the authors include a figure or table showing the dataset size increase needed to achieve the reported speedups would be highly valuable.
