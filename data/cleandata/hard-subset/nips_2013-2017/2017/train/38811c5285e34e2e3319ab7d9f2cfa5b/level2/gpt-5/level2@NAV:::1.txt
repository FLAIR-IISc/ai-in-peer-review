Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for convex regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean hypothesis spaces. It trains base learners on iid data shards and aggregates models via iterated Radon points on a tree, yielding a double-exponential reduction in error probability with tree height h. Under assumptions (finite Radon number, δ ≤ 1/(2r) per shard), the authors prove that, to match a given (ε, Δ), the scheme can achieve polylogarithmic runtime in the required sample size using quasi-polynomially many processors. Empirically, on several real datasets and with 150 processors, the method attains 80–700× speedups versus single-node base learners and competitive AUC versus Spark MLlib and averaging-at-the-end. This connects to NC-learning (Vitter & Lin) and the open question on efficiently parallel learning (Long & Servedio), and contrasts with averaging and distributed SGD/ADMM which typically require many communication rounds and cannot reach polylog time.

Quality: The theoretical results (Proposition 2, Theorem 3, Theorem 4) are clearly stated, with proofs and assumptions. The confidence amplification via Radon points is novel and plausible. However, the polylog-time result requires r=d+2 to be polylogarithmic in sample complexity and quasi-polynomial processor counts; the empirical section does not validate the δ prerequisite on shards nor the bounds’ tightness, focusing on AUC rather than confidence calibration. Experimental methodology is otherwise solid and code is provided.

Clarity: Well-written and organized; algorithm and assumptions are explicit. Some conditions (e.g., feasibility of δ ≤ 1/(2r) on small shards) could be highlighted earlier, and numerical stability of Radon point computation could be discussed.

Originality: Using iterated Radon points for model aggregation with provable confidence amplification is original relative to averaging, geometric median, and MOM-style schemes, and the NC framing is a fresh angle.

Significance: Practically useful as a black-box scheme when data are abundant and models are low-dimensional; limitations arise for high-dimensional or kernel models without embeddings, and the required processor counts may be unrealistic in some settings.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, general aggregation with formal confidence amplification.
- Clear link to NC-learning; advances theory-practice bridge.
- Strong speedups and competitive accuracy; code available.

Cons for acceptance:
- Polylog-time requires quasi-polynomial processors and d=O(polylog N).
- Empirical evaluation does not assess confidence/error bounds or δ condition.
- Limited coverage of high-dimensional and nonlinear settings in practice.

Constructive suggestions:
- Empirically measure calibration/regret vs h and verify δ ≤ 1/(2r) on shards.
- Compare against geometric median/median-of-means aggregators.
- Report communication and numerical stability; add experiments with limited processors (c<r^h).
- Demonstrate nonlinear models via random features/Nyström and ablations on dimension reduction.
