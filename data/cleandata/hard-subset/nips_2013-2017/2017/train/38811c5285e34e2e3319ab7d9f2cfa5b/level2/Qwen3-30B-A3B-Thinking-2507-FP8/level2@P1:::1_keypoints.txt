- Introduces the "Radon Machine", a novel parallelization scheme using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses for regularized risk minimization.  
- Key insight: aggregation via Radon points yields a doubly exponential increase in confidence per aggregation level.  
- Theoretical claim: can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in sample complexity using quasi-polynomially many processors and extra samples.  
- Strong theoretical contribution linking parallel learning to Nick's Class (NC) and addressing an open question about efficient parallel learning.  
- Analysis and proofs (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) are sound and clearly presented.  
- Distinguishes from averaging-based parallelization, showing averaging cannot achieve polylogarithmic runtime.  
- Empirical results: 80–700× speedups on 150 processors while maintaining comparable predictive performance.  
- Thorough comparisons against Spark MLlib and averaging-at-the-end baselines, showing both speed and quality advantages.  
- Strengths: clear theory, well-structured presentation, black-box applicability requiring no changes to base learners.  
- Novel use of convex geometry and Radon's theorem to frame parallel aggregation.  
- Practical limitation: requires quasi-polynomially many processors — practical implications not fully explored.  
- Dimensionality concern: Radon number r = d+2 implies large r for high-dimensional data, potentially making the approach impractical for d ≫ 1,000.  
- Suggested mitigation (low-rank approximations) is mentioned but not elaborated or empirically validated for high-dimensional settings.  
- Implementation detail gap: requires knowing the Radon number for the hypothesis space; more concrete examples of determining it per algorithm are needed.  
- Empirical scope: promising results on a 5M-instance SUSY dataset, but additional experiments on larger datasets and with fixed realistic processor counts (100–1,000) would strengthen practical claims.  
- Presentation and related work: well-written, logically organized, and effectively situated within parallel learning and complexity literature.  
- Overall significance: important theoretical advance with practical promise; main barrier is processor-count requirement — paper recommends acceptance.
