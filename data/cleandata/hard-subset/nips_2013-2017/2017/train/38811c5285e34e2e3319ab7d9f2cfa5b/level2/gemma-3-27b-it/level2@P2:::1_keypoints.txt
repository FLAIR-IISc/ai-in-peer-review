- Proposes the "Radon machine" to parallelize regularized risk minimization, aiming for polylogarithmic runtime with quasi-polynomial processors.  
- Radon machine applies the base learner to random data subsets and aggregates models using Radon points.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees for the proposed scheme.  
- Empirical results report significant speedups versus standard approaches and Spark MLlib.  
- Paper is well-motivated and situates itself within parallel computation and learning theory literature.  
- Novel application of Radon points for model aggregation in machine learning.  
- Theoretical analysis is abstract and hard to follow without deep mathematical background.  
- Unclear how increased data size affects practical performance; potential real-world bottleneck not fully explored.  
- Discussion of the Radon number r and its dependence on hypothesis-space dimension is hand-wavy and needs concreteness.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited in dataset diversity and lacks very high-dimensional datasets.  
- Comparison to Spark MLlib is superficial; lacks explanation of why Radon machine outperforms and which cases benefit most.  
- Communication costs and distributed-environment overheads are mentioned but not analyzed in sufficient detail.  
- Claim of being "black-box" is misleading because it requires knowledge of properties (e.g., Radon number) of the hypothesis space.  
- Needs clearer distinction and justification of improvement over existing averaging-based parallelization methods.  
- Work could be significant if theoretical guarantees hold in practice and overheads are manageable, but this is not yet demonstrated.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel idea, theoretical polylog guarantees, empirical speedups, clear problem presentation.  
- Rejection arguments: abstract theory, limited empirical scope, missing trade-off and communication analysis, misleading black-box claim, unclear practical impact of polylog runtime.  
- Overall recommendation: Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after deeper empirical evaluation, clearer theoretical exposition, and more detailed analysis of overheads and applicability.
