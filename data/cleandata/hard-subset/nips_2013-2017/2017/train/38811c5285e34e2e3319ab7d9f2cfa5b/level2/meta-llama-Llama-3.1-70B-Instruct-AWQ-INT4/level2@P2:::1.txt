This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, which simplifies the adaptation of learning algorithms to growing amounts of data and provides theoretical performance guarantees. The scheme, called the Radon machine, can be applied to a broad class of learning algorithms without further mathematical derivations and without writing dedicated code.

The main theoretical contribution of this paper is to show that algorithms satisfying certain conditions can be parallelised effectively. The Radon machine achieves the same confidence and error bounds as the sequential execution of the algorithm in much shorter time, reducing the runtime of learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic. The cost of this parallelisation is in the form of an increased data size and in the number of processing units used.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the Radon machine, including its sample and runtime complexity. The authors also present an empirical evaluation of the Radon machine, comparing it to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms from the Spark machine learning library. The results show that the Radon machine achieves a substantial reduction of computation time in realistic applications, while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The paper is well-written and well-organised, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the Radon machine and its theoretical and practical contributions. The paper also provides a thorough discussion of related work and future directions.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, which provides theoretical performance guarantees and can be applied to a broad class of learning algorithms.
* The Radon machine achieves a substantial reduction of computation time in realistic applications, while maintaining comparable predictive performance.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the Radon machine, including its sample and runtime complexity.
* The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the Radon machine and its theoretical and practical contributions.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes that the data is abundant and that the hypothesis space can be parametrised in a fixed, finite dimensional Euclidean space. This assumption may not hold in all cases, and the authors should discuss the limitations of their approach.
* The paper does not provide a detailed comparison of the Radon machine with other parallelisation schemes, such as stochastic gradient descent. The authors should provide a more thorough discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of their approach compared to other methods.
* The empirical evaluation of the Radon machine is limited to a few datasets and learning algorithms. The authors should provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their approach using a wider range of datasets and learning algorithms.
