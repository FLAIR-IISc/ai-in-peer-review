This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, which can be applied to a broad class of learning algorithms without further mathematical derivations and without writing dedicated code. The scheme, called the Radon machine, is able to reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units. This is a significant step towards a general answer to an open question on the efficient parallelisation of machine learning algorithms in the sense of Nick's Class (NC).

The paper is well-written and clearly organised, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the work and explains the significance of the results. The technical sections are well-structured and provide a thorough analysis of the Radon machine.

The main theoretical contribution of the paper is to show that algorithms satisfying certain conditions can be parallelised effectively. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the sample and runtime complexity of the Radon machine and compare it to the base learning algorithm. The results show that the Radon machine can achieve the same guarantee as the base learning algorithm in polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units.

The empirical evaluation of the Radon machine confirms its potential in practical settings. The results show that the Radon machine can achieve a substantial reduction in computation time compared to the base learning algorithm, while maintaining similar predictive performance. The comparison with state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms from the Spark machine learning library shows that the Radon machine can achieve similar predictive performance while requiring only 15-85% of their runtime.

The paper also discusses the limitations of the Radon machine and potential future work. The authors note that the basic version of the parallelisation scheme presented in this paper cannot directly be applied to cases in which the size of the dataset is not at least a multiple of the Radon number of the hypothesis space. However, they suggest that random projections or low-rank approximations can alleviate this problem.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning and provides a thorough analysis of the Radon machine. The results show that the Radon machine can achieve a substantial reduction in computation time while maintaining similar predictive performance, making it a promising approach for large-scale machine learning tasks.
