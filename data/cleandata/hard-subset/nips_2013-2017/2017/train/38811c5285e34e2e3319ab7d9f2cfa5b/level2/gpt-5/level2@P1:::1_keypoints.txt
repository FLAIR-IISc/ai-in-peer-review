- Proposes a black-box parallelization scheme ("Radon machine") that trains base learners on iid subsets and aggregates via Radon points instead of averaging.  
- Provable guarantee: under convexity and finite Radon number r, failure probability of aggregated hypothesis is Δ = (rδ)^{2^h}, i.e., doubly-exponential decay in tree height h.  
- With sample complexity NL0(ε,δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k and TL(n) ∈ O(n^κ), claims polylogarithmic runtime in target sample size M using c = r^h quasi-polynomial processors if r is polylogarithmic in M (Theorem 4).  
- Empirical results: up to 80–700× wall-clock speedups vs single-node base learners and competitive AUC vs Spark MLlib on several large datasets with ≤150 processors.  
- Novelty: replaces averaging with Radon aggregation to obtain rapidly improving high-probability bounds; connects distributed ERM to parallel (NC) complexity.  
- Black-box applicability: works for a broad class of convex regularized ERM algorithms without changing optimization code.  
- Strength: clear theoretical bounds (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) and NC framing (Theorem 4).  
- Strength: practical discussion of communication, limited processors, high-dimensional settings, and feature embeddings for non-linear models.  
- Weakness: practicality of Theorem 4 hinges on r = d+2 being polylog(M); for high-dimensional problems d often scales with M, making required c = r^h impractical.  
- Weakness: method increases total data usage N = r^h·n; paper lacks empirical evaluation of sample-complexity inflation vs. confidence gains.  
- Weakness: Radon point computation cost O(r^3) per node lacks discussion of numerical stability, degeneracies, and practical algorithms for Radon partitions.  
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to other robust aggregators (e.g., geometric median, median-of-means) that could offer similar robustness properties.  
- Weakness: Baseline fairness concerns—need more details on hyperparameter tuning, convergence criteria, and resource allocation for Spark/Weka/scikit comparisons.  
- Weakness: Experiments are almost entirely linear models; lacks concrete non-linear case (e.g., explicit random feature maps).  
- Clarity: paper is generally clear; request to expand Radon computation details, add a symbol table, and clarify n vs N vs M.  
- Evaluation: theory is sound but the crucial r polylog(M) assumption should be highlighted and discussed in typical ML regimes.  
- Questions for authors: provide concrete scaling examples for d ∈ {50,500,5000} showing required h, c=r^h, and resulting N,n.  
- Questions for authors: report sensitivity and mitigation of numerical issues when computing Radon points.  
- Questions for authors: include comparisons to geometric median or median-of-means aggregation.  
- Questions for authors: empirically demonstrate the doubly-exponential improvement in Δ as h increases (e.g., via holdout estimates).  
- Pros for acceptance: novel, elegant theory with black-box applicability and promising empirical speedups.  
- Cons for acceptance: practical constraints (dimension, processor count), missing empirical validation of confidence gains and sample-overhead tradeoffs, and lack of other robust-aggregator baselines.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — theoretically interesting and empirically promising, but needs stronger practical validation and additional comparisons.
