- Proposes the "Radon machine": train base learners on many small disjoint subsets in parallel and hierarchically aggregate hypotheses via Radon points instead of averaging.  
- Aggregation via Radon points yields doubly-exponentially improving confidence bounds with aggregation-tree height (Theorem 3).  
- Claims polylogarithmic runtime for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms at the cost of quasi-polynomially many processors and increased sample complexity.  
- Frames the work in the context of NC-style parallelizability of machine learning.  
- Originality/significance: using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation is novel and theoretically powerful.  
- Quality: technically sound with solid proofs; experiments show 80–700x speedups and competitiveness with Spark MLlib and averaging baselines.  
- Clarity: paper is very well-written, algorithm and theory are clearly and intuitively presented.  
- Practicality concern: dependence on the Radon number (d+2) makes required base models scale with feature dimension, limiting applicability to high-dimensional problems.  
- Theoretical assumptions concern: reliance on quasi-polynomial processors deviates from standard NC (polynomial processors) and the increased sample complexity is a significant trade-off.  
- Overall: valuable theoretical and empirical contribution, but dimensional dependence and resource/sample trade-offs limit practical applicability.
