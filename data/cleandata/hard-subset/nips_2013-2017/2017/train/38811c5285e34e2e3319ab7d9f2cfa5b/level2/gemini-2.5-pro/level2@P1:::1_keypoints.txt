- Proposes the "Radon Machine," a black-box parallelization scheme that partitions data, trains base learners on subsets, and aggregates hypotheses via iterated Radon points in a tree.  
- Proves aggregation yields a doubly-exponential reduction in failure probability with tree height (Theorem 3), enabling confidence boosting.  
- Shows under many processors the scheme can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic for a broad class of learners (Theorem 4), at the cost of increased sample complexity.  
- Empirically demonstrates substantial speedups (up to ~700x) and competitive accuracy versus sequential learners and Spark MLlib across multiple datasets.  
- Technical quality is high: elegant theoretical analysis that ties Radon-point properties to parallel learning complexity.  
- Presentation is clear and well-organized, with stepwise theoretical development and transparent experiments.  
- Originality is strong: novel use of iterated Radon points combining computational geometry and statistical learning theory.  
- Significance is both theoretical (advances toward NC learnability) and practical (black-box parallelization of existing algorithms).  
- Major concern: dependence on Radon number r = d+2 makes the approach potentially impractical in high-dimensional settings.  
- Questionable practicality due to base-learner requirement δ ≤ 1/(2r), which may be hard to meet when r is large and each learner sees little data.  
- Empirical advantage over simple averaging is consistent but modest; clearer characterization of regimes where Radon aggregation outperforms averaging is needed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically solid, practical black-box utility, and strong empirical speedups.  
- Argument against acceptance: limited practical applicability in high-dimensional problems due to dependence on r, which needs more prominent discussion.
