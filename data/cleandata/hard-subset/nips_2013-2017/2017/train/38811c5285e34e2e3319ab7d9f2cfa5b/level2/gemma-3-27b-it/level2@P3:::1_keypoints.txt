- Introduces the "Radon machine," a novel parallelisation scheme applying learning algorithms to random data subsets and aggregating hypotheses via Radon points.  
- Claims polylogarithmic runtime using a quasi-polynomial number of processors while preserving theoretical performance guarantees.  
- Empirical results show substantial speedups over standard and Spark MLlib implementations with comparable predictive performance.  
- Well-motivated: tackles scalability of learning algorithms on large datasets.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: grounded in regularised risk minimisation and leverages Radon points with runtime/sample complexity analysis.  
- Links runtime improvements to complexity classes (Nick’s Class), providing broader theoretical context.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing both base learners and distributed baselines.  
- Central concept of “effectiveness” (same confidence/error in less time) is useful but the sample complexity trade-off is not clearly shown in experiments.  
- Paper should visualise the quasi-polynomial increase in data size alongside runtime reduction to clarify overall resource implications.  
- Discussion of the Radon number r and its impact on applicability is limited; practical overheads of suggested fixes (low-rank embeddings, block coordinate descent) need more detail.  
- The claim of being a “black-box” method is overstated—choice of base learner and parameter tuning still require expertise.  
- Analysis in Section 3 depends on base-learner assumptions (e.g., sample complexity NL0); implications if these don’t hold should be stated.  
- Communication complexity analysis (Section 5) is brief and would benefit from expansion.  
- Some sections are dense with notation; proofs (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) need more explanatory text for accessibility.  
- Appendix C.1’s linear-system derivation for Radon points is complete but would benefit from a more intuitive explanation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty of approach, solid theoretical guarantees, strong empirical validation, and high significance for ML scalability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: practical overhead from increased data, reliance on specific complexity assumptions, and superficial discussion of limitations for high-dimensional/non-linear models.  
- Overall: significant and promising work with a strong accept recommendation contingent on addressing practical implications of data-size increase and robustness to different base learners (e.g., include a figure/table showing dataset size needed for reported speedups).
