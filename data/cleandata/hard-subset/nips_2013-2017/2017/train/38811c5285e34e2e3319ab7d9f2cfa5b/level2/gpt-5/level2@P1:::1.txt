Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization, termed the Radon machine. The key idea is to train base learners independently on many iid data subsets and aggregate hypotheses along a tree using Radon points rather than averaging. Under convexity and finite Radon number (e.g., F ⊆ R^d → r = d+2), the authors prove that the probability of returning an ε-bad hypothesis decreases doubly-exponentially with tree height h: if a base learner has failure probability δ, the aggregated hypothesis has failure probability Δ = (rδ)^{2^h}. With an assumed sample complexity form NL0(ε,δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k and runtime TL(n) ∈ O(n^κ), the scheme achieves polylogarithmic runtime in the target sample size M on quasi-polynomially many processors c = r^h, provided r is upper-bounded by a polylogarithmic function of M (Theorem 4). Empirically, with ≤150 processors, the method reports 80–700× speedups over single-node base learners and competitive AUC vs Spark MLlib algorithms on several large datasets.

This work sits at the intersection of distributed learning and parallel complexity. It complements prior NIPS work on averaging-based parallelization, distributed SGD and ADMM, and the NC-learnability literature (e.g., Vitter & Lin; Long & Servedio). The novelty is replacing averaging with Radon point aggregation to obtain provable, rapidly improving confidence bounds, and analyzing conditions under which runtime can be polylogarithmic in sample size.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: uses Radon points for hypothesis aggregation, yielding clean, general, and robust bounds on failure probability for convex risks (Proposition 2, Theorem 3).
- Black-box applicability: applies to a broad class of convex regularized ERM algorithms without modifying optimization code.
- Clear connection to parallel complexity (NC): Theorem 4 frames when polylogarithmic runtime is achievable (with quasi-polynomial processors).
- Empirical evidence: consistent wall-clock improvements on realistic datasets and implementations; comparisons to both averaging-at-the-end and Spark baselines.
- Practical considerations: discussion of communication complexity, limited processors, high-dimensional settings, and non-linear models via feature embeddings.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Processor scaling and dimensionality: Achieving the theoretical polylog-time regime requires c = r^h processors with r = d+2. Theorem 4 assumes r is polylog in M; in many ML tasks d is large and can scale with M (e.g., text, kernels), making r^h enormous and undermining practicality unless aggressive dimensionality reduction is feasible. Please clarify the range of (d, M, h) where the quasi-polynomial processor requirement is tractable, with concrete numbers.
- Sample complexity overhead: The method trades runtime for increased data usage N = r^h·n. The empirical section does not directly evaluate the sample complexity inflation or the promised doubly-exponential confidence improvement in practice. A controlled study of error vs. Δ, n, h (and comparison to averaging) would strengthen the case.
- Radon point computation: The cost is stated as O(r^3) per node, but numerical stability and degeneracies (e.g., near-collinearity) are not discussed. Practical algorithms to find Radon partitions and handle degeneracy should be detailed (even in the appendix), including conditioning and runtime in higher d.
- Comparisons: Averaging-at-the-end is a natural baseline, but alternatives like geometric median or median-of-means aggregators (robust to outliers and heavy tails) are not considered. These may share some of the robustness properties attributed to Radon points; including such baselines would clarify the specific advantage of Radon aggregation.
- Fairness of baselines: Spark algorithms and Weka/scikit implementations may differ in optimization details and regularization. Please provide more details on hyperparameter tuning, convergence criteria, and resource allocation to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Scope of models: Although the theory targets convex settings, experiments are almost entirely linear. The paper would benefit from an additional non-linear case via explicit feature maps (e.g., random features) to illustrate broader applicability.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The main theoretical claims are well-motivated and appear sound; the convexity of Q(f) is justified. The crucial assumption (r polylog M) for Theorem 4 should be made more prominent and discussed in terms of typical ML regimes. Empirical evaluations are thorough on classification tasks but do not probe the confidence improvements or sample-overhead trade-offs.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Algorithm 1 is easy to follow. Please expand the Radon point computation details and discuss numerical issues. A table of symbols and a clearer separation between n, N, M would help readability.
- Originality: High. Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation in distributed ERM with explicit high-probability guarantees and NC framing is novel.
- Significance: Mixed. The theoretical contribution is interesting and could influence thinking about parallel ERM. Practical significance depends on dimensionality and the number of processors; nevertheless, the observed speedups with modest processor counts are promising.

Questions for the authors:
1. Can you provide concrete scaling examples: for d ∈ {50, 500, 5000}, what h and c = r^h are needed to reach Δ from a typical δ, and what are the resulting N, n? A plot of Δ vs. (h, r, n) would be helpful.
2. How sensitive are results to numerical issues in Radon point computation? Did you encounter instability, and how was it mitigated?
3. Could you compare against geometric median or median-of-means aggregation?
4. Do you observe the doubly-exponential improvement in Δ empirically (e.g., via concentration bounds or holdout estimates) as h increases?

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, elegant idea with solid theory linking distributed aggregation to strong confidence improvements.
- Black-box applicability to many convex learners.
- Empirical speedups with competitive accuracy.

Cons for acceptance:
- Practical constraints (dimension, processors) limit when theoretical guarantees bite.
- Missing empirical validation of the confidence improvements and sample-complexity tradeoffs.
- Lack of comparisons to other robust aggregators.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a fresh and theoretically grounded perspective on parallelizing learning via Radon points, with encouraging empirical results. Addressing the practical concerns and adding comparisons to robust aggregation baselines would further strengthen the submission.
