

The paper presents a parallelization scheme called the "Radon machine" that enables efficient parallel execution of a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The main theoretical contribution demonstrates that the scheme reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processors while maintaining theoretical error guarantees, albeit with increased sample complexity. The approach treats the underlying learning algorithm as a black box, requiring no additional mathematical derivations or dedicated code for parallelization. The Radon machine works by dividing data into subsets, applying the base learning algorithm in parallel to obtain weak hypotheses, and then iteratively aggregating these hypotheses using Radon points, which provides doubly-exponential confidence improvement with the height of the aggregation tree.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning, particularly in relation to Nick's Class (NC) of efficiently parallelizable problems. It addresses an open question about whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be achieved in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors. The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear proofs of the key propositions and theorems that establish the error bound guarantees and runtime complexity. The empirical evaluation demonstrates practical utility, showing 80-700x speedups compared to base learning algorithms on various datasets while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The comparison to Spark MLlib implementations shows the Radon machine achieves similar or better predictive quality with substantially reduced runtime.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured presentation of the theoretical concepts and algorithm. The Radon point definition and aggregation process are clearly explained, and the connection between the theoretical guarantees and empirical results is well-maintained. The discussion of limitations and future work, including considerations for high-dimensional data and non-linear models, demonstrates a thoughtful understanding of the broader applicability of the approach.

Regarding originality, the paper distinguishes itself from prior work on parallel learning, particularly by using Radon points instead of averaging for aggregation. The paper properly situates its contribution within the literature, noting that prior averaging-based approaches do not achieve polylogarithmic runtime guarantees. The black-box nature of the parallelization scheme is a notable practical contribution that differentiates it from other approaches requiring algorithm-specific parallelization.

The significance of the work is substantial, addressing a fundamental challenge in machine learning parallelization. The theoretical results provide meaningful bounds on the trade-off between runtime, sample complexity, and error guarantees. The empirical results demonstrate practical relevance across multiple learning tasks (classification, regression, multi-class) and datasets of varying sizes. The work has clear potential for adoption in real-world machine learning systems where parallel computation is needed for large-scale data.

Minor weaknesses include the somewhat limited discussion of the practical implications of the quasi-polynomial processor requirement, which may be challenging to realize in typical distributed computing environments. The paper acknowledges this limitation but could provide more concrete guidance on how to handle scenarios with fewer processors. Additionally, while the Radon number is bounded for Euclidean spaces (r = d+2), the paper could more explicitly address how this impacts practical implementation for high-dimensional problems.

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental theoretical question with practical implications, and it makes significant contributions to both the theoretical understanding of parallel learning and practical implementation of parallel machine learning. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and makes a meaningful advance over previous approaches to parallel machine learning.

The theoretical results provide a step toward answering an open question about efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms, and the empirical results confirm the practical potential of the approach. The Radon machine's black-box nature makes it broadly applicable to many existing learning algorithms without requiring algorithm-specific modifications. The comparison to Spark MLlib implementations demonstrates tangible practical benefits in terms of speed without sacrificing predictive performance.

The paper's contribution is sufficiently distinct from prior NIPS work, particularly from the theoretical treatments of parallel learning by Vitter and Lin, and Long and Servedio. The Radon machine's use of Radon points for aggregation rather than averaging represents a novel technical approach that enables the theoretical guarantees the paper establishes.

Overall, the paper makes a strong contribution to the field with both theoretical and practical significance. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the practical implications are well-demonstrated. The minor limitations noted do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution to understanding and enabling efficient parallel machine learning.
