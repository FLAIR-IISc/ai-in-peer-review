## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces a novel parallelisation scheme for learning algorithms based on iterative Radon point aggregation. The core idea is to achieve polylogarithmic runtime on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units, addressing a longstanding question about efficient parallelisation in machine learning, potentially relating to Nick’s Class (NC). The authors argue their approach is broadly applicable, requiring no algorithm-specific derivations, and provides theoretical performance guarantees while demonstrating empirical speedups on several datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the need for efficient parallelisation, especially in high-stakes applications. The theoretical contribution – reducing runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic – is significant *if* the conditions hold, specifically the requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processors and a potentially larger sample complexity. The novelty lies in applying Radon points for hypothesis aggregation, providing a doubly-exponential confidence boost compared to averaging methods.  The empirical evaluation, using Spark and WEKA/scikit-learn, provides initial support for the proposed scheme, showcasing substantial speedups (up to 700x) compared to sequential execution and competitive performance against existing Spark MLlib implementations.

However, the practical applicability and cost of the approach require further scrutiny. The theoretical requirement of a quasi-polynomial number of processors is a significant hurdle. While the authors acknowledge the communication complexity, a detailed analysis of this cost, particularly in a realistic distributed environment, is lacking. The empirical evaluation, while encouraging, is limited in scope. The datasets used are relatively modest in size, and the comparisons to Spark MLlib are not exhaustive. More detailed analysis of the impact of the Radon number 'r' and the aggregation parameter 'h' on performance and scalability would be valuable.  The discussion around handling high-dimensional data via embeddings feels somewhat cursory; a more thorough treatment of this aspect would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The theoretical results are interesting and potentially impactful, contributing to a fundamental question in machine learning. The empirical results provide preliminary evidence of the effectiveness of the scheme. The black-box nature of the approach is appealing.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The practical requirements (quasi-polynomial processors) are demanding. The empirical evaluation is limited. The analysis of communication costs is missing. The treatment of high-dimensional data is insufficient. 

**Overall:**  This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution with promising initial empirical validation. While the practical limitations are significant, the potential benefits warrant consideration. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically expanding the empirical evaluation to include larger datasets, providing a more detailed analysis of communication costs, and elaborating on the handling of high-dimensional data. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
