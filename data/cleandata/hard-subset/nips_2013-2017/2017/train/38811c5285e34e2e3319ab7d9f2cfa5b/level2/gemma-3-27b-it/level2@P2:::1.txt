This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms, aiming to reduce runtime to polylogarithmic time with a quasi-polynomial number of processors. The core idea is the "Radon machine," which applies the base learning algorithm to random data subsets and aggregates the results using Radon points. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the scheme and empirical results demonstrating significant speedups compared to standard approaches and Spark MLlib.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the critical need for efficient parallelization in machine learning. The proposed Radon machine is a clever application of Radon points for aggregation, offering a theoretically sound approach to achieve polylogarithmic runtime. The inclusion of empirical validation is valuable, and the comparison to Spark MLlib provides a practical benchmark. The paper clearly situates its work within the broader context of parallel computation and learning theory, referencing relevant work by Nick, Vitter, Long & Servedio, and others.

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. The theoretical analysis, while present, feels somewhat abstract and difficult to fully grasp without deep familiarity with the underlying mathematical tools.  The explanation of how the increase in data size affects practical performance isn’t fully explored – is the increased data size a major bottleneck in real-world scenarios? The discussion of the Radon number 'r' and its dependency on the hypothesis space dimension is crucial, but remains a bit hand-wavy. A more concrete discussion of how this impacts applicability for various models would strengthen the paper. 

The empirical section, while showcasing speedups, lacks a deeper analysis of the trade-offs. The datasets used are somewhat limited in scope. Expanding the evaluation to more diverse datasets – including very high-dimensional ones – is critical.  Furthermore, the comparison to Spark MLlib could be more thorough. A deeper dive into *why* the Radon machine outperforms Spark, beyond simply stating the runtime difference, would be beneficial.  Are there specific datasets or model types where the advantage is most pronounced? The communication costs of the Radon machine are mentioned briefly but deserve more detailed consideration, especially in distributed environments. The claim that the scheme is "black-box" is potentially misleading; while it doesn't require changes to the *implementation* of the base learner, it still necessitates knowing properties like the Radon number of the hypothesis space. This isn't a trivial requirement.

Regarding originality, the application of Radon points to machine learning aggregation appears novel. However, the broader area of averaging-based parallelization is well-established. The paper needs to more clearly delineate its contribution as a significant improvement *over* existing averaging methods, particularly in achieving the polylogarithmic runtime.

The significance of the work lies in its potential to bridge the gap between theoretical parallel computation models (like NC) and practical machine learning algorithms. If the theoretical guarantees hold in practice, and the practical overheads (data size, communication) are manageable, this could be a significant step forward. However, this potential is not yet fully demonstrated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Radon points for parallel learning.
*   Theoretical guarantees for polylogarithmic runtime.
*   Demonstrated speedups compared to existing approaches.
*   Clear presentation of the problem and its context.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Theoretical analysis is abstract and difficult to follow.
*   Limited scope of empirical evaluation.
*   Lack of detailed trade-off analysis (data size, communication).
*   The claim of being "black-box" is potentially misleading.
*   Needs stronger justification of the practical impact of polylogarithmic runtime.



**Overall Assessment:**  Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper presents a promising idea with strong theoretical foundations, but the practical implications and limitations need to be explored more thoroughly.  A more comprehensive empirical evaluation, a clearer discussion of the overheads, and a more nuanced analysis of the theoretical guarantees are essential for elevating this work to a publishable standard for NIPS. The authors should also address the comments regarding the "black-box" nature of the approach and provide a more detailed comparison to related work on averaging-based parallelization.
