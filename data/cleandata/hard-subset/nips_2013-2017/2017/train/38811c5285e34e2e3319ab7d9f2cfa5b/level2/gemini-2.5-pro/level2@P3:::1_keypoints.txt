- Introduces the Radon machine: train base learners on disjoint subsets, hierarchically aggregate hypotheses using the Radon point instead of averaging.  
- Theoretical claim: aggregation reduces the probability of high-error hypotheses doubly-exponentially with tree height.  
- Proposition 2: single Radon aggregation gives a quadratic reduction in error probability; extends via Theorem 3 to hierarchical aggregation and Theorem 4 for complexity.  
- Shows many learning algorithms can be parallelized to polylogarithmic time assuming a quasi-polynomial number of processors, relating to NC.  
- Empirical results: substantial speed-ups (80–700x) over sequential learners and competitive or faster than Spark MLlib on multiple datasets.  
- Quality: high-quality work with rigorous proofs, clear assumptions (convexity, finite Radon number), thorough experiments, and reproducible code.  
- Clarity: well-written and organized; clear algorithm description and logical progression from definitions to theory and experiments.  
- Originality: novel use of Radon points from computational geometry for hypothesis aggregation and a distinctive connection to parallel complexity theory.  
- Theoretical significance: makes tangible progress toward parallel complexity of learning by achieving polylogarithmic runtime under strong processor assumptions.  
- Practical significance: black-box design enables scaling existing convex learners without redesign; strong empirical performance suggests broad applicability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: high novelty, strong theoretical contributions, excellent practical potential, and clear, complete presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: main theoretical result depends on a quasi-polynomial number of processors (not polynomial as in NC); scalability hampered by Radon number r = d+2 in high dimensions.  
- Suggestions for improvement: more prominently emphasize the gap between quasi-polynomial and polynomial processor requirements; highlight the dimensionality-related limitations earlier in the paper.
