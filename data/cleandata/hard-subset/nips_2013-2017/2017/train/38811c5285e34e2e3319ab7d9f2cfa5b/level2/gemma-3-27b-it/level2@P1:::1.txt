This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of learning algorithms, aiming to reduce runtime to polylogarithmic time with a cost of increased sample complexity. The core idea is the “Radon machine,” which leverages Radon points for aggregating hypotheses computed on random subsets of the data. The authors provide theoretical guarantees and empirical validation demonstrating significant speedups compared to both sequential learning and existing parallel approaches like Spark’s MLlib.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial challenge in modern machine learning: scaling algorithms to massive datasets. The problem of efficiently parallelizing learning algorithms is indeed important, and the connection to Nick’s Class (NC) provides a compelling theoretical framework. The authors clearly articulate the trade-off between runtime and sample complexity, which is a fundamental aspect of any parallelization strategy. The use of Radon points as an aggregation technique is a novel and interesting idea, and the theoretical analysis demonstrates its potential for achieving polylogarithmic runtime. The empirical results are encouraging, showing substantial speedups in practice. The comparison with Spark’s MLlib is valuable, demonstrating the competitiveness of the proposed approach.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, the theoretical analysis relies on the assumption of a finite Radon number for the hypothesis space. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest using dimensionality reduction techniques for cases where this assumption is not met, the practical implications of this requirement are not fully explored. The reliance on Euclidean spaces and convex functions also limits the applicability of the method. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of scenarios where the Radon number is large or infinite and how these scenarios impact performance.

Second, the empirical evaluation, while promising, is somewhat limited in scope. The experiments primarily focus on moderately sized datasets and a relatively small number of algorithms. While the comparison with Spark’s MLlib is valuable, it would be beneficial to see the performance of the Radon machine on a wider range of datasets and algorithms, including more complex models and high-dimensional data. Furthermore, the communication costs, though acknowledged, are not quantified or thoroughly analyzed. In a distributed computing environment, communication can often be a significant bottleneck.

Third, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical challenges associated with implementing the Radon machine in a distributed setting. Issues such as data partitioning, synchronization, and fault tolerance are not addressed. While the authors mention the possibility of using fewer processors than the theoretical optimum, they do not provide concrete guidance on how to optimize the implementation for different hardware configurations.

Fourth, the notation is occasionally dense and could benefit from more clarity. While the authors provide definitions for key concepts, the overall presentation could be more accessible to a wider audience.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically grounded parallelization scheme.
*   Potential for achieving polylogarithmic runtime.
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating significant speedups.
*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in machine learning.
*   Clear articulation of the trade-offs between runtime and sample complexity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on the assumption of a finite Radon number.
*   Limited scope of the empirical evaluation.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of practical implementation challenges.
*   Dense notation and occasional lack of clarity.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The Radon machine is a novel and promising approach with strong theoretical foundations and encouraging empirical results. However, the limitations regarding the Radon number assumption, limited empirical scope, and lack of practical implementation details prevent me from giving a strong recommendation for acceptance. I recommend **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised above, particularly expanding the empirical evaluation and providing a more detailed discussion of the practical considerations. A more thorough exploration of the limitations and potential extensions would significantly strengthen the paper.
