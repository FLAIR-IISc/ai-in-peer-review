

This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme called the "Radon Machine" that aims to efficiently parallelize a broad class of learning algorithms, particularly regularized risk minimization approaches. The key insight is using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses from parallel runs of the base learning algorithm, achieving a doubly exponential increase in confidence with each aggregation level. The authors prove that this scheme can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity, requiring quasi-polynomially many processors and increased sample complexity.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by connecting machine learning parallelization to Nick's Class (NC), addressing an open question about efficient parallel learning. The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear proofs for Proposition 2 and Theorem 3, and the authors effectively distinguish their approach from averaging-based parallelizations, which fail to achieve polylogarithmic runtime.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial practical benefits, with the Radon Machine achieving 80-700x speedups over sequential base learners on 150 processors while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The comparison against Spark MLlib implementations and averaging-at-the-end baselines is thorough, showing the Radon Machine's advantages in both speed and predictive quality.

The paper's strengths include its clear theoretical foundation, well-structured presentation, and compelling empirical results. The black-box nature of the parallelization scheme is particularly valuable, as it requires no modifications to the underlying learning algorithms. The connection to Radon's theorem and convex geometry is novel and well-executed, and the paper provides a useful theoretical framework for understanding parallel learning limitations.

Several aspects could be strengthened to improve the paper. First, while the paper acknowledges the quasi-polynomial processor requirement, the practical implications of this constraint aren't fully addressed. The authors could better explain how the scheme would perform with a fixed, realistic number of processors (e.g., 100-1000) rather than the idealized quasi-polynomial setting. The empirical results on the SUSY dataset (5 million instances) are promising, but the paper could benefit from showing results on larger datasets to demonstrate scalability.

Second, the relationship between dimensionality and the Radon number (r = d+2) is noted in Section 5, but the practical impact of this for high-dimensional problems isn't thoroughly explored. For many modern machine learning applications, d is large (e.g., 10,000+ features), which would require a correspondingly large number of processors to achieve the claimed benefits. The authors briefly mention low-rank approximations as a solution, but this connection could be elaborated.

Third, while the paper claims the scheme is "black-box," it still requires knowledge of the Radon number for the hypothesis space, which varies by algorithm. The authors could provide more concrete examples of how to determine this parameter for different learning algorithms.

The paper is well-written and clear, with a logical flow from theory to practice. The related work section effectively situates the contribution within the literature on parallel learning and complexity theory.

In terms of significance, this work makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding the limits of parallel machine learning while providing a practical scheme with demonstrable benefits. The connection to Nick's Class is particularly valuable as it frames machine learning parallelization within a well-established theoretical framework. The practical speedups shown in the empirical evaluation suggest real-world applicability, especially for critical applications requiring high-confidence predictions.

The main limitation is the quasi-polynomial processor requirement, which may limit practical adoption in settings with fixed hardware constraints. However, the authors appropriately acknowledge this and suggest ways to address it through deparallelization or low-dimensional embeddings.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution while demonstrating practical utility. The work effectively bridges theoretical computer science and machine learning, addressing an important open question in the field. The empirical results are convincing, and the paper is well-structured and clearly written.

The paper meets all four criteria:
- Quality: Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous proofs, reasonable empirical validation
- Clarity: Well-organized, clear explanations, effective use of mathematical notation
- Originality: Novel application of Radon points to machine learning parallelization
- Significance: Addresses an important open problem with practical implications

The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of practical constraints (processor count limitations) and how they impact real-world deployment, but these are minor issues for a strong contribution. The work is clearly relevant to NIPS and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
