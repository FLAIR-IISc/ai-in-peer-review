- Proposal: Radon machine — black-box parallelization for convex regularized risk minimization that aggregates base learners via iterated Radon points on a tree.
- Theoretical claim: Iterated Radon aggregation yields double-exponential reduction in error probability with tree height h under finite Radon number and shard confidence δ ≤ 1/(2r).
- Complexity result: To reach (ε, Δ), the scheme can achieve polylogarithmic runtime in sample size using quasi-polynomially many processors (requires r = d+2 small).
- Empirical claim: With 150 processors, achieves 80–700× speedups over single-node base learners and competitive AUC vs Spark MLlib and simple averaging.
- Relation to prior work: Connects to NC-learning and addresses open questions on efficient parallel learning, contrasting with averaging and distributed SGD/ADMM.
- Theoretical quality: Propositions and theorems are clearly stated with proofs; Radon-based confidence amplification is novel and plausible.
- Theoretical limitation: Polylog-time guarantee hinges on r = d+2 being polylogarithmic in sample size and requires quasi-polynomial processor counts.
- Empirical limitation: Experiments do not validate the shard confidence prerequisite (δ ≤ 1/(2r)) or tightness of theoretical bounds and focus on AUC rather than confidence calibration.
- Clarity: Paper is well-written and organized with explicit algorithm and assumptions; some conditions should be highlighted earlier.
- Numerical concern: Stability of computing Radon points is not discussed and could affect practical applicability.
- Originality: Using iterated Radon points for provable aggregation is original relative to averaging, geometric median, and MOM schemes.
- Practical significance: Useful as a black-box scheme when data are abundant and models are low-dimensional, but limited for high-dimensional or kernel models without embeddings.
- Pros for acceptance: Novel general aggregation with formal guarantees, strong empirical speedups, clear NC-learning link, and available code.
- Cons for acceptance: Requires many processors and low dimensionality, empirical evaluation misses confidence/error bound checks and high-dimensional/nonlinear coverage.
- Constructive suggestions: Empirically measure calibration/regret vs h and verify δ on shards; compare to geometric median/MOM; report communication cost and numerical stability; test with fewer processors and nonlinear models via random features/Nyström.
