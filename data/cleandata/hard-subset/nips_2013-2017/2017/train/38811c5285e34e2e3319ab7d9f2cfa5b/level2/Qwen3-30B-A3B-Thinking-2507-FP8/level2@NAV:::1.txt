

This paper introduces the Radon machine, a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processing units while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The main claim is that this black-box approach can be applied to a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms without requiring mathematical derivations or dedicated code, with the cost being increased sample complexity. The theoretical contribution establishes that the Radon machine reduces the probability of generating an ε-bad hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree, leading to polylogarithmic runtime for consistent and efficient learning algorithms.

The paper provides strong theoretical support through Proposition 2 and Theorem 3, demonstrating the double-exponential confidence increase through Radon point aggregation. The empirical evaluation shows significant speedups (80-700x) compared to base learning algorithms using 150 processors, with comparable predictive performance to Spark MLlib algorithms. The comparison to averaging-at-the-end demonstrates that Radon aggregation outperforms simple averaging in predictive quality while maintaining similar runtime characteristics.

The paper demonstrates good field knowledge, correctly situating its work within the context of Nick's Class (NC) and parallel learning literature. The Radon point approach represents a meaningful novelty over existing aggregation methods that rely on averaging. The theoretical analysis is sound and properly contextualized within the machine learning and parallel computing literature.

However, the paper could strengthen its claims by providing more detailed comparisons with other parallelization techniques beyond Spark MLlib, particularly those explicitly targeting polylogarithmic runtime. While the paper acknowledges limitations regarding high-dimensional data and non-linear models, it would benefit from more concrete discussion of when the Radon machine would be preferable to existing approaches. The empirical section would also be improved by including more comprehensive runtime comparisons across different dataset sizes and dimensions.

The paper's main strength is its elegant theoretical framework that provides provable guarantees while maintaining practical applicability as a black-box parallelization scheme. The work makes a significant contribution to understanding the parallelization of machine learning algorithms within the context of Nick's Class. With more thorough empirical comparisons and expanded discussion of practical limitations, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the field.
