- Presents a novel parallelisation scheme called the Radon machine that simplifies adapting learning algorithms to large data without bespoke code or derivations.  
- Shows that algorithms meeting certain conditions can be parallelised to achieve the same confidence and error bounds as sequential execution in much shorter time.  
- Claims runtime reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic at the expense of increased data requirements and number of processing units.  
- Provides thorough theoretical analysis including sample complexity and runtime complexity.  
- Empirical evaluation against Spark ML shows substantial computation-time reductions while maintaining comparable predictive performance.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organised, and clearly explains theoretical and practical contributions.  
- Strength: broadly applicable scheme with theoretical performance guarantees.  
- Strength: significant practical speedups in realistic applications with maintained accuracy.  
- Weakness: assumes abundant data and a hypothesis space parameterisable in fixed finite-dimensional Euclidean space, limiting applicability.  
- Weakness: lacks detailed comparisons with other parallelisation approaches such as stochastic gradient descent.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited to a few datasets and learning algorithms; needs broader testing.  
- Recommendation: accept with revisions to discuss limitations and provide a more comprehensive empirical comparison and evaluation.
