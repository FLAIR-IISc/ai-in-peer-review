## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms aiming to reduce runtime to polylogarithmic time on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units. The core idea revolves around a “Radon machine” which applies the base learning algorithm in parallel to data subsets and aggregates the results using Radon points, offering a provable guarantee of maintaining confidence levels while achieving significant speedups. The authors position this work as a step towards addressing the challenge of efficiently parallelising machine learning algorithms, potentially bridging the gap towards Nick’s Class (NC).

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical need for scalable machine learning. The theoretical contribution – demonstrating polylogarithmic runtime under specific conditions – is significant. The authors clearly articulate the trade-off: increased data complexity for reduced runtime. The empirical evaluation, comparing the Radon machine to Spark MLlib and averaging-at-the-end, demonstrates substantial speedups (up to 700x) with comparable performance, validating the theoretical claims. The inclusion of communication complexity considerations and discussion of applicability to streaming data and non-linear models strengthens the work.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the assumptions regarding the Radon number and the need for quasi-polynomial processing units might limit practical applicability. The empirical evaluation, while compelling, is somewhat limited in scope. The datasets used are moderately sized, and the focus is primarily on linear models. A more extensive evaluation on larger, high-dimensional datasets and with a wider range of learning algorithms would bolster the claims. The discussion of handling scenarios with fewer than the ideal number of processors feels brief. Furthermore, the connection to Nick’s Class, while mentioned, could be more thoroughly explored – a clearer discussion of the implications for P-completeness would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound parallelisation scheme.
*   Demonstrated significant speedups in empirical evaluation.
*   Addresses a crucial challenge in scalable machine learning.
*   Clear articulation of trade-offs and limitations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Practical limitations due to assumptions about Radon number and processor count.
*   Limited scope of empirical evaluation.
*   Connection to Nick’s Class could be more thoroughly explored.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a promising approach to parallelising machine learning. Despite the limitations, the theoretical contributions and empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically expanding the empirical evaluation to include larger datasets and more diverse learning algorithms, and elaborating on the implications of the work within the context of computational complexity theory. The authors should also address the practical considerations of implementing this scheme with a limited number of processors in more detail.
