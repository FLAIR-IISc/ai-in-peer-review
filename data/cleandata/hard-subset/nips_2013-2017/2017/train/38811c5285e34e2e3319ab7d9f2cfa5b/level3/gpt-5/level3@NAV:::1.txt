This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme that aggregates base learners by replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point rather than averaging. The method targets regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean spaces and yields (ε, Δ) guarantees via a PAC-style analysis: the probability of an ε-bad aggregate decays doubly exponentially with the aggregation depth. Complexity results argue that, under bounded Radon number (r = d + 2) and with quasi-polynomially many processors, runtime becomes polylogarithmic in the sample complexity—linking to NC-style efficient parallel learning. Empirically, the method is compared against Spark MLlib and Weka linear models, showing large speedups and comparable AUC. Relative to prior work at NIPS and elsewhere, this offers a principled alternative to averaging-at-the-end/bagging schemes and complements distributed SGD and ADMM approaches that typically require multiple communication rounds and do not attain polylogarithmic-time guarantees; it also relates to NC-learnability (Vitter & Lin) and to parallel learning of halfspaces (Long & Servedio).

Quality: The theory is careful and sound for the stated setting. The Radon-point aggregation guarantee is clean and its iterative application is well-motivated. Assumptions (finite-dimensional convex hypothesis class, δ ≤ 1/2r) are explicitly stated. However, the practicality of r = d + 2 is critical: aggregation costs O(r^3) per node and the required splitting into rh parts leads to sample/processor blow-up as d grows. Empirically, all datasets are very low dimensional (~18 features), so the claimed efficiency for realistic, high-dimensional regimes remains untested.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with proofs and implementation details deferred appropriately. It would help to make explicit the dependence of all costs on d, r, and h in the main text, and to discuss the δ ≤ 1/2r requirement operationally.

Originality: Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation in a general black-box framework is novel and differentiates clearly from averaging and from optimization-centric parallelization.

Significance: If it scales to high-dimensional and sparse settings, the approach could be impactful as a plug-and-play parallelization tool with guarantees. The current evidence is limited to low-dimensional, linear models.

Suggestions:  
- Demonstrate scaling to high-dimensional sparse data (e.g., text, bag-of-words; d ≫ 10^4), report wall-clock, accuracy, communication, and memory.  
- Provide sensitivity analyses over d, r, and h, and experiments with c < rh processors.  
- Evaluate with random projections/sketching or block-coordinate variants advocated in the discussion, and quantify the accuracy-speed trade-off.  
- Clarify the regimes where Theorem 4’s assumptions (Radon number polylog in sample complexity) plausibly hold when d grows with N.

Arguments for acceptance:  
- Solid and novel theoretical framework with clear guarantees.  
- Simple black-box applicability and promising speedups.  
- Clear relation to NC learning and prior distributed methods.

Arguments against acceptance:  
- Unclear practicality for high-dimensional models due to r = d + 2.  
- Empirical evaluation limited to low-dimensional data and linear models.  
- Missing evidence for performance with limited processors and communication constraints.

Recommendation: Borderline; strengthen with high-dimensional experiments and scaling analyses.
