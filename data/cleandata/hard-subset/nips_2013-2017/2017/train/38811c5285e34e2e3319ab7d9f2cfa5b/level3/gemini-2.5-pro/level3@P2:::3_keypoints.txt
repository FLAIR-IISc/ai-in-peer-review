- Introduces the Radon machine: partition data, train base learners in parallel, and hierarchically aggregate hypotheses using Radon points instead of averaging.
- Theoretical claim: aggregation via iterated Radon points yields doubly-exponential confidence amplification with tree height.
- Main theoretical result (Theorem 4): under certain conditions, runtime reduces from polynomial to polylogarithmic using a quasi-polynomial number of processors.
- Empirical claim: demonstrates substantial speedups (80–700x) and competitive predictive performance versus base learners, averaging baseline, and Spark MLlib.
- Quality: paper is high-quality and complete with thorough theoretical analysis (Section 3, proofs in Section 7).
- Experimental evaluation: comprehensive across datasets and tasks, comparing to relevant baselines and parallel libraries.
- Transparency: authors acknowledge limitations (high-dimensional data, non-linear models, communication costs) and discuss them.
- Clarity: generally well-written and organized; Radon machine algorithm is easy to follow and motivation is strong.
- Minor presentation issues: typos (e.g., "the the"), inconsistent math font sizes, small figure fonts, color-reliant figures, missing spaces, and inconsistent citation formatting.
- Originality: novel application of iterated Radon points for aggregation; connects convex geometry to parallel learning and NC-learnability.
- Theoretical significance: constructive path toward polylogarithmic-time learning algorithms and addressing parallel complexity of learning.
- Practical significance: black-box parallelization applicable to many learners, with large empirical speedups and maintained accuracy.
- Clarification requested: specify how the reported "≥10%" AUC improvement over averaging baseline was calculated (per-dataset vs. average).
- Language suggestion: avoid terms like "significant" unless backed by formal statistical tests.
- Multi-class results suggestion: integrate multi-class comparisons into main figures or better justify the small gains versus longer runtimes.
- Pros for acceptance: novel, theoretically strong, empirically effective, broadly applicable black-box method.
- Cons against acceptance: mainly minor presentation and clarity issues needing fixing and some empirical claims needing clearer support.
- Overall recommendation: accept.
