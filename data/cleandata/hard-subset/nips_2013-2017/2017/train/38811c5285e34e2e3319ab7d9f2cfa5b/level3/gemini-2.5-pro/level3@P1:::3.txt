**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel black-box parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to partition the dataset, train a base learning algorithm on each subset in parallel to obtain a set of initial hypotheses, and then iteratively aggregate these hypotheses using a tree-based structure. At each node in the aggregation tree, a new hypothesis is formed by computing the Radon point of its children's hypotheses. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this aggregation scheme boosts the confidence in the final hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. This allows the proposed method to reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic, at the cost of quasi-polynomially many processors and an increased sample complexity. This work positions itself as a significant step towards answering the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization belongs to Nick's Class (NC) of efficiently parallelizable problems. The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups over sequential base learners and competitive or superior performance in terms of both runtime and predictive accuracy compared to averaging-based parallelization and state-of-the-art Spark MLlib implementations.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is the novel application of iterated Radon points for aggregating learned hypotheses. This is a departure from more common parallelization schemes like parameter averaging. The theoretical motivation is strong and well-grounded. By connecting the parallelization of machine learning to the complexity class NC, the paper addresses a fundamental and important theoretical question, making a significant contribution to our understanding of the limits and possibilities of parallel learning.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. Theorem 3, which demonstrates the doubly-exponential reduction in the probability of obtaining a bad hypothesis, provides a compelling theoretical justification for the proposed aggregation scheme over simple averaging. This theoretical result is then used to derive the main complexity claim in Theorem 4, showing a path to polylogarithmic runtime. The claims are well-supported by this analysis.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive and convincing. The Radon Machine is benchmarked against appropriate baselines: the sequential base learner, a standard averaging-at-the-end approach, and optimized parallel implementations from Spark's MLlib. The results demonstrate clear and often dramatic improvements in training time (speedups of 80x to over 700x are reported) while maintaining or improving predictive performance (AUC). The breadth of the evaluation, covering different base learners and datasets, strengthens the claims of the paper's general applicability.

4.  **Clarity and Proactive Discussion:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job in the Discussion section of anticipating and addressing potential limitations and questions from the reader, such as applicability to high-dimensional or non-linear models, scenarios with a limited number of processors, and communication complexity. This demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of the practical implications of their work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, there are several areas where clarity and presentation could be improved. These are mostly minor points that would further strengthen the manuscript.

1.  **Presentation and Readability:**
    *   The font size in Figures 1, 2, and 3 is quite small, making them difficult to read, especially the axis labels and legends. Increasing the font size would significantly improve readability.
    *   Figure 3 may be difficult for readers to distinguish when printed in black and white. Using different markers or line styles in addition to color would resolve this.
    *   On Line 79, the math font for the equation `L(D) = ...` appears smaller than the surrounding text, which is slightly jarring.

2.  **Clarification of Empirical Claims:**
    *   Lines 318–320 state that the Radon machine outperforms the averaging baseline "on all datasets by at least 10%" in terms of AUC. It would be helpful to clarify how this 10% figure is derived. Is this an average of paired comparisons across the 10 folds, or a comparison of the mean AUC scores? Providing more detail on the comparison methodology would add rigor to this strong claim.
    *   On Line 324, the term "significant" is used informally. To avoid ambiguity, it is better to reserve this term for statistically significant results or to rephrase (e.g., "substantially higher predictive performance").

3.  **Results Presentation:**
    *   In the multi-class classification experiments (Lines 341–349), the reported performance difference between the Radon machine and the averaging-at-the-end baseline appears to be minor, especially when compared to the gains shown in binary classification. While the result is still positive, its presentation in the text feels somewhat disconnected from the main figures. The authors could consider integrating these results into Figure 1 for a more direct and comprehensive comparison, or provide a stronger justification for the benefits of the Radon machine in this context, even with a small performance margin.

4.  **Minor Typos and Formatting:**
    *   Line 51: "the the" -> "the".
    *   Lines 340–341: Missing spaces in "machineachieved" and "machineis".
    *   Line 377: The citation style for "Long and servedio [22]" is inconsistent with others, which typically do not include author names in the main text.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated method for parallelizing machine learning algorithms. The connection to computational complexity theory provides a deep and significant context for the work. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate practical utility. The identified weaknesses are minor and mainly relate to presentation, and they do not detract from the core contributions of the paper. I strongly recommend acceptance.
