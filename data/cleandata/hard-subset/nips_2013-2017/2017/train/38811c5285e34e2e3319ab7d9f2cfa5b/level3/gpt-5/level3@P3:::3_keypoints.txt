- Proposal: Introduces the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization that aggregates weak hypotheses via Radon points instead of averaging.  
- Key theory: Radon aggregation yields double-exponential reduction in ε-bad hypothesis probability with aggregation-tree height, enabling polylog-time learning on quasi-polynomial processors under convexity and finite Radon number.  
- Relation to literature: Positions work relative to averaging-at-the-end, distributed SGD/ERMs (ADMM, DANE/CoCoA), and complexity-theoretic views (Long & Servedio, NC).  
- Empirical results: Shows large training-time reductions (80–700× on 150 processors) while maintaining or improving AUC across datasets and base learners.  
- Baseline comparisons: Often outperforms Spark MLlib in runtime and frequently in predictive quality.  
- Theoretical soundness: Propositions and Theorem 3 clearly formalize confidence amplification; assumptions and r3 aggregation cost are explicit and reasonable for many linear models.  
- Empirical support quality: Experiments use multiple datasets, WEKA/scikit-learn base learners, Spark baselines, and 10-fold CV, showing strong speed–AUC trade-offs.  
- Practical considerations: Authors discuss dimensionality, kernels, limited processors, communication, streaming, and privacy, strengthening practical relevance.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-organized with intuitive proof sketches and clear algorithm description.  
- Presentation issues: Some figures, fonts, and minor typos reduce accessibility (grayscale printing, small fonts, caption/readability issues).  
- Originality: Using iterated Radon points for hypothesis aggregation with rigorous guarantees is novel and distinct from method-specific distributed optimizers.  
- Significance: Offers a general, plug-and-play parallelization with provable guarantees and compelling empirical speedups; relevant to theory and systems communities.  
- Pros for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution, clear confidence amplification mechanism, substantial empirical speedups, black-box applicability, and conceptual link to NC.  
- Cons: Requires quasi-polynomial processor counts and increases sample complexity; aggregation cost scales with r = d+2, raising scalability concerns for high d.  
- Practical margins: In some plots runtime/AUC differences versus averaging-at-the-end are small; practical advantage needs stronger substantiation (statistical tests/effect sizes).  
- Requested clarification: Specify how “outperforms averaging-at-the-end” was measured (average AUC, paired comparisons, etc.) and whether paired tests were run.  
- Statistical reporting: Avoid “significant” without tests; report statistical significance, p-values, CIs, or effect sizes for AUC differences across CV folds.  
- Results presentation: Include the Avg baseline directly in figures or better motivate Radon’s practical advantages (robustness, theory), and add per-dataset deltas or tests.  
- Dimensionality detail: Report exact hypothesis-space dimensionality d used in experiments to contextualize r and aggregation cost.  
- Sensitivity analysis: Discuss sensitivity to aggregation height h and subset sizes; provide ablation or guidance for practitioners with limited processors.  
- Communication cost: Specify hypothesis/message size (bytes) transmitted to substantiate “low communication” claims.  
- Reproducibility: Ensure Bitbucket contains all scripts/configs, dataset versions, and instructions.  
- Minor fixes: Correct typos (e.g., “the the”), citation capitalization/numbering, increase math/axis/legend font sizes, and ensure figures are B/W friendly.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — novel, well-motivated algorithm with solid theory and convincing empirical results, pending the suggested presentation and clarity improvements.
