- Proposes the "Radon machine," a novel parallelization that aggregates hypotheses via Radon points to achieve polylog runtime with quasi-polynomial processors.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees including complexity analysis and a PAC bound.  
- Frames results in complexity-theoretic context, noting a connection to Nick’s Class (NC).  
- Offers a black-box, algorithm-agnostic parallelization technique applicable to many learning algorithms.  
- Empirical results show substantial speed-ups versus Spark and Weka on the tested datasets.  
- Major limitation: experiments are only on very low-dimensional datasets (~18 features), undermining claims of practical scalability.  
- Authors propose remedies for high dimensionality (random projections, low-rank approximations, block coordinate descent) but do not empirically validate them.  
- Clarity is generally good but mathematical notation is dense; needs more intuitive explanations or visuals for Radon points.  
- Related work coverage is reasonable but lacks a detailed trade-off comparison with existing parallel methods (e.g., distributed SGD).  
- Originality is strong: using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation is novel and theoretically advantageous over averaging.  
- Significance depends on demonstrated scalability; current empirical evidence is promising but inconclusive for real-world, high-dimensional problems.  
- Arguments for acceptance: solid theory, novelty, clear presentation, and observed speed-ups on tested data.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical evaluation, no high-dimensional demonstration, uncertain practical impact, and unclear practical relevance of the NC connection.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically sound and novel but insufficient empirical validation on high-dimensional data; currently unsuitable for acceptance.  
- Recommendation: reject but encourage revision and resubmission after extended experiments (include at least one dataset with >1000 features) and demonstration of proposed dimensionality fixes.
