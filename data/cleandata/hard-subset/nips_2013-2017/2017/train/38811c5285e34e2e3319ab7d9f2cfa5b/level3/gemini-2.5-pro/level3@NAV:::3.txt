This paper introduces the "Radon machine," a novel black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The method trains base learners on data subsets and recursively aggregates the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, which increases confidence doubly-exponentially with the aggregation depth. The work addresses the fundamental open problem of whether machine learning can be efficiently parallelized in the sense of Nick’s Class (NC), a question previously explored by Long and Servedio [21]. It provides a compelling alternative to more common parallelization techniques like parameter averaging or distributed SGD.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The core theoretical contribution is novel, well-motivated, and significant. Applying Radon partitioning to aggregate hypotheses is an elegant approach that provides strong theoretical guarantees, representing a valuable step forward in understanding parallel learning.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. Claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and a thorough empirical evaluation. The experiments convincingly demonstrate substantial training-time speedups and/or improved AUC over multiple baselines (including averaging and Spark MLlib) across various datasets.
*   **Clarity:** The authors have preemptively and effectively addressed several potential concerns in the Evaluation and Discussion sections, such as applicability to high-dimensional or non-linear models and limited processor availability.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is promising but could be strengthened with minor revisions to improve clarity and presentation.
*   **Presentation:** The text in Figures 1–3 needs a larger font for readability, and the color scheme in Figure 3 should be tested for black-and-white printing. The comparison with the "AverageAtTheEnd" baseline (Lines 341–349) feels understated; given the seemingly minor difference, this result should be better visualized (e.g., in Figure 1) or its advantages more strongly advocated for.
*   **Clarification:** On Lines 318–320, it is unclear if the Radon machine’s outperformance of the averaging baseline is based on an average of paired comparisons. This detail is crucial for result interpretation.
*   **Minor Corrections:** The manuscript would benefit from careful proofreading to fix several typos (e.g., "the the" on L51; "machineachieved" on L340), formatting issues (L79 math font), and a citation inconsistency (L377). The term "significant" (L324) should be used precisely, ideally referring to statistical significance.

**Recommendation:**
This is a high-quality paper with a strong contribution. The proposed algorithm is supported by both theory and experiments. I recommend acceptance, provided the authors address the minor but important issues of clarity and presentation noted above.
