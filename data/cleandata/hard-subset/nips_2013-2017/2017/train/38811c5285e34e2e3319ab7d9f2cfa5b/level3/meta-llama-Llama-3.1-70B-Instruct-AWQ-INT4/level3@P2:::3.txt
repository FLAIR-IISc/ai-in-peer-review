This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme, called the Radon machine, for a broad class of machine learning algorithms. The main idea is to apply the underlying learning algorithm in parallel to random subsets of the data and then aggregate the resulting hypotheses using a Radon point. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the scheme, showing that it can reduce the runtime of learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units. They also provide an empirical evaluation of the scheme, comparing it to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms and a natural baseline of averaging hypotheses.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere in the area of parallel machine learning. The authors discuss the connection to Nick's Class of parallelisable decision problems and its variants, and how their scheme contributes to understanding the extent to which efficient parallelisation of polynomial time learning algorithms is possible. They also mention related work on parallel learning, including stochastic gradient descent and distributed optimisation.

The paper's main contribution is the theoretical analysis of the Radon machine, which shows that it can achieve a reduction in runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units. The authors also provide an empirical evaluation of the scheme, which shows that it can achieve a substantial reduction in computation time in realistic applications. The results are compared to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms and a natural baseline of averaging hypotheses.

The paper is well-written and well-organised. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the Radon machine and its theoretical analysis. The empirical evaluation is also well-presented, with clear and readable figures.

* Theoretical contribution: The paper provides a novel and interesting theoretical analysis of the Radon machine, which shows that it can achieve a reduction in runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units.
* Empirical results: The paper provides an empirical evaluation of the scheme, which shows that it can achieve a substantial reduction in computation time in realistic applications.
