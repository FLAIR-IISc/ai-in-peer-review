Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a generic parallelization scheme for convex, finite-dimensional, regularized risk minimization. Instead of aggregating base models by averaging (as in bagging/model averaging), it partitions data into rh subsets, trains r^h base learners independently, and aggregates them along a tree using Radon points (in Rd, r = d + 2). A key theoretical result shows that taking the Radon point reduces the probability of an ε-bad hypothesis doubly exponentially with the tree height h, yielding the same (ε, Δ) as a much larger sequential learner but in polylogarithmic time—assuming quasi-polynomially many processors and that the Radon number is polylogarithmic in sample size. The paper also provides a PAC-style bound and a complexity analysis of runtime, sample complexity, and communication.

Empirically, using linear models (WEKA and scikit-learn) and comparisons to Spark MLlib, the Radon machine achieves 80–700× speedups while maintaining comparable AUC on large but low-dimensional datasets (e.g., SUSY/HIGGS, ~18 features). It consistently outperforms simple averaging-at-the-end in accuracy at similar runtime.

Relative to prior parallel learning work, this approach differs from:
- Averaging/bagging and one-shot model averaging, which lack doubly-exponential confidence amplification and typically cannot justify polylogarithmic runtime.
- Distributed SGD/mini-batch and ADMM/second-order frameworks, which require iterative communication and do not achieve polylogarithmic runtime in general.
- NC-learnability and results on parallel PAC learning (Vitter & Lin) and halfspaces (Long & Servedio): this paper contributes an aggregation mechanism with guarantees that, under stated assumptions, moves regularized risk minimization closer to NC-style efficiency.

Strengths
- Clear, novel aggregation mechanism: replacing subsets with their Radon point is a simple, black-box alternative to averaging with stronger robustness and confidence amplification guarantees.
- Solid theory: a clean bound showing double-exponential decrease in failure probability across aggregation levels; sample and runtime complexity that make explicit the tradeoff among data, processors, and aggregation depth; discussion of communication complexity.
- Practical demonstration: substantial wall-clock speedups using commodity cluster infrastructure; uniform improvement over averaging-at-the-end in accuracy; comparisons to Spark MLlib baselines.

Weaknesses and major concerns
- Practicality in high dimensions is unclear. Since r = d + 2, both the number of children per aggregation node and the O(r^3) cost of each Radon-point computation scale poorly with d. Moreover, the data splitting into r^h parts grows explosively with d and h, inflating sample complexity. The paper suggests random projections/Nyström/feature embeddings or block-coordinate schemes, but does not empirically validate these workarounds or provide guidance on their accuracy–efficiency tradeoffs.
- Experiments are limited to very low-dimensional settings (~18 features) and linear models. This does not reflect many modern ML scenarios (high-dimensional sparse text, recommender systems with >10^5 features, image features, deep embeddings).
- The conditions for the NC-style efficiency require that the Radon number be polylogarithmic in the sample size, which essentially assumes low-effective dimension. This limitation should be made more explicit in the claims and positioned as a regime of applicability.
- Assumption δ ≤ 1/2r for base learners may be non-trivial for large r; clarity on how to ensure/verify this in practice would help.

Quality
- Technical results appear sound and are carefully argued. The Radon point arguments (robustness vs averaging; double-exponential reduction) are elegant and intuitive.
- The complexity analysis is transparent about the tradeoffs in data and processors. However, the empirical section does not stress-test the method where theory predicts difficulties (large d), nor does it quantify the practical overheads of Radon computations as d grows.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. Algorithm 1 is clear; proofs and supporting material are well-structured.
- Suggested clarifications: make the dependence on d explicit and prominent in theorems and discussion; provide a practical recipe for choosing h given available processors and target (ε, Δ); discuss numerics/stability for computing Radon points (e.g., solving the r×r system via QR/SVD).

Originality
- Aggregating via Radon points instead of averaging is novel to ML parallelization, with a distinctive theoretical benefit (double-exponential confidence amplification).
- The connection to NC and the conditions under which polylog runtime is achievable for regularized risk minimizers is a meaningful conceptual contribution.

Significance
- If the approach can be shown to work in moderate-to-high dimensional regimes via dimensionality reduction or coordinate-block strategies, it would offer a broadly usable, black-box parallelization tool with strong guarantees.
- As is, significance is strongest for low-dimensional or low-effective-dimensional problems.

Requests for authors
- Provide experiments in high-dimensional, sparse settings (e.g., logistic regression on text with 10^5–10^6 features) and report:
  - End-to-end runtime, accuracy, and communication versus Spark/parameter-averaging/CoCoA/DANE baselines.
  - Cost of Radon-point computations and sensitivity to h when d is large.
- Demonstrate and quantify the suggested remedies:
  - Random projections or random features: show accuracy degradation and speedup tradeoffs.
  - Block-coordinate Radon aggregation: detail how features are partitioned and how blocks are reconciled, with empirical results.
- Clarify the conditions and practicality of δ ≤ 1/2r for base learners as d grows.
- Add experiments where processors are much fewer than r^h (common in practice), reporting the performance of the “deparallelized” regime.

Pros
- Strong theory with clean guarantees and robustness properties.
- Black-box, simple to implement; low communication.
- Large speedups on realistic low-dimensional datasets; better than averaging-at-the-end.

Cons
- Scalability with dimensionality is not demonstrated; overhead grows with d.
- Limited empirical scope (linear, low-dimensional).
- Applicability to nonparametric or nonconvex models remains indirect.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The theoretical contribution is solid and of interest to NIPS, and the black-box nature is appealing. However, acceptance should be contingent on clarifying and empirically substantiating scalability to high-dimensional data, or explicitly scoping claims to low-effective-dimensional settings and demonstrating principled, effective dimensionality-reduction pipelines.
