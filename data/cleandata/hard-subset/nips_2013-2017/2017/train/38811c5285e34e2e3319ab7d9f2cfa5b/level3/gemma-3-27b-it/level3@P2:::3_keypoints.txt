- Proposes the Radon machine: run a base learner on random data subsets and iteratively aggregate hypotheses using Radon points to achieve polylogarithmic runtime with quasi-polynomial increases in data and processors.  
- Addresses a long-standing problem by offering a theoretically grounded scheme for efficient parallelization of machine learning.  
- Theoretical analysis is sound, providing explicit bounds on runtime, data increase, and processor requirements.  
- Empirical evaluation shows substantial speed-ups and comparable or improved predictive performance versus baselines including Spark MLlib and simple averaging.  
- Novelty: the Radon machine is a distinct, aggregation-based parallelization approach that improves over averaging techniques via Radon-point aggregation and stronger confidence bounds.  
- Theoretical guarantees: rigorous proofs demonstrate the claimed polylogarithmic runtime and associated bounds.  
- Practical relevance: validated on realistic experiments, demonstrating meaningful speed and performance gains.  
- Generality: presented as a black-box method applicable to a wide class of regularized risk minimization algorithms without algorithm-specific changes.  
- Thoroughness: authors anticipate and discuss limitations (high-dimensional data, non-linear models, limited processors) and potential extensions.  
- Math readability issue: some mathematical notation (e.g., line 79) is printed small and hard to read.  
- Figure readability issue: Figures 1–3 use small fonts; Figure 3 may be unclear in black-and-white print.  
- Result magnitude concern: the empirical advantage over averaging-at-the-end is relatively small and needs stronger justification or clearer presentation.  
- Typographical errors present (e.g., “the the”, “machineachieved”, “machineis”) that should be corrected.  
- Citation inconsistency: an atypical citation format ("Long and servedio [22]") deviates from the paper’s other citations.  
- For acceptance: tackles a fundamental ML scaling problem with significant theoretical results and convincing empirical support.  
- For acceptance: well-written and accessible presentation of a complex idea with proactive discussion of limitations and future work.  
- Against acceptance: practical gains over existing methods may not be large enough to ensure broad adoption in all settings.  
- Against acceptance: limitations for high-dimensional and non-linear models require further empirical and theoretical investigation.  
- Overall assessment: strengths outweigh minor weaknesses; the Radon machine is a valuable, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated contribution and the reviewer recommends acceptance.  
- Suggested fixes: increase font sizes in Figures 1–3 and consider alternate visualizations for Figure 3 for black-and-white printing.  
- Suggested fixes: expand discussion/analysis of the performance gap versus averaging-at-the-end and clarify whether comparisons use paired averages.  
- Suggested fixes: correct typographical errors, ensure consistent citation style, and avoid using "significant" without clarifying statistical meaning.
