Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a generic, black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean spaces. The method, the Radon machine, trains a base learner independently on rh random subsets, then aggregates hypotheses up a tree whose internal nodes compute Radon points (instead of averages), yielding a doubly-exponential reduction in the probability of an ε-bad hypothesis in terms of the tree height h. Under assumptions on sample complexity and convexity, the scheme achieves the same (ε, Δ) guarantees as the sequential learner, with polylogarithmic runtime in the target sample size when provided quasi-polynomially many processors and more data. Experiments on Spark compare against the base learners, averaging-at-the-end, and MLlib algorithms, showing large wall-clock speedups with similar or better predictive performance.

The work builds on Radon points and centerpoint depth (Radon 1921; Clarkson et al., 1996) and relates to parallel learning in NC (Vitter & Lin, 1992; Long & Servedio, 2010), distributed averaging approaches (e.g., Zinkevich et al., 2010; Rosenblatt & Nadler, 2016), and distributed optimization (ADMM; Shamir et al.). The core novelty is the use of Radon aggregation for hypothesis combination with formal confidence amplification and its translation into an end-to-end parallelization framework with complexity bounds and empirical evidence.

A notable concern is potential overlap with prior theoretical work on parallelizing randomized convex optimization (e.g., work sometimes referred to as “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization”), which is not cited. The authors should clarify the exact novelty relative to such work and to robust aggregation schemes (geometric median, median-of-means) that also improve confidence under weak hypotheses.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, black-box scheme applicable to many convex ERM learners without algorithm-specific derivations or code changes.
- Clear theoretical guarantees: Theorem 3 shows doubly-exponential reduction in failure probability with h via Radon aggregation; Theorem 4 links to NC by showing polylog runtime using quasi-polynomial processors (given bounded Radon number).
- Robustness argument for Radon aggregation compared to averaging is insightful and practically relevant.
- Solid empirical study on realistic datasets; large speedups (80–700×) with comparable AUC to base learners and Spark baselines; communication-efficient “aggregate-at-the-end” design.
- Discussion acknowledges limitations (high d, non-linear models) and outlines practical remedies (random projections, low-rank embeddings).

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Novelty/positioning: The relation to prior work on parallel randomized convex optimization and robust aggregation is not addressed; add citations and articulate differences (assumptions, guarantees, algorithms).
- Assumptions vs. practice: The theory assumes iid hypotheses drawn from the base learner’s output distribution; the implementation partitions one dataset into rh disjoint subsets. Strict iid assumptions are not fully matched—please clarify whether the proofs extend to sampling without replacement or provide bounds for finite partitions.
- Processor complexity: The polylog-time result requires quasi-polynomial processors; in practice, c ≪ rh. Provide a more explicit scalability analysis/plots vs. c, reporting rh used, the “deparallelized” overhead factor max{1, rh/c}, and actual h.
- High-dimensionality: Radon number r = d + 2 leads to O(d^3) per-node aggregation. Experiments focus on d ≪ N; show results in higher-dimensional regimes, or more detailed analysis of when aggregation becomes the bottleneck.
- Empirical validation of confidence: While Δ shrinks doubly-exponentially in h, experiments fix N and report predictive performance, not empirical confidence/error bars; include calibration or conformal-style assessments that speak to the promised high-confidence benefits.
- Baselines: Include strong single-node baselines (e.g., LIBLINEAR/Vowpal Wabbit multi-threaded) and distributed ERM/ADMM tuned baselines; ensure fair hyperparameter parity and report end-to-end I/O overheads.
- Comparison to other robust aggregators: Evaluate against geometric median/median-of-means or coordinate-wise median to isolate the advantage of Radon aggregation.

Quality
- Theoretical results are sound given the stated assumptions; however, constants in Proposition 2 (union bound over pairs) could be tightened; spell out exact constants/binomial factors.
- Practical section is convincing, but missing sensitivity to h, d, and communication costs; add ablation of h vs. accuracy/runtime and report message sizes and wall-clock aggregation time.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and structured. Minor issues: “divide D into rh iid subsets” is imprecise; say “random, disjoint subsets drawn without replacement.” Explicitly state all assumptions (finite-dimensional Euclidean F, convex loss/regularizer, abundant data).
- Include a self-contained description of Radon-point computation costs and numerical stability considerations.

Originality
- The aggregation-by-Radon approach for hypothesis combination in parallel ERM appears novel relative to standard averaging. Potential overlap with earlier randomized convex optimization work must be clarified.

Significance
- If properly positioned, the work provides a practical, broadly applicable parallelization primitive with provable confidence amplification and strong speedups, of interest to both theory (NC connections) and systems/ML practitioners.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, black-box parallelization with formal guarantees and practical speedups.
- Robust aggregation via Radon points with clear theoretical advantages over averaging.
- Empirical validation across multiple datasets and comparison to standard distributed frameworks.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient discussion of related theoretical work and robust aggregators; novelty could be questioned without clearer positioning.
- Practical limitations in high dimensions; lack of experiments demonstrating behavior when d is large.
- The iid assumption and finite-partition gap not addressed theoretically.

Questions for the authors
- Please cite and contrast with prior work on parallel randomized convex optimization and robust aggregation (geometric median, median-of-means). What is strictly new here?
- Can you formalize the effect of sampling without replacement when partitioning a single dataset on the guarantees in Theorem 3?
- Report rh, c, and the realized deparallelization factor in experiments; provide scaling curves vs. c and h.
- Provide results for higher-dimensional settings and quantify aggregation time and numerical stability.
- Add baselines with strong single-node solvers and alternative robust aggregators; include calibration metrics to substantiate confidence improvements.

Overall recommendation
Promising and well-executed work with clear theory and strong empirical results, but acceptance should hinge on clarifying novelty relative to closely related theory and strengthening the empirical and theoretical discussion around assumptions and high-dimensional regimes.
