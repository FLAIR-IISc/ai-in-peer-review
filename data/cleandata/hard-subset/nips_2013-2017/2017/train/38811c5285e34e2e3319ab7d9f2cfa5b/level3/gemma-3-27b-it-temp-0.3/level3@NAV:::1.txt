## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, termed the Radon machine, for machine learning algorithms. The core idea is to combine base learners by replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point, offering a black-box alternative to methods like bagging. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, including complexity analysis demonstrating efficiency gains when samples are split across processing units, and a PAC bound guaranteeing performance. Empirical evaluation compares the Radon machine to parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Weka, showing promising speedups.

The work builds upon existing parallelization techniques like averaging-based methods [32, 45, 46] but distinguishes itself by utilizing Radon points for aggregation, offering a potentially faster convergence rate due to its doubly-exponential confidence increase. It also connects to the broader theoretical question of efficiently parallelizing machine learning algorithms, relating to Nick’s Class (NC) [9] and recent work on parallel half-space learning [21]. The authors correctly position their contribution within the context of distributed optimization [34] and communication efficiency [22].

A significant strength of the paper lies in its solid theoretical foundation. The complexity analysis and PAC bound provide valuable guarantees on the method’s performance. The empirical results, while limited in scope, demonstrate substantial speedups compared to base learners. The clear presentation of the Radon machine algorithm (Algorithm 1) and the detailed proofs in the appendix contribute to the paper's clarity.

However, a major concern is the practicality and scalability of the method, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The experiments are conducted on datasets with a relatively low number of features (~18), which may not be representative of many modern machine learning problems. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide sufficient evidence or discussion on how the method scales to higher dimensions or how techniques like random projections [15] or low-rank approximations [2, 28] would be integrated. The discussion of applying the scheme to non-linear models also feels somewhat cursory. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Solid theoretical analysis, provable guarantees, promising empirical results on low-dimensional data, novel approach to parallelization.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited empirical evaluation on low-dimensional datasets, unclear scalability to high-dimensional data, insufficient discussion of practical considerations for real-world applications.

**Overall:** The paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. However, the lack of demonstration on high-dimensional data significantly weakens its impact. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement for the authors to address the scalability concerns through further theoretical analysis and more comprehensive experiments. Specifically, the authors should either demonstrate the method’s effectiveness on higher-dimensional datasets or provide a clear roadmap for adapting the approach to such scenarios.
