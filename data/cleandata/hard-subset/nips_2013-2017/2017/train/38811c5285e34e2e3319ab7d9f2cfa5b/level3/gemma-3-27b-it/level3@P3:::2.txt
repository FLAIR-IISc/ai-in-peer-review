This paper proposes a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms based on Radon points. The central idea is to apply a base learning algorithm to random subsets of the data and then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, offering theoretical guarantees of polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial number of processors. The authors evaluate their “Radon machine” against several baselines, demonstrating reduced runtime and comparable or improved accuracy.

The paper addresses an important problem: scaling machine learning algorithms to large datasets. Existing parallelization techniques often require algorithm-specific derivations or suffer from limitations in runtime reduction. This work offers a more general, black-box approach. The use of Radon points, rather than simple averaging, is presented as a key novelty, with a theoretical justification based on a doubly-exponential increase in confidence during aggregation. The empirical evaluation appears comprehensive, comparing against no parallelization, averaging, and Spark-based algorithms across multiple datasets. The reported results demonstrate a significant speedup, particularly when compared to Spark, and maintain competitive accuracy.

However, the paper has a notable weakness regarding originality. The theoretical foundations, particularly the use of Radon points for aggregation, appear closely related to work by Braverman & Moscibroda (2013) – “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” – which is not cited. That work also leverages Radon points in a similar manner to reduce communication costs in parallel convex optimization, including applications to learning. The authors need to explicitly address this connection, clarify the unique contributions of their work beyond applying this technique to regularized risk minimization, and explain how their theoretical analysis differs or extends the prior work. Is the focus on the black-box nature of the approach, the specific runtime guarantees, or the empirical validation what truly sets this work apart? This lack of acknowledgment diminishes the perceived novelty.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty (Potential):** The application of Radon points to parallelizing a broad class of learning algorithms is a potentially valuable contribution, *if* differentiated from existing work.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical results demonstrating polylogarithmic runtime under certain conditions, a significant claim.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate a substantial speedup compared to baselines, and maintain accuracy.
*   **Generality:** The black-box nature of the approach is appealing, reducing the need for algorithm-specific modifications.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Citation/Discussion of Related Work:** The omission of Braverman & Moscibroda (2013) is a major concern. This prior work significantly undermines the claimed novelty.
*   **Originality Concerns:** Without addressing the connection to prior work, it’s unclear what the unique contribution of this paper is.
*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantees rely on assumptions about the hypothesis space (finite dimensionality, convexity, finite Radon number) which may not hold in all practical scenarios. While the paper acknowledges workarounds for some cases, this limitation should be more thoroughly discussed.
*   **Complexity:** The analysis, particularly regarding sample and runtime complexity, can be dense and difficult to follow without a strong background in optimization.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate a practical benefit in terms of runtime.
*   The theoretical analysis, while potentially overlapping with prior work, provides a formal justification for the approach.
*   The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning: scaling to large datasets.
*   The black-box nature of the method makes it broadly applicable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of acknowledgement and discussion of Braverman & Moscibroda (2013) significantly weakens the novelty claim.
*   The theoretical contribution may be incremental if it largely reiterates or builds directly upon existing results without significant extension.
*   The assumptions regarding the hypothesis space may limit the practical applicability of the method.




**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject** pending major revisions. The core idea is interesting and the empirical results are promising. However, the failure to acknowledge and address the connection to Braverman & Moscibroda (2013) is a critical flaw. The authors *must* thoroughly discuss this related work, clarify the unique contributions of their approach, and justify its novelty. If they can convincingly demonstrate that their work offers a substantial advancement over existing techniques, the paper could be reconsidered. Without that, the contribution feels incremental at best, and potentially derivative. The paper's quality suffers from this significant omission.
