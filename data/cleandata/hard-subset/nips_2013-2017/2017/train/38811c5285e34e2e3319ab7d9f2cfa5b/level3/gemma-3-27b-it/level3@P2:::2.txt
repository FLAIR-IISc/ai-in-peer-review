This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms based on the use of Radon points for aggregating hypotheses generated on random subsets of the data. The core idea is to achieve polylogarithmic runtime on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units, at the cost of increased sample complexity. The authors demonstrate empirical improvements in runtime compared to both sequential learning and parallel implementations within the Spark MLlib library, while maintaining comparable or superior predictive performance.

The work builds upon existing research in parallel learning and randomized convex optimization. The authors appropriately frame the problem in the context of regularised risk minimisation and highlight the challenge of efficiently parallelising polynomial-time algorithms, referencing Nick’s Class (NC). The proposed “Radon machine” offers a black-box approach, requiring no modifications to the underlying learning algorithm, which is a significant practical advantage. The theoretical analysis provides guarantees on the performance of the parallelised algorithm, relating the sample and runtime complexity to that of the base learner.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty (with clarification needed - see weaknesses):** The application of Radon points to aggregate hypotheses in a parallel learning setting appears to be a novel contribution, offering a potentially more efficient alternative to simple averaging. The claim of achieving polylogarithmic runtime is significant if validated.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a theoretical framework for analysing the parallelisation scheme, including bounds on sample complexity and runtime. The connection to concepts like the Radon number and quasi-convexity adds mathematical rigor.
* **Practicality:** The black-box nature of the approach is appealing, as it allows for easy adaptation to a wide range of learning algorithms without requiring specialized code or mathematical derivations.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate substantial speedups compared to both sequential learning and Spark-based implementations, while maintaining competitive accuracy. The use of multiple datasets and comparison with established libraries strengthens the findings.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the Radon machine.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Related Work – Significant Omission:** The paper *completely* fails to cite or acknowledge the work of Allen-Zhu et al. (2017) "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization," which directly addresses the parallelization of similar algorithms using a different, yet related, aggregation scheme. This is a critical omission. The relationship between the Radon machine and the approach in Allen-Zhu et al. needs to be thoroughly discussed. Is the Radon machine a generalization? Does it offer improvements in specific scenarios? A clear positioning relative to existing work is crucial.
* **Originality – Conditional on Addressing Weakness 1:**  Without a clear discussion of Allen-Zhu et al. (2017), the claim of novelty is weakened. The authors must clearly articulate what distinguishes their approach and why it is a valuable contribution beyond existing methods.
* **Radon Number Calculation:** The computational cost of calculating the Radon point (mentioned as r^3 in Section 3) may become a bottleneck for high-dimensional hypothesis spaces. While the authors acknowledge this in the discussion, further analysis of this cost and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., approximate Radon point calculations) would be beneficial.
* **Practical Considerations – Processor Availability:** The paper assumes a sufficient number of processors (rh) are available. While the authors briefly mention de-parallelization, a more detailed discussion of the performance impact of using fewer processors than the theoretical optimum would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation focuses primarily on linear models. While the authors acknowledge the need to extend the analysis to non-linear models, including some preliminary results would strengthen the claims of general applicability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel (potentially, *after* addressing the related work omission) approach to parallelising machine learning algorithms.
* The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the performance of the proposed scheme.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant practical benefits in terms of runtime.
* The black-box nature of the method makes it widely applicable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The critical omission of related work (Allen-Zhu et al., 2017) significantly weakens the claims of novelty and originality.
* The computational cost of Radon point calculation may limit scalability in high-dimensional settings.
* The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. However, the critical omission of relevant related work (Allen-Zhu et al., 2017) is a major concern. If the authors can adequately address this issue by clearly positioning their work relative to existing methods and highlighting its unique contributions, the paper has the potential to be accepted.  I recommend a **weak reject** contingent on a major revision that addresses the related work issue and includes a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of Radon point calculation.  Further experimentation with non-linear models would also be beneficial.



**Confidence:** 6/10 (primarily due to the related work omission).
