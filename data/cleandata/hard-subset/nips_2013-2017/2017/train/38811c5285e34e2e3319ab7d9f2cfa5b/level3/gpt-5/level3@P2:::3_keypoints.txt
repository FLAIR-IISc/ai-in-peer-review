- Proposes the Radon machine: a black-box parallelization that aggregates base learners via Radon points, yielding doubly exponential confidence improvement with tree height.  
- Key theoretical claim: probability of an ε-bad aggregate decreases doubly exponentially when internal nodes compute Radon points of r = d+2 hypotheses.  
- Trade-off: reduced wall-clock time (polylog runtime) at the cost of more data and quasi-polynomially many processors (Theorems 3–4).  
- Aggregation is computationally light per node, requiring only solving r×r linear systems.  
- Empirically achieves large speedups (80–700× on 150 processors) while maintaining comparable AUC to strong baselines and competitive with Spark MLlib.  
- Discusses extensions to regression/multiclass, communication complexity, limited processors, and handling high-dimensional/nonlinear models via embeddings or dimensionality reduction.  
- Connects to NC-learnability and prior open problems on parallel learning for halfspaces, distinguishing itself from averaging and distributed SGD/ADMM.  
- Theory is careful with clear assumptions (finite-dimensional convex class, iid subsamples, consistent base learner, δ ≤ 1/2r) and rigorous proofs (Proposition 2, Theorem 3).  
- Needs clarification on how the δ ≤ 1/2r precondition is achieved in practice for chosen base learners and subset sizes.  
- Theorem 4 is precise about polylog runtime but requires r to be bounded polylogarithmically in sample complexity; practical ranges for d/r should be clarified.  
- Empirical evaluation is broad and shows strong speedups, but the advantage over averaging-at-the-end is sometimes small and needs statistical tests and clearer presentation.  
- Request to include Avg baseline results directly in Figure 1 or justify their exclusion and to report confidence intervals/p-values for paired comparisons.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, with an easy-to-follow Algorithm 1 and early statement of assumptions.  
- Readability issues: small figure fonts, grayscale legibility for Figure 3, and one math display in smaller font.  
- Novelty: iterative Radon-point aggregation as a confidence-amplifying method is novel and yields stronger confidence amplification than averaging.  
- Significance: advances practical parallelization of ERM-type learners with provable guarantees and low communication, though limited by finite-dimensional convex model assumptions.  
- Limitations: increased sample complexity, dependence on finite-dimensional convex parametrizations, and need for many processors for strongest theoretical claims.  
- Requests for clarification: specify aggregation protocol for paired comparisons, regimes where Radon aggregation is preferable, empirical base δ values, and quantify r^3 aggregation cost in experiments.  
- Minor editorial fixes: several typos, inconsistent citation style, and figure/font adjustments recommended.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept, contingent on minor clarifications and improved result presentation.
