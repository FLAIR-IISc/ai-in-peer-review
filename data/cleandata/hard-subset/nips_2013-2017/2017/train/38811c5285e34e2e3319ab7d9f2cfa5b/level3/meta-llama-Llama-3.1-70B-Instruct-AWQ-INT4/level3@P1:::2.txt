This paper proposes a novel parallelisation scheme for a broad class of learning algorithms, which simplifies the adaptation of these algorithms to growing amounts of data and the need for accurate and confident predictions in critical applications. The proposed method, called the Radon machine, uses Radon points to aggregate hypotheses in a way that increases confidence doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the Radon machine, including its sample and runtime complexity, and shows that it can achieve polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processing units. The authors also provide an empirical evaluation of the Radon machine, comparing it to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms from the Spark machine learning library, as well as the natural baseline of averaging hypotheses instead of calculating their Radon point.

The results show that the Radon machine achieves a substantial reduction in computation time while maintaining similar predictive performance to the Spark algorithms and substantially higher predictive performance than averaging-at-the-end. The paper also discusses the limitations of the proposed method, including its applicability to high-dimensional models and non-linear models, and suggests possible extensions to address these limitations.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning, providing a novel and effective method for parallelising learning algorithms and demonstrating its potential in practical applications.
