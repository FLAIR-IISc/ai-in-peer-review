Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization that aggregates base learners via Radon points instead of averaging. The key theoretical insight is that the probability of an ε-bad aggregate decreases doubly exponentially with the height h of an aggregation tree when each internal node computes a Radon point of r = d+2 hypotheses (for Euclidean Rd). This yields a trade-off: more data and many processors in exchange for shorter wall-clock time, with theoretical bounds on sample complexity and confidence. Under standard consistency assumptions and an explicit δ ≤ 1/2r base-error precondition, the scheme reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the target sample complexity, using quasi-polynomially many processors (Theorems 3–4). The aggregation requires only solving r×r linear systems per internal node.

Empirically, the Radon machine achieves substantial speedups (80–700× on 150 processors) while maintaining comparable AUC to strong baselines on several large datasets, and performs competitively against Spark MLlib methods. The authors also discuss applicability to regression and multiclass problems, communication complexity, limited processor scenarios, and high-dimensional/nonlinear settings via dimensionality reduction or explicit embeddings.

This approach connects to classic NC-learnability work (Vitter & Lin) and the “fundamental open problem” on efficient parallel learning for halfspaces (Long & Servedio). Compared to averaging-based schemes and distributed SGD/ADMM, the Radon aggregation provides stronger confidence amplification and lower communication, at the cost of increased sample complexity.

Quality
- Theory: The analysis is careful and clearly states assumptions (finite-dimensional convex hypothesis class with finite Radon number; iid subsamples; consistent base learner with polynomial sample/runtime complexity; δ ≤ 1/2r). Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are well-motivated and proved via properties of quasi-/convex functions and Radon partitions. Theorem 4’s claim of polylog runtime with quasi-polynomial processors is precise, and the authors candidly discuss the conditions (r bounded polylogarithmically in sample complexity) and practical remedies for large d. One point to clarify: how δ ≤ 1/2r is achieved in practice for chosen base learners and subset sizes (see questions below).
- Empirics: The evaluation is broad (multiple datasets, base learners, comparisons to MLlib), shows large speedups at similar AUC, and includes a discussion of communication and de-parallelization. However, the advantage over averaging-at-the-end appears small in some plots; please clarify the averaging of paired comparisons and provide statistical tests where feasible. Also, include the Avg baseline results directly in Figure 1 or motivate why not.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Algorithm 1 is easy to follow, and the assumptions are stated early.
- Readability issues: figures’ font sizes are small; Figure 3 may be difficult in grayscale; one math display appears in a smaller font.
- Several minor typos and citation style inconsistencies (see detailed comments).

Originality
- Using iterated Radon points as a confidence-amplifying aggregator for parallel learning is novel in the ML parallelization literature. The double-exponential confidence reduction with tree height distinguishes it from averaging and from distributed SGD/ADMM approaches.
- The conceptual bridge to NC-style parallel complexity in learning is timely and interesting.

Significance
- The work advances our understanding of when and how consistent ERM-type learners can be parallelized effectively, with provable guarantees and practical speedups. As a drop-in, black-box scheme with low communication, it is likely to be adopted or extended.
- Limitations include dependence on finite-dimensional convex models (or explicit feature maps), increased sample complexity, and the requirement of many processors for the strongest theoretical claims. Nonetheless, the empirical results with 150 processors are persuasive.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Lines 318–320: When claiming the Radon machine “outperforms” averaging-at-the-end, is this based on average paired comparisons across folds/datasets? Please clarify the aggregation protocol and add confidence intervals or statistical tests where appropriate.
- Lines 341–349: The text suggests differences with AverageAtTheEnd are minor; please either integrate these results into Figure 1 for direct comparison or articulate more clearly the regimes where Radon aggregation is preferable (e.g., high-confidence requirements, robustness to single bad hypotheses per Proposition 2).
- δ ≤ 1/2r: Provide guidance for selecting subset sizes to satisfy this precondition for typical base learners (e.g., logistic regression, SVM) and report the achieved base δ empirically.
- Theorem 4: Give concrete ranges for d (and hence r = d+2) where the “r polylog in sample size” condition is realistic, and quantify the r3 aggregation cost for your experimental settings.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Novel, well-motivated aggregation mechanism with clean theory (double-exponential confidence amplification).
- Bridges ML parallelization with NC-style parallel complexity; clear step toward a long-standing open question.
- Black-box applicability and low communication cost.
- Strong empirical speedups with comparable AUC on large, real datasets; open-source implementation.
- Thorough discussion of limitations and practical workarounds (dimensionality reduction, feature embeddings).

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Theoretical guarantees require finite-dimensional convex parametrizations and can be less applicable for very high-dimensional or non-convex models without additional approximations.
- Sample complexity increases; guidance on this trade-off could be more quantitative.
- Advantage over averaging-at-the-end is not uniformly large in all experiments; statistical significance and clearer presentation would strengthen claims.
- The polylogarithmic runtime guarantee relies on quasi-polynomially many processors; practical implications deserve further quantification.

Minor comments and editorial suggestions
- Line 51: “the the” → “the”.
- Line 79: math font appears smaller; make consistent with the rest.
- Figures 1–3: increase font sizes; ensure Figure 3 is interpretable in grayscale/print.
- Lines 318–320: clarify whether improvements are averaged over folds/datasets; add error bars.
- Line 324: avoid “significant” unless statistically supported; use “substantial” or report p-values.
- Lines 340–341: fix “machineachieved” and “machineis”.
- Lines 341–349: better integrate or justify the AverageAtTheEnd comparison (see above).
- Line 377: citation style inconsistent (“Long and servedio [22]”); standardize capitalization and author-year/number style with the rest of the paper.

Overall recommendation
Accept. This is a solid theoretical and practical contribution that proposes a broadly applicable, low-communication parallelization scheme with strong guarantees and compelling empirical results. With minor clarifications and improved result presentation, it would make a valuable addition to NIPS.
