

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning" (NIPS 2017)

This paper introduces the Radon Machine, a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The approach treats the base learning algorithm as a black box, requiring no additional mathematical derivations or dedicated code, which represents a significant practical advantage over existing parallelization techniques. The authors connect their work to the fundamental open question of whether efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms is possible in the sense of Nick's Class (NC), providing a step toward answering this question for regularized risk minimization algorithms.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

The paper makes a clear connection to theoretical computer science concepts, particularly Nick's Class (NC), which defines efficient parallel algorithms as those that can run in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors. While previous work has investigated parallel learning for specific cases (e.g., Long and Servedio [21] for half-spaces), this paper provides a general framework applicable to a broad class of learning algorithms. The authors build upon theoretical work on Radon points (Radon [30]) and convex geometry, while connecting to practical parallel learning approaches like averaging-based parallelizations (e.g., [32, 45, 46]).

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's theoretical contribution is strong, well-motivated, and addresses a fundamental open question in parallel machine learning. The authors provide a rigorous analysis showing how the Radon Machine achieves doubly-exponential confidence improvement with the height of the aggregation tree, and demonstrate that runtime can be reduced from polynomial to polylogarithmic. The proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are elegant and clearly presented.

**Empirical Results:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The Radon Machine achieves 80-700x speedups over base learners on 150 processors while maintaining comparable or better AUC performance. Crucially, it outperforms the averaging-at-the-end baseline (the natural alternative) by at least 10% in AUC across all datasets, while being substantially faster than Spark MLlib implementations (10x faster on SUSY and HIGGS datasets). The study covers multiple learning algorithms (SVM, logistic regression, regularized least squares) and data types (binary classification, regression, multi-class).

**Preemptive Addressing of Concerns:** The authors anticipate and address key concerns in the Discussion section, including applicability to high-dimensional data (via random projections), non-linear models (via low-rank approximations), and limited processing units (by "deparallelizing" the scheme). They also thoughtfully discuss communication complexity and applicability to data streams.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity and Presentation:** Several minor issues affect readability. Line 51 contains a typo ("the the" should be "the"). Line 79 has math font that appears smaller and harder to read. Figures 1-3 require larger text for readability, especially Figure 3 which may be hard to distinguish when printed in black and white. The results in Lines 341-349 regarding the difference with AverageAtTheEnd seem minor; this should be better justified or incorporated into Figure 1.

**Language and Precision:** Line 324 uses "significant" without statistical context. The citation "Long and servedio [22]" has inconsistent author naming (should be "Long and Servedio"). Lines 340-341 contain typos ("machineachieved" and "machineis").

**Clarification Needed:** Lines 318-320 ask whether the Radon machine's outperformance over the averaging baseline is based on average paired comparisons. This should be clarified.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper with significant theoretical contributions that address a fundamental question in parallel machine learning. The Radon Machine provides a novel, provably effective parallelization scheme that works as a black box for a broad class of learning algorithms, with strong empirical validation. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical results are compelling, and the authors thoughtfully address limitations and future directions.

The minor presentation issues noted above do not detract from the paper's scientific contribution. The authors have clearly demonstrated that the Radon Machine can achieve substantial speedups while maintaining or improving predictive performance, and their theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the limits of parallel machine learning.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the presentation issues noted above. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, advancing our understanding of parallel machine learning and providing a practical tool that could be widely adopted in industry and academia.

This work is clearly within NIPS scope as it bridges theoretical computer science with practical machine learning, addressing a fundamental question with both theoretical and empirical rigor. The results are significant for practitioners seeking to scale machine learning to larger datasets while maintaining confidence in predictions.
