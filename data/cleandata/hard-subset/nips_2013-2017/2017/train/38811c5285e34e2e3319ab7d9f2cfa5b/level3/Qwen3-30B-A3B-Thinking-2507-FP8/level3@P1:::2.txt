

This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, the Radon Machine, for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The authors propose using Radon points for aggregation instead of standard averaging, which provides a doubly exponential improvement in confidence with each aggregation step. The main theoretical contribution is showing that for consistent and efficient learning algorithms (e.g., SVMs, regularized least squares), the Radon Machine achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processing units while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The key insight is that the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis decreases as (rδ)^2^h, where r is the Radon number, δ is the base algorithm's failure probability, and h is the aggregation tree height.

The paper relates this work to Nick's Class (NC), the standard for efficient parallel algorithms, and makes progress toward answering the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be parallelized in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including sample complexity bounds and runtime complexity comparisons with the base learning algorithm.

The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing the Radon Machine against several baselines: the base learning algorithm, averaging-at-the-end (which computes the average of hypotheses instead of Radon points), and Spark MLlib algorithms. On moderate-sized datasets, the Radon Machine achieves 80-700x speedup with comparable or better predictive performance (measured by AUC). On large datasets like SUSY, it achieves a 721x speedup while maintaining similar AUC to the base learner. Compared to Spark MLlib, the Radon Machine achieves similar predictive performance with 15-85% of the runtime, and outperforms the averaging-at-the-end baseline by at least 10% in AUC across all datasets.

The paper's strengths include a strong theoretical foundation with clear proofs of the confidence improvement property (Theorem 3), a practical implementation that treats the base learning algorithm as a black box, and comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and learning tasks (including regression and multi-class classification). The method is particularly appealing for high-confidence applications where error costs are prohibitive.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the theoretical contribution appears to overlap significantly with "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (not cited), which presents similar aggregation techniques using Radon points. The authors must clarify how their work differs from this prior research and why it represents a novel contribution. Without this clarification, the originality claim is weakened.

Second, the assumption of a hypothesis space with finite Radon number (e.g., Euclidean spaces with Radon number r = d+2) restricts the method's applicability. While the authors discuss high-dimensional data and non-linear models in Section 5, the practical limitations of this assumption are not sufficiently addressed. For instance, it's unclear how the method would handle text data or high-dimensional embeddings without additional preprocessing.

Third, the paper claims to "answer an open question" about efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms in the sense of Nick's Class, but the results only apply to regularized risk minimization algorithms with specific properties. This overstates the scope of the contribution.

The paper is well-written with clear organization and good mathematical exposition. The empirical section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors effectively address related work in Section 6, though they should strengthen their position relative to the cited prior work on parallel randomized convex optimization.

In terms of significance, the Radon Machine offers a practical approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms with theoretical guarantees, which could be valuable for critical applications requiring high confidence. The speedups observed in experiments (80-700x) are impressive and could have substantial impact in real-world applications.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Clearly differentiate their work from "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" and explain why their contribution is novel
2. More thoroughly discuss the limitations of the finite Radon number assumption
3. Provide more concrete guidance on when the Radon Machine should be preferred over other parallelization approaches

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in parallel machine learning. The theoretical analysis is sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the black-box nature of the approach makes it widely applicable. With clarification of the relationship to prior work and better articulation of limitations, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. As it stands, the theoretical novelty is questionable, but the practical approach is promising. The authors should address these concerns in revision.
