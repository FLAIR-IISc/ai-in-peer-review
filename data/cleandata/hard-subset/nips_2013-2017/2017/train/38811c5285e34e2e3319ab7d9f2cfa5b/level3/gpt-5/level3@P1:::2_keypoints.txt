- Proposes the Radon machine: a black-box parallelization that trains base learners on random subsets and aggregates via Radon points, yielding doubly-exponential reduction in failure probability with tree height h.
- Shows under convexity and sample-complexity assumptions the same (ε, Δ) guarantees as sequential ERM and polylog runtime using quasi-polynomial processors and more data.
- Empirical Spark experiments report large wall-clock speedups (80–700×) with comparable or better AUC versus base learners and Spark baselines.
- Builds on Radon points and centerpoint depth and connects to prior parallel learning, distributed averaging, and distributed optimization literature.
- Core novelty claimed: Radon-point aggregation for hypothesis combination with formal confidence amplification and end-to-end complexity bounds.
- Reviewer concerned about missing citations/overlap with prior work on parallel randomized convex optimization.
- Needs explicit comparison and positioning relative to robust aggregation methods (geometric median, median-of-means) and related theory.
- Theory assumes iid draws of hypotheses, but implementation partitions a single dataset without replacement—this assumption gap must be clarified or addressed.
- Polylog-time guarantee depends on quasi-polynomial processor counts; practical processor limits (c ≪ rh) require explicit scalability analysis and reported deparallelization overhead max{1, rh/c}.
- High-dimensionality is a practical bottleneck: Radon number r = d+2 implies O(d^3) per-node aggregation; experiments focus on small d and need high-d results or detailed analysis.
- Empirical claims about confidence amplification (Δ shrinking doubly-exponentially) are not directly validated; include calibration/error bars or conformal-style assessments.
- Baselines are incomplete; add strong single-node solvers (LIBLINEAR, Vowpal Wabbit) and well-tuned distributed ERM/ADMM baselines with I/O overhead reporting.
- Empirically compare Radon aggregation to other robust aggregators (geometric median, median-of-means, coordinate-wise median) to isolate advantages.
- Theoretical results appear sound under assumptions, but constants (e.g., in Proposition 2) and union-bound factors should be tightened and spelled out.
- Practical experiments are convincing but missing sensitivity analyses for h, d, and communication costs; report ablations, message sizes, and aggregation wall-clock times.
- Writing is clear overall but some imprecise statements (e.g., “rh iid subsets”); explicitly state sampling-without-replacement and all model/assumption details.
- Include a self-contained description of Radon-point computation complexity and numerical stability considerations.
- Aggregation-by-Radon seems novel relative to averaging, but potential overlap with earlier randomized convex optimization must be clarified to confirm originality.
- If properly positioned and extended, the method is significant as a practical parallel primitive with provable confidence amplification attractive to theory and systems communities.
- Pros for acceptance: simple, general black-box approach, formal guarantees, robust Radon aggregation, and strong empirical speedups.
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient related-work discussion, limited high-dimensional evaluation, and unaddressed iid vs. partitioning theoretical gap.
- Questions for authors: cite/contrast parallel randomized convex optimization and robust aggregation work; formalize sampling-without-replacement effects; report rh, c, deparallelization factor, and scaling curves vs. c and h.
- Additional author requests: provide high-dimensional experiments, quantify aggregation time and numerical stability, add stronger baselines and alternative aggregators, and include calibration metrics to substantiate confidence benefits.
- Overall recommendation: promising and well-executed but acceptance should depend on clarifying novelty relative to related theory and strengthening theoretical/empirical treatment of assumptions and high-dimensional regimes.
