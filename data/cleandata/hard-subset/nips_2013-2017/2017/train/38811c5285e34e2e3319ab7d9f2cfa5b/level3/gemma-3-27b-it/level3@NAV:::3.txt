## Review of “Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning”

This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms based on Radon partitioning, aiming to reduce runtime while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm to random data subsets and iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, offering a potentially significant step toward efficient parallelisation within Nick’s Class (NC). The authors motivate this work by highlighting the need for confident predictions in critical applications and the limitations of existing parallelisation techniques.

The contribution is theoretically sound, with a clear formalisation of the parallelisation scheme and a compelling analysis demonstrating the potential for reducing runtime to polylogarithmic time on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units – a notable result. The empirical evaluation, while limited in scope due to base learner scalability, provides evidence supporting the theoretical claims, demonstrating substantial speedups over sequential execution and competitive performance compared to existing parallel implementations in Spark. The authors proactively address potential concerns in their discussion, acknowledging limitations and outlining future research directions.

However, the paper is not without minor shortcomings. A minor typo exists on line 51 (“the the”). The math font on line 79 is noticeably smaller and hinders readability, and the font size in Figures 1-3 should be increased for better clarity. Figure 3, in particular, might be difficult to interpret if printed in grayscale. There’s a minor ambiguity in Lines 318-320 regarding whether the Radon machine's outperformance over averaging is based on paired comparisons or an average. Additionally, the use of "significant" on line 324 should be avoided unless referring to statistical significance. Typos appear on lines 340-341 ("machineachieved" and "machineis."). The results presentation on lines 341-349 suggests a minor difference between the proposed method and AverageAtTheEnd, which could benefit from being included in Figure 1 or from a more robust justification of the proposed method. Finally, the citation style is inconsistent on line 377 ("Long and servedio [22]").

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically supported algorithm for parallelising machine learning. The potential for achieving polylogarithmic runtime is significant, and the authors have clearly articulated the contribution and its limitations. The work addresses a challenging problem and provides a promising direction for future research.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Minor presentation issues (typos, font sizes) and a slight lack of clarity in the results presentation detract slightly from the overall quality. The empirical evaluation, while positive, is somewhat limited.

**Overall Recommendation:** Despite the minor issues, the strengths of this work significantly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** of this paper for NIPS. It represents a valuable contribution to the field and aligns well with the conference’s focus on innovative and impactful research in machine learning.
