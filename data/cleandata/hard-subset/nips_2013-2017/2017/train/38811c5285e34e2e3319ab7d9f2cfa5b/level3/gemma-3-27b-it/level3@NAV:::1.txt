## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, dubbed the Radon machine, designed to simplify adapting learning algorithms to large datasets and increasing predictive confidence. The core idea is to combine base learners by replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point, rather than through simple averaging, offering a black-box approach applicable to a wide class of algorithms. The authors provide a theoretical analysis including complexity analysis and a PAC bound, and validate their approach empirically with comparisons to Spark algorithms and base learners in Weka.

The work builds upon existing research in parallel learning, notably relating to averaging-based methods [32, 45, 46] and Nick's Class (NC) [9] concerning efficient parallel computation. The use of Radon points as a method for aggregation is a fresh perspective, particularly aiming for polylogarithmic runtime, a significant goal in parallel algorithm design. The connection drawn to the open question of efficient parallelization of regularized risk minimization is valuable, situating the work within a broader theoretical context.

A significant strength of this paper lies in its solid theoretical foundation. The complexity analysis and the PAC bound provide strong guarantees about the method’s performance. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the underlying theoretical principles and articulate them well. The claim of achieving polylogarithmic runtime, if validated, would be a substantial contribution. 

However, a major concern is the practicality and performance of the Radon machine on high-dimensional data, a common scenario in modern machine learning. The experiments, as rightly acknowledged by the authors, utilize relatively low-dimensional datasets (~18 features). While demonstrating the effectiveness on these datasets is a good starting point, it doesn’t address the crucial question of scalability. The Radon number, defined as d+2 for d-dimensional Euclidean spaces, becomes increasingly problematic as dimensionality grows. 

Furthermore, while the comparisons with Spark are informative, they are largely focused on scenarios where the Radon machine benefits from a lower dimensionality.  It's unclear how the method would fare in more complex, high-dimensional scenarios where Spark’s optimized implementations might excel.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical grounding with complexity analysis and PAC bounds.
*   Novel approach to parallelization leveraging Radon points.
*   Clear presentation and well-defined contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation on low-dimensional datasets.
*   Unclear scalability and performance on high-dimensional data.
*   Practical applicability to modern ML problems remains uncertain.



**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors should significantly expand their empirical evaluation to include high-dimensional datasets, investigate techniques to mitigate the impact of dimensionality on the Radon number (e.g., dimensionality reduction), and demonstrate the method’s competitive performance in realistic, high-dimensional machine learning scenarios. Addressing these concerns is crucial to establishing the practical value and broad applicability of the Radon machine.
