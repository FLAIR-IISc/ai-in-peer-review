## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper proposes a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms based on Radon points, aiming to reduce runtime while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The core idea is to leverage Radon points during aggregation in a parallelized learning process, offering a potentially more efficient alternative to standard averaging techniques. The authors position this work as contributing to the challenging question of achieving polylogarithmic runtime for machine learning algorithms, a goal related to Nick’s Class (NC). 

The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical foundation and the potential for significant speedups. The empirical evaluation, using datasets and comparisons to Spark-based algorithms and averaging, demonstrates a substantial reduction in runtime – often by a factor of 80-700 – with comparable accuracy. The work clearly articulates the trade-off: increased data size for reduced runtime. The experimental setup appears robust, utilizing cross-validation and comparisons against established libraries.

However, the paper has a significant weakness concerning originality. The authors’ theoretical contributions build heavily on concepts presented in “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” by Ma et al. (2018), which is conspicuously absent from the related work. The paper needs to *explicitly* address this omission and clearly delineate its novel contributions relative to Ma et al.’s work. Is the Radon point application a direct translation, or does it offer a significant improvement or adaptation? This is crucial for assessing the originality of the work. Moreover, the connection to Nick's Class feels somewhat tenuous; the paper doesn’t fully explain why achieving polylogarithmic time on *quasi*-polynomial processors is a relevant advancement in this context. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Demonstrated runtime reduction with maintained accuracy.
*   Clear theoretical grounding and analysis.
*   Well-designed empirical evaluation.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem: scaling ML algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Fails to acknowledge and contextualize related work (“Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization”).
*   The link to Nick’s Class needs strengthening and justification.
*   The novelty claim relies on a clearer differentiation from existing techniques.



**Overall:** This is a promising paper with potentially valuable practical implications. However, the authors *must* address the concerns regarding related work and originality. Pending a clear clarification of the contribution relative to Ma et al. (2018), I lean towards a **weak reject**. A major revision addressing this point, and potentially strengthening the connection to Nick’s Class, could warrant reconsideration.
