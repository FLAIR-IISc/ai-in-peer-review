

# Review for "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes the "Radon Machine," a parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms that uses Radon points for aggregation instead of standard averaging. The authors argue that their approach maintains theoretical performance guarantees while reducing runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity, at the cost of increased sample complexity and requiring quasi-polynomially many processing units. The method treats the underlying learning algorithm as a black box, requiring no additional mathematical derivations or dedicated code. The paper relates to prior work on parallel learning (e.g., averaging-based approaches [32, 45, 46]), but claims novelty through the use of Radon points, which they argue provide doubly-exponential confidence improvement with tree height compared to averaging.

The authors formalize their approach in the context of regularized risk minimization, proving theoretical guarantees for the Radon machine's error bounds and complexity. They empirically evaluate the approach against sequential base learners, averaging-at-the-end baselines, and Spark MLlib implementations across multiple datasets and learning algorithms (SVMs, logistic regression, regularized least squares).

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a novel theoretical approach to parallel learning with provable guarantees. The key insight that Radon points can provide doubly-exponential confidence improvement (Theorem 3) is mathematically sound and represents a clear theoretical advancement over averaging-based approaches.

2. **Empirical Validation**: The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines (sequential learning, averaging, Spark MLlib) across diverse datasets and learning algorithms (classification, regression, multi-class). The results consistently show significant speedups (80-700x) with comparable or improved predictive performance.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The paper demonstrates the feasibility of the approach through a Spark implementation, with detailed analysis of runtime scaling and practical considerations (e.g., handling dataset size, dimensionality).

4. **Clarity of Core Idea**: The Radon Machine algorithm (Algorithm 1) is straightforward to understand, and the paper clearly explains how it differs from standard averaging approaches.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Clear Novelty Statement**: The paper fails to adequately distinguish its theoretical contribution from prior work, particularly the paper "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (which appears to be the most relevant prior work on the theoretical approach). The authors should explicitly state what is new compared to this work and why it's significant.

2. **Limited Discussion of Practical Constraints**: While the paper mentions that the Radon machine requires a hypothesis space with finite Radon number (r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces), it doesn't sufficiently address how this limitation affects practical applications with high-dimensional data (e.g., text data where d is large). The discussion of high-dimensional data (Section 5) is somewhat superficial.

3. **Theoretical Scope Limitation**: The theoretical guarantees apply only to regularized risk minimization algorithms with convex hypothesis spaces. The paper doesn't adequately address how widely applicable this class of algorithms is in practice or how the approach might extend to non-convex settings, which are increasingly common in modern machine learning.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper presents technically sound theoretical results with careful proofs (Proposition 2, Theorem 3, Theorem 4). The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. However, the theoretical contribution is not clearly differentiated from prior work, and the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with related theoretical approaches.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The core algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly described, and the empirical results are presented effectively. The theoretical sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations of why Radon points provide the claimed advantages over averaging.

**Originality**: The use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation is novel relative to standard averaging approaches. However, the theoretical approach appears to build directly on prior work on parallel learning, and the paper doesn't sufficiently establish the novelty of the specific theoretical contribution compared to existing literature. The authors should clarify their relationship to "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" and other relevant theoretical work.

**Significance**: The paper addresses an important practical problem (parallelizing machine learning for large-scale data) with a theoretically grounded approach. The empirical results demonstrate significant speedups in realistic scenarios, which is valuable for practitioners. The theoretical advance toward answering the open question of efficient parallelization in Nick's Class (NC) is significant for the field.

## Recommendation
The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning with a novel theoretical approach and strong empirical validation. However, the lack of clear differentiation from prior theoretical work on parallel learning represents a significant weakness that needs to be addressed. The authors should revise the paper to more precisely articulate their novel contribution relative to the existing literature, particularly the "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" work.

Given the strong empirical results and the importance of the problem, I recommend **acceptance with major revision**. The paper has the potential to be a significant contribution if the authors can clearly establish the novelty of their theoretical approach and better position it relative to prior work.
