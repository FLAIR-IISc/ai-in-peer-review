

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme called the "Radon Machine" that effectively parallelises a broad class of regularised risk minimisation algorithms (e.g., SVMs, regularised least squares, logistic regression) without requiring mathematical derivations or dedicated code. The key insight is using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses from parallel learning runs, which enables doubly-exponential confidence improvement with the height of an aggregation tree. The authors prove that this scheme achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees, addressing an open question regarding efficient parallelisation in Nick's Class (NC).

The paper makes strong contributions in both theory and practice. The theoretical analysis (Propositions 2, 3, and Theorem 4) provides rigorous bounds on the sample complexity and runtime reduction, connecting their work to fundamental questions in parallel computing. The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups (80-700x) with comparable or better predictive performance across multiple datasets and algorithms compared to both base learners and Spark MLlib implementations. The authors thoughtfully address limitations in the Discussion section, including applicability to high-dimensional and non-linear models, communication complexity, and scalability with limited processors.

Regarding the four review criteria:

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs are rigorous and the empirical methodology is sound with 10-fold cross-validation and multiple baselines. The authors appropriately acknowledge the trade-off between runtime reduction and increased sample complexity. The theoretical guarantees are carefully derived and well-motivated.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, though some formatting issues detract from readability. The math font appears smaller in line 79, and figures 1-3 require larger text for better readability. The authors could improve clarity by addressing the minor language issues (e.g., avoiding "significant" without statistical context in line 324) and fixing typos (e.g., "machineachieved" in lines 340-341).

**Originality**: This work significantly advances the field by providing a general, provably effective parallelisation scheme that differs substantially from prior approaches (e.g., averaging-based methods or stochastic gradient descent). The connection to Radon points from convex geometry is novel for machine learning parallelisation. The authors properly contextualize their work against relevant prior literature, including Vitter and Lin (1985), Long and Servedio (2010), and recent Spark MLlib approaches.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a critical bottleneck in machine learning - scaling algorithms to handle growing data volumes while maintaining confidence in predictions. The practical speedups demonstrated (80-700x) with comparable predictive performance represent a meaningful advance for real-world applications, particularly in high-stakes domains where confidence is paramount. The black-box nature of the parallelisation scheme makes it broadly applicable to existing ML algorithms.

**Pro-acceptance arguments**:
- Novel theoretical approach with strong guarantees
- Substantial empirical speedups across multiple datasets
- Clear connection to fundamental open questions in parallel computing
- Thorough discussion of limitations and future work
- Practical implementation with real-world validation

**Con-acceptance arguments**:
- Minor formatting and language issues (e.g., figure readability, typo in "machineachieved")
- The difference between Radon machine and averaging-at-the-end baseline appears modest in some cases (lines 341-349)
- Slightly inconsistent citation style ("Long and servedio [22]" vs. other citations)

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of machine learning parallelisation, addressing a significant practical challenge with both theoretical rigor and empirical validation. The minor formatting and language issues noted are easily addressable in the camera-ready version. The work is well-positioned to advance the state of the art in scalable machine learning and aligns well with NIPS's focus on high-quality scientific contributions to machine learning.

I recommend acceptance. The theoretical contributions are novel and well-supported, and the empirical results demonstrate clear practical value. The authors have thoughtfully addressed potential concerns in their discussion, making this a strong candidate for presentation at NIPS.
