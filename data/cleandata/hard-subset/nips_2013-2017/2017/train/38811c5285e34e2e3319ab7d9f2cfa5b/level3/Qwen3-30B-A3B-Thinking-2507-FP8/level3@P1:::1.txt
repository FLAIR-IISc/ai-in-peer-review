

This paper introduces the Radon Machine, a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The key insight is replacing the standard averaging of hypotheses with Radon points in an aggregation tree, which provides doubly exponential confidence improvements with tree height. The authors demonstrate that this approach can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms, addressing an open question about efficient parallelization in the sense of Nick's Class.

The paper makes several strong theoretical contributions. The authors prove that the Radon Machine achieves error probability bounds that decrease doubly exponentially with the number of aggregation steps (Theorem 3), which is a significant improvement over previous averaging-based approaches that don't achieve such strong guarantees. The formal analysis is thorough, with clear proofs of the relationship between the base algorithm's sample complexity and the Radon Machine's performance. The theoretical results are well-positioned within the context of parallel learning, with appropriate references to previous work by Long and Servedio (2014) and Vitter and Lin (1985) on NC-learnability.

The empirical evaluation is convincing within its scope, showing 80-700x speedups on moderate-sized datasets using 150 processors compared to sequential implementations. The Radon Machine outperforms both averaging-at-the-end baselines (by at least 10% in AUC) and Spark MLlib implementations (by up to an order of magnitude in runtime). The authors carefully validate their approach across multiple datasets and learning algorithms, including SVMs, logistic regression, and regularized least squares, with appropriate statistical validation through 10-fold cross-validation.

The paper's greatest strength is its solid theoretical foundation with clear, provable guarantees that address a fundamental question in parallel machine learning. The black-box nature of the approach—requiring no modifications to the base learning algorithm—is particularly appealing for practitioners. The work significantly advances the state of the art in parallel learning by providing a method that achieves polylogarithmic runtime, which previous approaches could not demonstrate.

However, the paper has a significant limitation in its experimental validation. All experiments use datasets with very low dimensionality (at most 18 features), which represents a narrow scope compared to modern machine learning challenges. The Radon number for Euclidean spaces is d+2, meaning the complexity grows with dimensionality, yet the authors do not demonstrate how the method scales to high-dimensional data where this could become a bottleneck. The discussion of high-dimensional data in Section 5 is limited to theoretical suggestions about random projections without empirical validation.

The connection to practical applications is somewhat underdeveloped. While the authors mention critical applications requiring high confidence (e.g., medical domains), they don't provide specific case studies demonstrating the Radon Machine's value in such scenarios. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the method handles the communication overhead in distributed settings, particularly since communication costs can dominate parallel implementations.

The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-structured sections and clear mathematical notation. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is particularly accessible, and the theoretical results are well-motivated. The writing is professional and appropriate for NIPS.

In terms of significance, the work addresses a fundamental open problem in parallel learning with clear theoretical and practical implications. The potential for substantial speedups across a broad class of learning algorithms is highly significant for the machine learning community. However, the limited experimental scope on low-dimensional data somewhat diminishes the perceived significance, as it's unclear how the method will perform on the high-dimensional datasets that dominate modern machine learning applications.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contributions with provable guarantees, clear black-box parallelization approach, significant empirical speedups on moderate datasets, well-positioned within the literature.

Arguments against acceptance: Limited experimental validation on high-dimensional data, insufficient discussion of scalability challenges, and minimal connection to real-world high-stakes applications beyond theoretical claims.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the theory of parallel machine learning with clear practical benefits. The main concern is the limited scope of the empirical evaluation, which should be addressed through additional experiments on higher-dimensional datasets or a more thorough theoretical discussion of the dimensionality limitations. The authors should clarify how the method would scale to modern high-dimensional problems, as this is critical for assessing the paper's significance. With these additions, this work would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, the theoretical contributions are compelling, but the practical significance is somewhat limited by the narrow experimental scope.
