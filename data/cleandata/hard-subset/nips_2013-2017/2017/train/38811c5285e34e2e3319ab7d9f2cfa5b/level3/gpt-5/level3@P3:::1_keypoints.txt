- Proposes the Radon machine: partition data into r^h subsets, train r^h base learners, and aggregate via Radon points (r = d+2) along a tree to parallelize regularized risk minimization.
- Main theoretical claim: taking Radon points yields double-exponential reduction in failure probability with tree height h, enabling (ε,Δ)-performance in polylogarithmic time under processor and Radon-number assumptions.
- Provides PAC-style bounds and a complexity analysis for runtime, sample complexity, and communication.
- Empirically demonstrates 80–700× wall-clock speedups on low-dimensional datasets (≈18 features) with linear models, maintaining comparable AUC.
- Consistently outperforms simple averaging-at-the-end in accuracy at similar runtimes.
- Novelty: using Radon-point aggregation instead of averaging is a new, black-box mechanism with stronger robustness and confidence-amplification guarantees.
- Strength: solid, elegant theory showing double-exponential confidence amplification and transparent tradeoffs among data, processors, and aggregation depth.
- Strength: practical demonstration on commodity clusters and clear algorithmic presentation (Algorithm 1 and proofs).
- Major concern: scalability in high dimensions is unclear because r = d+2 inflates branching, Radon computation costs O(r^3), and data splitting grows explosively with d and h.
- Experiments are limited to very low-dimensional linear models and do not address common high-dimensional ML settings (sparse text, large recommender systems, deep embeddings).
- The NC-style efficiency claim hinges on the Radon number being polylogarithmic in sample size, effectively assuming low effective dimension—this limitation should be made explicit.
- Practical assumption δ ≤ 1/2r for base learners may be nontrivial for large r; the paper needs guidance on ensuring or verifying this in practice.
- Empirical section does not stress-test the method where theory predicts difficulties (large d) nor quantify Radon computation overhead as d grows.
- Clarity suggestions: make dependence on d explicit in theorems, provide a recipe for choosing h given processors and (ε,Δ), and discuss numerical stability/solvers for Radon-point computation.
- Requests: add high-dimensional sparse experiments (10^5–10^6 features) comparing runtime, accuracy, and communication to Spark/parameter-averaging/CoCoA/DANE.
- Requests: measure cost of Radon-point computations, sensitivity to h in large d, and evaluate proposed remedies (random projections/random features and block-coordinate Radon aggregation) with empirical tradeoffs.
- Requests: clarify practicality of the δ ≤ 1/2r assumption and provide experiments when available processors are far fewer than r^h (deparallelized regime).
- Pros summary: strong theory, simple black-box aggregation, low communication, and large speedups on low-dimensional data.
- Cons summary: unproven scalability with dimensionality, limited empirical scope, and unclear applicability to nonparametric or nonconvex models.
- Recommendation: borderline accept pending clarification and empirical substantiation of scalability to high-dimensional settings or explicit scoping to low-effective-dimensional regimes.
