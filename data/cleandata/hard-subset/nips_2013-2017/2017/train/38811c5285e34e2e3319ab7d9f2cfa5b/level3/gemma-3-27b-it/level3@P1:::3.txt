This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme, the Radon machine, for machine learning algorithms, offering a compelling combination of theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The core idea leverages Radon points to aggregate hypotheses computed on random data subsets, achieving polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial number of processors, and a trade-off of increased sample complexity. The work is well-motivated, addressing a crucial challenge in modern machine learning—scaling to large datasets—and positions itself within the context of Nick’s Class and existing parallel learning approaches. The authors convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of their scheme through comprehensive experiments, showing improvements in both runtime and, in some cases, predictive accuracy compared to established baselines.

The theoretical contribution is particularly noteworthy. The authors clearly define the conditions under which their parallelization scheme is effective, providing a rigorous analysis of its sample and runtime complexity. By connecting the problem to the theoretical framework of Nick’s Class, they place their work within a broader landscape of parallel computation and contribute to the ongoing discussion of efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms.  The concept of using Radon points for aggregation is innovative and provides a provable advantage over simpler averaging techniques, as highlighted by Proposition 2 and Theorem 3.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-presented. The authors compare the Radon machine against both base learners and state-of-the-art parallel algorithms from Spark’s MLlib, utilizing a variety of datasets and carefully controlling for experimental parameters. The consistent speed-up observed across multiple datasets, particularly the 700-fold improvement on the SUSY dataset, is substantial. The fact that the Radon machine maintains comparable predictive performance to other methods while significantly reducing runtime is a strong endorsement of its practical potential.  The investigation into the runtime dependency on dataset size (Figure 3) is insightful and adds to the understanding of the scheme’s scalability.

The authors demonstrate a commendable awareness of potential limitations and proactively address them in the Discussion section. They thoughtfully consider the implications of high-dimensional data, non-linear models, limited processing units, and communication complexity, offering potential avenues for future research.  The exploration of applicability to distributed data streams and connections to federated learning further broaden the scope of their work.

However, there are a few areas where the presentation could be improved. A minor typo exists on line 51 ("the the" should be "the"). The mathematical notation on line 79 appears slightly smaller and less readable than the surrounding text. Furthermore, the font size in Figures 1-3 is a bit small, potentially hindering readability, especially in printed form. Figure 3 might be difficult to interpret when printed in black and white.

A point of clarification is needed regarding the comparison with the averaging baseline (lines 318-320). It would strengthen the argument to explicitly state whether the observed outperformance is based on an average of paired comparisons or another statistical measure. The use of the word "significant" on line 324 should be avoided unless referring to statistical significance. Minor typographical errors exist on lines 340-341 ("machineachieved" and "machineis"). Finally, while the authors acknowledge a minor difference in performance compared to averaging-at-the-end, this result could be better integrated into Figure 1 or further justified to emphasize the advantages of the proposed Radon machine. There is a citation style inconsistency on line 377 (“Long and servedio [22]”).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with provable guarantees.
*   Compelling empirical results demonstrating significant speed-up and comparable accuracy.
*   Clear and well-motivated problem statement.
*   Thorough and insightful discussion of limitations and future work.
*   Novel use of Radon points for parallel learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor presentation issues regarding font sizes and typographical errors.
*   Need for clarification regarding the statistical significance of the averaging baseline comparison.
*   The performance difference against the averaging baseline could be more strongly justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite these minor points, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The Radon machine offers a practical and theoretically sound approach to scaling learning algorithms, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated its effectiveness.  I recommend acceptance to NIPS 2017.
