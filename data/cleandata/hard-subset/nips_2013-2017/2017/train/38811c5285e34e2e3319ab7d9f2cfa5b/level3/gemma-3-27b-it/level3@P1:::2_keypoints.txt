- Introduces a novel parallelization scheme for ML using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses, aiming for polylog runtime with quasi-polynomial processors.
- Claims black-box applicability across learning algorithms without algorithm-specific derivations or coding.
- Builds on regularized risk minimization and leverages Radon point properties for faster (doubly exponential) convergence compared to averaging.
- Provides theoretical guarantees on runtime reduction and bounds relating sample complexity, data size, and processing units.
- Empirical evaluation shows significant speedups over single-processor, averaging-based, and Spark MLlib baselines with comparable or better predictive performance.
- Strength: novel use of Radon points offering a different parallel-aggregation perspective with potentially superior convergence.
- Strength: solid theoretical analysis that justifies effectiveness and limitations of the scheme.
- Strength: well-conducted practical experiments across multiple datasets demonstrating consistent speedups.
- Strength: clear presentation and organization, making mathematical concepts and scheme accessible.
- Weakness: lacks thorough related-work discussion, notably omission of Jaggi et al. (2014) on parallel randomized convex optimization, which questions novelty.
- Weakness: limited discussion of practical considerations like communication overhead and synchronization costs in parallel settings.
- Weakness: reliance on finite Radon number for hypothesis spaces may limit applicability; suggested workarounds need deeper exploration.
- Weakness: empirical evaluation focuses on moderately sized datasets; scalability to very large datasets is insufficiently demonstrated.
- Weakness: missing direct empirical validation linking the theoretical runtime reductions to observed speedups.
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, empirically promising, broadly applicable black-box method addressing scalable learning.
- Against acceptance: inadequate related-work positioning, unresolved assumptions (finite Radon number), limited scalability and practical analysis.
- Overall assessment: promising approach but major revision needed to address related work (especially Jaggi et al.), expand scalability experiments, and provide detailed practical analysis; current recommendation — reject with invitation to revise.
