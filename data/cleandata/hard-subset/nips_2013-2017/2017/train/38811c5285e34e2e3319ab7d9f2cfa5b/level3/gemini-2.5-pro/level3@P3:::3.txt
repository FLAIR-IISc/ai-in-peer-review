### Summary

This paper introduces the "Radon machine," a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to partition the data, train a base learning algorithm on each subset in parallel to obtain initial hypotheses, and then hierarchically aggregate these hypotheses using Radon points. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this aggregation method reduces the probability of generating a poor hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. This theoretical result allows them to argue that their scheme can reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic on quasi-polynomially many processors, making a significant contribution to the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization is in Nick's Class (NC). The theoretical claims are supported by an empirical study showing substantial speedups and competitive, or superior, predictive performance compared to sequential base learners, state-of-the-art parallel implementations in Spark MLlib, and the common baseline of hypothesis averaging.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this work lies in its novel application of Radon points for model aggregation. This is a departure from more common techniques like parameter averaging. The theoretical motivation is strong, and the analysis leading to a doubly-exponential reduction in the failure probability (Theorem 3) is elegant and interesting. Connecting this result to the potential for polylogarithmic runtime (Theorem 4) places the work within a fundamental line of inquiry in theoretical computer science and machine learning concerning the parallel complexity of learning.

2.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper includes a convincing set of experiments. The Radon machine demonstrates significant training time improvements (speedups of 80-700x) over sequential base learners while maintaining comparable predictive accuracy. More importantly, it shows competitive or superior performance in terms of both runtime and AUC against established parallel learning algorithms in Spark MLlib and a direct "averaging-at-the-end" baseline across multiple large datasets.

3.  **Black-Box Applicability:** A key practical advantage is that the proposed scheme treats the base learning algorithm as a black box. This makes it broadly applicable without requiring algorithm-specific derivations or re-implementations, lowering the barrier to adoption for practitioners.

4.  **Thoroughness and Honesty:** The authors proactively address several potential limitations and concerns in the Discussion section, such as applicability to high-dimensional or non-linear models, scenarios with a limited number of processors, and communication complexity. This demonstrates a careful and complete consideration of the work's scope and context.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are several points that could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact.

1.  **Presentation and Minor Errors:** The manuscript would benefit from a careful proofreading pass to correct minor but distracting issues.
    *   Typo: Line 51, "the the" should be "the".
    *   Typos/Spacing: Lines 340–341, "machineachieved" and "machineis." are missing spaces.
    *   Formatting: The math font in Line 79 appears smaller than the surrounding text, making it difficult to read.
    *   Figure Readability: The text in Figures 1–3 is too small and should be enlarged for better readability. Furthermore, Figure 3 relies on color distinctions that may be lost when printed in black and white; using different markers or line styles would be beneficial.
    *   Citation Style: The citation in Line 377, "Long and servedio [22]," is inconsistent with the format used for other citations.

2.  **Clarification on Empirical Comparison:** The comparison with the averaging baseline needs to be more precise.
    *   Lines 318–320: The text states, "the Radon machine outperforms the averaging-at-the-end baseline on all datasets by at least 10%." It is unclear if this refers to an average of the AUC scores across datasets, or if the Radon machine outperforms averaging by at least 10 percentage points of AUC on every single dataset. Please clarify how this comparison was calculated. Is it based on an average of paired comparisons?
    *   Line 324: The authors should avoid using the word "significant" (or "substantially") unless statistical significance has been established and is reported.

3.  **Justification of Advantage over Averaging:** The paper's core novelty is the use of Radon points over simple averaging. However, the empirical justification for this added complexity could be stronger.
    *   Lines 341–349: The text notes that the runtime of the Radon machine is "comparable to averaging-at-the-end," and Figure 4 seems to visually support this. Given that calculating a Radon point is more complex than averaging, the benefit in predictive performance must be clear. While a 10% improvement in AUC is mentioned, the visual difference in Figure 4 between the Radon machine and Avg-h=max appears minor on some datasets (e.g., wikidata). The authors should consider including the "AverageAtTheEnd" results directly in Figure 1 for a more side-by-side comparison, or otherwise provide a more forceful argument to advocate for their method over this simpler and highly intuitive baseline.

### Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a novel, theoretically-grounded, and empirically-supported algorithm for parallelizing a wide class of machine learning models. The connection to fundamental questions in parallel complexity theory is a significant intellectual contribution, and the empirical results are compelling. The weaknesses are primarily related to presentation and the need for minor clarifications, which can be addressed in a revision. I recommend acceptance.
