This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, based on Radon partitioning. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the scheme, as well as empirical results demonstrating its effectiveness.

The paper's main contribution is a theoretical framework for parallelising consistent and efficient regularised risk minimisation algorithms, which can reduce the runtime of these algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units. This is a significant step towards answering an open question on the efficient parallelisation of machine learning algorithms.

The authors also present an empirical evaluation of their parallelisation scheme, comparing it to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms from the Spark machine learning library, as well as the natural baseline of averaging hypotheses instead of calculating their Radon point. The results show that the proposed parallelisation scheme achieves a substantial reduction in computation time while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The paper is well-written, and the authors have done a good job of addressing potential concerns and limitations of their approach. The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-motivated, and the empirical results are convincing.

However, there are a few minor issues with the paper. There are a few typos and formatting issues, and some of the figures could be improved for readability. Additionally, the authors could provide more clarification on some of the points they make, such as the comparison between the Radon machine and the averaging baseline.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is interesting and well-motivated, and the empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed parallelisation scheme.
