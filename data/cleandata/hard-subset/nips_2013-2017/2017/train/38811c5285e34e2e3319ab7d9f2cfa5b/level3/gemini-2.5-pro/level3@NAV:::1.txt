This paper introduces the Radon machine, a novel parallel machine learning framework for regularised risk minimisation. The core idea is to train base learners on disjoint subsets of data and then aggregate the resulting hypotheses through a tree-based structure. Unlike popular ensemble methods like bagging that rely on averaging or voting, this method iteratively replaces subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point. The authors position this as a general, black-box parallelisation scheme. The paper's main theoretical contributions are a complexity analysis suggesting the potential for polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors—connecting the problem to Nick’s Class—and a PAC-style bound demonstrating a doubly-exponential improvement in confidence with each aggregation step.

The primary strength of this work lies in its solid theoretical underpinning. The derivation of the PAC bound for the aggregated hypothesis is elegant and provides strong formal guarantees, a welcome contribution in the domain of parallel ensembles where analysis is often based on looser bounds or empirical results. The application of Radon's theorem to model aggregation is original and offers a fresh perspective on combining weak learners.

However, a major concern arises from the method's practicality on high-dimensional data. The Radon number in a d-dimensional space is d+2, implying that the number of base learners required for each aggregation step scales linearly with the feature dimension. This dependence is a significant practical limitation for many modern machine learning problems where the dimensionality can be in the thousands or millions. This theoretical concern is amplified by the empirical evaluation. The experiments demonstrate impressive speed-ups but are confined to very low-dimensional datasets (e.g., 18 features). While these results are promising, they do not provide evidence that the Radon machine is a viable approach for the high-dimensional settings where parallelisation is often most critical. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest dimensionality reduction as a potential solution, but without empirical validation, the method's significance for a broad range of real-world applications remains unclear.

In summary, the paper presents a theoretically sound and original idea. However, its claimed practical impact is not sufficiently supported due to the scalability concerns with high-dimensional data. To strengthen the paper, I strongly recommend the authors explicitly address this issue. This could be achieved by including experiments on higher-dimensional datasets (perhaps combined with the proposed dimensionality reduction techniques) or providing a more detailed theoretical discussion on the method's behavior and limitations as dimensionality grows. Without this, the contribution, while theoretically interesting, may be of limited use to practitioners.
