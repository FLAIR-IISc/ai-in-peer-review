This paper proposes a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, which combines base learners by replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point instead of simple averaging. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed Radon machine, including complexity analysis and a PAC bound, demonstrating its efficiency and effectiveness. The empirical evaluation compares the Radon machine with parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Weka, showing promising results.

The paper relates to previous work on parallelising machine learning algorithms, particularly in the context of Nick's Class (NC) and its variants. The authors discuss the limitations of existing approaches, such as averaging-based parallelisations and stochastic gradient descent, and highlight the advantages of their proposed method. The paper also touches on the topic of high-dimensional data, mentioning the potential use of random projections or low-rank approximations to alleviate concerns.

The strengths of the paper lie in its solid theoretical analysis and guarantees. The authors provide a comprehensive derivation of the sample and runtime complexity of the Radon machine, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates its potential in practice. The paper is well-written, and the authors have made a clear effort to explain complex concepts in an accessible manner.

However, there are some concerns regarding the practicality and performance of the method on high-dimensional data. The experiments use very low-dimensional datasets (~18 features), which may not reflect modern machine learning problems. It is unclear how the method would scale to and handle high-dimensional data, and the authors should clarify or demonstrate this in future work.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques, specifically the use of Radon points for aggregating hypotheses. While the idea of using Radon points is not new, its application in the context of machine learning is innovative and shows promise.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of machine learning algorithms, particularly in high-confidence domains. The authors demonstrate the applicability of the Radon machine to various learning problems, including classification and regression, and show that it can achieve similar predictive performance to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms while requiring substantially less runtime.

Overall, the paper presents a well-researched and theoretically sound contribution to the field of machine learning. While there are some concerns regarding its practicality and performance on high-dimensional data, the authors have made a clear effort to address these concerns and provide a solid foundation for future work.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* Solid theoretical analysis and guarantees
* Novel combination of familiar techniques
* Potential to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of machine learning algorithms
* Applicability to various learning problems
* Empirical evaluation demonstrates promising results

Arguments con acceptance:

* Practicality and performance on high-dimensional data are unclear
* Experiments use very low-dimensional datasets, which may not reflect modern machine learning problems
* Further work is needed to demonstrate the scalability and effectiveness of the method on high-dimensional data.

Recommendations for the authors:

* Clarify or demonstrate how the method scales to and handles high-dimensional data
* Provide additional experiments or analysis to address concerns regarding practicality and performance
* Consider exploring the application of the Radon machine to other learning problems or domains.
