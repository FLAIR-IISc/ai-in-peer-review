- Proposal: Introduces the "Radon machine" that trains base learners on random data subsets and iteratively aggregates hypotheses with Radon points, claiming polylogarithmic runtime using a quasi-polynomial number of processors.  
- Empirical evaluation: Compares to no parallelization, averaging, and Spark-based baselines, showing substantial runtime speedups and comparable or improved accuracy.  
- Problem significance: Tackles the important problem of scaling ML algorithms to large datasets with a general, black-box approach.  
- Key novelty claim: Uses Radon points instead of averaging for aggregation, argued to yield a doubly-exponential increase in confidence during aggregation.  
- Generality advantage: Black-box method reduces need for algorithm-specific parallelization derivations.  
- Theoretical guarantees: Provides theoretical results (polylogarithmic runtime) under specific assumptions about the hypothesis space.  
- Presentation quality: Paper is generally well-written and organized.  
- Major omission: Fails to cite and discuss Braverman & Moscibroda (2013), which also leverages Radon points—this omission is a serious concern.  
- Originality concern: Unclear what is uniquely contributed beyond prior work; authors must explicitly differentiate their contributions.  
- Assumptions limitation: Theoretical guarantees depend on finite dimensionality, convexity, and finite Radon number, which may not hold broadly in practice.  
- Need for clearer discussion: Workarounds are mentioned but the limitations and applicability should be more thoroughly discussed.  
- Complexity of analysis: Sample and runtime complexity analysis is dense and may be hard to follow without strong optimization background.  
- Acceptance arguments: Strong empirical results, formal analysis, important problem addressed, and broad applicability support acceptance.  
- Rejection arguments: Missing related-work citation undermines novelty, possible incremental theory, and restrictive assumptions support rejection.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject pending major revisions—authors must address the prior work (Braverman & Moscibroda), clarify unique contributions, and justify novelty to be reconsidered.
