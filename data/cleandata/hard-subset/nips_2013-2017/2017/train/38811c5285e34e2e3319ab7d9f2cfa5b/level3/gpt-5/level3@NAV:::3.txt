Summary and relation to prior work: This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization that aggregates base learners via iterated Radon points rather than averaging. The core theoretical result shows doubly-exponential confidence amplification with tree height and, under standard assumptions (finite Radon number, convex hypothesis class), achieves polylogarithmic training time on quasi-polynomially many processors. This connects parallel learning to Nick’s Class and complements prior parallelization lines, including averaging-based schemes, distributed SGD/ADMM, and NC-learnability results (e.g., Long and Servedio on half-spaces). Empirically, the method yields large training-time reductions and comparable or improved AUC relative to strong Spark baselines and averaging-at-the-end.

Quality: The theory is sound and well-motivated; Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are clear and useful. The runtime/sample complexity discussion is careful about costs (increased data and processors). Experiments span multiple datasets and baselines, and the authors proactively discuss limitations (Radon number, processor counts, high-dimensional/nonlinear cases), which I appreciate. Please add statistical tests for AUC/runtime where claims of superiority are made.

Clarity: Generally well-organized, but several presentation issues hinder readability (see minor notes). Please also clarify how comparisons are aggregated.

Originality: Using Radon points for aggregation to amplify confidence and obtain NC-style guarantees for a broad class of learners is novel and interesting, beyond prior averaging-based approaches.

Significance: The work is likely to influence practical parallel training where confidence guarantees matter, and it advances theoretical understanding of when polylog-time parallelization is attainable.

Specific questions and minor issues:
- Clarification (Lines 318–320): When stating that the Radon machine outperforms averaging, is this based on an average of paired comparisons across folds/datasets? Report variance and paired tests.
- Results presentation (Lines 341–349): The difference vs AverageAtTheEnd appears minor in runtime; please include this in Figure 1 or more strongly justify the method’s practical advantage.
- Language (Line 324): Avoid “significant” unless referring to statistical significance; add tests or rephrase.
- Typos/formatting: Line 51: “the the” -> “the”; Line 79: math font is smaller/hard to read; Lines 340–341: “machineachieved”, “machineis”.
- Figures: 1–3 need larger fonts; Fig. 3 should be distinguishable in grayscale (use markers/line styles).
- Citation style: Line 377: “Long and servedio [22]” should match other citations (capitalize “Servedio”).

Pros for acceptance: strong, general theoretical contribution; black-box practicality; substantial runtime reductions; broad empirical support; thorough discussion of concerns. Cons: reliance on many processors/data; readability issues; need clearer, statistically grounded comparisons.

Recommendation: Accept.
