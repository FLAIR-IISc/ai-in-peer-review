- Proposal: Radon machine—black-box parallelization that aggregates base learners by replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point instead of averaging.  
- Setting and guarantees: Targets regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean spaces with PAC-style (ε, Δ) guarantees; probability of an ε-bad aggregate decays doubly exponentially with aggregation depth.  
- Complexity claim: With Radon number r = d + 2 and quasi-polynomially many processors, runtime can be polylogarithmic in sample complexity, connecting to NC-style efficient parallel learning.  
- Empirical results: Large speedups and comparable AUC versus Spark MLlib and Weka linear models on low-dimensional (~18 features) datasets.  
- Theoretical quality: Analysis is careful and sound; Radon-point aggregation guarantee is clean and iteratively well-motivated; assumptions are explicitly stated (finite-dimensional convex class, δ ≤ 1/2r).  
- Practicality concern: Dependence on r = d + 2 is critical—aggregation costs O(r^3) per node and splitting into rh parts causes sample/processor blow-up as d grows.  
- Empirical limitation: All datasets are very low-dimensional, so scalability to realistic high-dimensional regimes remains untested.  
- Clarity suggestions: Make dependence of costs on d, r, and h explicit in the main text and operationally discuss the δ ≤ 1/2r requirement.  
- Originality: Novel application of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation, clearly distinct from averaging and optimization-centric parallel methods.  
- Potential significance: Could be impactful as a plug-and-play parallelization tool with guarantees if it scales to high-dimensional, sparse settings.  
- Experimental suggestions: Demonstrate scaling on high-dimensional sparse data, report wall-clock/accuracy/communication/memory, perform sensitivity analyses over d, r, h, and try c < rh processors.  
- Method-extension suggestions: Evaluate random projections/sketching or block-coordinate variants and quantify the accuracy–speed trade-off.  
- Theoretical clarification needed: Explain regimes where Theorem 4’s assumption (Radon number polylog in sample complexity) holds when d grows with N.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Solid and novel theoretical framework, simple black-box applicability, promising speedups, and clear links to NC learning and prior distributed methods.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Unclear practicality for high-dimensional models, empirical evaluation limited to low-dimensional linear models, and missing evidence for performance with limited processors/communication.  
- Recommendation: Borderline—acceptance contingent on strengthening high-dimensional experiments and scaling analyses.
