- Introduces the Radon machine, a novel parallelisation scheme that replaces subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point instead of averaging.
- Acts as a black-box alternative to bagging, enabling combination of weak learners without algorithm-specific code.
- Provides solid theoretical analysis, including complexity analysis and a PAC bound showing efficiency when samples are split across parts.
- Claims potential to reduce runtime to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processors, addressing NC-style parallelisation.
- Paper is well-organised and clearly written, making arguments easy to follow.
- Empirical evaluation compares Radon machine to Spark parallel algorithms and Weka base models, showing practical potential.
- Strength: general framework applicable to a broad class of learning algorithms without further mathematical derivations.
- Strength: thorough analysis of sample and runtime complexity.
- Concern: experiments use very low-dimensional datasets (~18 features), which may not reflect modern ML problems.
- Concern: practicality and performance on high-dimensional data are unclear and need demonstration.
- Concern: applicability to non-linear models is not shown and requires further research.
- Originality: novel use of Radon points to combine hypotheses, related to prior parallel learning work.
- Significance: advances understanding of how efficiently polynomial-time learning algorithms can be parallelised.
- Recommendation: accept, but request additional experiments/analysis to demonstrate scaling to high-dimensional data and clarify applicability to non-linear models.
