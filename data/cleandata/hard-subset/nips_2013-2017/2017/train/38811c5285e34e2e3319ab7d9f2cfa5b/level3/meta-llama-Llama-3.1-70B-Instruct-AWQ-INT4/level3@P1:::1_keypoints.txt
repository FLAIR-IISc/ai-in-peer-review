- Proposes the Radon machine, a novel parallelisation scheme that combines base learners by replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point instead of averaging.  
- Claims theoretical and empirical efficiency gains over traditional bagging and parallel methods.  
- Provides solid theoretical analysis including complexity results showing advantages when samples are split and a PAC bound guaranteeing performance.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing the Radon machine to Spark parallel algorithms and base linear models in Weka.  
- Experiments use very low-dimensional datasets (~18 features), raising concerns about applicability to modern, high-dimensional problems.  
- Unclear how the Radon machine scales and performs on high-dimensional data; practicality is not demonstrated.  
- Recommendation: clarify or demonstrate scaling behavior and handling of high-dimensional datasets.  
- Recommendation: add experiments on higher-dimensional datasets to better evaluate performance.  
- Strengths: novel combination method, strong theoretical guarantees, and well-executed empirical work.  
- Weaknesses: potential limitations in practicality for high-dimensional problems and limited experimental dimensionality.  
- Originality: presents a significant, original approach to combining base learners and novel theoretical contributions.  
- Clarity and quality: paper is well-written, easy to follow, and technically sound.
