- Proposes the Radon machine: a black-box, tree-based aggregation using Radon points for parallelized regularized ERM in Euclidean parameter spaces.
- Main theoretical guarantee: h aggregation levels yield P(Q(r_h) > ε) ≤ (r δ)^(2^h), giving doubly-exponential reduction in confidence error with tree height.
- Runtime claim: dominated by base learner on small subsets plus h aggregations of r×r linear systems; authors argue for polylog runtime in target sample size under strong Radon-number assumptions.
- Empirically implemented on Spark and compared to base learners, averaging baseline, and Spark MLlib.
- Strong wall-clock speedups observed (80–700× vs. base learner on 150 processors) with similar or better predictive performance than averaging.
- Distinguishing novelty: use of Radon points and their robustness/depth properties for aggregation, connected to classical Radon/centerpoint theory.
- Missing related work: insufficient discussion and comparison to “parallelizing randomized convex optimization” and robust aggregators (geometric median, median-of-means).
- Critical limitation: NC-style polylog-time result depends on Radon number r being polylogarithmic in sample complexity, but in R^d r = d+2, so theory requires d = O(polylog N).
- Processor/sample blowup: achieving the doubly-exponential guarantee requires rh base models and N = rh n, leading to large processor and data requirements for moderate h and d.
- Empirical section does not validate theoretical sample-complexity tradeoffs since it fixes processors and N; need quantification of practical h given realistic N and d.
- Scope of experiments limited to linear models; unclear how random features/low-rank preprocessing affect theoretical guarantees and sample-complexity constants.
- Complexity accounting for Radon computation is incomplete: O(r^3) per node ignores dependence on d (e.g., forming combinations costs O(r d)); full cost should be clarified.
- Independence assumptions in proofs should be made explicit: data partitioning ensures independence across tree nodes and must be stated in main text.
- Theory is sound under assumptions and clearly presented, but strongest claims hinge on restrictive dimensionality and large processor budgets.
- Paper is well organized and readable; Algorithm 1 and main propositions/theorems are clear.
- Originality: Radon-point aggregation in this ML parallelization context is novel, though similar divide-and-conquer + robust aggregation themes exist.
- Practical significance: attractive black-box speedup method with minimal code changes and convincing real-system speedups.
- Theoretical significance is limited by dimensionality/Radon-number assumptions and quasi-polynomial processor needs.
- Pros for acceptance: simple broadly applicable method, clean theoretical confidence-boosting analysis, robust alternative to averaging with strong empirical speedups.
- Cons for acceptance: restrictive NC claims, missing related-work discussion and empirical comparisons (e.g., geometric median), and untested sample/processor blowup tradeoffs.
- Suggested revisions: add and contrast related work on randomized convex optimization and robust aggregation, make dimensionality/Radon-number implications explicit, quantify cost vs. d and h, and provide ablations on h and dimensionality (e.g., random projections).
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept; valuable contribution if limitations and related work are clarified. Confidence: medium.
