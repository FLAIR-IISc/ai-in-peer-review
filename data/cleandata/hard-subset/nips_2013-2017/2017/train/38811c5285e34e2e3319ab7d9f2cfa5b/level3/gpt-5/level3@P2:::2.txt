Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a black-box parallelization scheme, the Radon machine, for regularized risk minimization in Euclidean parameter spaces. Given a base learner L, the data are split into rh iid subsets, L is run independently on each subset to produce weak hypotheses, and these are aggregated up a tree via Radon points rather than averaging. The central theoretical guarantee shows that if P(Q(F) > ε) ≤ δ for a single base hypothesis F, then after h aggregation levels the output r_h satisfies P(Q(r_h) > ε) ≤ (r δ)^(2^h), yielding a doubly-exponential reduction in confidence error with the tree height. The runtime of the parallelized scheme is dominated by the base learner on small subsets plus h aggregations of r×r linear systems; under assumptions (notably, that the Radon number r can be bounded polylogarithmically in sample complexity), the authors argue for polylogarithmic runtime in the target sample size M using quasi-polynomially many processors.

Empirically, the Radon machine is implemented on Spark and compared to: (i) the base learners (run sequentially), (ii) a parallel averaging-at-the-end baseline, and (iii) Spark MLlib algorithms. On several classification and regression datasets, the method achieves substantial wall-clock speedups (80–700× vs. the base learner on 150 processors) with similar or better predictive performance than averaging and with runtime competitive with or better than Spark baselines.

The paper appropriately situates itself within parallel learning (distributed SGD, empirical risk minimization with distributed optimization, averaging-based methods, NC learnability, and concept/halfspace learning). A key distinguishing element is the use of Radon points (and their depth/robustness properties) for aggregation; this builds on classic results (Radon’s theorem, centerpoint/Tukey depth) and provides an analysis that leads to the doubly-exponential confidence improvement.

A potential gap in related work is the connection to prior results often referred to as “parallelizing randomized convex optimization,” which also study divide-and-conquer aggregation with robust estimators; the paper should cite and carefully contrast to that line of work, and to related robust aggregations such as geometric median or median-of-means schemes that have been used in distributed learning/federated settings.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, black-box parallelization that applies to a broad class of convex, regularized ERM learners in Euclidean spaces.
- Clear, self-contained theoretical guarantees: Proposition 2/Theorem 3 give clean confidence compounding under Radon aggregation; analysis is intuitive and technically neat.
- Empirical results demonstrate strong wall-clock speedups with competitive accuracy on realistic workloads and limited numbers of processors (e.g., 150 cores), including comparisons with Spark MLlib.
- Robustness motivation for Radon aggregation over averaging is well-argued and supported (average can be corrupted by one bad hypothesis; Radon point is more stable).
- Discussion section is thoughtful about limitations (high dimensionality, nonlinearity, fewer processors, communication).

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- NC framing and assumptions: Theorem 4 relies critically on the Radon number r being bounded polylogarithmically in the sample complexity; in Euclidean R^d, r = d + 2. Thus, unless d = O(polylog N), the stated NC-style polylog-time result on quasi-polynomial processors is not realizable. Please make this limitation explicit in the main text (not only in appendices/discussion) and delineate regimes where the theory is meaningful.
- Processor/sample blowup: Achieving the doubly-exponential reduction in ∆ requires rh base models and total sample size N = rh n. For sizable h and moderate/large d, both the processor count and data requirement become very large. The empirical section fixes the number of processors and N, so it does not validate the theoretical sample-complexity tradeoffs. Please quantify the practical ranges of h attainable under realistic N and d, and provide guidance for choosing h.
- Relation to prior work: The manuscript should discuss prior work on parallelizing randomized convex optimization and robust aggregation (e.g., geometric median, median-of-means) and clarify the novelty of Radon points relative to those. A head-to-head empirical comparison with a geometric-median aggregator would strengthen the case against averaging specifically.
- Scope of base learners: Although the theory targets convex regularized ERM in Euclidean parameterizations, the experiments are primarily linear models. The paper suggests random features/low-rank tricks for non-linear/high-dimensional cases; it would be valuable to quantify how such preprocessing affects the guarantees (does it preserve the convexity and the constants α_ε, β_ε in the sample complexity bound?).
- Complexity accounting for Radon computation: The stated O(r^3) cost per internal node ignores dependence on d in computing the convex combination (e.g., forming the Radon point as a linear combination of r vectors in R^d). Please clarify the full cost (e.g., O(r^3 + r d)) and its effect when d is large.
- Independence assumptions: The proof sketch treats Radon points at level h as iid draws from P_h−1. Given the tree structure with disjoint partitions, this is plausible; it would help to make explicit in the main text the independence ensured by data partitioning at each level.

Quality
- The theory appears sound given the assumptions and is clearly stated. The experimental methodology is generally solid (10-fold CV, baseline comparisons on a cluster) and the speedups are convincing. However, the strongest theoretical claims hinge on restrictive dimensionality assumptions and very large processor/data budgets, which should be emphasized more clearly.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and readable. Algorithm 1 and the statements of Proposition 2/Theorem 3 are clear. Suggested clarifications: (i) move key assumptions (finite r, r polylog in sample complexity) to the main text; (ii) explicitly state the computational cost per aggregation level including dependence on d; (iii) provide guidance for setting h.

Originality
- Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation in this context is interesting and, to my knowledge, not standard in parallel ML literature. That said, the general divide-and-conquer + robust aggregation theme has precedents (randomized/robust convex optimization); the paper should position itself more precisely with respect to that body of work.

Significance
- Practically, the approach is attractive as a black-box speedup method with minimal code changes and strong wall-clock gains on real systems. Theoretically, the NC connection is intriguing but limited in scope due to the dimensionality/Radon-number assumptions and the quasi-polynomial processor requirement.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, broadly applicable parallelization with strong empirical speedups.
- Clean, interpretable confidence-boosting analysis via Radon points.
- Useful alternative to averaging that is robust and provably beneficial.

Cons for acceptance
- NC-style theoretical claim relies on restrictive assumptions; practical import of Theorem 4 is limited.
- Missing/insufficient discussion of closely related work on parallel randomized convex optimization and robust aggregation; no empirical comparison to geometric median-type aggregators.
- Sample/processor blowup not evaluated empirically; guidance for h is limited.

Suggestions for revision
- Add and discuss the “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” line of work and robust aggregation (geometric median/median-of-means); compare theoretically and empirically.
- Make the dimensionality/Radon-number assumptions and their implications explicit; quantify costs as a function of d and h.
- Provide ablations on h (accuracy vs. runtime vs. number of processors) and on dimensionality (possibly with random projections) to map the practical operating regime.

Overall recommendation: borderline accept. The empirical practicality and the neat Radon-based analysis make this a valuable contribution, provided the related-work positioning and limitations are clarified. Confidence: medium.
