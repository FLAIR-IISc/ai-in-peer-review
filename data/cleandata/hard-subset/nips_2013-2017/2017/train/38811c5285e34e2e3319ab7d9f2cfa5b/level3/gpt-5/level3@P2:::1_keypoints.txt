- Proposal: Radon machine — black-box parallelization for regularized risk minimization that aggregates hypotheses via iterated Radon points instead of averaging.
- Algorithm: Train base learner on r^h i.i.d. subsets in parallel, aggregate bottom-up in a tree computing Radon points for each group of r hypotheses.
- Theoretical guarantee: Double-exponential reduction in probability of a bad hypothesis with tree height h, yielding (ε, Δ)-guarantees comparable to sequential learner at lower wall-clock time with many processors.
- Aggregation cost and Radon number: Per-node aggregation is O(r^3) with r equal to the Radon number (r = d+2 in R^d).
- Key limitation: Dependence on r = d+2 implies aggregation cost O(d^3) and requirement δ ≤ 1/(2r) for leaf learners, restricting scalability to modest d.
- Polylog runtime claim: Achievable only if r is bounded polylogarithmically in sample complexity, effectively requiring low/controlled dimensionality.
- Relation to prior work: Contrasts with averaging-at-the-end, parallel SGD, and ADMM-based ERM; Radon aggregation is more robust to bad leaves and offers confidence amplification.
- Origin and novelty: Iterated Radon points come from computational geometry (center-point approximations) and are a novel aggregation mechanism in ML.
- Theory quality: Core probabilistic results (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) are sound, proved via quasi-convexity and union bounds; runtime/sample complexity analysis is transparent.
- Empirics quality: Experiments show substantial wall-clock speedups (80–700× on 150 processors) and competitive AUC versus Spark baselines.
- Empirics limitation: Datasets are mostly low-dimensional (e.g., SUSY with 18 features), so practical behavior for high-dimensional or kernelized models is unclear.
- Clarity: Paper is well written and organized; assumptions are stated, algorithm is crisp.
- Suggested clarity fixes: Collect preconditions for Theorem 4, state practical meaning of r = d+2 for linear models, provide guidance for choosing h, and clarify leaf hyperparameter tuning and iid assumptions.
- Originality: Using iterated Radon points for hypothesis aggregation and NC-style framing is novel and original.
- Significance: Conceptually significant and broadly applicable for low/moderate dimensions; practical impact limited by high-dimensional scalability concerns.
- Pros: Elegant, general, black-box scheme; strong theoretical guarantees; robustness of Radon aggregation; solid empirical speedups and low communication.
- Cons: Heavy dependence on dimension via r = d+2; empirical validation limited to low-d regimes; remedies like random projections are un-analyzed; comparisons omit some strong distributed ERM/SGD baselines.
- Key methodological questions: Need experiments in very high-dimensional regimes (d = 10^4–10^5), empirical evaluation of required leaf sample sizes for δ ≤ 1/(2r), and sensitivity to h.
- Practical extensions requested: Demonstrate Radon machine with random features or Nyström, analyze impact of embeddings on guarantees, and address numerical stability in solving r×r systems.
- Baselines: Recommend adding CoCoA/CoCoA+ and stronger parameter-server SGD baselines with tuned communication rounds.
- Overall assessment: Well-motivated, theoretically solid, and novel but practicality in high-dimensional/nonlinear settings is the main concern; reviewer leans weak accept pending stronger high-d experiments and analysis.
