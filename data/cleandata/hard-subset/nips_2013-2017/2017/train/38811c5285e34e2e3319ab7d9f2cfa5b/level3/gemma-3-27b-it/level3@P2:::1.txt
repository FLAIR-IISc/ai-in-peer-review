This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, the “Radon machine,” for machine learning algorithms. The core idea is to iteratively aggregate hypotheses obtained by running a base learning algorithm on random subsets of the data, using Radon points instead of simple averaging. This approach offers theoretical guarantees for runtime reduction—potentially achieving polylogarithmic time on a quasi-polynomial number of processors—at the cost of increased sample complexity. The authors provide a theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation comparing the Radon machine to baseline algorithms and Spark’s MLlib.

The paper builds upon a well-established line of work in parallelizing machine learning algorithms. Early approaches focused on parallel PAC learning (Valiant, 1984; Blumer et al., 1989), while more recent work explores distributed stochastic gradient descent (Shamir et al., 2013) and communication-efficient distributed optimization (Zhang et al., 2017; McMahan et al., 2017). The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches in achieving truly polylogarithmic runtime, a goal related to Nick’s Class (NC) of parallelizable problems (Cook, 1978). The connection to Radon points, drawing on geometric concepts (Radon, 1921; Clarkson et al., 1996), is a novel and interesting contribution. The idea of averaging-based parallelizations is also acknowledged (Freund et al., 2003), and the Radon machine is presented as an improvement over simple averaging due to its faster convergence.

The strengths of this paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and the clear presentation of the Radon machine. The authors provide a formal framework for understanding the trade-off between runtime, sample complexity, and the number of processors. Theorem 4, establishing the polylogarithmic runtime under certain conditions, is a significant result. The empirical evaluation, while limited in scope, demonstrates a substantial speed-up compared to the base learners and competitive performance with Spark’s MLlib. The discussion of communication complexity and potential applications to streaming data is also valuable.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The most significant concern is the limited scope of the empirical evaluation. The datasets used are relatively low-dimensional (e.g., SUSY with 18 features), which may not be representative of many modern machine learning applications dealing with high-dimensional data such as images or text. The authors acknowledge this limitation and discuss potential remedies like random projections or low-rank approximations, but these are not evaluated experimentally. It remains unclear how the Radon machine performs in scenarios where the number of features is large, and the dataset size is insufficient to directly apply the proposed scheme.

Another point is the practicality of the Radon number. The theoretical analysis depends on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. While the authors mention that the Radon number of Euclidean space is d+2, the implications of a large Radon number on the computational cost of finding Radon points are not fully explored. The runtime for calculating Radon points is stated as r^3, which could become a bottleneck for higher-dimensional hypothesis spaces.

Furthermore, the discussion of “deparallelization” (handling cases where the number of processors is less than the number of subsets) could be expanded. A more detailed analysis of the performance degradation as the number of processors decreases would be beneficial. The connection to Nick's Class, while mentioned, could also be discussed in more depth, including a discussion of the criticisms surrounding this concept (Kruskal et al., 1990).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound parallelization scheme.
*   Rigorous analysis providing runtime guarantees.
*   Demonstrated speed-up in empirical evaluations, even with limited dataset sizes.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Addresses an important open question in parallel machine learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation on low-dimensional datasets.
*   Unclear scalability to high-dimensional data.
*   Computational cost of finding Radon points may be a bottleneck.
*   Discussion of practical considerations (e.g., deparallelization) could be more detailed.

**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The Radon machine is a conceptually elegant and theoretically well-grounded approach to parallelizing learning algorithms. However, the limited empirical evaluation and the potential scalability issues raise concerns. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the empirical evaluation to include at least one high-dimensional dataset or investigate the performance of the scheme with random projections/low-rank approximations.
2.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of finding Radon points, particularly as the dimensionality increases.
3.  Elaborate on the performance degradation when the number of processors is less than the number of subsets, potentially with some experimental results.
