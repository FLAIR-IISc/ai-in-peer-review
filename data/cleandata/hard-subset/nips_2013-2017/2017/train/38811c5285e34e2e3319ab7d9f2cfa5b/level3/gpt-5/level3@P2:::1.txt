Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization that aggregates multiple hypotheses by computing Radon points rather than simple averages. The scheme trains the base learner on rh iid subsets in parallel, then aggregates bottom-up along a tree by computing a Radon point for each group of r hypotheses at each level. Theoretical results show a double-exponential reduction in the probability of a bad hypothesis with the tree height h, yielding (ε, Δ)-guarantees comparable to the sequential base learner but at much lower wall-clock time when sufficiently many processors are available. Under assumptions on the base learner’s sample complexity and hypothesis space convexity, the runtime can be reduced from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the target sample complexity when using quasi-polynomially many processors; aggregation cost per node is O(r3), where r is the Radon number (r = d+2 in Rd).

The method relates to prior distributed learning by averaging (“averaging-at-the-end”), parallel SGD (including distributed mini-batch and parameter-server approaches), and ADMM-based ERM solvers. Unlike simple averaging, Radon aggregation is shown to be more robust (a single bad leaf cannot flip the aggregate), and the confidence amplification enables the polylogarithmic runtime claim. The iterated Radon point originates in computational geometry (approximation of center points), and the paper connects these tools with NC-style parallel complexity for learning, complementing prior theoretical work on NC-learnability and parallel learning of half-spaces.

Quality
- Theory: The core probabilistic guarantee (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3) is sound and clearly proved via quasi-convexity and union bounds. The runtime/sample complexity analysis is transparent about the cost: more data and many processors. The central limitation is the dependence on the Radon number r = d+2: aggregation cost O(r3) and the requirement δ ≤ 1/(2r) for the leaf learners. Theorem 4’s polylogarithmic runtime requires r to be bounded polylogarithmically in the sample complexity, which effectively means d must be modest relative to N0(ε, Δ). The paper acknowledges this and suggests embeddings/random projections, but the guarantees under such preprocessing are not analyzed.
- Empirics: The implementation and experiments are carefully executed, show substantial wall-clock speedups (80–700× on 150 processors), and competitive AUC versus Spark baselines. However, most datasets are low-dimensional (e.g., SUSY with 18 features); the demonstrated regime favors the method (small r). Practical behavior for high-dimensional (d ≫ 10^3) or kernelized models remains unclear.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. Assumptions (convexity, finite-dimensional Euclidean parametrization, δ ≤ 1/(2r)) are stated, and the algorithm is crisp. Suggestions:
- Collect all preconditions for Theorem 4 in one place and explicitly state the practical meaning of r = d+2 for linear models.
- Provide guidance for choosing h given target (ε, Δ), number of processors, and base learner characteristics.
- Clarify whether leaf-level hyperparameters are shared or tuned per leaf, and whether that affects iid assumptions across leaves.

Originality
Using iterated Radon points to aggregate learned hypotheses is, to my knowledge, novel in ML. The double-exponential confidence amplification over aggregation depth is an original angle relative to averaging. The NC-based framing is also a distinctive contribution.

Significance
Conceptually significant: a broadly applicable, black-box parallelization with formal guarantees and a clean algorithm. Practically promising in low/moderate dimensions and for linear models. The main open question is scalability to high-dimensional and nonlinear settings; without stronger evidence here, broad impact may be limited.

Pros
- Elegant, general, black-box scheme requiring minimal changes to base learners.
- Strong, clear theoretical guarantees (double-exponential confidence reduction; polylog runtime under stated conditions).
- Robustness of Radon aggregation versus averaging.
- Solid empirical speedups and competitive accuracy versus strong Spark baselines; low communication footprint.

Cons
- Heavy dependence on dimension via r = d+2: aggregation cost O(d3), quasi-polynomial processors, and δ ≤ 1/(2r) requirement may be impractical in high dimensions.
- Empirics largely in low-dimensional regimes; limited evidence for modern high-d problems (text, sparse features, deep features).
- Remedies (random projections/low-rank embeddings) are suggested but not analyzed or validated empirically in this framework; impact on guarantees is unclear.
- Comparison set omits some strong recent distributed ERM/CoCoA-style methods and more advanced parameter-server SGD baselines.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- High-dimensional regime: Please include experiments with d in the 10^4–10^5 range (e.g., sparse text or clicked ads) and report aggregation time, overall speedup, and accuracy. How large can d be before aggregation dominates runtime?
- δ requirement: For typical d and r = d+2, what subset sizes n are needed for leaves to achieve δ ≤ 1/(2r)? Provide empirical δ estimates and sensitivity to h.
- Choosing h: Provide a practical recipe for setting h given processors, data size, and desired (ε, Δ), and show sensitivity plots.
- Nonlinear models: Demonstrate Radon machines with random features or Nyström approximations, and discuss how embeddings affect the (ε, Δ) guarantees.
- Numerical stability: Radon point computation involves solving r×r systems. How do you regularize or handle ill-conditioning? Any impact on guarantees?
- Baselines: Consider adding CoCoA/CoCoA+ and stronger distributed logistic regression/linear SVM baselines with tuned communication rounds.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated and theoretically solid paper with an elegant algorithm and promising results in low/moderate dimensions. The principal concern is practicality in high-dimensional settings, where the Radon number and aggregation cost grow linearly and cubically in d, respectively, and the leaf-confidence requirement becomes stringent. If the authors can provide convincing empirical evidence and/or refined analysis for high-dimensional and nonlinear models, this work would merit acceptance. As it stands, I lean toward weak accept, primarily for its conceptual novelty and solid theory, with a request to strengthen the experimental section accordingly.
