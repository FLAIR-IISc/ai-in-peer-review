- Proposal: Introduces the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme that aggregates base learners via iterated Radon points instead of averaging.  
- Theory: Proves doubly-exponential confidence amplification with tree height and, under standard assumptions, polylogarithmic training time on quasi-polynomially many processors.  
- Relation to prior work: Complements averaging-based schemes, distributed SGD/ADMM, and NC-learnability results (e.g., Long & Servedio).  
- Empirical results: Reports large training-time reductions and comparable or improved AUC versus strong Spark baselines and averaging-at-the-end.  
- Theoretical quality: Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are clear, useful, and the runtime/sample complexity discussion is careful about extra cost.  
- Experimental quality: Experiments cover multiple datasets and baselines; authors proactively discuss limitations (Radon number, processor counts, high-dimensional/nonlinear cases).  
- Requested improvement: Add statistical tests (and report variances) for AUC and runtime where claims of superiority are made.  
- Clarity: Generally well-organized but presentation issues hinder readability; clarify how comparisons are aggregated.  
- Originality: Novel and interesting use of Radon points to amplify confidence and obtain NC-style guarantees beyond averaging approaches.  
- Significance: Likely to influence practical parallel training when confidence guarantees matter and advances theoretical understanding of polylog-time parallelization.  
- Specific clarification: State whether Radon-vs-averaging comparisons are based on paired comparisons across folds/datasets and report paired tests.  
- Results presentation: The runtime difference vs AverageAtTheEnd appears minor—include this in Figure 1 or better justify practical advantage.  
- Language/typos: Avoid using “significant” without statistical tests; fix typos/formatting (e.g., “the the”, math font size, “machineachieved”, “machineis”).  
- Figures: Increase font sizes and make Fig. 3 distinguishable in grayscale (use markers/line styles).  
- Citation/style: Fix citation formatting/capitalization (e.g., “Long and Servedio [22]”).  
- Pros: Strong, general theoretical contribution; black-box practicality; substantial runtime reductions; broad empirical support.  
- Cons: Requires many processors/data; readability issues; comparisons need clearer, statistically grounded support.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
