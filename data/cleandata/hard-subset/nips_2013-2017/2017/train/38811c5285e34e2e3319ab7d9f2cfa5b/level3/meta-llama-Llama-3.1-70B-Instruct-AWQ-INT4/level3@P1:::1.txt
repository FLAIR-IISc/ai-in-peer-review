This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, which the authors call the Radon machine. The main contribution of the paper is the introduction of a new method for combining the outputs of base learners, which replaces subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point instead of simple averaging. This approach is shown to be more efficient than traditional bagging methods, both theoretically and empirically.

The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of the Radon machine, including a complexity analysis that shows the method is more efficient when samples are split across multiple parts. Additionally, the authors provide a Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) bound, which guarantees the performance of the method. The empirical evaluation of the Radon machine is also thorough, with comparisons to parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Weka.

However, there are some concerns regarding the practicality and performance of the Radon machine on high-dimensional data. The experiments in the paper use very low-dimensional datasets (~18 features), which may not reflect modern machine learning problems. Therefore, it is unclear how the method would scale to and handle high-dimensional data.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. However, to fully assess the practicality of the Radon machine, the authors should clarify or demonstrate how the method scales to and handles high-dimensional data.

Strengths:

* Solid theoretical analysis and guarantees
* Novel approach to combining base learners
* Empirical evaluation is thorough and well-executed

Weaknesses:

* Practicality and performance on high-dimensional data are unclear
* Experiments use very low-dimensional datasets, which may not reflect modern machine learning problems

Recommendations:
