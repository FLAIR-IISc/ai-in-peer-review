Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization that aggregates independently trained “weak” models via iterated Radon points. The key theoretical insight is that, in a hypothesis space with finite Radon number r (e.g., Rd with r = d+2), replacing averaging with Radon-point aggregation yields a doubly exponential reduction in the probability of an ε-bad hypothesis with the height h of the aggregation tree: P(Q(rh) > ε) ≤ (r·δ)^(2^h). Under standard consistency assumptions for the base learner and a mild δ ≤ 1/2r condition, this allows achieving the same (ε, Δ)-guarantees as the base learner but in polylogarithmic time (in the relevant sample complexity) on quasi-polynomially many processors, provided r is polylogarithmic in the sample complexity. Empirically, the scheme delivers substantial wall-clock speedups over single-node base learners and competitive or better AUC compared to Spark MLlib baselines, while also outperforming simple averaging in most settings. The work connects to classical results on NC, P-completeness, and distributed learning (e.g., aggregation/averaging, distributed SGD, ADMM), and leverages properties of Radon/Tukey depth and centerpoint approximations (Clarkson et al.).

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated theory: The Radon-point confidence amplification (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) is elegant and practically meaningful; the robustness vs averaging is compelling.
- Generality and black-box appeal: Works for a broad family of convex ERM learners without algorithm-specific derivations or code.
- Complexity result: A concrete path toward polylogarithmic runtime (Theorem 4) under explicit structural conditions, with a thoughtful discussion of NC and limits.
- Practical relevance: Large empirical speedups (up to ~700×) on realistic clusters with competitive AUC vs Spark baselines; addresses communication efficiency and deparallelization.
- Anticipates deployment concerns: Discussion of high-dimensional settings, non-linear models via embeddings/random features, limited processors, and streaming/federated scenarios.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Processor and data requirements: Achieving the polylogarithmic time bound requires quasi-polynomially many processors and increased sample complexity; the practical regime where this is favorable should be delineated more sharply.
- Dependence on Radon number and dimension: r = d+2 can be large; the theory requires r to be polylogarithmic in sample complexity. The practicality in very high-dimensional regimes may hinge on dimensionality reduction; clearer guidance and experiments would help.
- Numerical stability and cost of Radon points: Computing r×r systems at each node (cost O(r^3)) could be unstable for large r; no discussion of conditioning/regularization or approximate solvers.
- Comparative baselines: While results vs MLlib are encouraging, fairness and tuning details (e.g., strong regularization, early stopping, parameter search budgets) should be fully specified. Averaging sometimes shows close performance; emphasise when Radon points give clear gains.
- Assumption δ ≤ 1/2r and iid splitting: The theoretical guarantees rely on specific δ and iid subsets; discuss robustness when these are violated (e.g., covariate shift, stratified splitting).

Quality
- The theoretical claims appear sound and are well-supported with proofs and clear assumptions. The empirical study is broad and shows consistent speedups with comparable AUC to strong baselines. Clarify statistical testing and paired comparisons (see questions below).

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The algorithm and assumptions are explicit, and the connection to NC is carefully framed. Some figures are hard to read (font size, grayscale), and a few textual/formatting issues need fixing (see minor comments).

Originality
- Novel aggregation via Radon points for parallel ERM with formal confidence amplification and runtime implications toward NC is original relative to averaging-based parallel training and distributed SGD/ADMM. The black-box positioning is also valuable.

Significance
- Conceptually important: introduces a robust, theoretically grounded aggregation mechanism that improves over averaging and informs the debate on efficient parallel learning. Practically promising: strong speedups and competitive accuracy on real systems.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Lines 318–320: When stating that the Radon machine outperforms averaging-at-the-end, is this based on an average of paired comparisons across folds/datasets? Please report paired improvements and statistical tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank across folds) or CIs.
- Significance claims: Avoid “significant” unless statistical tests are performed; otherwise use “substantial” or “consistent” and provide effect sizes (e.g., ΔAUC, speedup distributions).
- High-dimensional regimes: Provide guidance on when random projections/low-rank approximations preserve the Radon aggregation benefits; any sensitivity analyses?
- Numerical aspects: Comment on numerical stability of Radon point computation for larger r, and whether regularization or QR/SVD-based solvers are used.

Suggestions for improving the manuscript
- Results presentation: Lines 341–349 suggest that differences with averaging are sometimes minor. Consider integrating these comparisons into Figure 1 (or a new figure) with error bars and paired differences to make the case concrete.
- Figures 1–3: Increase font sizes; ensure color/marker choices are distinguishable in grayscale. Figure 3 may be hard to read when printed B/W—use different markers/linestyles and add annotations.
- Discuss processor scaling: Provide a plot of speedup vs number of processors and vs h (including c < r^h), and report wall-clock of aggregation vs training to visualize tradeoffs.
- Clarify tuning: Specify hyperparameter search space/budgets for all baselines, early stopping criteria, and whether time budgets were equalized.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong and original theoretical contribution with practical implications.
- Black-box, broadly applicable scheme with robustness advantages over averaging.
- Substantial empirical speedups and competitive accuracy across datasets and systems.
- Clear connection to NC and thoughtful discussion of limitations and extensions.

Cons for acceptance
- Requires many processors and more data to realize theoretical gains; practical envelope should be further quantified.
- High-dimensional and numerical stability aspects need deeper treatment and empirical evidence.
- Some claims about outperforming averaging need stronger statistical backing and clearer visualization.

Overall recommendation
- Accept. The paper combines a compelling theoretical advance with practical performance, is clearly within NIPS scope, and will stimulate further work on principled, general-purpose parallelization of learning algorithms.
