Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a black-box parallelization scheme (“Radon machine”) for regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean hypothesis spaces. The method runs a base learner independently on rh random data shards and aggregates the resulting hypotheses through an h-level tree of Radon-point computations (r = Radon number, d+2 in Rd). A central theoretical claim is that, under convexity and consistency assumptions on the base learner, Radon aggregation reduces the failure probability doubly-exponentially in the tree height, enabling polylogarithmic runtime (in the target sample complexity) on quasi-polynomially many processors, at the cost of increased sample complexity. Empirically, the method is compared against the base learners (no parallelization), averaging-at-the-end, and Spark MLlib algorithms on classification and regression datasets; results show large wall-clock speedups (80–700× on 150 processors) and similar or better predictive performance.

The paper positions the approach relative to averaging-based divide-and-conquer methods [32, 45, 46], distributed ERM/SGD (e.g., ADMM, DANE, CoCoA), and NC-style parallel learning literature [21, 42]. The main novelty is replacing averaging with Radon points, leveraging their robustness and “confidence amplification.”

A major gap is the lack of discussion of “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” (not cited). Several theoretical elements here—randomized convex optimization, divide-and-conquer with robust aggregation, and probability amplification—appear closely related. The paper should precisely delineate which guarantees (e.g., the double-exponential reduction in failure probability via Radon points, the specific NC-style runtime statement) are new beyond that work and beyond robust-aggregation alternatives (geometric median, median-of-means).

Quality
- Theory: The key probabilistic bound (Proposition 2) and its recursive application (Theorem 3) are clear and plausible under the stated convexity assumptions. The analysis carefully separates sample and runtime complexity, and identifies the trade-off: increased sample size and processors for reduced runtime. The NC-style result (Theorem 4) is conditional on the Radon number being polylogarithmic in sample complexity; since r=d+2, this effectively requires low effective dimension or embeddings—this limitation should be more explicitly highlighted in the main text.
- Assumptions and scope: The approach requires a finite-dimensional Euclidean parameterization preserving convexity and a base learner with known (ε, δ)-sample complexity. The paper discusses embeddings and random projections as workarounds for high d, but the statistical impact of such preprocessing (and its interaction with Radon aggregation) is not analyzed.
- Empirics: Experiments are well-executed and show compelling speedups with comparable AUC. Comparisons include averaging-at-the-end and Spark baselines. However, evaluations are largely in linear models and moderate feature dimensions; there are no tests in truly high-dimensional regimes (where r becomes large) nor under heterogeneous/non-iid data shards. Communication and aggregation costs are discussed qualitatively but not measured empirically. The “deparallelized” regime (c << rh) is noted; a systematic study of scalability with varying processor counts would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. Algorithm 1 is clear. Proof ideas for Proposition 2/Theorem 3 are intuitive. Suggestions:
- State the computational model and assumptions (e.g., PRAM vs. cluster with bounded bandwidth, synchronization) up front and reconcile NC-style claims with measured wall-clock times.
- Move key limitations (dependence on r=d+2; quasi-polynomial processors) from Discussion to the main narrative.
- Expand related work: discuss robust aggregators (geometric median, median-of-means) and especially “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization,” clarifying conceptual and technical differences.
- Provide more implementation detail on Radon-point computation and numerical stability for large r.

Originality
The use of Radon points as an aggregation operator for distributed learning is novel relative to common averaging schemes and robust mean estimators. The confidence amplification guarantee via Radon trees is a nice idea. That said, the theoretical framework overlaps with prior randomized convex optimization work; the authors should explicitly position their results and NC-style runtime claims relative to that literature to establish clear novelty.

Significance
If the stated conditions hold (low-to-moderate dimension, convex models, ample data, and sufficient processors), the Radon machine offers a practical, black-box speedup with theoretical guarantees—useful for practitioners who cannot modify solver internals. The NC-style framing is conceptually interesting, though limited by the dependence on r and the quasi-polynomial number of processors. Broader impact would be strengthened by:
- Evidence for high-dimensional settings via dimensionality reduction with quantified accuracy/runtime trade-offs.
- Robustness under data heterogeneity and label noise (where robust aggregation should help).
- Comparisons to robust aggregators beyond averaging (e.g., geometric median), and to recent distributed ERM frameworks (e.g., CoCoA) under matched conditions and communication budgets.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Please cite and discuss “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization.” What aspects are genuinely new here (e.g., Radon-specific amplification vs. general randomized convex optimization results)? Are the doubly-exponential failure bounds unique to Radon points?
- Provide ablations: Radon vs. averaging vs. geometric median/median-of-means aggregation, both in accuracy and runtime.
- Evaluate performance with c << rh processors to validate the claimed degradation factor and practical scalability.
- Report communication volume and synchronization rounds, and plot runtime vs. dataset size and processor count with error bars.
- Explore non-iid partitions and adversarial outliers to test robustness.
- High-dimensional case study: apply random projections or explicit kernel feature maps; quantify the effect on accuracy and aggregator cost.
- Clarify how base-learner hyperparameters were tuned to ensure fair comparisons to Spark MLlib.

Pros
- Simple, black-box parallelization applicable to many convex learners.
- Clear theoretical analysis with an intuitive confidence amplification mechanism.
- Strong empirical speedups with comparable or better accuracy vs. baselines.
- Useful discussion connecting to NC and parallel learning theory.

Cons
- Missing and potentially overlapping prior work (“Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization”) not cited; originality needs clarification.
- Practical limitations when d is large (r=d+2) and when processor counts are limited; limited empirical exploration of these regimes.
- Comparisons omit robust aggregation baselines and detailed communication analysis.
- NC-style result relies on conditions that may seldom hold in modern high-dimensional applications.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea of Radon-point aggregation for black-box parallel learning is interesting and practically promising, and the empirical results are strong. However, acceptance should be contingent on clarifying novelty relative to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” and expanding the related work and limitations, ideally with additional experiments or analyses addressing robustness, high-dimensional settings, and scalability with limited processors.
