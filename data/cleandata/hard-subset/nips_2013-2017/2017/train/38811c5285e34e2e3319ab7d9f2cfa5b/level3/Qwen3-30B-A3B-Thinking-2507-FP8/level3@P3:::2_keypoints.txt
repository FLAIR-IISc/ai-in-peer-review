- Proposes the "Radon Machine": aggregate hypotheses with Radon points instead of averaging, claiming doubly exponential confidence gains and polylogarithmic runtime on many processors; reports 80–700x empirical speedups with comparable or better accuracy.  
- Theoretical analysis (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) is technically sound and shows the claimed doubly exponential confidence improvement.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, comparing against no parallelization, averaging, and Spark baselines across classification, regression, and multi-class tasks.  
- Paper connects to theoretical questions about parallelizing learning in Nick’s Class and to Long & Servedio’s open problem on half-spaces.  
- Novelty concern: central idea (parallel aggregation theory) appears similar to prior work "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization," which is not cited.  
- Fails to properly contextualize and distinguish its theoretical contribution from existing related work, undermining originality claims.  
- Clarity is strong: well-written, organized, clear algorithm presentation (Algorithm 1), consistent notation, and appropriate visualizations (Figures 1–4).  
- Significance is high: potential runtime reduction from polynomial to polylog is important for high-confidence applications and the method’s black-box applicability is practitioner-friendly.  
- Strengths: clear theoretical results, substantial empirical speedups, broad applicability, Spark implementation, and open-source code.  
- Weaknesses: missing citation/context for prior theoretical foundations, insufficient justification for why Radon points are fundamentally superior to other aggregations, and unaddressed practical limitations (e.g., dataset size requirements tied to Radon number).  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address citation and clarify how this work extends or differs from prior theoretical results.
