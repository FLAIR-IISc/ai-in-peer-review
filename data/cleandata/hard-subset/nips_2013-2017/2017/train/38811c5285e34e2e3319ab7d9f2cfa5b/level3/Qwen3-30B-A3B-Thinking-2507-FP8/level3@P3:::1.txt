

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the Radon machine, a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that treats the underlying learning algorithm as a black box. The authors propose replacing the typical averaging of hypotheses in parallel learning with a Radon point aggregation strategy, which they theoretically demonstrate can achieve doubly exponential improvements in confidence bounds with increasing aggregation depth. The approach aims to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

The paper connects to prior work on parallel learning (e.g., Vitter & Lin [42], Long & Servedio [21]) and distinguishes itself from averaging-based approaches (e.g., [32, 45, 46]) by providing theoretical guarantees for confidence improvement. The Radon machine is framed as a solution to the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be parallelized efficiently within Nick's Class (NC).

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with a clear complexity analysis showing that the Radon machine achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processing units. The proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are rigorous, and the PAC bound (Equation 4) provides a concrete theoretical advantage over simple averaging. The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups (80-700x) on small-to-moderate datasets while maintaining comparable predictive performance to both base learners and Spark's MLlib implementations. However, the paper fails to address a critical limitation: the experiments use datasets with only ~18 features (e.g., SUSY, HIGGS), which are extremely low-dimensional by modern standards. The authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 5 but do not provide any evidence that the approach scales to high-dimensional data, which is a significant gap for a paper claiming broad applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough theoretical development and empirical validation. The Radon machine algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented accessibly, and the connection to Radon's theorem is well-explained. However, the discussion of practical implementation challenges (e.g., handling high-dimensional data, non-linear models) in Section 5 is superficial and lacks concrete evidence.

**Originality:** The core idea of using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation rather than averaging represents a novel contribution to parallel machine learning. While the general concept of aggregating multiple weak learners has been explored (e.g., bagging), the specific theoretical properties of Radon points for this purpose are new. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from previous work, particularly in its theoretical guarantees about confidence improvement.

**Significance:** The theoretical contribution is significant as it provides a step toward answering the open question of efficient parallel learning within Nick's Class. However, the practical significance is limited by the lack of evaluation on high-dimensional data, which is the norm in modern machine learning. The empirical results on low-dimensional datasets are promising but do not demonstrate that the approach solves the real-world problem of scaling to modern, high-dimensional datasets. The claim that the Radon machine "is able to reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units" is theoretically sound but lacks practical validation for the most relevant application scenarios.

**Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is strong, with clear proofs of the key theorems. The paper provides a comprehensive comparison with existing parallel learning approaches and demonstrates substantial speedups in practice on the datasets tested.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant weakness is the lack of evaluation on high-dimensional data. The paper's claim of broad applicability is undermined by the absence of evidence that the approach works well when the hypothesis space dimensionality is large (e.g., text data with thousands of features). The Radon number for high-dimensional spaces would be prohibitively large (d+2 for Rd), making the approach impractical without dimensionality reduction. The paper mentions dimensionality reduction techniques (random projections, low-rank approximations) but doesn't demonstrate their effectiveness in practice with the Radon machine.

**Recommendation:** This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to parallel learning, but the empirical evaluation is insufficient to support its claims of broad applicability. The authors should clarify or demonstrate how the method scales to high-dimensional data, which is the most relevant setting for modern machine learning applications. Without this, the practical significance of the work remains questionable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Strong theoretical contribution with rigorous proofs
- Demonstrated speedups in practice on the datasets tested
- Novel approach to parallel learning that differs from existing averaging-based methods
- Clear black-box nature of the approach, allowing application to many learning algorithms

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- No evidence of performance on high-dimensional data (the most common ML problem)
- Experiments on datasets with only ~18 features do not reflect modern ML challenges
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to low-dimensional spaces
- The practical relevance of the approach is significantly diminished by the lack of high-dimensional validation

The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to parallel machine learning, but the lack of empirical validation on high-dimensional data significantly weakens its significance for NIPS. The authors should address this critical limitation before the work can be considered a substantial contribution to the field.
