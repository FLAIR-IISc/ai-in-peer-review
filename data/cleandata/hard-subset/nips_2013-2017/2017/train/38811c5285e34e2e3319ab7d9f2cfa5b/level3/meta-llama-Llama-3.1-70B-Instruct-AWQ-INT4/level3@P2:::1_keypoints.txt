- Proposes a novel parallelisation scheme (Radon machine) that aggregates base learners by replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point instead of averaging.  
- Provides thorough theoretical analysis including sample/runtime complexity and a PAC bound demonstrating efficiency and effectiveness.  
- Empirical evaluation compares Radon machine to Spark parallel algorithms and Weka linear models, showing promising runtime and predictive results.  
- Situates work relative to prior parallelisation methods (e.g., NC variants) and contrasts with averaging-based schemes and SGD.  
- Mentions potential strategies (random projections, low-rank approximations) for addressing high-dimensional data concerns.  
- Strength: solid theoretical guarantees and comprehensive complexity derivations.  
- Strength: well-written and accessible exposition of complex concepts.  
- Strength: empirical results indicate applicability across classification and regression tasks.  
- Concern: practicality and performance on high-dimensional data are unclear.  
- Concern: experiments use very low-dimensional datasets (~18 features), which may not reflect modern ML problems.  
- Originality: novel application of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation—combines familiar techniques in a new context.  
- Significance: potential to improve efficiency and effectiveness of ML algorithms, especially in high-confidence settings.  
- Acceptance pros: theoretical rigor, novelty, potential efficiency gains, broad applicability, supportive empirical results.  
- Acceptance cons: unclear scalability to high-dimensional data, limited experimental scope, need for further validation.  
- Recommendations: clarify/scalability to high dimensions, add experiments or analyses to address practicality, and explore more application domains.
