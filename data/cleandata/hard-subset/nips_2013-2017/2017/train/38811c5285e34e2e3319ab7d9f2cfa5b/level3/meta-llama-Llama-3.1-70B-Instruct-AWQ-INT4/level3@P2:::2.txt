This paper proposes a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms using Radon points. The main idea is to apply the underlying learning algorithm in parallel to random subsets of the data, and then aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points. The Radon machine, as described in Algorithm 1, is able to effectively parallelise consistent and efficient learning algorithms, achieving a reduction in runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of their parallelisation scheme, including a bound on the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis, which is doubly exponentially reduced with the parameter h of the Radon machine. The paper also presents an empirical evaluation of the Radon machine, comparing it to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms from the Spark machine learning library, as well as the natural baseline of averaging hypotheses instead of calculating their Radon point.

The results show that the Radon machine achieves a substantial reduction in computation time in realistic applications, while maintaining similar predictive performance to the Spark algorithms. The authors also demonstrate the general applicability of their scheme by applying it to regression and multi-class classification problems.

The paper's main contribution is the use of Radon points instead of standard averaging as the main aggregation method. This allows for a more robust and efficient parallelisation of machine learning algorithms. However, the theoretical part of the paper appears to have some overlap with previous work, specifically "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (not cited). The authors should clarify their original contribution and relation to that work.

Strengths of the paper include:

* The proposal of a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms using Radon points.

Weaknesses of the paper include:
