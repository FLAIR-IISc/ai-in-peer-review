This paper proposes the “Radon machine,” a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization. The method trains a base learner independently on rh iid data shards and aggregates the resulting hypotheses via iterated Radon points along a tree, yielding a doubly exponential reduction in the failure probability with tree height h. Under standard consistency assumptions and a finite Radon number (r = d+2 in Rd), the analysis shows that to match a target (ε, Δ), the Radon machine achieves polylogarithmic time (in the sequential sample complexity) using quasi-polynomially many processors, at the cost of increased sample size. Empirically, the paper reports 80–700× wall-clock speedups over single-processor baselines and similar or better accuracy than averaging-at-the-end and Spark MLlib algorithms.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself against model averaging/divide-and-conquer, distributed SGD/ADMM, and NC-inspired notions of parallel efficiency. The key novelty claimed over averaging-based approaches is Radon aggregation, which is more robust and, theoretically, compounds confidence faster. However, the theoretical core appears closely related to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” (not cited), which also leverages Radon points/centerpoint ideas for randomized convex optimization. The authors should explicitly position their contributions relative to that work and to robust aggregators (geometric median/median-of-means, Krum/coordinate-wise median).

Quality: The main results (Proposition 2, Theorem 3, Theorem 4) are technically sound under the stated assumptions (finite Radon number, δ ≤ 1/2r, iid splits). The NC-oriented runtime claim hinges on r being polylogarithmic in the sequential sample complexity—nontrivial in high dimensions—and on ample processors. Experiments are convincing for low-to-moderate d, but omit communication costs, fault tolerance, and more challenging high-dimensional or non-linear settings beyond brief sketches.

Clarity: Generally well-written with helpful algorithmic description; guidance for choosing h and verifying δ assumptions could be clearer.

Originality: Using Radon aggregation as the core primitive for ML parallelization is interesting, but the overlap with prior randomized convex optimization work must be clarified and properly cited.

Significance: If positioned and validated more broadly (robust aggregators, high-d regimes, kernels via random features), the approach could become a useful, principled alternative to averaging.

Pros:
- Black-box, easily applicable scheme with code.
- Provable confidence amplification and polylog-time under assumptions.
- Strong empirical speedups; accuracy competitive with Spark.

Cons:
- Novelty relative to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” unclear.
- Strong assumptions (finite/small r, δ bound, iid shards); scalability in high d.
- Missing comparisons to robust aggregators; limited analysis of communication and system effects.
