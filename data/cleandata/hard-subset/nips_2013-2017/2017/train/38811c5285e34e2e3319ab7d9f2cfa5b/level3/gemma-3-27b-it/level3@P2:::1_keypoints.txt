- Proposes the "Radon machine": iteratively aggregate hypotheses from random subsets using Radon points instead of averaging.  
- Claims theoretical runtime reduction, potentially polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomial processors, at the cost of increased sample complexity.  
- Provides a formal framework and rigorous analysis (Theorem 4) establishing runtime–sample–processor trade-offs.  
- Connects to prior parallel learning work and NC, and introduces a novel use of geometric Radon points for faster convergence than averaging.  
- Empirical evaluation shows substantial speed-up over base learners and competitive performance with Spark’s MLlib.  
- Datasets used in experiments are low-dimensional (e.g., SUSY with 18 features), limiting empirical generality.  
- Scalability to high-dimensional data is unclear; suggested remedies (random projections/low-rank approximations) are not experimentally validated.  
- Computing Radon points has stated runtime r^3, which could become a computational bottleneck in high-dimensional hypothesis spaces.  
- Discussion of deparallelization (fewer processors than subsets) is brief and needs deeper analysis of performance degradation.  
- The connection to Nick’s Class is mentioned but lacks a more thorough discussion, including known criticisms.  
- Strengths: novel, theoretically sound scheme, rigorous analysis, clear presentation, and relevant communication/streaming discussion.  
- Weaknesses: limited empirical scope, potential high-dimensional inefficiency, and incomplete practical-cost analysis.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: expand empirical evaluation to high-dimensional data or test projections/low-rank methods; analyze computational cost of finding Radon points as dimensionality grows; elaborate on performance when processors < subsets, ideally with experiments.
