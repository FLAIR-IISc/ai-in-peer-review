

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the Radon Machine, a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that aggregates hypotheses using Radon points instead of simple averaging. The authors claim their approach can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic while maintaining theoretical guarantees, and can be applied to a broad class of learning algorithms without requiring mathematical derivations or dedicated code.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting machine learning parallelization to Nick's Class (NC), a fundamental complexity class in parallel computing. The authors show that for regularized risk minimization problems with finite Radon number (which holds for Euclidean spaces), their parallelization scheme achieves a doubly exponential reduction in error probability with each aggregation level. This theoretical framework builds upon earlier work on parallel learning (Vitter & Lin, 1985; Long & Servedio, 2014) while addressing an open question about efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms.

The main technical contribution is Theorem 4, which establishes that the Radon Machine achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors for consistent and efficient regularized risk minimization algorithms. The authors provide a formal proof of the error probability reduction (Theorem 3) that demonstrates how the Radon point aggregation outperforms simple averaging, which requires more samples to achieve the same confidence guarantees.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups (80-700x) compared to base learning algorithms on moderately sized datasets when using 150 processors. The Radon Machine also outperforms averaging-at-the-end baselines by at least 10% in AUC across all datasets and achieves comparable or better predictive performance than Spark MLlib implementations while requiring 15-85% of their runtime.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated, with careful attention to the trade-offs between runtime, sample complexity, and confidence guarantees. The authors correctly address the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need for finite Radon number, the requirement for sufficient data relative to the Radon number). The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with appropriate baselines and metrics. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of Radon point calculation (Appendix C.1) and the practical limitations of the theoretical guarantees.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, with clear definitions of key concepts like the Radon point and Radon machine. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is straightforward. However, the paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of why Radon points are better than averages for aggregation, particularly for readers unfamiliar with convex geometry.

**Originality:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning. While averaging-based parallelization has been studied (e.g., by Dean et al., 2012), the use of Radon points to achieve doubly exponential confidence improvements is novel. The connection to Nick's Class (NC) is particularly insightful and addresses an important open question in parallel learning. The black-box nature of the approach (applicable to any learning algorithm without modification) is a significant practical advantage over other parallelization schemes.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental question in parallel machine learning: can we achieve polylogarithmic runtime for learning algorithms with theoretical guarantees? This has important practical implications as data volumes continue to grow. The demonstrated speedups (80-700x) are impressive, though the authors should clarify whether these gains would hold for larger, more complex datasets. The practical applicability of the approach is somewhat limited by the requirement for low-dimensional data (the experiments use datasets with ≤18 features), raising questions about scalability to modern high-dimensional problems.

**Key Strengths:**
- Solid theoretical foundation with formal guarantees
- Clear connection to fundamental complexity classes (Nick's Class)
- Black-box parallelization approach applicable to broad class of algorithms
- Strong empirical results on speedup and predictive performance

**Key Weaknesses:**
- Experiments limited to low-dimensional datasets (≤18 features), which may not reflect modern ML challenges
- Unclear how the method scales to high-dimensional data or non-Euclidean spaces
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges (e.g., communication complexity)
- Theoretical guarantees assume sufficient data relative to the Radon number, which may not hold in practice

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the understanding of parallel machine learning and presents a novel, provably effective parallelization scheme. The empirical results are compelling, though the limited dimensionality of the datasets used in experiments raises concerns about generalizability to modern machine learning problems. I recommend acceptance with the expectation that the authors address the scalability concerns in the final version.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical contribution addressing an open question in parallel machine learning
- Rigorous theoretical guarantees with clear connection to complexity theory
- Demonstrated speedups in empirical evaluation
- Black-box nature makes it widely applicable

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited experimental validation on high-dimensional data
- Practical applicability unclear for modern ML problems
- Theoretical guarantees may not hold in real-world scenarios with limited data

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning and presents a promising approach with strong empirical results. While the current experimental validation is limited to low-dimensional datasets, the theoretical foundation is robust and the approach has clear potential for broader application. With minor revisions addressing the scalability concerns, this paper would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS 2017 proceedings.
