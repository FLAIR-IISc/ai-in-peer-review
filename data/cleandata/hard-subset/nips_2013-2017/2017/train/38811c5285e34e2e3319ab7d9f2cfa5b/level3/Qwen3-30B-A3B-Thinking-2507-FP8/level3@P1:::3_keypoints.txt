- Introduces the Radon Machine, a novel parallelization scheme achieving polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors with theoretical guarantees.  
- Addresses an open question about efficient parallelization of learning algorithms in Nick’s Class (NC), advancing theoretical understanding.  
- Core theory: uses Radon points for aggregation instead of averaging to get doubly-exponential confidence improvement with tree height.  
- Proves Radon Machine preserves (ε,δ)-guarantees of sequential algorithms at the cost of increased sample complexity.  
- Shows probability of an ε-bad hypothesis is reduced by (rδ)^{2^h}, where r is Radon number and h is tree height.  
- Empirically achieves large speedups (e.g., 721× on SUSY with 150 processors) while maintaining comparable AUC.  
- Beats Spark MLlib runtimes, achieving similar predictive performance in 15–85% of the runtime.  
- Outperforms averaging-at-the-end baselines by ≥10% AUC across datasets with negligible runtime differences.  
- Strong points: novel, well-motivated theoretical contribution that complements prior work and resolves a fundamental open problem.  
- Practical strengths: black-box parallelization applicable to many algorithms and preemptive discussion of concerns (high-dim, non-linear models, communication).  
- Paper is clear, well-organized, reproducible, and includes solid empirical methodology and baselines.  
- Minor issues: typographical errors and figure text readability need fixing.  
- Minor concern: sometimes small performance gains over averaging baseline could use stronger practical justification.  
- Overall judgment: significant, well-supported contribution bridging theory and practice; recommendation to accept.
