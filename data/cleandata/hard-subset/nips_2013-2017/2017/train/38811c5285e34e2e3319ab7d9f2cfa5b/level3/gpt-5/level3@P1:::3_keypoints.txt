- Proposal: Introduces the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization that aggregates independently trained models via iterated Radon points for ERM, yielding doubly-exponential confidence amplification in aggregation height.
- Theoretical result: Replacing averaging with Radon-point aggregation gives P(Q(r^h) > ε) ≤ (r·δ)^(2^h), enabling (ε,Δ)-guarantees in polylogarithmic time under r polylogarithmic in sample complexity and mild δ assumptions.
- Empirical result: Shows substantial wall-clock speedups (up to ~700×) and competitive or better AUC vs Spark MLlib, often outperforming simple averaging.
- Strength: Clear, well-motivated and elegant theoretical analysis demonstrating robustness over averaging.
- Strength: General, black-box applicability to convex ERM without algorithm-specific changes.
- Strength: Concrete complexity result toward polylogarithmic runtime with discussion of NC and limits.
- Strength: Practically relevant experiments addressing communication efficiency and deployment scenarios.
- Strength: Anticipates deployment concerns (high-dim, random features, federated/streaming settings).
- Weakness: Polylogarithmic time requires quasi-polynomially many processors and increased sample complexity; practical regime unclear.
- Weakness: Dependence on Radon number (r = d+2) may be impractical in high dimensions; needs clearer guidance on dimensionality reduction.
- Weakness: Computational cost and numerical stability of solving r×r systems (O(r^3)) for Radon points not discussed.
- Weakness: Comparative baselines need clearer tuning/fairness details; averaging sometimes performs similarly.
- Weakness: Theoretical guarantees rely on δ ≤ 1/2r and iid splitting; robustness to violations (covariate shift, stratified splits) not addressed.
- Quality: Theoretical claims are sound and proofs clear; empirical study is broad but needs clearer statistical testing and paired comparisons.
- Clarity: Well-written overall, but some figures are hard to read and there are minor textual/formatting issues.
- Originality: Novel use of Radon points for aggregation with formal confidence amplification and runtime implications; original relative to averaging/SGD/ADMM.
- Significance: Conceptually important and practically promising — a robust, general-purpose aggregation mechanism with strong speedups.
- Questions: Request paired comparison statistics and tests (e.g., Wilcoxon) for averaging vs Radon; avoid “significant” without tests and report effect sizes.
- Questions: Ask for guidance/sensitivity analyses on random projections/low-rank approximations in high-dimensional settings.
- Questions: Request discussion of numerical stability and solver choices for Radon point computation.
- Suggestions: Integrate averaging comparisons with error bars/paired differences into main figures and report processor-scaling plots (speedup vs processors/h).
- Suggestions: Improve figure readability (font sizes, grayscale markers), clarify hyperparameter search/tuning and time budgets for baselines.
- Minor issues: Typo corrections and citation/style fixes (e.g., “the the”, font/math sizing, line-specific typos, citation formatting).
- Pros for acceptance: Strong, original theory with practical implications; black-box applicability; substantial empirical speedups; meaningful connection to NC.
- Cons for acceptance: Requires many processors and more data to realize theoretical gains; high-dimensional and numerical stability issues need more evidence; some claims need stronger statistical backing.
- Overall recommendation: Accept.
- Reviewer confidence: High (familiar with distributed learning and convex ERM).
