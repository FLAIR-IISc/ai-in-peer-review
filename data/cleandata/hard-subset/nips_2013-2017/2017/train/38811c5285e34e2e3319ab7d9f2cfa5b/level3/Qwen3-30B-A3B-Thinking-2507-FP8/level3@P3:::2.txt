

# Review: "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper proposes a novel parallelization scheme called the "Radon Machine" that uses Radon points instead of standard averaging for aggregating hypotheses from parallel learning runs. The key theoretical contribution is showing that Radon points provide a doubly exponential improvement in confidence with each level of aggregation, enabling polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The empirical evaluation demonstrates significant speedups (80-700x) with comparable or better accuracy than baselines.

The paper relates to theoretical work on parallel learning, particularly the open question of whether machine learning algorithms can be parallelized efficiently in Nick's Class (NC). The authors connect their work to Long and Servedio's fundamental open problem about parallel learning of half-spaces. The Radon Machine approach differs from prior work by using geometric aggregation (Radon points) instead of averaging, which the authors claim provides superior confidence guarantees.

**Quality**: The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with clear proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 showing the doubly exponential confidence improvement. The empirical evaluation is well-designed, comparing against no parallelization, averaging, and Spark-based algorithms across multiple datasets and learning tasks (classification, regression, multi-class). However, the theoretical foundation appears to be previously studied in "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (not cited), which is a significant concern. The paper must clarify its original contribution and relation to this prior work.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the Radon Machine algorithm (Algorithm 1) and its theoretical properties. The empirical results are presented with appropriate visualizations (Figures 1-4) and detailed experimental setup. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined.

**Originality**: The main claim of novelty is using Radon points instead of averaging for aggregation. However, the theoretical underpinnings appear to be based on prior work without proper citation, undermining the novelty claim. The paper should explicitly distinguish its contribution from "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" and other relevant theoretical work. The connection to Radon's theorem and convex geometry is appropriate, but the paper fails to properly contextualize its theoretical contribution within the existing literature.

**Significance**: The potential to reduce learning time from polynomial to polylogarithmic is significant for applications requiring high confidence in critical domains. The empirical results demonstrate substantial practical speedups (80-700x) while maintaining or improving accuracy, making the approach broadly applicable as a black-box parallelization scheme. The approach's ability to treat underlying learning algorithms as black boxes is particularly valuable for practitioners.

**Strengths**:
- Clear theoretical analysis showing doubly exponential confidence improvement
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and learning tasks
- Demonstrated substantial speedups (80-700x) with comparable or better accuracy
- Practical implementation in Spark with open-source code
- Applicability to a broad class of learning algorithms without modification

**Weaknesses**:
- Theoretical contribution is not properly contextualized with respect to prior work on parallel convex optimization
- The connection to "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" is not addressed, despite the similar theoretical foundation
- The paper does not sufficiently explain why Radon points provide a fundamental advantage over other aggregation methods
- The practical limitations (e.g., requirement for dataset size to be a multiple of Radon number) are acknowledged but not fully addressed

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions (to address the citation issue and clarify theoretical contribution).

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning, but the failure to properly cite and contextualize the theoretical foundation is a serious shortcoming. The authors should explicitly acknowledge the connection to "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" and clarify how their work extends or differs from this prior research. With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field, demonstrating a theoretically sound and practically effective approach to parallel machine learning that could be broadly applicable to many learning algorithms. The empirical results are compelling and well-supported, confirming the theoretical claims in realistic application scenarios. The Radon Machine approach represents a significant step toward answering the open question of efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms in the sense of Nick's Class.
