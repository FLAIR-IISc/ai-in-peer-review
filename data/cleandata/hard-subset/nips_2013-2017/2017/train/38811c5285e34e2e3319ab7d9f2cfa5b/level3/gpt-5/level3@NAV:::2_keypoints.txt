- Proposes the "Radon machine": train base learner on r^h iid shards and aggregate via iterated Radon points on a tree to get doubly exponential failure-probability reduction with height h.  
- Claims polylogarithmic time to match target (ε, Δ) using quasi-polynomial processors at the cost of increased total sample size, under finite Radon number (r = d+2) and consistency assumptions.  
- Empirical results show 80–700× wall-clock speedups and similar or better accuracy than averaging-at-the-end and Spark MLlib baselines.  
- Paper situates itself against model averaging, distributed SGD/ADMM, and NC-inspired parallel efficiency notions.  
- Key novelty is Radon aggregation being more robust and compounding confidence faster than averaging-based methods.  
- Theoretical core appears closely related to (uncited) "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization," which also uses Radon/centerpoint ideas—this relation must be clarified.  
- Should compare and position against robust aggregators (geometric median, median-of-means, Krum, coordinate-wise median).  
- Main propositions and theorems are technically sound under stated assumptions (finite Radon number, δ ≤ 1/2r, iid splits).  
- NC-style runtime claim depends on r being polylogarithmic in sequential sample complexity, which is nontrivial in high dimensions and requires many processors.  
- Experiments are convincing for low-to-moderate dimensional problems but omit communication costs, fault tolerance, and more challenging high-dimensional or nonlinear settings.  
- Paper is generally clear and well written, but guidance on choosing tree height h and verifying δ assumptions is lacking.  
- Original idea of using Radon aggregation for ML parallelization is interesting, but overlap with prior randomized convex optimization work needs citation and clarification.  
- Potentially significant if validated and extended to robust aggregators, high-dimensional regimes, and kernelized methods (e.g., random features).  
- Pros: black-box and easily applicable with code; provable confidence amplification and polylog-time under assumptions; strong empirical speedups and competitive accuracy.  
- Cons: unclear novelty vs prior work; strong/possibly unrealistic assumptions (small r, δ bound, iid shards); missing comparisons to robust aggregators and limited analysis of communication/system effects.
