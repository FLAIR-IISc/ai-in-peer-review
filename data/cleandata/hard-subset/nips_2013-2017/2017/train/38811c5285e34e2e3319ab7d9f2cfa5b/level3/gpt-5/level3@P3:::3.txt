Summary
This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization that aggregates multiple weak hypotheses via Radon points, rather than averaging. The key theoretical insight is that Radon aggregation reduces the probability of an ε-bad hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of an aggregation tree, enabling polylogarithmic-time learning (in sample size) on quasi-polynomially many processors, under standard convexity assumptions and finite Radon number (r = d + 2 in Rd). The analysis connects learning to Nick’s Class (NC) and positions the work within the literature on distributed/parallel learning, contrasting it with averaging-at-the-end, distributed SGD, and distributed ERM/optimization methods (e.g., ADMM, DANE/CoCoA variants), as well as the complexity-theoretic perspective of Long and Servedio on parallel learning.

Empirically, the Radon machine yields substantial training-time reductions (80–700× using 150 processors) relative to base learners while maintaining AUC, and it compares favorably to Spark MLlib baselines in both runtime and, often, predictive quality. The paper also anticipates practical concerns (high dimensionality, non-linearity, limited processors, communication, streaming/federated settings) and discusses mitigations and applicability.

Quality
- Theoretical soundness: The propositions and Theorem 3 clearly formalize confidence amplification via Radon points, and the runtime/samples tradeoff is carefully derived. Assumptions (convex F, finite Radon number, iid subsets, δ ≤ 1/2r) are explicit and reasonable for many linear models; the cost of r3 per aggregation layer is transparent.
- Empirical support: Experiments cover multiple datasets, base learners (WEKA/scikit-learn), and comparisons to Spark baselines, with 10-fold cross-validation. Results show large training-time gains and comparable or better AUC versus both base learners and averaging-at-the-end, as well as favorable speed–AUC trade-offs versus Spark MLlib.
- Care in evaluation: The Discussion thoughtfully addresses common concerns (dimension, kernels, processor scarcity, communication, streaming, privacy). This strengthens the case that the scheme is practically relevant beyond toy settings.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with a clean description of Algorithm 1 and assumptions. Proof sketches are intuitive, and pointers to appendices are clear.
- Some presentation issues (see specific line edits and figure readability below) detract slightly from accessibility, particularly for readers printing in grayscale or with small fonts.

Originality
- The use of iterated Radon points for hypothesis aggregation in parallel learning, with rigorous confidence amplification and complexity results, is novel and well-motivated. The black-box nature of the scheme distinguishes it from method-specific distributed optimizers or SGD variants.

Significance
- The work offers a general, plug-and-play parallelization approach with provable guarantees and practical gains, likely to be adopted and extended. Its link to the NC framework provides a useful lens for the community, and the empirical speedups are compelling for large-scale practice.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and well-justified theoretical contribution; clear confidence amplification mechanism.
- Strong empirical improvements in training time and competitive AUC across multiple datasets and baselines.
- Broad applicability and black-box implementation; authors anticipate and address many practical concerns.
- Connection to NC and prior theory on parallel learning adds conceptual depth.

Cons / points to improve
- Requires quasi-polynomially many processors and increases sample complexity; practical limits for very high-dimensional models hinge on projections/embeddings.
- Aggregation cost scales with r = d + 2; guidance on typical d for tested settings is helpful, but more discussion on scalability when d is large would be useful.
- The difference from averaging-at-the-end in some plots appears minor on runtime, and in a few cases the practical AUC margin may not be large; presentation could better substantiate practical advantages in those cases (e.g., statistical tests or per-dataset deltas).

Specific comments, questions, and requested revisions
- Clarification (Lines 318–320): When stating that the Radon machine “outperforms the averaging-at-the-end baseline,” is this based on the average AUC over folds, an average of paired comparisons across folds, or some other aggregation? Please clarify the metric and whether paired tests were conducted per dataset.
- Language (Line 324): Avoid “significant” unless reporting statistical significance with a test and p-value or confidence interval; otherwise use “substantial,” “pronounced,” etc.
- Results presentation (Lines 341–349): The runtime difference versus AverageAtTheEnd appears small; please (a) include the Avg baseline directly in Figure 1 for easy comparison, or (b) more strongly motivate why Radon aggregation is preferable in practice (e.g., consistent AUC gains, robustness to outliers, theoretical guarantees), ideally with statistical tests or effect sizes.
- Minor typos/formatting:
  - Line 51: “the the” → “the.”
  - Line 79: Math font appears smaller than body text; increase font size for readability.
  - Figures 1–3: Increase axis and legend font sizes; currently hard to read.
  - Figure 3: Use color/marker shapes/patterns that remain distinguishable in black-and-white prints.
  - Lines 340–341: Missing spaces in “machineachieved” and “machineis.”
  - Citation style (Line 377): “Long and servedio [22]” → “Long and Servedio [22]”; ensure consistent capitalization and citation style throughout, and reconcile [21]/[22] numbering to match references.
- Methodological details:
  - Please report whether AUC differences were tested for statistical significance across CV folds, and include CIs or standard errors in figures or a table.
  - State the exact dimensionality d of the hypothesis spaces used, to contextualize r and the r3 aggregation cost.
  - Briefly discuss sensitivity to h and to the subset size per base learner; an ablation or appendix plot would help practitioners choose h given limited processors.
  - Communication details: specify hypothesis size (bytes) transmitted per message to substantiate the “low communication” claim.
  - Reproducibility: The Bitbucket link is appreciated; ensure all scripts/configs are included and that datasets and versions are specified.

Overall recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated and novel algorithmic idea—Radon-based aggregation—for effective parallelization, with solid theoretical guarantees and convincing empirical results showing substantial training-time reductions and good predictive performance. With the suggested presentation and clarity improvements, this work will be of interest to both the theory and systems communities.
