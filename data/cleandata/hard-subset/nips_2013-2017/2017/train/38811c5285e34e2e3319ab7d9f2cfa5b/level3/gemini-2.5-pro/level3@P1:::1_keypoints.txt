- Proposes the "Radon Machine": partition data, train base learners on subsets, hierarchically aggregate hypotheses using Radon points instead of averaging.  
- Theoretical claim: aggregation reduces the probability of a poor hypothesis doubly-exponentially with aggregation-tree height.  
- Main theoretical result: for many regularised risk minimisation problems, desired accuracy/confidence can be achieved in polylog time with a quasi-polynomial number of processors.  
- Connects the method to NC-learnability and fundamental complexity questions, increasing theoretical significance.  
- Black-box design: wraps any consistent, efficient regularised risk minimiser (e.g., SVM, logistic regression) without modifying base learners.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly structured; algorithm and parameter roles (`h`, `r`) are clearly explained.  
- Empirical results show large speed-ups (80x–700x) over sequential learners and competitive performance versus Spark MLlib.  
- Major practical concern: Radon number r = d+2 implies required base models/processors scale as (d+2)^h, making the method impractical for moderate-to-high dimensions.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited to low-dimensional datasets (e.g., d≈18) and does not address high-dimensional scenarios where the method may fail.  
- Suggested fix of dimensionality reduction is only proposed, not empirically or theoretically validated.  
- Empirical superiority over averaging (AUC improvements >10%) is notable but lacks deeper theoretical or intuitive explanation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel elegant use of Radon's theorem, strong non-asymptotic guarantees, NC connection, and wide applicability via black-box approach.  
- Arguments against acceptance: polynomial dependence on dimensionality undermines scalability, limited empirical validation on high-dimensional tasks, and untested dimensionality-reduction remedy.  
- Recommendation: paper is theoretically compelling but borderline—accept if authors address the dimensionality scalability either theoretically or with high-dimensional empirical results.
