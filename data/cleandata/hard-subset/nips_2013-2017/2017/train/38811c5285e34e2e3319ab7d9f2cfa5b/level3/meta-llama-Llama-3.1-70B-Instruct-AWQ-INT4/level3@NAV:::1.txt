This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme, called the Radon machine, that simplifies the adaptation of learning algorithms to growing amounts of data and accurate predictions in critical applications. The scheme effectively combines weak learners and acts as a black-box alternative to bagging, maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, including complexity analysis and a PAC bound, demonstrating that the Radon machine is more efficient when samples are split across parts.

The empirical evaluation of the Radon machine compares it to parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Weka, showing a substantial reduction in computation time in realistic applications. The results indicate that the Radon machine achieves a speed-up of up to 700 times faster than the base learner using 150 processors, while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of the Radon machine, which replaces subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point instead of simple averaging. This approach allows for a more efficient combination of weak learners, leading to improved performance and scalability.

The paper's strengths include its solid theoretical analysis and guarantees, as well as its empirical evaluation, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the Radon machine in practice.

However, a major concern is the practicality and performance of the Radon machine on high-dimensional data, which is unclear. The experiments use very low-dimensional datasets (~18 features), which may not reflect modern machine learning problems. Therefore, it is essential to clarify or demonstrate how the method scales to and handles high-dimensional data.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and effective parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms. While it has several strengths, including solid theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation, its practicality and performance on high-dimensional data need to be further investigated.

**Recommendation for acceptance:** Yes, but with the condition that the authors address the concern about the practicality and performance of the Radon machine on high-dimensional data.

**Arguments pro acceptance:**

* Solid theoretical analysis and guarantees
* Empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the Radon machine in practice
* Novel approach to combining weak learners

**Arguments con acceptance:**

* Unclear practicality and performance on high-dimensional data
