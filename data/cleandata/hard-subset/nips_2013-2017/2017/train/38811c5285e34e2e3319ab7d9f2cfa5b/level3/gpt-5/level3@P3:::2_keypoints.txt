- Proposes a black-box parallelization ("Radon machine") that aggregates hypotheses via an h-level Radon-point tree to amplify confidence and enable polylogarithmic runtime with increased sample complexity.  
- Claims doubly-exponential reduction in failure probability with tree height under convexity and consistency assumptions, yielding large wall-clock speedups in experiments.  
- Empirical results show 80–700× speedups on 150 processors with comparable or better predictive performance versus base learners, averaging, and Spark MLlib.  
- Positions work against averaging-based divide-and-conquer, distributed ERM/SGD, and NC-style parallel learning; novelty is using Radon points instead of averaging.  
- Major omission: does not cite or compare to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization,” which appears closely related.  
- Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 probabilistic bounds are clear and plausible; analysis separates sample vs runtime complexity well.  
- NC-style result (Theorem 4) depends on Radon number r being polylogarithmic in sample complexity, which requires low effective dimension and should be emphasized.  
- Method requires finite-dimensional Euclidean parameterization and a base learner with known (ε, δ)-sample complexity.  
- Use of embeddings/random projections as workarounds for high d is discussed but their statistical impact and interaction with Radon aggregation are not analyzed.  
- Experiments are well-executed but limited to linear models and moderate feature dimensions; no evaluation in truly high-dimensional regimes.  
- No empirical tests under heterogeneous/non-iid shards or adversarial outliers to validate robustness claims.  
- Communication and aggregation costs are discussed qualitatively but not measured or reported.  
- The “deparallelized” regime (c << rh) is noted but lacks systematic scalability studies across varying processor counts.  
- Paper is generally well-written; Algorithm 1 and proof sketches are clear.  
- Needs explicit computational model (PRAM vs cluster) and reconciliation of NC-style claims with wall-clock measurements.  
- Key limitations (r = d+2 dependence; quasi-polynomial processors) should be moved from Discussion into the main narrative.  
- Related work should be expanded to include robust aggregators (geometric median, median-of-means) and the omitted randomized convex optimization literature.  
- Implementation details on Radon-point computation and numerical stability for large r should be provided.  
- Use of Radon points as an aggregation operator is novel and the confidence amplification idea is appealing.  
- However, theoretical contributions overlap with prior randomized convex optimization work; novelty must be clarified.  
- Significance is strong under stated conditions (low-to-moderate dimension, convex models, ample data and processors), but limited for high-dimensional modern applications.  
- Suggested experiments: ablations vs averaging/geometric median/median-of-means, c << rh scaling, communication volume and sync rounds, runtime vs dataset size/processor count with error bars, non-iid and adversarial tests, and high-dimensional case studies with quantified projection effects.  
- Clarify base-learner hyperparameter tuning to ensure fair comparisons with Spark MLlib.  
- Pros: simple black-box parallelization, clear theoretical analysis, strong empirical speedups, connects to NC theory.  
- Cons: missing related work citation, limited applicability when d large or processors limited, lacks robust-aggregator baselines and communication analysis.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept pending clarification of novelty relative to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization,” expanded related work and limitations, and ideally additional experiments on robustness, high-dimensional settings, and limited-processor scalability.
