## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for learning algorithms based on Radon partitioning, aiming to reduce runtime while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The core idea is to apply the learning algorithm to random subsets of the data and iteratively aggregate the results using Radon points, offering a doubly-exponential confidence increase compared to averaging. The authors motivate this work by highlighting the need for efficient parallelisation in the context of growing datasets and the desire to achieve polylogarithmic runtime, potentially addressing a long-standing open question related to Nick’s Class (NC). 

The theoretical contribution is interesting and well-motivated, providing a formal analysis of the proposed scheme and establishing bounds on sample complexity and runtime. The empirical evaluation demonstrates significant speedups (up to 700x) compared to sequential execution of the base learners and competitive performance against existing parallel implementations from Spark’s MLlib across multiple datasets. The authors proactively address potential concerns regarding high-dimensional data and communication complexity in the discussion section, strengthening the paper. 

However, there are several areas for improvement. While the empirical results are promising, the difference in performance between the proposed method and AverageAtTheEnd (lines 341-349) appears minor and could be better justified or visually represented in Figure 1. The paper also contains several minor issues: a typo on line 51 ("the the"), formatting issues on line 79 (small math font), and readability concerns with Figures 1-3 (font size). Figure 3 may also be difficult to interpret when printed in black and white. Clarification is needed regarding the statistical basis for the Radon machine outperforming the averaging baseline (lines 318-320). Additionally, the language on line 324 should avoid using "significant" without specifying statistical significance. Minor typos/missing spaces exist on lines 340-341 ("machineachieved" and "machineis."). Finally, the citation style on line 377 ("Long and servedio [22]") is inconsistent with other citations.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound parallelisation scheme with strong empirical support. It addresses a relevant and challenging problem in machine learning and offers a potentially significant contribution to the field. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of related work and proactively address potential limitations.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The minor technical issues (typos, formatting) and the need for further clarification regarding some empirical results and statistical claims.

**Overall Recommendation:** Despite the minor issues, I recommend acceptance. The theoretical contribution is compelling, the empirical results are promising, and the authors have demonstrated a strong understanding of the problem and related work. Addressing the noted issues would further strengthen the paper.
