This paper proposes the "Radon Machine," a novel black-box method for parallelizing a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to partition the data, train a base learner on each subset in parallel, and then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points. This work builds on the well-established paradigm of parallel learning via averaging (e.g., Zinkevich et al., 2010; Zhang et al., 2015), but its main contribution is the use of Radon points for aggregation, which the authors claim provides a doubly-exponential improvement in confidence with each aggregation step. The theoretical goal is to achieve polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors, connecting the work to complexity theory concepts like Nick's Class.

The paper has several significant strengths. The proposed method is elegant and broadly applicable, treating the base learner as a black-box, which is a major practical advantage. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The authors compare their Radon Machine against no parallelization, simple averaging, and state-of-the-art Spark MLlib algorithms. The results are impressive, demonstrating substantial speed-ups (up to 700x) with comparable or improved predictive accuracy over the baselines. This clearly demonstrates the significance and potential impact of the work for practitioners.

However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its originality and relation to prior work. The theoretical analysis concerning the aggregation of learners to boost confidence appears to have been studied previously, bearing a strong resemblance to the framework presented in "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" by Agarwal & Duchi (COLT 2011). This highly relevant paper is not cited, and the current manuscript fails to distinguish its theoretical contribution from this prior work. This omission undermines the claimed novelty of the theoretical results.

In summary, the paper presents a compelling and empirically validated parallelization scheme with significant practical benefits. The experimental results alone make a strong case for the method's utility. However, for acceptance, the authors must perform a major revision to address the concerns about theoretical originality. They need to include a thorough discussion of the work by Agarwal & Duchi, and clearly delineate what aspects of their theoretical analysis are novel. If the authors can convincingly clarify their specific contribution in this context, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
