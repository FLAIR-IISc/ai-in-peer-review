## Review of “Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning”

This paper proposes a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms based on Radon points. The core idea is to apply a learning algorithm in parallel to random subsets of the data and then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points instead of standard averaging. This approach aims to reduce runtime to polylogarithmic time on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units, while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The authors evaluate their method against no parallelization, averaging, and Spark-based algorithms, demonstrating reduced learning time and comparable or improved accuracy.

The work builds upon existing parallelization techniques, particularly those based on averaging. However, the key novelty lies in the use of Radon points, which the authors claim provide a doubly-exponential improvement in confidence compared to averaging. This is a potentially significant contribution, as efficient parallelization remains a crucial challenge in scaling machine learning to large datasets. The experimental results are promising, showing substantial speedups compared to both sequential learning and other parallel approaches like those found in Spark’s MLlib. The comparison to Spark is particularly valuable, as it positions the proposed method within the current landscape of distributed machine learning frameworks.

However, a significant concern is the lack of clear differentiation from prior work on parallelizing randomized convex optimization, specifically the paper “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” (not cited). The authors need to explicitly address how their contribution builds upon or differs from this earlier work. Is the application to a broader class of learning algorithms the primary novelty, or is there a refinement in the analysis or implementation of the Radon point aggregation? This clarification is crucial for establishing the originality of the work. 

Furthermore, while the theoretical analysis is presented, the assumptions required for the polylogarithmic runtime (e.g., finite Radon number, abundant data) may limit the practical applicability of the method. A more detailed discussion of these limitations and potential workarounds (e.g., using data augmentation or dimensionality reduction) would strengthen the paper. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of Radon points for parallelizing learning algorithms.
*   Promising empirical results demonstrating significant speedups.
*   Clear presentation of the theoretical framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of discussion and citation of related work on parallel randomized convex optimization.
*   Potential limitations of the theoretical assumptions.
*   Need for a more in-depth analysis of the communication complexity.



Overall, this paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. Addressing the concerns regarding related work and clarifying the practical limitations would significantly strengthen the manuscript and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing these points.
