This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, called the Radon machine, which combines base learners by replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point instead of simple averaging. The method acts as a black-box alternative to bagging, effectively combining weak learners. The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, including complexity analysis and a PAC bound, showing that the Radon machine is more efficient when samples are split across parts. The empirical evaluation compares the Radon machine with parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Weka, demonstrating its potential in practical settings.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a new parallel ML framework that can be applied to a broad class of learning algorithms without further mathematical derivations and without writing dedicated code. The authors show that the Radon machine can reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units. This is a significant step towards a general answer to an open question on the efficient parallelisation of machine learning algorithms in the sense of Nick’s Class (NC).

The paper is well-organised and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The theoretical contributions are solid, and the empirical evaluation provides a good understanding of the Radon machine's performance in practice. The authors also discuss the limitations of their approach, including the potential issues with high-dimensional data and the need for further research on the applicability of the Radon machine to non-linear models.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to provide a general framework for parallelising machine learning algorithms. The Radon machine is a black-box parallelisation scheme that can be applied to a wide range of machine learning algorithms, making it a valuable contribution to the field. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the Radon machine's performance, including its sample and runtime complexity.

However, there are some concerns regarding the practicality and performance of the Radon machine on high-dimensional data. The experiments use very low-dimensional datasets (~18 features), which may not reflect modern ML problems. The authors should clarify or demonstrate how the method scales to and handles high-dimensional data.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to parallelising machine learning algorithms. The use of Radon points to combine hypotheses is a new idea, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of its performance. The paper also relates to previous work on parallel learning, including the work of Long and Servedio on learning half-spaces.

The significance of this paper lies in its ability to provide a general framework for parallelising machine learning algorithms. The Radon machine has the potential to speed up learning while achieving a similar hypothesis quality as the base learner. The paper also contributes to the understanding of the extent to which efficient parallelisation of polynomial time learning algorithms is possible.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and effective parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms.
* The authors provide a solid theoretical analysis, including complexity analysis and a PAC bound.
* The empirical evaluation demonstrates the potential of the Radon machine in practical settings.
* The paper contributes to the understanding of the extent to which efficient parallelisation of polynomial time learning algorithms is possible.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The experiments use very low-dimensional datasets, which may not reflect modern ML problems.
* The practicality and performance of the Radon machine on high-dimensional data are unclear.
* Further research is needed to demonstrate the applicability of the Radon machine to non-linear models.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper, but request that the authors provide additional experiments or analysis to demonstrate the performance of the Radon machine on high-dimensional data.
* Suggest that the authors clarify or demonstrate how the method scales to and handles high-dimensional data.
* Encourage the authors to explore the applicability of the Radon machine to non-linear models in future research.
