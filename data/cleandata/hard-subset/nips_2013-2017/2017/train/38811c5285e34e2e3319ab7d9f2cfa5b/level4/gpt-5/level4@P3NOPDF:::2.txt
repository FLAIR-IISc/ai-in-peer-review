Summary:
The paper proposes a parallelization strategy for learning algorithms that satisfy certain convexity and stability assumptions, replacing standard parameter averaging with aggregation via Radon points. The method partitions data across workers, trains local models, and combines them using a Radon-point operator rather than arithmetic mean. The authors compare against no parallelization, a simple averaging scheme, and Spark-based baselines, reporting reduced training time with equal or better accuracy.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty in using Radon points as an aggregation operator. The idea is intuitively appealing: Radon points can be more robust than simple averaging and may better preserve optimality under convexity assumptions.
- The method is generic and, in principle, applicable to a family of randomized convex learners, which could make the approach broadly useful.
- The experimental results indicate meaningful wall-clock time reductions while matching or improving accuracy over averaging and Spark-based baselines on the reported tasks.
- The paper attempts to bridge theory and practice by grounding the aggregation operator in geometric and convex analysis.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Positioning relative to prior work is unclear. Much of the theoretical foundation appears closely related to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” (likely by the same authors). This work is not cited, and the paper does not clearly delineate what is new here (e.g., new theorems, tighter bounds, broader assumptions, or purely empirical validation). This is a major issue that needs to be addressed.
- Assumptions and scope are not stated with sufficient precision. The exposition should clarify exactly which properties of the base learner are required (e.g., convex loss, Lipschitz/smoothness, strong convexity, stability, randomized initialization) and whether the guarantees depend on these properties holding at the objective or algorithmic level.
- Computational overhead of Radon-point computation is not fully discussed. In high-dimensional parameter spaces, computing Radon points can be nontrivial (potentially requiring solving systems with dimension-dependent cost). The paper should quantify this overhead relative to training time, discuss numerical stability, and explain how the method scales as the number of parameters grows.
- Experimental evaluation, while promising, leaves several questions:
  - Fairness and strength of baselines: Spark-based methods can be configured in many ways; details on hyperparameters, communication frequency, and tuning effort are essential. Comparisons to stronger distributed optimization baselines (e.g., parameter-server SGD, ADMM/CoCoA variants, mini-batch and variance-reduced distributed methods) appear to be missing.
  - Scalability analysis: The paper reports reduced learning time, but there is limited analysis of strong/weak scaling, speedup curves versus number of workers, and communication/aggregation overhead breakdowns.
  - Robustness: It is unclear how the method performs under non-i.i.d. data partitions, varying partition sizes, or heterogeneous worker speeds. These are common in distributed settings and may affect aggregation quality.
  - Statistical rigor: Please report multiple runs with confidence intervals, and clarify whether improvements are statistically significant.
- Clarity: The paper could better motivate why Radon points should outperform averaging in this setting—e.g., by giving geometric intuition and small synthetic examples, or by relating Radon aggregation to bias/variance trade-offs of the local estimators.
- Reproducibility: It is not stated whether code, scripts, and detailed configurations will be released. Exact datasets, pre-processing, and hyperparameter grids should be specified.

Detailed comments and questions:
- Clearly cite and position against “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization.” What is the incremental contribution of this submission? For example:
  - New theoretical results (tighter bounds, weaker assumptions, finite-sample guarantees)?
  - New algorithms (improved Radon computation, hierarchical aggregation, communication-efficient variants)?
  - New empirical coverage (larger-scale experiments, more tasks, ablations)?
- Provide a complexity analysis of Radon aggregation in terms of dimension d, number of local models m, and number of aggregation rounds. If using recursive/hierarchical aggregation, specify the depth and the total computation/communication cost.
- Discuss applicability to non-convex models. If the method is restricted to convex problems, state this clearly. If applied to non-convex objectives (e.g., deep networks), what behavior should be expected, and are there empirical results to support this?
- Provide ablations isolating the effect of the aggregator:
  - Radon vs. mean vs. geometric median vs. trimmed mean or other robust aggregators.
  - Sensitivity to number of partitions and sample size per partition.
  - Effect under synthetic distribution shift across partitions.
- Expand related work to cover distributed and federated optimization methods commonly compared in the literature circa NIPS 2017 (e.g., parameter averaging in parallel SGD, ADMM-based approaches, CoCoA, SVRG/SAGA variants in distributed settings, elastic averaging). Explain theoretical and empirical differences.
- Include a breakdown of wall-clock time by component (local training, communication, aggregation) to demonstrate that Radon aggregation does not become a bottleneck at scale.
- Clarify whether the Spark baselines are model-parallel or data-parallel and how fault tolerance and stragglers are handled, since these can affect wall-clock comparisons.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents a clean and potentially impactful idea: replacing naive averaging with Radon-point aggregation for parallelizing learners satisfying certain convexity/stability assumptions. The empirical results are encouraging and suggest practical gains without sacrificing accuracy. However, the novelty relative to closely related prior work is insufficiently articulated, a key citation appears to be missing, and the experimental evaluation lacks depth on scalability, robustness, and baseline strength. With clearer positioning, a more comprehensive and transparent experimental study, and a careful discussion of computational overhead and assumptions, this work could be a strong contribution. In its current form, it is promising but borderline due to the unresolved novelty and evaluation concerns.
