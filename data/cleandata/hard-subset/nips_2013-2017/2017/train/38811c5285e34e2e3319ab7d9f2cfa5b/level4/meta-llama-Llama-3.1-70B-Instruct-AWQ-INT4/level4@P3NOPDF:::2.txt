The authors propose a novel approach to parallelizing learners that rely on certain assumptions, leveraging Radon points to achieve this goal. The key contribution of the paper, distinguishing it from similar works, lies in its use of Radon points as an alternative to the traditional averaging method, which serves as a baseline approach in the paper. The performance of the proposed method is evaluated against three benchmarks: 'no parallelization', 'averaging schema', and 'Spark-based' algorithms. The experimental results demonstrate a reduction in learning time, while maintaining similar or higher accuracy compared to the other algorithms.

However, it appears that the theoretical foundation of this work has been previously explored in another paper, "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization". Notably, this preceding work is not cited in the current paper, which raises questions about the originality of the theoretical contributions presented here. To strengthen the paper, the authors should explicitly elaborate on what new insights or advancements the current paper brings to the theoretical aspects of parallelizing randomized convex optimization, distinguishing it from the prior work. This clarification is essential to establish the paper's unique contribution and significance in the field.
