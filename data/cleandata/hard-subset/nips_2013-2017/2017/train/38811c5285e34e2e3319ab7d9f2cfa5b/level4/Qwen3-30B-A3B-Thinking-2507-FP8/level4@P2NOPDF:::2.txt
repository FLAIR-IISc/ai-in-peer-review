

The paper proposes a parallelization framework for learners operating under specific assumptions, leveraging Radon points instead of conventional averaging techniques. The primary contribution lies in this methodological shift, with experimental validation demonstrating reduced training time compared to "no parallelization," "averaging-based," and "Spark-based" baselines, while maintaining comparable or superior accuracy.  

However, a significant concern arises regarding theoretical novelty. The core theoretical foundation—parallelizing randomized convex optimization—appears to be directly addressed in the authors' prior work, "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (cited as [1] in the draft). This prior paper establishes the theoretical groundwork for the approach, yet the current submission fails to cite or explicitly differentiate its contribution from this existing work. The paper must clarify whether the current contribution is:  
(i) an application of this framework to a new problem domain,  
(ii) a refinement of the theoretical guarantees, or  
(iii) a novel implementation for specific learner architectures. Without this elaboration, the theoretical novelty is obscured, undermining the paper’s claim of advancing the state of the art.  

The experimental section adequately compares performance metrics but lacks depth in analyzing *why* Radon points outperform averaging in specific scenarios (e.g., sensitivity to data distribution or communication overhead). Additionally, the Spark-based baseline is mentioned without specifying which implementation (e.g., MLlib) was used, limiting reproducibility.  

While the empirical results are compelling, the omission of critical citations and the unclear theoretical distinction weaken the paper’s positioning. Addressing these gaps would substantially strengthen the contribution. The work has potential but requires revision to explicitly contextualize its novelty relative to prior theoretical work and to deepen the analysis of the proposed method’s advantages.
