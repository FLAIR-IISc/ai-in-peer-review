The authors present a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms based on Radon points. The core idea is to apply the underlying learning algorithm in parallel to random subsets of the data, and then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points. This approach aims to reduce runtime to polylogarithmic time on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units, at the cost of increased sample complexity. The work contrasts with averaging-based parallelization techniques and demonstrates empirical improvements in runtime while maintaining comparable or superior accuracy against both sequential learning and established parallel frameworks like Spark’s MLlib.

This paper builds upon a growing body of work exploring parallelization strategies for machine learning. Averaging-based methods [32, 45, 46] have been previously explored, and the authors correctly position their work as an alternative that offers theoretical advantages in runtime reduction. The connection to Nick’s Class (NC) [9] provides a theoretical framework for evaluating the efficiency of the parallelization and positions the work within a broader context of parallel computation. The use of Radon points for aggregation is a key novelty, differentiating the approach from simpler averaging schemes.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretical grounding and practical demonstration. The authors provide a clear theoretical analysis showing the potential for polylogarithmic runtime, along with corresponding bounds on the increased sample complexity. The empirical evaluation is well-conducted, comparing the Radon machine against relevant baselines (sequential learning, averaging, and Spark MLlib) across several datasets. The reported speedups and comparable performance are compelling. The authors also acknowledge limitations related to high-dimensional data and suggest potential remedies using techniques like random projections [15] and low-rank approximations [2, 28]. 

However, the paper would benefit from several clarifications. While the authors mention related work on parallelizing randomized convex optimization, they do not explicitly cite or discuss their previous work in this area, leaving the reader wondering about the precise contribution of *this* paper relative to their earlier efforts.  A more detailed comparison to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” would be valuable. Furthermore, the discussion of communication complexity is brief and could be expanded, particularly given the importance of communication costs in distributed learning. While the authors mention low communication compared to periodic schemes, providing more concrete analysis or experimental data would strengthen this claim. The section discussing datasets where the number of dimensions is much smaller than the number of instances could be clarified. The statement feels somewhat dismissive; a more thorough explanation of how the suggested remedies (random projections, low-rank approximations) address the core challenge would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The use of Radon points for parallelizing learning algorithms is a novel approach.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis supporting the claimed runtime reduction.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate substantial speedups and competitive accuracy compared to existing methods.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Missing Citation:** The lack of explicit discussion and citation of the authors’ prior related work hinders a full understanding of the contribution.
*   **Limited Communication Analysis:** The analysis of communication complexity is brief and could be more detailed.
*   **High-Dimensional Data Discussion:** The discussion of applying the scheme to high-dimensional models feels incomplete.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling, and the approach has the potential to significantly accelerate the training of various learning algorithms. Addressing the minor concerns regarding the related work citation, communication analysis, and high-dimensional data discussion would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
