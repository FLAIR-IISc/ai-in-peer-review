

The authors present a novel parallelization scheme, the Radon machine, which effectively parallelizes a broad class of learning algorithms while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. This work addresses a fundamental open question in parallel machine learning regarding efficient parallelization in the sense of Nick's Class (NC), where the goal is to achieve polylogarithmic runtime on polynomially many processors. The key insight is using Radon points for aggregation rather than averaging, which enables doubly exponential confidence improvement with the height of the aggregation tree. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that runtime can be reduced from polynomial to polylogarithmic on quasi-polynomially many processing units, with empirical results confirming substantial speedups (80-700x) compared to sequential implementations and Spark MLlib algorithms while maintaining comparable predictive performance across multiple datasets and problem types.

Strengths:
- The theoretical contribution is significant, providing a step toward answering the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be performed in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors.
- The Radon machine represents a true black-box parallelization scheme applicable to a wide range of learning algorithms without requiring mathematical derivations or dedicated code.
- The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against both base learning algorithms and state-of-the-art Spark MLlib implementations across multiple datasets and problem types (classification, regression, multi-class).
- The authors effectively address potential concerns about high-dimensional data, non-linear models, and limited processing units in the Discussion section.

Weaknesses:
- Minor formatting inconsistencies in the manuscript affect readability.
- The performance difference between the Radon machine and the averaging baseline is small in practical terms, and this result should be better highlighted in the main figures.

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed empirical validation. It is clearly written (with minor formatting exceptions), original in its approach to parallel learning, and significant for addressing a fundamental question in the field. The Radon machine represents a meaningful contribution to both the theoretical and practical aspects of parallel machine learning.
