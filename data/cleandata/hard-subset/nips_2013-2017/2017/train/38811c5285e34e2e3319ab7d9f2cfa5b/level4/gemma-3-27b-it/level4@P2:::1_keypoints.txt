- Introduces the "Radon machine": parallelization scheme that aggregates hypotheses via Radon points from random data subsets.  
- Claims runtime reduction to polylogarithmic time using a quasi-polynomial number of processors, trading off increased sample complexity.  
- Provides strong theoretical foundations, including PAC-style bounds and formal complexity analysis.  
- Frames contribution within parallel computation/NC, addressing efficient parallelization of ML algorithms.  
- Empirical results show significant speed-ups over sequential execution and competitive performance versus Spark/Weka baselines.  
- Main weakness: experiments use mostly low-dimensional datasets (e.g., ~18 features), limiting evidence of real-world scalability.  
- Authors suggest remedies (random projections, low-rank approximations) but present no empirical validation for high-dimensional settings.  
- Discussion of communication complexity is limited and should be expanded given its importance in distributed learning.  
- Argument for acceptance: novelty of Radon-based aggregation as an alternative to averaging.  
- Argument for acceptance: rigorous theoretical analysis and complexity guarantees.  
- Argument for acceptance: empirical validation showing speed-ups and competitiveness with existing frameworks.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited scalability demonstration and insufficient communication-cost analysis.  
- Recommended revisions: expand empirical evaluation to high-dimensional datasets and test proposed mitigation techniques.  
- Recommended revisions: provide a more detailed communication-complexity analysis and clarify tradeoffs with averaging methods.  
- Overall assessment: worthwhile contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing scalability, communication, and relation to averaging.
