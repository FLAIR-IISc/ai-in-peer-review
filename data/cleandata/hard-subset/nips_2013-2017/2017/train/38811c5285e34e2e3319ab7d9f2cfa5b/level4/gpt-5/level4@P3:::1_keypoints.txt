- Proposes the Radon machine: a black-box, tree-structured Radon-point aggregation scheme for parallelizing convex regularized risk minimization.  
- Key theoretical claim: Radon aggregation yields doubly-exponential reduction in failure probability (≈(rδ)^(2^h)) vs tree height h, enabling polylog runtime with enough processors.  
- Runtime/result trade-off: polylog time achievable with quasi-polynomial processors; aggregation cost O(r^3) per level and total O(log^κ M + r^3 log M) under base-learner O(n^κ).  
- Assumptions clearly stated: finite-dimensional Euclidean parametrization, consistent/efficient base learner, and δ ≤ 1/(2r).  
- Empirical results: large speedups (80x–700x on 150 cores) and comparable AUC to base learners and Spark MLlib on several large datasets.  
- Comparison baselines: positioned appropriately against averaging schemes, distributed SGD/ADMM, NC literature, and prior parallel half-space learning.  
- Novelty: leveraging Radon points for confidence amplification in a black-box parallelization is original and nontrivial.  
- Main technical caveat: scalability issues in high dimensions since r = d+2 leads to O(d^3) aggregation cost and minimum batch-size requirements.  
- Mitigations discussed (random projections, low-rank approximations) but their effects on theoretical guarantees are not quantified.  
- Practical requirement concern: ensuring base-learner failure δ ≤ 1/(2r) may be demanding in high-d settings and lacks sensitivity analysis.  
- Clarity strengths: manuscript well organized, Algorithm 1 clear, and NC/practical simulation discussion useful.  
- Clarity suggestions: move Radon linear system from appendix to main text and discuss uniqueness/numerical stability.  
- Clarity suggestions: add a practitioner guide for choosing h given data size, r, ε, Δ, and processor budget.  
- Clarity suggestions: explicitly state communication dependence on r and h and include wall-clock breakdown of learning vs aggregation.  
- Experimental suggestions: include high-dimensional experiments (≥10^4 dims) with projections/random features and quantify impact on accuracy/runtime and guarantees.  
- Processor-scaling suggestions: report scaling when c ≪ r^h and show how speedup degrades with limited cores.  
- Evaluation/tuning suggestions: clarify Spark hyperparameter tuning, include confidence intervals, training/communication breakdowns, and consider distributed mini-batch SGD baselines.  
- Nonlinearity suggestion: add at least one experiment with explicit nonlinear features (e.g., random Fourier features).  
- Arguments for acceptance: original theory, clear NC connection, strong empirical speedups, low communication, and implementation-agnostic appeal.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited high-dimensional evaluation, reliance on quasi-polynomial processors for polylog guarantees, and missing sensitivity analysis for δ ≤ 1/(2r).  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept; promising framework that would be stronger with experimental/theoretical treatment of high-dimensional embeddings and limited-core behavior.
