This paper introduces a method for parallelizing machine learning algorithms that rely on specific assumptions, leveraging the concept of Radon points. The primary contribution lies in substituting the traditional averaging approach – commonly used as a baseline for parallel learning – with a technique based on Radon points. The authors demonstrate, through empirical evaluation, that this approach reduces learning time while maintaining comparable or improved accuracy when compared to several alternatives: a non-parallelized implementation, an averaging-based parallelization scheme, and a Spark-based algorithm.

The experimental results appear promising, showing a clear benefit in terms of computational efficiency. However, the theoretical foundations of this work seem closely related to the authors’ previous work, “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization.” This prior publication is not cited within the manuscript, and the relationship between the two papers – specifically, what novel theoretical advancements are presented *in this work* beyond the prior publication – requires clarification. It is unclear whether this paper builds upon, extends, or merely applies the theoretical results established previously. A detailed explanation of the theoretical contribution, differentiating it from the earlier work, is crucial for a full understanding of the paper's novelty. 

Without this elaboration, it’s difficult to assess the independent merit of the theoretical components presented here. The practical benefits demonstrated through experimentation are valuable, but a clear understanding of the theoretical underpinning is necessary for a strong acceptance.
