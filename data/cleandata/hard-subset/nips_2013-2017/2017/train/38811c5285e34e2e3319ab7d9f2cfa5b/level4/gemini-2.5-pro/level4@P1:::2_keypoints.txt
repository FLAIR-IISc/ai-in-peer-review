1. Proposes the "Radon Machine": partition data, train base learners in parallel, and iteratively aggregate hypotheses via Radon points instead of simple averaging.  
2. Theoretical guarantee: aggregation doubly-exponentially reduces the probability of producing a poor hypothesis (Theorem 3).  
3. Complexity implication: can lower runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic with a quasi-polynomial number of processors and increased sample complexity, linking to NC.  
4. Black-box applicability: works with any consistent, efficient learner in a convex hypothesis space with finite Radon number without modifying the base learner.  
5. Strong empirical results: reports large speedups (≈80x–700x) and competitive or improved predictive performance versus sequential learners, averaging baselines, and Spark MLlib.  
6. Novelty/overlap concern: potential overlap with prior work ("Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization") is not discussed and must be clarified to assess originality.  
7. High-dimensional limitation: Radon number = d+2 implies more partitions/processors/sample size in high dimensions; practical impact (e.g., NLP, kernels) needs deeper analysis or mitigation.  
8. Overall verdict: promising and well-motivated work; recommend acceptance if the authors clearly delineate novel contributions relative to related prior work.
