Summary
This paper proposes a simple, black-box parallelization scheme—the Radon machine—for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The method runs the base learner in parallel on multiple iid subsets to produce weak hypotheses, and then aggregates them up a tree using Radon points instead of averaging. Theoretically, the aggregation reduces the probability of a bad hypothesis doubly exponentially in the tree height, enabling polylogarithmic runtime (in the target sample complexity) on quasi-polynomially many processors, at the cost of increased sample complexity. Empirically, the authors report substantial training-time reductions and competitive or improved AUC compared to averaging-at-the-end and several Spark MLlib baselines across multiple datasets. The paper situates the work within averaging-based parallelization, distributed SGD, and the literature on Nick’s Class (NC), and addresses practical concerns (e.g., high-dimensional data, limited processors) in the Discussion.

Evaluation
- Quality: The theoretical results (Proposition 2, Theorem 3, Theorem 4) are clear and compelling, showing robust aggregation properties of Radon points and the resulting confidence amplification. Assumptions (convexity, finite-dimensional Euclidean parameterization, abundant data) are stated, and empirical evaluations are thorough for the considered settings. The cost trade-offs (sample size and number of processors) are appropriately acknowledged.
- Clarity: Overall well written and organized, with a clean description of the algorithm and its guarantees. Some presentation issues (figure legibility, a few typos, and citation style inconsistencies) should be corrected; see specific notes below.
- Originality: Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation to obtain doubly exponential confidence improvement is novel in this context and distinct from averaging-based approaches and distributed SGD. The explicit connection to NC-style parallel efficiency is interesting and relatively uncommon in ML papers.
- Significance: The scheme is broadly applicable as a black-box parallelization for regularized risk minimizers and shows strong practical speedups with comparable predictive performance to standard baselines. Although requiring many processors and increased data, the approach makes a meaningful step toward efficient parallel learning with guarantees.

Pros
- Black-box applicability to many convex regularized risk minimizers.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: confidence amplification via Radon aggregation; polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomial processors.
- Consistent empirical speedups and competitive AUC across datasets.
- Thoughtful discussion of limitations and practical scenarios (e.g., high dimensionality, fewer processors, communication).

Cons / Questions
- Dependence on a finite (and potentially large) Radon number may limit direct applicability for very high-dimensional or kernelized models without additional approximations.
- The required number of processors and increased sample complexity may be substantial in some settings.
- Some empirical claims would benefit from statistical testing or clarified comparison methodology (see below).
- Figures need improved legibility.

Recommendation: Accept.

Specific notes and minor comments
- Line 51: “the the” → “the”.
- Line 79: The math font appears smaller than elsewhere and is hard to read; please standardize.
- Figures 1–3: Increase font sizes and line weights. Ensure axes and legends are legible when printed.
- Figure 3: Consider patterns or different markers; the current color scheme may be hard to distinguish in black-and-white print.
- Lines 318–320: “the Radon machine outperforms the averaging baseline…” Please clarify the evaluation protocol: Is this conclusion based on paired comparisons across folds/seeds? Report whether differences are statistically significant (e.g., paired tests) or include confidence intervals.
- Line 324: Please avoid “significant” unless referring to statistical significance; use “substantial,” “marked,” or provide statistical evidence.
- Lines 340–341: Missing spaces: “machineachieved” → “machine achieved”; “machineis” → “machine is.”
- Lines 341–349: The observed difference versus AverageAtTheEnd appears modest. Could you (i) include these results in Figure 1 for a side-by-side runtime/quality view, and (ii) better motivate when Radon aggregation yields clear gains over averaging (e.g., regimes of δ, r, h, and dimension)?
- Line 377: “Long and servedio [22]” Use consistent citation style and capitalization of names throughout, matching other references.
- Throughout: Where feasible, report variability (e.g., standard errors) and/or significance tests for AUC to support comparative claims.
- Minor: Consider a brief implementation note or pointer on computing Radon points efficiently/stably in high dimensions (even though Appendix C.1 outlines the linear system), and any numerical conditioning issues encountered.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and carefully analyzed contribution with a clean algorithm and solid empirical support. With the clarity fixes and a bit more rigor in the empirical comparisons (especially versus averaging), the paper will be stronger.
