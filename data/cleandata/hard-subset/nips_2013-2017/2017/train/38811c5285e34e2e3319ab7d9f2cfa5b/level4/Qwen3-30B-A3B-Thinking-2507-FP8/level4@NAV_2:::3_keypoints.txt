- Paper introduces the Radon machine, a novel parallelization scheme that parallelizes many learning algorithms while preserving theoretical guarantees.
- Key insight: use Radon points for aggregation instead of averaging, yielding doubly exponential confidence gains with aggregation-tree height.
- Theoretical result: runtime can be reduced from polynomial to polylogarithmic using quasi-polynomially many processors, addressing an open NC-style question.
- Empirical results show substantial speedups (80–700×) over sequential implementations and Spark MLlib while maintaining comparable predictive performance.
- The method is a true black-box parallelization applicable to a wide range of algorithms without re-derivation or dedicated code.
- Theoretical contribution is significant for consistent regularized risk minimization in parallel settings.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough, covering base learners, Spark MLlib baselines, multiple datasets, and problem types (classification, regression, multi-class).
- Discussion addresses concerns about high-dimensional data, non-linear models, and limited processing units.
- Paper is technically sound, original, and mostly clearly written.
- Minor formatting inconsistencies reduce readability and should be fixed (fonts, figure text size, spacing).
- Practical performance difference between Radon machine and averaging baseline is small and should be highlighted more prominently in figures.
- Several textual and citation errors need correction (typos, inconsistent math font size, figure distinguishability in B/W, citation formatting).
