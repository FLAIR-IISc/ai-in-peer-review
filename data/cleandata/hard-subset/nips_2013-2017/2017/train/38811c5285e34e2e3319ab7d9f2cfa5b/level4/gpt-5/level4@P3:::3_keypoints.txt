- Method: Proposes the Radon machine — black-box parallelization that trains on iid subsets and aggregates groups of r=d+2 hypotheses via Radon points in an r-ary tree of height h.  
- Theory: Shows doubly exponential reduction in error probability with tree height (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) and a path to polylogarithmic runtime under consistency and finite Radon number (Theorem 4).  
- Costs: Achieves speedups at the expense of quasi-polynomial processor counts and increased total sample complexity.  
- Empirics: Large wall-clock speedups (up to ~700x with 150 processors), matching/exceeding AUC of base learners and beating an averaging baseline across several datasets and Spark MLlib alternatives.  
- Quality: Theoretical analysis is clear and technically sound, with an elegant confidence-amplification via Radon aggregation.  
- Quality caveats: Several assumptions need discussion — requirement for quasi-polynomial processors, need for δ ≤ 1/2^r for base learners, Euclidean embedding’s effect on r, and increased sample complexity.  
- Empirical limitations: Experiments lack statistical tests, error bars, and deeper sensitivity analysis to h, r, and dimensionality.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-organized and readable, but figures have small fonts and there are minor typographical and citation inconsistencies.  
- Originality: Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation and confidence amplification is novel and distinct from averaging and distributed SGD.  
- Significance: Significant generic, implementation-agnostic contribution with theoretical guarantees and practical speedups; applicability limited by processor and r dependencies.  
- Pro: Black-box applicability requiring minimal changes to base learners.  
- Pro: Strong theoretical results — doubly exponential failure reduction and potential for polylog runtime (with caveats).  
- Pro: Extensive experiments across tasks showing large speedups and competitive accuracy.  
- Pro: Thoughtful discussion of communication, deparallelization, and extensions.  
- Con: Polylogarithmic runtime requires quasi-polynomial processors; stronger NC-like guarantees remain open.  
- Con: Dependence on r=d+2 makes high-dimensional application challenging without dimensionality reduction.  
- Con: Empirical analysis needs statistical significance tests, error bars, and clearer paired comparisons.  
- Con: Presentation issues (figure legibility, typos, citation style) reduce clarity.  
- Question: Is the outperforming of the averaging baseline based on paired comparisons per split; please report mean ± std/CI and paired-test results across folds.  
- Question: How sensitive are results to h and r (dimensionality); provide ablation AUC/runtime vs h.  
- Question: For c < r^h processors, can you report empirical scaling (e.g., 16/32/64/128 cores) to validate the theoretical max{1, r^h/c} factor?  
- Question: What numerical conditioning issues arise when solving r×r systems for Radon points in higher dimensions and what stability safeguards are used?  
- Suggestion: Increase figure font sizes, use grayscale-friendly styles, add error bars, and centralize Avg vs Radon comparisons.  
- Suggestion: Include paired statistical tests for AUC/runtime and avoid the word “significant” without tests.  
- Suggestion: Clarify how h is chosen, report δ achieved by base learners if measurable, provide sensitivity to subset size, and add calibration metrics.  
- Suggestion: Precisely state NC-related claims and discuss when r can be bounded by polylog(sample complexity).  
- Suggestion: Add a brief experiment with random features or Nyström for high-dimensional regimes and provide reproducibility scripts and key hyperparameters.  
- Minor edits: Fix typos (“the the”), standardize math fonts, improve figure legibility, clarify paired-comparison claims, avoid unsupported use of “significant,” fix spacing errors, and ensure consistent citation style.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
