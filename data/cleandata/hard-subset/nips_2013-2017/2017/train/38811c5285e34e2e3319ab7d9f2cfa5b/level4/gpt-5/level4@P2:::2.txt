Summary
The paper proposes a generic, black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization, termed the Radon machine. The method trains a base learner independently on many random data subsets and aggregates the resulting hypotheses through an r-ary tree using Radon points instead of averaging. Under standard convexity and consistency assumptions and with a finite Radon number (r = d+2 in Rd), the authors prove that the probability of an ε-bad hypothesis shrinks doubly exponentially with the tree height h, enabling the same (ε, Δ) guarantees as the sequential learner in polylogarithmic time, at the cost of larger sample size and quasi-polynomially many processing units. The Radon point at each node is computed via a small linear system, and the overall aggregation overhead is O(hr^3) when done in parallel.

Empirically, the paper reports substantial speedups over single-processor base learners and competitive or better accuracy/runtime trade-offs compared to Spark MLlib baselines, on classification, regression, and multiclass tasks. The approach outperforms “averaging at the end” in accuracy with similar runtime.

Relation to prior work
The method contrasts with well-known averaging-based schemes (e.g., bagging-style averaging, parameter averaging in distributed learning) and distributed SGD/ADMM approaches, which typically require many communication rounds and do not achieve polylogarithmic time. The theoretical framing connects to NC-style parallel complexity and prior results on PAC learnability in parallel. A closely related line appears to be work on iterated Radon points and “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization.” The latter seems highly relevant but is not cited; the paper should clearly delineate what is new here (e.g., generalization from optimization to learning guarantees, improved bounds, new empirical evidence, or different aggregation analysis).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: replacing averaging with iterated Radon points yields strong confidence amplification and a clean compositional parallelization scheme applicable as a black box to many convex learners.
- Theoretical guarantees: clear (ε, Δ) performance transfer from base learner to the parallel scheme, with doubly exponential reduction in error probability as a function of tree depth; transparent aggregation cost O(hr^3).
- Clear connection to parallel complexity (NC) and discussion of the processor/sample trade-offs.
- Empirical results: impressive speedups (up to ~700x with 150 processors) while maintaining or improving accuracy, and consistent gains over naïve averaging in predictive performance.
- Practicality: simple to implement and low communication (aggregation at the end), with code provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty and attribution: The theoretical core appears closely related to prior work on Radon-based aggregation for randomized convex optimization. The paper must cite “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” (likely by overlapping authors) and explain the incremental contributions (theory and practice).
- Practical scalability limits: The scheme assumes quasi-polynomially many processors; while Section 5 discusses deparallelization, the main runtime claims rely on strong parallel resources. More systematic experiments with c < r^h processors would strengthen the practical message.
- Dimensionality dependence: r = d+2 makes aggregation O(r^3) potentially costly in high dimensions, and the requirement δ ≤ 1/2^r for base learners becomes stringent as d grows. While the authors mention random projections/low-rank embeddings, the experiments focus on relatively low-dimensional linear models. Additional empirical validation in higher dimensions or with explicit embeddings would help.
- Sample complexity inflation: The method trades runtime for larger sample size. The paper would benefit from reporting the actual sample size multipliers, the achieved Δ, and comparisons at fixed data budgets (accuracy vs. runtime).
- Baseline fairness and scope: Comparisons mix WEKA/scikit-learn base learners with Spark MLlib baselines. It would be useful to clarify hyperparameter tuning, optimization tolerances, and whether the Spark baselines are configured comparably. Also, results on the “wikidata” set show uniformly weak performance; more analysis would improve trust.
- Assumptions and constants: Some assumptions (e.g., form of N0(ε, δ), bound on Radon number being polylog in sample complexity in Theorem 4) are stated succinctly; it would help to make explicit when these hold for common learners and to include concrete αε, βε, k for typical models in the main text (currently deferred to the appendix).

Quality
The theoretical development is sound and clearly presented, assuming the correctness of the Radon aggregation bounds (Proposition 2, Theorem 3). The jump to polylogarithmic runtime under NC-inspired criteria is well motivated but hinges on processor availability and dimensionality considerations. The empirical section is thorough for linear models and provides compelling speedups; however, more transparency on experimental protocol and resource constraints would enhance reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized. Algorithm 1 is clear. Suggestions:
- Summarize the Radon point computation in the main text with a brief linear system description and its numerical stability considerations.
- Provide a concise table mapping base learners to their sample complexity forms and the resulting r, h, and runtime terms.
- Clearly state the independence assumptions for the subset training and how data splitting is performed.

Originality
Using iterated Radon points for aggregation in parallel learning is interesting and, as framed here, yields new learning guarantees. However, the overlap with prior Radon-based parallel optimization work must be acknowledged and the novel aspects emphasized.

Significance
The work offers a compelling perspective on black-box parallelization with theoretical and practical benefits. Even if the NC-level claims require strong parallelism, the empirical speedups and accuracy gains over averaging make this a useful contribution. The approach could influence future designs of distributed learners that aim for stronger generalization guarantees with minimal communication.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical guarantees for a broadly applicable scheme.
- Significant empirical speedups with competitive accuracy.
- Low communication and simple implementation.
- Clear contribution relative to averaging-based baselines.

Cons against acceptance
- Insufficient discussion of novelty relative to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization.”
- Practical constraints in high dimensions and with limited processors not fully explored.
- Limited evidence for non-linear/high-dimensional settings beyond discussion.

Requested changes
- Cite and compare to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization,” clarifying the new contributions.
- Add experiments or analysis for c < r^h processors and report sample-size/Δ trade-offs.
- Provide high-dimensional or embedded-kernel experiments, or a more detailed justification.
- Clarify experimental settings and ensure comparable baselines.
