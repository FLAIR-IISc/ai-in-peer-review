

This paper introduces the "Radon machine," a novel framework for parallelizing machine learning algorithms that significantly reduces runtime while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The authors propose a black-box parallelization scheme that replaces the common practice of averaging hypotheses with a more effective aggregation using Radon points. The main theoretical contribution demonstrates that the Radon machine achieves the same (ε,δ)-guarantees as sequential learning but in polylogarithmic time using quasi-polynomially many processors. Empirical results show 80-700x speedups over sequential implementations while maintaining comparable predictive performance to Spark's MLlib algorithms.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning. It directly addresses an open question about efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms in the sense of Nick's Class (NC), which has been previously posed as a "fundamental open problem" by Long and Servedio. The theoretical analysis builds upon and extends prior work on NC-learnable concept classes (Vitter and Lin) and parallel learning of half-spaces. The Radon machine framework is particularly notable for being a black-box approach that can be applied to a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms without requiring mathematical derivations or code modifications.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical contributions are technically sound, with well-structured proofs for Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 that clearly establish the probabilistic guarantees of the Radon machine. The authors carefully analyze both the theoretical complexity (sample and runtime) and practical implementation considerations. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with comparisons to both baseline averaging methods and state-of-the-art Spark MLlib algorithms across multiple datasets. The authors provide detailed runtime and performance metrics, including the critical observation that the Radon machine achieves comparable predictive performance to Spark algorithms while being substantially faster.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from theoretical foundations to empirical validation. The Radon machine algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly described, and the connection between Radon points and the aggregation process is well-explained. The paper adequately references relevant prior work on parallel learning, including discussions of related approaches such as averaging-based parallelizations and stochastic gradient descent methods.

The main weakness of the paper is the limited scope of the empirical evaluation to relatively low-dimensional datasets. The experiments primarily use datasets with 18 or fewer features (e.g., SUSY with 18 features), which does not reflect the high-dimensional nature of many modern machine learning applications. The authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 5 but do not sufficiently elaborate on how their method would scale to high-dimensional problems. While they mention techniques like random projections and low-rank approximations as potential solutions, they do not provide empirical evidence or detailed analysis of how these approaches would integrate with the Radon machine for high-dimensional data. This is a significant concern, as most contemporary machine learning applications involve high-dimensional feature spaces.

The significance of this work is substantial. The theoretical results directly address an important open question in machine learning parallelization, and the practical speedups demonstrated (80-700x) are impressive for real-world applications. The black-box nature of the approach makes it broadly applicable across different learning algorithms without requiring algorithm-specific modifications, which is a significant practical advantage. For critical applications where high-confidence predictions are essential (e.g., medical diagnosis, intrusion detection), the Radon machine could provide substantial benefits by enabling faster training without sacrificing prediction quality.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical contribution addressing a fundamental open question
- Novel use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation (superior to averaging)
- Clear black-box parallelization approach applicable to multiple algorithms
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing significant speedups
- Well-structured paper with clear theoretical and practical connections

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical evaluation to low-dimensional datasets (18 features or fewer)
- Insufficient discussion of high-dimensional data handling
- Theoretical results assume sufficient processing units (rh), with limited discussion of practical constraints
- The practical impact on high-dimensional problems remains uncertain

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a significant open question in machine learning parallelization
- Provides both strong theoretical guarantees and empirical validation
- Introduces a novel and broadly applicable parallelization technique
- Demonstrates substantial practical speedups (80-700x) with comparable predictive performance
- Well-written and clearly organized paper

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical evaluation to low-dimensional datasets, which may not reflect real-world applicability
- Insufficient discussion of how the method would scale to high-dimensional problems
- Theoretical results require quasi-polynomially many processors, which may not be practical in all settings

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a theoretically sound and practically effective approach to parallel machine learning. While the limited scope of the empirical evaluation to low-dimensional datasets is a notable weakness, the theoretical framework and empirical results on the datasets tested are strong. The authors could significantly strengthen the paper by expanding their empirical evaluation to include high-dimensional datasets or providing a more detailed analysis of how their method would handle high-dimensional data. Given the importance of the open question addressed and the promising results presented, this paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS.
