

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that uses Radon points for aggregation instead of averaging. The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processing units while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The main theoretical contribution is showing that the probability of generating an ε-bad hypothesis decreases doubly exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree, compared to the single-exponential decrease with averaging. Empirically, the Radon Machine achieves 80-700x speedups over sequential implementations using 150 processors, with comparable or better predictive performance than Spark-based algorithms and the averaging-at-the-end baseline.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning, particularly in relation to Nick's Class (NC) and the question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be performed in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors. The authors connect their work to Long and Servedio's fundamental open problem regarding parallel learning of half-spaces, positioning their scheme as a step toward answering this question.

Regarding quality, the paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with well-motivated propositions and theorems establishing the performance guarantees of the Radon Machine. The proof techniques are sound and well-presented, particularly Proposition 2 which shows the robustness of Radon points compared to averages. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against both the sequential base learner, the averaging-at-the-end baseline, and Spark's MLlib implementations across multiple datasets and learning algorithms. The experimental design is appropriate, using 10-fold cross-validation and comparing both runtime and predictive performance (measured by AUC and RMSE).

The paper's main weakness concerns the theoretical novelty. The authors cite no connection to their previous work "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (which appears to contain similar theoretical foundations), despite being authored by the same group. This omission is significant because it's unclear what distinguishes the current theoretical contribution from their prior work. The paper should explicitly address this relationship and clarify how the current work advances beyond their previous results.

The paper is generally well-written and clear, with Algorithm 1 providing a concise description of the Radon Machine. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, though some technical details (like the Radon number calculation) might benefit from additional explanation for readers less familiar with convex geometry. The organization is logical, progressing from theoretical foundations to empirical validation.

In terms of originality, the core idea of using Radon points for aggregation rather than averaging represents a meaningful departure from standard parallelization approaches. The paper effectively demonstrates how this approach provides a significant theoretical advantage (doubly-exponential confidence improvement) over averaging. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature, referencing key papers on parallel learning including Vitter and Lin (1985), Long and Servedio (2010), and others. However, the connection to the authors' own prior work needs clarification to fully establish the novelty of the current contribution.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses a practically important problem: the need for high-confidence predictions in critical applications (e.g., medical diagnosis, intrusion detection) where errors have high costs. The demonstrated speedups (80-700x) with comparable accuracy make this approach highly relevant for real-world applications with growing data volumes. The black-box nature of the parallelization scheme (requiring no modifications to the base learning algorithm) further enhances its practical appeal.

The Radon Machine's applicability to a broad class of learning algorithms (SVMs, regularized least squares, logistic regression) represents a significant advantage over specialized parallelization methods. The empirical results showing comparable or better accuracy than Spark implementations while being substantially faster (up to 10x faster on large datasets) are particularly compelling. The paper also thoughtfully addresses limitations, discussing how the scheme can be adapted for high-dimensional data using low-rank approximations and for non-linear models using feature engineering.

Pro acceptance:
- Novel use of Radon points for aggregation with theoretical guarantees
- Demonstrated significant speedups (80-700x) with comparable accuracy
- Well-structured theoretical foundation addressing an open question in parallel learning
- Practical black-box parallelization approach applicable to many algorithms
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation against multiple baselines

Con acceptance:
- Unclear relationship to authors' previous work "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (not cited)
- Theoretical novelty needs stronger justification compared to prior work by authors
- Practical applicability depends on having quasi-polynomially many processors (not always available)
- Limited discussion of communication complexity and scalability to very large numbers of processors

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with significant practical implications. The paper would be substantially strengthened by clarifying its relationship to the authors' prior work and more explicitly distinguishing the current theoretical contribution. With these clarifications, the paper would make a compelling contribution to NIPS. As currently written, the theoretical novelty is somewhat compromised by the lack of citation to prior work by the same authors, though the empirical results and practical significance remain strong.
