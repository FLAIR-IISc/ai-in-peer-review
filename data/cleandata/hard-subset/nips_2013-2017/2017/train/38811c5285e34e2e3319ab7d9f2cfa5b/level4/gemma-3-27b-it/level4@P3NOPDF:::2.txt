This paper proposes a novel approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms that rely on certain underlying assumptions, leveraging the concept of Radon points. The primary contribution of this work lies in the utilization of Radon points for parallelization, distinguishing it from existing methods that typically employ simple averaging schemes – a baseline extensively evaluated in this paper. The authors demonstrate empirically that their approach reduces learning time while maintaining comparable, and in some cases improved, accuracy compared to baselines including non-parallelized learning, averaging-based parallelization, and a Spark-based implementation.

The experimental results are promising, clearly illustrating the benefits of the Radon point approach in terms of reduced training time. The comparison to a Spark-based method is particularly valuable, as it positions the proposed technique within the landscape of established distributed computing frameworks. The authors adequately demonstrate the practical advantages of their method through a well-defined experimental setup. 

However, the paper lacks a crucial connection to related theoretical work. The authors’ methodology bears a strong resemblance to concepts explored in their earlier paper, “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization.” While the current work focuses on practical implementation and empirical validation, the theoretical foundations appear largely pre-established. This prior work is not cited within the manuscript, and the paper would benefit significantly from a clear articulation of its incremental contribution *beyond* the initial theoretical developments. Specifically, the authors should explicitly address how this paper extends the previous theoretical results, whether through novel applications, refinements to the methodology, or demonstration of scalability in practical settings that were not previously explored. A discussion of the practical challenges overcome in translating the theoretical framework into a working implementation would also strengthen the paper.

Further clarity regarding the assumptions underlying the applicability of this method would be beneficial. While the paper mentions that the approach is suited for learners with "certain assumptions," these assumptions are not explicitly stated or thoroughly discussed. Detailing these assumptions – for instance, convexity, smoothness, or specific data distributions – would allow readers to better understand the scope and limitations of the proposed technique.  Are there classes of problems for which this approach would be less effective? 

Minor points: The terminology regarding "Radon points" could be more consistently applied. A more formal definition and explanation of Radon points and their relevance to parallel learning early in the paper would improve readability.  Additionally, the presentation of the experimental results could be enhanced with statistical significance testing to support the claims of improved performance. While the observed improvements appear substantial, quantifying the statistical significance would bolster the confidence in the results.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the Radon point approach in reducing learning time. However, addressing the concerns regarding the connection to prior work and a more thorough discussion of underlying assumptions are necessary for a stronger, more complete presentation.
