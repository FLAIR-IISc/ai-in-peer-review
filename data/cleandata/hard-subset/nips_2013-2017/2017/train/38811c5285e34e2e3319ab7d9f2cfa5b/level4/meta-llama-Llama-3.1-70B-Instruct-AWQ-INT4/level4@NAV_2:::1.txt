The paper "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning" presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, which simplifies the adaptation of learning algorithms to growing amounts of data and accurate predictions in critical applications. The authors propose a Radon machine that effectively parallelises consistent and efficient learning algorithms without further mathematical derivations and without writing dedicated code. The main theoretical contribution of this paper is to show that algorithms satisfying certain conditions can be parallelised effectively, achieving the same confidence and error bounds as the sequential execution of the algorithm in much shorter time.

The paper relates to previous work in the field, including Nick's Class (NC) and the concept of efficient parallel algorithms. The authors discuss the limitations of current parallelisation techniques and highlight the potential of their approach to address the open question of efficient parallelisation of machine learning algorithms. The paper also provides an empirical evaluation of the Radon machine, comparing its performance to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms from the Spark machine learning library and the natural baseline of averaging hypotheses.

The strengths of the paper include its novel approach to parallelising machine learning algorithms, its theoretical analysis of the complexity of the Radon machine, and its empirical evaluation. The authors demonstrate that their approach can achieve a substantial reduction in computation time while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The paper also highlights the potential of the Radon machine to address the challenge of high-confidence error bounds in critical application scenarios.

However, there are some weaknesses and limitations to the paper. One major concern is the practical aspect, particularly for high-dimensional data. The experiments presented in the paper seem to be limited to low-dimensional datasets, which may not be representative of modern machine learning problems in most domains. The authors could strengthen the paper by emphasizing how their method can handle high-dimensional data and providing additional experiments to demonstrate its effectiveness in such scenarios.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the Radon machine and potential avenues for future research. The authors mention that the basic version of the parallelisation scheme presented in the paper cannot directly be applied to cases where the size of the dataset is not at least a multiple of the Radon number of the hypothesis space. However, they do not provide a detailed analysis of the implications of this limitation and potential solutions to address it.

In terms of the criteria for evaluation, the paper scores well on quality, as it presents a technically sound and well-supported approach to parallelising machine learning algorithms. The paper is also well-written and clearly organised, making it easy to follow and understand. However, the originality of the paper could be improved by providing more context and comparison to existing work in the field. The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a fundamental challenge in machine learning and provides a novel solution to the problem of parallelising machine learning algorithms.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to parallelising machine learning algorithms.
* The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the complexity of the Radon machine.
* The empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the Radon machine in achieving a substantial reduction in computation time while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper is limited to low-dimensional datasets, which may not be representative of modern machine learning problems in most domains.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the limitations of the Radon machine and potential avenues for future research.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the Radon machine and its potential applications in different domains.
