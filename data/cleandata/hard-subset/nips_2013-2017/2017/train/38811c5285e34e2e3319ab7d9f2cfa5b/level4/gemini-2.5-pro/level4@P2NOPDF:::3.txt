This paper presents a novel parallel algorithm for aggregating weak learners, leveraging the concept of Radon partitioning. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis to motivate their method, complemented by empirical results that validate the theory. The experiments, conducted on multiple datasets, demonstrate that the proposed "Radon machine" can achieve improvements in training time and/or AUC when compared against several baseline algorithms. Furthermore, the paper is strengthened by the authors' proactive approach in the Evaluation and Discussion sections, where they anticipate and convincingly address a number of potential questions and concerns. Overall, this is a strong contribution, and I recommend acceptance.

**Strengths**
*   The theoretical foundation of the algorithm, based on Radon's theorem, is interesting and provides a solid justification for the proposed approach.
*   The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating the method's effectiveness across multiple datasets and against appropriate baselines. The results clearly show practical benefits in performance and computational efficiency.
*   The discussion is well-written and anticipates potential critiques, which adds to the paper's overall quality and persuasiveness.

**Areas for Improvement and Questions for the Authors**
*   The comparison with the `AverageAtTheEnd` baseline (lines 341-349) could be more compelling. The reported performance difference does not seem substantial, and it is unclear why this specific result was omitted from the main visualizations in Figure 1. Could the authors elaborate on the advantage of their method in this scenario or provide a more detailed analysis? A stronger justification here would be beneficial.
*   In lines 318-320, the paper states that "the Radon machine outperforms the averaging baseline..." To clarify the experimental methodology, is this claim based on an average of paired comparisons across different datasets or cross-validation folds?
*   On line 324, the word "significant" is used to describe an improvement. To maintain scientific precision, this term is best reserved for claims supported by statistical significance testing. I would suggest using alternative phrasing, such as "substantial" or "notable," unless a formal statistical test was conducted.

**Minor Comments and Presentation Suggestions**
*   **Figures:** The readability of the figures could be improved. The text within Figures 1-3 is quite small and difficult to read; increasing the font size would be a significant help. Additionally, please consider that Figure 3 may be difficult to interpret if printed in black and white. Using distinct markers or line styles, in addition to color, would enhance its accessibility.
*   **Formatting:** The math font on line 79 appears smaller than in other parts of the text, which slightly hinders readability.
*   **Citations:** The citation style appears inconsistent. For example, the use of author names in "Long and servedio [22]" (line 377) differs from the citation style used elsewhere in the paper.
*   **Typos:** A few minor typos were noted:
    *   Line 51: "the the" should be "the".
    *   Lines 340-341: Missing spaces in "machineachieved" and "machineis".
