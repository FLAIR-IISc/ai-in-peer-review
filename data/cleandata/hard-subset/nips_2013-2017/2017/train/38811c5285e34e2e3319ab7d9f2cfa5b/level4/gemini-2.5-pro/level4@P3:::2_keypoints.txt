- Introduces the "Radon Machine": partition data, train base learners in parallel, aggregate hypotheses via Radon points hierarchically.  
- Theoretical claim: iterative Radon aggregation yields doubly-exponentially decreasing probability of a poor hypothesis (Theorem 3).  
- Asymptotic implication: can reduce runtimes from polynomial to polylogarithmic with a quasi-polynomial number of processors, relating to NC parallelizability.  
- Empirical results: 80x–700x speedups over sequential training while matching or exceeding predictive accuracy; outperforms averaging baseline and competes with Spark MLlib.  
- Strength: novel, geometry-based aggregation (Radon points) offers a principled alternative to averaging.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical guarantees (doubly-exponential confidence boost) stronger than typical averaging results.  
- Strength: addresses fundamental parallelizability questions linking ML to complexity theory (NC).  
- Strength: black-box method usable with off-the-shelf learners, improving practical applicability.  
- Strength: comprehensive experiments showing substantial speedups and validation of theoretical advantage over averaging.  
- Weakness: possible uncited overlap with "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (Boley et al., AISTATS 2017); originality needs clarification.  
- Weakness: scalability limited by feature dimension d because Radon number r = d+2 → required partitions/processors scale polynomially with d.  
- Weakness: gap between theory and practice—polylog runtime requires quasi-polynomial processors, but experiments use far fewer (150), so asymptotic claims and empirical validation are disconnected.  
- Weakness: theoretical assumptions require base learner failure probability δ ≤ 1/(2r) on small subsets, which may be unrealistic for hard problems or tiny partitions.  
- Questions: clarify differences from the AISTATS 2017 work; quantify practical limits of r = d+2 and compatibility with random projections; compare communication complexity to iterative distributed methods like Spark.
