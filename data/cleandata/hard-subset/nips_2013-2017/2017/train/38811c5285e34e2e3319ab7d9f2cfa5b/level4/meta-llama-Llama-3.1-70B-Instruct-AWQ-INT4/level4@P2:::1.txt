This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, which simplifies the adaptation of learning algorithms to growing amounts of data and accurate predictions in critical applications. The authors propose a Radon machine that effectively parallelises consistent and efficient learning algorithms, achieving a significant reduction in runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the Radon machine, including a bound on the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis and a proof of its consistency.

The authors also conduct an empirical evaluation of the Radon machine, comparing it to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms from the Spark machine learning library and the natural baseline of averaging hypotheses. The results indicate that the Radon machine achieves a substantial reduction in computation time while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a black-box parallelisation scheme that can be applied to a wide range of machine learning algorithms. The theoretical analysis of the Radon machine's complexity and its empirical evaluation demonstrate its potential for efficient parallelisation of machine learning algorithms.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel parallelisation scheme that can be applied to a broad class of learning algorithms.
* The theoretical analysis of the Radon machine provides a thorough understanding of its complexity and consistency.
* The empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the Radon machine in reducing computation time while maintaining predictive performance.

Weaknesses:

* The paper's focus on low-dimensional datasets may not be representative of modern machine learning problems, which often involve high-dimensional data.
* The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how their method can be applied to high-dimensional data.
