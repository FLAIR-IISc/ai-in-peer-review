This paper introduces a novel approach for parallelizing a class of machine learning algorithms by leveraging the concept of Radon points. The core contribution is the use of a Radon point-based combination scheme as an alternative to the more common practice of model averaging, which serves as a primary baseline in the experimental evaluation. The authors compare their method against three baselines: a non-parallelized implementation, a standard model averaging scheme, and a Spark-based implementation. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method can significantly reduce learning time while achieving accuracy that is comparable or superior to the baseline algorithms.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty:** The application of Radon points to the problem of parallelizing learners is an interesting and non-obvious idea. It provides a principled alternative to simple averaging.
2.  **Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and show promising results. The evidence suggests that the proposed method offers a practical speedup in training without sacrificing, and in some cases improving, model performance. The comparison against relevant baselines effectively highlights the benefits of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary concern with this submission lies in the novelty and context of its theoretical contribution. The theoretical foundation of the proposed method appears to be closely related to, or potentially derived from, the work presented in a paper titled "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization."

Crucially, this prior work is not cited in the current manuscript. This omission makes it very difficult for a reviewer to assess the unique theoretical contributions of the present paper. The authors need to urgently and clearly address the following:

1.  **Acknowledge Prior Work:** The authors must cite and discuss the relationship between their work and "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization."
2.  **Clarify Contribution:** If the theoretical framework has indeed been established previously, the authors must explicitly state this and reposition the paper's contribution. Is the contribution a novel application of the existing theory, an empirical validation on a new set of problems, or an extension of the prior theoretical work? Without this clarification, the paper's claims of theoretical novelty are unsubstantiated.

**Conclusion:**

While the proposed method and its empirical validation are promising, the potential overlap with uncited prior work on the theoretical front is a major issue that undermines the paper's contribution. I recommend that the authors thoroughly revise the manuscript to properly situate their work within the existing literature and clearly delineate their novel contributions. Addressing this point is critical for the paper to be considered for publication.
