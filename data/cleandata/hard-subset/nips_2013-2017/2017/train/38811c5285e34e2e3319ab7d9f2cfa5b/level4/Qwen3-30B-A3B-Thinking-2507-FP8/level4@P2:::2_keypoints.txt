- Introduces the Radon Machine: a parallelization scheme using Radon-point aggregation with doubly exponential confidence gains per aggregation level.
- Theoretical claim: reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic time while preserving guarantees, at the cost of increased sample complexity.
- Empirical claim: achieves 80–700× speedups on multiple datasets with comparable or better predictive performance than Spark and averaging baselines.
- Builds on parallel learning theory and connects to Nick’s Class (NC) and prior work on parallel randomized convex optimization.
- Strength — Quality: rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proofs and probability bounds for ε-bad hypotheses.
- Strength — Originality: novel use of Radon points instead of averaging and a black-box parallelization that needs no algorithm-specific derivations.
- Strength — Significance: addresses fundamental question of parallelizing polynomial-time learners to polylog time on many processors.
- Strength — Clarity: well-organized presentation, concrete Algorithm 1, and thorough empirical comparisons.
- Weakness — Citation gap: fails to cite the authors’ prior related work ("Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization"), obscuring novelty.
- Weakness — Practical implementation: scheme is labeled “black-box” but requires careful tuning of parameters (h, r) with little guidance provided.
- Weakness — Limitations: discussion of failures on high-dimensional data and non-linear models is weak and needs concrete examples and mitigations.
- For acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, substantial empirical speedups, broad applicability, and important NC context.
- Against acceptance: missing citation/clarification of prior work undermines novelty, and experiments are limited to moderately sized datasets.
- Required revisions: cite and compare to prior work, explicitly state advances over that work, and expand discussion of parameter selection and approach limitations.
