This paper proposes a black-box parallelization scheme—the Radon machine—for regularized risk minimization. The method trains base learners on many iid subsets and aggregates the resulting hypotheses via iterated Radon points along a tree, rather than simple averaging. The analysis shows that, under standard convexity and consistency assumptions with a finite Radon number, the approach reduces the probability of a bad hypothesis doubly exponentially with tree height, achieving the same (ε, Δ) guarantees in polylogarithmic time using quasi-polynomially many processors. Empirically, the method yields large speedups (often one to two orders of magnitude) with comparable predictive performance to strong baselines, and generally outperforms averaging-at-the-end in AUC. The paper situates the results in the context of Nick’s Class and prior averaging and distributed SGD approaches.

Quality: The theoretical results are well-motivated and clearly tied to the algorithmic design (Proposition 2, Theorem 3, Theorem 4). Assumptions are explicit (convex hypothesis space with finite Radon number; abundant data; finite-dimensional parameterization), and the cost trade-off—more data and processors for lower wall-clock time—is transparent. Experiments cover multiple datasets and baselines and support the claims about speedup and accuracy.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, with a clear algorithm description and a thoughtful discussion of limitations and applicability. Some presentation issues (figure readability, minor typos, citation consistency) should be addressed.

Originality: Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation to obtain double-exponential confidence amplification and polylogarithmic-time parallelization (under quasi-polynomial processors) is a compelling and, to my knowledge, novel angle relative to averaging-based schemes and distributed SGD.

Significance: The work contributes both theoretically—connecting effective parallelization to Radon numbers and NC-style efficiency—and practically, offering a plug-in method for a broad class of learners. If adopted, it could influence how practitioners parallelize convex learners when high confidence is required.

Pros
- Clear, general, and black-box parallelization scheme applicable to many convex learners.
- Strong theoretical guarantees, including doubly exponential reduction in error probability with depth and polylogarithmic runtime under stated resources.
- Solid empirical results showing substantial speedups and competitive or better AUCs against Spark baselines and averaging-at-the-end.
- Thoughtful discussion of scope, limitations, and potential extensions.

Cons
- Requires quasi-polynomially many processors for the strongest runtime guarantees; practicality depends on available resources.
- Aggregation relies on the Radon number tied to dimensionality; very high-dimensional settings may need additional preprocessing (e.g., random projections).
- Statistical significance of empirical improvements is not always reported; some claims use “significant” colloquially.
