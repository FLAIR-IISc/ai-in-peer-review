This paper introduces a framework for parallelizing machine learning by combining base learners more effectively than simple averaging. The key idea is to replace subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point, and to use this operation hierarchically to aggregate results. The authors provide complexity bounds for the approach and empirically compare it against parallel baselines implemented in Spark, as well as against ensembles of linear models in Weka.

The work is compelling as it offers a largely black-box method for aggregating weak learners, serving as an alternative to bagging. The theoretical contribution is substantial: the analysis of the Radon machine’s complexity shows that partitioning the training data enables a parallel scheme that is more efficient than a single base learner trained on the full dataset. The paper also presents a PAC-style generalization bound for the Radon machine. Overall, the theoretical development appears solid and well-motivated.

My primary concern is practical performance, especially in high-dimensional settings. It is unclear how the proposed method scales with the number of features or how the computation of Radon points behaves in high dimensions. The experimental evaluation seems to focus on low-dimensional datasets (around 18 features), which is not representative of many contemporary applications. This raises questions about the method’s robustness, runtime, and memory footprint when d is large, as well as its sensitivity to sparsity or feature redundancy.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
- Providing experiments on high-dimensional datasets and reporting both accuracy and wall-clock performance under varying numbers of features and partitions.
- Discussing the computational cost of computing Radon points as a function of dimensionality, and outlining strategies to handle high-d settings (e.g., exploiting sparsity, using approximate Radon points, or incorporating dimensionality reduction).
- Clarifying whether the method remains effective with stronger base learners and more modern parallel baselines.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound framework for parallel hypothesis aggregation via Radon points. With a clearer treatment of high-dimensional behavior and more representative experiments, it would make a stronger practical contribution.
