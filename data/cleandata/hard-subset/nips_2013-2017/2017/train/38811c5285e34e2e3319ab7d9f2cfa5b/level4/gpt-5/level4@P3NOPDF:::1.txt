Summary
This paper proposes a new framework for parallelizing machine learning algorithms by aggregating base learners using Radon points rather than simple averaging. The method repeatedly replaces subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point, yielding a black-box scheme for combining weak learners. The authors provide complexity analyses showing that, because the original training set can be partitioned, the resulting parallel method can be substantially more efficient than training a single base learner on all data. They also present a PAC-style generalization bound for the proposed “Radon machine.” Empirically, the approach is compared against parallel implementations in Spark and against linear models in Weka.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Using Radon points as an aggregation operator is an elegant alternative to averaging or bagging and is applicable as a black-box wrapper around a variety of base learners.
- Theoretical backing: The paper offers clear complexity results demonstrating potential computational advantages through data partitioning, as well as a PAC bound that helps justify the aggregation procedure.
- Practical relevance: A generic, parallelizable methodology for combining weak learners is timely and potentially valuable across many application domains.
- Empirical scope: Comparisons to established platforms (Spark) and standard baselines (linear models in Weka) help situate the method within current practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited evaluation on high-dimensional data: The experiments appear to target very low-dimensional datasets (on the order of ~18 features). This setting is atypical for many modern tasks, where hundreds to tens of thousands of features (or more) are common. It is thus unclear how the method scales and performs in higher-dimensional regimes.
- Practical feasibility in high dimensions: The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the computational overhead introduced by Radon point computations as dimensionality increases, and how this overhead compares to the savings from parallelization. Without this, it is hard to assess end-to-end efficiency on realistic workloads.
- Breadth of empirical study: While the platform comparisons are useful, the evaluation would be stronger with a wider range of base learners (including non-linear models), datasets with varying dimensionalities, and sensitivity analyses over key hyperparameters (e.g., subset sizes, depth of aggregation).

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- High-dimensional behavior: Please clarify how the complexity of computing and aggregating Radon points scales with feature dimension and the number of hypotheses per subset. Are there algorithmic or numerical challenges in high dimensions (e.g., stability, regularization) and, if so, how are they addressed?
- Empirical validation at scale: Consider adding experiments on higher-dimensional datasets (e.g., text, genomics, or vision features), including both dense and sparse representations. Reporting wall-clock times, memory usage, and accuracy/utility would help quantify the trade-offs.
- Base learner diversity: How sensitive is the method to the choice of base learner and its regularization? For instance, does the approach preserve or amplify the benefits of strong regularization in high dimensions?
- Communication and system costs: In distributed settings, what are the communication patterns and costs associated with the Radon aggregation steps? A comparison of these costs to those of common parameter-averaging schemes would be informative.
- Robustness and parameters: Please include ablations on (i) the number and size of partitions, (ii) the depth/recursion of Radon aggregation, and (iii) noise/outlier robustness relative to averaging or median-based methods.
- Bound interpretation: The PAC bound is a valuable theoretical result; it would help to contextualize it with practical guidance—e.g., which regime (sample size, dimension, partitioning strategy) most benefits from the bound, and how that aligns with your experimental settings.

Clarity and presentation
The paper is generally clear and well-motivated. The contribution of using Radon points as a principled aggregation operator is well articulated, and the theoretical results are presented as a coherent narrative. That said, the practical implications for high-dimensional settings need more emphasis and empirical substantiation.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and well-founded contribution that proposes a generic, theoretically grounded alternative to averaging for parallel learning. The theoretical results are solid and the idea is compelling. However, the current empirical evaluation appears restricted to low-dimensional datasets, making it difficult to judge the method’s practicality for modern, high-dimensional applications. Strengthening the experimental section with high-dimensional benchmarks, detailed runtime/memory analyses, and sensitivity studies would significantly improve the paper and its impact.
