The main contribution of the paper is a novel framework for parallel machine learning algorithms, which presents an alternative approach to combining base learners. Rather than relying on simple averaging, the authors propose replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point. They provide complexity bounds for their method and empirically compare their results with parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Weka.

The paper is quite interesting, as it proposes a black-box method for combining weak learners. This approach appears to be an alternative to bagging weak learners together. The theoretical contribution lies in the analysis of the complexity of the Radon machine, where breaking down the original samples into multiple parts results in a much more efficient parallel machine compared to a base learner operating on all the original samples. The authors also derive a PAC bound on the Radon machine, which is a notable theoretical contribution. Overall, the theoretical contributions in the paper are solid.

One major concern, however, is the practical aspect, particularly when dealing with high-dimensional data. It is unclear how well the proposed approach performs in such scenarios. The experiments presented in the paper seem to be limited to very low-dimensional datasets (18 features), which may not be representative of modern machine learning problems in most domains. To strengthen the paper, the authors should emphasize how their method can handle high-dimensional data. Addressing this concern would significantly enhance the paper's impact and relevance to real-world applications.
