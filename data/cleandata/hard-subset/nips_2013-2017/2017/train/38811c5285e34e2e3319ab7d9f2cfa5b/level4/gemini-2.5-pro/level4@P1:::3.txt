This paper introduces the "Radon machine," a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to train a base learner on numerous small, independent subsets of the data and then recursively aggregate the resulting hypotheses. Unlike common averaging-based approaches, this aggregation step uses the concept of a Radon point. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this aggregation method reduces the probability of generating a poor hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. This powerful result allows the framework to achieve polylogarithmic runtime on a quasi-polynomial number of processors, representing a significant theoretical step towards answering a fundamental open question about the limits of efficient parallel learning within the framework of Nick's Class (NC). The paper supports these theoretical claims with a comprehensive empirical evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Theoretical Soundness:** The primary strength of this work lies in its novel application of Radon points for model aggregation in a parallel learning context. This approach is well-motivated and supported by a clear theoretical analysis (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3), which establishes a strong guarantee on the confidence amplification. This theoretical contribution connecting regularized risk minimization to polylogarithmic time complexity is a significant advance.

2.  **Generality and Practicality:** A key advantage of the Radon machine is its "black-box" nature. It can be applied to any consistent and efficient learning algorithm that operates in a convex hypothesis space with a finite Radon number, without requiring algorithm-specific derivations or modifications to the base learner's implementation. This makes the method highly practical and broadly applicable.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method not only to the sequential base learner and a standard averaging baseline but also to highly optimized parallel algorithms from Spark's MLlib. Across multiple datasets, the Radon machine demonstrates substantial speedups (from 80x to over 700x) while maintaining comparable, and in some cases superior, predictive accuracy. These results effectively translate the theoretical promise into practical performance gains.

4.  **Thoughtful Discussion:** The authors proactively address several potential limitations and practical considerations in the Discussion section, including the challenges of high-dimensional data, non-linear models, and scenarios with a limited number of processors. This thoughtful analysis strengthens the paper by clearly defining the scope and applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is strong, and the following points are primarily suggestions for improving the final manuscript's clarity and presentation.

*   **Figure Legibility:** The text within Figures 1-3 is too small and difficult to read. Increasing the font size would significantly improve their legibility. Furthermore, Figure 3 relies on color to distinguish between methods, which may not be clear when printed in black and white. Using different markers or line styles in addition to color would make the plot more accessible.

*   **Clarification of Results:**
    *   The claim that "the Radon machine outperforms the averaging baseline..." (Lines 318-320) would be clearer if the basis for comparison was specified (e.g., an average across all datasets, a paired comparison, etc.).
    *   In Lines 341-349, the performance difference with the `AverageAtTheEnd` method on multi-class problems appears relatively small. To better advocate for the method here, it would be beneficial to integrate this result into a main figure (e.g., Figure 1) for a more direct visual comparison and to elaborate on why even a modest performance gain is meaningful in this context.
    *   The authors use the word "significant" in a colloquial sense. To strengthen the claims, this term should be reserved for results that have been validated with statistical tests.

*   **Minor Edits:** The manuscript would benefit from a final proofreading pass to correct minor typographical errors (e.g., "the the" on Line 51), missing spaces ("machineachieved" on Line 340), and stylistic inconsistencies (e.g., the citation format for "Long and Servedio [21]" on Line 377 differs from others).

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective method for parallelizing machine learning algorithms. The use of Radon points for aggregation is a creative and powerful idea, and the authors support it with rigorous theory and compelling empirical evidence. The work makes a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
