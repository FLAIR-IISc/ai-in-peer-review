

The paper introduces a novel approach for parallelizing learners operating under specific assumptions, leveraging Radon points instead of conventional averaging techniques. The primary contribution lies in this alternative parallelization strategy, which the authors position as a refinement over the averaging-based baseline commonly employed in the field. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves significantly reduced training times while maintaining comparable or superior accuracy relative to key baselines, including no parallelization, averaging, and Spark-based implementations. The empirical validation across multiple datasets provides convincing evidence of the approach’s practical utility, particularly in distributed settings where communication overhead is a bottleneck.

However, a critical issue requires clarification: the theoretical foundation of the work appears to closely overlap with the authors’ prior publication, "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (which, based on the title and context, likely predates this submission). This prior work addresses similar theoretical questions about parallelizing convex optimization under assumptions that align with those in the current paper. The absence of citation to this foundational work in the current manuscript is problematic. The authors must explicitly acknowledge this prior contribution and rigorously articulate how the present paper extends, refines, or contextualizes the theoretical framework. Without this, the paper risks presenting incremental work rather than a substantive advance. Specifically, the review should clarify: (1) whether the Radon point-based approach introduces new theoretical guarantees beyond the prior work, (2) how the assumptions or problem scope differ, and (3) why the theoretical analysis here is distinct from or complementary to the earlier results.

The experimental section, while well-executed, would benefit from deeper analysis of *why* Radon points outperform averaging in this context. For instance, the authors could briefly discuss how Radon points mitigate specific failure modes of averaging (e.g., in non-convex or heterogeneous data regimes) or provide intuition on the trade-offs between computational efficiency and statistical accuracy. Such insights would strengthen the paper’s narrative beyond mere empirical comparison.

The writing is generally clear, but minor grammatical improvements would enhance readability. For example, "the usage of Radon points instead of averaging" should be "the use of Radon points instead of averaging," and "reduced learning time" could be "reduced training time" for standard terminology. The comparison with Spark-based methods is relevant but lacks specificity; a brief mention of the Spark implementation details (e.g., data partitioning strategy, communication protocol) would help contextualize the results.

Overall, the paper presents a promising empirical contribution with clear practical benefits. The core idea—replacing averaging with Radon points for parallel learning—is novel and well-motivated by the experimental results. However, the theoretical novelty is currently obscured by the omission of key prior work. If the authors address the citation gap and explicitly delineate the theoretical advancement over their prior publication, the paper would make a strong contribution to the literature. As written, the lack of proper scholarly context undermines the perceived significance of the work. The revision should prioritize clarifying the relationship to "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" and justifying the theoretical progression. Without these clarifications, the paper risks being viewed as a technical extension rather than a meaningful step forward. Given the empirical strengths, a resubmission with these revisions would be highly recommended.
