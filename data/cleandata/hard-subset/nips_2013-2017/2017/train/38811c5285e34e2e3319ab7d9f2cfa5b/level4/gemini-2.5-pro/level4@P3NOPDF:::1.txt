This paper introduces a novel framework for parallelizing machine learning algorithms. The core idea is to combine base learners, trained on partitions of the data, more effectively than through simple averaging. Specifically, the proposed "Radon Machine" replaces subsets of hypotheses with their geometric median, the Radon point. The authors provide theoretical contributions in the form of complexity and PAC-learning bounds for their method, alongside empirical comparisons against parallel algorithms in Spark and baseline linear models in Weka.

The paper's primary strength lies in its interesting and well-founded theoretical proposal. It presents a general, black-box methodology for combining hypotheses, offering a compelling alternative to established ensemble methods like bagging. The theoretical analysis is a significant contribution. The authors derive complexity bounds that demonstrate the potential efficiency gains of the parallelized Radon Machine compared to a single base learner trained on the entire dataset. The inclusion of a PAC bound further solidifies the theoretical underpinnings of the approach.

Despite the strong theoretical foundation, a major concern is the practical applicability and evaluation of the method, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The empirical validation appears to be limited to datasets with very low dimensionality (e.g., a maximum of 18 features). This experimental setup does not reflect the scale and complexity of many modern machine learning problems, where the number of features can be in the thousands or millions. Consequently, it remains unclear how the proposed approach scales or performs as dimensionality increases. The computational geometry aspects of finding a Radon point could become challenging in high-dimensional spaces, and this is a critical point that is not sufficiently addressed.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically elegant and promising framework for parallel machine learning. However, its practical impact is currently diminished by the lack of evaluation on high-dimensional data. The paper would be significantly strengthened if the authors could provide either experimental results on high-dimensional datasets or a thorough theoretical discussion on how the Radon Machine is expected to behave and scale with dimensionality. Addressing this concern would greatly enhance the paper's contribution and relevance to the broader machine learning community.
