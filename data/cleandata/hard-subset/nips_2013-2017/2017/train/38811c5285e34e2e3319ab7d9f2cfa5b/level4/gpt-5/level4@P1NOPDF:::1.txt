The paper proposes a new framework for parallelizing machine learning algorithms by combining base learners more effectively than simple averaging. Specifically, it replaces subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point. The authors provide complexity bounds for the method and empirically compare it against parallel algorithms in Spark, as well as against base linear models implemented in Weka.

The approach is interesting because it offers a largely black-box way to aggregate weak learners and appears to be an alternative to bagging. On the theoretical side, the paper analyzes the complexity of the Radon machine and shows that, since the original dataset can be partitioned into multiple parts, the resulting parallel scheme is much more efficient than a base learner trained on the full dataset. The authors also derive a PAC bound for the Radon machine. Overall, the theoretical contributions are solid.

My main concern is the practical applicability, especially for high-dimensional data. It is unclear how well the proposed approach scales or performs in such settings. The experiments appear to use very low-dimensional datasets (around 18 features), which is not representative of many modern machine learning problems. If the authors could clarify how high-dimensional data are handled by their method—and, ideally, provide empirical evidence or a discussion of expected performance in such regimes—the paper would be significantly stronger.
