This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, the Radon machine, for accelerating regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm to random subsets of the data and iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, a technique that demonstrably increases confidence in the aggregate. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing potential for polylogarithmic runtime on a quasi-polynomial number of processors, along with empirical validation demonstrating significant speedups and comparable performance to state-of-the-art methods. This work builds on previous research in parallel learning [5, 32, 42] and addresses limitations of existing approaches like averaging [20, 35, 45] and distributed stochastic gradient descent [34]. The clear presentation, strong theoretical grounding, and compelling empirical results make this a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Radon points for parallelizing machine learning algorithms is a novel and promising approach.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating the potential for significant speedups and bounding the cost in terms of data and processing units. The connection to Nick’s Class [9] is particularly insightful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results on multiple datasets demonstrate substantial speedups compared to both baseline algorithms and established Spark MLlib implementations, while maintaining comparable or better predictive performance.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors proactively address potential limitations and concerns, such as the handling of high-dimensional data and varying numbers of processors, and suggest solutions.
* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the complex ideas accessible.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Figure Readability:** The font size in Figures 1-3 is too small, hindering clear interpretation.
* **Statistical Rigor in Comparisons:** The claim of outperforming the averaging baseline (line 318) would be strengthened by explicitly stating if this is based on paired statistical testing.
* **Terminology:** The use of "significant" without referencing statistical significance (line 324) is potentially misleading.
* **Minor Typos/Formatting:** A few minor typos and formatting inconsistencies are present (lines 51, 79, 318-320, 340-341).
* **Limited Discussion of Communication Costs:** While mentioned briefly, a more detailed discussion of the communication complexity of the scheme could be beneficial.



**Detailed Comments:**

* **Line 51:** Correct "the the" to "the".
* **Line 79:** The mathematical font used here seems smaller than elsewhere in the paper and is difficult to read. Consider using a consistent and larger font size for equations.
* **Figures 1-3:** Please increase the font size within the figure images to improve readability, particularly for presentation or printed versions of the paper.
* **Figure 3:** The color scheme used in this figure might be problematic for black and white printing. Consider using distinguishable patterns or labels.
* **Lines 318-320:**  To clarify, is the statement about outperforming the averaging baseline based on a statistically significant difference (e.g., a paired t-test)?  Explicitly stating this would strengthen the claim.
* **Line 324:** Consider rephrasing “significant” to avoid ambiguity. Perhaps “notable” or “substantial” would be more appropriate in this context, as it doesn’t relate to a p-value.
* **Lines 340-341:** Correct “machineachieved” to “machine achieved” and “machineis” to “machine is”.
* **Lines 341-349:**  The difference in performance between the Radon machine and the Averaging-at-the-End method, while consistent, appears modest. I recommend elaborating on why the Radon machine’s approach is advantageous, even with a relatively small performance gain, and ideally including a visual comparison of these two methods in Figure 1.  A more compelling justification would strengthen the paper.
* **Line 377:**  The citation style for "Long and servedio" is inconsistent with the rest of the paper. Ensure consistent author names and citation format.
* **Discussion (Section 5):** The discussion of communication complexity is brief. While acknowledged, a more in-depth analysis would be valuable, particularly considering the practical implications of distributed learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in machine learning – scaling algorithms to large datasets – with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the Radon machine in practice, showing substantial speedups without compromising performance.
* The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of related work and clearly position their contribution within the existing literature.
* The paper is well-written and organized, making the complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
* The proactive discussion of limitations and future work demonstrates a thoughtful and comprehensive approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* While the figures are informative, their readability is hampered by the small font size. This detracts from the overall presentation.
* The lack of explicit statistical testing for some performance comparisons (e.g., with the averaging baseline) weakens the strength of certain claims.
* The minor typos and formatting inconsistencies, while not major, detract from the overall polish of the paper.



**Overall:**

Despite a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the drawbacks. The Radon machine represents a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning, and I strongly recommend its acceptance to NIPS 2017. Addressing the suggested improvements regarding figure readability and statistical rigor would further enhance the quality of the paper.
