

# Review for "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper presents the Radon Machine, a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime while maintaining theoretical guarantees on error bounds. The authors address a fundamental open question in parallel computing: whether consistent learning algorithms can be parallelized efficiently in the sense of Nick's Class (NC), i.e., with polylogarithmic runtime on polynomially many processors. The key insight is using Radon points for aggregation instead of averaging, which provides doubly exponential improvement in confidence with respect to the height of the aggregation tree.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by proving that consistent and efficient learning algorithms (e.g., SVMs, regularized least squares, logistic regression) can be parallelized to achieve polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processing units. The authors establish a clear connection between their approach and Nick's Class, demonstrating that their scheme represents a step toward answering the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be solved in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors. This connects to Long and Servedio's (2014) work on learning half-spaces, which they cite as a "fundamental open problem" in parallel learning.

The Radon Machine (Algorithm 1) executes the base learning algorithm on random subsets of data, then iteratively aggregates the resulting hypotheses using Radon points. The authors prove that the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis decreases doubly exponentially with the height parameter h (Theorem 3), providing a strong theoretical foundation for their approach. Empirically, the method achieves 80-700x speedup over sequential execution while maintaining comparable or better predictive performance (measured by AUC) across multiple datasets.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear proofs of the key propositions and theorems. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against both baseline approaches (averaging-at-the-end) and state-of-the-art Spark implementations. The authors effectively address potential concerns about applicability to high-dimensional data and non-linear models in the Discussion section. The black-box nature of the parallelization scheme is particularly valuable, as it can be applied to a broad class of learning algorithms without requiring mathematical derivations or code modifications.

The paper excels in addressing the four key review criteria:

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound, with well-supported claims about sample complexity, runtime complexity, and confidence guarantees. The empirical results corroborate the theoretical findings across multiple datasets and algorithms. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the requirement for sufficient processing units and the potential need for dimensionality reduction for high-dimensional data.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and generally clearly written. The mathematical notation is precise, though the manuscript would benefit from improved figure formatting (smaller text in figures, better distinction in black-and-white printing). The algorithm description is particularly clear.

**Originality:** The use of Radon points for aggregation represents a novel approach to parallel learning. While averaging-based parallelization has been studied extensively (e.g., [32, 45, 46]), the Radon point approach provides fundamentally different theoretical guarantees. The connection to Nick's Class and the open question of efficient parallel learning for regularized risk minimization is particularly significant.

**Significance:** The 80-700x speedup demonstrated in practice is highly significant for real-world applications where training time is a bottleneck. The method's applicability to a broad class of learning algorithms without requiring special implementation makes it valuable for practitioners. The theoretical contribution advances our understanding of the fundamental limits of parallel learning.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed in revision. The figures (particularly Figures 1-3) would benefit from larger text and better differentiation for black-and-white printing. The comparison with the averaging-at-the-end baseline (which is a natural baseline for the method) could be more compellingly presented - the paper notes a 10% AUC improvement but doesn't clearly show this in the main figures. Additionally, the manuscript contains a few minor formatting issues (e.g., "machineachieved" on line 340, inconsistent citation formatting on line 377).

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental theoretical question in machine learning with clear practical implications. The connection to Nick's Class provides a nice theoretical framework for understanding the parallelization limits of learning algorithms. The empirical results demonstrate meaningful improvements in practice, and the black-box nature of the approach makes it widely applicable.

In conclusion, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of parallel machine learning with significant theoretical and practical implications. The Radon Machine provides a novel and effective approach to parallelizing learning algorithms while maintaining theoretical guarantees, and the empirical results confirm its practical utility. The paper is well-written, rigorously argued, and makes an important step toward answering a fundamental open question in parallel learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept
