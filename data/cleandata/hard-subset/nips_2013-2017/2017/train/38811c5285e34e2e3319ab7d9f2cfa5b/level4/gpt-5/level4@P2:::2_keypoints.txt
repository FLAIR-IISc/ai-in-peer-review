- Proposes the "Radon machine": black-box parallelization that trains base learners on random subsets and aggregates via an r-ary tree using Radon points (r = d+2).

- Theoretically shows doubly exponential reduction in ε-bad hypothesis probability with tree depth h, giving sequential (ε, Δ) guarantees in polylogarithmic time at cost of larger samples and quasi-polynomial processors.

- Aggregation per node computed via a small linear system; overall parallel aggregation overhead is O(hr^3).

- Empirically reports large speedups (up to ~700x with 150 processors) and competitive or better accuracy/runtime vs Spark MLlib; outperforms end-of-process averaging.

- Contrasts with averaging-based and distributed SGD/ADMM schemes; frames results in NC-style parallel complexity and PAC parallel learning.

- Fails to cite closely related prior work “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization”; needs to delineate novel contributions relative to that line.

- Strength: conceptual novelty—iterated Radon points yield strong confidence amplification and a clean compositional black-box scheme for convex learners.

- Strength: clear theoretical guarantees transferring base-learner performance to the parallel scheme and transparent aggregation cost.

- Strength: explicit processor/sample trade-off discussion and connection to parallel complexity theory.

- Strength: practical benefits—low communication, simple implementation, provided code, and strong empirical speedups.

- Weakness: relies on quasi-polynomial processor counts; main runtime claims depend on strong parallel resources and need experiments for c < r^h.

- Weakness: dimensionality dependence (r = d+2) makes aggregation O(r^3) costly and base-learner δ requirements stringent in high dimensions.

- Weakness: method inflates required sample size; paper should quantify sample-size multipliers, achieved Δ, and comparisons at fixed data budgets.

- Weakness: baseline comparisons mix WEKA/scikit-learn with Spark MLlib without clear tuning/optimization parity; wikidata results are weak and underanalyzed.

- Weakness: some key assumptions/constants (e.g., form of N0(ε,δ), bounds used in Theorem 4) are only sketched; need explicit instantiations for common learners in main text.

- Quality: theoretical development is sound assuming aggregation bounds hold; polylogarithmic runtime claim is plausible but contingent on processor and dimensionality limits.

- Empirical quality: experiments are thorough for linear models and show compelling speedups, but reproducibility would benefit from more protocol/resource detail.

- Clarity: paper is generally well organized and Algorithm 1 is clear.

- Clarity suggestions: summarize Radon-point computation and numerical stability in main text; add a table mapping base learners to sample-complexity and runtime terms; state data-splitting/independence assumptions explicitly.

- Originality: idea is interesting and yields new learning guarantees, but overlap with prior Radon-based parallel optimization work must be acknowledged and novel aspects emphasized.

- Significance: offers a useful black-box parallelization perspective with theoretical and practical benefits, though NC-level claims require strong parallelism.

- Pros for acceptance: strong theoretical guarantees, significant empirical speedups, low communication, simple implementation, and clear advantages over averaging baselines.

- Cons against acceptance: insufficient discussion of prior related work, practical constraints (high-dimensions, limited processors) not fully explored, and limited evidence for non-linear/high-dimensional settings.

- Requested changes: cite/compare to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization”; add experiments/analysis for c < r^h and sample-size/Δ trade-offs; include high-dimensional or embedding experiments or stronger justification; clarify experimental settings and baseline configurations.
