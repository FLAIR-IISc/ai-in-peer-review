The authors propose a method for parallelizing learners—under certain assumptions—based on Radon points. The paper’s main contribution, compared to related work, lies in the use of Radon points rather than averaging, which is used as a baseline approach in the experiments. Performance is evaluated against “no parallelization,” an averaging scheme, and Spark-based algorithms. The experimental results demonstrate reduced learning time and comparable or improved accuracy.

The theoretical foundations of this approach appear to have been previously investigated in the paper “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization,” potentially by the same authors. However, this prior work is not cited, and the paper needs to clearly articulate its specific contribution with respect to this existing theory. It’s unclear whether this work extends the previous results, offers a different perspective, or provides a novel application.

The experimental setup is reasonably well-defined, and the results are presented clearly. The comparison to Spark is particularly valuable, as it benchmarks the proposed method against a widely-used parallel computing framework. The reported speedups are encouraging, and the maintenance of accuracy while achieving these speedups is a strong point.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the assumptions underlying the Radon point-based parallelization. Specifically, the authors should clarify how sensitive the performance is to violations of these assumptions in real-world scenarios. A brief analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, compared to the baselines, would also be helpful. 

Furthermore, while the results show performance gains on the tested datasets, the paper lacks an analysis of the scalability of the approach. How does performance degrade as the number of learners and the size of the dataset increase? Addressing this point would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

Finally, the writing quality could be improved in several places. For example, phrases like "w.r.t." should be avoided in favor of more common phrasing ("compared to"). A more thorough proofread would also help to eliminate minor grammatical errors and improve overall clarity.

Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to parallelizing learners. Addressing the points above – particularly clarifying the theoretical contribution and analyzing scalability – would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
