The main contribution of this paper is a new framework for parallel machine learning algorithms. The idea is to combine weak learners more effectively than simply averaging them. Specifically, the subset of hypotheses is replaced by their Radon point. They also prove the complexity limits for their method and empirically compare their results using parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Veka.

This paper is quite interesting because it essentially proposes a black-box method for combining weak learners. This approach seems like an alternative to bagging weak learners. The theoretical contribution is an analysis of the complexity of the Radon machine, where, since the original samples can be broken into several parts, the resulting parallel machine is much more efficient than a base learner working on all the original samples. They also show a PAC bound on the Radon machine. The theoretical contributions seem quite good in the paper. A major concern was the practical aspect, especially for high-dimensional data. It is not clear how well the proposed approach performs here. The experiments seem to be for very low-dimensional datasets (18 features?), which is probably not realistic for most modern machine learning problems. The paper would be even stronger if the authors could emphasize how their method can handle high dimensions.
