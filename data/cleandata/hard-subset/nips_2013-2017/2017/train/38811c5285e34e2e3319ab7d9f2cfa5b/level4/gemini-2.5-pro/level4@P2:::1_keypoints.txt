- Proposes the "Radon Machine": parallelize learning by aggregating base hypotheses via Radon points in a tree instead of simple averaging.  
- Main theoretical result: error probability falls doubly-exponentially with aggregation depth, enabling polylogarithmic runtime given quasi-polynomial processors and connecting to NC.  
- Black-box method: can wrap existing sequential learners without modifying their internals.  
- Empirical results show large speedups (80x–700x) over sequential learners and competitive performance versus Spark MLlib.  
- Key weakness: scalability depends on Radon number r = d+2, so required base learners/processors scale exponentially with tree height and polynomially with dimension d.  
- Experiments use only low-dimensional datasets, avoiding the high-dimensional scalability problem.  
- Proposed mitigations (random projections, block coordinate descent) are speculative and not empirically validated.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized; algorithm and theoretical statements are precise.  
- Theoretical contributions are strong and technically sound; empirical scope limits practical claims.  
- Would benefit from a clearer comparison to bagging to explain theoretical advantages of Radon aggregation.  
- Practicality concern: headline results assume a quasi-polynomial number of processors; costs of using a fixed processor count need clearer emphasis.  
- Conclusion: significant theoretical novelty but practical utility is uncertain without experiments or validation on higher-dimensional settings.
