- Main contribution: novel framework (Radon machine) for parallel ML that combines subsets of hypotheses by replacing them with their Radon point instead of simple averaging.  
- Theoretical analysis: thorough complexity analysis showing breaking samples into parts yields more efficient parallel learning than running the base learner on all samples.  
- PAC guarantee: authors derive a Probably Approximately Correct bound for the Radon machine, strengthening the theoretical contribution.  
- Empirical evaluation: comparisons against parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Weka demonstrate efficiency on tested tasks.  
- Novelty: presents a black-box method to combine weak learners, offering an alternative to bagging.  
- Major practical concern: unclear scalability and performance on high-dimensional data; experiments are limited to very low-dimensional datasets (≈18 features).  
- Suggested remedies: authors should discuss/experiment with strategies for high-dimensional settings (e.g., random projections, low-rank approximations).  
- Clarity issues: proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 need clearer explanations or examples to aid understanding.  
- Conference criteria: originality and paper quality are strong, but significance is limited by unresolved practical concerns.  
- Overall: promising and potentially significant contribution contingent on addressing high-dimensional performance and clarifying theoretical proofs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, solid complexity analysis, empirical comparisons, and a PAC bound.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited low-dimensional experiments, unclear/high-dimensional performance, and potential limitations not fully addressed.
