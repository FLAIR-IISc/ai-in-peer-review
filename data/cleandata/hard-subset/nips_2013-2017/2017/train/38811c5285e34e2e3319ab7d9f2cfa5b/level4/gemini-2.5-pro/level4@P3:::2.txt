### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of learning algorithms that fall under the regularized risk minimization framework. The core idea is to partition the dataset, train a base learning algorithm on each subset in parallel to obtain initial hypotheses, and then aggregate these hypotheses hierarchically. The key innovation lies in the aggregation step, which uses the geometric concept of a Radon point instead of simple averaging.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this iterative Radon point aggregation reduces the probability of generating a poor hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the number of aggregation levels (Theorem 3). Leveraging this strong confidence-boosting property, the authors argue that their scheme can reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic, provided a quasi-polynomial number of processors is available. This result constitutes a significant step towards addressing the open question of whether machine learning problems can be efficiently parallelized in the sense of Nick's Class (NC).

Empirically, the Radon Machine is compared against sequential base learners, an averaging-based parallelization baseline, and state-of-the-art parallel algorithms from Spark's MLlib. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method achieves substantial speedups (80x to 700x) over sequential training while maintaining comparable or superior predictive accuracy, particularly outperforming the averaging baseline.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Theoretically Sound Aggregation:** The use of Radon points for aggregating hypotheses is a creative and elegant idea. It is well-grounded in convex geometry and provides a principled alternative to heuristic or averaging-based approaches.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, culminating in Theorem 3, which establishes a doubly-exponential improvement in the confidence bound. This is a powerful result and a significant theoretical advantage over methods like parameter averaging, whose guarantees are typically much weaker.

3.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The work makes a valuable contribution by connecting a practical parallelization method to the fundamental complexity theory of NC classes. By showing a path to polylogarithmic runtime, it advances the community's understanding of the inherent parallelizability of machine learning.

4.  **Black-Box and Practical:** A major strength of the proposed scheme is its "black-box" nature. It can be applied to a wide range of existing, off-the-shelf learning algorithms without requiring modifications to their internal implementation. This greatly enhances its practical utility and potential for adoption.

5.  **Convincing Empirical Evidence:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and compelling. The reported speedups are substantial, and the method demonstrates competitive or superior performance against highly optimized industrial-strength libraries like Spark MLlib. The clear outperformance of the averaging baseline validates the theoretical benefits of using Radon points.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Clarity on Originality and Related Work:** There appears to be a highly relevant prior work titled "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (Boley et al., AISTATS 2017), which seems to involve the same authors and explore a very similar theoretical foundation. This work is not cited in the current manuscript. It is crucial for the authors to cite this paper and clearly delineate the novel contributions of the current submission. Without this clarification, the originality of the core theoretical results is difficult to assess.

2.  **Scalability in High Dimensions:** The practicality of the scheme is limited by its dependence on the feature dimension `d`. The Radon number for `R^d` is `r = d+2`, and the number of required data partitions and processing units (`r^h`) scales polynomially with `d`. This "curse of dimensionality" could make the method prohibitively expensive in terms of data and computation for high-dimensional problems common in fields like natural language processing or bioinformatics. While the paper mentions dimensionality reduction as a potential solution, this is a significant limitation that deserves a more thorough discussion.

3.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The headline theoretical result of polylogarithmic runtime is conditioned on the availability of a quasi-polynomial number of processors. The experiments, however, are conducted with a fixed and much smaller number of processors (150). While the practical speedups are impressive, there is a disconnect between the asymptotic theoretical claims and the empirical validation. It would strengthen the paper to more explicitly bridge this gap, for instance, by analyzing the performance under a fixed processor budget.

4.  **Assumptions on Base Learner Quality:** The theoretical analysis relies on the assumption that the base learner, when trained on a small data subset, can achieve a failure probability `δ ≤ 1/(2r)`. This may be a strong requirement in practice, especially for complex problems or when the data partitions are very small. If the base hypotheses are too weak, the conditions for the theorem may not be met.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   Please clarify the relationship between this paper and "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (AISTATS 2017). What are the specific novel contributions of this NIPS submission?
*   Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the `r = d+2` dependency? At what dimensionality `d` do you foresee the method becoming impractical, and are techniques like random projections theoretically compatible with your framework?
*   The comparison with Spark MLlib is very valuable. However, the Spark algorithms are often iterative and communicate gradients or other updates periodically. Your method, like other "averaging-at-the-end" approaches, communicates only once. Could you comment on the communication complexity and how it compares to these other methods? This would be a useful dimension for comparison.

In summary, this paper presents a highly interesting and novel approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms with strong theoretical backing and impressive empirical results. The primary concern is the potential overlap with uncited prior work, which must be addressed. If the originality is established, the paper would be a strong contribution to the conference.
