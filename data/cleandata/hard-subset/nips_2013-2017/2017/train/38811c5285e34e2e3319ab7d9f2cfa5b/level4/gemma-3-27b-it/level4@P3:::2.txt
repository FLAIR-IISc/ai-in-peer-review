This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms based on the concept of Radon points. The core idea is to apply a base learning algorithm in parallel to random subsets of the data and then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, offering a potentially significant speedup while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. This work builds upon existing approaches to parallel learning, particularly those based on averaging, but distinguishes itself by leveraging Radon points to achieve a doubly-exponential increase in confidence during aggregation – a key theoretical advantage. The authors demonstrate empirical improvements in runtime compared to both sequential learning, averaging-based parallelization, and implementations within the Spark machine learning library, while maintaining competitive accuracy.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the crucial need for efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms as datasets grow. The problem statement is clearly articulated, and the connection to the broader theoretical question of efficient parallelization (Nick’s Class – NC) is thoughtfully discussed. The authors appropriately frame their work within the regularized risk minimization setting, providing a solid theoretical foundation. The use of Radon points is a clever idea, and the theoretical analysis appears sound, suggesting a potential for polylogarithmic runtime scaling with a quasi-polynomial number of processors. The empirical evaluation, while limited in scope by the computational expense and scalability limitations of the base learners, provides compelling evidence of the method’s practical benefits. The comparison against Spark’s MLlib is particularly valuable, demonstrating competitive performance in terms of both runtime and predictive accuracy.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the authors mention related work on parallel learning and parallel computing, a more thorough discussion of existing approaches to parallelizing *convex optimization* problems would be beneficial. Specifically, the relationship to techniques like ADMM (Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers) or distributed stochastic gradient descent should be clarified. The statement about the theoretical part being potentially investigated in "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" is concerning. If this is the case, the authors *must* thoroughly cite and differentiate their work from that prior publication. What novel theoretical contributions are presented here, beyond the application of Radon points to this specific context? The paper needs to explicitly highlight what is new and different about this approach compared to that previous work.

Furthermore, the discussion of the limitations in high-dimensional settings feels somewhat hand-wavy. While acknowledging the need for dimensionality reduction techniques, a more detailed explanation of how these techniques would integrate with the proposed scheme would be helpful. The treatment of communication complexity is also brief. While the authors claim low communication cost, quantifying this cost – even approximately – would strengthen the analysis. The empirical evaluation could also be expanded. While the authors acknowledge the scalability limitations of the base learners, exploring the method’s performance on a wider range of datasets and with different base learners would bolster the claims of general applicability.  A more in-depth ablation study investigating the impact of the parameter ‘h’ (the aggregation tree height) on performance and runtime would also be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Radon points for parallelizing learning algorithms is a novel approach with a strong theoretical grounding.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis demonstrating the potential for significant speedup and guarantees on sample complexity.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results show promising performance improvements compared to existing methods.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a critical challenge in modern machine learning: scaling algorithms to large datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Clear Differentiation from Prior Work:** The potential overlap with "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" requires further clarification and a clear articulation of the paper's unique contributions.
*   **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation is limited by the scalability of the base learners and could be broadened.
*   **Insufficient Discussion of Communication Complexity:** The communication cost is acknowledged but not adequately quantified.
*   **Limited Discussion of High-Dimensional Data:** The treatment of high-dimensional data is somewhat superficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field, with a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms. However, the potential overlap with prior work and the limited scope of the empirical evaluation raise some concerns.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, the authors *must* address the relationship to "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" by either properly citing and differentiating their work, or demonstrating why that prior work is not directly applicable.  Additionally, expanding the empirical evaluation and providing a more detailed discussion of communication complexity and high-dimensional data would significantly strengthen the paper.
