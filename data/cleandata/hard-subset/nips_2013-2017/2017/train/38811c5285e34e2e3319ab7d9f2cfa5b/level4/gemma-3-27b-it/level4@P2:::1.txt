This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms, termed the “Radon machine,” which aims to improve efficiency by strategically aggregating hypotheses generated from random subsets of the data. Instead of simple averaging, the method utilizes Radon points to combine these hypotheses, offering theoretical guarantees on runtime reduction to polylogarithmic time with a quasi-polynomial number of processors, at the cost of increased sample complexity. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation, comparing it to implementations in Spark and baseline learners in Weka.

The core idea of leveraging Radon points for hypothesis aggregation is a valuable contribution, offering a potentially powerful alternative to existing parallelization techniques like averaging. The paper’s strength lies in its theoretical foundation, providing a formal analysis of the Radon machine’s complexity and establishing a PAC-style bound. This rigorously demonstrates the benefits of the proposed approach. The authors successfully position their work within the context of parallel computation and Nick’s Class (NC), highlighting its potential to address fundamental questions regarding the efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms. The empirical results further support the theoretical claims, showing significant speed-ups compared to sequential execution and competitive performance against existing parallel frameworks like Spark’s MLlib.

However, a primary weakness lies in the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. The datasets used appear to be relatively low-dimensional (e.g., 18 features in some cases), which may not accurately reflect the challenges of modern machine learning applications dealing with high-dimensional data. While the paper acknowledges this limitation and suggests potential solutions like random projections and low-rank approximations, the absence of experimental results demonstrating the method’s scalability to high-dimensional settings weakens the practical impact of the work. Further investigation into the performance with more complex, high-dimensional datasets is crucial. Additionally, the discussion of communication complexity could be expanded, as this is a significant factor in distributed learning systems. Although touched upon, a more detailed analysis would be beneficial. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The Radon machine presents a novel approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms, offering a distinct alternative to averaging-based methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a strong theoretical analysis, establishing complexity bounds and PAC guarantees.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate significant speed-ups and competitive performance against existing frameworks.
*   **Potential Impact:** The work addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning – efficient parallelization – and could have broad implications for scaling algorithms to large datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scalability Demonstration:** The empirical evaluation primarily focuses on low-dimensional datasets, raising concerns about scalability to real-world, high-dimensional problems.
*   **Lack of Detailed Communication Analysis:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the communication overhead associated with the Radon machine.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The theoretical analysis is strong, and the empirical results are promising. However, the limited scope of the experimental evaluation and the lack of a detailed communication complexity analysis raise some concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting the authors to:

1.  **Expand the empirical evaluation** to include high-dimensional datasets and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed techniques for handling such data (e.g., through random projections or low-rank approximations).
2.  **Provide a more in-depth discussion** of the communication complexity of the Radon machine and potential strategies for mitigating it.
3. **Clarify the relation to existing work on averaging techniques**, and better position the contribution in terms of the tradeoffs between computation time, communication cost, and statistical accuracy.
