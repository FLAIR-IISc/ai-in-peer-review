This paper introduces the "Radon machine," a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of learning algorithms within the regularized risk minimization framework. The core idea involves a divide-and-conquer strategy: a base learning algorithm is first trained in parallel on multiple disjoint subsets of the data to produce a set of initial hypotheses. These hypotheses are then iteratively aggregated in a tree-like structure. The key innovation lies in the aggregation step, which, unlike common approaches such as averaging, computes the Radon point of small groups of hypotheses. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this aggregation strategy leads to a doubly-exponential reduction in the probability of generating an ε-bad hypothesis with the height of the aggregation tree. This theoretical result is leveraged to argue that the scheme can reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms to be polylogarithmic on a quasi-polynomial number of processors, thus contributing to the open question regarding the efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms in the sense of Nick's Class (NC).

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The use of Radon points for aggregating learned hypotheses is a creative and well-motivated departure from the more conventional averaging-based techniques prevalent in parallel learning literature. The theoretical analysis demonstrating a doubly-exponential confidence amplification is a significant result and provides a strong foundation for the proposed method. The paper's connection to the fundamental open problem of parallel learning in NC further underscores its theoretical importance.
*   **Practical Design:** A major strength of the proposed scheme is its "black-box" nature. It can be applied to a wide range of existing, consistent learning algorithms without requiring internal modifications or specialized code, which greatly enhances its practical utility and potential for adoption.
*   **Technical Quality and Empirical Validation:** The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical derivations. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the Radon machine against strong baselines: the sequential base learner, an averaging-at-the-end approach, and state-of-the-art distributed algorithms in Spark's MLlib. The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating substantial speed-ups (up to 700x) while achieving comparable or superior predictive performance.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

*   **Clarity on Novel Contribution:** A primary concern is the potential overlap with prior work, which is not currently discussed. The theoretical framework appears to be very similar to that presented in "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (Boley et al., AISTATS 2017), which seems to involve the same authors. The current submission does not cite this paper or explain how its own contributions are distinct. For a proper assessment of originality, the authors should explicitly address the relationship to this prior work and clearly delineate the novel theoretical results or extensions presented here.
*   **Limitations in High-Dimensional Scenarios:** The scheme's data and processor requirements scale with the Radon number of the hypothesis space, which is `r = d+2` for a `d`-dimensional Euclidean space. This dependency on the feature dimension `d` could pose a significant practical challenge in high-dimensional settings where `d` can be very large. While the authors briefly mention dimensionality reduction techniques as a potential solution, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of these limitations and the practical trade-offs involved when applying the Radon machine to high-dimensional data.
