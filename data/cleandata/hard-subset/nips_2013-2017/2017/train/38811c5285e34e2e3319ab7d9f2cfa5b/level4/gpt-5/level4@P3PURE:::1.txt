This paper introduces a new framework for parallel machine learning that combines base learners more effectively than simple averaging by replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point. The authors provide complexity bounds for the method and empirically compare it against parallel algorithms in Spark and against base linear models in Weka.

The approach is interesting in that it functions as a largely black-box method for aggregating weak learners and serves as an alternative to bagging. On the theoretical side, the paper analyzes the complexity of the Radon machine, showing that because the original sample can be partitioned into multiple parts, the resulting parallel algorithm is substantially more efficient than a base learner trained on all the data. The paper also presents a PAC bound for the Radon machine. Overall, the theoretical contributions are strong.

My main concern is the practical performance, especially in high-dimensional settings. It is unclear how well the proposed approach scales or performs when the feature dimension is large. The experiments appear to focus on very low-dimensional datasets (around 18 features?), which is unlikely to reflect many modern machine learning applications. Clarifying how the method handles high-dimensional data—ideally with empirical evidence or a concrete discussion of computational and statistical behavior—would significantly strengthen the paper.
