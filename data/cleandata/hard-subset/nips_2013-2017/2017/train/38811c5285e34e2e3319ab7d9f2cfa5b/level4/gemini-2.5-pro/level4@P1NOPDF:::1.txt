This paper introduces a new framework for parallelizing machine learning algorithms. The core idea is to combine base learners more effectively than simple averaging; specifically, the proposed method replaces subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point. The authors provide theoretical complexity bounds for their method and empirically compare its performance against parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Weka.

The paper presents an interesting approach, offering what is essentially a black-box method for combining weak learners. The approach appears to be a novel alternative to standard ensemble methods like bagging. A key theoretical contribution is the complexity analysis of the proposed 'Radon machine'. This analysis demonstrates that by partitioning the original data, the resulting parallel machine can be significantly more efficient than a single base learner trained on the entire dataset. The authors also derive a PAC bound for the Radon machine. Overall, the theoretical contributions appear to be a significant strength of the paper.

A major concern, however, is the practical applicability of the method, particularly for high-dimensional data. It is not clear how well the proposed approach would perform in such settings. The experiments are conducted on what appear to be low-dimensional datasets (e.g., one with only 18 features), which may not be representative of the challenges posed by high-dimensional problems common in many modern machine learning domains. The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could elaborate on how their method handles, or could be adapted for, high-dimensional data.
