The main contribution of the paper is a novel framework for parallel machine learning algorithms. The authors propose a method to combine base learners more effectively than simply averaging, by replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point. They provide a thorough analysis of the complexity of their approach, known as the Radon machine, and demonstrate its efficiency through empirical comparisons with parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Weka.

The paper is interesting, as it presents a black-box method to combine weak learners together, offering an alternative to bagging weak learners. The theoretical contribution is the analysis of the complexity of the Radon machine, where breaking down the original samples into multiple parts results in a much more efficient parallel machine compared to the base learner operating on all original samples. The authors also derive a Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) bound on the Radon machine, further solidifying their theoretical contribution.

However, a major concern is the practical aspect, particularly for high-dimensional data. It is unclear how well the proposed approach performs in such scenarios. The experiments seem to be limited to very low-dimensional datasets (18 features), which may not be representative of modern machine learning problems in most domains. To strengthen the paper, the authors should emphasize how their method can handle high-dimensional data. This could involve discussing potential strategies, such as random projections or low-rank approximations, to alleviate the limitations of their approach in high-dimensional settings.

In terms of the conference's criteria, the paper excels in originality, as it presents a novel approach to parallel machine learning. The quality of the paper is also strong, with well-supported claims and a thorough analysis of the Radon machine's complexity. However, the significance of the paper could be improved by addressing the practical concerns related to high-dimensional data. The clarity of the paper is generally good, but some sections, such as the proof of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3, could benefit from additional explanations or examples to facilitate understanding.

Overall, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. With some revisions to address the practical concerns and provide more insight into handling high-dimensional data, the paper could be even stronger.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* Novel approach to parallel machine learning
* Thorough analysis of the complexity of the Radon machine
* Empirical comparisons with parallel algorithms in Spark and base linear models in Weka
* Derivation of a PAC bound on the Radon machine

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* Limited experiments on low-dimensional datasets
* Unclear performance on high-dimensional data
* Potential limitations of the approach in high-dimensional settings not fully addressed
