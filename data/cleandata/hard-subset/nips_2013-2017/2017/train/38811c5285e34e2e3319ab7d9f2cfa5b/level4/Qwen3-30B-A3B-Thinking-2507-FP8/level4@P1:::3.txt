

The authors present the Radon machine, a novel parallelization scheme that enables effective parallel execution of a broad class of learning algorithms while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. This work makes a significant contribution to addressing an open question in parallel machine learning: whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be performed in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors (Nick's Class, NC).

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents both theoretical and empirical contributions. The theoretical analysis demonstrates that the Radon machine reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units while providing doubly-exponential confidence improvements with the height of the aggregation tree. The key insight—using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation instead of averaging—provides a substantial theoretical advantage, as the Radon point maintains confidence even when a small fraction of hypotheses are poor performers, whereas averaging does not.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and compelling. On multiple datasets (including SUSY with 5 million instances), the Radon machine achieves 80-700x speedups over sequential implementations while maintaining comparable predictive performance to state-of-the-art Spark MLlib algorithms. The paper also demonstrates that the Radon machine significantly outperforms averaging-at-the-end baselines in terms of predictive quality (by at least 10% AUC) while maintaining comparable runtime. In practical comparisons with Spark MLlib, the Radon machine achieves either substantially faster runtime (up to an order of magnitude) or better predictive performance on multiple datasets.

The paper's main strengths include:
1. A theoretically rigorous approach to parallel machine learning that directly addresses a fundamental open question
2. A practical black-box parallelization scheme applicable to a wide range of learning algorithms without requiring mathematical derivations or dedicated code
3. Comprehensive empirical validation across diverse datasets and learning algorithms
4. Thoughtful discussion of limitations and future work, including applicability to high-dimensional data and non-linear models

The authors effectively position their work within the context of existing theoretical treatments of parallel learning, particularly the work by Long and Servedio on learning half-spaces. The paper also makes a valuable contribution by clarifying that the Radon machine's advantage is not merely computational—it provides better confidence guarantees than existing parallelization approaches.

The paper is well-written with clear explanations of both the theoretical contributions and empirical results. The Radon machine's theoretical guarantees are supported by rigorous proofs, and the empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors have thoughtfully addressed potential concerns about applicability to high-dimensional data and non-linear models in the discussion section.

The minor issues noted in the draft review (e.g., formatting inconsistencies, figure readability) are easily addressable in the final version and do not detract from the paper's substantial contributions. The paper's contribution is significant and well-supported, making it an excellent fit for NIPS.

I recommend acceptance with confidence. The work advances the state of the art in parallel machine learning by providing a theoretically grounded and practically effective solution to a long-standing open question, with clear implications for both theoretical understanding and practical implementation of machine learning algorithms.
