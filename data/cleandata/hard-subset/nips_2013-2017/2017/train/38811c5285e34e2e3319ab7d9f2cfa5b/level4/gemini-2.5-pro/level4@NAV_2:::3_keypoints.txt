- Introduces the Radon machine: a novel parallelization scheme that trains base learners on disjoint subsets and aggregates hypotheses via iterative Radon-point aggregation.  
- Core theoretical claim: Radon-point aggregation increases confidence doubly-exponentially with tree height (Theorem 3).  
- Complexity result: Can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic given a quasi-polynomial number of processors (Theorem 4), advancing the NC-learnability question.  
- Novelty: Uses a geometric Radon-point construct for aggregation, differing from common parameter-averaging methods and offering stronger guarantees.  
- Broad applicability: The method is "black-box" and can be applied to many regularized risk minimization algorithms without modifying the base learner.  
- Empirical speed-ups: Demonstrates substantial wall-clock speed-ups (80x to 700x) over sequential learners while maintaining comparable accuracy.  
- Empirical baselines: Shows competitive or superior runtime and accuracy versus Spark MLlib and averaging-at-end baselines.  
- Clarity and completeness: Paper is well-written, structured, and proactively discusses limitations (high-dimensional data, non-linear models, communication complexity).  
- Main strength summary: Novel, theoretically grounded parallel scheme that addresses a significant open problem and shows strong empirical results.  
- Main weakness summary: Issues are minor and mostly presentational rather than technical.  
- Figure legibility: Figures 1–3 have text too small and Figure 3's colors may not be distinguishable in grayscale; increase font sizes and adjust color scheme.  
- Multiclass experiments: The claim of better performance than averaging with slightly longer runtime needs visualization and stronger justification that the accuracy gain outweighs added complexity.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept — significant contributions to theory and practice of parallel ML.  
- Minor editorial fixes: Typos at lines 51 ("the the"), 340–341 ("machineachieved", "machineis"), and consistency in citation style (line 377).  
- Formatting/math issues: Equation 4 font appears smaller (line 79) and should be fixed for readability.  
- Clarification request: Specify whether the claim about outperforming the averaging baseline is based on averaged paired comparisons across datasets (lines 318–320).  
- Language suggestion: Avoid using "significant" without formal statistical tests; use "substantial" or "large" where appropriate (line 324).
