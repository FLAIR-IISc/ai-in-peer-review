Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The method partitions the data into rh subsets, trains the base learner independently on each subset, and aggregates the resulting models along a tree using Radon points instead of simple averaging. The key theoretical result is that, under convexity and finite Radon number assumptions (e.g., Euclidean Rd with r = d + 2), the probability that the aggregated hypothesis is ε-bad decays doubly exponentially with the tree height h: P(Q(rh) > ε) ≤ (r P(Q(F) > ε))^{2^h}. Consequently, for base learners with polynomial sample and runtime complexity, the Radon machine achieves polylogarithmic runtime in the target sample complexity by using quasi-polynomially many processors, at the cost of increased data requirements. Empirically, the authors show large wall-clock speed-ups (80–700× on 150 processors) with comparable or better predictive performance relative to (i) the base learner on a single processor, (ii) an averaging-at-the-end baseline, and (iii) Spark MLlib implementations.

This work connects parallel learning to NC, echoing prior NIPS and broader TCS discussions on the limits of parallelization for polynomial-time learning (e.g., Vitter & Lin; Long & Servedio). The proposed aggregation relates to classical robust aggregation ideas (e.g., Tukey depth and center points) and contrasts with model averaging and distributed SGD approaches in the literature.

Quality
- Theory: The Radon aggregation guarantees (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) are clearly stated and rely on quasi-/convexity and Radon-point properties, yielding a clean confidence amplification bound. The runtime comparison against a sequential base learner targeting the same (ε, Δ) is informative. The assumptions (finite-dimensional parametrization; finite Radon number; δ ≤ 1/(2r) for the base learner) are explicit, and the cost in sample size is discussed. The NC discussion (Theorem 4) is well-motivated, though it hinges on bounding the Radon number polylogarithmically in the sample complexity, which in Euclidean spaces translates to a dependence on d. It would help to clarify the regime in which this assumption is realistic.
- Empirics: The experiments are carefully designed (10-fold CV; multiple datasets; comparisons to Spark and averaging). The reported speed-ups and competitive AUCs are encouraging. However, additional details would strengthen confidence: (i) whether timing includes data loading and hyperparameter search; (ii) sensitivity to h and subset size; (iii) scaling with dimension due to the r×r linear systems; and (iv) communication overheads in practice.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. A few notation/typo issues should be cleaned up:
- “ld” should be defined explicitly as log2, and exponents such as (rδ)^{2^h} should be typeset clearly (the manuscript currently reads as “2 h” in several places).
- The symbol r is used both as Radon number and as the Radon point random variable; consider disambiguating.
- State more explicitly the dependence of r on d and recap where r enters the runtime.

Originality
Using Radon points for parallel aggregation in learning, with rigorous guarantees that yield doubly-exponential confidence amplification, appears novel relative to averaging-at-the-end and standard distributed ERM/SGD frameworks. However, there seems to be closely related prior work “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” (possibly by the same authors). The paper should cite it and delineate the new contributions here (e.g., broader class of learners, improved bounds, new analyses for sample/runtime/NC, and/or new empirical evaluations). A comparison to other robust aggregators (geometric median, coordinate-wise median, median-of-means) would also help contextualize novelty.

Significance
If widely applicable, a black-box scheme that reduces runtime to polylogarithmic (in sample complexity) with formal guarantees is impactful. The practical speed-ups and comparable accuracy are promising. Limitations include reliance on abundant data, finite-dimensional convex parametrizations, and the quasi-polynomial processor requirement for the strongest theoretical result. The discussion on deparallelization (c < rh) is appreciated; quantifying the empirical speed-up versus processor count would strengthen practical significance.

Pros
- Clear, general, and theoretically grounded aggregation scheme with strong confidence amplification.
- Black-box applicability to many convex regularized risk minimizers; minimal code changes.
- Strong empirical speed-ups with competitive predictive performance across diverse datasets.
- Thoughtful discussion of NC, communication costs, and extensions (streaming, non-linear models via feature maps).

Cons
- Novelty relative to “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization” is unclear; missing citation and comparison.
- Theoretical polylog-time result depends on quasi-polynomial processors and on r (thus on d); practical regimes where this translates to polynomial processors are not fully clarified.
- Limited analysis of high-dimensional settings where r = d + 2 makes Radon computations expensive; no comparison to other robust aggregators.
- Some notation/clarity issues; experimental reporting could better isolate aggregation overheads and data-loading/hyperparameter costs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Cite and contrast with “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization,” clearly stating what is new (theory, scope, or experiments).
- Compare Radon aggregation to geometric median/coordinate-wise median/median-of-means in both theory and practice.
- Provide scaling experiments vs. d, including time for computing Radon points and numerical stability considerations.
- Report end-to-end wall-clock time, including data I/O and hyperparameter tuning; ablate the effect of h, subset size, and number of processors.
- Clarify the conditions under which r can be upper bounded by a polylog function of sample complexity; discuss practical implications when d grows with N.
- Fix notation and exponent formatting; define “ld” and ensure consistent use of symbols.

Overall evaluation
A solid and potentially impactful contribution that bridges theory and practice for parallel learning via a principled aggregation mechanism. With clearer positioning relative to closely related prior work and additional empirical/clarity refinements, the paper would be a strong NIPS submission.

Preliminary recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on addressing the novelty/related-work gap and improving clarity and empirical reporting.
