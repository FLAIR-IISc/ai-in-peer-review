Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms with convex loss and regularizer. The method trains a base learner independently on many iid data subsets, then aggregates hypotheses via an iterated Radon-point procedure arranged as a tree. The key theoretical claim is that replacing averaging with Radon-point aggregation yields a doubly-exponential reduction in the probability of returning an ε-bad hypothesis as a function of the tree height h, while enabling polylogarithmic runtime (in the target sample complexity) when quasi-polynomially many processors are available. The cost is increased sample complexity and processor count. Empirically, the method shows large speedups over single-machine baselines and competitive performance vs Spark MLlib implementations.

The paper is clearly positioned relative to averaging-based parallelization and “averaging-at-the-end” schemes [e.g., 32, 45, 46], distributed SGD and mini-batch approaches [34, 10], and distributed ERM/ADMM-style optimization [6, 33]. It also connects to the NC literature on efficient parallelism [1, 42] and to Long and Servedio’s work on parallel learning of half-spaces [21]. The novelty here is leveraging Radon points as an aggregator with provable confidence amplification and turning this into an end-to-end, implementation-agnostic parallelization scheme.

Quality
The theoretical contributions are sound and carefully scoped. Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 formalize the robustness of Radon points vs averaging and show that the failure probability reduces as (rδ)^(2^h), under a finite Radon number r and convexity assumptions. The runtime discussion (Theorem 4) is nuanced: polylogarithmic time is achieved on quasi-polynomially many processors, with aggregation cost O(r^3) per level and overall cost O(log^κ M + r^3 log M) for appropriate h, when the base learner runs in O(n^κ). The assumptions (finite-dimensional Euclidean parametrization, consistent and efficient base learner, δ ≤ 1/2r) are clearly stated.

Empirically, the evaluation shows strong speedups (80x–700x on 150 processors) with comparable AUC to both base learners and Spark MLlib on several large datasets. Comparisons to an averaging-at-the-end baseline are appropriate and underscore the benefit of Radon aggregation in accuracy with negligible runtime overhead.

The main technical caveat is scalability in high-dimensional spaces, since r = d + 2 and computing Radon points incurs O(r^3) time with a requirement that each batch has at least r samples. While the paper discusses random projections and low-rank approximations as practical mitigations, the consequences for the theoretical guarantees (e.g., how such embeddings affect sample complexity and Radon-number bounds) are not quantified. The requirement that base learners achieve δ ≤ 1/2r before aggregation can be demanding when d is large.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well organized, and the high-level idea is easy to follow. Algorithm 1 is clear. The discussion of NC and practical simulation when c < r^h is helpful. To improve clarity:
- Move the linear system used to compute Radon points (currently in the appendix) into the main text, with a short discussion of uniqueness and numerical stability.
- Provide a concise practitioner guide for choosing h given data size, dimension (r), target (ε, Δ), and processor budget c < r^h.
- Explicitly state the dependence of communication on r and h in the main text and include a wall-clock breakdown of learning vs aggregation.

Originality
Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation in a distributed learning context, with rigorous confidence amplification and runtime analysis, is original. The work goes beyond prior model averaging and distributed optimization by delivering a black-box scheme with a sharper confidence vs runtime trade-off and a concrete link to NC-style parallel efficiency.

Significance
The method provides a generic, implementation-agnostic way to parallelize many convex learners with theoretical guarantees and practical gains. The connection to fundamental questions about efficient parallel learning adds conceptual significance. If the high-dimensional limitations are addressed (empirically and theoretically), the approach could impact practitioners who need fast, high-confidence training across diverse models.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- High dimensionality: Because r = d + 2, both the aggregation cost (O(d^3)) and the minimum per-leaf sample size scale with d. Provide experiments on high-dimensional sparse text or image features (e.g., ≥10^4 dimensions), together with random projections or random features, and report the effect on accuracy and runtime. Ideally, quantify how embeddings affect r and the guarantees.
- Processor assumptions: The headline polylog-time guarantee requires quasi-polynomial processors. Include scaling results when c ≪ r^h (deparallelized execution), and report how speedup degrades with limited cores.
- Base learner confidence: Discuss how to ensure δ ≤ 1/2r in practice and how sensitive overall performance is to this precondition. An ablation varying δ (via subset size) would be informative.
- Comparisons and tuning: Clarify hyperparameter tuning for Spark baselines, include confidence intervals over cross-validation folds, and report training/communication breakdowns. Consider additional baselines (e.g., distributed mini-batch SGD) where applicable.
- Nonlinear models: Provide at least one experiment with explicit nonlinear features (e.g., random Fourier features) to demonstrate applicability beyond linear models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and elegant theoretical contribution leveraging Radon points to amplify confidence with a simple, black-box scheme.
- Clear connection to NC and parallel learning theory, with concrete runtime guarantees.
- Strong empirical speedups with competitive accuracy across multiple datasets and tasks.
- Practical appeal: minimal modification to base learners, low communication, and open-source implementation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited evaluation on high-dimensional settings where r and O(r^3) may be prohibitive; mitigation strategies are discussed but not demonstrated or analyzed.
- Polylog-time guarantee hinges on quasi-polynomial processors; practical performance under limited cores needs deeper analysis.
- Assumption δ ≤ 1/2r may be hard to meet in some regimes; sensitivity analysis is missing.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a principled and broadly applicable parallelization framework with solid theory and promising empirical results. Addressing the high-dimensional case experimentally (and, if possible, tightening the analysis under dimensionality reduction) would substantially strengthen the contribution.
