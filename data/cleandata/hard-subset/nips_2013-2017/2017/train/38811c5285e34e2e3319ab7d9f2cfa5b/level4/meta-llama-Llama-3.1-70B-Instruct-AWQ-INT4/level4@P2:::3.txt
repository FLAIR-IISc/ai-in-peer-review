The paper "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning" presents a novel parallelisation scheme for a broad class of learning algorithms. The scheme, called the Radon machine, is able to reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units. This is a significant step towards a general answer to an open question on the efficient parallelisation of machine learning algorithms in the sense of Nick’s Class (NC). The cost of this parallelisation is in the form of a larger sample complexity.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the Radon machine, including a proof that the scheme is consistent and efficient. They also present empirical results that demonstrate the potential of the Radon machine in practical application scenarios. The results show that the Radon machine achieves a substantial reduction in computation time compared to the underlying learning algorithm, while maintaining similar predictive performance.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors do a good job of explaining the technical details of the Radon machine and its theoretical analysis. The empirical results are also clearly presented and easy to understand.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address several questions and concerns that the reader may have. For example, the authors discuss the applicability of the Radon machine to high-dimensional models, non-linear models, and its potential use in distributed data streams.

The paper also has several practical benefits. It is a black-box parallelisation scheme, meaning that it can be applied to a wide range of machine learning algorithms without requiring changes to the algorithm itself. The Radon machine also speeds up learning while achieving similar hypothesis quality as the base learner.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of the paper. It presents a novel and effective parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, along with a thorough theoretical analysis and empirical results to support the theory.
