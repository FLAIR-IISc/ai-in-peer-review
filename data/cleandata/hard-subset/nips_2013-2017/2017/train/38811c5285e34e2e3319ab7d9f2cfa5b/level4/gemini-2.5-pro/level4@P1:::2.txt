This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme, the "Radon Machine," for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to partition the data, train a base learner on each partition in parallel, and then aggregate the resulting hypotheses. Unlike common approaches that average the models, this work proposes an iterative aggregation process based on computing Radon points. A Radon point of a set of hypotheses is a point in the convex hull intersection of a partition of that set. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this aggregation technique reduces the probability of generating a poor hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the number of aggregation levels. This allows the scheme to reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic, given a quasi-polynomial number of processors and an increased sample complexity. This result is framed as a significant step toward answering whether machine learning can be efficiently parallelized in the sense of Nick's Class (NC). The paper's empirical evaluation compares the Radon Machine to sequential base learners, an averaging-based parallelization scheme, and state-of-the-art algorithms from Spark's MLlib, demonstrating substantial speedups with comparable or improved predictive performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Theoretical Significance:** The use of Radon points for model aggregation is a novel and elegant idea in the context of parallel machine learning. The theoretical analysis is a major strength, providing formal guarantees (Theorem 3) on the confidence amplification of the aggregation process. The connection drawn to the NC complexity class and the fundamental open question of efficient parallel learning for risk minimization places the work in a significant theoretical context.
2.  **Practicality and Generality:** A key advantage of the proposed method is its "black-box" nature. It can be applied to any consistent and efficient learning algorithm that operates in a convex hypothesis space with a finite Radon number, without requiring modification of the base learner's code. This makes the scheme highly practical and broadly applicable.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and provide compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness. The reported speedups over sequential learners (ranging from 80x to over 700x) are impressive. Furthermore, the Radon Machine is shown to be competitive with, and often faster than, highly optimized parallel implementations in Spark MLlib, while consistently outperforming the more naive averaging-at-the-end baseline in terms of predictive accuracy.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity on Original Contribution:** My main concern is the potential overlap with prior work that is not cited or discussed. The theoretical framework appears very similar to that presented in another paper, "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization," which seems to involve some of the same authors. The authors must clearly articulate the novel contributions of this manuscript in relation to that work. Without such a clarification, it is difficult for a reviewer to accurately assess the originality and contribution of the theoretical results presented here.
2.  **Limitations in High-Dimensional Settings:** The method's applicability is constrained by the Radon number of the hypothesis space, which is `d+2` in a `d`-dimensional Euclidean space. This implies that the number of data partitions required, and thus the number of processors and the total sample size, grows with the dimensionality of the model. The paper briefly mentions this and suggests remedies like random projections, but the practical implications and limitations for high-dimensional problems (e.g., those common in NLP or with kernel methods) could be discussed in more detail.

### Summary of the review

The paper presents a promising and well-motivated parallelization framework with strong theoretical underpinnings and impressive empirical results. The Radon point aggregation is a creative and powerful alternative to model averaging. However, the lack of discussion regarding a seemingly related prior publication is a significant issue that clouds the assessment of the paper's originality. I would be inclined to recommend acceptance if the authors can satisfactorily address this point by clearly delineating the novel contributions of this submission.
