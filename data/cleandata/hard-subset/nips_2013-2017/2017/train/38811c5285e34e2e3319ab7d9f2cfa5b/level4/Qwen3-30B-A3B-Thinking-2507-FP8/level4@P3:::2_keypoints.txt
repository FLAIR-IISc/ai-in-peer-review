- Proposes the Radon Machine, a novel parallelization scheme that reduces runtime while keeping theoretical guarantees.  
- Aggregating hypotheses with Radon points yields doubly exponential confidence improvement with aggregation-tree height.  
- Main theoretical claim: consistent, efficient learners can be parallelized to run in polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processors, addressing an NC-style open question.  
- Properly situates work relative to averaging-based and SGD parallel methods, noting their inability to achieve polylogarithmic runtime.  
- Fails to cite related theoretical work ("Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization") by the same authors, which may overlap and undermines claimed novelty.  
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound with clear proofs for key theorems (Theorems 2–4).  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing to averaging and Spark implementations across tasks and datasets.  
- Experimental results show substantial speedups (80–700x) with comparable or improved predictive performance.  
- Originality: using Radon points for aggregation is a novel idea that achieves guarantees averaging cannot.  
- Significance: addresses an important large-scale ML problem and demonstrates practical benefits.  
- Clarity: paper is well-organized, with a detailed algorithm and accessible theoretical exposition.  
- Practical weakness: limited implementation details for large-scale systems and omission of communication cost analysis.  
- Needs clarification on overlap with prior uncited theoretical work and how this paper extends/differs from it.  
- Lacks sufficient discussion and empirical validation for high-dimensional settings where Radon numbers grow (random projection mention but no evidence).  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to fix citation issues and better address practical limitations and implementation considerations.
