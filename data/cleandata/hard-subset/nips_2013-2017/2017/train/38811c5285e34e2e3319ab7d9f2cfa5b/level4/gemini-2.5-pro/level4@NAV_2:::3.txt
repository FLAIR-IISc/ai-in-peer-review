This paper introduces the Radon machine, a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to first train a base learning algorithm on multiple, disjoint subsets of the data in parallel to generate a set of initial hypotheses. These hypotheses are then iteratively aggregated in a tree-like structure, where each aggregation step involves computing the Radon point of a small set of hypotheses from the previous level. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this aggregation method increases the confidence in the resulting hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. This allows the scheme to reduce the runtime of many learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic, given a quasi-polynomial number of processors, which represents a significant contribution towards answering the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization is possible in Nick's Class (NC). This work differs from common parallelization schemes like parameter averaging by using a geometric construct (the Radon point) for aggregation, which provides stronger theoretical guarantees.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of Radon points from computational geometry as a mechanism for aggregating hypotheses is a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel machine learning. This work provides a provably effective parallelization scheme that, unlike many existing methods, comes with strong performance guarantees and makes a tangible step towards resolving a fundamental open problem in parallel learning theory concerning NC-learnability.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically solid. The theoretical analysis clearly demonstrates how the probability of generating a poor hypothesis decreases doubly-exponentially with each level of Radon point aggregation (Theorem 3). These theoretical claims are then directly linked to a reduction in runtime complexity to polylogarithmic time (Theorem 4).
*   **Empirical Validation:** The claims are well-supported by a thorough empirical study. The experiments show that the Radon machine provides substantial speed-ups (from 80x to over 700x) compared to sequential base learners while maintaining comparable predictive accuracy. Furthermore, it demonstrates competitive or superior performance in both runtime and accuracy when compared against state-of-the-art parallel algorithms in Spark MLlib and a standard averaging-at-the-end baseline.
*   **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is well-written and well-structured. The authors do an excellent job of preemptively addressing potential questions and limitations in the discussion section, covering topics such as high-dimensional data, non-linear models, and communication complexity. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the practical and theoretical implications of their work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The paper is very strong, and the weaknesses are minor, mostly concerning presentation. Addressing them would further improve the manuscript.
*   **Clarity of Figures:** The text within Figures 1, 2, and 3 is too small to be easily legible, which may hinder comprehension, particularly in print. I suggest increasing the font size of all labels, legends, and axis ticks. Additionally, the color scheme in Figure 3 might not be easily distinguishable if printed in grayscale.
*   **Empirical Comparison Advocacy:** In the multi-class classification experiments (lines 341-349), the Radon machine is reported to perform better than the averaging-at-the-end baseline with only a "slightly longer runtime." It would be beneficial to visualize these results (perhaps in an appendix or a new figure) and to advocate more strongly for why the performance gain justifies the (albeit small) increase in runtime and algorithmic complexity compared to simple averaging in this setting.

**Summary of Arguments:**
*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and elegant parallelization scheme with strong theoretical foundations.
    *   Addresses a significant and long-standing open problem in parallel learning theory.
    *   Demonstrates impressive empirical speed-ups and strong predictive performance against relevant baselines.
    *   The "black-box" nature of the scheme makes it broadly applicable to many existing learning algorithms without modification.
*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The paper has some minor presentational issues (e.g., figure text size) that should be addressed.

Overall, this is an excellent paper with significant contributions to both the theory and practice of parallel machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
