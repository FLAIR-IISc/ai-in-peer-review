

This paper introduces a parallelization algorithm for aggregating weak learners using Radon partitioning, supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The theoretical framework provides a compelling motivation for the approach, while the experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements in training time and AUC across multiple datasets and baselines. The authors effectively preempt and address key concerns in the Evaluation and Discussion sections, strengthening the paper's overall contribution. The work presents a solid advance in scalable ensemble learning, and I recommend acceptance.

The paper is generally well-written, but several minor revisions would enhance clarity and professionalism:
- Line 51: Correct "the the" to "the".
- Line 79: Ensure consistent math font size; the current rendering appears smaller and less readable than other mathematical notation.
- Figures 1–3: Increase font size for all labels and text elements to ensure readability, particularly in printed versions. Figure 3 should be revised to ensure clear differentiation in black-and-white printing.
- Lines 318–320: Clarify whether the claim "the Radon machine outperforms the averaging baseline" is based on an average of paired comparisons or a single aggregate metric. The current phrasing is ambiguous.
- Line 324: Avoid using "significant" without statistical context; the term should be reserved for statistically significant results.
- Lines 340–341: Correct spacing errors ("machineachieved" → "machine achieved", "machineis" → "machine is").
- Lines 341–349: The modest performance difference between the Radon method and the AverageAtTheEnd baseline appears underemphasized. Including this comparison in Figure 1 (or providing a more detailed justification in the main text) would better contextualize the method's advantages.
- Line 377: Correct citation format inconsistency ("Long and servedio [22]" → "Long and Servedio [22]" to match standard citation style).

The empirical results are robust and well-structured, but the presentation of the AverageAtTheEnd comparison warrants stronger justification. The authors should explicitly address why this particular baseline was selected and how the observed performance gap (while small) aligns with their theoretical claims. This would strengthen the narrative around the method's practical utility.

The theoretical analysis is sound and well-motivated, though the connection between the Radon partitioning framework and the observed empirical gains could be slightly more explicit. The paper successfully bridges theory and practice, and the preemptive handling of potential critiques (e.g., scalability limits, dataset sensitivity) demonstrates thoroughness.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to scalable ensemble learning. The minor technical and presentation issues noted above are easily resolvable and do not undermine the paper's core merits. The work advances the state of the art in parallel ensemble methods with clear theoretical grounding and practical validation. I believe the paper meets NIPS's standards for technical rigor and impact.
