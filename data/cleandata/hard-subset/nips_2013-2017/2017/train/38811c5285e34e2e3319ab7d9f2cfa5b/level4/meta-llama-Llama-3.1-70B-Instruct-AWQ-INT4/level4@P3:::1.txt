The paper proposes a novel parallelisation scheme for machine learning algorithms, which simplifies the adaptation of learning algorithms to growing amounts of data and maintains theoretical performance guarantees. The main contribution of the paper is the introduction of the Radon machine, a black-box parallelisation scheme that can be applied to a broad class of learning algorithms without further mathematical derivations and without writing dedicated code.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the work and explains the significance of the proposed parallelisation scheme. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the theoretical properties of the Radon machine, including its sample and runtime complexity.

The empirical evaluation of the Radon machine is also well-conducted, comparing its performance to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms from the Spark machine learning library and the natural baseline of averaging hypotheses instead of calculating their Radon point. The results show that the Radon machine achieves a substantial reduction in computation time while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

However, one major concern is the practical aspect of the proposed approach, particularly for high-dimensional data. The experiments seem to be limited to very low-dimensional datasets (18 features), which may not be representative of modern machine learning problems in most domains. It is not clear how well the proposed approach performs on high-dimensional data, and the authors should emphasize how their method can handle such cases.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is also well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

Regarding originality, the paper proposes a novel parallelisation scheme that is different from previous approaches. The use of Radon points to aggregate hypotheses is a new idea, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of its theoretical properties. The paper also relates to previous work on parallel machine learning and provides a clear comparison with existing approaches.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in machine learning, namely the efficient parallelisation of learning algorithms. The results show that the Radon machine can achieve a substantial reduction in computation time while maintaining comparable predictive performance, which is a significant contribution to the field.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a significant contribution to the field of machine learning. However, the authors should address the concern about the practical aspect of their approach, particularly for high-dimensional data.
