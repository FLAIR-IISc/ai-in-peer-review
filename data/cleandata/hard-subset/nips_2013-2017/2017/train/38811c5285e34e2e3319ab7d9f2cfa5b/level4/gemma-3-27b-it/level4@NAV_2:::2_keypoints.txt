- Introduces a novel parallelization scheme ("Radon machine") that aggregates hypotheses via Radon points to speed up learning.  
- Core idea: run base learner on random subsets in parallel and iteratively aggregate with Radon points, with theoretical guarantees.  
- Builds on averaging-based parallel methods and claims robustness improvements by using Radon points.  
- Empirically shows up to 700x speedups while maintaining accuracy comparable to averaging schemes and Spark MLlib.  
- Demonstrates runtime reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic at the cost of increased sample complexity and many processors.  
- Provides rigorous analysis of sample and runtime complexity, highlighted by Theorem 4.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly explains theory and methodology.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough across multiple datasets and relevant baselines.  
- Discussion addresses limitations and future work (high-dimensional models, online learning, federated settings).  
- Weakness: limited related-work discussion beyond averaging-based methods; needs broader coverage of parallel learning literature.  
- Weakness: omission of an apparently related prior work ("Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization"); must cite and clarify incremental contribution.  
- Needs more discussion on practical implications of the Radon number and strategies when it is large or unknown.  
- Should elaborate implementation challenges in real distributed settings (communication overhead, fault tolerance).  
- Suggested related additions: include Dekel et al. and Shamir et al. on communication-efficient parallel algorithms.  
- Recommendation: strong paper; accept with minor revisions to address related work, citation, Radon-number practicality, and implementation trade-offs.
