Summary
This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization that aggregates hypotheses via Radon points rather than averaging. The method (i) trains base learners independently on iid subsets, (ii) aggregates r = d+2 hypotheses at a time using Radon points in an r-ary tree of height h, and (iii) achieves doubly exponential reduction in error probability with tree height (Proposition 2, Theorem 3). Under standard consistency assumptions on the base learner and finite Radon number (e.g., Euclidean Rd), the authors show that the Radon machine can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the target sample complexity, at the cost of quasi-polynomially many processors and increased total sample size (Theorem 4). Empirically, the method yields large wall-clock speedups (up to ~700x with 150 processors) while matching or exceeding AUC of base learners and outperforming an averaging-at-the-end baseline. The method compares favorably to several Spark MLlib alternatives on multiple large datasets. The work connects parallel ML to the NC literature (e.g., Vitter & Lin; Long & Servedio), and contrasts with averaging-based and distributed SGD methods.

Evaluation
- Quality: The theoretical analysis is clear and technically sound under the stated assumptions. The confidence amplification result via Radon aggregation is elegant and well-motivated. The runtime bound TR,h(n) = TL(n) + h r^3 is reasonable given r = d+2, and the doubly exponential improvement in ∆ is compelling. That said, several assumptions merit discussion: (1) the requirement of quasi-polynomially many processors; (2) the need for δ ≤ 1/2^r for base learners; (3) finite-dimensional Euclidean embedding and its effect on r; and (4) the increased sample complexity. The empirical study is thorough and shows strong speedups with competitive accuracy, but lacks statistical tests and a deeper analysis of sensitivity to h, r, and dimensionality. Overall, the claims are appropriately bounded and supported.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The algorithm and assumptions are stated cleanly, and the connection to NC is helpful. However, figures are difficult to read (small fonts) and some minor typographical issues and citation inconsistencies remain (see Specific comments).
- Originality: Using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses for confidence amplification in a black-box parallelization scheme is novel and distinct from averaging and distributed SGD lines of work. The link to centerpoint approximations and quasi-convex analysis is interesting.
- Significance: As a generic, implementation-agnostic scheme with theoretical guarantees and strong practical speedups, the contribution is significant. The need for many processors and the dependence on r may limit some applications, but the approach offers a promising path for high-confidence ML at scale and advances our understanding of parallelizability toward NC-like efficiency.

Pros
- Clear, general framework with minimal changes to base learners (black-box applicability).
- Strong theory: doubly exponential reduction in failure probability and a path to polylogarithmic runtime (with caveats).
- Extensive experiments across classifiers, regression, and multiclass, showing large speedups and competitive AUC.
- Sensible discussion of communication, limited processors (deparallelization), and extensions to nonlinear settings.

Cons
- Polylogarithmic runtime requires quasi-polynomial processors; stronger NC guarantees (polylog time on polynomial processors) remain open.
- Performance and runtime depend on r = d+2; high-dimensional settings may be challenging without additional dimensionality reduction.
- Empirical analysis would benefit from statistical significance tests, error bars, and clearer treatment of paired comparisons.
- Some presentation issues (figure legibility, typos, citation style) detract from clarity.

Questions for the authors
- When stating that the Radon machine “outperforms the averaging baseline,” is this based on paired comparisons per split? Please report mean ± std (or CI) and results of paired tests across folds.
- How sensitive are results to h and to r (i.e., dimensionality)? Can you provide a small ablation showing AUC/runtime vs h?
- For cases with c < r^h processors, can you report empirical scaling (e.g., with 16/32/64/128 cores) to complement the theoretical factor max{1, r^h/c}?
- What numerical conditioning issues arise when solving the r×r systems for Radon points in higher dimensions? Any stability safeguards?

Suggestions for improvement
- Figures: Increase font sizes substantially; ensure line styles/colors are distinguishable in grayscale (especially Fig. 3). Consider moving key Avg vs Radon comparisons into Fig. 1 to centralize the main empirical story; add error bars.
- Statistical reporting: Include paired statistical tests for AUC and perhaps runtime; avoid the term “significant” unless reporting statistical significance.
- Experimental details: Clarify how h is chosen (beyond h = max), report the actual δ achieved by base learners (if measurable), and provide sensitivity to subset size. Add calibration metrics if “confidence” is a central motivation.
- Scope and claims: Be precise about NC—your result achieves polylog time on quasi-polynomial processors; discuss whether and when r can be bounded by polylog(sample complexity) in common settings.
- Nonlinear/high-dimensional regimes: Include a brief experiment with random features or Nyström to demonstrate applicability when d is large.
- Reproducibility: Provide scripts to reproduce experiments; summarize key hyperparameters in the main text.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper presents a novel and well-supported approach to parallelizing a broad class of learning algorithms with both theoretical and practical impact. Addressing the presentation issues and adding basic statistical reporting would further strengthen the submission.
