- Novel parallelization scheme (Radon machine) applies base learners to random subsets and aggregates via Radon points to boost confidence.
- Theoretical analysis claims potential polylogarithmic runtime on a quasi-polynomial number of processors and connects to Nick’s Class.
- Empirical results show substantial speedups and comparable or better predictive performance versus baselines and Spark MLlib.
- Builds on and improves over prior parallel learning methods, addressing limitations of simple averaging and distributed SGD.
- Practical concerns (high-dimensional data, varying processor counts) are acknowledged and potential solutions suggested.
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, making complex ideas accessible.
- Figures 1–3 have unreadably small font sizes, reducing interpretability.
- The claim of outperforming the averaging baseline needs clarity on whether paired statistical tests were used.
- Use of the word “significant” without statistical reference is potentially misleading and should be rephrased.
- Several minor typos and formatting inconsistencies (specific lines called out) need correction.
- Equation font sizing is inconsistent and some math text is too small compared to body text.
- Figure 3’s color scheme may fail in black-and-white printing; use patterns or clearer labels.
- More detailed analysis of communication complexity and costs in distributed settings is needed.
- The modest empirical gain over “Averaging-at-the-End” should be better justified and visually compared in figures.
- Inconsistent citation/author formatting (e.g., “Long and servedio”) should be fixed.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses scaling challenge with a novel, theoretically sound approach and strong empirical evidence.
- Arguments for rejection: presentation/readability issues, lack of explicit statistical testing, and minor polish problems weaken the submission.
- Overall recommendation: accept, contingent on addressing figure readability, statistical rigor, communication-cost discussion, and minor typos.
