- Proposes the "Radon machine": parallelize a base learner on random data subsets and iteratively aggregate hypotheses via Radon points.  
- Theoretical claim: polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial number of processors, linking to Nick’s Class (NC).  
- Empirically shows large speedups over sequential, averaging-based parallelization, and Spark MLlib while maintaining comparable or better accuracy.  
- Distinguishes itself from averaging methods by using Radon points for more robust aggregation and improved confidence bounds.  
- Situates work within extensive parallel/distributed ML literature, citing parallel PAC learning, parallel half-space learning, and communication-efficient distributed optimization.  
- Missing citation: "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (potentially by same authors) must be included and its relation clarified.  
- Strength — Theoretical guarantees: provides solid proofs under stated assumptions.  
- Strength — Generality: black-box approach applicable to many learning algorithms without algorithm-specific changes.  
- Strength — Empirical validation: convincing experiments and valuable comparison to Spark’s MLlib.  
- Strength — Presentation: clear exposition of Radon points and methods.  
- Weakness — Related work omission undermines clarity about originality and prior contribution overlap.  
- Weakness — Practical limits: reliance on finite Radon number; challenges for high-dimensional or nonlinear models need deeper discussion and concrete remedies.  
- Weakness — Communication complexity: claims low communication cost but lacks rigorous distributed communication analysis.  
- Weakness — Dataset-size dependence: runtime depends on dataset size; requires more detailed analysis and mitigation strategies.  
- Recommendation: overall positive — significant contribution; recommend acceptance conditional on addressing related-work citation and practical limitation concerns.
