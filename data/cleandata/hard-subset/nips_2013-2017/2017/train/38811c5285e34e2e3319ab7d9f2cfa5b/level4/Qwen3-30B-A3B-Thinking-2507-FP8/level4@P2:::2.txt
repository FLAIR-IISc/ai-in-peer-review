

This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme, the Radon Machine, for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The authors propose using Radon points for aggregation instead of averaging, which they claim achieves doubly exponential confidence improvements with each level of aggregation in a tree structure. The theoretical contribution demonstrates that the scheme can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic time while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees, with the cost being increased sample complexity. The empirical evaluation shows significant speedups (80-700x) on multiple datasets compared to sequential implementations, with comparable or better predictive performance than Spark-based implementations and averaging baselines.

The paper builds on the theoretical foundations of parallel learning, connecting to Nick's Class (NC) and the open question of efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms. The authors relate their work to previous theoretical treatments of parallel learning (e.g., Vitter and Lin [42], Long and Servedio [21]) and distinguish their approach from existing parallelization methods that rely on averaging or stochastic gradient descent.

**Strengths:**

1. **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-structured proofs for the key propositions and theorems. The authors provide clear bounds on the probability of obtaining ε-bad hypotheses, demonstrating a doubly exponential reduction in error probability with each aggregation level. The connection to Radon's theorem and convex geometry is well-motivated.

2. **Originality:** The core innovation of using Radon points for aggregation rather than averaging represents a significant conceptual advance over existing approaches. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from previous work (e.g., averaging-based parallelizations [32, 45, 46]), and the theoretical guarantees are novel in this context. The black-box nature of the parallelization scheme, requiring no mathematical derivations or dedicated code for the underlying algorithm, is particularly valuable for practitioners.

3. **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental question in parallel computing for machine learning: whether polynomial-time learning algorithms can be parallelized to run in polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processors. This is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning, with clear practical implications for scaling machine learning to large datasets.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions and a logical flow from theoretical development to empirical validation. Algorithm 1 provides a concrete implementation of the proposed approach. The empirical section is thorough, with multiple comparative analyses against baselines and state-of-the-art Spark implementations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Citation Gap:** The theoretical foundation appears to overlap substantially with the authors' previous work "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization," which is not cited in the current paper. This is a significant issue as it obscures the novelty of the current contribution. The authors should explicitly clarify how this paper differs from their prior work and why the new contribution is significant enough for NIPS.

2. **Practical Implementation Details:** While the paper claims the scheme is "black-box," the empirical evaluation requires careful tuning of parameters (h, r) to achieve the best results. The paper could provide more guidance on how to select these parameters for different datasets or learning algorithms.

3. **Limitations of the Approach:** The paper acknowledges limitations regarding high-dimensional data and non-linear models, but the discussion could be strengthened by providing concrete examples of datasets where the approach would fail and how to mitigate these issues (e.g., through low-rank approximations).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

- The theoretical contribution is strong and addresses a fundamental question in parallel learning.
- The empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups with comparable or better predictive performance than existing approaches.
- The black-box nature of the parallelization scheme makes it broadly applicable to many learning algorithms.
- The connection to Nick's Class (NC) provides important theoretical context for the problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

- The lack of citation to the authors' previous work on similar theoretical foundations raises questions about novelty.
- The paper does not sufficiently explain how this work advances beyond the authors' previous research.
- While the empirical results are promising, the evaluation is limited to moderately sized datasets; larger-scale experiments would strengthen the claims.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of parallel machine learning, with clear practical implications. The Radon Machine represents a novel approach that achieves provably better confidence guarantees than existing parallelization techniques while maintaining reasonable computational complexity. However, the lack of citation to the authors' previous work on parallel randomized convex optimization significantly undermines the perceived novelty of the contribution.

To address this issue, the authors should:
1. Explicitly cite and discuss their previous work "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" in the related work section.
2. Clearly articulate the specific advances of this paper over their prior work, particularly in terms of theoretical guarantees, algorithmic improvements, or practical applications.
3. Expand the discussion of limitations to include concrete examples of when the approach would be less effective and how those limitations might be addressed.

With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the theoretical contribution is significant, but the lack of proper attribution to prior work by the same authors weakens the novelty claim, which is a critical criterion for NIPS.

Given the high quality of the theoretical analysis and empirical results, and with the necessary clarifications regarding prior work, this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. The authors should address the citation issue before final acceptance.
