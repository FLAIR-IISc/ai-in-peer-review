This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of learning algorithms, leveraging Radon points to achieve significant runtime reductions. The core idea is to apply the underlying learning algorithm in parallel to random subsets of the data and then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, offering theoretical performance guarantees. This work builds upon existing research in parallelizing machine learning, particularly averaging-based approaches [32, 45, 46], and attempts to move beyond their limitations by utilizing the more robust aggregation properties of Radon points [30]. The authors demonstrate empirically that their “Radon machine” can achieve substantial speedups—up to 700x compared to sequential execution—while maintaining comparable accuracy to both averaging schemes and established Spark MLlib implementations.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the theoretical underpinnings of the Radon machine. The authors convincingly demonstrate that the proposed scheme can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic, albeit at the cost of increased sample complexity and the need for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units. The rigorous analysis of the sample and runtime complexity, including Theorem 4, is a significant strength. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing the Radon machine against relevant baselines across multiple datasets and demonstrating its effectiveness in practice. The discussion section thoughtfully addresses potential limitations and avenues for future research, such as adapting the scheme to high-dimensional models and exploring its applicability to online learning and federated settings.

However, a key weakness is the limited discussion of related work beyond averaging-based methods. While the authors acknowledge the broader field of parallel computing [18, 7], and briefly mention Nick's Class [9], a more comprehensive review of existing parallel learning algorithms, particularly those targeting similar theoretical guarantees (e.g., polylogarithmic runtime), would strengthen the contribution. Furthermore, the authors mention a related work, "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization," potentially authored by the same group, without citing it. Clarifying the relationship and incremental contribution of this paper *relative* to that prior work is crucial. 

Specifically, the following points could be addressed:

* **Related Work:** Expand the discussion of related work to include more recent advances in distributed optimization and parallel learning, such as the work of Dekel et al. [22] and Shamir et al. [34], which also aim for communication-efficient and theoretically grounded parallel algorithms.
* **Citation:** Explicitly cite and contrast with "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" if it directly informs this work. Detail the novel contributions beyond that prior publication.
* **Radon Number:** While the paper acknowledges the dependence on the Radon number, a more detailed discussion of its practical implications for different hypothesis spaces would be valuable. What strategies can be employed when the Radon number is large or unknown?
* **Practical Considerations:** Elaborate on the challenges and trade-offs associated with implementing the Radon machine in real-world distributed environments, such as communication overhead and fault tolerance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Radon points to parallel learning is a novel and promising approach.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the scheme, with clear guarantees on runtime and sample complexity.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the Radon machine.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the underlying concepts and methodology.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Related Work:** The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive.
* **Missing Citation:** The lack of citation of a potentially relevant prior work weakens the presentation of novelty.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further enhance its impact and make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
