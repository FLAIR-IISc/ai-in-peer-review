The paper’s primary contribution is a novel framework for parallel machine learning algorithms, aiming to improve upon simple averaging of base learners. Specifically, the authors propose representing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point. They provide complexity bounds for this method and demonstrate its performance empirically, comparing it to parallel algorithms implemented in Spark and to base linear models within Weka.

This paper presents an interesting, essentially black-box approach to combining weak learners, offering an alternative to techniques like bagging. The theoretical work focuses on analyzing the complexity of the Radon machine, highlighting its efficiency gains due to the decomposition of original samples – enabling parallel computation and reducing the computational burden compared to a base learner operating on the entire dataset. A PAC bound for the Radon machine is also established, representing a strong theoretical contribution. A key concern, however, lies in the practical applicability of the method, particularly for high-dimensional data. It is unclear how well the approach scales to such scenarios, as the presented experiments utilize relatively low-dimensional datasets (approximately 18 features), which may not be representative of many modern machine learning problems. Addressing this limitation by discussing strategies for handling high dimensionality would significantly strengthen the paper.
