- Proposes the "Radon Machine": partition data, train base learners in parallel, and hierarchically aggregate hypotheses using Radon points.  
- Uses Radon points (convex-hull intersection) for aggregation instead of simple averaging.  
- Theorem 3: hierarchical Radon aggregation yields a doubly-exponential reduction in the probability of a poor hypothesis with tree height.  
- Claims asymptotic speed-up: runtime can drop from polynomial to polylogarithmic given quasi-polynomial processors and higher sample complexity; links to NC complexity discussion.  
- Strong empirical results: 80x–700x speed-ups, comparable or better predictive performance than sequential baselines and Spark MLlib; Radon aggregation improves AUC vs end-averaging.  
- Presented as a "black-box" wrapper compatible with standard regularized risk minimization algorithms (e.g., SVM, logistic regression).  
- Major originality concern: appears to overlap with a 2017 arXiv paper by the same authors ("Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization") and does not cite/clarify differences.  
- Dimensionality limitation: Radon number = d+2 implies r grows with d, making required subsets/processors scale poorly in high-dimensional problems; dimensionality reduction may be required.  
- Practical processor gap: theoretical guarantees need a quasi-polynomial number of processors, while experiments use ~150; paper should better analyze trade-offs among processors, speed-up, and sample complexity.  
- Overall verdict: novel, theoretically strong, and empirically convincing work, but acceptance hinges on clarifying novelty relative to prior arXiv work and addressing high-dimensional/practicality concerns.
