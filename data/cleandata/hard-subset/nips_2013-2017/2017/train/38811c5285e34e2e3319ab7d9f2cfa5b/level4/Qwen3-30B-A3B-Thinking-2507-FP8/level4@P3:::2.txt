

This paper proposes a novel parallelization scheme called the Radon Machine for machine learning algorithms, which significantly reduces runtime while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The authors demonstrate that by using Radon points instead of averaging for hypothesis aggregation, they can achieve doubly exponential confidence improvement with the height of the aggregation tree. The main theoretical contribution is showing that consistent and efficient learning algorithms can be parallelized to run in polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomially many processing units, addressing an open question regarding the efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms in the sense of Nick's Class (NC).

The paper makes a clear connection to previous work on parallel learning, particularly noting the limitations of existing approaches like averaging-based parallelizations [32, 45, 46] and stochastic gradient descent methods [34]. The authors correctly identify that current methods cannot achieve polylogarithmic runtime, which is the key theoretical advance of their work. However, they fail to properly cite related theoretical work, including "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" by the same authors, which appears to cover similar theoretical ground. This omission is problematic as it undermines the claimed novelty of their theoretical contribution.

Strengths:
1. Quality: The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with clear proofs of the key theorems (Theorems 2-4) showing the doubly exponential confidence improvement and polylogarithmic runtime. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against both baseline averaging and state-of-the-art Spark implementations across multiple datasets and learning tasks (classification, regression, multi-class). The results consistently show substantial speedups (80-700x) with comparable or better predictive performance.

2. Originality: The use of Radon points for aggregation instead of averaging represents a novel approach to parallel learning. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work by demonstrating how this approach achieves theoretical guarantees that averaging cannot provide, particularly the doubly exponential confidence improvement with tree height.

3. Significance: The problem addressed is highly relevant for large-scale machine learning applications, where both data volume and the need for high-confidence predictions are growing. The practical benefits of the approach are well-demonstrated through extensive experiments showing substantial speedups without sacrificing accuracy.

4. Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The Radon Machine algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented with sufficient detail, and the theoretical results are accessible to readers familiar with machine learning theory. The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-presented.

Weaknesses:
1. Quality: The paper makes a significant theoretical claim that the Radon Machine achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors, but the practical implementation details for large-scale systems are limited. The analysis assumes an idealized parallel environment without addressing communication costs, which can be a significant bottleneck in real distributed systems.

2. Originality: The theoretical contribution appears to overlap substantially with the authors' prior work "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization" (which is not cited). The paper should explicitly clarify how this work extends or differs from their previous theoretical contributions.

3. Clarity: While the core ideas are well-explained, the connection between the theoretical guarantees and practical implementation could be strengthened. The paper states that "the Radon machine can be applied with any number of processing units" but doesn't sufficiently elaborate on how the scheme degrades gracefully when the ideal number of processors (r^h) isn't available.

4. Significance: The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly for high-dimensional data where the Radon number may grow with dimensionality. The authors briefly mention using random projections for high-dimensional data but don't provide empirical evidence of this approach's effectiveness.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical contribution to parallel machine learning with provable guarantees
- Strong empirical results showing substantial speedups without sacrificing accuracy
- Clear connection to the fundamental question of efficient parallelization in machine learning
- Well-written paper with comprehensive experimental evaluation

Arguments against acceptance:
- Failure to properly cite related theoretical work by the authors
- Insufficient discussion of practical limitations for high-dimensional data
- Limited analysis of communication costs in distributed implementations
- Theoretical guarantees assume idealized parallel environments not reflective of real-world constraints

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning, particularly in addressing the theoretical question of efficient parallelization. While the theoretical novelty could be clearer with proper citation of related work, the empirical results are compelling and the approach has significant practical potential. The authors should address the citation issue and provide more detailed discussion of the practical limitations and implementation considerations to strengthen their contribution.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the relationship with prior work and to better address the practical limitations of the approach. The paper's theoretical insights and empirical results are strong enough to warrant publication at NIPS, but the authors should take care to properly position their work relative to their own prior contributions.
