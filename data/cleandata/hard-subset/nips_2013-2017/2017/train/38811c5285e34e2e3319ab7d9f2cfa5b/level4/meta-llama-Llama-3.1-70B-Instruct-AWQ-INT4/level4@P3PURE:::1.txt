The main contribution of the paper is a new framework for parallel machine learning algorithms, which combines base learners more effectively than simple averaging. Specifically, subsets of hypotheses are replaced by their Radon point. The authors prove complexity bounds for their method and empirically compare their results with parallel algorithms in Spark and using base linear models in Weka.

The paper is quite interesting, as it proposes a black-box method to combine weak learners together, which seems to be an alternative to bagging weak learners together. The theoretical contribution lies in the analysis of the complexity of the Radon machine, where the original samples can be broken down into multiple parts, resulting in a parallel machine that is much more efficient compared to the base learner that operates on all the original samples. Additionally, the authors provide a PAC bound on the Radon machine, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

The theoretical contributions in the paper appear to be strong. However, a major concern is the practical aspect, particularly for high-dimensional data. It is unclear how well the proposed approach performs in such scenarios. The experiments seem to be limited to very low-dimensional datasets (18 features), which may not be representative of modern machine learning problems in most domains. If the authors could provide more emphasis on how their method can handle high-dimensional data, the paper would be significantly strengthened.
