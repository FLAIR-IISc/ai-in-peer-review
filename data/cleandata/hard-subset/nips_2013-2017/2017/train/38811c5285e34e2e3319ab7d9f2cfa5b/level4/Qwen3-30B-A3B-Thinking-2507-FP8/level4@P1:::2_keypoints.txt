- Introduces the "Radon Machine," a parallelization scheme using Radon points for aggregation instead of averaging.
- Shows polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors while maintaining theoretical guarantees.
- Main theoretical result: probability of an ε-bad hypothesis decreases doubly exponentially with aggregation tree height (vs single-exponential for averaging).
- Empirical results: 80–700× speedups over sequential implementations using 150 processors with comparable or better predictive performance.
- Outperforms or matches Spark MLlib and averaging-at-the-end baselines in runtime and predictive metrics (AUC, RMSE).
- Strong theoretical contribution linking to NC and the open question of polylog time parallel learning for consistent regularized risk minimization.
- Positions work relative to Long and Servedio’s open problem on parallel learning of half-spaces.
- Rigorous proofs and well-motivated propositions, with Proposition 2 highlighting robustness of Radon points vs averages.
- Thorough experimental design: multiple datasets, base learners, 10-fold cross-validation, and appropriate baselines.
- Main weakness: omission of the authors’ prior related work "Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization," leaving the novelty unclear.
- Presentation is clear and well-organized; Algorithm 1 concisely describes the method.
- Some technical details (e.g., Radon number calculation) could use more explanation for non-expert readers.
- Originality: using Radon points for aggregation is a meaningful departure from standard averaging approaches.
- Practical significance: black-box parallelization applicable to many algorithms (SVM, RLS, logistic) and valuable for high-confidence, high-stakes applications.
- Limitations: relies on availability of quasi-polynomially many processors and has limited discussion of communication complexity and extreme scalability.
- Recommendation: strengthen paper by explicitly citing and distinguishing prior related work to clarify theoretical novelty.
