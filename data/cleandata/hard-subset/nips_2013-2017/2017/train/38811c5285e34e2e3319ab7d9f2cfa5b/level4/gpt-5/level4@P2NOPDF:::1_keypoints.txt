- Introduces a framework that parallelizes learning by aggregating base learners using Radon points hierarchically instead of simple averaging.  
- Provides complexity bounds showing partitioning enables a parallel scheme more efficient than training one base learner on full data.  
- Presents a PAC-style generalization bound for the Radon machine; theoretical development is solid and well-motivated.  
- Empirically compares the method to parallel baselines implemented in Spark and to ensembles of linear models in Weka.  
- Method is compelling as a largely black-box alternative to bagging for aggregating weak learners.  
- Primary concern: unclear practical performance and scalability in high-dimensional settings (behavior as feature count d grows).  
- Unclear how computing Radon points scales with dimensionality and how it affects runtime and memory.  
- Experiments focus on low-dimensional datasets (~18 features), which are not representative of many modern applications.  
- Raises questions about robustness to sparsity, feature redundancy, and performance with large d.  
- Recommendation: add experiments on high-dimensional datasets reporting both accuracy and wall-clock time across feature counts and partition settings.  
- Recommendation: discuss computational cost of Radon point computation vs. dimensionality and propose strategies (exploit sparsity, approximate Radon points, dimensionality reduction).  
- Recommendation: clarify effectiveness when using stronger base learners and against more modern parallel baselines.  
- Overall: interesting and theoretically sound, but needs clearer treatment of high-dimensional behavior and more representative experiments to strengthen practical contribution.
