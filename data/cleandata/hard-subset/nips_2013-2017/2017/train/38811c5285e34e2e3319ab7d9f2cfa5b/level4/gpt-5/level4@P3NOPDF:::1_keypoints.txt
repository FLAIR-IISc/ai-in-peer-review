- Proposal: Introduces a black-box parallel aggregation scheme using Radon points to replace subsets of hypotheses instead of simple averaging.
- Theory: Presents complexity analyses showing parallel efficiency from data partitioning and a PAC-style generalization bound for the “Radon machine.”
- Experiments: Empirically compared against Spark parallel implementations and linear models in Weka.
- Novelty: Using Radon points as an aggregation operator is an elegant, general alternative to averaging/bagging.
- Theoretical backing: Clear complexity results and a PAC bound provide solid theoretical justification.
- Practical relevance: A generic, parallelizable method for combining weak learners is timely and broadly applicable.
- Empirical scope strength: Comparisons to established platforms and baselines help situate the method.
- Limited evaluation: Experiments focus on very low-dimensional datasets (~18 features), limiting conclusions for modern high-dimensional tasks.
- High-dimensional feasibility concern: Lacks discussion and measurement of computational/numerical overhead of Radon computations as dimensionality grows.
- Narrow empirical breadth: Needs more base learners (especially non-linear), datasets of varying dimensionality, and sensitivity analyses over key hyperparameters.
- Request for clarification: Explain complexity/scaling of Radon computation with feature dimension and hypotheses per subset, including numerical/stability issues.
- Request for experiments at scale: Add high-dimensional datasets (dense and sparse) and report wall-clock time, memory, and accuracy to quantify trade-offs.
- Base-learner sensitivity: Assess how choice and regularization of base learners affect performance, especially in high dimensions.
- Distributed costs: Detail communication patterns and compare aggregation communication costs to parameter-averaging schemes.
- Ablations requested: Provide studies on partition number/size, aggregation depth/recursion, and robustness to noise/outliers versus averaging/median methods.
- Bound interpretation: Contextualize the PAC bound with practical regimes (sample size, dimension, partitioning) and relate to experiments.
- Clarity: Paper is generally clear and well-motivated but needs stronger emphasis on practical implications for high-dimensional settings.
- Overall assessment: Interesting and theoretically solid contribution, but empirical evaluation is currently too limited for judging practicality on modern high-dimensional workloads.
