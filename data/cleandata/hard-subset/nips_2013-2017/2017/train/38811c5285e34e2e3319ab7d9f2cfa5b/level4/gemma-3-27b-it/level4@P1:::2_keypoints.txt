- Novel parallelization scheme using Radon points to iteratively aggregate hypotheses trained on random data subsets.  
- Core claim: reduces runtime to polylogarithmic time on quasi-polynomial many processors, at the cost of higher sample complexity.  
- Contrasts with averaging-based parallelization; Radon-point aggregation is the main novelty.  
- Theoretical grounding ties the approach to NC and provides rigorous runtime and sample-complexity bounds.  
- Empirical results show substantial runtime speedups and comparable or superior accuracy versus sequential training and Spark MLlib.  
- Experimental evaluation compares against relevant baselines across multiple datasets and reports compelling speed/accuracy trade-offs.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations for high-dimensional data and suggest remedies like random projections and low-rank approximations.  
- Paper is generally well-written and clearly presents core concepts and analysis.  
- Missing citation/discussion of the authors’ prior related work (e.g., “Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization”) limits clarity about the new contribution.  
- Communication-complexity analysis is brief and needs expansion or concrete experimental support given its importance in distributed settings.  
- Discussion of datasets where dimension ≪ instances is underdeveloped and the proposed remedies deserve a more thorough explanation.  
- Overall assessment: strong, compelling theoretical and empirical contribution; recommend acceptance after addressing minor clarifications.
