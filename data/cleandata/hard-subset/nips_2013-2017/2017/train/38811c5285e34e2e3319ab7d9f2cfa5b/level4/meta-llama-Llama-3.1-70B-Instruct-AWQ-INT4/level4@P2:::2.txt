The authors propose a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms based on Radon points. The main contribution of this paper is to show that algorithms satisfying certain conditions can be parallelized effectively, achieving the same confidence and error bounds as sequential execution in significantly shorter time. The proposed Radon machine can reduce the runtime of learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic, albeit at the cost of increased data size and the number of processing units used.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the Radon machine, including its sample and runtime complexity. The authors also present an empirical evaluation of the Radon machine, comparing its performance to state-of-the-art parallel machine learning algorithms from the Spark machine learning library, as well as the natural baseline of averaging hypotheses instead of calculating their Radon point.

The experimental results indicate that the Radon machine achieves a substantial reduction in computation time while maintaining comparable predictive performance to the Spark algorithms. The authors also demonstrate the general applicability of the scheme by applying it to regression and multi-class classification problems.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed parallelization scheme and its theoretical underpinnings. The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides a comprehensive comparison of the Radon machine to other parallel machine learning algorithms.

However, the authors could improve the paper by providing more context and background information on the theoretical part of the paper. Specifically, they should elaborate on the contribution of the paper with respect to the theoretical part, which seems to have been investigated in another paper ("Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization"). The authors should also provide more details on how the Radon machine can be applied to non-linear models and high-dimensional data.
