This paper introduces a new framework for parallel machine learning that combines base learners more effectively than simple averaging. The core idea is to replace subsets of hypotheses with their Radon point. The authors provide complexity bounds for their method and empirically compare it with parallel algorithms in Spark, using linear base models in Weka.

The work is compelling because it offers a largely black-box way to aggregate weak learners, serving as an alternative to bagging. The main theoretical contribution is the analysis of the Radon machine’s complexity: by partitioning the original dataset into multiple parts, the parallel system becomes much more efficient than a base learner trained on the full dataset. The authors also present a PAC bound for the Radon machine. Overall, the theoretical results are strong. My primary concern is the practical performance, especially for high-dimensional data. It is unclear how the proposed approach scales with dimensionality; the experiments appear to use very low-dimensional datasets (around 18 features), which may not reflect modern machine learning scenarios. If the authors could clarify how their method handles high-dimensional settings, the paper would be significantly stronger.
