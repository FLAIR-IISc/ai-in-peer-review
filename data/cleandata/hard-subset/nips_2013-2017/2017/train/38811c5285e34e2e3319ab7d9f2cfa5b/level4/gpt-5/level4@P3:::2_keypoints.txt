- Proposes the Radon machine: partition data, train base learners independently, and aggregate models via Radon points along a tree.  
- Main theoretical result: under convexity and finite Radon number, probability of an ε-bad aggregate decays doubly exponentially with tree height h.  
- Consequence: for polynomial-time base learners, Radon machine can achieve polylog runtime in target sample complexity using quasi-polynomial processors, at the cost of more data.  
- Connects to NC/parallel learning literature and classical robust aggregation (Tukey depth, center points), contrasting with averaging and distributed SGD.  
- Theory is clearly stated with explicit assumptions (finite-dimensional parametrization, finite Radon number, δ ≤ 1/(2r)) and a clean confidence-amplification bound.  
- NC claim depends on bounding Radon number polylogarithmically in sample complexity, which in Euclidean spaces implies dependence on dimension d and needs clarification.  
- Runtime comparison to sequential learners is informative, but sample-size cost and regimes where assumptions hold should be clarified.  
- Empirics show large wall-clock speed-ups (80–700× on 150 processors) and competitive or better AUCs versus single-processor, averaging, and Spark MLlib baselines.  
- Experimental design is solid (10-fold CV, multiple datasets), but results lack details on data loading, hyperparameter search, sensitivity to h/subset size, scaling with d, and communication overheads.  
- Notation and typesetting issues: define “ld” (log2), fix exponent formatting, and disambiguate symbol r (Radon number vs random variable).  
- Using Radon points for aggregation with rigorous doubly-exponential confidence amplification appears novel, but a related work (“Parallelizing Randomized Convex Optimization”) is missing and should be cited.  
- Paper should compare Radon aggregation to other robust aggregators (geometric median, coordinate-wise median, median-of-means) in theory and experiments.  
- Significance: potentially impactful black-box parallel scheme with formal guarantees and practical speed-ups, but limited by heavy data needs, finite-dimensional convexity, and quasi-polynomial processor requirement.  
- Pros: general, theoretically grounded aggregation; black-box applicability; strong empirical speed-ups; discussion of NC, communication, and extensions.  
- Cons: unclear novelty relative to prior work; dependence on r (hence d) for polylog-time; expensive Radon computations in high dimensions; limited comparison to other robust methods; some clarity/experimental reporting gaps.  
- Suggested improvements: cite and contrast prior related work, compare to other robust aggregators, provide scaling experiments vs d and Radon computation cost, report end-to-end timings including I/O and hyperparameter tuning, ablate h/subset size/processor count, and fix notation.  
- Overall evaluation: solid and potentially impactful contribution; recommend weak accept conditional on addressing related-work, clarity, and empirical reporting issues.
