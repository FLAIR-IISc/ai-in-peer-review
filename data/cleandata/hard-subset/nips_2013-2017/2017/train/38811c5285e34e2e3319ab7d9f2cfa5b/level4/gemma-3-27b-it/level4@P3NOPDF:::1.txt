## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

This paper introduces a novel framework for parallel machine learning algorithms based on the concept of replacing subsets of hypotheses with their Radon points. The core idea offers a potentially more effective method for combining base learners than simple averaging, and the authors provide both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation of their approach.

The paper’s strength lies in its exploration of a “black-box” method for ensemble construction, offering a compelling alternative to traditional techniques like bagging. The theoretical contribution, specifically the complexity analysis of the “Radon machine,” is well-developed. The authors convincingly argue that partitioning the original samples allows for a more efficient parallel machine, as computations can be distributed without requiring individual base learners to operate on the entire dataset. The derivation of a PAC bound further strengthens this theoretical foundation, demonstrating a formal understanding of the method’s generalization capabilities.

However, a significant concern centers on the practical applicability of the proposed method, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating promising results, is limited to datasets with a relatively low number of features (approximately 18). This raises questions about the scalability and performance of the Radon machine when applied to the high-dimensional data commonly encountered in modern machine learning applications. 

The authors compare their method with parallel algorithms implemented in Spark and base linear models within Weka. While these comparisons are helpful, the low dimensionality of the datasets used diminishes the relevance of these results to many real-world problems. The performance gains observed in these experiments may not translate to higher-dimensional spaces, where feature interactions and the curse of dimensionality often dominate.

To significantly strengthen the paper, the authors should address the challenges posed by high-dimensional data. Potential avenues to explore include: 

* **Theoretical Discussion of High-Dimensionality:** Expanding the theoretical analysis to incorporate the impact of dimensionality on the Radon machine’s complexity and generalization ability. Specifically, discussing how the Radon transform behaves in high dimensions and if it introduces any specific challenges.
* **Scalability Analysis:** Providing a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the Radon machine as a function of both sample size and feature dimensionality. 
* **Experiments with Higher-Dimensional Datasets:** Conducting experiments on datasets with a significantly higher number of features, ideally drawn from domains where high-dimensionality is a defining characteristic (e.g., text classification, genomics).  If computational costs are prohibitive, perhaps simulations focusing on the impact of dimensionality could be presented.
* **Dimensionality Reduction Integration:** Exploring the integration of dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA, feature selection) with the Radon machine to mitigate the challenges of high-dimensional data. 

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound framework for parallel machine learning. However, its practical impact remains uncertain due to the limited experimental evaluation. Addressing the concerns regarding high-dimensional data through either theoretical discussion, experimental results, or a combination of both, would substantially improve the paper's overall strength and appeal.
