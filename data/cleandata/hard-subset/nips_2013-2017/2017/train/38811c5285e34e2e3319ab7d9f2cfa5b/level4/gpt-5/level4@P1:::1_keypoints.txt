- Summary: Introduces the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization that aggregates hypotheses via Radon points up a tree to boost confidence doubly-exponentially and achieve polylogarithmic runtime with enough processors at the cost of more samples.  
- Relation to prior work: Novel alternative to averaging/bagging, positioned within NC-style complexity discussions to show when regularized risk minimization can run in polylog time.  
- Strength: Conceptual novelty—using Radon points for aggregation yields stronger confidence gains than simple averaging.  
- Strength: Black-box applicability—the scheme requires no changes to base learners and works as a module.  
- Strength: Theoretical guarantees—provides PAC-style bounds showing doubly-exponential error decay with tree height and conditions for polylogarithmic runtime.  
- Strength: Empirical performance—reports large speedups (80–700× with 150 processors) while maintaining predictive quality comparable to base learners and Spark MLlib.  
- Weakness: Practicality in high dimensions—Radon number r = d + 2 may make aggregation and required subset sizes impractical as d grows; experiments focus on low-dimensional data.  
- Weakness: Empirical scope—the evaluation does not convincingly address high-dimensional regimes common in modern ML.  
- Quality: Theoretical analysis is well-motivated and appears sound; empirical results support claimed speedups under tested conditions.  
- Clarity: Well-written and organized, but would benefit from explicit discussion/guidelines about implications of r = d + 2 for high-dimensional models.  
- Originality: Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation is original and contrasts with averaging-based techniques; linking to NC complexity is interesting.  
- Significance: Potentially impactful if made practical in high dimensions due to black-box nature and strong guarantees, but current empirical evidence is limited to low dimensions.  
- Suggestions: Add detailed discussion and experiments on high-dimensional behavior, clarify trade-offs from r = d + 2, and recommend strategies for handling high dimensionality.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel aggregation mechanism with strong theory, broad black-box applicability, and substantial empirical speedups.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Unclear applicability to high-dimensional problems and limited empirical evaluation in such regimes.  
- Overall assessment: The paper is an interesting, theoretically solid contribution with promising empirical results; demonstrating and clarifying performance in high-dimensional settings would strengthen it significantly.
