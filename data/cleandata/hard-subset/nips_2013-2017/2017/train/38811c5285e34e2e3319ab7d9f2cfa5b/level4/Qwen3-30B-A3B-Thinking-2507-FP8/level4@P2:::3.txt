

This paper introduces the Radon machine, a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of learning algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processing units while maintaining theoretical performance guarantees. The authors present a theoretically grounded approach to parallelizing consistent and efficient learning algorithms by leveraging Radon points for aggregation, which provides doubly-exponential confidence improvement with each level of aggregation. This work directly addresses an open question regarding efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms in the sense of Nick's Class (NC).

The paper makes several significant contributions. Theoretically, it establishes a framework for parallelizing regularized risk minimization algorithms, showing how the Radon machine reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic while increasing sample complexity only quasi-polynomially. The authors provide rigorous proofs demonstrating that the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis decreases doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree (Theorem 3). Empirically, the Radon machine achieves substantial speedups (80-700x) compared to sequential implementations while maintaining comparable predictive performance (AUC) across multiple datasets and learning algorithms. The evaluation also demonstrates that the Radon machine outperforms both averaging-at-the-end baselines and state-of-the-art Spark MLlib implementations in terms of runtime and predictive quality.

The paper excels in quality, presenting technically sound theoretical analysis supported by comprehensive empirical validation. The authors carefully address the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, acknowledging the trade-off between reduced runtime and increased sample complexity. The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-organized sections that effectively guide readers through the theoretical foundations, algorithmic details, and empirical validation. The Radon machine's black-box nature—requiring no modifications to underlying learning algorithms—makes it broadly applicable to many machine learning tasks.

The originality of this work is significant. While previous parallelization approaches (e.g., averaging-based methods) have been proposed, they lack theoretical guarantees for polylogarithmic runtime. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work, particularly addressing the theoretical limitations of existing methods. Their connection to Nick's Class and the open question about efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms positions this work within a meaningful theoretical context.

The significance of this contribution is substantial. The authors successfully address a fundamental challenge in machine learning: how to scale learning algorithms to handle growing data volumes while maintaining confidence in critical applications. The practical speedups demonstrated in empirical evaluations (particularly on the SUSY dataset with 5 million instances) suggest immediate applicability to real-world machine learning problems. The black-box nature of the Radon machine makes it accessible to practitioners without requiring deep theoretical expertise, potentially accelerating the adoption of parallel learning techniques across the field.

The paper's strengths are numerous and well-supported. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and rigorously proven, with clear connections to existing work in parallel computing and statistical learning theory. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines across diverse datasets and learning tasks (classification, regression, and multi-class classification). The authors preemptively address potential concerns about high-dimensional data, non-linear models, and limited processing units through thoughtful discussion and practical solutions.

Minor concerns include a few typographical errors (e.g., "the the" on line 51, "machineachieved" on line 340) and some presentation issues with figures (small text, potential difficulty distinguishing elements in black-and-white print). The claim about the Radon machine "outperforming the averaging baseline" on all datasets (lines 318-320) could be clarified with more specific statistical evidence. The authors should also ensure consistent citation formatting (e.g., "Long and servedio [22]" should be "Long and Servedio [22]").

The paper meets all four review criteria exceptionally well. It is technically sound with strong theoretical guarantees, clearly written with well-organized sections, significantly advances the field by addressing a fundamental open question, and has substantial practical significance for real-world machine learning applications. The Radon machine's ability to provide substantial speedups without compromising predictive quality represents a meaningful contribution to both the theory and practice of parallel machine learning.

This work provides a compelling step toward answering the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be performed in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors. By demonstrating both theoretical guarantees and practical effectiveness, the authors have made a significant contribution to the field that will likely influence future research on parallel machine learning algorithms. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and addresses a critical challenge in machine learning scalability.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2017. The Radon machine represents a substantial theoretical and practical advance that addresses a fundamental limitation in machine learning parallelization, with clear potential for real-world impact.
