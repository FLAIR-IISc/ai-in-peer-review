## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically sound and practically promising approach to parallelizing regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea of utilizing Radon points for aggregation offers a compelling alternative to averaging-based methods and achieves a significant reduction in runtime under reasonable assumptions. However, the paper feels somewhat dense and lacks a detailed discussion of limitations and practical considerations beyond the presented experiments. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on addressing the concerns outlined below.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the critical problem of scaling machine learning algorithms to large datasets. It proposes the “Radon machine,” a parallelization scheme that leverages the properties of Radon points to achieve a polylogarithmic runtime reduction compared to sequential execution, at the cost of increased data and processing units. The key contribution lies in providing provable guarantees for this parallelization, showing that it maintains the same confidence and error bounds as the sequential algorithm.

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of parallel machine learning. They acknowledge averaging-based approaches (e.g., [32, 45, 46]) and highlight the advantages of Radon point aggregation – specifically, its doubly-exponential confidence increase. The connection to Nick’s Class [9] and the limitations of P-complete problems are appropriately noted, providing valuable context. The related work section, while not exhaustive, covers relevant concepts like Rademacher complexity, convex optimization, and parallel computing.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides concrete bounds on sample complexity and runtime. The proof of Theorem 3 is particularly compelling, demonstrating the doubly-exponential improvement in confidence.
* **Practical Relevance:** The empirical results demonstrate significant speedups in realistic scenarios using a Spark cluster. The comparison to Spark's MLlib shows the Radon machine can achieve comparable performance with reduced runtime.
* **Black-Box Applicability:** The algorithm's ability to treat the underlying learning algorithm as a black box is a major strength, increasing its versatility and ease of implementation.
* **Novelty:** The use of Radon points for parallel aggregation in the context of machine learning is a novel idea and represents a significant contribution.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the problem of effective parallelization and establishes a precise notion of what it means for a parallelization scheme to be "effective."

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Density & Accessibility:** The paper is quite dense and requires a strong background in theoretical machine learning and convex optimization. The numerous equations and proofs, while rigorous, can make it challenging for a broader audience to follow the core ideas.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Constraints:** The paper glosses over practical challenges beyond the presented experiments. For example:
    * **Communication Costs:** While mentioned briefly, the communication cost associated with aggregating Radon points, especially in distributed environments with limited bandwidth, deserves a more thorough discussion.
    * **Radon Number Calculation:**  Calculating Radon points can be computationally expensive, particularly in high-dimensional spaces. The paper mentions a system of linear equations, but doesn't detail the computational complexity of solving it in practice.
    * **Handling Non-Convex Problems:** The paper explicitly focuses on convex optimization. A discussion of how the approach might be adapted or extended to non-convex problems would be valuable.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to a relatively small set of datasets and algorithms. Expanding the evaluation to more diverse scenarios, including datasets with high dimensionality and non-linear models, would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **"Quasi-Polynomial" Vagueness:** The term "quasi-polynomial" is used frequently but lacks precise definition.  Clarifying the specific dependence on parameters (e.g., 1/ε, log(1/Δ)) would enhance the clarity of the analysis.
* **Minor Typo/Formatting:** Several minor typos are present (e.g., "rh iid subsets Di" – should be "r iid subsets D1...Drh").

**4. Significance & Originality:**

The paper’s significance lies in its potential to enable the efficient training of machine learning models on extremely large datasets. The theoretical guarantees provide confidence in the reliability of the parallelized algorithms.  The originality of applying Radon points to this problem is substantial. While averaging is a common technique, the Radon point aggregation provides a provably superior convergence rate.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong theoretical guarantees.
* Demonstrated practical speedups.
* Novel approach to parallelization.
* Black-box applicability.
* Addresses a critical problem in scaling machine learning.

**Con:**

* Density and potential lack of accessibility for a broader audience.
* Limited discussion of practical constraints and scalability challenges.
* Relatively narrow experimental scope.
* “Quasi-polynomial” lacks precision.



**6. Recommendations for Revision:**

* **Improve Clarity:**  Consider adding more intuitive explanations and visualizations to illustrate the Radon point aggregation process.  Break down complex equations into smaller steps and provide more context.
* **Address Practical Concerns:**  Expand the discussion of communication costs, Radon point calculation complexity, and potential adaptations for non-convex problems.
* **Expand Experiments:**  Include experiments with high-dimensional datasets, non-linear models, and different cluster configurations to assess the algorithm's scalability and robustness.
* **Clarify “Quasi-Polynomial”:** Define the specific dependence of the quasi-polynomial terms on relevant parameters (e.g., 1/ε, log(1/Δ)).
* **Minor Edits:**  Correct the identified typos and ensure consistent formatting.




In conclusion, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. While some improvements in clarity and a more comprehensive discussion of practical considerations would strengthen the paper, the strong theoretical foundation, promising empirical results, and novel approach warrant acceptance.
