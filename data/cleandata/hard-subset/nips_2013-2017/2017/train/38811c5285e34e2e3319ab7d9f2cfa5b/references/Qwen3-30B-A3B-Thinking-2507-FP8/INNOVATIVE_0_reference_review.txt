

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime complexity while maintaining the same statistical guarantees as sequential execution. The key insight is using Radon points for aggregation instead of averaging, which leads to doubly exponential confidence improvements with each aggregation level. The authors prove theoretically that their scheme reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity, while requiring only quasi-polynomially many processors and slightly more data.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a theoretically grounded approach to parallel machine learning that addresses a fundamental question in the field: whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be performed in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors. This connects machine learning to Nick's Class (NC) of parallelizable problems, a significant theoretical contribution. Second, the Radon Machine is a black-box parallelization scheme that works without modifying the underlying learning algorithm, making it broadly applicable. Third, the empirical results demonstrate impressive speedups (80-700x) while maintaining comparable predictive performance to sequential implementations and state-of-the-art Spark MLlib algorithms.

The theoretical foundation is strong, with Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 establishing the key probabilistic guarantees. The Radon point aggregation approach is clearly differentiated from previous averaging-based parallelization methods (citing [32, 45, 46]), which cannot achieve the same confidence guarantees with polylogarithmic runtime. The paper also properly contextualizes its work within the broader literature on parallel learning, including the work of Long and Servedio [21] who identified the question as "a fundamental open problem."

The empirical evaluation is well-executed, comparing against both sequential baselines and Spark MLlib implementations. The results on moderately sized datasets (SUSY, HIGGS, CASP9) show clear advantages in runtime with comparable predictive performance. The extension to regression and multi-class classification further demonstrates the approach's generality.

The main limitations of the work are practical rather than theoretical: the approach requires sufficient processing units (rh) and works best with low-dimensional data (though the paper discusses potential workarounds using dimensionality reduction). The empirical evaluation is limited to linear models and moderate dataset sizes, though the authors appropriately acknowledge these as directions for future work.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical contributions, I find this paper exceptionally compelling. The Radon Machine represents a genuinely new approach to parallel machine learning with significant theoretical implications. The key insight of using Radon points for aggregation to achieve doubly exponential confidence improvements is both elegant and powerful. While the empirical results are promising, it's the theoretical contribution that truly sets this paper apart - it provides a concrete step toward answering the fundamental open question about parallel machine learning in Nick's Class.

The paper's black-box nature is particularly valuable for practitioners, as it allows parallelization without modifying existing learning algorithms. This makes the approach immediately applicable to a wide range of machine learning tasks, which is a significant practical advantage.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear technical contributions and a thorough empirical evaluation. The theoretical proofs are rigorous, and the relationship to prior work is properly established.

I acknowledge that the paper has some limitations, particularly regarding the practical constraints of requiring many processors and the focus on low-dimensional data. However, as a reviewer who values novel concepts over methodological perfection, I believe these limitations are natural for a bold new approach and should not overshadow the paper's significant theoretical contribution. The Radon Machine represents a genuinely new direction in parallel machine learning with the potential to influence both theory and practice.

The paper makes a strong case for its significance, addressing a fundamental open problem in the field and providing a theoretically sound solution that is also empirically validated. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the field of machine learning that advances both the theoretical understanding of parallel learning and provides practical benefits for practitioners.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical contribution addressing a fundamental open problem in parallel machine learning
- Radon point aggregation provides doubly exponential confidence improvements
- Black-box parallelization scheme applicable to a wide range of algorithms
- Strong theoretical guarantees with rigorous proofs
- Significant empirical speedups (80-700x) with comparable predictive performance
- Clear differentiation from previous work on parallel learning

**Weaknesses:**
- Practical constraints related to processor requirements and dimensionality
- Limited empirical evaluation to moderate-sized datasets and linear models
- Limited discussion of communication complexity in distributed settings

**Overall:** This paper presents a bold, novel approach to parallel machine learning with significant theoretical and practical implications. The Radon Machine is a genuinely new concept that addresses a fundamental open problem in the field. While there are practical limitations, the theoretical contribution is substantial and the empirical results are compelling. The paper advances the state of the art in parallel machine learning in a demonstrable way and is likely to influence both theoretical research and practical implementations.
