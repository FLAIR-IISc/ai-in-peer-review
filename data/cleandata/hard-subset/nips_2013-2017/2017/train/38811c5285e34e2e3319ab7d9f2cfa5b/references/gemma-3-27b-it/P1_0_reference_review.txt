## NIPS 2017 Review: Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea leverages Radon points to aggregate hypotheses generated from parallel computations on data subsets. The authors demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that this scheme can achieve polylogarithmic runtime complexity (compared to polynomial for sequential execution) at the cost of increased data usage and processing units. The work aims to address the challenge of scaling machine learning algorithms to increasingly large datasets, particularly in high-stakes applications where confidence in results is paramount. This builds on existing averaging-based parallelization methods by offering faster convergence through the use of Radon points.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within the landscape of parallel machine learning, acknowledging related work on averaging-based approaches (e.g., [32, 45, 46]) and distributed optimization (e.g., [34]). It draws connections to theoretical computer science concepts like Nick’s Class [9] and P-completeness, framing the contribution as a step towards efficient parallelization of polynomial-time learning algorithms. The paper also references relevant work on convex optimization, Radon’s theorem, and low-dimensional embeddings as potential pre-processing steps. The authors clearly situate their work within a broader context, referencing recent advances like federated learning [24] and distributed SGD [10].

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the Radon Machine, including bounds on sample complexity, runtime, and confidence guarantees. Theorem 3 and Theorem 4 are particularly strong contributions, formalizing the polylogarithmic runtime achieved through parallelization.
* **Black-Box Applicability:** A significant strength is the “black-box” nature of the scheme. It can be applied to a wide range of consistent and efficient learning algorithms without requiring modifications to the base learner. This broad applicability is a key advantage.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate a substantial speedup (80-700x) compared to the sequential execution of base learners, confirming the theoretical benefits. The comparison with Spark’s MLlib algorithms shows competitive performance in terms of quality and runtime.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The concepts are explained clearly, and the notation is consistent. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the Radon Machine.
* **Addressing a Crucial Problem:** Scaling machine learning algorithms to massive datasets is a fundamental challenge. This work provides a valuable contribution to addressing this problem, especially in applications requiring high confidence.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Quasi-Polynomial Processing Units:**  The polylogarithmic runtime comes at the cost of a *quasi*-polynomial number of processing units. While the theoretical benefits are significant, the practical feasibility may be limited by the availability of such a large number of processors. The paper touches on simulating this with fewer processors, but a more in-depth discussion of the implications would be beneficial.
* **Radon Number Dependency:** The runtime complexity depends on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. The authors acknowledge this and mention low-dimensional embeddings as a potential solution for high-dimensional data, but a detailed discussion of practical limitations for different hypothesis spaces would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** While the empirical evaluation is convincing, it's somewhat limited to moderately sized datasets for the base learners.  Extending the experiments to even larger datasets, and exploring scenarios with limited processing units would further validate the scheme’s practical value.
* **Communication Complexity not fully addressed:** The discussion of communication complexity is brief. While it’s stated as relatively low, a more detailed analysis, particularly in a distributed environment, would be valuable.
* **Comparison to state-of-the-art is not always fair**: Comparisons to Spark MLlib are done using WEKA and scikit-learn as base learners. Spark MLlib is written from the ground up for distributed computing, providing opportunities for optimizations not available to these base learners.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound parallelization scheme with significant potential for improving the scalability of machine learning algorithms.
* The black-box nature of the approach makes it broadly applicable.
* The empirical results confirm the theoretical benefits and demonstrate competitive performance.
* The work addresses a crucial problem in machine learning and contributes to a growing body of research on parallel and distributed learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units may limit practical applicability.
* The dependence on the Radon number of the hypothesis space could be a limitation for certain data types and models.
* The scope of the empirical evaluation could be broadened.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. While the quasi-polynomial processing unit requirement is a limitation, the theoretical guarantees and empirical results are compelling. The black-box nature of the approach is a major advantage, and the work addresses a critical challenge in machine learning.

**Rating:** **Accept (Strongly)**

**Confidence:** 8/10. The paper’s strengths clearly outweigh its weaknesses, and it has the potential to make a significant impact on the field. Minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above could further strengthen the paper.
