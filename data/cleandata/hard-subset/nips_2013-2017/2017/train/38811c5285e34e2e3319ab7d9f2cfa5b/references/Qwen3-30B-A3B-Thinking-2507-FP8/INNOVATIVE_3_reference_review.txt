

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme called the "Radon Machine" that achieves a dramatic reduction in runtime for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The key insight is using Radon points (from convex geometry) to aggregate hypotheses from parallel learning runs, which provides a doubly exponential improvement in confidence with each aggregation level. The authors prove that this scheme reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in sample size while maintaining the same error guarantees as sequential learning, requiring only a quasi-polynomial number of processing units.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting parallel machine learning to Nick's Class (NC) and addressing the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be achieved in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors. The Radon point aggregation mechanism represents a bold departure from the common practice of averaging hypotheses, with the authors demonstrating theoretically that Radon points are significantly more robust to bad hypotheses than averages.

The theoretical analysis is clean and well-structured. Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 provide strong guarantees about the confidence improvement, showing that the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis is reduced doubly exponentially with each aggregation level. The proof techniques using quasi-convex functions and Radon partitions are elegant and appropriately rigorous. The authors correctly position their work in relation to Long and Servedio's fundamental open problem about parallel learning of half-spaces.

The empirical evaluation is compelling, showing 80-700x speedups on real datasets using 150 processors while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The comparison to Spark MLlib implementations and to the averaging-at-the-end baseline demonstrates the practical advantages of their approach. The authors correctly note that the Radon machine outperforms averaging in terms of predictive performance while being only slightly slower in runtime (due to the slightly higher computational cost of Radon points versus averages).

Strengths:
- Truly novel approach that fundamentally changes how we think about parallelizing learning algorithms
- Strong theoretical guarantees with clear connections to computational complexity theory
- Impressive empirical speedups that validate the theoretical claims
- Black-box nature of the parallelization makes it widely applicable to many learning algorithms
- Clear connection to an important open question in parallel learning (Nick's Class)

Weaknesses:
- The quasi-polynomial number of processors requirement is substantial, though the authors acknowledge this
- The Radon number r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces means high-dimensional problems may require many processors
- The empirical evaluation is limited to linear models and moderately sized datasets
- The paper doesn't fully address how to handle datasets where the size isn't a multiple of the Radon number

I believe the paper's novelty and bold theoretical contribution outweigh these limitations. The authors have made a genuinely new contribution to the field of parallel machine learning that addresses a fundamental question about the limits of parallelization. While the practical implementation might face some challenges with high-dimensional data, the core insight is profound and has significant theoretical importance.

This paper stands out from the typical incremental parallelization papers by offering a fundamentally new approach with strong theoretical guarantees. The authors successfully demonstrate that parallelization isn't just about splitting data but about fundamentally changing how we aggregate results to achieve confidence improvements. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values.

I would recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning, provides a genuinely new algorithmic technique, and demonstrates impressive empirical results. The paper is well-written, clear, and properly contextualized within the relevant literature.

The main weakness is that the authors don't fully address the high-dimensional case in practice, but this is a limitation of the approach rather than a flaw in the theoretical contribution. The paper provides a clear path forward for handling high-dimensional data through techniques like random projections, which the authors already mention in Section 5.

In summary, this paper offers a novel, theoretically grounded, and practically effective approach to parallel machine learning that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. It addresses a fundamental question in parallel learning with a bold new perspective, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
