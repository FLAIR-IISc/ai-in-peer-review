## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially impactful approach to parallelizing regularized risk minimization algorithms. While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the presentation could be improved for clarity, and the practical evaluation, while promising, feels somewhat limited in scope. I lean towards **accepting** with minor revisions, primarily focusing on clarifying the exposition and expanding the empirical analysis.

**Summary:** The paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a parallelization scheme for consistent and efficient learning algorithms. The core idea is to run the base learning algorithm on random subsets of the data, then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points. The authors prove that this approach can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic, at the cost of increased data size and processing units. They demonstrate empirical speedups on several datasets, outperforming Spark’s MLlib in some cases.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including bounds on sample complexity and runtime. The proof of polylogarithmic runtime is a significant result, addressing a fundamental question in parallel machine learning.
*   **Generality:** The Radon Machine is presented as a “black-box” parallelization scheme, applicable to a broad class of learning algorithms satisfying basic consistency and efficiency requirements. This is a major advantage.
*   **Provable Guarantees:** The paper provides formal guarantees on the (ε, δ) performance of the parallelized algorithm, ensuring it maintains the same confidence level as sequential execution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups compared to sequential execution and competitive performance against Spark’s MLlib.
*   **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Efficiently parallelizing machine learning algorithms is crucial for handling ever-increasing datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is dense and at times difficult to follow. Concepts like Radon points are introduced without sufficient intuition for a general NIPS audience.  More illustrative examples and a more gradual introduction of the mathematical machinery would improve readability.  The connection to Nick’s Class, while interesting, feels somewhat tacked on and could be more clearly explained.
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation focuses heavily on a few datasets. Expanding the evaluation to a wider range of datasets, including high-dimensional data (and addressing the limitations discussed in the paper), would strengthen the claims.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper acknowledges the need for a sufficient number of processing units (rh). A more detailed discussion of how the scheme performs with a limited number of processors (i.e., rh/c > 1) and the associated performance trade-offs would be beneficial.
*   **Communication Costs:** While the paper briefly mentions communication complexity, a more detailed analysis of the communication overhead involved in the Radon point aggregation step would be valuable. This is particularly important for distributed environments.
*   **Comparison to Averaging:** While the paper shows improvements over simple averaging, it would be beneficial to discuss why the Radon point aggregation is superior in more detail, beyond just the doubly-exponential confidence increase.

**Review Criteria Breakdown:**

*   **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis. The mathematical arguments appear rigorous. It’s a complete piece of work in terms of theoretical development, but the practical side feels less fleshed out.
*   **Clarity:** This is the weakest aspect. The paper is difficult to read and understand without a strong background in convex optimization and related fields.  The notation is dense, and the explanations could be more intuitive.
*   **Originality:** The approach of using Radon points for parallel aggregation is novel and a clever application of existing geometric concepts to machine learning. The connection to the theoretical limits of parallel computation is also interesting.
*   **Significance:** The results are potentially significant, as they offer a provably effective way to parallelize a broad class of learning algorithms. This could have a substantial impact on the scalability of machine learning applications.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical guarantees of polylogarithmic runtime.
*   Generality and applicability to a wide range of learning algorithms.
*   Empirical evidence of substantial speedups.
*   Addresses a critical challenge in large-scale machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity of exposition needs significant improvement.
*   Empirical evaluation is limited in scope.
*   Practical considerations (processor requirements, communication costs) are not fully addressed.



**Recommendation:** **Accept with minor revisions.** The authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, expanding the empirical evaluation to include more diverse datasets, and providing a more detailed discussion of the practical considerations involved in deploying the Radon Machine. Addressing these points will significantly enhance the impact of this promising work.
