### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to partition the data, train a base learner on each partition in parallel to obtain "weak" hypotheses, and then aggregate these hypotheses hierarchically. The key innovation lies in the aggregation step, which uses the concept of a Radon point—a point in the intersection of the convex hulls of a partition of a set of points. The authors prove that this aggregation method boosts the confidence in the resulting hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. This strong theoretical result allows them to show that their scheme can reduce the runtime of polynomial-time learning algorithms to polylogarithmic, at the cost of using a quasi-polynomial number of processors and more data. The paper connects this result to the theoretical computer science question of whether learning problems are in Nick's Class (NC). Empirically, the Radon Machine is shown to be significantly faster than sequential base learners and competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art parallel implementations in Spark MLlib in both runtime and predictive accuracy on several datasets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high quality, presenting a theoretically sound and well-motivated algorithm. The core theoretical result (Theorem 3), which establishes the doubly-exponential decrease in the probability of generating a bad hypothesis, is powerful and elegant. The subsequent derivation of polylogarithmic runtime (Theorem 4) is a direct and significant consequence. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against appropriate baselines including sequential learners, simple averaging, and industry-standard parallel frameworks like Spark MLlib. The results strongly support the claims of practical speed-up and effectiveness.

However, there are some weaknesses that temper the claims:
1.  **High-Dimensionality Curse:** The method's complexity is critically dependent on the Radon number `r` of the hypothesis space, which for `R^d` is `d+2`. The number of processors (`r^h`) and the total sample size required grow exponentially with the dimension `d`. The paper acknowledges this and suggests dimensionality reduction as a remedy, but this is a major practical limitation for many modern machine learning problems (e.g., in NLP or with one-hot encoded features) where `d` can be very large. The experiments are conducted on datasets with low dimensionality (e.g., `d=18` for SUSY), which is precisely the setting where the method is most viable. The paper would be strengthened by a more direct confrontation with this limitation, perhaps with an experiment showing how performance degrades as `d` increases.
2.  **Restrictive Assumption:** The analysis in Section 3 requires that the base learner's failure probability `δ` satisfies `δ ≤ 1/2r`. Since `r = d+2`, this means the base learner, trained on a small subset of the data, must already be very accurate, especially in high dimensions. This assumption seems to limit the applicability to "high-confidence" scenarios and may not hold for complex problems where base learners on small data partitions are truly weak.
3.  **Proof Sketch Precision:** The proof for Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 in Section 7 is a bit imprecise. The argument is that if the Radon point `r` is `ε-bad`, at least two of the input hypotheses must have been `ε-bad`. The paper then bounds the probability of this by `(r * P(Q(F) > ε))^2`. A more standard application of the union bound over all `(r choose 2)` pairs of hypotheses would yield a bound of `(r choose 2) * (P(Q(F) > ε))^2`. While these bounds are of the same order `O(r^2)`, the derivation in the paper is non-standard and could be clarified.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, Algorithm 1 is easy to understand, and the theoretical development is presented logically. The connection to established concepts in computational geometry (Radon's Theorem) and complexity theory (Nick's Class) is well-explained and adds valuable context. The empirical results are presented clearly in figures and tables. Using the Appendix for detailed proofs and experimental setup is appropriate.

**Originality:** The core contribution—using iterated Radon points to aggregate hypotheses for parallel learning—is highly original. While divide-and-conquer and model averaging are common paradigms in parallel ML, the use of a Radon point aggregator is novel. Unlike simple averaging, which can be sensitive to outliers, the Radon point provides a more robust aggregation mechanism, which is elegantly captured by the theoretical analysis. This work successfully bridges concepts from computational geometry and learning theory to address a fundamental problem in parallel computation.

**Significance:** The paper makes significant contributions on both theoretical and practical fronts.
-   **Theoretically,** it provides a new, positive result on the parallelizability of learning, making a concrete step toward answering the open question of whether regularized risk minimization is in NC (or a related class). The doubly-exponential confidence amplification is a powerful tool that could inspire further research.
-   **Practically,** it offers a "black-box" parallelization scheme that does not require modification of the underlying learning algorithm. This is a huge advantage for practitioners. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating that the Radon Machine can be substantially faster than highly optimized libraries like Spark MLlib while achieving comparable or better accuracy. This suggests the method could have a real-world impact, particularly in settings with low-to-moderate dimensional data where massive parallelism is available.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

-   It would be beneficial to be more upfront about the distinction between the standard NC class (polynomial processors) and the requirements of the Radon Machine (quasi-polynomial processors). While mentioned, the implications of this stronger requirement could be discussed in more detail.
-   The comparison with Spark MLlib could be more specific. What algorithms were used in Spark (e.g., L-BFGS for Logistic Regression, Distributed Dual Coordinate Ascent for SVMs)? Understanding this would help contextualize the performance comparison, as Spark's methods are often specialized parallel solvers for the global optimization problem, whereas the Radon Machine is an aggregation scheme.
-   Given the `r=d+2` dependency, a brief discussion on alternative notions of convexity and their associated Radon numbers [16, 33] might be interesting, even if only as a direction for future work to tackle the high-dimensionality issue.

### Recommendation

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, theoretically principled, and practically effective method for parallelizing machine learning algorithms. The originality of using Radon points for aggregation and the strength of the resulting theoretical guarantees are major highlights. While the method has significant limitations with respect to data dimensionality, these do not invalidate the core contribution. The paper is well-written, the results are significant, and it is of high interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
-   Presents a highly novel and elegant parallelization scheme based on Radon points.
-   Provides a strong theoretical result (doubly-exponential confidence boosting) leading to a provable reduction in runtime to polylogarithmic.
-   Addresses a fundamental open problem in parallel learning theory.
-   Demonstrates impressive practical performance, outperforming strong baselines including Spark MLlib in extensive experiments.
-   The "black-box" nature of the method makes it widely and easily applicable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
-   The method's performance and sample complexity scale poorly with the dimensionality `d` of the hypothesis space, a major limitation not fully explored empirically.
-   The theoretical analysis relies on a potentially restrictive assumption (`δ ≤ 1/2r`) on the accuracy of the base learners.
-   The claimed polylogarithmic runtime requires a quasi-polynomial number of processors, a very strong computational model.
