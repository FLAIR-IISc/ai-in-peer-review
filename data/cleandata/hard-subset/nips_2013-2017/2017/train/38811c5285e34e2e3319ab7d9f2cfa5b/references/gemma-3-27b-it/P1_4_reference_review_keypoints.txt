- Presents the "Radon machine": apply base learner to random data subsets and iteratively aggregate hypotheses via Radon points to parallelise regularised risk minimisation.
- Theoretically shows runtime can drop from polynomial to polylogarithmic at the cost of increased data size and many processing units.
- Empirical results on Spark clusters show substantial runtime speedups versus the sequential learner and MLlib, with comparable predictive performance.
- Positions work among averaging-based parallel methods, claiming Radon points give doubly-exponential confidence gains over simple averaging.
- Relates to theoretical limits of parallelisation (e.g., Nick’s Class) and builds on distributed SGD and other parallel learning literature.
- Strong theoretical foundation with formal proofs, notably Theorem 3’s doubly-exponential confidence increase per iteration.
- Black-box applicability: works with any consistent, efficient regularised risk minimiser without algorithm-specific changes.
- Empirically validated with realistic Spark experiments and informative runtime-scaling analysis (Figure 3).
- Clear, well-structured presentation and algorithm description; useful definitions improve clarity.
- Authors acknowledge limitations and outline future work, showing awareness of broader challenges.
- Reliance on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units may limit practical applicability in resource-constrained settings.
- Performance depends on the Radon number of the hypothesis space, which can be large for complex spaces and reduce benefits.
- Communication complexity is only briefly discussed and lacks quantitative analysis, risking hidden distributed bottlenecks.
- Experimental scope is limited to well-behaved datasets; performance on high-dimensional, imbalanced, or complex datasets is untested.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically effective, broadly applicable, and addresses a challenging problem.
- Arguments against acceptance: impractical processor requirements, Radon-number dependency, and insufficient communication-cost analysis.
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant contributions; strengths outweigh weaknesses; recommends acceptance to NIPS 2017.
- Minor suggestions: give a concrete trade-off example between runtime/data/processors, expand communication-complexity discussion, discuss cost of computing Radon points by dimension, and add methods to verify a space’s Radon number.
