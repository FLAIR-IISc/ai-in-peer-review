**Summary**

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel black-box parallelisation scheme for a broad class of regularised risk minimisation algorithms. The proposed method operates by dividing the dataset into numerous subsets, running a base learning algorithm on each subset to obtain a set of initial hypotheses, and then hierarchically aggregating these hypotheses. The key innovation lies in the aggregation step, which uses the concept of a Radon point—a point in the intersection of the convex hulls of a partition of the hypothesis set. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this aggregation strategy boosts the confidence (i.e., reduces the failure probability) doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. This theoretical result is leveraged to claim that the Radon Machine can reduce the runtime of polynomial-time learners to polylogarithmic, at the expense of a quasi-polynomial number of processors and an increased sample size. The paper supports these claims with an empirical study demonstrating significant speed-ups over sequential learners and competitive performance against established parallel learning libraries like Spark MLlib.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty:** The central idea of using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation is, to my knowledge, new in the context of parallel machine learning. It is an elegant theoretical concept that provides a principled alternative to simple averaging, which is known to have limitations.
2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The derivation of a doubly-exponential improvement in the confidence bound (Theorem 3) is a strong and interesting theoretical result. It provides a solid justification for the aggregation strategy beyond simple intuition.
3.  **Broader Context:** The paper successfully connects its contribution to the fundamental question of parallelisability in computational complexity theory (specifically, Nick's Class), positioning the work as a step toward understanding the limits of efficient parallel learning.
4.  **Practical Potential:** The method's black-box nature is a significant practical advantage, allowing it to be applied to many existing learning algorithms without modification. The empirical results, showing speed-ups of 80-700x, are compelling and suggest the method could be highly impactful in practice.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novel core idea and promising results, the paper suffers from a significant number of flaws ranging from minor formatting issues to substantial technical omissions and a lack of precision in its central claims.

1.  **Clarity and Technical Precision:**
    *   The claim of achieving polylogarithmic runtime on "quasi-polynomially many processing units" is not substantiated with sufficient rigor. The number of required processors is `c = r^h`. For the chosen `h ≈ ld M`, this becomes `c ≈ r^(ld M) = M^(ld r)`. Given that the Radon number `r = d+2` for `R^d`, the number of processors is `M^(ld(d+2))`. This is polynomial in `M` only if `d` is constant. If `d` is polylogarithmic in `M`, the number of processors becomes quasi-polynomial (`M^polylog(log(M))`), and if `d` is polynomial in `M`, it is exponential. The paper fails to state these dependencies and assumptions clearly, using the term "quasi-polynomial" loosely.
    *   The sample complexity notation `NL0(ε, δ) = (αε + βε ld 1/δ)^k` is unconventional. The subscripts on `α` and `β` suggest they are functions of `ε`, but this is not explained. Standard forms typically separate terms related to `ε` and `δ`.
    *   There is a lack of clarity regarding sample consumption. The introduction claims the scheme "consumes more data," which is true for the practical setup where `n` is small (`n ≈ ld M`), leading to a total data size of `r^h n ≈ M^(ld r) ld M`. However, the formal sample complexity analysis in Section 3 (`NR0` vs. `NL0`) does not make it obvious that the Radon machine necessarily requires more data than an equivalent sequential learner. This disconnect between theory and practice should be explicitly addressed.
    *   The proof of Proposition 2 in Section 7 is overly brief. It attributes the result to a union bound over pairs, but a more explicit derivation would be appropriate for a key result. A union bound over all `(r choose 2)` pairs would clarify the origin of the `r^2` term.

2.  **Understated Limitations and Assumptions:**
    *   The most significant weakness is the paper's handling of the dependency on dimensionality `d`. The entire theoretical framework and the main result (Theorem 4) hinge on the Radon number `r`, which is `d+2`. The theorem's condition that `r` must be "upper bounded by a function polylogarithmic in the sample complexity" is a critical restriction that confines the theoretical guarantees to low-dimensional problems. This is a major limitation for modern machine learning. While the discussion section briefly mentions dimensionality reduction techniques, this is an insufficient treatment of a central issue that fundamentally challenges the method's applicability. The costs associated with calculating the Radon point (`O(r^3) = O(d^3)`) are also non-trivial and understated.
    *   The choice of `h=max` in the experiments, defined by a minimum subset size of 100, is purely heuristic and disconnected from the theory. The paper provides no justification for this choice or analysis of its effect on the `(ε,Δ)`-guarantees.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:**
    *   The comparison against Spark MLlib is potentially misleading. The Radon Machine, as implemented, acts as a wrapper that parallelises single-threaded base learners (from WEKA/scikit-learn). Spark MLlib algorithms, in contrast, are often natively distributed implementations with fine-grained communication and parallelisation. The reported speed-up may stem more from the straightforward "embarrassingly parallel" nature of the initial training phase rather than the superiority of the overall Radon Machine approach compared to other advanced distributed learning algorithms.
    *   The paper notes the poor performance of all methods on the `wikidata` dataset but provides no investigation. This is a missed opportunity to understand the failure modes or boundary conditions of the proposed method.

4.  **Formatting and Professionalism:**
    *   The presence of a conference header ("st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)...") on the first page is inappropriate for a manuscript under review and suggests a lack of careful preparation.
    *   Crucial details, including the proof of the main complexity result (Theorem 4) and the algorithm for computing a Radon point, are relegated to appendices. While standard, the extent to which the paper relies on appendices for core technical components makes the main text difficult to assess fully.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and theoretically elegant method for parallelising ML algorithms.
*   The doubly-exponential confidence bound is a strong and non-trivial theoretical contribution.
*   Empirical results demonstrate impressive speed-ups in practice.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Central theoretical claims about runtime and processor complexity lack rigor and depend on strong, understated assumptions about data dimensionality.
*   The severe impact of dimensionality `d` on both the number of processors and the aggregation runtime is not adequately addressed.
*   Numerous points of unclarity in the theoretical analysis and a disconnect between theory and experimental setup.
*   The paper exhibits a general lack of meticulousness in its presentation and argumentation.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the core idea of the Radon Machine is innovative and potentially valuable, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. The lack of rigor in the complexity analysis, the glossing over of the critical dependency on dimensionality, and the accumulation of other minor flaws significantly weaken the contribution. The authors should undertake a major revision to address these issues by providing a more precise and honest assessment of their method's complexity and its domain of applicability before resubmission.
