This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, termed the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms, aiming to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic by increasing data size and processing units. The core idea leverages Radon points for aggregating hypotheses obtained from parallel processing of data subsets. The theoretical contribution establishes conditions under which this parallelization is “effective,” preserving confidence and error bounds. The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups compared to base learners and Spark’s MLlib, with comparable predictive performance.

The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in machine learning: efficiently scaling algorithms to large datasets. The motivation, particularly regarding applications where errors are costly, is clearly articulated. The authors correctly position their work within the context of parallel computing and existing parallelization approaches like averaging-based methods. The theoretical framework, built on consistent and efficient learning algorithms and the properties of Radon points, appears sound and well-developed. The use of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 to formally characterize the error bounds and runtime reduction is commendable. The empirical validation, while limited in scope (datasets, base learners), provides encouraging results demonstrating substantial speedups. The paper is well-written and logically organized, and the connection to Nick's Class is an interesting and thought-provoking addition.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. Firstly, the assumption of a finite Radon number, while mathematically convenient, may limit its applicability to certain hypothesis spaces or require data preprocessing (low-dimensional embeddings) which introduces additional complexity and potential information loss. This limitation isn't fully discussed or explored beyond a brief mention. Secondly, the theoretical analysis focuses on asymptotic behavior. While proving polylogarithmic runtime is valuable, the practical constants involved – particularly related to the Radon number and the scaling with data size – are not adequately addressed.  A more detailed discussion of these practical considerations is necessary. Thirdly, the comparison with Spark’s MLlib is somewhat superficial. While the Radon machine is faster, a more granular comparison considering specific algorithms and hyperparameter tuning could provide more insightful results. Moreover, the datasets used are relatively small in comparison to the scale at which many modern machine learning problems are tackled. Demonstrating the scheme's effectiveness on truly massive datasets would significantly strengthen the claims. Finally, the discussion of communication complexity is brief and lacks quantitative analysis.  In distributed learning scenarios, communication costs can often dominate runtime, and a more detailed assessment of this aspect is crucial. The lack of a detailed discussion regarding the practical implications of the `rh/c` factor when the number of processors is less than the ideal is also a limitation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound parallelization scheme with a provable runtime reduction.
*   Clear presentation and strong mathematical foundation.
*   Empirically demonstrates substantial speedups compared to baseline methods.
*   Addresses an important problem in scaling machine learning algorithms.
*   Connection to relevant theoretical concepts in parallel computing (Nick’s Class).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited applicability due to the finite Radon number assumption.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of practical constants and scalability.
*   Superficial comparison with existing parallel learning frameworks.
*   Limited dataset size in empirical evaluation.
*   Insufficient discussion of communication complexity and the implications of `rh/c` factor.

**Suggestions for improvement:**

*   Expand the discussion of the Radon number assumption and explore potential workarounds for hypothesis spaces with infinite Radon numbers.
*   Provide a more detailed analysis of the practical constants involved in the runtime reduction.
*   Conduct a more thorough comparison with Spark’s MLlib and other state-of-the-art parallel learning frameworks, including a discussion of hyperparameter tuning and algorithm selection.
*   Evaluate the scheme on larger, more representative datasets.
*   Include a quantitative analysis of the communication complexity and its impact on overall performance.
*   Elaborate on the practical implications of using fewer than `rh` processors and provide guidance on selecting appropriate parameters.
*   Consider adding an analysis of the sensitivity of the method to noise or outliers in the data.



**Overall assessment:** Reject with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper presents a promising and theoretically interesting approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms. However, the limitations outlined above need to be addressed before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. Addressing the suggested improvements would substantially strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
