This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, dubbed the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea leverages Radon points for aggregation, achieving a theoretical runtime reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic, albeit at the cost of increased data and processing units. The authors convincingly argue for the broad applicability of their scheme as a black-box approach, applicable to various learning algorithms. This is a bold and exciting direction, and the potential impact on scaling machine learning is significant.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Radon points to parallel learning is a genuinely novel idea. While parallelization is well-trodden ground, this approach offers a distinct theoretical advantage—a potential shift from polynomial to polylogarithmic runtime—which is rarely seen.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, including proofs for the consistency and efficiency of the Radon machine. The analysis regarding the trade-off between runtime, data size, and processing units is comprehensive.
*   **Generality:** The “black-box” nature of the scheme is a major strength. The authors explicitly state it doesn't require modifications to the underlying learning algorithm.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups, particularly with a larger number of processors, and comparable performance to state-of-the-art Spark implementations. The comparison to averaging-at-the-end further highlights the benefits of using Radon points.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the Radon machine and its theoretical underpinnings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practicality of Quasi-Polynomial Processing Units:** The requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units is a significant practical limitation. While the paper acknowledges this and discusses scenarios with fewer processors, the theoretical gain is largely predicated on having sufficient parallelism. This needs to be more thoroughly addressed.
*   **Radon Number Dependence:** The performance is highly dependent on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. While the paper touches on this, a more detailed discussion of how this affects applicability to different model types and dimensionality would be beneficial.
*   **Limited High-Dimensional Data Exploration:** The experiments predominantly focus on datasets where the number of instances is significantly larger than the dimensionality. The authors acknowledge the challenges with high-dimensional models, but a more in-depth exploration, even with approximations or alternative strategies, would strengthen the paper.
*   **Communication Complexity:** While mentioned briefly, a more detailed analysis of the communication overhead associated with the iterative aggregation would be valuable, especially in the context of distributed systems.
*   **Datasets:** While sufficient, the variety of datasets could be expanded to include more challenging benchmarks or real-world applications.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a significantly novel approach to parallel learning with a potentially groundbreaking theoretical result.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported.
*   The empirical validation, while not exhaustive, provides compelling evidence of the scheme's effectiveness.
*   The black-box nature of the scheme broadens its applicability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units is a major practical limitation.
*   The paper does not fully address the challenges associated with high-dimensional data.
*   The communication complexity is not thoroughly analyzed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and potentially impactful contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The theoretical result is particularly exciting, and the empirical validation provides a solid foundation. While the practical limitations regarding processing unit requirements and high-dimensionality are real, they do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work. This paper represents a bold step forward and opens up new avenues for research in scalable machine learning.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept.** I believe the novelty and potential significance of this work outweigh the remaining limitations. The authors have presented a compelling case for their approach, and I am confident that this paper will stimulate further research in this area. I recommend the authors address the concerns about communication complexity and high-dimensionality in future work. I also suggest clarifying the practicality of achieving the theoretical speedup in real-world deployments.
