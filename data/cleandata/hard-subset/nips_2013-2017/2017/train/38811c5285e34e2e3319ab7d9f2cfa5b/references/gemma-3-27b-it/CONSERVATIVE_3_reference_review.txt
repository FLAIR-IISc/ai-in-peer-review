This paper proposes a parallelization scheme, termed the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm to random subsets of the data and iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, aiming for polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial cost in data size and processing units. The authors claim this offers a significant speedup over standard parallelization techniques like averaging and potentially unlocks efficient parallel learning for problems previously considered intractable.

While the theoretical framework presented is interesting and rigorously developed – particularly the connection to Radon’s theorem and the resulting guarantees on confidence and error bounds – I find the paper’s claims of practical significance significantly overstated and insufficiently supported. The premise, offering a reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic runtime, is a bold one, and requires far more compelling evidence than what’s provided. My primary concern is the heavy reliance on theoretical results coupled with a somewhat limited and potentially cherry-picked empirical evaluation.

The theoretical analysis, while mathematically sound, operates under several simplifying assumptions. The requirement of a finite Radon number for the hypothesis space, while addressed with mention of embeddings, isn’t fully explored in terms of practical applicability. Many real-world hypothesis spaces, particularly those arising in deep learning, don’t readily satisfy this condition, limiting the scheme's direct application. Furthermore, the quasi-polynomial cost in processing units is a substantial practical hurdle. While acknowledged, the paper downplays the implications – a quasi-polynomial number of processors may be prohibitive for many applications. The analysis also glosses over the communication costs associated with aggregating hypotheses, especially in distributed settings; a more detailed consideration is warranted. 

The empirical evaluation, although demonstrating speedups compared to the base learner and some Spark implementations, feels somewhat limited in scope. The datasets used are not representative of the complexities encountered in many modern machine learning tasks. The comparison to averaging-at-the-end, while showing improvement, is not particularly illuminating, as averaging is a relatively naive parallelization technique. The comparison to Spark's MLlib is more relevant, but the authors fail to thoroughly analyze *why* the Radon machine performs similarly (or slightly better) – is it due to the algorithm itself, or simply due to more efficient implementation details? The claim of comparable performance to Spark algorithms while requiring less runtime is also stated without sufficient detail. What specific Spark algorithms were used, and under what parameter settings? The section on dataset size dependency, while interesting, doesn’t account for the cost of the Radon point calculation increasing with dimensionality. 

The discussion section acknowledges potential limitations (high-dimensional data, non-linear models, communication costs), but these are dismissed somewhat lightly, with suggestions for future work rather than a robust assessment of the current scheme’s applicability. The claim that the scheme is “black-box” is also somewhat misleading; while it doesn't require modifications to the base learner’s *code*, it still relies heavily on the properties of that learner and the hypothesis space (e.g., convexity, finite Radon number).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Theoretically sound framework with rigorous analysis.
*   Demonstrated speedups in a limited empirical setting.
*   Addresses an important problem – parallelizing machine learning algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Overstated claims of practical significance.
*   Limited empirical evaluation with potentially cherry-picked datasets.
*   Simplifying assumptions (finite Radon number, quasi-polynomial processing units) that limit applicability.
*   Insufficient discussion of communication costs.
*   The "black-box" claim is misleading.
*   Lacks a thorough comparison to state-of-the-art parallel learning algorithms beyond basic performance metrics.



Overall, this paper presents a technically interesting but ultimately unconvincing contribution. The theoretical results are valuable, but the lack of a compelling practical demonstration and the significant limitations of the scheme outweigh its strengths. I recommend **rejection** due to a disconnect between the theoretical promises and the empirical evidence. The authors should focus on addressing the limitations outlined above and providing a more robust evaluation of the scheme's performance on a wider range of datasets and problem settings before resubmitting. A more detailed analysis of the communication overhead and the impact of dimensionality is also crucial.
