Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a black-box parallelization scheme, the Radon machine, for regularized risk minimization in convex, finite-dimensional hypothesis spaces. The method trains base learners independently on rh iid subsets and aggregates hypotheses via iterated Radon points along a tree of height h. The central theoretical claim is that, compared to the failure probability δ of a base learner, the failure probability of the Radon machine decreases doubly-exponentially with h: P(Q(rh) > ε) ≤ (r δ)^{2^h}, where r is the Radon number of the hypothesis space (r = d + 2 in Rd). When the base learner has polynomial runtime TL(n) and polynomial sample complexity N0(ε, δ), the authors choose h to make each worker see n ≈ log M samples (M being the sequential sample requirement for the target (ε, Δ)), yielding overall runtime O((log M)^κ + r^3 log M) on c = r^h processors. Under the additional assumption that r can be upper bounded by a function polylogarithmic in sample complexity, they conclude polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors.

Empirically, on a 150-core Spark cluster, the Radon machine achieves 80–700× speedups over single-node base learners with comparable predictive performance, and runs 15–85% the time of Spark MLlib baselines with similar AUC. The scheme outperforms “averaging-at-the-end” in AUC with comparable runtime. The paper relates its complexity results to NC and prior work on parallel learning (PAC/NC-learnability, distributed SGD and ERM, and averaging-based aggregations).

Quality:
– Theory. The probabilistic improvement of Radon aggregation is formalized via Proposition 2 and Theorem 3. The proofs rely on the Radon partition property and quasiconvexity of Q, yielding a clean union-bound-based argument for (r · P-bad)^2 contraction per aggregation level and an exponential iteration. The runtime analysis is consistent with the algorithmic structure: TL(n) for parallel base training on rh workers plus hr^3 for solving r×r linear systems at each level. The conclusions hinge on two nontrivial assumptions: (i) δ small enough (δ ≤ 1/(2r), the notation “1/2r” should be clarified) so that (r δ) < 1, and (ii) r bounded by polylog(sample complexity) (in Rd, r = d + 2). The latter is a strong structural condition that is central to the “polylogarithmic time” claim.

– Empirics. The experimental setup uses standard libraries (WEKA, scikit-learn, Spark MLlib), 10-fold CV, and large-scale datasets. Reported speedups are clear, and comparisons to averaging and Spark baselines support the practical viability of the method and its robustness advantage over averaging. However, the experiments do not probe regimes with large d relative to n (where r^3 costs could dominate), nor do they explicitly validate the claimed double-exponential failure-probability reduction (e.g., via calibrated confidence estimates). The comparison to single-node base learners is expected to favor the cluster; the comparison to MLlib is more informative.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. Algorithm 1 is clear. Definitions and the Radon machinery are introduced succinctly. Some notational and assumption clarifications would help:
– Clarify δ ≤ 1/(2r) (notation “1/2r” is ambiguous).
– Explicitly state the PRAM/parallel model assumptions when invoking NC-style efficiency.
– Make explicit that in Euclidean spaces r = d + 2; thus polylog runtime requires d = polylog(sample complexity), and discuss practical regimes where this holds.
– Summarize the exact system for computing Radon points (Appendix C.1) in the main text with complexity constants.

Originality:
Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation in distributed learning is, to my knowledge, novel relative to averaging, geometric median, or median-of-means aggregators. The double-exponential confidence improvement and its connection to NC-style parallel efficiency provide a distinctive theoretical contribution. The black-box nature of the scheme for convex regularized risk minimization is also attractive.

Significance:
The work provides a conceptually simple, broadly applicable aggregation mechanism with provable confidence amplification and a clear path to parallel speedups. The practical significance is moderated by the assumptions: quasi-polynomial number of processors; increased total data (r^h factor); requirement that the hypothesis dimension be modest (or reduced via embeddings) to keep r^3 aggregation costs and the NC-style claim meaningful. Nevertheless, the approach is likely to influence robust aggregation in distributed settings and spur further work on dimension reduction within such schemes.

Strengths:
– Clear, general scheme that treats base learners as black boxes.
– Provable double-exponential reduction in failure probability via Radon aggregation.
– Runtime analysis that explains when polylog time is attainable.
– Empirical speedups and competitive accuracy versus strong baselines.
– Low communication cost characteristic of “aggregate-at-the-end” approaches.

Weaknesses and limitations:
– The “polylogarithmic time” guarantee requires r = d + 2 to be polylog in sample complexity; this excludes many high-dimensional regimes without additional embeddings/projections.
– Total sample complexity increases by r^h; achieving small δ ≤ 1/(2r) on each shard may require large per-shard n, reducing the practical h and thus the theoretical gains.
– Aggregation cost O(hr^3) can be significant for moderate-to-large d; empirical evaluation largely focuses on low-dimensional linear models.
– The empirical results do not measure actual confidence/error-probability reductions; improvements are reported in AUC, not in calibrated risk bounds.
– Comparisons to robust aggregators (e.g., geometric median, coordinate-wise median, trimmed mean) are missing; these might offer different robustness–cost trade-offs.

Suggestions for improvement:
– Clarify all probabilistic and complexity assumptions, especially δ ≤ 1/(2r) and the dependence of r on d versus sample complexity.
– Include experiments with higher-dimensional models and/or with random projections/low-rank embeddings to demonstrate practicality when d is large.
– Compare against robust aggregators beyond averaging (geometric median, median-of-means), both theoretically (failure-probability contraction) and empirically.
– Provide sensitivity analyses over h, r, per-shard sample size, and number of processors c < r^h (including wall-clock with scheduling/queuing).
– Report calibration or empirical confidence metrics to connect with the (ε, Δ) narrative.
– Discuss numerical stability and conditioning of Radon point computations for large r.

Arguments for acceptance:
– Novel and principled aggregation method with strong, intuitive guarantees.
– Black-box applicability to a broad class of convex learners.
– Solid theoretical development with clear proofs and connections to NC.
– Meaningful empirical speedups with competitive accuracy on large datasets.

Arguments against acceptance:
– Core polylog-time claim depends on strong dimensionality assumptions that are not validated empirically.
– Increased total sample requirement and processor count may limit practical applicability.
– Missing comparisons to other robust distributed aggregators.
– Empirical evaluation focuses on AUC rather than the claimed confidence guarantees.

Overall, this is a technically sound and original contribution with promising practical implications under realistic conditions (moderate d, ample compute and data). Addressing the noted limitations would strengthen its impact.
