This paper introduces a compelling, and surprisingly simple, parallelization scheme – the Radon Machine – for regularized risk minimization. The core idea of iteratively applying a base learner to data subsets and aggregating the results using Radon points offers a theoretically sound path to polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial cost in data and processing units. This is a *bold* approach, directly tackling a fundamental bottleneck in scaling machine learning, and the theoretical results are strong. The potential to treat any consistent and efficient learner as a black-box for parallelization is exceptionally appealing. The empirical validation, while not exhaustive, provides encouraging evidence of substantial speed-ups.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The Radon Machine presents a genuinely novel approach to parallel learning, diverging from common averaging or stochastic gradient descent-based methods. The connection to Radon points and the resulting doubly-exponential confidence increase are clever and not readily apparent.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating a reduction in runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic. The bounds on data and processing unit costs, while quasi-polynomial, are clearly stated and analyzed.
*   **Generality:** The black-box nature of the scheme is a significant strength. Its applicability to a wide range of learning algorithms without modification is highly desirable.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, despite being limited in scope, demonstrate substantial speedups compared to both sequential learning and existing parallel implementations (Spark MLlib).
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Quasi-Polynomial Costs:** The polylogarithmic runtime comes at the cost of quasi-polynomial data and processing unit requirements. While theoretically sound, this limits practical applicability to problems where these resources are available. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed discussion of scenarios where these costs are acceptable would be valuable.
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation, while promising, is somewhat constrained. Expanding the experiments to include a wider range of datasets, learning algorithms, and cluster configurations would strengthen the claims. Notably, the analysis of different *h* values is lacking; simply stating *h*=max and *h*=1 feels insufficient to understand the trade-offs.
*   **Communication Complexity:** While the paper touches on communication costs, a more detailed analysis is needed, particularly concerning the overhead of transferring hypotheses during aggregation.
*   **High-Dimensional Data:** The discussion of high-dimensional data relies on pre-processing steps (random projections, low-rank approximations). The impact of these pre-processing steps on the overall accuracy and runtime should be more thoroughly investigated.
*   **Radon Number Dependency:** The performance of the Radon Machine is tied to the Radon number of the hypothesis space.  The implications of a large Radon number (and the associated computational cost of finding Radon points) are not fully explored.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a fundamentally new approach to parallel learning with strong theoretical foundations.
*   The potential for significant speedups is compelling.
*   The generality of the scheme is a major advantage.
*   The paper opens up new avenues for research in parallel and distributed machine learning.
*   The minor flaws are outweighed by the novelty and significance of the contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The quasi-polynomial costs may limit practical applicability.
*   The empirical evaluation is not comprehensive.
*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of communication complexity and the impact of dimensionality reduction techniques.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a highly promising paper that introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallel machine learning. While the quasi-polynomial costs and limited empirical evaluation are concerns, they are outweighed by the significant novelty and potential impact of the work.  I believe this paper warrants acceptance, despite these shortcomings. The core idea is brilliant, and the theoretical results are convincing. The authors are pushing the boundaries of what's possible in parallel learning, and that's precisely the type of work NIPS should champion. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above (particularly expanding the empirical evaluation and providing a more detailed discussion of communication costs).



**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
