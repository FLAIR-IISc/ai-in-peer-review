## NIPS 2017 Review: Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially impactful contribution to the challenging problem of efficiently parallelizing machine learning algorithms. The core idea of leveraging Radon points for aggregation offers a compelling path towards polylogarithmic runtime scaling, a significant improvement over many existing parallelization strategies. While the practical evaluation is promising, the reliance on specific assumptions about the hypothesis space and the potential overhead of Radon point computation warrant careful consideration. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions, primarily focused on clarifying the practical implications and limitations.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the critical need for efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms as datasets continue to grow. It proposes a novel parallelization scheme, dubbed the "Radon machine," built upon iteratively aggregating hypotheses generated from random data subsets using Radon points. The authors rigorously demonstrate that under certain conditions (consistent and efficient base learner, finite Radon number of the hypothesis space), this scheme achieves a reduction in runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic, at the cost of increased data usage and processing units.

The work builds on prior research in parallel learning, particularly averaging-based methods [32, 45, 46]. However, it distinctly differentiates itself by utilizing Radon points, which provide a doubly-exponential improvement in confidence compared to simple averaging, as highlighted by Proposition 2 and Theorem 3. The paper also appropriately connects its work to broader theoretical concepts in computational complexity, such as Nick's Class (NC) [9], and acknowledges relevant work on parallel optimization and distributed learning [6, 10, 24, 34]. The discussion of limitations related to P-completeness is particularly insightful.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The core result – achieving polylogarithmic runtime for a broad class of learning algorithms – is a significant theoretical advance. The rigorous proof of Theorem 4, demonstrating this reduction under clearly stated conditions, is a major strength.
* **Novel Approach:** The use of Radon points for aggregation represents a novel and clever idea. The theoretical justification for their superior confidence-building properties (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) is compelling.
* **Black-Box Parallelization:**  The Radon machine's "black-box" nature is a significant practical advantage. It doesn't require algorithm-specific modifications, making it widely applicable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results, while conducted on a limited number of datasets, demonstrate substantial speedups (up to 700x) compared to sequential learning and competitive performance against established parallel libraries like Spark MLlib.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear and the key concepts are explained in a relatively accessible manner.
* **Thorough Discussion of Limitations:** The authors proactively address potential limitations related to high dimensionality, non-linear models, communication complexity, and the requirement for a sufficient number of processing units.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Radon Number Dependency:** The performance guarantee hinges on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. While the paper acknowledges this, the practical implications for high-dimensional or complex hypothesis spaces are not fully explored.  What happens when the Radon number becomes prohibitively large? The authors mention low-dimensional embeddings, but a more detailed discussion of their practical efficacy and potential drawbacks would be beneficial.
* **Overhead of Radon Point Computation:** Calculating Radon points involves solving a system of linear equations (Appendix C.1). While the authors state this can be done in parallel, the computational cost, particularly for high-dimensional spaces, could offset the benefits of parallelizing the base learner.  A more detailed analysis of this overhead would be valuable.
* **Scalability of Aggregation:**  As the number of initial hypotheses increases (determined by 'r' and 'h'), the cost of the aggregation stages could become a bottleneck.  Exploring the scaling behavior of the aggregation phase is important.
* **Dataset Size Requirement:**  The theoretical analysis assumes a dataset size exceeding a multiple of the Radon number. This could be a limiting factor for certain applications.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** While the empirical results are promising, they are limited to a relatively small number of datasets and base learners. More comprehensive evaluation, including diverse datasets and learning algorithms, would strengthen the conclusions.




**4. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*  Significant theoretical contribution addressing a fundamental challenge in machine learning.
*  Novel and well-justified approach with potential for substantial speedups.
*  Black-box nature makes it broadly applicable.
*  Empirical results demonstrate practical benefits.
*  Authors acknowledge and discuss limitations.

**Con:**

*  Radon number dependency could limit applicability in some scenarios.
*  Potential overhead of Radon point computation.
*  Limited empirical validation.




**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of calculating Radon points, particularly in high-dimensional spaces?
*   How does the performance of the Radon machine degrade as the Radon number increases? Are there practical guidelines for choosing appropriate values for 'r' and 'h' based on the dimensionality of the hypothesis space?
*   Could you elaborate on the effectiveness of low-dimensional embeddings for mitigating the Radon number issue in high-dimensional scenarios?
*   How does the scaling of the aggregation phase affect the overall runtime as the number of initial hypotheses increases?
*   Do you have any insights into extending the Radon machine to handle non-linear models directly, without relying on embeddings?




**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable and innovative contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the Radon number dependency and the computational overhead of Radon point calculation, will further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. The clarity of the presentation and the strong theoretical foundation justify its inclusion in NIPS 2017.
