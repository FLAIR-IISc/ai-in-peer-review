Summary
This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for a broad class of convex regularized risk minimizers. The idea is to train many weak models on iid subsets in parallel and aggregate them via iterated Radon points on a tree. The key theoretical result is that aggregation by Radon points reduces the probability of an ε-bad hypothesis doubly exponentially with tree height h: P(Q(rh) > ε) ≤ (r δ)^(2^h), where r is the Radon number (d+2 in Rd) and δ is the base learner’s failure probability. Under standard sample-complexity assumptions, the authors argue one can choose h so that the total runtime becomes polylogarithmic in the target sample size needed to achieve a given (ε, Δ)-guarantee, at the cost of using quasi-polynomially many parallel workers (≈ r^h). Empirically, on several medium-to-large datasets and linear models, the method achieves substantial speedups (80–700× using ~150 processors) over base learners and compares favorably to Spark MLlib baselines in runtime while achieving similar AUC.

Relation to prior work
Compared to averaging-based model aggregation, the novelty is replacing the mean by the Radon point, yielding a provable doubly-exponential boost in confidence under convexity. This distinguishes it from well-studied averaging and distributed SGD/ERM methods (e.g., Zinkevich et al., CoCoA, DANE/ADMM), which typically need iteration counts depending at least poly(1/ε) and cannot reach polylog time with polynomial processors. The connection to NC and parallel complexity is interesting and appropriately contextualized.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Using Radon points for aggregation in distributed learning is original and elegant, bridging computational geometry (Radon/Tukey depth) with statistical learning.
- Strong, simple core theory: Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 give clean, interpretable confidence amplification under convexity. The robustness intuition versus averaging is compelling.
- Ambitious complexity claim: Theorem 4 provides a path to polylogarithmic runtime (with quasi-polynomial processors), addressing a long-standing question around efficient parallel learning and NC.
- Black-box applicability: Works for any convex regularized risk minimizer in finite-dimensional Euclidean spaces; aggregation cost is independent of data size (O(r^3) per internal node).
- Empirical promise: Large practical speedups with comparable AUC; clear win over averaging-at-the-end in AUC with similar runtime; competitive to Spark MLlib in both runtime and accuracy on tested tasks.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strength of assumptions vs practice:
  - Independence: The theory assumes iid points from the hypothesis distribution at each aggregation step; Algorithm 1 partitions a fixed dataset D into disjoint subsets. Strict iid resampling from the population is not possible without replacement or bootstrapping. Please clarify whether subsets are sampled with replacement and how this impacts the guarantees.
  - Base learner requirement δ ≤ 1/(2r): Feasibility and how to achieve this in practice (data size vs δ) should be discussed quantitatively.
  - Radon number scaling: In Rd, r = d + 2; r must be “polylog in sample complexity” for Theorem 4. In high dimensions this is unrealistic, and r^h can explode. The paper acknowledges this and suggests projections or feature embeddings, but the practical regime where this yields real gains needs clearer guidance (e.g., tradeoffs d, h, subset size).
- Processor budget: The theoretical speedups need rh processors (quasi-polynomial in 1/ε). Experiments use ~125–150 cores; for datasets like SUSY (d=18, r=20, h=3) rh=8000, so most work must be sequentialized. While deparallelization is mentioned, an explicit accounting of the scheduling factor max{1, rh/c} in the reported speedups would help assess practical scalability.
- Bounds tightness and scope:
  - The union-bound based analysis is conservative; can the constants be improved (e.g., using structure of Radon partitions) to reduce r-dependence?
  - The regret function Q is assumed convex; this holds in the presented setting, but the result does not extend to non-convex models (which dominate modern ML), limiting applicability.
- Experimental scope:
  - Focuses on linear models and relatively low-dimensional features where r is small; no experiments showing behavior as d grows or under dimensionality reduction pipelines.
  - Limited ablations on h (only h=1 and h=max). A systematic sweep of h vs runtime/accuracy would be informative.
  - Fairness details for Spark baselines (tuning budgets, number of passes/epochs, regularization matching) could be expanded.
  - Communication and memory footprint are discussed qualitatively; quantitative measurements would strengthen the practical claims.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. Suggestions:
- Be explicit about how subsets are drawn (with/without replacement) and the stochastic model bridging Algorithm 1 to the theory.
- Move the requirement δ ≤ 1/2r and implications for N0(ε, δ) into the main analysis earlier.
- Briefly restate how to compute a Radon point in Rd and address numerical stability for ill-conditioned configurations.

Significance
If the community embraces Radon aggregation as a primitive, this could seed a new line of work on confidence amplification and parallel generalization for convex learning. Even with strong assumptions, the idea is bold and could inspire variants for high-dimensional and non-convex settings, or robust aggregators beyond averaging in federated/distributed contexts.

Questions for the authors
- Can you quantify, on at least one dataset, the effective δ of the base learner and show the doubly-exponential reduction as h increases?
- How sensitive are results to violations of iid splitting (e.g., stratified or slightly biased partitions)?
- For high d, can you provide an empirical study combining random projections with Radon machines, including end-to-end accuracy/runtime tradeoffs?
- How does the Radon point computation behave numerically for near-degenerate point sets? Any regularization needed?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and compelling central idea with clean theory
- Ambitious connection to NC and polylog-time parallel learning
- Practical speedups with minimal engineering and strong empirical signals
- Black-box applicability to a wide class of convex learners

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumptions (iid, δ threshold, small r) and gap to practical large-d settings
- Processor scaling requirement is very high to realize theoretical guarantees
- Experimental evaluation, while positive, is limited in scope and missing some fairness/ablation details

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept to accept). Despite some gaps between theory and practice and strong assumptions, this is a bold, original contribution with a clear new concept (Radon aggregation) and promising empirical evidence. It is precisely the kind of idea that can catalyze follow-up work, and the paper is technically sound within its stated scope.
