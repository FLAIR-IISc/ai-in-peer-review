## Peer Review: Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel parallelisation scheme, termed the "Radon machine," for regularised risk minimisation algorithms. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm to random subsets of the data, then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points. The authors theoretically demonstrate that this scheme can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic, at the cost of increased data size and processing units. They provide empirical evidence supporting the speedup, showing significant runtime reductions compared to both the sequential base learner and parallel implementations in Spark's MLlib, while maintaining comparable performance.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper situates itself within the broader context of parallel learning. It acknowledges averaging-based approaches (e.g., [32, 45, 46]) and highlights the advantage of Radon points, which provide a doubly-exponential increase in confidence compared to simple averaging. The work also connects to theoretical limitations of parallel computation, referencing Nick’s Class (NC) [9] and the challenges of parallelising P-complete problems.  It correctly positions itself as contributing to the ongoing investigation of efficient parallelisation of polynomial-time learning algorithms, citing relevant work on distributed stochastic gradient descent [34] and related approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, formally proving the runtime reduction and bounding the cost in terms of data size and processing units. The proof of Theorem 3, demonstrating the doubly-exponential confidence increase with each iteration, is particularly compelling.
* **Black-Box Approach:** The Radon machine is presented as a general-purpose parallelisation scheme, applicable to any consistent and efficient regularised risk minimisation algorithm without requiring algorithm-specific modifications. This is a significant advantage.
* **Empirically Validated:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups in realistic scenarios using Spark clusters. The comparison with Spark’s MLlib algorithms provides a valuable benchmark.  The detailed analysis of runtime scaling with dataset size (Figure 3) is insightful.
* **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly presented and the use of definitions (e.g., Definition 1) enhances clarity.
* **Acknowledgement of Limitations & Future Work:** The authors thoughtfully discuss potential limitations (e.g., high-dimensional data, non-linear models) and outline promising directions for future research, indicating a good understanding of the broader research landscape.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Quasi-Polynomial Processing Units:** While achieving polylogarithmic runtime is a major achievement, the reliance on a *quasi*-polynomial number of processing units could limit practical applicability in resource-constrained environments.  The paper touches on this in the discussion but could benefit from a more detailed exploration of the trade-offs between runtime, data size, and processing unit requirements.
* **Radon Number Dependency:** The performance of the Radon machine depends on the Radon number of the hypothesis space.  For some complex hypothesis spaces, the Radon number might be large, potentially offsetting the benefits of parallelisation. A deeper discussion of this dependency and possible mitigation strategies (e.g., dimensionality reduction) would be helpful.
* **Communication Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions communication complexity but lacks a quantitative analysis. In a distributed setting, communication costs can be a major bottleneck.  Providing estimates or discussing potential strategies for minimizing communication would strengthen the paper.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the empirical evaluation is thorough, it primarily focuses on relatively well-behaved datasets.  Evaluating the performance on more challenging datasets (e.g., datasets with high dimensionality, imbalanced classes, or complex relationships) would increase confidence in the robustness of the scheme.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound parallelisation scheme with the potential to significantly accelerate machine learning algorithms.
* The empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups in realistic scenarios.
* The black-box nature of the scheme makes it broadly applicable.
* The authors acknowledge limitations and propose directions for future research.
* The paper addresses a challenging problem and contributes to the theoretical understanding of parallel learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The quasi-polynomial requirement for processing units might limit practical applicability.
* The dependency on the Radon number could be a bottleneck for some hypothesis spaces.
* A more detailed analysis of communication complexity would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The Radon machine offers a promising approach to parallelising machine learning algorithms, and the authors have provided a compelling case for its effectiveness. While the reliance on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units and the Radon number dependency are potential limitations, they are acknowledged and discussed by the authors. I believe the strengths of this paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance** to NIPS 2017.

**Minor Suggestions:**

* In the discussion of practical applicability, provide a more concrete example of the trade-offs between runtime, data size, and processing units.
* Expand the discussion of communication complexity, potentially including estimates or strategies for minimization.
* Consider adding a brief discussion of the computational cost of calculating Radon points for different dimensions.
*  Consider adding a short subsection on the computational cost of verifying the Radon number r of a space.
