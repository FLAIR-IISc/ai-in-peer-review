

# Review: "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to run a base learning algorithm on random subsets of data in parallel, then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points (instead of averaging) in a tree structure. The key theoretical contribution is that this approach achieves error probability bounds that decrease doubly exponentially with the depth of the aggregation tree, enabling runtime reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic in sample complexity.

The paper makes a strong connection between machine learning parallelization and theoretical computer science, specifically Nick's Class (NC), by showing that the Radon Machine achieves polylogarithmic runtime with quasi-polynomial processing units. The empirical evaluation demonstrates 80-700x speedups over sequential base learners while maintaining comparable predictive performance to Spark's MLlib implementations.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are well-constructed, demonstrating how Radon points provide stronger error bounds than averaging. The authors carefully establish the conditions under which their approach applies (finite Radon number, convex hypothesis space) and acknowledge limitations. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against both the base learner, averaging-at-the-end, and Spark MLlib implementations across multiple datasets. However, the empirical section would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the specific datasets used and why they were chosen.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. Key concepts like Radon points are well-defined (Definition 1), and Algorithm 1 provides a concrete implementation. The connection to theoretical computer science (Nick's Class) is effectively explained. The writing is generally accessible to machine learning researchers, though some readers unfamiliar with convex geometry might need to consult the appendix. The paper could improve by adding a brief intuitive explanation of why Radon points provide stronger error bounds than averaging.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution to the parallel machine learning literature. While averaging-based parallelizations have been explored [32, 45, 46], this is the first to use Radon points to achieve doubly exponential error reduction. The connection to Nick's Class and the theoretical question of whether consistent learning is possible in polylogarithmic time with polynomial processors is novel. The paper adequately references related work, particularly Long and Servedio's open problem about parallel learning of half-spaces [21].

## Significance
The significance of this work is high. The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in machine learning: how to effectively parallelize learning algorithms without sacrificing error guarantees. The theoretical results (polylogarithmic runtime) and empirical speedups (80-700x) are compelling. The "black-box" nature of the approach—requiring no modifications to base learners—makes it broadly applicable. The paper advances the field by providing a provably effective parallelization scheme for a broad class of learning algorithms.

## Strengths
- Theoretical contribution is rigorous and novel (Radon points instead of averaging)
- Clear connection to theoretical computer science (Nick's Class)
- Strong empirical results showing substantial speedups
- The "black-box" nature makes the approach widely applicable
- Comprehensive analysis of limitations and extensions

## Weaknesses
- The requirement for finite Radon number (r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces) may limit applicability without dimensionality reduction
- Limited empirical evaluation on extremely large datasets where parallelization is most needed
- Does not address non-convex optimization problems (common in deep learning)
- The "quasi-polynomial number of processing units" requirement may be impractical for real-world systems
- The paper claims "polylogarithmic runtime" but this is achieved with quasi-polynomial processors, which is a significant caveat

## Recommendation

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The theoretical results are novel and rigorously proven, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates practical benefits. The connection to Nick's Class is particularly valuable for theoretical machine learning researchers. While the requirement for quasi-polynomial processors is a limitation, the paper acknowledges this and discusses practical workarounds (e.g., dimensionality reduction).

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel theoretical approach that also has practical significance. The work advances the state of the art in parallel learning and provides a clear path for future research.

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The weaknesses identified are important but do not undermine the paper's core contributions. With minor clarifications (particularly on the practical implications of the quasi-polynomial processor requirement), this paper would be an excellent addition to NIPS.
