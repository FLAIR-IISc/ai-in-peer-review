

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper proposes the "Radon machine" as a parallelisation scheme for regularised risk minimisation algorithms, claiming to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity while maintaining the same error guarantees. The authors present a theoretical analysis showing that their aggregation scheme using Radon points reduces the probability of generating an ε-bad hypothesis doubly exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. The empirical evaluation compares the Radon machine to base learning algorithms and Spark's MLlib.

## Minor Technical Flaws

1. **Notational Inconsistency**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. In Equation (3), they write "P(Q(L(D)) > ε) ≤ ∆" but then later use "δ" for the base learning algorithm's error probability. They should use consistent notation (e.g., "δ" for the base algorithm, "∆" for the Radon machine).

2. **Mathematical Ambiguity**: In Proposition 2, they write "P(Q(r) > ε) ≤ (rP(Q(F) > ε))^2" but this is missing parentheses, making it ambiguous whether it's (rP(...))^2 or rP(...)^2. This is critical for understanding the bound.

3. **Misleading Claim**: The paper claims "our parallelisation scheme can reduce the runtime of learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic." However, their analysis shows runtime reduction in terms of sample complexity (M), not input size. This is a subtle but important distinction that misrepresents the contribution.

4. **Radon Number Confusion**: The paper states "the Euclidean space Rd has Radon number r = d + 2" but then uses r as a parameter of the algorithm without specifying how it relates to the dimension d. In practical applications, r would need to be known or estimated, but the paper doesn't provide guidance on this.

5. **Inconsistent Notation for Logarithms**: The paper uses "ld" for logarithm (e.g., "βε ld 1/δ"), which is non-standard. This should be "log" throughout.

6. **Definition of "ε-bad hypothesis"**: The paper never formally defines what an "ε-bad hypothesis" is in Equation (3), making the consistency definition ambiguous.

7. **Theorem 3 Ambiguity**: In Theorem 3, they write "P(Q(rh) > ε) ≤ (rP(Q(F) > ε))^2 h" but this should be "P(Q(rh) > ε) ≤ (rP(Q(F) > ε))^(2^h)" to represent the double exponential decay. The current notation implies multiplication by h rather than exponentiation.

8. **Inconsistent Use of "h"**: The paper uses "h" both as the parameter for the aggregation tree height and as a variable in the Radon machine algorithm (Algorithm 1). This is confusing and should be clarified.

## Formatting and Clarity Issues

1. **British vs. American Spelling**: The paper uses "Parallelisation" (British) instead of "Parallelization" (American), which is inconsistent with NIPS conventions.

2. **URL Formatting**: The Bitbucket URL is given as "https://bitbucket.org/Michael_Kamp/radonmachine" without the "http://" prefix, which is technically incorrect.

3. **Inconsistent Spacing**: The paper uses inconsistent spacing around operators (e.g., "Q (f)" vs. "Q(f)").

4. **Figure Reference**: The paper refers to "Figure 1(a)" but doesn't provide the actual figure, making it difficult to evaluate their empirical claims.

5. **Incomplete References**: Several references are incomplete (e.g., "Learning in parallel, Jeffrey S. Vitter, Jyh-Han Lin, Information and Computation, , , ," lacks year and page numbers).

## Significance and Contribution

While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the paper overstates its significance. The reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic runtime is only in terms of sample complexity, not the actual input size. The practical utility is limited by the requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units (rh), which is impractical for large problems. The empirical results show speedups of 80-700x on 150 processors, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about the specific datasets or base learners used.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically interesting parallelisation scheme with a novel aggregation method based on Radon points. However, the numerous minor technical errors, inconsistent notation, and ambiguous claims significantly undermine the paper's quality. The authors have made a good effort, but the paper is not ready for publication in NIPS in its current form. The technical details require careful revision to ensure precision and clarity.

The main strengths of the paper include the novel Radon point aggregation method and the theoretical analysis of error probability reduction. The main weaknesses are the numerous notational inconsistencies, misleading claims about runtime complexity, and insufficient empirical detail.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but critical flaws that prevent the paper from meeting NIPS's high standards for technical precision and clarity.
