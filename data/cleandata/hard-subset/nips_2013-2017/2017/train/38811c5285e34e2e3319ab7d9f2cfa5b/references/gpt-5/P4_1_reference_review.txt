Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a black-box parallelization scheme, the Radon machine, for regularized risk minimization over convex, finite-dimensional hypothesis spaces. The method trains a base learner on rh iid data subsets in parallel and aggregates the resulting hypotheses bottom-up along an r-ary tree using Radon points (with r the Radon number, r = d+2 in Rd). The key theoretical result (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3) shows that if a base learner attains an (ε, δ) guarantee, aggregating via Radon points over h levels yields a hypothesis with failure probability at most (rδ)^{2^h}, i.e., a doubly-exponential reduction in the failure probability with tree height. The analysis then compares the sample and runtime complexity of (i) running the base learner sequentially to achieve a given (ε, Δ) and (ii) running the Radon machine to reach the same (ε, Δ). Under a stylized sample complexity form NL0(ε, δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k and a polynomial-time base runtime TL(n) ∈ O(n^κ), the Radon machine attains polylogarithmic runtime in NL0(ε, Δ) when using rh processors and consuming rh·NL0(ε, δ) samples, provided the Radon number is bounded polylogarithmically in sample complexity (Theorem 4). Empirical results on several datasets using WEKA/scikit-learn base learners and comparisons to Spark MLlib show large wall-clock speedups (80–700× on 150 processors) with comparable AUC to baselines; Radon aggregation outperforms averaging-at-the-end in AUC with similar runtime.

The method builds on classical notions from convex geometry (Radon’s theorem) and iterated Radon points for centerpoint approximation (e.g., Clarkson et al.). It contrasts with averaging-based distributed learning and with parallel SGD/ADMM-type optimization approaches; the paper reviews connections to NC parallel complexity and prior work on parallel PAC learning and halfspace learning.

Quality
- Theory: The analysis of Radon aggregation is concise and correct under the stated assumptions. Proposition 7 ensures at least two inputs are no better than the Radon point for any quasi-convex Q, enabling a union-bound argument yielding P(Q(r) > ε) ≤ (r P(Q(F) > ε))^2 and its iterative application. The translation of confidence amplification into sample/runtime trade-offs is clear. The requirement δ ≤ 1/2^r for base learners is explicit. The runtime bound assumes access to rh processors and uses O(r^3) aggregation per level. The sample complexity template is justified via VC/Rademacher arguments (appendix).
- Empirics: The experiments indicate substantial speedups with comparable predictive performance against averaging and Spark baselines. However, they primarily assess AUC and wall-clock time, not the promised (ε, Δ) confidence guarantees, and do not report failure probability estimates. Fairness details (e.g., threading of baselines, hardware utilization) are partially described; examining the effect of h on both accuracy and time would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well written and organized. Definitions, algorithm pseudocode, and the high-level proof ideas are clear. A few points need clarification:
- “Divide D into rh iid subsets” is imprecise; with a finite dataset, iid subsets require sampling with replacement rather than partitioning without replacement.
- The conditions under which “polylogarithmic time” is achieved and the number of processors becomes polynomial (e.g., constant or polylogarithmic dimension) should be stated more explicitly in the main text, not only in the theorem discussion.

Originality
Using Radon points as an aggregation operator for distributed learning appears novel and distinct from averaging, geometric median, or median-of-means style aggregations commonly used in distributed/robust learning. The doubly-exponential reduction in failure probability with tree height is a noteworthy theoretical contribution. The connection to NC-style parallel efficiency for regularized risk minimization is also interesting. Related robust aggregation literature (e.g., geometric median, trimmed mean, coordinate-wise median, median-of-means) is not discussed and deserves citation and comparison.

Significance
The black-box, model-agnostic parallelization technique is of practical and conceptual interest. The confidence amplification result is clean and could be useful where high confidence is critical. However, the practical impact depends on constraints: the required number of processors is rh = r^h, which can be quasi-polynomial in the target sample complexity unless the dimension (and thus r) is small; aggregation costs scale as O(d^3) per level; and the assumption δ ≤ 1/2^r may require substantial per-subsample sizes. The empirical evidence suggests utility on moderate-dimensional problems using standard linear models.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general, and black-box parallelization scheme with clear algorithmic description.
- Theoretical guarantee of doubly-exponential failure probability reduction via Radon aggregation; constructive and easy to implement (linear system solves).
- Concrete runtime/sample complexity trade-offs and a clear path to polylog time under specified conditions.
- Empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups with maintained predictive performance; systematic comparison to Spark and averaging baselines.

Cons against acceptance
- Achieving polylogarithmic time requires quasi-polynomially many processors unless dimension is small; practical implications of r = d+2 are significant and only partially addressed via projections/low-rank approximations.
- The empirical evaluation does not validate the confidence bounds (Δ) directly; it focuses on AUC.
- The assumption δ ≤ 1/2^r and the independence of subsamples (iid subsets) are strong; the data splitting procedure and implications of dependence are not fully discussed.
- Limited discussion and no empirical comparison to robust aggregation alternatives (geometric median, median-of-means), which might offer similar confidence amplification with different dimensional dependence.
- Communication and aggregation costs are analyzed briefly; scaling in high dimensions may be prohibitive.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the sampling procedure for generating iid subsets and discuss effects of sampling without replacement.
- Provide experiments varying h to illustrate the empirical trade-off between runtime and failure probability (or surrogate measures), and to examine the δ ≤ 1/2^r regime.
- Compare against robust aggregation baselines (geometric median, coordinate-wise median, median-of-means) and discuss dimensional dependence versus Radon aggregation.
- Report fairness details (e.g., single/multi-threaded baselines, parameter tuning budgets) and perform ablations on aggregation cost (Radon vs averaging) and communication overhead.
- Discuss conditions under which the number of processors is polynomial (e.g., constant d) and provide guidance for applying dimensionality reduction while preserving predictive performance and the convexity assumptions.

Overall, the paper provides a clear and interesting aggregation-based approach to parallel learning with solid theoretical grounding and promising empirical results, while facing practical limitations tied to dimensionality and processor requirements.
