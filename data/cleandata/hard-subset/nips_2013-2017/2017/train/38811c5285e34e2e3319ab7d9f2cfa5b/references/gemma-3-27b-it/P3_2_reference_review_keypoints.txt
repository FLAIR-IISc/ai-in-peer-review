- Presents the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses for polylogarithmic runtime on regularized risk minimization.
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous, proving consistency and a polylogarithmic runtime guarantee under reasonable assumptions.
- Radon point aggregation offers stronger confidence boosts than averaging and is a theoretically justified alternative to existing methods.
- The approach is black-box: applicable to many consistent, efficient learning algorithms without algorithm-specific changes.
- Empirical results show encouraging speedups, especially with many processors, and include comparisons to Spark’s MLlib.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and uses consistent mathematical notation.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope: few datasets, primarily linear models, and reliance on Weka/scikit-learn base learners.
- Requires a quasi-polynomial number of processing units, posing significant practical limitations and unclear simulation overheads.
- Runtime heavily depends on the Radon number of the hypothesis space; strategies for large or unknown Radon numbers are underexplored.
- Communication complexity with many processors is a potential bottleneck and is not thoroughly analyzed or empirically assessed.
- Limited comparison to more recent or advanced distributed learning frameworks beyond Spark’s MLlib.
- Questions for authors include: mitigating communication overhead, performance degradation with higher Radon numbers, costs of simulating many processors, applicability to non-linear models and low-rank approximations, and adaptation to streaming or federated settings.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept with minor revisions; score 8.5/10, contingent on addressing practical concerns and expanding empirical evaluation.
