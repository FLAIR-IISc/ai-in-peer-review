This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for a class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to partition the dataset, train a base learning algorithm on each subset in parallel to obtain an initial set of hypotheses, and then hierarchically aggregate these hypotheses. The aggregation step is the main novelty: instead of averaging, the authors propose computing the Radon point of small subsets of hypotheses. The central theoretical claim is that this aggregation method boosts the confidence in the final hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. This allows the scheme to achieve the same error and confidence bounds as a sequential learner in polylogarithmic time, provided a quasi-polynomial number of processors and a larger total dataset are available. The authors relate this result to the theoretical computer science question of whether learning problems are in Nick's Class (NC) of efficiently parallelizable problems.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The paper addresses a fundamental and challenging problem in machine learning theory: understanding the parallel complexity of learning algorithms. The application of Radon's theorem and the concept of Radon points to aggregate model hypotheses is a novel and elegant idea.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The main theoretical result, Theorem 3, which demonstrates a doubly-exponential reduction in the probability of obtaining a poor hypothesis, is powerful. If the assumptions hold, this provides a much stronger guarantee than existing bounds for averaging-based ensemble methods.
3.  **Black-Box Approach:** The proposed method treats the base learning algorithm as a black box. This is a significant practical advantage, as it allows the scheme to be applied to various existing algorithms without requiring their re-implementation or modification.
4.  **Promising Empirical Speed-ups:** The experimental results in low-dimensional settings are impressive. The reported speed-ups of 80x to 700x over sequential base learners (Figure 1a) and superior performance/runtime trade-off against Spark MLlib implementations (Figure 1c) demonstrate the potential of the approach under favorable conditions.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Severe Limitations due to Dimensionality:** The paper's primary weakness lies in a critical assumption that is not sufficiently emphasized: the dependency on the Radon number `r`. For a hypothesis space in `R^d`, the Radon number is `r = d+2`. The number of initial parallel jobs and the required number of processors scales as `r^h = (d+2)^h`. This exponential dependence on the dimensionality `d` makes the method fundamentally impractical for the high-dimensional problems that are common in modern machine learning (e.g., text processing, genomics, large-scale image features). The authors acknowledge this in Section 5, suggesting pre-processing via dimensionality reduction. However, this is a major caveat, not a minor detail. The proposed "Radon Machine" does not solve the parallelization problem for high-dimensional data; it only works after another method has made the problem low-dimensional. This severely curtails the claimed generality of the scheme.
2.  **Unrealistic Resource Requirements for Theoretical Guarantees:** The headline result of polylogarithmic runtime (Theorem 4) relies on a quasi-polynomial number of processors. This is an extremely high theoretical requirement, far exceeding the polynomial number of processors typically considered for complexity classes like NC. While theoretically interesting, it distances the result from practical computational models and makes the connection to efficient parallelization tenuous from a practical standpoint.
3.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation of Weaknesses:** The experiments are conducted almost exclusively on datasets with a small number of features (e.g., SUSY has 18 features). This is precisely the regime where `r = d+2` is small and the method is expected to perform well. The lack of experiments on even moderately high-dimensional data (e.g., d > 100) is a significant omission. A thorough empirical evaluation should probe the limitations of a method, and in this case, the scaling with dimension `d` is the most critical aspect to investigate. Without this, the empirical results feel confirmatory rather than exploratory, failing to validate the approach's robustness.
4.  **Strong and Under-explored Assumptions:** The analysis relies on the assumption that the base learner, trained on a small data partition, can achieve an initial error probability of `δ ≤ 1/(2r)`. For high-dimensional problems, `r` would be large, requiring an extremely small `δ`. Achieving such a low failure probability on a small data subset might require that subset to be very large, which undermines the entire parallelization strategy. This critical trade-off is not adequately discussed or analyzed. Furthermore, the condition in Theorem 4 that the Radon number `r` is polylogarithmic in the sample complexity seems to be a very strong assumption that is not justified for general learning problems.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-Acceptance:** The paper introduces a genuinely novel theoretical idea for parallel learning with strong, doubly-exponential performance guarantees. The black-box nature and the impressive speed-ups shown in low-dimensional settings are compelling. It makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical discourse on the parallel complexity of machine learning.
*   **Con-Acceptance:** The method's practicality is severely undermined by its exponential dependency on the data's dimensionality, a limitation that is downplayed by the authors. The theoretical results rest on unrealistic assumptions about the number of available processors and the performance of the base learners. The empirical evaluation is not comprehensive enough, as it fails to test the method under high-dimensional conditions where it is expected to falter.

**Recommendation:**

While I appreciate the novelty of the theoretical approach, I am skeptical of the work in its current form. The gap between the ambitious claims of a "provably effective parallelisation scheme for a broad class of learning algorithms" and the method's severe practical limitations in high-dimensional settings is too large. The paper presents an interesting theoretical concept but does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of broad practical utility. For a NIPS paper, a more rigorous and honest evaluation of the method's limitations, particularly through experiments that test its sensitivity to dimensionality, is essential. Therefore, I lean towards rejection.
