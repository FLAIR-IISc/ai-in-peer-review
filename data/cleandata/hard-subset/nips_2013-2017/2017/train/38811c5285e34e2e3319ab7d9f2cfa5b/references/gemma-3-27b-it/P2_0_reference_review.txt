## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, dubbed the "Radon machine," for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea leverages Radon points for aggregating hypotheses computed in parallel on data subsets. The authors theoretically demonstrate that this approach can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic, at the cost of increased data usage and processing units. Empirical evaluation shows substantial speedups compared to sequential learning and competitive performance against existing parallel implementations (Spark MLlib). The authors position this work as contributing to the fundamental question of whether efficient parallelization is possible for polynomial-time learning algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the parallelization scheme, establishing guarantees on confidence and error bounds. The polylogarithmic runtime reduction is a significant claim and is backed by mathematical reasoning.
* **Generality:** The Radon machine is presented as a black-box approach, applicable to a broad class of consistent and efficient learning algorithms without requiring algorithm-specific modifications. This is a notable strength, increasing its potential impact.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate significant speedups in practice, particularly compared to Spark MLlib. The comparison against averaging-at-the-end highlights the benefit of using Radon points for aggregation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the Radon machine.
* **Addresses a Fundamental Question:** The work directly tackles the important question of parallelizing polynomial-time learning algorithms, a long-standing open problem in theoretical computer science.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Limitations:** The theoretical guarantee of polylogarithmic runtime relies on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units. While the authors discuss "deparallelization," the practicality of achieving this speedup with limited resources is not fully explored. The reliance on the Radon number 'r' being manageable is also a concern.
* **Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge that the basic scheme might not be directly applicable to high-dimensional models without employing techniques like random projections or low-rank approximations. While these techniques are mentioned, a more detailed discussion of their impact on the theoretical guarantees and practical performance would be beneficial.
* **Communication Complexity:** The paper touches on communication complexity, but a more thorough analysis would be valuable.  In a distributed setting, the cost of communicating the hypotheses between nodes could become a bottleneck.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation focuses on a relatively small set of datasets and algorithms. Expanding the evaluation to a wider range of tasks and datasets would strengthen the findings.
* **Missing Discussion of Fault Tolerance:** In a real-world parallel computing environment, fault tolerance is a critical consideration. The paper does not discuss how the Radon machine handles processor failures or data corruption.




**Addressing Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The derivation of the sample and runtime complexity are convincing. However, the practical implications of the quasi-polynomial processor requirement could be a concern.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The definitions and notation are well-defined. However, a deeper explanation of the Radon point calculation (Appendix C.1) might be helpful for readers unfamiliar with the concept.
* **Originality:** The use of Radon points for parallel learning is a novel idea. While averaging-based parallelization schemes exist, the authors demonstrate a significant improvement in confidence bounds.
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant, as they offer a theoretical pathway to achieving polylogarithmic runtime for a broad class of learning algorithms. If the practical limitations can be addressed, this work could have a substantial impact on the scalability of machine learning.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound parallelization scheme.
* Strong empirical validation demonstrating significant speedups.
* Addresses a fundamental open problem in parallel machine learning.
* Potential to significantly improve the scalability of machine learning algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on a quasi-polynomial number of processors, which may limit practical applicability.
* Scalability concerns for high-dimensional models.
* Limited scope of the empirical evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The theoretical results are compelling, and the empirical validation is promising. While the practical limitations regarding processor requirements and scalability need to be carefully considered, the novelty and potential impact of this work are substantial. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1.  **Discussion of Practicality:**  Expand the discussion of "deparallelization" and the impact of limited processor resources on the runtime.
2. **Communication Complexity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the communication complexity of the Radon machine.
3. **Scalability Analysis:**  Elaborate on how techniques like random projections or low-rank approximations can mitigate scalability issues for high-dimensional models.
4. **Fault Tolerance:** Briefly discuss potential strategies for handling processor failures and data corruption in a distributed setting.
