- Summary: Proposes the Radon Machine, aggregating hypotheses via Radon points to get doubly exponential reduction in ε-bad hypothesis probability, enabling polylogarithmic runtime with quasi-polynomial processors; reports 80–700× speedups on 150 processors.

- Strength (Quality): Rigorous theoretical contributions with clear definitions, precise theorems, full proofs, and comprehensive empirical comparisons showing strong speedups and comparable predictive performance.

- Strength (Originality): Novel use of Radon points for aggregation, links to Nick’s Class, and a black-box method applicable to any base learner.

- Strength (Significance): Tackles fundamental parallelization limits with practical implications for large-scale learning and advances theoretical understanding of parallel learning.

- Strength (Clarity): Well-organized paper, consistent notation, a concrete algorithm, and a thoughtful discussion of limitations and future work.

- Weakness (Quality): Assumes finite Radon number and suggests low-rank fixes without quantifying their effect; quasi-polynomial processor counts may be impractical.

- Weakness (Significance): Empirical evaluation limited to moderately large datasets (up to 5M); scalability to billions of instances and comparison to modern distributed SGD methods are missing.

- Weakness (Clarity): Communication complexity not quantified in experiments and handling of dataset sizes not divisible by r^h is not specified.

- Overall assessment: Strong, technically sound paper with significant theoretical and empirical contributions, tempered by practical scalability and communication concerns.

- Recommendation: Accept.
