**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces the "Radon machine," a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The method operates by first training a base learning algorithm on a large number of small, disjoint subsets of the data in parallel. The resulting hypotheses are then aggregated hierarchically in a tree structure. At each internal node of the tree, a new hypothesis is formed by computing the Radon point of the hypotheses from its children nodes. The main theoretical contribution is the claim that this scheme can reduce the runtime of a polynomial-time base learner to polylogarithmic, at the cost of a quasi-polynomial increase in the number of processors and the total amount of data required. The authors prove that the probability of obtaining a poor hypothesis decreases doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. Empirically, the Radon machine is shown to achieve significant speedups over sequential base learners and to be competitive with, or superior to, state-of-the-art parallel implementations in Spark's MLlib in both runtime and predictive performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of Approach:** The application of Radon points for aggregating model hypotheses in a parallel learning framework is a genuinely novel and creative idea. It offers a principled alternative to the more common approach of simple model averaging.

2.  **Theoretical Ambition:** The paper makes a laudable attempt to connect practical parallel machine learning to fundamental concepts in computational complexity theory, specifically Nick's Class (NC). The theoretical analysis, which aims to prove a reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic runtime, addresses a significant open question in the field.

3.  **Black-Box Applicability:** A major practical advantage is that the proposed scheme treats the base learning algorithm as a black box. This allows for the parallelization of existing, well-understood algorithms without requiring modifications to their internal workings, which is highly desirable for practitioners.

4.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation reports substantial speedups (up to 700x) over sequential learners. Furthermore, the Radon machine demonstrates strong performance against established parallel learning libraries like Spark MLlib, in some cases achieving better accuracy in a fraction of the time.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novel core idea, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws, ranging from minor formatting issues to critical gaps in the theoretical analysis.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript lacks the polish and precision expected of a NIPS submission.
    *   There are numerous formatting and typesetting errors. For instance, a NIPS 2017 conference header appears mid-sentence in the introduction. The bibliography is sloppily formatted, with many entries lacking publication years or other essential details.
    *   Notational conventions are abused. In Eq. (1), `L(D)` is used to denote the output of the `argmin` operation. In Eq. (2), `argmin` is incorrectly used where `min` is meant. These inaccuracies hinder a clear understanding.
    *   The description of the algorithm and its analysis contains confusing terminology. Algorithm 1 and the surrounding text repeatedly describe partitioning a fixed set of data or hypotheses into "iid subsets," which is a contradiction in terms. This should be "random partitions," but the distinction is important for the analysis.
    *   Key proofs are relegated to appendices (e.g., the proof of Theorem 4 in Appendix A.1) which were not provided with the manuscript. This makes it impossible to fully vet the central claims of the paper.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Assumptions:** The theoretical foundations appear shaky upon closer inspection.
    *   The core result relies on the hypothesis space `F` having a finite Radon number `r`. For a `d`-dimensional Euclidean space, `r = d+2`. This creates a severe, and inadequately addressed, dependency on the dimensionality of the parameter space. The number of required processors (`r^h`) and data samples grows with `(d+2)^h`. For high-dimensional models common in modern machine learning, this requirement makes the method completely impractical. The discussion section dismisses this fundamental limitation too casually.
    *   The proof sketch for Proposition 2 in Section 7 is unconvincing and potentially flawed. The argument states that `P(Q(r) > ε)` is bounded by the probability of a specific pair of points being `ε`-bad, and then invokes a union bound. A more careful analysis using binomial probabilities yields a bound of approximately `(r(r-1)/2) * P(Q(F) > ε)^2`, which differs from the paper's claimed bound of `(r * P(Q(F) > ε))^2`. The provided reasoning does not rigorously lead to the stated result.
    *   Theorem 4, the central result on polylogarithmic runtime, hinges on the assumption that the Radon number `r` "can be upper bounded by a function polylogarithmic in the sample complexity." This is an extremely strong assumption for which no justification is provided. Since `r = d+2`, this implies that the model's dimension `d` must be polylogarithmic in the sample complexity. This is not a general property of learning algorithms and seems to severely restrict the applicability of the main theorem.
    *   There is a critical disconnect between the algorithm's operation on a fixed dataset `D` and the theoretical analysis, which assumes hypotheses are generated from learners trained on iid samples from the true underlying distribution. Partitioning a finite dataset introduces dependencies that the current analysis does not account for.

3.  **Evaluation:**
    *   The empirical study only explores `h=1` and `h=max`. A more thorough investigation of the key hyperparameter `h` is needed to understand the trade-offs between parallelism, runtime, and accuracy.
    *   The argument that the method can handle non-linear models via explicit feature embeddings is weak, as this directly leads to the high-dimensional setting where the method's requirements become prohibitive.

**Recommendation**

I recommend **rejection** for this paper.

While the core concept of the Radon machine is intriguing and potentially significant, the execution in this manuscript is far from satisfactory. The paper suffers from a pervasive lack of rigor, demonstrated by sloppy presentation, notational errors, and—most importantly—critical gaps and unsubstantiated assumptions in its theoretical analysis. The practical applicability of the method appears to be severely limited by its dependence on low dimensionality, a weakness that is not given due prominence. The omission of key proofs in the appendices further prevents a complete evaluation. The accumulation of these flaws, both minor and major, overshadows the novelty of the idea and undermines confidence in the paper's conclusions. The work is promising but requires a substantial overhaul to meet the standards of NIPS.
