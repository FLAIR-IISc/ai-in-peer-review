

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The authors propose using Radon points (from convex geometry) to aggregate hypotheses from parallel learning on random data subsets, claiming a reduction in runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in sample size while maintaining the same error guarantees. The approach is presented as a black-box parallelization method applicable to a broad class of learning algorithms including SVMs, regularized least squares, and logistic regression.

The work connects to theoretical computer science through its relation to Nick's Class (NC) of parallelizable problems. It builds on previous parallel learning approaches like averaging-based methods [32, 45, 46] and stochastic gradient descent [34], but claims to provide a provable polylogarithmic runtime improvement where previous approaches fail to achieve such guarantees. The paper also relates to Long and Servedio's open question about parallel learning of half-spaces [21].

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis showing that the Radon Machine achieves doubly-exponential improvement in confidence with each aggregation level (Theorem 3). The connection to Radon's theorem and the careful derivation of sample complexity (Equation 5) are strong theoretical contributions.

2. **Practical Validation:** The empirical results are compelling, showing 80-700x speedups on real datasets while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The comparison with Spark MLlib algorithms and averaging-at-the-end baselines is well-designed and demonstrates the approach's practical advantages.

3. **Novel Aggregation Mechanism:** The use of Radon points instead of averaging is a genuinely novel approach to hypothesis aggregation, with the paper convincingly showing its superiority in maintaining confidence bounds.

4. **Clarity of Core Concept:** The Radon Machine algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly presented, and the explanation of how Radon points work in the context of convex optimization is accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practical Applicability Concerns:** The paper requires a quasi-polynomial number of processing units (rh) to achieve the claimed polylogarithmic runtime. For large datasets, this could mean thousands of processors, which is not always available in practice. The discussion of handling fewer processors (Section 5) only addresses this as a linear slowdown, but doesn't explore how this impacts the practical feasibility.

2. **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments are limited to moderately sized datasets (e.g., SUSY with 5M instances) and specific learning algorithms (SVM, logistic regression). The paper doesn't demonstrate the approach on extremely large datasets or with more complex models (e.g., deep learning), which would better showcase the scalability claims.

3. **Theoretical Assumptions:** The claim that runtime is "polylogarithmic" is technically correct but potentially misleading. The runtime is polylogarithmic in the sample size N, but the number of processors required grows as rh (quasi-polynomial in 1/ε and log 1/δ), which may not be practical for very high confidence requirements.

4. **Missing Comparison to Recent Work:** The paper cites recent parallel learning work but doesn't adequately address more recent approaches like those in [22] or [17], which have made progress on communication-efficient distributed learning. This weakens the claim of novelty.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to understanding the parallelizability of learning algorithms. The Radon Machine is a novel approach with strong theoretical guarantees and promising empirical results. However, the practical limitations regarding processor requirements and the limited empirical scope on large-scale problems prevent it from being a top-tier NIPS contribution.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Expand the empirical evaluation to include larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) and more diverse learning algorithms (e.g., deep neural networks) to better demonstrate scalability.

2. Provide a more nuanced discussion of the practical trade-offs between the number of processors required and the achievable speedup. A table showing the processor requirements for different confidence levels would be valuable.

3. Address recent work on communication-efficient distributed learning (e.g., [17, 22]) to better position the contribution within the current literature.

4. Clarify the relationship between the Radon number (r = d+2) and the dimensionality of real-world problems. For high-dimensional data (e.g., text), the required number of processors would be prohibitively large without additional dimensionality reduction.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a novel theoretical approach that addresses an important question in parallel machine learning. While the practical limitations prevent it from being a breakthrough contribution, the theoretical insights are valuable. With expanded empirical validation and clearer discussion of practical constraints, this could be a strong contribution to NIPS.

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Pro-accept arguments:** Novel theoretical approach, strong mathematical foundation, compelling empirical results showing significant speedups while maintaining accuracy, clear presentation of the core idea.

**Con-accept arguments:** Practical processor requirements limit real-world applicability, empirical scope is limited to moderately sized datasets, theoretical claims about runtime could be more precisely qualified.
