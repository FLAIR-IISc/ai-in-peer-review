- Proposes the Radon machine: train base learners on iid subsets and aggregate hypotheses by iterated Radon points in a tree instead of averaging.
- Main theory: for hypothesis spaces with finite Radon number, the probability an aggregated hypothesis is ε-bad decreases doubly-exponentially with tree height h.
- Under sample-complexity assumptions and when Radon number r grows at most polylogarithmically with required sample size M, Radon machine matches sequential (ε, Δ) guarantees in polylogarithmic time using many processors and more total samples.
- Empirically shows large wall-clock speedups over single-machine baselines, competitive accuracy/runtime vs Spark MLlib, and AUC improvements over end-point averaging; code released.
- Connects to PAC/NC-learnability, robust aggregation (centerpoint/Tukey depth), and distributed ERM/SGD literature, arguing averaging/mini-batch SGD bounds don’t yield polylog time.
- Clear, general black-box scheme applicable to many convex regularized learners with finite-dimensional parametrization.
- Novel use of Radon points for aggregation with an elegant doubly-exponential decay bound assuming iid hypotheses.
- Provides explicit runtime/sample trade-offs, NC discussion, and aggregation cost O(r^3) per node.
- Solid empirical coverage across classification, regression, and multiclass settings showing substantial speedups and competitive accuracy.
- Paper acknowledges limitations (dimension dependence, non-linear models) and suggests mitigations like random projections or explicit features.
- Notational ambiguity: several instances use “2 h” where “2^h” appears intended; needs fixing (Theorem 3, Eq. (4)).
- Strong assumptions for polylog runtime: requires Radon number r to be polylogarithmic in sample size M and aggregation cost O(r^3) may be prohibitive in high dimensions.
- Independence assumption concerns: theory treats hypotheses as newly iid at each aggregation level but algorithm uses a single partition—needs explicit justification that independence holds.
- Sample-complexity assumed specific form NL0(ε, δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k; should state precise conditions/constants and robustness to other forms in main text.
- Practical aggregation overheads: O(r^3) can be heavy; experiments mostly on moderate-dimensional linear models—need to report aggregation time fraction and scaling with d and h.
- Comparisons limited: should include other robust aggregators (geometric median, coordinate-wise median/MoM) and ablations with identical base learners but different aggregators.
- Empirics don’t validate theoretical confidence amplification: need measurements showing Δ or tail error shrinkage with h to confirm doubly-exponential decay.
- Quality: theory is crisp and proved, but gaps remain in exponent clarity, finite-partition independence, and practical relevance of r polylog M condition.
- Clarity: generally well written; fix exponent notation, define “ld” on first use, and highlight exact conditions for Theorem 4.
- Suggest moving key Appendix C.2 instantiations to main text and include algorithmic description and numerical stability notes for computing Radon points.
- Originality: iterated Radon-point aggregation in distributed learning is novel and motivates robust-depth arguments.
- Significance: conceptually significant and practically promising for moderate-dimensional convex models when many processors are available, but r-dependence limits impact in high-dimensional/non-convex settings.
- Questions/suggestions: clarify partitioning scheme that ensures iid child sets; empirically show Δ shrinking with h on synthetic data.
- Questions/suggestions: report aggregation vs training time as d and h vary, include high-d experiments with random projections and quantify projection error.
- Questions/suggestions: compare to geometric median / median-of-means aggregators and discuss numerical stability solving r×r systems with collinear features.
- Pros for acceptance: novel theoretical contribution, strong conceptual link to robust geometry, positive empirical speedups, and clear potential for follow-on work.
- Cons for acceptance: restrictive practical guarantees due to dimension/Radon-number, clarity issues around exponents and independence, and limited high-dimensional/alternative-aggregator exploration.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — promising and grounded but needs clarifications and stronger empirical/theoretical treatment of assumptions.
