This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, the "Radon machine," for regularized risk minimization algorithms, aiming to achieve polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial increase in data and processing units. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm to random subsets of data and iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, which offer doubly-exponential confidence gains compared to averaging. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the runtime and sample complexity and demonstrate empirical improvements over both the sequential base learner and Spark MLlib on several datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical need for efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms as datasets grow. The theoretical contributions, particularly the polylogarithmic runtime guarantee, are significant. The use of Radon points for aggregation is a novel and interesting approach, offering a strong theoretical advantage over simple averaging. The empirical evaluation provides compelling evidence of the scheme’s practical effectiveness, showing substantial speedups compared to standard approaches. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, with key concepts defined and results clearly stated.

However, there are several weaknesses. The theoretical analysis, while impressive, relies on several assumptions (convexity, finite Radon number) that may not hold in all practical scenarios. The paper acknowledges these limitations but doesn't thoroughly discuss their implications or potential mitigation strategies.  The empirical evaluation, while comprehensive, primarily focuses on datasets where the number of features is significantly smaller than the number of instances. The performance on high-dimensional datasets or more complex models remains less clear. The paper does mention potential workarounds for high dimensionality (random projections, low-rank approximations) but doesn't include empirical results demonstrating their effectiveness.  The discussion of related work is thorough but could be more critical; for example, a more detailed comparison with existing distributed stochastic gradient descent methods (and their limitations) would be valuable.  The practical overhead of computing Radon points, especially in high dimensions, isn’t fully explored – while the theoretical analysis shows polylogarithmic scaling, the constant factors might be significant. The Spark cluster setup is briefly described, but details on the cluster’s configuration (e.g., memory per node) are lacking, hindering reproducibility. The connection to Nick’s Class is made but feels somewhat tangential, and the discussion of its limitations could be expanded.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The Radon machine introduces a novel approach to parallel learning based on Radon points, offering a unique theoretical guarantee.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis of the scheme’s runtime and sample complexity.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate substantial speedups and comparable performance to existing methods.
*   **Significance:** Efficient parallelization is crucial for scaling machine learning to large datasets, making this contribution potentially impactful.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantees rely on potentially restrictive assumptions (convexity, finite Radon number).
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The evaluation focuses on datasets with a specific dimensionality characteristic.
*   **Overhead of Radon Point Computation:** The practical cost of computing Radon points is not fully addressed.
*   **Tangential Connection to Complexity Theory:** The link to Nick's Class feels somewhat forced and underdeveloped.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable and potentially significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The novelty of the approach, the strength of the theoretical analysis, and the compelling empirical results outweigh the limitations.  While the assumptions and limited scope of the empirical evaluation are concerns, they do not invalidate the core contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the limitations regarding dimensionality, computational overhead, and a more in-depth comparison to related distributed optimization methods. A stronger discussion of the assumptions and their practical impact would also be beneficial.
