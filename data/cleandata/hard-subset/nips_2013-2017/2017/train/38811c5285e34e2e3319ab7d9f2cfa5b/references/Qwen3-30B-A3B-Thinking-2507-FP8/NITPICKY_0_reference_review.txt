

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper proposes the "Radon machine," a parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms that claims to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic while maintaining error guarantees. The key insight is using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses from parallel learners, with theoretical guarantees showing doubly-exponential confidence improvement with tree height.

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically grounded contribution to parallel machine learning. The Radon point aggregation technique is novel and well-motivated, with clear theoretical guarantees (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3) showing how confidence improves with aggregation depth. The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups (721x on SUSY with 150 processors) while maintaining predictive performance comparable to Spark's implementations. The black-box nature of the parallelization scheme is a significant practical advantage.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
1. **Mathematical Notation**: Critical errors in equations (5) and (6) where "ld 1 δ" should be "log(1/δ)" and the exponent in Theorem 3 is miswritten as "2 h" instead of "2^h" (equation 4). This fundamentally misrepresents the theoretical claims.

2. **Confusing Terminology**: The paper states the Radon machine runs "in polylogarithmic time on quasipolynomially many processing units" (Theorem 4) but then claims "quasi-polynomial" in the abstract (typo) and later "quasipolynomial" (correct term). These should be consistent.

3. **Theoretical Claim Contradiction**: The paper claims to answer "is consistent regularised risk minimisation possible in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors" (Theorem 4), but Theorem 4 explicitly requires "quasipolynomially many processing units," contradicting the claim. This undermines the central theoretical contribution.

4. **Algorithm Description**: In Algorithm 1, line 6 states "calculate Radon points r(S1), ..., r(Sri) in parallel" where each Si should be a set of r points, but the notation suggests Si has size ri, which is incorrect.

### Experimental Inconsistencies
1. **Processor Count**: The paper states "using 150 processors" for speedups but describes a cluster with "5 worker nodes, 25 processors per node" (125 processors total). This inconsistency is confusing.

2. **Speedup Numbers**: Claims "between 80 and around 700-times faster" but then specifies "721 times faster" for SUSY. The "around 700" is vague and inconsistent with the specific value.

3. **Comparison Metrics**: Claims "outperforms by at least 10%" in AUC but doesn't specify the metric (AUC? accuracy?).

### Other Concerns
1. **Missing Citations**: The paper cites [27, 36] as references for "critical application scenarios" but doesn't include these references in the bibliography.

2. **Theoretical Scope**: The paper claims to answer an "open problem" about parallel machine learning, but doesn't clearly state what the open problem is or how this specifically resolves it.

3. **Empirical Details**: The paper states "the Radon machine is one order of magnitude faster" on SUSY/HIGGS but 721x is more than one order of magnitude (which would be 10x).

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical approach with promising empirical results, the technical errors in the mathematical formulation are severe enough to undermine the theoretical contribution. The inconsistency between the claimed polynomial processor requirement and the actual quasipolynomial requirement, combined with the misrepresentation of the exponential improvement (2^h vs 2h), significantly weakens the paper's main claim. These are not minor formatting issues but fundamental mathematical errors that change the interpretation of the results.

The empirical results are strong, but the theoretical foundation is compromised by these errors. The paper would be much stronger with a clear, consistent presentation of the mathematical results, particularly in the key theorems. The confusion around the number of processors required (polynomial vs quasipolynomial) is particularly problematic for the claimed contribution to Nick's Class (NC).

The paper has significant potential but requires substantial revision to correct the mathematical errors and clarify the theoretical contribution. As it stands, the technical flaws prevent us from accepting the paper in its current form. The errors in the core theoretical claims are too severe for a NIPS submission, regardless of the promising empirical results.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper's theoretical claims are undermined by multiple mathematical errors and inconsistencies that prevent proper evaluation of the core contribution. While the empirical results are strong, the theoretical foundation is too flawed to support the paper's main claims. The authors should address these issues before resubmission.
