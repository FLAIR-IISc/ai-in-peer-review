- Proposes the Radon machine: black-box parallelization for convex RRM in Rd that trains base learners on rh subsets and aggregates via an h-level tree of Radon points (r=d+2 per node).  
- Main theoretical result: Radon aggregation reduces the ε-bad probability doubly-exponentially with depth: if base error probability is δ then aggregate error ≤ (rδ)^(2^h).  
- With suitable h the approach can turn polynomial-time learning into polylogarithmic-time in sample complexity, at the cost of using rh processors and more data.  
- Empirical results show large wall-clock speedups (80–700×) vs single-node base learners and competitive performance vs Spark MLlib across classification, regression, and multi-class tasks.  
- Connects to convex geometry (Radon’s theorem, center points/Tukey depth) and contrasts with model averaging, ADMM, and SGD, claiming provable robustness to individual bad models.  

Strengths:
- Clear, simple, black-box algorithm that is easy to implement and reason about.  
- Elegant and tight core probabilistic bound based on requiring two ε-bad children for an ε-bad parent.  
- Conceptual advance linking robust geometric aggregation (Radon points) to general convex ERM/RRM parallelization.  
- Careful theoretical runtime discussion, including aggregation cost (O(r^3) per node) and processor/data trade-offs.  
- Persuasive empirical evaluation showing substantial speedups with comparable AUC/RMSE.  
- Honest discussion of limitations and possible extensions (random projections, block coordinate descent).  

Weaknesses / Detailed concerns:
- Sampling/independence: "divide D into rh iid subsets" is imprecise—needs precise sampling protocol and clarification whether sibling hypotheses are independent.  
- Polylog-time claim depends on a strong assumption that d is polylogarithmic in sample complexity; this dependence should be made explicit in the main text and regimes where it fails discussed.  
- Requirement δ ≤ 1/(2r) can make necessary n large when r=d+2 is big; quantification for common learners (logistic regression, SVM) is needed.  
- Empirics do not test the theoretical confidence improvement with increasing h (no repeated-trials showing Δ vs h).  
- Missing comparisons/discussion of robust aggregation baselines (geometric median, coordinate-wise median, median-of-means).  
- Aggregation cost O(r^3) and numerical stability for large d may dominate; need aggregation-time breakdowns and conditioning discussion.  

Clarity / Presentation:
- Fix the “iid subsets” phrasing and precisely state sampling assumptions.  
- Restate Theorem 4 and explicitly define “polylogarithmic time” and “quasi-polynomial processors” with dependencies on ε, Δ, d in the main text.  
- Include the explicit linear system for computing Radon points (or a figure) in the main text and note computational/numerical considerations.  

Originality:
- Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation appears novel and interesting; add references and contrasts to robust aggregation methods for completeness.  

Significance:
- Theoretical framing is conceptually significant, but practicality depends on dimensionality and processor availability; empirical results support practical value for moderate d and small h.  

Pros (summary):
- Novel, provably robust aggregation with strong probability bounds; black-box applicability and strong empirical speedups.  

Cons (summary):
- Ambiguous sampling/independence assumptions, restrictive dimensionality assumption for polylog claims, potentially large aggregation costs for high d, missing robust-baseline comparisons, and no empirical validation of confidence gains with h.  

Recommendation:
- Borderline accept: promising idea with solid theory and experiments; requires clarifications on sampling/independence, explicit dimensional/processor assumptions, and comparisons to robust aggregation methods to be a stronger contribution.
