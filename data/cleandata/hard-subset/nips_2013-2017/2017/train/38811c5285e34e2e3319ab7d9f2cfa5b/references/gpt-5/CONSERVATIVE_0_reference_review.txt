Summary
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization. The method trains a base learner independently on rh disjoint data shards and aggregates the resulting hypotheses along a depth-h tree via Radon points (r = Radon number, d+2 in Rd). Theoretically, if a base learner achieves an ε-regret with failure probability δ, then one level of Radon aggregation reduces the failure probability to at most (rδ)2, and h levels give (rδ)2^h. Assuming a base learner with polynomial sample/runtime complexity and r bounded polylogarithmically in the sample complexity, the authors argue that, with rh processors and increased data, one can achieve polylogarithmic runtime in the sample size needed to reach a target (ε, Δ). Empirically, they report large speedups (80–700x with 150 processors) on several datasets, comparable AUC to Spark MLlib methods, and superiority over naive averaging-at-the-end.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, black-box scheme that leverages a clean geometric idea (Radon points) for confidence amplification.
- Clear theoretical statement that Radon aggregation contracts failure probability doubly exponentially in tree height h, under convexity and finite Radon number.
- Theoretical connection to parallel complexity (Nick’s Class) is interesting and potentially thought-provoking.
- Working implementation and empirical studies on multiple datasets; code availability is appreciated.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- The headline “polylogarithmic time” rests on strong assumptions that seem rarely met in practice: (i) quasi-polynomial number of processors rh, (ii) substantial extra data (r^h more samples), (iii) finite Radon number r = d+2 bounded by a function polylogarithmic in the requisite sample size (not typical when d is moderate/large), and (iv) base shard-level learners must already achieve δ ≤ 1/(2r) on potentially very small shards (n ≈ log M under the parameter choice used to derive polylog time).
- The practical independence assumption is shaky: Algorithm 1 “divides” the dataset into rh subsets called iid; partitioning without replacement yields dependent shards. The theory requires iid draws from D for the distributional arguments.
- The sample complexity form NL0(ε, δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k is asserted without tight conditions; typical bounds are additive (e.g., O((d + log 1/δ)/ε^2)). It would help to specify precise conditions for which the assumed form holds.
- Theorem 4’s statement is somewhat circular (“if r can be upper bounded by a function polylogarithmic in sample complexity…”). Since r = d+2 in Euclidean settings, this effectively requires d = polylog(N), which is restrictive and undermines the generality claimed.
- Aggregation cost: computing Radon points via solving r×r systems costs O(r^3) per node per level. With r = d+2 this is O(d^3); the paper downplays this, but in moderate/high-d settings aggregation can dominate.

Clarity
- Several claims overreach or are imprecise (e.g., “reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic” without simultaneously foregrounding the quasi-polynomial processor and data blowups).
- It is unclear whether shards are sampled with or without replacement, and how “iid subsets” is enforced.
- The link between the theoretical δ and empirical metrics (AUC, RMSE) is not established; there is no estimation of failure probabilities per shard to validate prerequisites for the guarantees.

Originality
- Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation is novel in this context and technically appealing. The broader idea of “learn on shards, aggregate” is well-known; the paper positions itself against averaging-at-the-end and SGD-based methods, but some key related lines are underexplored (e.g., Hogwild!/asynchronous SGD, parameter server/Downpour SGD, CoCoA variants). Some are mentioned (ADMM, distributed mini-batch), but the experimental comparison omits these stronger baselines.

Significance/empirical validation
- Reported speedups are impressive, but the comparison set is limited and may not be fully fair. It is unclear whether Spark baselines were extensively tuned, whether multi-threaded or distributed-optimized base learners were used, and whether communication/computation trade-offs are on par.
- The averaging baseline appears underpowered; claims of ≥10% AUC advantage need significance testing and error bars. For regression, the Radon machine degrades RMSE notably (12.57 vs 13.64 on YearPredictionMSD), which calls into question the practical utility when accuracy matters.
- No experiments validate the doubly-exponential decay of failure probability or the δ ≤ 1/(2r) condition on shards. No systematic study of the effect of h, d, and Radon aggregation’s numerical stability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Tighten and temper the theoretical claims. Clearly state the exact processor/data blowups needed for the polylog-time conclusion and the conditions under which r is polylogarithmic.
- Empirically verify the theory: estimate shard-level failure probabilities, vary h systematically, and demonstrate the predicted confidence amplification on controlled synthetic setups.
- Include stronger baselines (e.g., Hogwild!, parameter-server SGD, CoCoA, modern communication-efficient methods) and provide rigorous tuning and statistical significance.
- Analyze and report aggregation cost vs d, and discuss numerical stability of Radon computation. Evaluate random projections/low-rank embeddings with theory-backed guarantees if they are needed to keep r small.
- Clarify sampling (with/without replacement), independence assumptions, and provide full reproducibility details.

Pros
- Clean, general idea; black-box applicability.
- Nontrivial theoretical guarantee for confidence amplification using Radon points.
- Implementation and empirical results indicating substantial speedups.

Cons
- Strong assumptions and quasi-polynomial resource requirements; limited path to NC-style efficiency in common settings.
- Overstated claims about polylog runtime; lack of empirical validation of key theoretical preconditions.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair empirical comparison to state-of-the-art distributed methods; no significance analysis.
- Practical limitations in moderate/high dimensions due to O(d^3) aggregation.

Recommendation
Leaning reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current paper overclaims relative to what is rigorously established and empirically validated. A revised version with tighter theory, stronger baselines, and experiments directly probing the claimed confidence/runtime trade-offs would be much more compelling.
