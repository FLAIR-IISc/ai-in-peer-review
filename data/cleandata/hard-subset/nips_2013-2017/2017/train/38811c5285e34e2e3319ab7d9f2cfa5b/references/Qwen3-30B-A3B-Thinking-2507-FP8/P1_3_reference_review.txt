

# Peer Review: "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

## Summary

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The key innovation is using Radon points (a geometric concept from convex geometry) for aggregating hypotheses from parallel training on data subsets, rather than the commonly used averaging approach. The authors prove that their scheme achieves the same confidence and error bounds as sequential learning, but with runtime reduced from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity. The theoretical contribution establishes that the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis decreases doubly exponentially with each level of aggregation (h). Empirically, the Radon Machine achieves 80-700x speedups over sequential learning on 150 processors while maintaining comparable predictive performance to Spark's MLlib implementations.

The paper connects to theoretical work on parallel learning (e.g., Vitter and Lin [42], Long and Servedio [21]) and addresses the fundamental open question: "Is consistent regularized risk minimization possible in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors?" It distinguishes itself from existing approaches like averaging-based parallelization [32, 45, 46] by providing rigorous theoretical guarantees for the confidence improvement.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides a rigorous proof of the doubly exponential confidence improvement (Theorem 3), which is a significant theoretical advance over existing work. The connection to Nick's Class (NC) of parallelizable problems provides a meaningful theoretical context.

2. **Novel Approach**: The use of Radon points instead of averaging for hypothesis aggregation is conceptually elegant and provides a clear theoretical advantage. The authors demonstrate that Radon points are more robust to ε-bad hypotheses than averages.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against both a naive averaging baseline and state-of-the-art Spark implementations. The results consistently show significant speedups without sacrificing predictive performance.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The black-box nature of the parallelization scheme (requiring no algorithm-specific modifications) makes it broadly applicable across different learning algorithms, which is a major practical advantage.

5. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-structured with clear mathematical definitions, theorems, and proofs. The empirical results are effectively presented through well-designed figures.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Theoretical Analysis**: While the paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, it restricts the analysis to Euclidean spaces with finite Radon number (r = d+2). This excludes many non-linear models and high-dimensional problems, though the authors acknowledge this limitation in Section 5. More concrete discussion of how to handle these cases would strengthen the paper.

2. **Implementation Details**: The paper claims the scheme is "black-box" but requires calculation of Radon points (via solving linear systems), which may require algorithm-specific implementation. The connection between the theoretical Radon point calculation and practical implementation isn't fully fleshed out.

3. **Inconsistency in Terminology**: The abstract mentions "quasi-polynomial" number of processing units, but Theorem 4 states "polynomially many." This inconsistency needs clarification.

4. **Communication Complexity**: While the paper mentions low communication costs, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of communication complexity compared to other parallel approaches, which is critical for distributed settings.

5. **Limited Empirical Scope**: The empirical evaluation focuses on moderately sized datasets and linear models, with less emphasis on very large datasets or non-linear models (despite acknowledging these as future work).

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of parallel machine learning with a novel and provably effective approach. The Radon Machine addresses a fundamental problem in the field and provides a clear path toward achieving polylogarithmic runtime for a broad class of learning algorithms.

The main weaknesses are related to the scope of the theoretical analysis and some implementation details that could be clarified. However, these do not undermine the paper's core contributions. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate practical relevance.

The paper advances the state of the art in parallel machine learning by providing a theoretically grounded approach with rigorous guarantees, rather than relying on heuristics. It makes a strong contribution to the field that will be of interest to both theoretical and applied machine learning researchers.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS. The paper addresses an important problem, makes a novel contribution, provides strong theoretical and empirical validation, and is well-written. While there are some limitations to the scope of the analysis, these are acknowledged by the authors and represent natural directions for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution.

## Key Arguments for Acceptance

* Novel use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation with rigorous theoretical guarantees
* Doubly exponential confidence improvement with aggregation depth
* Substantial empirical speedups (80-700x) without sacrificing predictive performance
* Black-box parallelization scheme applicable to a wide range of algorithms
* Clear connection to theoretical computer science (Nick's Class)

## Key Considerations for Authors

1. Clarify the consistency between "quasi-polynomial" and "polynomial" in the abstract and Theorem 4.
2. Expand the discussion of how to handle non-Euclidean spaces or high-dimensional problems in practice.
3. Provide more concrete details about the implementation of Radon points to better justify the "black-box" claim.
4. Consider adding a brief analysis of communication complexity in distributed settings.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field and deserves publication at NIPS.
