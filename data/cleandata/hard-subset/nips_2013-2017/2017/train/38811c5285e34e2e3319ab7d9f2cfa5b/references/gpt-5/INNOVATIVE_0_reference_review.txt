Summary
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization that aggregates many weak hypotheses using Radon points in an r-ary tree. The key theoretical ingredient is that the Radon point of r hypotheses reduces the probability of being ε-bad from δ to at most (rδ)^2 for any convex (indeed quasi-convex) regret, and iterating this aggregation h times yields a doubly-exponential reduction: Δ ≤ (rδ)^{2^h}. Under assumptions on the base learner’s sample complexity and hypothesis space (finite Radon number; Euclidean parameterization of dimension d with r=d+2), the authors argue that one can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the target sample complexity by using rh processors and more data. Empirically, on a 150-core Spark cluster, the scheme is 80–700× faster than the base learners while achieving comparable predictive performance, and is competitive with Spark MLlib baselines; it also outperforms “averaging-at-the-end” in AUC with similar runtime.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against averaging-based distributed learning and stochastic-optimization-based schemes (mini-batch, ADMM, LBFGS in MLlib), arguing that existing confidence bounds for averaging do not lead to polylog runtime, and that standard distributed optimization still scales in iterations with precision. It builds on classical geometry (Radon’s theorem), centerpoint approximations via iterated Radon points, and standard generalization tools (VC/Rademacher complexity). To my knowledge, using Radon points explicitly as a model aggregator in parallel learning with double-exponential confidence amplification is novel.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: Leveraging Radon points as an aggregation primitive is fresh and intellectually appealing; the double-exponential reduction in failure probability is a clean and striking result.
- Black-box applicability: The scheme composes with many convex regularized learners without algorithm-specific derivations, which is practically attractive.
- Theory–complexity connection: The discussion linking to NC and polylog-time criteria (with caveats) is thought-provoking and advances the conversation on the limits of parallel learning.
- Empirics: Clear speedups in wall-clock time on a realistic cluster; performance competitive with strong MLlib baselines; averaging-at-the-end baseline is included and (appropriately) outperformed in accuracy.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions limiting practicality: r=d+2 makes Radon computation O(r^3) per node. For large d, this is prohibitive; the theory requires r to be polylog in the needed sample size. Experiments avoid high-d regimes; the proposed remedies (random projections, low-rank features, block-coordinate variants) are not validated empirically.
- Processor and data requirements: Achieving polylog runtime requires quasi-polynomially many processors and more data (rh subsets). While the de-parallelization argument is sensible, the gap between theory (quasi-polynomial processors) and practice (≤150 cores) remains.
- Starting confidence δ: The analysis requires δ ≤ 1/(2r) for the base learner on small subsets; guidance on how to ensure/estimate this in practice is limited, and the empirical section does not relate observed AUC to theoretical (ε,Δ) guarantees.
- Clarity/notation: Some technical parts could be clearer (e.g., “ld” notation for logarithms; precise conditions under which “one bad, r−1 good implies Radon output good” holds; communication and memory costs are not quantified end-to-end).
- Baselines: It would strengthen the case to compare against robust aggregation alternatives (e.g., geometric median/median-of-means style aggregators) and strong distributed solvers configured for the same models (e.g., MLlib LBFGS/ADMM with tuned communication).

Quality
The core probability bound (Proposition 2) and its iterative application (Theorem 3) are simple and compelling; the quasi-convex argument is neat. The runtime accounting is reasonable under the stated parallel model. However, the practical feasibility hinges on r and δ, and the paper does not fully close the loop on those constraints in experiments. Empirical comparisons are solid, though additional ablations (aggregation cost vs learning cost; sensitivity to h and r; communication breakdown) would improve rigor.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. Algorithm 1 is clear. I recommend clarifying: (i) “ld” vs log base and constants, (ii) how independence across aggregation subsets is maintained, (iii) practical guidance on choosing h given d, N, and target Δ, and (iv) explicit reporting of aggregation time vs learning time.

Originality
High. The Radon-point–based aggregation and its doubly-exponential confidence amplification for parallel learning appears new and is a meaningful shift from averaging or optimization-centric distributed methods.

Significance
Conceptually significant: it opens a promising direction for black-box parallelization with strong confidence guarantees. Practically promising in low-to-moderate dimensional linear settings; broader impact will depend on validating proposed strategies for high-d/nonlinear models.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please clarify the “ld” notation and constants in sample complexity formulas, and provide a concrete recipe for choosing h in practice.
- Report the aggregation cost separately, and show how total runtime scales with d and h.
- Evaluate a high-dimensional case using random features or projections to demonstrate feasibility when d is large.
- Compare against geometric median or median-of-means aggregations if possible.
- Discuss memory footprint (storing rh models) and end-to-end communication volume on your cluster.
- Provide an empirical estimate of δ on subsets and how it relates to your chosen h.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a genuinely new aggregation mechanism with elegant theory.
- Black-box, broadly applicable concept that could inspire follow-up work.
- Strong empirical speedups without sacrificing accuracy on several datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical constraints (large d, δ requirement) not fully addressed; high-d experiments missing.
- Gap between theoretical processor assumptions and experimental scale.
- Limited comparisons to alternative robust aggregations.

Overall recommendation
Weak Accept. The idea is original and bold, with clean theory and encouraging empirical results. Despite limitations and open questions about scalability in high dimensions and practical parameter selection, the paper charts a compelling new direction that the community can build on.
