Summary
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization. The method splits the data into r^h chunks, trains the base learner on each chunk in parallel, and aggregates hypotheses in a tree via Radon points, allegedly improving confidence doubly-exponentially with tree height. Under assumptions on sample complexity and Radon numbers (r = d + 2 in Rd), the authors claim that, for a fixed (Œµ,Œî), the Radon machine achieves polylogarithmic runtime in the sequential sample complexity using quasi-polynomially many processors. Experiments on several datasets compare the scheme to the base learners, to averaging-at-the-end, and to Spark MLlib, showing substantial wall-clock speedups and similar AUC.

Strengths
- Simple, model-agnostic parallelization that treats the learner as a black box.
- Clever use of Radon points/Tukey depth; robustness properties versus averaging are interesting.
- A clean conceptual separation between learning subproblems and aggregation.
- Empirical results indicate large speedups (80‚Äì700√ó on 150 processors) and competitive AUC across several datasets.
- Code is provided; discussion connects to NC and P-completeness, and considers communication aspects.

Detailed comments and questions
- Clarify precisely how subsets are generated to ensure independence (with replacement?) and quantify the effect on total sample usage.
- Correct Theorem 3 (and all downstream corollaries, including Eq. (4), Eq. (6), and the choice of h) to reflect the true exponent on r, or provide a tighter Proposition 2 that removes r dependence (unlikely with current argument).
- Provide explicit Œ±Œµ,Œ≤Œµ,k for the VC/Rademacher cases claimed, and show how they imply the adopted sample complexity.
- Fix communication size to O(d) and discuss memory/serialization costs for high d.
- Report full experimental details: hyperparameters searched, stopping criteria, threading/BLAS configs, data loading, per-fold timings, and confidence intervals.
- Include stronger baselines and ablations (weighted averaging, depth-based aggregations vs Radon, different h vs processors constraints).
- Discuss the effect of d on runtime empirically; even simple synthetic experiments varying d would be informative.

Pros
- Elegant, broadly applicable idea; robust aggregation via Radon points is novel in this context.
- Clear high-level motivation; practical speedups demonstrated on several datasets.

Cons
- Central theoretical bound appears incorrect; independence assumptions are mismatched to the algorithm as described.
- Strong/unrealistic assumptions needed for the flagship polylog-time claim; data inefficiency is severe and under-discussed.
- Empirical comparisons lack crucial baselines and methodological rigor; reporting is incomplete.
- Significant clarity/formatting issues.

Recommendation: Reject
The idea is promising and could be impactful with corrected theory, clearer assumptions, and stronger empirical validation, but the current draft contains a major technical error in the main bound and numerous methodological and clarity shortcomings.
