

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to use Radon points (from convex geometry) instead of averaging for aggregating hypotheses from parallel runs of a base learning algorithm. The authors claim that their approach achieves the same confidence/error guarantees as sequential execution but in polylogarithmic time, at the cost of using more data and quasi-polynomially many processing units. Theoretical analysis shows the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis decreases doubly exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree, and empirical results report speedups of 80-700x compared to sequential execution.

## Strengths
- The paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to parallel machine learning, with clear formalization of the Radon machine algorithm (Algorithm 1).
- The theoretical analysis is generally sound, with well-structured proofs for Proposition 2 and Theorem 3.
- The empirical evaluation demonstrates impressive speedups (80-700x) compared to sequential execution, and the Radon machine outperforms "averaging-at-the-end" in predictive performance.
- The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., applicability to low-dimensional problems, need for more data) and discusses potential extensions to high-dimensional and non-linear models.

## Weaknesses
### Theoretical Claims and Practicality
The paper's most significant weakness is the disconnect between theoretical claims and practical applicability. The paper claims to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic, but this requires quasi-polynomially many processing units (r^h, where r = d+2 is the Radon number for d-dimensional space). For high-dimensional problems (e.g., text, images), d can be large (thousands or more), making r enormous and rendering the "polylogarithmic time" claim practically meaningless. The paper acknowledges this limitation in Section 5 but does not provide concrete solutions for high-dimensional cases that are common in real-world applications.

Furthermore, the paper's theoretical claim of "achieving the same confidence and error bounds as sequential execution" is misleading. The Radon machine requires more data to achieve the same confidence (as shown in Equation 5), but the empirical evaluation claims "given the same amount of data as the underlying learning algorithm, the Radon machine achieves a substantial reduction of computation time." These are inconsistent statements: with the same data, the Radon machine achieves worse confidence (as shown in Equation 4), not the same confidence. The empirical results should clarify this trade-off.

### Empirical Validation
The empirical validation is insufficient for a NIPS paper. The comparison to Spark MLlib is vague—the paper mentions "various Spark machine learning algorithms" but does not specify which ones were used, their configurations, or why they were chosen. The paper claims the Radon machine is "one order of magnitude faster than Spark implementations" on some datasets, but without proper comparison to the most relevant Spark algorithms (e.g., distributed versions of the same base algorithms), these claims are difficult to verify.

The paper also lacks proper baselines. While they compare to "averaging-at-the-end," this is a weak baseline for parallel learning. More relevant comparisons would include state-of-the-art distributed methods like distributed SGD, mini-batch algorithms, or other aggregation strategies. The paper cites some related work but fails to thoroughly benchmark against the most competitive approaches.

### Overstated Claims
The paper makes several overreaching claims:
- "reduces the runtime of learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic" — this is technically true for the specific computational model described, but the requirement of quasi-polynomial processors makes this impractical for most real-world applications.
- "the Radon machine can be parallelised without further mathematical derivations and without writing dedicated code" — this is misleading; implementing Radon points requires specific knowledge of the hypothesis space's geometry, which varies by algorithm and problem.
- "the Radon machine achieves hypotheses of similar quality to Spark algorithms" — while the AUC is comparable, the paper does not demonstrate whether the Radon machine achieves the same or better confidence bounds with the same data, which is the theoretical claim of the paper.

## Overall Assessment
While the Radon machine presents an interesting theoretical approach to parallel learning, the paper's claims are overly ambitious without sufficient practical validation. The theoretical results are sound but rely on assumptions (fixed, low-dimensional hypothesis space) that are not realistic for many modern machine learning problems. The empirical evaluation is insufficiently rigorous and lacks proper comparison to state-of-the-art parallel methods. The paper would benefit from addressing the high-dimensional problem directly (e.g., by showing how low-rank approximations or random projections can be integrated into the scheme), providing more detailed empirical comparisons, and clarifying the data-confidence trade-off.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. The paper's core contribution is theoretically interesting but lacks the necessary practical validation and thorough comparison to establish its significance for the machine learning community. The claims about polylogarithmic runtime with quasi-polynomial processors are misleading for real-world applications, and the empirical results do not sufficiently support the theoretical claims. While the approach is novel, the paper does not provide enough evidence to convince a skeptical reviewer that this method represents a meaningful advance over existing parallel learning techniques. For acceptance, the authors would need to demonstrate practical applicability to high-dimensional problems, provide more rigorous empirical validation against relevant baselines, and clarify the trade-offs between data usage, confidence, and runtime.
