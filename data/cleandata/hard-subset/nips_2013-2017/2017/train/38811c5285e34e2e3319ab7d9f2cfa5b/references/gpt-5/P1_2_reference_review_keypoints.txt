- Proposes the Radon machine: partition data into r^h subsets, train base learner in parallel, and aggregate up a tree using Radon points.  
- Main theoretical claim: failure probability of aggregated model bounded by (rδ)^(2^h), yielding doubly-exponential confidence amplification across tree height h.  
- Under a stylized sample-complexity NL0(ε,δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k and TL(n)=O(n^κ), choosing h≈log M − log log M gives polylogarithmic runtime in M with quasi-polynomial processors.  
- Empirically achieves 80–700× speedups on Spark vs base learners and competitive AUC vs MLlib, with runtimes 15–85% of MLlib and better robustness than simple averaging.  
- Connects to NC/parallel complexity and the open question of polylog-time parallel learning; contrasts with averaging and multi-round distributed optimization (CoCoA, ADMM, distributed SGD).  
- Uses Radon points and centerpoint-depth ideas from computational geometry, distinguishing it from traditional averaging approaches.  
- Theory (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) is clear and technically sound; key insight: Radon point dominated by ≥2 points for any quasiconvex Q gives (rδ)^2 via union bound and composes across levels.  
- Runtime analysis separates base learning and aggregation costs; aggregation cost reported as O(h r^3).  
- Assumptions are explicit: finite-dimensional convex F with finite Radon number (r), base sample-complexity form, and δ ≤ 1/(2r).  
- Empirical strengths: large-wall-clock gains, competitive accuracy, code released; empirical weaknesses: no error bars, missing hyperparameter/system configuration details, and potential unfairness if base learners are multithreaded.  
- Clarity issues: communication message size stated as O(r) but should scale with parameter dimension d; NL0 assumption and VC/Rademacher derivations deferred; independence across levels should be explicitly stated.  
- Originality: novel use of iterated Radon aggregation for black-box parallel learning and distinctive double-exponential confidence amplification.  
- Significance: advances understanding of when polylog-time parallelization is feasible and offers a low-communication, robust black-box scheme, but limited by processor and dimensionality requirements.  
- Strength: clean, general black-box parallelization with simple proofs and explicit dependence on r and h; solid empirical speedups and released code.  
- Weakness: requires c=r^h processors which explodes with r=d+2, δ ≤ 1/(2r) may force large subsets, aggregation cost O(h r^3) may dominate for large d, and empirical comparisons lack stronger baselines and variability reporting.  
- Practicality concern: reliance on quasi-polynomial processors and small Radon number limits applicability in high-dimensional regimes; random-features ideas suggested but unanalyzed.  
- Questions for authors requested: sensitivity to violating δ ≤ 1/(2r) and empirical Δ vs h curves; largest d with acceptable aggregation overhead and experiments in d~10^3–10^4; possibility of replacing Radon aggregation with approximate centerpoint methods; and reporting confidence intervals/per-fold runtimes.  
- Suggestions: clarify message-size scaling with d and include end-to-end communication costs; add experiments with random features/Nyström and analyze embedding effects; include error bars and ablations on h; compare to stronger distributed ERM baselines like tuned CoCoA/ADMM.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept — novel and theoretically elegant with promising speedups, but needs expanded empirical evaluation and clearer system-level and high-dimensional applicability analysis.
