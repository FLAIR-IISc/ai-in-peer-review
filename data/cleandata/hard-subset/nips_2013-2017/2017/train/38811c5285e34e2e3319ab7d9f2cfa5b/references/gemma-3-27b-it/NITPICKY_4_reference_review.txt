This paper proposes a parallelization scheme, the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms, aiming to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic by increasing data size and processing units. The core idea leverages Radon points for aggregating hypotheses obtained from parallel execution on data subsets. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the consistency and efficiency of the parallelized algorithm and empirical validation demonstrating substantial speedups.

While the theoretical contributions—specifically the polylogarithmic runtime claim—are potentially significant, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and a concerning level of imprecision that ultimately undermine confidence in its claims. The writing style is often verbose and repetitive, hindering clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Radon points to parallelizing machine learning algorithms is a novel idea.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper attempts to provide formal guarantees on the parallelization’s performance, which is commendable.
*   **Empirical Results:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates promising speedups compared to baseline algorithms.
*   **Black-Box Nature:** The scheme’s claim of being applicable to a wide range of learning algorithms without modification is attractive.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Imprecise Notation and Definitions:** The use of “N0” as a function representing sample complexity is inconsistent with standard notation. Equation (5) introduces a new “NR0” without adequately explaining its relation to N0.  The statement “δ ≤ 1/2r” is arbitrary and lacks justification; why this particular threshold?
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim of reducing runtime from “polynomial to polylogarithmic” is overly strong and requires significant qualifications. Theorem 4 states this reduction *if* the Radon number is polylogarithmic in the sample complexity, a condition that may not hold for many practical scenarios. The paper does not sufficiently address this dependency.
*   **Lack of Rigor in Assumptions:** The assumption of “abundant data” and a fixed-dimensional Euclidean hypothesis space are limiting. The discussion of low-dimensional embeddings feels like an afterthought rather than a properly integrated component of the scheme.
*   **Ambiguous Experimental Setup:** The experimental section lacks detail.  The datasets used, particularly “SUSY” and “wikidata,” are mentioned but not adequately described.  The optimization process for hyperparameters is vaguely described (“optimized on an independent split”), and specifics regarding the WEKA and scikit-learn configurations are missing. What specific algorithms were used from WEKA and scikit-learn? The claim of 150 processors is somewhat misleading if this is simulated.
*   **Inconsistent Terminology:**  “Quasi-polynomial” is used without precise definition.
*   **Redundant Information:** The inclusion of proofs in the main body of the paper (Section 7) is unnecessary and disrupts the flow. These should be relegated to the appendix.
*   **Minor Errors & Typos:** Numerous minor errors and stylistic inconsistencies throughout. For example, “ldM” is used to denote the logarithm of M without prior definition. Equation (4) is labelled as equation (5) in the text.
*   **Insufficient Discussion of Communication Costs:** While the paper acknowledges communication complexity, it does not provide a detailed analysis or comparison with other parallel learning algorithms.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The discussion of related work is incomplete. The paper should discuss recent advances in distributed stochastic gradient descent and data-parallel algorithms in more detail.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** The idea of utilizing Radon points for parallelization is novel and could potentially lead to significant speedups in specific scenarios. The theoretical framework, although imperfect, attempts to provide formal guarantees.

**Con:** The numerous minor flaws, imprecise notation, overstated claims, and lack of experimental detail severely detract from the paper's quality. The assumptions and limitations are not adequately addressed, and the theoretical result’s practical applicability is questionable. The presentation lacks clarity and rigor.



**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. While the paper presents an interesting idea, the multitude of issues—ranging from minor stylistic errors to significant flaws in the technical presentation and justification—are too substantial to overlook. The paper requires significant revisions to address these concerns before it can be considered for acceptance at NIPS. Specifically, the authors need to clarify their notation, provide more rigorous proofs, conduct a more thorough experimental evaluation, and address the limitations of their approach. The claim of polylogarithmic runtime needs careful scrutiny and qualification. The current version does not meet the NIPS standards for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
