Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization with convex loss in finite-dimensional Euclidean parameter spaces. The method trains many “weak” models in parallel on random data subsets and aggregates them along a tree by repeatedly computing Radon points of r = d+2 hypotheses (d = dimension). Leveraging properties of Radon points and quasi-/convexity, the authors prove that if a base learner produces an ε-good hypothesis with probability 1−δ, then after h Radon aggregation levels the failure probability reduces doubly-exponentially: P(Q(rh) > ε) ≤ (rδ)2^h. They analyze sample and runtime complexity under a stylized form of base sample complexity NL0(ε,δ) and polynomial-time training TL(n) = O(n^κ), showing that with rh processors and an appropriate choice of h one can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample size required to achieve the same (ε,Δ), at the cost of (i) quasi-polynomially many processors and (ii) higher total sample usage. Empirically, on several datasets using WEKA/scikit-learn base learners and Spark clusters, the approach achieves large speedups over single-node base learners and competitive AUC and runtime compared to Spark MLlib baselines; it also outperforms simple averaging aggregation in AUC with similar runtime.

The work connects to several strands: PAC/NC-learnability and parallel learning (Vitter & Lin; Long & Servedio), averaging-based parallelization and distributed ERM/SGD (e.g., Zinkevich et al.; Shamir & Srebro; Rosenblatt & Nadler), aggregation via centerpoint/Tukey depth and iterative Radon points (Clarkson et al.). It is also related to robust aggregation methods such as geometric median or median-of-means in distributed learning (which are not discussed; see, e.g., Minsker 2015 and subsequent work), and to low-rank/random feature approximations that the authors suggest for high-dimensional or nonlinear models.

Quality
- Theory: The key bound (Proposition 2/Theorem 3) is simple and credible, relying on quasi-convexity and Radon’s theorem; the union bound could be tightened but is fine. The runtime argument is correct under the paper’s assumptions. However, the overall claim of “polylogarithmic runtime” hinges on strong conditions: (i) availability of rh processors; (ii) base sample complexity of the particular additive form; and especially (iii) Radon number r bounded polylogarithmically in the required sample size (Theorem 4). For linear models with VC dimension ~ d, r = d+2 is typically linear in sample complexity, so the condition is seldom met without dimensionality reduction. The paper suggests random projections/low-rank features, but this changes the learning problem and is not analyzed.
- Statistical assumptions: The analysis assumes iid hypotheses at each aggregation node; in the algorithm, hypotheses are trained on disjoint partitions of a single iid sample, which are not strictly iid draws from D. This distinction and how to ensure independence (e.g., subsampling with replacement) should be clarified.
- Practicalities: Computing Radon points is O(r^3) per node and could dominate when d is large; the paper acknowledges this but does not quantify the trade-off with d. The requirement δ ≤ 1/(2r) for the leaf models can be demanding for large d; whether n ≈ log M suffices to reach such δ is not addressed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear algorithm and proofs (Section 7). The role and implications of the Radon number, the independence assumption, and the exact conditions under which Theorem 4 gives NC-like efficiency could be emphasized more transparently. The sample complexity template NL0(ε,δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k is somewhat nonstandard; pointers from Appendix C.2 to the main text would help.

Originality
Using iterative Radon points to aggregate learned hypotheses and turn weak guarantees into high-confidence ones with doubly-exponential improvement appears novel in the ML parallelization literature. The connection to NC is interesting. While ensemble aggregation is well-studied (averaging, geometric median, MOM), Radon aggregation brings a different, theoretically-motivated guarantee.

Significance
Theoretical significance is tempered by the strength of the conditions for achieving polylogarithmic runtime and by the need for quasi-polynomial processors. Practically, the method seems promising for low- to moderate-dimensional convex models where r is small, offering a simple black-box parallelization. Its generality and robustness properties could make it useful in distributed or privacy-constrained settings. However, applicability to high-dimensional or nonlinear models is limited unless additional approximations are used, and the empirical comparisons omit several strong distributed baselines (e.g., modern SGD/coordinate-descent implementations, robust aggregators).

Pros
- Clear, general aggregation scheme with black-box applicability to convex, finite-dimensional models.
- Elegant theoretical guarantee: doubly-exponential reduction in failure probability with tree height.
- Thoughtful connection to NC and parallel complexity.
- Empirical speedups vs. single-node baselines, competitive performance vs. Spark MLlib; consistent advantage over averaging aggregation in AUC.

Cons
- Polylog-time claim requires r polylog in sample complexity and rh processors; often unrealistic when d is large.
- Independence assumptions and the requirement δ ≤ 1/(2r) are not fully justified in the presented setting.
- Limited empirical baselines (no comparison to robust aggregations like geometric median/MOM or strong distributed SGD/coordinate descent with tuned settings).
- Scalability with dimension not thoroughly evaluated; aggregation cost and memory/communication for large r are not quantified.
- Sample complexity model and choice of h vs. required δ could be clearer.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify how to obtain iid hypotheses (e.g., sampling with replacement) and analyze the impact of using disjoint partitions.
- Provide an explicit analysis of when δ ≤ 1/(2r) holds for leaf learners at the chosen n, and discuss the trade-off between h, n, and total data N.
- Quantify aggregation overhead and end-to-end runtime/memory as a function of d and h; include results in higher-dimensional regimes.
- Compare against robust aggregation baselines (geometric median, median-of-means) and strong distributed ERM/SGD methods with communication-efficient variants.
- Add ablations on h, number of processors c < r^h (the “deparallelized” regime), and sensitivity to dimensionality reductions (random projections/random features), including their effect on accuracy.
- Make Theorem 4’s assumptions explicit in the main text and discuss realistic regimes where they hold.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The idea is elegant and potentially useful, but the strongest theoretical claims rely on stringent assumptions that limit practical significance, and some statistical/practical details need tightening. With clarified assumptions, richer baselines, and deeper empirical analysis—especially in higher-dimensional settings—this work could be a strong contribution.
