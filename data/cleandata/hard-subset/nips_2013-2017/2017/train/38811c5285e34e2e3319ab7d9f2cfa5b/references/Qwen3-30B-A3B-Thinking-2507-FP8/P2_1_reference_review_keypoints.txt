- Introduces the "Radon Machine": parallelize regularized risk minimization by training on data subsets and iteratively aggregating hypotheses via Radon points, achieving sequential error/confidence in polylogarithmic time at the cost of quasi-polynomial processors and extra data.

- Theoretical result: aggregation yields doubly exponential reduction in the probability of an ε-bad hypothesis per aggregation level; formalized in Proposition 2, Theorems 3 and 4, with connections to Nick’s Class.

- Empirical result: comprehensive experiments (multiple datasets, WEKA/scikit-learn base learners) show 80–700× speedups versus base learners and Spark MLlib while maintaining comparable predictive performance (AUC, RMSE).

- Strength: novel application of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation, providing a black-box parallelization that needs no modifications to base learners.

- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis tied to computational complexity, clearly distinguishing this method from averaging-based parallelization.

- Strength: thorough and well-structured empirical evaluation with clear baselines and metrics.

- Weakness: relies on the hypothesis space having a finite Radon number (practical only for fixed finite-dimensional Euclidean spaces); suggested low-rank approximations need more discussion.

- Weakness: requires a quasi-polynomial number of processors, posing a significant practical constraint on scalability.

- Clarity: exceptionally well-written, clear algorithm presentation, adequate background on Radon points, and comprehensive appendices.

- Originality: fundamentally different from existing averaging/SGD approaches and provides the first provably effective parallelization scheme for a broad class of learners.

- Significance: important theoretical advance toward polylogarithmic-time parallel learning with strong empirical speedups, though practical impact is limited by processor/data requirements.

- Recommendation: strong paper; recommend acceptance.
