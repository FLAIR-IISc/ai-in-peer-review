### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel black-box parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to partition the data into a large number of subsets, train a base learning algorithm on each subset in parallel to obtain a set of initial hypotheses, and then hierarchically aggregate these hypotheses. The key innovation lies in the aggregation step, which uses the concept of a Radon point—a point in the convex hull intersection of a partition of a set of points.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this aggregation method reduces the probability of generating a poor hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree (Theorem 3). This strong theoretical guarantee allows the authors to show that their scheme can reduce the runtime of consistent and efficient learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic, albeit at the cost of using a quasi-polynomial number of processors and more data (Theorem 4). This result is framed as a step toward answering a fundamental open question in learning theory regarding the parallelizability of learning problems, connecting it to complexity theory concepts like Nick's Class (NC).

Empirically, the Radon Machine is evaluated against its sequential base learners, an "averaging-at-the-end" baseline, and state-of-the-art parallel algorithms from Spark's MLlib. The experiments demonstrate substantial speed-ups (80x to 700x) over sequential learners and show that the Radon Machine can be significantly faster than Spark implementations while achieving comparable or superior predictive performance. It also consistently outperforms the averaging baseline in terms of accuracy.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Theoretical Significance:** The application of Radon points to parallelize machine learning algorithms is a highly novel and elegant idea. The core theoretical result (Theorem 3), showing a doubly-exponential improvement in the confidence bound `(rδ)^2^h`, is powerful and provides a much stronger foundation for this type of "divide-and-conquer" strategy than previous work based on averaging, which typically offers weaker guarantees. The paper's connection of this result to the fundamental complexity of parallel learning (Nick's Class) is ambitious and positions the work within a significant, long-standing theoretical context.

2.  **Practicality and Broad Applicability:** The proposed Radon Machine is a "black-box" scheme. It treats the base learner as an oracle and does not require any modification to its internal implementation. This is a major practical advantage, as it allows the method to be immediately applied to a wide range of existing, well-tuned algorithms (e.g., from scikit-learn or WEKA), as demonstrated in the paper.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against the most relevant baselines: (a) the sequential base learner to quantify speed-up, (b) averaging-at-the-end to demonstrate the superiority of Radon point aggregation, and (c) industrial-strength parallel implementations (Spark MLlib) to show competitiveness with the state-of-the-art. The reported results are impressive, showing massive runtime reductions while maintaining or even improving predictive performance.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and structured. The motivation is clear, Algorithm 1 is easy to understand, and the theoretical argument is developed logically from the properties of Radon points (Proposition 2) to the final complexity result (Theorem 4). The discussion section thoughtfully addresses potential limitations and avenues for future work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Dimensionality:** The primary weakness of the proposed method lies in its dependence on the dimensionality `d` of the hypothesis space. The Radon number for R^d is `r = d+2`. The number of processors (`r^h`) and the total data required (`r^h` * `n_leaf`) both grow exponentially with `h` and polynomially (often of high degree) with `d`. For high-dimensional problems (e.g., `d > 100`), `r` becomes very large, making it impractical to partition the set of hypotheses into subsets of size `r` or to have `r^h` processing units. The paper acknowledges this in the discussion but perhaps understates how severely this limits the direct applicability of the method to common high-dimensional tasks in NLP or computer vision. The suggested remedies (e.g., random projections) are valid but are external pre-processing steps, not integral solutions.

2.  **Quasi-polynomial Processor Requirement:** The headline theoretical result of achieving polylogarithmic runtime relies on a *quasi-polynomial* number of processors. While this is a valid result in complexity theory, it is a significantly higher requirement than the *polynomial* number of processors that defines the standard NC class. This distinction should be made clearer, as it tempers the claim of making a direct advance on the original P-vs-NC-style question for learning.

3.  **Empirical Scope:** The experiments, while strong, are conducted on datasets with a relatively low number of features (e.g., SUSY has 18, HIGGS has 28). This is precisely the setting where the Radon Machine is expected to perform best, as the Radon number `r` is small. The evaluation would be more complete if it included an analysis of performance on a higher-dimensional dataset, even if it just served to illustrate the practical trade-offs and limitations imposed by `d`.

4.  **Assumptions on Base Learner:** The analysis requires the base learner to achieve a reasonably small failure probability `δ` (specifically, `δ ≤ 1/2r`). For large `d`, this means `δ` must be very small, which may be difficult to achieve for a base learner trained on a small subset of the data, as is done in the Radon Machine. This practical constraint could be discussed in more detail.

### Questions and Suggestions for Improvement

*   Could the authors provide a more explicit analysis of the trade-off between dimensionality `d`, the aggregation height `h`, the number of processors, and the total required sample size? A table or plot illustrating these scaling relationships would make the practical limitations clearer.
*   In the proof sketch of Proposition 2, the bound `P(Q(r) > ε) ≤ (rP(Q(F) > ε))^2` is given. Proposition 7 states that at least two points `s` in `S` must satisfy `Q(s) ≥ Q(r)`. If `Q(r) > ε`, then at least two points must be `ε`-bad. The probability of this is bounded by `(r choose 2) * P(Q(F) > ε)^2` (assuming independence). This seems slightly different from `(r * P(...))^2`. While the orders of magnitude are similar, a clarification on the derivation would be helpful.
*   The discussion on non-linear models via explicit feature embeddings is good. It would be interesting to comment on whether the Radon point concept could be applied more directly in the dual space for kernel methods, potentially bypassing the need for explicit, high-dimensional embeddings.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, theoretically principled, and practically effective method for parallelizing machine learning algorithms. The core idea of using Radon points for aggregation is creative and leads to powerful theoretical guarantees that surpass existing methods like averaging. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate significant real-world benefits. The primary limitation is the method's scalability with respect to the dimensionality of the hypothesis space, which restricts its applicability for certain problem domains. Despite this, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a new tool for parallel learning and by advancing the theoretical discourse on the fundamental limits of parallelization. The work is likely to inspire follow-up research on overcoming its current limitations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and elegant application of a concept from convex geometry (Radon points) to machine learning.
*   Strong, doubly-exponential theoretical guarantees, a significant improvement over prior work.
*   Demonstrates impressive practical performance (speed and accuracy) against strong baselines.
*   The black-box nature makes the method broadly applicable and easy to adopt.
*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem in machine learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The method's performance and feasibility are critically dependent on the hypothesis space dimension `d`, limiting its use in high-dimensional settings.
*   The claim of polylogarithmic runtime requires a quasi-polynomial number of processors, a very strong assumption.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper's novelty, theoretical depth, and strong empirical results outweigh its practical limitations, which are openly discussed as avenues for future work. It is a high-quality contribution that will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
