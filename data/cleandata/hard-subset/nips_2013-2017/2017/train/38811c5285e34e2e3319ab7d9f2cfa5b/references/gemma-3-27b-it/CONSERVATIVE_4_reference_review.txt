This paper proposes a parallelization scheme, dubbed the Radon machine, for regularized risk minimization algorithms, aiming to achieve polylogarithmic runtime with a quasipolynomial increase in data and processing units. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm to random data subsets and iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, which offer faster confidence gains compared to averaging. The authors provide theoretical guarantees regarding runtime reduction and empirical validation across several datasets.

While the theoretical results are interesting, and the potential for speedup is appealing, I find the presentation lacking in concrete justification and the empirical evaluation insufficient to support the strong claims made. The central promise of reducing runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic is a significant one, and requires a much more rigorous and detailed analysis than provided. 

My primary concern revolves around the practicality of the quasipolynomial overhead in both data and processing units. The authors acknowledge this cost but downplay its significance. A polylogarithmic runtime is useless if the preprocessing step of generating the necessary datasets and the hardware requirements for a quasipolynomial number of processors are prohibitive. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of this trade-off, and doesn’t present data on the actual scaling of these costs with problem size and dimensionality. Specifically, the assumption of abundant data is critical, and its limitations need to be clearly stated.  Furthermore, the theoretical analysis relies on the Radon number 'r' of the hypothesis space, which is stated as d+2 for Euclidean spaces. While true, the paper doesn’t sufficiently address scenarios where the data requires preprocessing to a suitable Euclidean space (as mentioned), and how that preprocessing impacts the overall runtime and the Radon number. 

The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating speedups, is conducted on a relatively small-scale Spark cluster. The statement that the cluster consists of 5 worker nodes, 25 processors per node is not useful if the overall number of processors that can be practically utilized on those nodes is smaller.  The comparison to Spark’s MLlib is useful but incomplete.  The claim that the Radon machine achieves similar quality to Spark algorithms with reduced runtime needs more nuanced discussion. What specific algorithms were compared? Were all parameters optimized fairly? The paper doesn’t provide sufficient details to assess the validity of these comparisons. The discussion of runtime dependence on dataset size (Figure 3) is interesting, but the exponents of 1.57 and 1.17, while different, are not drastically so. A more thorough statistical analysis is warranted.

The paper also suffers from a lack of clarity in several areas. The connection to Nick’s Class, while mentioned, isn’t fully elucidated. The discussion of communication complexity is brief and lacks quantitative analysis.  The proposed extensions to streaming data and non-linear models are just sketched out, without any concrete details. 

**Strengths:**

*   The theoretical framework provides a formal analysis of the parallelization scheme.
*   The use of Radon points for aggregation is a novel idea with potential benefits.
*   The empirical results suggest that the scheme can achieve significant speedups in certain scenarios.
*   The black-box nature of the approach allows it to be applied to a wide range of learning algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The quasipolynomial overhead in data and processing units is not adequately addressed.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and lacks detailed comparisons.
*   The connection to Nick’s Class is not fully explained.
*   The paper lacks a thorough discussion of practical limitations and potential extensions.
*   The clarity of the presentation could be improved.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms.
*   The empirical results, while limited, provide evidence of the scheme's potential.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical limitations of the scheme, particularly the quasipolynomial overhead, are not adequately addressed.
*   The empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the strong claims made.
*   The paper lacks the level of rigor and detail expected of a NIPS publication.
*   The theoretical guarantees are less impactful if the constants hidden in the quasipolynomial terms are large.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is currently not suitable for acceptance to NIPS. While the ideas are interesting, the paper lacks the necessary rigor, detail, and practical justification to convince me that the proposed scheme represents a significant advance in the field. The authors need to address the concerns outlined above, particularly the practical limitations of the quasipolynomial overhead and the need for a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, before this work can be considered for publication in a top-tier conference like NIPS. A major revision addressing these points is required. I currently recommend rejection.
