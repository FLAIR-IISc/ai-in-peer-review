Summary
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization that aggregates independently trained “weak” models via iterated Radon points instead of averaging. The key theoretical insight is that, for hypothesis spaces with finite Radon number (e.g., Rd with r = d+2), the probability that a Radon-aggregated hypothesis is ε-bad contracts doubly-exponentially with the height h of the aggregation tree: P(Q(rh) > ε) ≤ (r·δ)^(2^h), where δ is the base learner’s ε-bad probability. This yields an end-to-end argument that, to match a given (ε, Δ) guarantee, the Radon machine can reduce runtime from polynomial in sample size to polylogarithmic, at the cost of (i) increased total data usage and (ii) quasi-polynomially many processing units. Empirically, the authors report substantial wall-clock speedups (up to ~700× on 150 cores) versus single-node baselines and favorable runtime/quality tradeoffs compared to Spark MLlib, with similar AUC and typically lower runtime. The method treats the base learner as a black box and applies broadly to convex ERM in finite-dimensional Euclidean parameterizations.

Novelty and relation to prior work
- The use of Radon points to aggregate models is unusual in ML parallelization and connects geometric depth notions (Radon/Tukey) with statistical confidence amplification. This is a fresh angle beyond averaging, geometric median, or majority vote schemes commonly seen in distributed learning.
- The paper frames parallel ERM through the lens of NC and polylog time, offering a crisp complexity-theoretic target, and shows conditions under which such behavior (polylog in M with many processors) is achievable.
- Prior distributed ERM/SGD works (e.g., parameter averaging, distributed minibatch, ADMM, communication-efficient ERM) do not deliver polylog-time guarantees in the same sense; the authors cite several. The double-exponential confidence contraction via iterated Radon aggregation appears new.

Strengths
- Bold conceptual contribution: a general, black-box scheme with a clean, compositional analysis (Prop. 2, Thm. 3) linking geometry to statistical confidence.
- Clear articulation of sample/runtime tradeoffs and the role of Radon number; explicit conditions for when polylog runtime is possible.
- Practical implementation and nontrivial empirical speedups on a real cluster; competitive accuracy versus MLlib while being faster.
- The scheme is simple to state and compatible with a wide range of convex ERM learners.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Processor/data budget: Achieving the strongest theoretical gains requires rh processors and rh data partitions, with r = d+2. For high-dimensional models d can be large; even with h modest, rh can be prohibitive. The paper acknowledges this and suggests projections/low-rank embeddings, but the statistical impact of such preprocessing in this framework is not analyzed.
- Assumption δ ≤ 1/2r: The base learner must already be reasonably strong for the doubly-exponential contraction to kick in. It would help to quantify how many samples are typically needed to reach this δ in practice across tasks, and how sensitive performance is when δ is looser.
- Practicality of “polylog-time”: The polylog result hinges on d (hence r) being polylogarithmic in the sample complexity and on access to quasi-polynomially many processors. This is an interesting theoretical waypoint toward NC-style parallel learning, but the practical regime in which these conditions hold is narrow; the empirical section works with relatively small d, which is consistent but limits generality.
- Comparisons: Averaging is a natural baseline, included. However, other robust model aggregation schemes (e.g., geometric median, coordinate-wise median, median-of-means style aggregation) are not considered. Given the robustness claims (e.g., one bad child can spoil averaging but not Radon), including such baselines would strengthen the case.
- Optimization error and constraints: The theory treats the base learner as producing a random hypothesis with certain regret probability. In practice learners are approximate and may solve constrained problems (e.g., L1). While convexity preserves feasibility under convex combinations, some discussion on how optimization error propagates through Radon aggregation would improve the practical robustness story.
- Clarity: The complexity-theoretic framing is engaging but sometimes overshadows the practical parameter regimes; a more explicit map from (ε, Δ, d, h, δ) to rh and total data costs on the evaluated datasets would aid reproducibility and interpretation.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide empirical curves of error vs. h (and δ of base learners) to illustrate the predicted doubly-exponential contraction and the practical h at which diminishing returns appear?
- How does Radon aggregation compare to geometric median or coordinate-wise median aggregation on your benchmarks (both accuracy and runtime)?
- For high-dimensional settings, can you quantify the trade-off when using random projections: how does reducing d (to make r manageable) affect AUC/error? Any theory that couples projection error with the Radon guarantees?
- How sensitive is the scheme to non-iid partitioning of data (heterogeneous shards), which is common in federated or sharded environments?
- Do you have results where c < rh processors (the “deparallelized” setting), to validate the stated slowdown factor and still-observed gains?

Minor comments
- Please move the Radon point linear system and any numerical stability considerations into the main text or a more prominent appendix summary; it’s central to implementation.
- Clarify whether runtime comparisons include I/O and orchestration overheads in Spark.
- A short discussion of communication vs. computation tradeoffs relative to MLlib would be helpful.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a novel, principled aggregation and a compelling direction for parallel learning with strong statistical guarantees.
- Bridges geometric depth, statistical confidence, and parallel complexity in a way that could inspire follow-up work.
- Demonstrates large empirical speedups with competitive accuracy and a black-box API.

Cons against acceptance
- Strong assumptions on processor/data availability and small effective dimension.
- Missing comparisons to other robust aggregators; limited analysis of optimization error and non-iid data.
- Practicality of the NC-style regime is not fully borne out beyond small-to-moderate d.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
This is a bold, original idea with clean theory and promising practice. While some assumptions are strong and comparisons could be broadened, the conceptual novelty and potential to spur a new line of parallel learning research outweigh the limitations.
