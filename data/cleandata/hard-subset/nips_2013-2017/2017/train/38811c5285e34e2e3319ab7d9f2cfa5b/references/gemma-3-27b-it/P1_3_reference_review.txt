## NIPS 2017 Review: Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, termed the "Radon Machine," for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea leverages Radon points for iteratively aggregating hypotheses computed on random subsets of the data. The authors claim that this scheme achieves the same confidence and error bounds as sequential execution but with a runtime reduced from polynomial to polylogarithmic – at the cost of increased data usage and processing units (quasi-polynomial in 1/ε and log(1/∆)). The paper provides theoretical guarantees for this speedup and validates it empirically, demonstrating significant runtime reductions compared to sequential execution and existing parallel implementations (Spark MLlib).

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on existing work in parallel machine learning, particularly averaging-based approaches [32, 45, 46]. However, it distinguishes itself by utilizing Radon points instead of simple averaging, offering a doubly-exponential increase in confidence per aggregation layer. The work also connects to the theoretical limitations of parallel computation (Nick’s Class [9]) and positions itself as contributing to the understanding of efficiently parallelizing polynomial-time learning algorithms. The authors appropriately cite relevant literature in parallel computing, convex optimization, and machine learning. The connection to iterative Radon point algorithms [8, 30] is also well established.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating the runtime reduction and bounding the cost in terms of data and processing units. The proofs, while potentially dense, appear sound and are referenced for detailed examination.
* **Novelty:** The application of Radon points to parallelizing learning algorithms is a genuinely novel idea, offering a different approach compared to traditional averaging techniques.
* **Black-Box Applicability:** A significant strength is the scheme's black-box nature. It can be applied to a wide range of consistent and efficient learning algorithms without requiring algorithm-specific modifications.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating substantial speedups (80-700x) compared to sequential execution and competitive performance against Spark MLlib. The comparison to averaging-at-the-end highlights the advantage of Radon point aggregation.
* **Clear Problem Statement and Contribution:** The paper clearly defines the problem of efficient parallelization of learning algorithms and articulates its contribution as a provably effective solution.
* **Discussion of Limitations and Future Work:** The authors acknowledge limitations (high dimensionality, non-linear models) and suggest potential extensions, demonstrating a thoughtful understanding of the work’s scope.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality of Quasi-Polynomial Processing Units:** While theoretically sound, the requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units might be a significant practical limitation, especially for very large-scale problems. The discussion around simulating this on fewer processors is helpful, but the overhead needs to be carefully considered.
* **Complexity of Radon Point Calculation:** The computational cost of calculating Radon points (O(r^3)) is noted, and while theoretically bounded, its impact on overall performance with larger dimensionality needs further investigation.
* **Limited Dataset Diversity in Empirical Evaluation:** While the results are positive, the datasets used in the empirical evaluation are relatively standard. Evaluating the scheme on a wider range of datasets, particularly those with differing characteristics (e.g., high dimensionality, imbalanced classes), would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Communication Cost:** The paper briefly mentions communication cost, but a more detailed analysis of communication overhead in a distributed setting would be valuable. How does communication scale with the number of processors and the dataset size?
* **Potential for Numerical Instability:** The calculation of Radon points involves solving linear equations.  The potential for numerical instability, particularly in higher dimensions or with ill-conditioned data, is not discussed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant runtime reductions and competitive performance.
* The black-box nature of the scheme makes it broadly applicable.
* The authors clearly articulate the contribution, limitations, and potential future directions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The quasi-polynomial requirement for processing units may limit practical applicability.
* The empirical evaluation could be broadened to include a more diverse set of datasets.
* A more detailed analysis of communication cost and potential numerical instability would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The theoretical foundations are solid, the empirical results are compelling, and the black-box nature of the approach makes it potentially valuable for a wide range of applications.  While the practical limitations of the processing unit requirement and the need for further investigation into communication costs exist, the potential benefits are substantial.  

**Rating:** **Strong Accept (8/10)**.  I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to NIPS and warrants acceptance. I recommend the authors address the weaknesses outlined above in a revision if possible, but I do not believe they are fatal flaws.
