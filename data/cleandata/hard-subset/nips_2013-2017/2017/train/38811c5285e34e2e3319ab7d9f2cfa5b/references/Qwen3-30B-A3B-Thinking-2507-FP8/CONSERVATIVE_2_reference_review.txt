

# Review: "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper proposes a "Radon Machine" for parallelizing regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to divide data into subsets, train weak models in parallel, and then iteratively aggregate these models using Radon points (a concept from convex geometry) to achieve a strong final model. The authors claim the approach reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic while maintaining the same error bounds, with error probability decreasing doubly-exponentially with aggregation depth.

## Strengths
The paper is mathematically rigorous, with clear theoretical results (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3) that demonstrate the error reduction properties of the aggregation scheme. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against Spark MLlib implementations and averaging baselines across multiple datasets and learning algorithms. The implementation details and code availability are commendable.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical claims lack practical grounding**: The paper's most significant claim—that runtime can be reduced from polynomial to polylogarithmic—is heavily qualified by requiring "quasi-polynomial" resources (data size and processing units). This is unrealistic for most applications, as quasi-polynomial resources grow too quickly to be practical. The authors acknowledge this limitation but fail to adequately discuss its implications for real-world use.

**Overstated significance**: The paper positions itself as addressing a "fundamental open problem" about parallel machine learning. However, the theoretical results only apply to a narrow class of convex, regularized risk minimization algorithms with finite Radon numbers. The authors cite Long and Servedio's work on half-spaces as a related open problem, but their approach does not actually solve it—only provides a partial solution under very specific conditions. The significance is greatly overstated.

**Inadequate comparison to established methods**: The paper fails to properly compare its approach to the state of the art in distributed ML. It dismisses distributed SGD approaches as not achieving "polylogarithmic time" but doesn't address how its own approach compares to the best existing distributed methods (e.g., the distributed mini-batch algorithm [10] or recent communication-efficient methods [34]). The empirical results don't show how the Radon Machine compares to these established approaches on larger datasets.

**Misleading "black-box" claim**: The paper claims the approach is a "black-box parallelization scheme" that "does not depend on the implementation" of algorithms. However, the method requires specific properties (finite Radon number, convexity) that most modern ML algorithms (deep learning, gradient boosting) do not satisfy. The empirical results only cover linear models, which represent a small fraction of practical ML applications.

**Theoretical claims appear too good to be true**: The doubly-exponential error reduction (Theorem 3) is a strong claim that requires careful validation. The proof relies on Proposition 2, which states that "if only one of r points is ε-bad, the Radon point is still ε-good." This seems counterintuitive for convex functions, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient intuition or examples to validate this claim. The empirical results don't demonstrate this doubly-exponential error reduction but instead show modest improvements in AUC.

**Practical limitations underplayed**: The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., dataset size must be a multiple of the Radon number), but doesn't discuss how common these limitations are in practice. For high-dimensional data (e.g., text), the Radon number (d+2) would be prohibitively large, requiring techniques like low-rank approximations. The paper mentions these techniques but doesn't show how they affect the theoretical guarantees.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical approach to parallelization, its claims are overly ambitious given the practical constraints. The theoretical results are limited to a narrow class of algorithms under highly specific conditions, and the empirical validation doesn't sufficiently address real-world scalability challenges. The paper doesn't provide a convincing argument for why this approach would be preferred over existing distributed ML methods, which have been thoroughly validated in practice.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More realistic resource constraints (not just quasi-polynomial)
2. Comprehensive comparison to established distributed ML methods
3. Discussion of limitations for non-convex, high-dimensional, or non-linear models
4. More detailed analysis of communication costs

Given these significant limitations, I recommend rejection. While the theoretical framework is sound, the practical relevance and significance of the contribution are not sufficiently demonstrated to meet NIPS standards. The paper presents an interesting mathematical idea but fails to establish it as a viable alternative to established parallelization approaches.
