- Introduces the Radon Machine: a parallelisation scheme that aggregates hypotheses via Radon points from random data subsets.  
- Proves doubly exponential error reduction with h aggregation levels, enabling runtime reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic in sample complexity.  
- Treats the base learning algorithm as a black box, requiring no modifications.  
- Empirical results: 80–700× speedup on 150 processors while maintaining accuracy comparable to Spark MLlib.  
- Theoretical contributions are technically sound with rigorous proofs for Proposition 2 and Theorem 3.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against averaging baseline and state-of-the-art Spark implementations across multiple datasets.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and discuss extensions to high-dimensional and non-linear models.  
- Practical concern: quasi-polynomial processor requirement may limit real-world applicability and is not fully addressed for cases where r^h exceeds available processors.  
- Dependence on the Radon number (r = d+2) is problematic in high dimensions due to exponential processor growth.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; Algorithm 1 and theoretical connections are well-presented.  
- Minor clarity issue: Theorem 4 should more explicitly relate the Radon number to sample complexity.  
- Originality: novel use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation and stronger error bounds than averaging-based approaches.  
- Significance: substantial theoretical and practical impact, addressing open questions about polylogarithmic-time parallel learning.  
- Strengths summarized: novel doubly exponential error reduction, strong empirical speedups, black-box applicability, comprehensive evaluation, clear TCS linkage.  
- Weaknesses summarized: quasi-polynomial processor requirement, limited non-linear model exploration, problematic dependence on Radon number, insufficient handling of non-multiple dataset sizes.  
- Recommendation: accept — high-quality paper with significant contributions despite the main practical limitation.  
- Specific suggestions: add discussion for dataset sizes not multiple of r^h, clarify relation between r and dimensionality for real datasets, and detail practical trade-offs of the quasi-polynomial processor requirement.
