Summary
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization that aggregates hypotheses learned on IID data shards via iterated Radon points instead of averaging. The key theoretical result is a doubly-exponential reduction in the failure probability with the height h of an aggregation tree: if a base learner returns an ε-good hypothesis with probability ≥1−δ, the Radon aggregation over r=d+2 hypotheses reduces the bad-event probability to (rδ)^(2^h). Leveraging this, the authors argue that, to achieve the same (ε,Δ)-guarantee as a sequential baseline, the Radon machine can run in time polylogarithmic in the required sample size, provided one uses quasi-polynomially many processors and more total data. They provide supporting proofs (Proposition 2, Theorem 3, Theorem 4), discuss the role of Radon number, and present experiments on classification, regression, and multi-class tasks, showing large wall-clock speedups over single-node base learners and favorable runtime–accuracy tradeoffs relative to Spark MLlib, while being competitive or better than averaging-at-the-end.

Relation to prior work
The approach is distinct from averaging-based distributed ERM/SGD (e.g., Zinkevich et al., Shamir & Srebro; Rosenblatt & Nadler on averaging optimality) and from distributed optimization (ADMM, approximate Newton, etc.) which typically require multiple communication rounds and cannot achieve polylogarithmic-time guarantees. The use of Radon points—classically from convex geometry and center-point approximations (Tukey depth, iterated Radon points)—as a model aggregator is novel in ML, contrasting with common aggregators such as averaging, coordinate-wise median, geometric median, or median-of-means used for robustness and heavy-tailed settings. The paper positions itself with PAC/NC-learnability literature (Vitter & Lin; Long & Servedio), providing a new angle toward polylog-time parallel learning for convex ERM.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation to amplify confidence doubly exponentially is a bold and elegant idea that goes beyond incremental tweaks to averaging.
- Theoretical clarity: Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are simple yet powerful; the doubly-exponential decay in δ via a tree of Radon aggregations is compelling and easy to reason about.
- Black-box parallelization: The scheme applies to a broad class of convex ERM learners without modifying solvers.
- Empirical evidence: On multiple datasets, the Radon machine achieves large speedups (80–700× with ~150 processors) while maintaining accuracy comparable to or better than baselines, and outperforming averaging-at-the-end in AUC.
- Discussion is thoughtful: The paper recognizes dimensionality/processor constraints and suggests practical mitigations (random projections, block coordinate strategies), and addresses communication costs.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Strong resource assumptions: The polylog-time result requires quasi-polynomially many processors and quasi-polynomial extra data (N grows as r^h with r=d+2). This falls short of NC (polynomial processors) and may be impractical when data is not “abundant.”
- Dimensionality dependence: Aggregation cost is O(r^3) with r=d+2. Theorem 4 effectively requires the Radon number to be polylog in the sample complexity, implying very low-dimensional hypothesis spaces. The practical story for high-dimensional models hinges on dimension reduction, but the effect on regret/generalization isn’t analyzed formally here.
- Base-learner confidence requirement: Each shard learner must achieve δ ≤ 1/(2r). For large d (large r), this can force relatively large shard sizes, potentially eroding the parallel gains or violating the “weak learner quickly” intuition. The paper empirically uses h=1 or small h with minimum shard size 100, which is far from the regime implied by the theory for very small Δ.
- Limited comparisons to robust aggregators: The paper contrasts primarily with averaging; comparisons to geometric median/coordinate-wise median/median-of-means (which also amplify robustness/confidence) would strengthen the empirical and conceptual positioning.
- Clarity gaps and minor issues: Some parts rely on appendices for crucial details (e.g., constructing Radon points). The independence assumptions across levels and grouping strategy could be stated more explicitly to align with the distributional setup in Theorem 3. Practical overheads for large r in the linear system solves could be quantified.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the core probabilistic bound is clean and the aggregation analysis is convincing. The runtime argument captures the asymptotics under its assumptions, albeit with caveats about processor/data scaling. Experiments are fairly comprehensive for linear models and provide credible speedups. A few derivations (e.g., mapping Δ to M and choosing h) are sketched; a tighter accounting of constants/log factors and the exact shard sizes needed to meet δ would help.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized, with a clear algorithm box and statements of results. Some notational overload and reliance on appendices make it heavier to parse than necessary. A small pass to polish typos and to restate the Radon-point linear system in the main text would improve readability.

Originality
High. The Radon-point aggregator for distributed ERM and the associated confidence amplification are novel and, to my knowledge, not previously explored in mainstream ML literature.

Significance
Medium-High. As a direction, this is exciting: it opens a new line on structure-aware aggregation in parallel learning that trades data and processors for time with provable guarantees. While the path to NC (polynomial processors) remains open, the conceptual contribution is substantial and likely to inspire follow-ups (e.g., variants for high-d, nonconvex settings, robust/federated scenarios).

Questions for the authors
1) Can you compare against geometric median or median-of-means aggregation, both theoretically (confidence amplification) and empirically?
2) Can you quantify shard sizes needed to ensure δ ≤ 1/(2r) for typical VC/Rademacher settings, and how this interacts with d and h in practice?
3) For random projections: can you provide a formal result showing that projecting to m=O(polylog N) preserves regret sufficiently to keep Theorem 4 meaningful?
4) How sensitive is aggregation to numerical instability when solving r×r systems for large r? Any regularization tricks?
5) Could Radon aggregation be combined with iterative optimization (e.g., local solvers with few passes) to reduce per-shard runtime further?

Pros
- Novel, elegant idea with clear theoretical justification.
- Black-box applicability; simple to implement.
- Strong empirical speedups; beats averaging in accuracy.

Cons
- Requires quasi-polynomial processors/data; not NC.
- High dependence on dimension; theory is weakest in high-d regimes.
- Missing comparisons to other robust aggregators.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). Despite limitations, the paper introduces a genuinely new concept with solid theory and promising practice, well aligned with NeurIPS’s interest in bold ideas. I would favor inclusion to stimulate further work on principled aggregation for parallel learning.
