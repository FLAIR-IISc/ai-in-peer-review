Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Radon machines, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization over convex, finite-dimensional Euclidean hypothesis spaces. The key idea is to train base learners independently on rh iid data shards and to aggregate models up a tree by computing Radon points (rather than averages) at each internal node. Using properties of Radon points and quasi-/convex functions, the authors prove that if the base learner produces an ε-bad hypothesis with probability δ, then after h levels of Radon aggregation the failure probability drops doubly-exponentially: Δ ≤ (rδ)^(2^h), where r is the Radon number (d+2 in Rd). Under standard sample complexity assumptions for the base learner and assuming enough processors (≈ rh), they show that one can match the (ε,Δ) guarantee of the sequential learner in polylogarithmic time in the required sample size, at the expense of quasi-polynomially more processors and total data. Empirically, on a Spark cluster, the method achieves substantial wall-clock speedups over single-node base learners and competitive performance against Spark MLlib baselines; it also outperforms simple averaging-at-the-end in AUC while being similar in runtime.

This connects to prior NIPS and related work on parallel learning in the PAC/NC framework (e.g., Vitter & Lin; Long & Servedio), distributed stochastic optimization (e.g., Zinkevich et al.; Dekel et al.; Shamir & Srebro; CoCoA/DANE-style methods), and averaging-based aggregation of local models. The novelty lies in using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation to obtain sharper (doubly-exponential) confidence amplification than averaging, and in analyzing the resulting parallel time/sample tradeoffs.

Quality
- Theory: The central probabilistic amplification result (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3) is simple and elegant, relying on properties of Radon points for quasi-/convex functions. The high-level runtime comparison to the base learner is sensible, and the conditions under which polylog runtime is achieved are stated. However:
  - The bound P(Q(r) > ε) ≤ (r P(Q(F)>ε))^2 appears to be a loosened union bound; a more natural derivation gives ≤ C(r,2) δ^2. The constant does not change asymptotics but should be clarified.
  - The analysis assumes δ ≤ 1/2r for the base models trained on small shards; the paper could more explicitly quantify how large shards must be (as a function of ε, r, and model class complexity) to attain such δ, and hence the overall data blow-up factor.
  - The main polylog-time statement (Theorem 4) critically requires that the Radon number be polylog in the sample complexity, i.e., essentially low-dimensional models. This is a strong restriction that limits scope; while the paper discusses random projections/low-rank approximations as a workaround, the theory does not cover the approximation error these introduce.
  - Communication and memory costs are only briefly discussed; the analysis abstracts away orchestration overheads and potential numerical stability issues in solving r×r systems when r=d+2 is large.

- Empirics: The implementation and comparisons are valuable and indicate large speedups and competitive accuracy. That said:
  - The baselines focus on MLlib implementations; comparisons to stronger modern distributed solvers for ERM (e.g., CoCoA/COCOA+, DANE, parameter-server SGD with tuned minibatching) would strengthen the case.
  - The reported 10% AUC gains over averaging-at-the-end are surprisingly large; further diagnostics would help (e.g., whether averaging was done in parameter vs. probability space, calibration effects, sensitivity to shard count).
  - Experiments mainly use settings where d is modest; an ablation with random projections to illustrate the high-dim workaround would be informative.
  - The breakdown between learning and aggregation time and scaling with h, r, and c processors could be reported more systematically.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful algorithm pseudocode and a logical flow from theory to practice. Minor issues:
- Define “ld” explicitly (log base 2?) and clean up the exponents/notation in the sample complexity assumptions.
- Proposition 2 states the bound for “expected regret Q” without stating convexity; later results require (quasi-)convexity. Make assumptions uniform.
- Be explicit about independence/identical distribution across levels in Theorem 3 and its relation to the actual partitioning scheme.
- Summarize the constants behind “quasi-polynomial” processors/data as a function of ε, Δ, d.

Originality
Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation with rigorous confidence amplification appears novel relative to averaging-based schemes. The theoretical framing in terms of NC-type polylog time is an interesting perspective connecting learning and parallel complexity. The empirical “black-box” instantiation is also useful.

Significance
The work advances our understanding of when and how black-box parallelization can reduce wall time while preserving statistical guarantees. However, the practical impact is tempered by the strong assumptions (low d, many processors, abundant data). The empirical speedups are promising, but broader baselines and high-dimensional scenarios would increase significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple theoretical mechanism yielding doubly-exponential confidence amplification via Radon aggregation.
- Bridges learning theory with NC-style parallel efficiency; contributes to ongoing discussion sparked by Long & Servedio.
- Black-box, implementation-agnostic scheme; strong speedups demonstrated on realistic datasets.
- Outperforms averaging-at-the-end in accuracy while retaining similar runtime.

Cons for acceptance
- Polylog-time guarantee requires radon number polylog in sample complexity (essentially low-dimensional); high-dimensional regimes not covered theoretically.
- Processor and data requirements are quasi-polynomial in accuracy/confidence; practicality at scale is uncertain.
- Baselines omit state-of-the-art distributed ERM/SGD solvers; averaging baseline details may affect fairness.
- Some theoretical details/notation need tightening; communication/computation overheads of aggregation underexplored.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Proposition 2 constants and assumptions; unify convexity assumptions throughout. Define “ld.”
- Quantify shard size needed to reach δ ≤ 1/2r and derive an explicit end-to-end sample/processor budget for given (ε, Δ, d).
- Provide an ablation on h and r, with a breakdown of learning vs. aggregation time and communication cost.
- Add comparisons to CoCoA/COCOA+, DANE/DiSCO, and tuned parameter-server SGD; report iterations/communication rounds.
- Demonstrate the high-dimensional workaround (random projections or Nyström/random features) with analysis of induced approximation error.
- Analyze and report why averaging-at-the-end underperforms (parameter vs. probability averaging, calibration, shard heterogeneity).

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper is thoughtful, well-written, and contains a neat idea with both theoretical and empirical support. However, the main theoretical guarantee applies under restrictive conditions (low Radon number) and with substantial processor/data budgets, and the empirical section lacks some important baselines and diagnostics. With clarifications and stronger experiments (especially in higher dimensions and against stronger distributed ERM/SGD baselines), I would be inclined to support acceptance.
