

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes a parallelization scheme called the Radon Machine for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The scheme divides the dataset into random subsets, applies the base learning algorithm in parallel to each subset, and then aggregates the resulting hypotheses using Radon points instead of averaging. The authors claim this leads to a doubly-exponential reduction in error probability with the height of the aggregation tree, enabling runtime reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample size. The paper provides theoretical guarantees and empirical results showing significant speedups over base learning algorithms and Spark MLlib implementations.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel approach to parallel machine learning with theoretical guarantees that are non-trivial. The Radon point aggregation method does offer a different approach than standard averaging, and the theoretical analysis is generally sound. The empirical results on moderate-sized datasets showing 80-700x speedups are impressive, and the comparison to Spark MLlib is relevant for practical applications.

## Weaknesses

### Major Technical Errors
The paper contains a critical typesetting error in Equation (4) where the bound is written as "∆ = (rδ) 2h" instead of "∆ = (rδ)^(2^h)". This is a fundamental error in expressing the doubly-exponential reduction claimed in the paper. The same error appears in Equation (6), where "N L 0 ( ε, (rδ)2 h )" should be "N_L^0(ε, (rδ)^(2^h))". This is not a minor formatting issue but a critical mathematical error that undermines the theoretical claims.

### Theoretical Inconsistency
In the proof of Theorem 3, the paper claims "the probability of Q(r) > ε is less than or equal to the probability of the pair a, b having Q(a), Q(b) > ε." This is incorrect. The correct reasoning would be that if Q(r) > ε, then at least two points must have Q(s) > ε (by Proposition 7), so P(Q(r) > ε) ≤ P(at least two points have Q(s) > ε) ≤ C(r,2)P(Q(F) > ε)^2. The paper claims the bound is (rP(Q(F) > ε))^2, which is a looser bound but not derived correctly. The authors should provide the correct derivation or justify why their bound is valid.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
The paper contains multiple formatting errors that undermine its credibility:
- "for some1 αε, βε ∈ R" (missing space)
- "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" (should be "The Conference")
- "5 worker nodes, 25 processors per node" but claims to use 150 processors (5*25=125, not 150)
- Inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions throughout

### Empirical Evaluation Weaknesses
The paper claims "the Radon machine is one order of magnitude faster" than Spark implementations but doesn't provide exact speedup numbers. The statement "using 150 processors, the Radon machine is between 80 and around 700-times faster" is vague without specifying the exact datasets, base algorithms, or problem sizes. The comparison to Spark MLlib lacks details about Spark configurations, making the results difficult to reproduce.

### Overstated Claims
The paper claims the Radon Machine "treats the underlying learning algorithm as a black-box" but the Radon point calculation requires solving a system of linear equations that depends on the specific hypothesis space. This is not a true black-box approach, as the authors must provide the Radon point calculation method for each hypothesis space. The paper also doesn't address how the Radon number r is determined for non-Euclidean spaces, which contradicts the claim of broad applicability.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical approach to parallel machine learning, the technical errors in the mathematical formulation, particularly the critical typesetting error in the doubly-exponential bound, significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The theoretical justification is incomplete, and the empirical evaluation lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. The formatting errors throughout the paper suggest a lack of careful proofreading, which is unacceptable for a NIPS submission.

The paper's contribution is interesting but not sufficiently supported by the technical content. The theoretical claims are marred by fundamental errors that would require substantial revision. Given the stringent standards of NIPS, I recommend rejection on the grounds of technical inaccuracies and insufficient detail in the empirical evaluation. The authors would need to completely rework the theoretical presentation and provide rigorous mathematical justification for their claims before this paper could be considered for publication.
