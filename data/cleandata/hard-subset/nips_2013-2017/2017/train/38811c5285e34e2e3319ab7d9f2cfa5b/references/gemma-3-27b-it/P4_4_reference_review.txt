This paper introduces the "Radon machine," a parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms aiming to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic. The approach leverages Radon points for aggregating hypotheses obtained from parallel runs on data subsets. The core idea is sound and builds upon existing work in averaging-based parallelization, offering a potentially significant improvement through the use of Radon points which provide faster confidence gains.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing guarantees on sample and runtime complexity. The connection to Nick's Class and the discussion of polylogarithmic runtime are valuable contributions.
*   **Generality:** The scheme is presented as a black-box approach, applicable to a broad class of learning algorithms satisfying certain consistency and efficiency conditions.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate substantial speedups compared to both sequential execution and other parallel machine learning libraries (Spark MLlib). The comparisons with averaging-at-the-end highlight the benefit of using Radon points.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and the core concepts are explained clearly, with a helpful algorithm description (Algorithm 1).
*   **Discussion of Limitations and Future Work:** The authors acknowledge potential limitations (high-dimensional data, non-linear models) and suggest avenues for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Radon Number Dependency:** The performance heavily relies on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. While the paper mentions that Euclidean spaces have a Radon number of *d*+2, it doesn't fully address the challenges associated with spaces having large Radon numbers. The practical impact of a large Radon number on the aggregation step's runtime isn't fully explored.
*   **Limited Exploration of Alternative Aggregation Schemes:** While the benefit of Radon points over averaging is shown empirically, a deeper discussion of alternative aggregation methods and their trade-offs could strengthen the work.
*   **Scalability with Processing Units:** The analysis assumes a sufficient number of processing units (rh). The "deparallelization" discussed briefly feels somewhat tacked on and a more detailed analysis of performance degradation with fewer than rh processors would be beneficial.
*   **Dataset Selection:** The experiments seem to favor datasets where the number of features is relatively small compared to the number of instances. More diverse datasets, including high-dimensional ones, would increase the robustness of the empirical evaluation.
*    **Limited Discussion of Communication Costs:** Although touched upon, a more detailed analysis of the communication costs associated with aggregating Radon points, especially in distributed settings, is missing.

**Originality:**

The paper's originality lies in the application of Radon points to parallel machine learning. While parallelization of learning algorithms is a well-studied area, the use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation and the resulting theoretical guarantees represent a novel contribution. The theoretical analysis linking this to Nick’s Class further strengthens the originality.

**Significance:**

The results have the potential to significantly improve the scalability of machine learning algorithms. Achieving polylogarithmic runtime, even under specific conditions, is a notable advancement. The practical demonstrations of substantial speedups are compelling and suggest real-world applicability. The black-box nature of the scheme makes it readily adaptable to various learning algorithms, potentially impacting a wide range of applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and guarantees.
*   Demonstrated empirical speedups on relevant datasets.
*   Novel application of Radon points to parallel learning.
*   Clear presentation and organization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Reliance on the Radon number and potential issues in high-dimensional spaces.
*   Limited exploration of alternative aggregation techniques.
*   Incomplete analysis of performance with fewer than rh processing units.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The theoretical contribution is solid, the empirical results are promising, and the work is clearly presented. While there are some limitations regarding the Radon number and scalability, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not negate the overall value of the work.  I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section, particularly a more thorough discussion of the Radon number's impact and performance with limited processing units.
