- Proposes the "Radon Machine": apply base learner to random subsets and aggregate hypotheses with Radon points for fast confidence boosting.
- Theoretical result: can achieve polylogarithmic runtime given a quasi-polynomial number of processors and increased data requirements (Theorem 4).
- Novelty: first application of Radon points to accelerate parallel machine learning aggregation.
- Strong mathematical analysis and clear connection to Radon number and scalability.
- Black-box claim: method can be applied to existing learners without algorithm-specific changes.
- Empirical results show significant speedups and comparable performance to Spark MLlib, but are limited in scope.
- Datasets are insufficiently described and experiments focus on feasibility rather than diverse problem types or high dimensionality.
- Comparisons are mainly to the sequential base learner; broader benchmarks against other parallel methods are needed.
- Practical implications of the Radon number r (which depends on hypothesis-space dimensionality) are under-discussed.
- Lack of detail on computational cost and feasibility of finding Radon points when r is large.
- Communication complexity in distributed environments is only briefly mentioned and needs quantitative analysis.
- Performance appears sensitive to the iteration parameter h; guidance on choosing h is missing.
- The claim of "no dedicated code" is optimistic—implementing Radon point computation likely requires nontrivial coding.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
- Requested revisions: expand empirical evaluation (higher-dimensional datasets, varied distributions, more baselines), analyze Radon-number practicality and Radon-point computation cost, detail communication complexity, clarify implementation requirements, and provide guidance on selecting h.
