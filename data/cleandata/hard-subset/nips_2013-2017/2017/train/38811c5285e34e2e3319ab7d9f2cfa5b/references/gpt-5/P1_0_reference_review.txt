Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization in convex, finite-dimensional Euclidean hypothesis spaces. The method trains a base learner in parallel on rh iid data subsets and aggregates the resulting hypotheses via an h-level tree of Radon points (each inner node computes the Radon point of r=d+2 children). The key theoretical result (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) shows a doubly-exponential reduction in the probability of producing an ε-bad hypothesis with the tree height h: if the base learner yields ε-bad with probability δ, the Radon aggregate yields ε-bad with probability at most (rδ)^(2^h). With a suitable choice of h, the approach can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity needed to attain a target (ε,Δ), at the cost of using rh processors and more data. The empirical study compares to averaging-at-the-end and Spark MLlib baselines, showing substantial wall-clock speedups and comparable predictive performance.

This draws on classic results in convex geometry (Radon’s theorem; connections to center points/Tukey depth) and relates to prior distributed learning schemes based on model averaging and mini-batch SGD. Unlike averaging, Radon aggregation is provably robust in the sense that a single bad model among r does not spoil the aggregate; the bound improves doubly-exponentially with depth. The work positions itself relative to NC-style parallel complexity and prior NIPS/ML work on distributed learning via averaging, ADMM, and stochastic optimization.

Strengths
- Clear, simple algorithmic idea with a black-box interface to base learners; easy to implement and reason about.
- Elegant and tight core bound: any ε-bad Radon aggregate implies at least two ε-bad children; iterating yields the (rδ)^(2^h) guarantee.
- Conceptual advance: connects robust geometric aggregation (Radon points) to parallelization for general convex ERM/RRM, not only specific learners.
- Theoretical runtime discussion is careful about the cost split between base training and aggregation (O(r^3) per node), and about the processor/data trade-offs.
- Empirical results are extensive enough to be persuasive: substantial speedups (80–700×) vs. single-node base learners at similar AUC; competitive with Spark MLlib at lower runtime; shows generality across binary classification, regression, and multi-class.
- Discussion section candidly addresses limitations (dimension dependence, nonlinearity, limited processors, communication, streaming).

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Key assumption on independence/iid: Algorithm 1 says “divide D into rh iid subsets,” which is not precise—partitioning cannot produce iid subsets. What is needed is that each base model is trained on an iid sample from the data-generating distribution (e.g., independent subsamples, possibly with replacement). Please clarify the sampling protocol and whether independence between sibling hypotheses holds. This matters to justify the iterative application of Proposition 2 at each level.
- The polylogarithmic-time claim relies on (i) choosing h≈log M and (ii) bounding the Radon number by a function polylogarithmic in sample complexity (i.e., dimension d polylog in M). This assumption is strong for many modern models; please make the dependence explicit in the main text (not only Appendix), and discuss regimes where d is moderate but not polylogarithmic. Also quantify rh under realistic d (e.g., d=10^3–10^5) and h used in practice (h≤3 in experiments).
- The base learner must achieve δ ≤ 1/(2r); when r=d+2 is large, the n required for this δ may be substantial. A discussion quantifying n(δ) for common learners (logistic regression, SVM) with realistic d would help bridge theory and practice.
- Empirically, the evaluation focuses on AUC/RMSE but does not probe the confidence improvement predicted by theory (e.g., repeated trials reporting empirical Δ vs. depth h). Even a small-scale demonstration would strengthen the theoretical claims.
- Comparisons: averaging-at-the-end is a natural baseline, but there is a growing literature on robust aggregation (geometric median, median-of-means) for distributed learning that offers improved robustness and confidence vs. simple averaging. Including (or at least discussing) geometric median aggregation would be informative and potentially strengthen the case that Radon points are preferable.
- Practical scalability: aggregation costs O(r^3) with r=d+2; for high d the cost and memory for solving many r×r systems may dominate. Please provide aggregation-time breakdowns vs. training time for different d, and discuss numerical stability/conditioning.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized; the algorithm and high-level intuition are clear.
- Please fix the “iid subsets” phrasing and precisely specify the sampling.
- Theorem 4 conditions and the precise meanings of “polylogarithmic time” and “quasi-polynomial processors” should be restated prominently with all dependencies (on ε, Δ, d).
- Provide the explicit linear system for Radon points in the main text or a figure referencing the appendix; a brief note on computational/numerical considerations would help practitioners.

Originality
- Using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation appears novel and interesting, going beyond common averaging/median schemes; connections to centerpoint depth are appropriate. The related work section is broad, but consider adding references and contrasts to robust distributed aggregation (e.g., geometric median, coordinate-wise median, median-of-means) for completeness.

Significance
- The theoretical framing—turning polynomial-time learning into polylogarithmic-time in sample complexity with many processors—is conceptually significant, though its practicality hinges on d and processor availability. The empirical results show meaningful speedups without sacrificing accuracy in realistic settings, supporting practical value for moderate d and small h.
- The black-box nature is attractive for practitioners who cannot modify solvers.

Pros
- Novel aggregation mechanism with clean, strong probability bounds.
- Black-box, widely applicable to convex ERM/RRM in Rd.
- Strong empirical speedups; comparable or better accuracy than baselines.
- Clear articulation of trade-offs and limitations; discussion of extensions (random projections, block coordinate descent).

Cons
- Independence/sampling assumptions need clarification; current wording is imprecise.
- Polylog-time result depends on restrictive dimensional assumptions; number of processors can be very large.
- Aggregation cost and numerical aspects for large d are not deeply evaluated.
- Missing comparison to robust aggregation baselines beyond averaging.
- No empirical validation of the predicted confidence improvement with increasing h.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a simple, elegant idea with solid theory and promising empirical results. With clarifications on the sampling/independence assumptions, a more explicit statement of dimensional/processor assumptions behind Theorem 4, and discussion (or comparison) to robust aggregation alternatives, this would be a strong contribution. Even if the NC-style claim is mainly of theoretical interest, the practical, black-box speedups for moderate d make the work valuable to the community.
