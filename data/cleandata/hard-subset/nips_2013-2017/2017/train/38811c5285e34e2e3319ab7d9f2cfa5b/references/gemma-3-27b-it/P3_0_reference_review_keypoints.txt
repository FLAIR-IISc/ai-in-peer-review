- Overall assessment: The paper offers a theoretically sound, novel parallelization using Radon points with potential impact, but needs clearer presentation and stronger practical evaluation — recommend Accept with Minor Revisions.  
- Core idea: Iteratively aggregate hypotheses from random data subsets using Radon points to achieve polylogarithmic runtime at the cost of increased data and processors.  
- Theoretical contribution: Provides rigorous sample, runtime complexity, and doubly-exponential confidence guarantees; propositions and theorems appear sound.  
- Novelty: Applying Radon points to parallelize learning is a fresh, nontrivial idea that outperforms simple averaging schemes.  
- Black-box applicability: The Radon Machine can be applied to a wide range of learning algorithms without internal modification.  
- Empirical validation: Experiments show significant speed-ups over base learners and competitive results with Spark MLlib, but are limited in scope.  
- Positioning/prior work: Well-situated relative to averaging-based parallel methods, convex optimization, Rademacher complexity, and parallel computation literature.  
- Clarity/accessibility: Paper is densely written with heavy notation; needs more intuitive explanations (e.g., Radon points) and a gentler introduction.  
- Practical constraints: Quasi-polynomial processor requirements and communication costs are not fully explored or quantified.  
- Radon number issues: Impact of high Radon number r on performance and mitigation strategies (e.g., dimensionality reduction) are insufficiently addressed.  
- High-dimensional data: Proposed remedies (random projections, low-rank approximations) are superficial and need concrete integration details.  
- Communication complexity: Claimed low communication cost lacks quantitative analysis and scaling discussion.  
- Empirical scope limitation: Datasets are relatively small; comparisons to Spark MLlib and other parallel methods should be broader and across more tasks.  
- For acceptance: Strong theoretical novelty, practical acceleration potential, and thorough literature grounding support acceptance.  
- Against acceptance: Dense presentation, limited empirical evidence, and shallow treatment of high-dimensional and practical scaling issues argue against acceptance.  
- Suggested revisions: Improve clarity (esp. Section 2), quantify communication costs, expand empirical evaluation to larger/diverse datasets, detail integration of dimensionality reduction, discuss Radon number mitigation, and provide communication complexity estimates.
