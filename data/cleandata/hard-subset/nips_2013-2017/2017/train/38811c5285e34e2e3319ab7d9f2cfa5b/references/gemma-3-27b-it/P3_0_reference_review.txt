## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially impactful approach to parallelizing regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea of leveraging Radon points for aggregation provides a compelling avenue for achieving polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial increase in data and processing units. While the theoretical results are strong, the presentation is dense and could benefit from greater clarity. The empirical evaluation, while promising, feels somewhat limited in scope and would benefit from more extensive analysis, particularly regarding practical limitations. I lean towards **Accept with Minor Revisions**, primarily because of the theoretical novelty and potential significance, but the authors need to address the clarity issues and strengthen the discussion of practical constraints.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the critical problem of scaling machine learning algorithms to larger datasets by proposing a novel parallelization scheme based on iteratively aggregating hypotheses generated from random subsets of the data using Radon points.  The authors formally demonstrate that this “Radon Machine” can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic, at the cost of increased data and processing units.  They position their work within the context of consistent and efficient learning algorithms and provide rigorous theoretical guarantees on the resulting confidence and error bounds.

The authors correctly situate their work within the broader landscape of parallel machine learning. They differentiate their approach from simple averaging-based parallelization schemes (e.g., [32, 45, 46]) by highlighting the doubly-exponential confidence increase provided by Radon points.  The connection to existing work on parallel computation and Nick's Class [9] is also well-articulated, correctly framing the problem in terms of the limitations of parallelizing inherently difficult problems.  The referencing of relevant work on convex optimization, Rademacher complexity, and parallel algorithms is comprehensive.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's strongest asset is its strong theoretical foundation. The derivation of the sample and runtime complexity, along with the proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3, appear sound and provide a compelling justification for the proposed scheme. The formal guarantees on confidence and error bounds are a significant contribution.
* **Novelty:** The application of Radon points to parallelize learning algorithms is a genuinely novel idea. While Radon points are known in geometry, their utilization in this context offers a fresh perspective on aggregation techniques and provides a distinct advantage over simpler averaging schemes.
* **Black-Box Parallelization:** The “black-box” nature of the Radon Machine is a substantial practical advantage.  The authors convincingly argue that the scheme can be applied to a wide range of learning algorithms without requiring specific modifications to their internal implementations.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates a significant speed-up compared to the base learners and competitive performance against Spark’s MLlib, offering practical evidence of the scheme's effectiveness.
* **Detailed Discussion:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting the algorithm and provides a thoughtful discussion of its limitations, potential extensions, and relation to other parallel computing paradigms.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Accessibility:** The paper is densely written and requires significant effort to follow. The mathematical notation is heavy, and the explanations of key concepts (e.g., Radon points) could be more intuitive.  A more gradual introduction of the mathematical framework would improve readability.
* **Practical Constraints:** While the theoretical analysis allows for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units, the practical implications of this are not fully explored.  The authors acknowledge the communication costs but don’t provide a detailed analysis of how these costs might scale with the number of processors.  Similarly, the impact of the Radon number 'r' on performance isn't fully addressed.  A discussion on how to mitigate high Radon numbers (e.g., dimensionality reduction) would be valuable.
* **Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation, while positive, feels somewhat limited.  The datasets used are relatively small, and the comparison with Spark MLlib is not comprehensive across all algorithms.  Expanding the empirical evaluation to larger datasets and more diverse learning tasks would strengthen the claims.
* **High-Dimensional Data:** The authors acknowledge the challenges with high-dimensional data, but the proposed solutions (random projections, low-rank approximations) feel somewhat superficial. A more detailed discussion of how these techniques would be integrated into the Radon Machine would be beneficial.
* **Communication Complexity:** The authors mention the low communication complexity but don't quantify it.  Providing an estimate of the communication cost would be helpful, especially in relation to the computational savings.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Significant theoretical contribution with rigorous guarantees.
* Novel approach to parallelizing learning algorithms.
* Practical potential for accelerating existing algorithms.
* Well-written (despite density) and thoroughly researched.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Density and lack of clarity hinder understanding.
* Limited empirical evaluation and insufficient discussion of practical limitations.
* Superficial treatment of high-dimensional data challenges.



**5. Specific Suggestions and Questions for the Authors:**

* **Clarity:** Rewrite key sections, particularly Section 2, with a focus on accessibility. Provide more intuitive explanations of concepts like Radon points and quasi-convexity.
* **Practical Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the communication costs associated with the Radon Machine, particularly as the number of processors increases.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** Expand the empirical evaluation to include larger datasets, more diverse learning tasks, and a more comprehensive comparison with other parallel learning algorithms.
* **High-Dimensional Data:** Elaborate on how dimensionality reduction techniques (random projections, low-rank approximations) would be integrated into the Radon Machine and how they would impact performance.
* **Radon Number:**  Discuss the impact of the Radon number ‘r’ on the performance of the algorithm and explore strategies for mitigating the computational cost of calculating Radon points when ‘r’ is large.
* **Communication Complexity:** Quantify the communication complexity of the Radon Machine.




**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The theoretical results are compelling, and the proposed scheme has the potential to significantly accelerate the training of learning algorithms. Addressing the clarity issues and strengthening the discussion of practical constraints would further enhance the paper’s impact and make it a more valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
