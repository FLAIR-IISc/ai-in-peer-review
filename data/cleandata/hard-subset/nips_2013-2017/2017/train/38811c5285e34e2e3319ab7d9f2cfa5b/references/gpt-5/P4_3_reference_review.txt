Summary
The paper proposes a black-box parallelization scheme for convex, regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean parameter spaces. The scheme, termed the Radon machine, trains a base learner on rh disjoint iid subsets in parallel and aggregates the resulting hypotheses up a balanced r-ary tree using Radon points (with r equal to the Radon number of the hypothesis space; in Rd, r = d + 2). The key theoretical result shows that if the base learner produces an ε-good hypothesis with probability at least 1 − δ, then after h aggregation levels the probability that the final hypothesis is ε-bad is at most (r δ)^{2^h}. Under standard sample complexity assumptions NL0(ε, δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k, the authors show that achieving the same (ε, Δ)-guarantee as the sequential learner can be done in polylogarithmic time (in the sequential sample requirement) using quasi-polynomially many processors, provided the Radon number is at most polylogarithmic in the sample complexity. The aggregation at each node requires solving a small linear system of size r × r, contributing h r^3 to the critical path.

Empirically, the method is implemented in Spark and evaluated with base learners (logistic regression, SVMs, ridge regression) and against averaging-at-the-end and Spark MLlib algorithms on classification and regression tasks, using up to 150 processors. The Radon machine achieves 80–700× speedups over single-processor base learners with comparable AUC, outperforms averaging in AUC with similar runtime, and is 15–85% faster than Spark baselines while achieving comparable accuracy.

Relative to prior work on averaging-based parallelization, distributed mini-batch SGD, and distributed ERM/ADMM, this work replaces averaging by Radon-point aggregation to obtain doubly-exponential confidence amplification in tree height, enabling the polylogarithmic-time result (with quasi-polynomial processors), thereby connecting to NC-style parallel efficiency.

Quality
- Theory: Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are well-motivated and correctly leverage properties of Radon points and quasi-convexity (the probabilistic bound via the existence of two children with higher or equal Q and a union bound over pairs). The constants are conservative ((r p)^2 vs. binomial factor) but sound. The runtime analysis is coherent: TR,h = TL(n) + h r^3, with n chosen so that M ≈ 2^h n, giving polylogarithmic dependence on M when r = polylog M and h ≈ log M − log log M. The assumption δ ≤ 1/(2r) is reasonable as it can be enforced by choosing n accordingly.
- Assumptions: The analysis presupposes Euclidean parameterization, convexity, and finite Radon number, and further that r can be upper-bounded by a function polylog in the sample complexity. This restricts applicability to problems with moderate effective dimension or with feature embeddings reducing dimension. The processors required are r^h, which is quasi-polynomial in the sequential sample complexity; the paper discusses de-parallelization for smaller c.
- Empirics: The experiments show substantial wall-clock gains on a real cluster with comparable predictive performance. Comparisons to averaging and Spark baselines are relevant. The setup focuses on linear models and relatively small h due to dimension.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Algorithm 1 is explicit. The properties of Radon points, quasi-convexity, and the aggregation analysis are clearly presented. Some notation (ld for log) should be defined explicitly. Equation (4) appears to miss the caret; it should read Δ = (r δ)^{2^h}. The precise parallel computation model is briefly referenced (PRAM) but could be stated when defining the runtime metric. The requirement and computation of r in practice (r = d + 2) could be highlighted earlier.

Originality
The use of Radon-point aggregation for confidence amplification in distributed learning, yielding a doubly-exponential reduction in error probability per level and enabling polylogarithmic time (with quasi-polynomial processors), appears novel compared to averaging-at-the-end and distributed SGD/ERM. The connection to NC-learnability is conceptually interesting.

Significance
The theoretical contribution clarifies limits and possibilities for black-box parallelization of a broad class of convex learners, offering a constructive scheme with provable guarantees. Practically, the scheme yields sizable speedups with comparable accuracy in realistic settings and minimal code changes. The need for quasi-polynomial processors and constraints on dimension temper the immediate impact but still make this relevant for large-scale, low/moderate-dimensional problems.

Strengths
- Clear, general scheme that treats the base learner as a black box.
- Sound theoretical guarantee: doubly-exponential confidence amplification and polylogarithmic time in the sequential sample requirement (assuming r polylog and enough processors).
- Robustness of Radon aggregation vs. averaging is formalized.
- Practical implementation demonstrating large speedups and good accuracy.
- Good discussion of scope, limitations, and related work.

Weaknesses
- Processor count grows as r^h (quasi-polynomial), which may be impractical for large d or tighter guarantees (large h).
- The polylogarithmic-time claim relies on r being polylog in sample complexity; this excludes high-dimensional settings without prior dimensionality reduction and is a key limitation.
- Empirics focus on linear models and small h; no comparisons to robust aggregators (e.g., geometric median) that might offer similar robustness with different trade-offs.
- Minor clarity issues: define ld; fix exponent typo in Eq. (4); state parallel model and communication cost assumptions more precisely; discuss how h is chosen in practice without knowing M.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the computational model (synchrony, PRAM vs. BSP) when stating runtime and communication complexity; quantify communication per level and per processor.
- Fix Eq. (4) and define ld. State explicitly r = d + 2 for Euclidean parameterization at first mention.
- Provide guidance for choosing h in practice (e.g., via pilot runs or bounds on NL0) without relying on unknown M.
- Add experiments with a robust aggregator baseline (geometric median/median-of-means) to disentangle the benefit of Radon aggregation from generic robust averaging.
- Include a case study with explicit dimensionality reduction (e.g., random features) to demonstrate applicability beyond linear/low-dimensional regimes.

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid, general, and novel theoretical result with clear assumptions.
- Practical algorithm with strong empirical speedups and comparable accuracy.
- Clear advancement over averaging-based parallelization with formal robustness and confidence amplification.
- Relevance to an open problem on efficient parallel learning and connection to NC.

Arguments against acceptance
- Requires quasi-polynomially many processors; impact limited when processor budget is modest.
- Applicability constrained by dimension; empirical evaluation limited to linear models with small h.
- Missing comparisons to other robust aggregation schemes; some clarity issues and minor notation errors.

Overall, the paper provides a meaningful theoretical and practical contribution to parallel learning under convex risk minimization with a black-box scheme and merits consideration, subject to clarifications and broader empirical comparisons.
