Summary
This paper proposes a black-box parallelization scheme, the Radon machine, for regularized risk minimization with convex loss/regularizer in finite-dimensional Euclidean hypothesis spaces. The algorithm runs the base learner L independently on rh iid subsets (r is the Radon number, h is tree height), then aggregates bottom-up by computing Radon points of r hypotheses at a time. The key theoretical result is a confidence-amplification bound: if a base hypothesis is ε-bad with probability δ, the Radon aggregation reduces this probability to (rδ)^{2^h} after h levels (Proposition 2, Theorem 3). Under standard sample complexity assumptions (polynomial in 1/ε and log(1/δ)), the authors show that by choosing h ≈ log M − log log M (M = samples required by L to reach a target (ε,Δ)), the Radon machine’s runtime becomes polylogarithmic in M provided one can use rh processors and the Radon number is polylogarithmic in M (Theorem 4). Empirically, an implementation on Spark shows 80–700× speedups over single-node base learners and competitive AUC vs. Spark MLlib on several datasets; averaging-at-the-end is consistently worse in AUC though slightly faster.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean, black-box scheme covering a broad class of convex ERM algorithms; minimal assumptions (convexity, finite-dimensional parametrization).
- Elegant use of Radon points to obtain doubly-exponential reduction in failure probability per aggregation level; the proof via quasi-convexity is simple and insightful.
- Clear separation of learning and aggregation phases; aggregation has low depth and low per-level computational cost (O(r^3) with parallel computation).
- Empirical results suggest practical speedups on moderate hardware with comparable predictive performance to strong distributed baselines.
- Connects parallel learning to NC-style complexity questions and articulates the trade-off among samples, processors, and time.

Weaknesses and questions
- Resource model and applicability: Achieving polylogarithmic runtime requires rh processors and N = rh n samples. With r = d+2 in Rd, c = rh grows extremely fast; even for modest d and h the required processors and data size become enormous. Theorem 4 effectively assumes d ≤ polylog(N0), which excludes many modern regimes (text, sparse high-dimensional models). The paper mentions random projections/low-rank approximations, but does not provide formal guarantees that these preserve regret and the convex structure needed by the analysis.
- Fairness of theoretical comparison: The “polylog time” is measured against the sample size M needed by the base learner to reach the same confidence Δ = (rδ)^{2^h}. Achieving that Δ via the Radon machine itself requires N = rh N0(ε,δ) samples and c = rh processors; the data and compute budgets are not held fixed. While this is explicit, the practical significance of polylog time under quasi-polynomial processors and substantially more data is limited. It would help to more carefully discuss regimes where N and c are constrained and quantify speedup as a function of c < rh (the paper alludes to a factor max{1, rh/c} but does not integrate this into the main theorem).
- Independence assumptions: The analysis treats each aggregation level as operating on r iid hypotheses drawn from the same distribution. In Algorithm 1, hypotheses are produced from disjoint subsamples drawn without replacement from a finite D; the resulting hypotheses are exchangeable but not strictly iid. This gap should be acknowledged more explicitly, with either an argument (e.g., via sampling with replacement) or bounds quantifying the deviation.
- Tightness and constants in Proposition 2: The bound P(Q(r) > ε) ≤ (r δ)^2 comes from a union bound over pairs; a more precise bound is ~ (r choose 2) δ^2. Constants won’t change the exponential-in-2^h behavior, but stating or justifying the exact multiplicative factor would improve rigor.
- Empirical evaluation: 
  - Baselines: The comparisons to Spark MLlib are useful, but they do not include state-of-the-art communication-efficient ERM methods (e.g., CoCoA, DANE, or distributed Newton-type methods) or modern distributed SGD variants. Given the claim of practical superiority, stronger baselines would be appropriate.
  - Metrics vs. theory: The theory is about convex regret Q; experiments report AUC (for classification) and RMSE (for regression). While fine for practical relevance, the connection to the theoretical failure probabilities is indirect and not validated.
  - Scaling in dimension: No experiments stress the algorithm at high d (where r=d+2 raises aggregation cost and increases c). Numerical stability of Radon point computation is not discussed.
  - Communication: Theoretical message complexity is O(r^{h+1}), which can be large; empirical wall-time and communication/serialization overheads on the cluster are not broken down.
- Related work: The paper positions Radon aggregation vs. averaging, but omits connections to robust aggregation schemes such as (geometric) median-of-means and related robust distributed estimators that also yield exponential tail improvements and are widely used for Byzantine-robust aggregation. A discussion and citations would situate the contribution better.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. Suggestions:
- Define “ld” clearly (log base 2?) and avoid ambiguous notation.
- Move the c < rh simulation and its impact on runtime into the main text.
- State assumptions (iid subsets, sampling with replacement, independence across levels) explicitly and consistently.

Originality
Using iterated Radon points to amplify confidence in a distributed learning setting appears novel and is an interesting addition to the repertoire of black-box parallelization schemes.

Significance
Asymptotically, the results are thought-provoking, tying parallel learning to NC despite nontrivial resource assumptions. Practically, the method is appealing as a simple, low-communication wrapper that can provide speedups and maintain performance for moderate d and modest cluster sizes. Its significance would be strengthened by (i) tighter modeling of realistic processor/data constraints, (ii) comparisons to stronger distributed ERM/SGD baselines, and (iii) formal treatment of dimensionality reduction’s effect on guarantees.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, general, and novel theoretical contribution with simple proof ideas.
- Black-box method with appealing empirical speedups and low communication.
- Bridges classical geometric constructs (Radon points) with modern distributed learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong theoretical claims hinge on resource assumptions (c, N, d) that are often unrealistic.
- Experimental baselines and analysis do not fully validate claims under realistic constraints or against the strongest methods.
- Independence and sampling assumptions deserve a more careful treatment.

Recommendation: Weak accept if the venue values conceptual advances in parallelization with clean theory and promising practice; otherwise borderline due to resource assumptions and empirical scope.
