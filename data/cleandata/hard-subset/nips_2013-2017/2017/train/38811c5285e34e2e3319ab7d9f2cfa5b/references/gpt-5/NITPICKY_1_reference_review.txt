Summary
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for convex, regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean hypothesis spaces with finite Radon number r=d+2. The method trains base learners on rh iid subsets and aggregates outputs via iterated Radon points along a tree. Theoretically, using Proposition 2 and Theorem 3, the probability of an ε-bad aggregate decays doubly exponentially in the tree height h, enabling (claimed) polylogarithmic runtime in the sample complexity required to reach a target (ε,Δ), when run on quasi-polynomially many processors and with increased sample usage. Empirically, on several datasets, the method attains similar AUC to baselines while achieving sizable wall-clock speedups over single-CPU base learners and various Spark MLlib implementations.

Strengths
- Simple, black-box scheme applicable to several convex learners; aggregation step is independent of base learner implementation.
- Elegant use of Radon points; Proposition 2/Theorem 3 are clean and show robustness versus averaging.
- Clear connection to parallel complexity (NC) and discussion of processors vs runtime trade-offs.
- Empirically demonstrates substantial speedups in practice with competitive AUC.
- Addresses communication complexity qualitatively and notes compatibility with distributed data.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
1) Mismatch between theory and experiments: The theory concerns high-confidence PAC-style guarantees (ε,Δ), yet experiments evaluate AUC and do not estimate Δ, verify δ≤1/(2r), or check if the base subsets achieve the preconditions. The claimed “same (ε,Δ)” regime is not validated empirically.

2) Massive sample blow-up is underemphasized: To achieve polylog runtime, the scheme uses N=rh n samples with h≈log M, yielding N≈M^{log r} log M (quasi-polynomial if r=polylog M, polynomial if r is constant). This can be orders of magnitude more data than the sequential learner needs for the same (ε,Δ). The practical feasibility/cost of acquiring and processing vastly more data is not analyzed.

3) Processor count realism: Achieving TR,h(N)=T_L(n)+h r^3 assumes c=rh processors. With h≈log M and r≥3, c grows super-polynomial unless d is very small (or polylog in M). The experimental setting (≤150 cores) cannot realize this degree of parallelism; the “deparallelized” factor max{1, rh/c} is mentioned but not quantified for the reported runs. Claimed 700× speedup with only 150 cores deserves a careful accounting (including Spark overheads, single-threaded baselines, and exact degree of parallelism).

4) Independence assumptions: Algorithm 1 “divides D into rh iid subsets,” but partitioning a fixed dataset yields dependent (and non-iid) blocks. Theorems are stated for iid draws from D; the consequences of sampling without replacement from a finite dataset are not discussed.

5) Proposition 2 constants: P(Q(r)>ε) ≤ (rδ)^2 is a loose bound (should be ≤ C(r,2) δ^2). Iterating constants through levels (Theorem 3) is handled sloppily; the dependence on r is nontrivial across levels yet is compacted into (rδ)^{2^h}. The subsequent sample-complexity algebra “ignoring logarithmic and constant terms” is too cavalier for the claimed runtime improvements.

6) Form of sample complexity: The assumed NL0(ε,δ)=(αε+βε ld 1/δ)^k (with footnote “1”?) is non-standard and insufficiently justified; αε,βε dependences are deferred to appendix. The crucial step that NL0(ε,Δ) “behaves as 2^h NL0(ε,δ)” lacks a clean derivation and hides constants/exponents that materially affect the runtime choice of h.

7) Numerical stability and cost of Radon points: Aggregation requires solving r×r linear systems in parameter space. No discussion of conditioning, stability, or regularization; the O(r^3) bound ignores precision/conditioning, which may matter in high dimensions or ill-scaled features.

8) Aggregation space vs prediction: Aggregating parameters via Radon points is only guaranteed in parameter space. The relationship to predictive performance (e.g., AUC) is not theoretically established; convex losses are handled but the empirical metric is AUC.

9) Baselines: The “averaging-at-the-end” baseline is weak; missing stronger robust aggregators (geometric median/median-of-means, centerpoint approximations) that are natural competitors in this context. Comparisons to state-of-the-art, optimized distributed solvers (e.g., well-tuned SGD/ADMM variants) are limited, and Spark overheads complicate fairness.

10) Clarity/notation issues: “ld” (log base 2?) is not defined. Several typos (“we proof”), broken text (“st Conference on NIPS 2017”), inconsistent use of M,N,n,δ,Δ, and occasional ambiguity between distribution-level and finite-sample settings. “iid subsets” misuse as noted.

11) Scope limitation: The scheme needs d small enough that r=d+2 is manageable; high-dimensional settings require projections/embeddings (suggested), but no theory is provided for preserving the guarantees under such transformations.

12) References/formatting: Many references are incomplete or malformed (missing years/pages; duplicated names; inconsistent styles). This is below NeurIPS standards.

Questions for authors
- Provide a precise derivation (without “ignoring log/const terms”) of the h choice and the resulting N, c, and runtime; include constants and explicit dependence on r,d,k.
- Quantify, in experiments, δ for the base learners on subsets and estimate Δ for the aggregates, or at least report surrogate bounds to connect to the theory.
- Report exact processor utilization, degree of parallelism at each level, and wall-clock breakdown (training vs aggregation vs orchestration).
- Compare against geometric median aggregation and a strong distributed SGD/ADMM baseline tuned to the same systems.
- Discuss numerical conditioning of the Radon linear systems and any regularization/stabilization used.

NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Novel theoretical angle, but several steps are informal; empirical validation does not test the theoretical regime. Concerns about constants, independence, and data/processor blow-ups.
- Clarity: Generally readable but marred by notation sloppiness, typos, and missing definitions. Appendices (not provided here) appear essential.
- Originality: Combining Radon points with black-box parallel learning is interesting and, to my knowledge, new in this form.
- Significance: Potentially interesting, but practical impact is limited by data/processor requirements and lack of strong head-to-head baselines; theoretical contribution is incremental without tighter complexity accounting.

Minor editorial issues
- Define “ld,” clean typos (“proof”→“prove”), fix broken text at page header, ensure consistent symbols (N,M,n,δ,Δ).
- Replace “iid subsets” with “independent draws” or clarify finite-sample partitioning.
- Complete and standardize references.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is appealing, but the theoretical runtime claims rely on informal approximations and strong assumptions (iid blocks, quasi-polynomial data/processor blow-ups), while the experiments do not validate the promised high-confidence regime nor compare against stronger baselines. With tighter analysis, clearer assumptions, stronger empirical validation, and improved presentation, this could be a solid future submission.
