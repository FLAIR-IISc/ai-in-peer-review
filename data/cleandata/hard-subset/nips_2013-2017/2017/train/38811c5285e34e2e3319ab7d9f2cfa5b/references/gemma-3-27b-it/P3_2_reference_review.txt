## NIPS 2017 Review: Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially impactful contribution to the challenging problem of parallelizing machine learning algorithms. The core idea of leveraging Radon points for aggregation offers a promising avenue for achieving polylogarithmic runtime, albeit with the cost of increased data and processing units. While the theoretical analysis is rigorous, the practical evaluation, though encouraging, feels somewhat limited in scope. I believe the paper merits acceptance, leaning towards a strong accept, contingent on addressing some minor concerns detailed below.

**Summary:**

The authors tackle the critical issue of scaling machine learning algorithms to massive datasets. They propose a novel parallelization scheme, the “Radon Machine,” that aims to achieve polylogarithmic runtime for regularized risk minimization problems. This is achieved by parallelizing the base learning algorithm on data subsets and then iteratively aggregating the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, which provide a stronger confidence boost than simple averaging.  The key theoretical result is demonstrating that this scheme achieves the same confidence and error bounds as sequential execution, but in significantly reduced time, at the expense of increased data size and processing units.  The paper relates the work to existing parallel learning strategies, particularly averaging-based methods, and emphasizes the benefits of using Radon points for faster convergence.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical analysis of the Radon Machine, proving its consistency and efficiency under reasonable assumptions. The theorem demonstrating the polylogarithmic runtime is a significant result. The connection to Radon’s theorem and the analysis of the Radon point’s properties are well-executed.
* **Novelty:** The use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation in a parallel learning setting is a novel approach. It offers a theoretically justified alternative to averaging and attempts to address the limitations of existing parallelization techniques.
* **Black-Box Applicability:**  The scheme's strength lies in its generality. It can be applied to a wide class of consistent and efficient learning algorithms without requiring algorithm-specific modifications. This ‘black-box’ characteristic is highly valuable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results, while not extensive, provide encouraging evidence that the Radon Machine can achieve substantial speedups in practice, particularly when using a large number of processors. The comparison to Spark’s MLlib is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The concepts are explained clearly, and the mathematical notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Limitations & Scope of Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is conducted on a limited number of datasets, and primarily focuses on linear models. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, more extensive experiments with non-linear models and larger datasets would bolster the claims of practical applicability. The reliance on Weka and scikit-learn base learners also feels somewhat restrictive.
* **Quasi-Polynomial Processing Units:** The requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units is a significant practical hurdle. While the paper discusses the possibility of simulating this using fewer physical processors, the overhead of such simulation is not quantified. The practical implications of this cost are crucial and deserve further discussion.
* **Radon Number Dependence:** The runtime performance is heavily dependent on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of strategies for handling cases where the Radon number is large (or unknown) would be beneficial. The paper touches on low-rank approximations, but doesn’t fully explore their practical feasibility.
* **Communication Complexity:** While briefly mentioned, the communication complexity of the Radon machine, especially with a large number of processors, is a potential bottleneck. A more detailed analysis or empirical assessment of communication costs would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:**  The comparison to Spark’s MLlib, while positive, could be expanded to include more recent and advanced distributed learning frameworks.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms.
* The polylogarithmic runtime guarantee is a significant theoretical contribution.
* The black-box nature of the scheme makes it broadly applicable.
* The empirical results, despite their limitations, are encouraging.
* The paper addresses a crucial problem in machine learning – scaling to massive datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The quasi-polynomial requirement for processing units may limit practical applicability.
* The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and doesn't fully address the challenges of non-linear models or large datasets.
* The communication complexity is not thoroughly analyzed.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you elaborate on strategies for mitigating the communication overhead, particularly in scenarios with a large number of processors?
* How does the performance of the Radon machine degrade as the Radon number of the hypothesis space increases?
* What are the computational costs associated with simulating a larger number of processing units on a smaller physical cluster?
* Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the applicability of the Radon machine to non-linear models, and perhaps explore the use of low-rank approximations or other techniques to address dimensionality issues?
* Would you consider adding a discussion about the potential for adapting the Radon Machine to operate on data streams or in a federated learning setting?



**Overall Score:** 8.5 / 10

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept.  I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field and has the potential to influence future research in parallel machine learning. Addressing the minor concerns outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
