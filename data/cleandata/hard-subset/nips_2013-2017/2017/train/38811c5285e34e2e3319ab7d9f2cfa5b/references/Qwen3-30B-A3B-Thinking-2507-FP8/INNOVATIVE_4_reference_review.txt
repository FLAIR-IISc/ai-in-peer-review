

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for machine learning algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime by leveraging Radon points for hypothesis aggregation. The key insight is that using Radon points instead of simple averaging provides a doubly-exponential improvement in confidence guarantees with each aggregation level. The authors prove that this approach reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic while maintaining the same error bounds, with a quasi-polynomial increase in data requirements and processing units.

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength is its exceptional novelty. The use of Radon points from convex geometry for hypothesis aggregation represents a bold, creative departure from existing averaging-based parallelization approaches. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a fundamentally new conceptual framework for parallel machine learning. The theoretical foundation is strong, with rigorous proofs showing the doubly-exponential confidence improvement (Theorem 3), and the connection to Nick's Class in theoretical computer science elevates the significance of the work.

The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating 80-700x speedups over sequential implementations while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The black-box nature of the approach is particularly valuable, as it can parallelize any regularized risk minimization algorithm without requiring algorithm-specific modifications. This practical applicability, combined with the theoretical novelty, makes the contribution highly significant for both theory and practice.

The paper directly addresses a fundamental open problem in parallel machine learning: "Is parallel machine learning possible in polylogarithmic time using a polynomial number of processors?" The Radon Machine provides a concrete, provably effective solution for a broad class of learning algorithms, which is a major contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
The approach requires the hypothesis space to have a finite Radon number (d+2 for Euclidean spaces), which implies the dimensionality must be known and bounded. This could limit applicability to high-dimensional or non-Euclidean spaces without additional preprocessing. The paper acknowledges this limitation in Section 5, noting that low-rank approximations or random projections could alleviate it, but the requirement for bounded dimensionality remains a constraint.

The quasi-polynomial increase in data size and processing units might be prohibitive for some applications, though the authors argue this is a reasonable cost for achieving polylogarithmic runtime. The empirical evaluation is limited to moderately sized datasets due to scalability constraints of the base learners, though the authors demonstrate the approach on large datasets (SUSY with 5 million instances) using 150 processors.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by introducing a genuinely novel approach to parallel machine learning with strong theoretical grounding and impressive empirical results. The Radon Machine concept is bold and addresses a fundamental open problem in the field. While the scope is limited to hypothesis spaces with finite Radon number, this is a common trade-off in theoretical work and is appropriately acknowledged by the authors.

The paper's novelty and significance far outweigh its minor limitations, especially considering the reviewer's preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection. The theoretical connection to Nick's Class provides a strong foundation for future research, and the practical benefits demonstrated in the empirical evaluation make this a valuable contribution to the machine learning community.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Radon point aggregation represents a genuinely novel concept with strong theoretical grounding
- Directly addresses a fundamental open problem in parallel machine learning
- Demonstrates impressive empirical speedups (80-700x) with comparable predictive performance
- Black-box parallelization is broadly applicable to many machine learning algorithms
- Theoretical connection to Nick's Class elevates the significance of the work

*Con:*
- Limited to hypothesis spaces with finite Radon number (requires bounded dimensionality)
- Requires quasi-polynomial increase in data and processing units
- Empirical evaluation limited to moderately sized datasets due to base learner scalability

Overall, the paper's bold approach, strong theoretical contribution, and practical significance make it a compelling candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The novelty and potential impact of the Radon Machine concept outweigh the minor limitations, and the authors have appropriately acknowledged the scope of their contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
