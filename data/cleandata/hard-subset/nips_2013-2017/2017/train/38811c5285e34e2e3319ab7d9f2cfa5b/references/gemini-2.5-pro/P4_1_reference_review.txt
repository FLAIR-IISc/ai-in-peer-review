**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme, named the "Radon Machine," for a broad class of regularized risk minimization (RRM) algorithms. The core idea is to partition the training data into a large number of subsets, train a base learning algorithm on each subset in parallel to obtain initial hypotheses, and then recursively aggregate these hypotheses using an aggregation tree. The key innovation lies in the aggregation step, which computes the Radon point of `r` hypotheses at each node, where `r` is the Radon number of the hypothesis space.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this aggregation method improves the confidence bounds on the final hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. Specifically, if the base learner on a data subset has a failure probability of `δ`, the Radon machine with an aggregation tree of height `h` has a failure probability bounded by `(rδ)^(2^h)`. Leveraging this powerful confidence amplification, the authors show that one can achieve the same (ε,∆)-guarantee as a sequential learner while reducing the runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the required sample size. This speedup, however, requires a quasi-polynomial number of processors and a corresponding increase in the total amount of data. The authors frame this result in the context of computational complexity, suggesting it is a step towards understanding whether RRM problems belong to Nick's Class (NC) of efficiently parallelizable problems.

This work relates to previous research on parallel learning, particularly averaging-based methods [32, 45] and distributed stochastic optimization [34]. It distinguishes itself by introducing Radon points as a new aggregation mechanism that provides much stronger theoretical guarantees than simple averaging, enabling the claimed polylogarithmic runtime. The paper also provides an empirical evaluation comparing the Radon Machine to sequential learners, an averaging-at-the-end baseline, and parallel algorithms from Spark's MLlib, demonstrating significant practical speedups and competitive predictive performance.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality:** The central idea of using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation in a parallel learning context is highly novel. It presents a new and principled alternative to more common techniques like parameter averaging or gradient aggregation. This approach is not an incremental improvement but a new direction for divide-and-conquer style parallelization.

2.  **Theoretical Significance:** The paper's main theoretical result (Theorem 3), showing a doubly-exponential reduction in the failure probability, is powerful and provides a solid foundation for the method. The subsequent analysis connecting this result to polylogarithmic runtime (Theorem 4) is a significant theoretical claim that contributes to the fundamental understanding of the parallel complexity of machine learning, directly addressing questions posed in prior work [21].

3.  **Practical Significance and Implementation:** The proposed scheme is "black-box," meaning it can be applied to any existing, compliant RRM algorithm without requiring its modification or reimplementation. This is a major practical advantage. The empirical results are compelling, showing speedups of over 100x against sequential learners and outperforming both an averaging baseline in accuracy and optimized Spark MLlib algorithms in runtime on several large datasets.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and structured. The motivation is clear, Algorithm 1 provides a concise description of the method, and the theoretical results are stated precisely. The connection between the confidence amplification and the runtime reduction is explained logically.

**Weaknesses (Arguments Against Acceptance)**

1.  **Dependence on Dimensionality:** The method's resource requirements are strongly dependent on the Radon number `r`, which for a `d`-dimensional Euclidean space is `d+2`. The number of required processors and data subsets is `r^h`. For problems with even moderately high-dimensional hypothesis spaces (e.g., `d` in the hundreds or thousands), `r` becomes very large, and the `r^h` term can quickly become computationally infeasible. While the paper acknowledges this limitation in the discussion and suggests dimensionality reduction as a remedy, this is a critical constraint on the applicability of the method and the theoretical results, and it could be emphasized more directly in the main analysis.

2.  **Clarity of Complexity Claims:** Theorem 4 claims polylogarithmic runtime on "quasi-polynomially many processing units." The exact form of this quasi-polynomial dependency, `(d+2)^h`, is significant. If the dimensionality `d` is constant, the number of processors is polynomial in the sample size. However, if `d` is, for instance, polylogarithmic in the sample size (a common scenario), the number of processors becomes `(polylog M)^(log M)`, which is a very strong requirement that goes beyond the standard polynomial resources assumed for the class NC. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how the dimensionality `d` relates to the sample complexity and what this implies for the processor count in different scenarios.

3.  **Completeness of Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted on datasets where the number of features `d` is relatively small (e.g., 18 for SUSY, 9 for CASP9), which is the most favorable setting for the Radon Machine due to the small Radon number `r=d+2`. An evaluation on a problem with higher dimensionality would provide a more complete picture of the method's practical performance and limitations.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and empirically promising method for parallelizing a wide class of machine learning algorithms. The core contribution—the use of Radon point aggregation to achieve doubly-exponential confidence amplification—is a significant intellectual achievement with important theoretical implications for the parallel complexity of learning. The practical, black-box nature of the algorithm and the strong empirical results further strengthen the paper.

The main weakness lies in the strong dependence of resource requirements on the dimensionality of the hypothesis space, which may limit its applicability for high-dimensional problems. However, the authors are transparent about this in the discussion. Despite this limitation, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the theoretical and empirical contributions are substantial. The work opens up a new avenue for research in parallel machine learning and provides a valuable step towards resolving fundamental questions in the field. Therefore, the paper is a strong contribution to the NIPS conference.
