This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms, aiming to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic by increasing data size and processing units. The core idea—aggregating hypotheses obtained from random data subsets using Radon points—is intriguing and, if successful, would represent a significant advance, particularly for high-cost error scenarios. The connection to Nick's Class and the theoretical guarantee of polylogarithmic runtime are ambitious and commendable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The use of Radon points for parallel aggregation in machine learning is a genuinely novel idea. While averaging-based parallelization is common, the theoretical benefits of Radon points (doubly-exponential confidence increase) are compelling.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a formal analysis of the parallelization scheme, including sample complexity and runtime analysis, and establishes a theoretical guarantee of polylogarithmic runtime under specific conditions. The proof of Theorem 3, demonstrating doubly exponential confidence increase, is a significant contribution.
*   **Generality:** The scheme is presented as a black-box approach, applicable to a broad class of consistent and efficient learning algorithms without requiring algorithm-specific modifications.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate substantial speedups (80-700x) compared to sequential execution and competitive performance against Spark’s MLlib, validating the practical potential of the Radon machine.
*   **Clear Writing:** Despite the complexity of the topic, the paper is generally well-written and logically structured.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Limitations & Scalability:** The requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units and a dataset size related to the Radon number of the hypothesis space may limit the applicability of the scheme to certain problem settings. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't fully explore potential mitigation strategies beyond mentioning low-rank approximations or block coordinate descent.
*   **Radon Number Dependency:** The performance hinges on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. Determining or bounding this number can be challenging for complex models, and its dependency on the dimensionality of the data is a potential bottleneck.
*   **Communication Cost:** While the paper mentions low communication compared to periodic communication schemes, a more detailed analysis of the communication cost, particularly in distributed settings, would be valuable.
*   **Limited Exploration of Parameter ‘h’:** The effect of the parameter 'h' on performance and scalability is not thoroughly investigated. The choice of ‘h=1’ and ‘h=max’ in the experiments feels somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from a more systematic exploration.
*   **Dataset limitations**: The datasets used in the empirical evaluation are somewhat limited in size and scope.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms.
*   The polylogarithmic runtime guarantee, even under certain assumptions, is a significant theoretical result.
*   The empirical results, while not exhaustive, provide strong evidence of the practical potential of the scheme.
*   The black-box nature of the approach makes it widely applicable.
*   The paper addresses a fundamental question about the limits of parallelization in machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical limitations regarding processing unit requirements and Radon number dependency may restrict the real-world applicability of the scheme.
*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of communication costs and a more systematic exploration of the parameter ‘h’.
*   The empirical evaluation could be expanded to include a wider range of datasets and model types.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a bold and innovative paper that tackles a challenging problem with a novel approach. While there are practical limitations and areas for improvement, the theoretical contributions and promising empirical results outweigh the weaknesses. The paper has the potential to significantly impact the field of parallel machine learning. I believe the novelty and ambition of this work justify acceptance, even if the practical implications are not yet fully realized. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses mentioned above in future work, particularly focusing on strategies to mitigate the processing unit requirements and Radon number dependency.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
