

# Peer Review: "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime while maintaining the same error guarantees as sequential execution. The key insight is using Radon points (from convex geometry) instead of averaging for aggregating hypotheses from parallel learning runs. The authors prove that the error probability decreases doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree, enabling dramatic runtime reductions from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample size. Empirical results demonstrate 80-700x speedups over sequential implementations while matching or exceeding the performance of Spark MLlib.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty**: The use of Radon points for aggregation is a creative application of convex geometry to machine learning parallelization. The proofs (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3) are technically sound and provide a rigorous foundation for the error reduction claim.

2. **Clear Contribution**: The paper successfully addresses a fundamental question in distributed learning: whether consistent learning can be parallelized in polylogarithmic time. The Radon Machine represents a meaningful step toward answering this question.

3. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The experiments convincingly demonstrate substantial speedups (721x on SUSY with 150 processors) with comparable predictive performance to base learners and Spark implementations. The comparison to averaging-at-the-end (which consistently underperforms) is particularly compelling.

4. **Practical Black-Box Approach**: The scheme's ability to treat the base learner as a black box is a significant practical advantage over other parallelization methods requiring algorithm-specific modifications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Dimensionality Limitation**: The Radon number for Euclidean spaces is d+2 (d = dimensionality), which becomes prohibitively large for high-dimensional problems. While the paper mentions dimensionality reduction techniques, it fails to provide a comprehensive analysis of how these affect the practicality of the approach in real-world high-dimensional applications (e.g., text or image data).

2. **Resource Assumption**: The theoretical guarantees assume "quasi-polynomially many processing units" (rh), which is unrealistic for most practical scenarios. The paper acknowledges this limitation but provides insufficient analysis of performance degradation when the number of available processors c < rh. The "deparallelization" approach (additional factor of max{1, rh/c}) could severely impact practical speedup.

3. **Limited Empirical Scope**: The experiments focus on linear models and moderately sized datasets (up to 5M instances). The paper claims general applicability to non-linear models but provides no empirical evidence beyond a single regression example. More comprehensive evaluation on high-dimensional datasets and non-linear models would strengthen the claims.

4. **Theoretical Complexity**: While the paper claims "polylogarithmic runtime," the actual runtime is O(ld^κ M + r^3 ld M). For high-dimensional problems (large r), the r^3 term could dominate, making the approach impractical despite the theoretical asymptotic improvement.

5. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The related work section cites foundational papers but doesn't engage sufficiently with more recent advances in distributed optimization (e.g., communication-efficient distributed learning methods from the last 3-5 years).

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of parallel machine learning, providing a sound framework for achieving polylogarithmic runtime with provable guarantees. The Radon point approach offers a clear theoretical advantage over averaging, and the empirical results are compelling for the scenarios tested.

However, the practical limitations regarding dimensionality and processor requirements significantly constrain the paper's applicability to real-world high-dimensional problems. The authors acknowledge these limitations but don't sufficiently address how to overcome them in practice. The paper would be stronger with a more detailed analysis of the tradeoffs between dimensionality, processor count, and speedup.

The work is technically sound and represents a meaningful step toward answering the fundamental question of whether consistent learning can be parallelized in polylogarithmic time. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical impact is currently limited by the dimensionality constraints. The authors should address these limitations in revisions to strengthen the paper's significance.

## Recommendation

Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of parallel machine learning with clear empirical validation. While the practical limitations are real, they don't invalidate the core theoretical insight. The Radon Machine represents a novel and provably effective approach that advances our understanding of parallelizable learning algorithms. With minor revisions addressing the dimensionality limitations and expanding the empirical scope to include more high-dimensional examples, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

## Key Points for Authors

1. Expand the discussion on practical implementation challenges for high-dimensional problems
2. Provide more thorough analysis of performance degradation when c < rh
3. Include more extensive empirical validation on non-linear models and high-dimensional datasets
4. Engage more deeply with recent work in communication-efficient distributed learning
