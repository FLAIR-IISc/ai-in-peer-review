**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme, the "Radon Machine," for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The method operates by splitting the dataset into a large number of subsets, training a base learner on each subset in parallel, and then hierarchically aggregating the resulting hypotheses. The key innovation lies in the aggregation step, which uses the Radon point of a set of hypotheses, rather than a simple average. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this scheme can reduce the runtime of a learning algorithm from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the effective sample size required for a given confidence level. This speedup comes at the cost of a quasi-polynomial increase in the number of required processors and the total amount of data. The authors connect this result to the fundamental question of whether learning problems are in Nick's Class (NC). Empirically, the Radon Machine is shown to achieve massive speedups over sequential base learners and to be faster than, while maintaining comparable accuracy to, state-of-the-art parallel implementations in Spark MLlib.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Novelty:** The core idea of using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation is, to my knowledge, completely new in the context of parallel machine learning. It provides a principled alternative to the ubiquitous averaging schemes (e.g., [32, 45]) and parameter-server architectures. The application of concepts from convex geometry to boost statistical confidence is elegant and insightful.

2.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's main theoretical result (Theorem 3), demonstrating a doubly-exponential reduction in the failure probability with the height of the aggregation tree, is powerful. This provides the foundation for the claimed polylogarithmic runtime (Theorem 4). This is a much stronger guarantee than those typically available for averaging-based methods, which often show only linear improvements in confidence. The explicit connection to complexity theory and the long-standing open problem of parallel PAC learning [21, 42] elevates the paper's importance beyond a mere algorithmic proposal.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the method's practical utility. The reported speedups of 80x-700x against sequential learners are dramatic. More importantly, the comparison with highly optimized, industry-standard Spark MLlib algorithms shows that the Radon Machine can be significantly faster (15-85% of the runtime) while achieving similar or even better predictive performance. This demonstrates that the theoretical speedups are not just an artifact of the asymptotic analysis but can be realized in practice.

4.  **Practicality and Broad Applicability:** The black-box nature of the Radon Machine is a major practical advantage. It can parallelize any consistent and efficient regularized risk minimizer without requiring modifications to the underlying algorithm. This makes it a widely applicable tool.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **The Curse of Dimensionality:** The most significant weakness of the proposed method is its dependence on the dimensionality of the hypothesis space, `d`. The Radon number `r` for `R^d` is `d+2`. The number of required subsets (and processors for full parallelism) is `r^h = (d+2)^h`. This is exponential in both `d` and `h`. While the authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion, its severity is understated in the main body of the paper. The theoretical claim of polylogarithmic runtime on quasi-polynomially many processors (Theorem 4) crucially relies on the assumption that `r` is polylogarithmic in the sample complexity. This assumption does not hold for many common learning problems where `d` is a fixed, large constant. The experiments, while impressive, are conducted on datasets with low dimensionality (`d=18` for SUSY), which is precisely the regime where the method is most feasible. How would the method fare on a text classification problem with `d=50,000`? The approach would be computationally infeasible. The authors should discuss this limitation more prominently and clarify the conditions under which the "quasi-polynomial" claim holds.

2.  **The Base Learner Requirement (`δ ≤ 1/2r`):** The analysis requires that the base learner, trained on a small data partition, already achieves a failure probability `δ ≤ 1/2r`. For high-dimensional spaces (large `r=d+2`), this condition becomes very stringent, requiring `δ` to be extremely small. This might necessitate larger initial data partitions (`n`), which would in turn reduce the overall level of parallelism `r^h` achievable for a fixed total dataset size `N`. This trade-off deserves more discussion.

3.  **Comparison to Averaging:** The paper shows empirically that Radon points outperform averaging in terms of accuracy. The theoretical justification provided is that the Radon point is robust to a single bad hypothesis, while the average is not. While true, a more detailed theoretical comparison to the bounds for averaging schemes (e.g., [20, 35, 45]) would strengthen the paper. For instance, under what conditions does the `(rδ)^(2^h)` bound of the Radon Machine become superior to the bounds for averaging, which typically depend on variance reduction?

4.  **Clarity of Runtime Analysis:** In Section 3, the runtime is derived as `O(ld^κ(M) + r^3 ld(M))`. The argument for choosing `h` to achieve this seems slightly fast. A more explicit derivation showing how `h ≈ ld(M) - ld(ld(M))` leads to `n ≈ ld(M)` would improve clarity for the reader.

**Overall Evaluation**

This is a very strong paper that presents a highly original and theoretically well-founded approach to parallel machine learning. The central idea of using Radon points for aggregation is novel and powerful, leading to impressive theoretical guarantees and practical performance. The connection to fundamental questions in computational complexity theory makes this a work of significant interest to the NIPS community.

The primary weakness is the method's exponential dependence on the dimensionality of the hypothesis space, which limits its direct applicability to high-dimensional problems and makes the experimental validation somewhat narrow. However, the authors do suggest valid workarounds like dimensionality reduction. Despite this limitation, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the results in the applicable domain are outstanding. This work opens up a new and promising direction for research in parallel learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper offers a significant and novel contribution to both the theory and practice of parallel machine learning.
