## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, dubbed the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea leverages Radon points to aggregate hypotheses generated from parallel executions on data subsets, achieving a theoretical reduction in runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic (with a quasi-polynomial cost in data size and processing units). The authors argue this is significant for applications requiring high confidence and where data is abundant. They support their claims with theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation demonstrating speedups on several datasets, outperforming Spark MLlib in some cases.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear mathematical framework for analyzing the parallelization scheme, including proofs for sample complexity and runtime reduction.  The connection to Radon points is a clever approach to aggregation, offering stronger confidence guarantees than simple averaging. The derivation of Theorem 4 is particularly compelling.
* **Generality:** The scheme is presented as a black-box approach, applicable to a broad class of consistent and efficient learning algorithms without requiring modifications to the base learner. This is a significant advantage.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups over sequential execution and, in some cases, competitive performance compared to established parallel frameworks like Spark MLlib. The comparison against averaging-at-the-end is also insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear structure and logical flow. Definitions and key concepts are well explained.
* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Parallelizing machine learning algorithms efficiently is crucial for handling ever-increasing datasets.  The work tackles a relevant and challenging problem.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Quasi-Polynomial Cost:** While achieving polylogarithmic runtime is a significant theoretical result, the dependence on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units and increased data size is a practical limitation. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed discussion of the tradeoffs is needed. How does the cost grow with problem dimensionality and desired accuracy?
* **Radon Number Dependency:**  The performance of the Radon machine is heavily reliant on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. The paper briefly mentions dealing with high-dimensional data via dimensionality reduction, but a more in-depth exploration of this aspect, including specific recommendations for different data types, would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scalability Demonstration:** The empirical evaluation is largely confined to moderately sized datasets due to limitations of the base learners.  Demonstrating the scalability of the Radon machine on truly massive datasets using more scalable base learners is crucial.
* **Communication Complexity Not Fully Addressed:** While mentioned, the communication complexity isn't quantified in detail. In a distributed environment, this can be a significant bottleneck.
* **Comparison to SOTA:** While comparisons to Spark MLlib are present, the paper doesn’t provide a comprehensive comparison against the state-of-the-art in distributed machine learning, such as parameter servers or other advanced distributed optimization techniques.



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound and the claims are generally well-supported. The theoretical analysis is rigorous. However, the practical limitations regarding processing unit requirements and data size are not fully addressed, slightly impacting the overall quality.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is generally accessible. 
* **Originality:** The application of Radon points to parallelizing machine learning is novel and represents a unique approach. The theoretical contribution is original.
* **Significance:** The potential for reducing runtime complexity is significant, particularly for high-confidence applications. However, the practical applicability is constrained by the cost factors.  The impact depends on the availability of substantial computational resources.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound parallelization scheme.
* Potential for significant speedups in certain scenarios.
* Broad applicability as a black-box method.
* Strong empirical validation on a range of datasets.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Quasi-polynomial cost in processing units and data size limits practical applicability.
* Limited scalability demonstration.
* Insufficient discussion of communication complexity.
* Comparison to SOTA distributed ML frameworks could be more thorough.

**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of parallel machine learning.  While the practical limitations are real, the novelty and potential impact of the Radon machine justify acceptance.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Expand discussion of practical limitations:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of how the cost (processing units, data size) scales with problem parameters.
* **Address scalability:** Include results on larger datasets, even if it requires using more scalable base learners.
* **Quantify communication complexity:** Provide estimates of the communication overhead.
* **Strengthen SOTA comparison:** Include a more comprehensive comparison against recent state-of-the-art distributed machine learning frameworks.
* **Discuss memory footprint:** Add a section addressing memory usage, which can be a significant bottleneck in large-scale learning.
