## NIPS 2017 Review: Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, dubbed the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea leverages Radon points to iteratively aggregate hypotheses learned on random subsets of the data, achieving a significant reduction in runtime – theoretically from polynomial to polylogarithmic – at the cost of increased data size and processing units. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the effectiveness of the scheme, maintaining the same confidence and error bounds as sequential execution, and demonstrate its practical benefits through empirical evaluation on several datasets, showing speedups compared to both sequential learning and other parallel approaches like Spark MLlib.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon existing parallelization techniques like averaging-based methods [32, 45, 46] but distinguishes itself through the use of Radon points, which offer a faster convergence rate – doubly exponential with the aggregation tree height – than simple averaging.  The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of parallel computing, including Nick’s Class (NC) [9] and the challenges of efficiently parallelizing polynomial-time algorithms. They also acknowledge and differentiate themselves from recent approaches based on stochastic gradient descent [34] and distributed optimization [22], highlighting the advantages of their black-box approach. The connection to iterative Radon point algorithms [8, 30] and their application in approximating center points is appropriately acknowledged.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating the effectiveness of the Radon machine in terms of sample complexity and runtime reduction. The derivation of Theorem 4, showing potential for polylogarithmic runtime, is a significant contribution.
* **Black-Box Applicability:** A key strength is the "black-box" nature of the scheme. It can be applied to a wide range of consistent and efficient learning algorithms without requiring specific modifications to their implementations. This broad applicability increases its practical impact.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results demonstrate significant speedups over both sequential algorithms and other parallel frameworks (Spark MLlib) while maintaining comparable performance. The detailed comparison and benchmarking are well-executed.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. Algorithm 1 provides a clear and concise description of the Radon machine. The inclusion of a roadmap outlining the paper’s structure is helpful.
* **Thorough Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach, such as the requirement for a finite Radon number and the increased data/processing unit needs, and discuss potential solutions (e.g., dimensionality reduction, block coordinate descent).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Quasi-Polynomial Processing Unit Requirement:** While the theoretical runtime reduction is compelling, the requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units may be a significant practical barrier. The discussion on handling cases with fewer processors is present, but a more detailed analysis of the trade-off between runtime and processing unit requirements would be beneficial.
* **Radon Number Dependency:**  The performance is directly tied to the Radon number of the hypothesis space.  For certain complex hypothesis spaces, this number could be large, impacting the efficiency of the aggregation steps. The paper acknowledges this but a deeper dive into how this affects real-world scenarios would be helpful.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the empirical evaluation is strong, it primarily focuses on relatively simple datasets and linear models. Expanding the experiments to include more complex models and higher-dimensional data would further strengthen the results.  The multi-class experiments are a good start, but more extensive evaluation is needed.
* **Communication Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions communication complexity, but a more detailed analysis of the communication overhead associated with the parallel aggregation steps would be valuable, particularly for distributed environments.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound parallelization scheme with the potential to significantly reduce runtime for a broad class of machine learning algorithms.
* The black-box nature of the scheme makes it highly practical and applicable to existing learning algorithms.
* The empirical results provide strong evidence of the scheme’s effectiveness and demonstrate substantial speedups over existing approaches.
* The authors acknowledge and discuss the limitations of their work, offering potential avenues for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The quasi-polynomial requirement for processing units could limit the practical applicability of the scheme.
* The dependency on the Radon number of the hypothesis space needs further investigation in real-world scenarios.
* The experimental evaluation could be broadened to include more complex models and datasets.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential to impact the field of parallel machine learning. The theoretical contributions are substantial, and the empirical results are encouraging. While the practical limitations related to processing unit requirements and the Radon number need to be considered, the novelty and potential benefits of the Radon machine outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-off between runtime and the number of processing units required.
2.  Further discuss the implications of the Radon number for different hypothesis spaces and potential strategies for mitigating its impact.
3.  Consider expanding the experimental evaluation to include more complex models and datasets.
4. Elaborate on the communication complexity aspects.
