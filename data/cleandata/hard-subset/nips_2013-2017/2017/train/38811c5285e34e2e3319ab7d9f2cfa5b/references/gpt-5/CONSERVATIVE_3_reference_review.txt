Summary
The paper proposes a black-box parallelization scheme (“Radon machine”) for convex regularized risk minimization. It trains the base learner on many iid data subsets in parallel, then aggregates intermediate models along a tree using Radon points rather than averaging. The key theoretical claim is that the probability of returning an ε-bad hypothesis decreases doubly exponentially with the tree height h (via Proposition 2 and Theorem 3), yielding, under specific assumptions, polylogarithmic runtime in the target sample complexity when using a quasi-polynomial number of processors, assuming the Radon number is suitably bounded. Empirically, on several datasets with relatively low dimensionality, the method achieves large speedups (80–700× with 150 cores) and comparable AUC to Spark MLlib baselines; it also outperforms a simple “averaging-at-the-end” baseline in AUC at similar runtime.

Relation to prior work
The work situates itself between averaging-based parallelization and distributed optimization/SGD methods. The novelty is the use of iterated Radon points as a robust aggregator, claiming stronger confidence amplification than averaging. However, there is limited discussion of alternative robust aggregation schemes (geometric median, median-of-means, or Tverberg-based aggregators) that have been studied in robust statistics and distributed learning and are natural comparators, both theoretically and empirically. The connection to NC and P-completeness is mentioned, but the processor requirements here are quasi-polynomial, not polynomial, so this does not quite meet NC as typically defined.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, black-box scheme that is broadly applicable to convex ERM in Euclidean parameterizations.
- Clean confidence amplification result for Radon aggregation; proofs are concise and accessible.
- Empirical speedups are substantial on low-dimensional datasets; code is made available.
- The discussion is candid about limitations (high d, non-linear models) and proposes practical workarounds (random projections, low-rank approximations).

Weaknesses
- Assumptions for the main complexity claim are strong and limit practical impact: (i) access to rh processors (quasi-polynomial in accuracy/confidence), (ii) small Radon number r = d+2 that must be polylog in sample complexity, and (iii) base learner already achieving δ ≤ 1/(2r) on tiny subsets. In many modern applications d is large and/or grows with N, making r and the aggregation cost r^3 prohibitive.
- Independence assumptions are not handled rigorously. The algorithm “partitions” a finite dataset into rh subsets but then analyzes as if iid draws; without replacement, subsets are not independent. If sampling with replacement is required, this should be stated and used in experiments; otherwise, the guarantees do not strictly apply.
- The theoretical speedup story hinges on total sample usage N = r^h n growing quickly with h; in practice the scheme consumes substantially more data than a single ERM run. The comparison to a single-machine baseline at fixed data budget is not always fair or clearly separated from the “more data” effect.
- Empirical evaluation lacks several critical baselines. In particular, robust aggregators (geometric median-of-means, coordinate-wise median) have relevant confidence properties and communication profiles and should be compared. Stronger distributed optimization baselines (parameter-server SGD/Adam, communication-efficient second-order methods) are also missing.
- Some claims appear overstated. “Polylogarithmic runtime” is obtained only with quasi-polynomial processors and polylog Radon number; Theorem 4 itself states quasi-polynomial processors, which falls short of NC. The practical regimes where d is polylog in sample complexity are narrow.
- Clarity issues: sample complexity form NL0(ε, δ) = (αε + βε ld(1/δ))^k uses parameters αε, βε “depending on ε,” which is unusual/circular; clarify and provide concrete instances (e.g., VC/Rademacher-based constants) in the main text, not just appendix. Notation “iid subsets” from a partition is misleading. Several minor typos and typesetting issues (e.g., ld).

Quality
The technical core (Proposition 2/Theorem 3) is sound and clearly proved, though constant factors are loose (union bound over pairs leading to (r p)^2 instead of a tighter combinatorial factor). The runtime analysis is internally consistent but relies on restrictive assumptions and omits detailed accounting of communication and memory contention. The empirical section is promising but insufficiently thorough for a new parallelization paradigm; more rigorous ablations (vary h, δ, d; fixed total data budget; communication overhead) and stronger baselines are needed.

Clarity
Generally readable but with several notational and expository issues. The algorithm and assumptions should be made precise (sampling with/without replacement, requirement δ ≤ 1/(2r), processor model). The conditions under which Theorem 4 is meaningful should be highlighted up front.

Originality
Using iterated Radon points for hypothesis aggregation is interesting and, to my knowledge, not widely explored in ML. However, the general idea of robust aggregation to amplify confidence is known, and the paper would benefit from deeper engagement with that literature.

Significance
Given the strong assumptions (small d, many processors, abundant data) and limited empirical comparisons, the broader impact is uncertain. The contribution feels more conceptual than ready-to-use for typical large-scale ML workloads, where d is large and communication/stragglers dominate.

Questions for the authors
- Do your guarantees still hold when subsets are formed by partitioning without replacement? If not, can you revise the algorithm to sample with replacement and reflect that in experiments?
- How sensitive are results to h? Please provide a systematic study of runtime/accuracy vs h and the achieved δ on base models.
- Can you compare against geometric median-/median-of-means-based aggregators, both in theory (confidence amplification rate) and practice?
- What are concrete ranges of d where r^3 aggregation cost is negligible relative to base training? Please report aggregation time separately.
- Under a fixed total data budget, how do Radon vs averaging vs single ERM compare?

Pros
- Simple black-box parallelization applicable to many convex learners.
- Clear, novel aggregation analysis with double-exponential confidence improvement.
- Strong practical speedups on small-d problems; code available.

Cons
- Heavy and sometimes unrealistic assumptions (processor count, small Radon number, iid subsets).
- Missing key robust aggregation and distributed optimization baselines.
- Theoretical claims overstate practical relevance (quasi-polynomial processors; d-dependence).
- Clarity/notation issues; independence assumptions not properly handled.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is interesting and the proofs are clean, but the theoretical conditions are restrictive, the empirical validation is not yet compelling against strong baselines, and some assumptions (iid partitions) are not matched by the implementation. With a tighter and more realistic theoretical treatment and a more comprehensive empirical study (including robust aggregators and fixed-budget comparisons), this could become a solid contribution.
