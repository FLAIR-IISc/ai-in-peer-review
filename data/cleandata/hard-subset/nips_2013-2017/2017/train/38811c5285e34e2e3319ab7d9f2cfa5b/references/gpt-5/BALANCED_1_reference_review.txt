Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a black-box parallelization scheme, the Radon machine, for regularized risk minimization in convex, finite-dimensional hypothesis spaces. The method trains a base learner in parallel on rh iid subsets (r = Radon number, typically d+2 in Rd), and then aggregates hypotheses up a tree by repeatedly computing Radon points of sets of size r. The key theoretical claim is that Radon aggregation reduces the probability of producing an ε-bad hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the tree height h: if a base hypothesis is ε-bad with probability δ, then after h aggregation levels the failure probability is at most (rδ)2^h. Building on this “confidence amplification,” the authors argue that to achieve the same (ε, Δ) guarantee as a sequential learner, one can run the base learner on much smaller shards and obtain polylogarithmic runtime in the target sample complexity, at the cost of using quasi-polynomially many processors and more data.

The work is positioned relative to prior averaging-based parallelization (e.g., model averaging, bagging, distributed SGD) and to computational complexity notions such as NC. Compared to averaging, Radon aggregation is argued to be more robust: a single bad hypothesis cannot spoil the Radon point as easily as it can the mean. The paper also connects to prior theoretical treatments of parallel learning (e.g., Long & Servedio), and to generalization bounds via VC/Rademacher complexity.

Quality:
- Theory: Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 establish the confidence amplification property under convexity of the loss/regularizer, finite Radon number, and independence across aggregated hypotheses. The reduction to a union bound yields P(Q(r)>ε) ≤ (rδ)^2 (a somewhat loose but serviceable bound), and iterating produces the doubly-exponential decay. The runtime analysis decomposes training and aggregation costs (TL(n) + hr^3), and chooses h so that shard size n ≈ log M, yielding polylog time in the target sample size M when r is polylogarithmic and rh processors are available. Assumptions are stated, and the proofs (sketched in the main text, with details in appendices) appear sound for the stated setting.
- Practicality: The implementation on Spark shows substantial speedups (80–700× vs. single-node base learners; competitive or faster than Spark MLlib), with predictive performance comparable to strong baselines and better than averaging-at-the-end in AUC on the reported datasets.
- Caveats: The polylog-runtime guarantee requires (i) abundant data, (ii) quasi-polynomially many processors c = rh, and (iii) Radon number bounded by polylog of sample complexity (effectively, dimension d ≤ polylog(M)), which is strong in many modern settings. The sample complexity form NL0(ε,δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k is convenient but nonstandardly parameterized; more careful derivations from VC/Rademacher bounds would strengthen the claims. The aggregation cost r^3 scales poorly with dimension, and experiments are limited to relatively low-dimensional linear models, with nonlinear/high-dimensional cases relegated to future work via random features or projections.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The description of the Radon machine is clear, and the role of the Radon number is explained. Some assumptions could be stated more prominently upfront (e.g., the need for δ ≤ 1/(2r) to start amplification; the dimension requirement underlying Theorem 4). The phrase “divide into rh iid subsets” would benefit from clarification that the subsets are disjoint sets of iid samples (hence independent across groups). Guidance on how to choose h and δ in practice would help.

Originality:
Using Radon points as an aggregation primitive for parallel learners and leveraging their geometric properties to obtain confidence amplification appears novel and interesting. The connection to complexity-theoretic notions of parallel efficiency is also a fresh angle for the ML community.

Significance:
The work provides a simple, black-box, and provably effective parallelization scheme for a broad class of convex learners. The idea that one can trade more data and processors for dramatic reductions in wall-clock time, with rigorous guarantees on confidence, is valuable. However, the strongest theoretical efficiency claim hinges on restrictive assumptions (small effective dimension; many processors), which tempers practical impact in high-dimensional and resource-constrained settings. Empirically, the method is competitive and sometimes substantially faster than MLlib on moderate-dimensional datasets.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Conceptually simple, general, and implementation-agnostic scheme with theoretical guarantees.
- Novel use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation; clear robustness advantage over averaging.
- Doubly-exponential confidence amplification result; clean, modular proofs.
- Empirical evidence of large speedups with comparable accuracy; open-source implementation.
- Interesting bridge between learning theory and parallel complexity theory.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Polylog-time result requires quasi-polynomial processors and d ≤ polylog(M); not NC and limiting in practice.
- Additional sample complexity and communication overhead are not fully quantified against strong baselines; trade-offs could be substantial.
- Aggregation cost r^3 and requirement r=d+2 hinder applicability to high-dimensional or kernelized models; experiments focus on linear, moderate-d settings.
- Baseline coverage is incomplete: lacks comparisons to robust aggregators (geometric median/median-of-means), modern distributed SGD/FedAvg variants, and multi-threaded single-node baselines.
- Empirical claims (e.g., AUC gains over averaging) lack statistical significance reporting; choice of h (≥100 points per shard) may bias results.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Quantify the sample and processor overhead more precisely: for fixed ε,Δ, compare N_R versus N_L across realistic parameter ranges; include communication and memory footprints.
- Evaluate on higher-dimensional problems using random projections or random features; report how r and r^3 aggregation cost affect runtime/accuracy as d grows.
- Add baselines with geometric median or median-of-means aggregation, and modern distributed optimization (e.g., minibatch SGD, FedAvg) with similar communication budgets.
- Provide ablations on h and δ, and guidance for their selection; report sensitivity analyses and confidence intervals for accuracy metrics.
- Clarify assumptions in Theorem 4, and temper NC-related messaging to reflect the quasi-polynomial processor requirement.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
The paper presents a neat and broadly applicable idea with solid theory and promising empirical evidence. While the strongest efficiency claims rest on restrictive assumptions and some experimental comparisons could be strengthened, the contribution is novel and of interest to the NeurIPS community.
