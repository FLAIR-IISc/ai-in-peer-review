

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper proposes a parallelisation scheme called the "Radon machine" for regularised risk minimisation algorithms, claiming to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic while maintaining the same confidence and error bounds. The main technical contribution is a theoretical analysis showing that the Radon point aggregation (instead of averaging) leads to doubly exponential improvement in confidence with each aggregation level.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work:** The paper builds on prior work in parallel learning [32, 45, 46] but claims to provide a theoretically grounded scheme with provable guarantees, unlike averaging-based approaches. It connects to Nick's Class (NC) complexity and the open question of whether consistent learning can be done in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors. However, the paper fails to clearly distinguish its contribution from existing work on parallel learning.

**Major Technical Flaws:**

1. **Critical Notational Inconsistencies:** The paper defines L(D) = argmin(...) (Equation 1), but later uses L as a learning algorithm (e.g., "run L in parallel"). This creates significant confusion. Similarly, Q(f) is defined as a scalar (Equation 2), but the notation suggests it's a function.

2. **Theoretical Inaccuracies:** Theorem 3's bound is misstated as P(Q(rh) > ε) ≤ (rP(Q(F) > ε))²ʰ, when it should be (rP(Q(F) > ε))^(2ʰ). This is a fundamental error that undermines the theoretical contribution.

3. **Misleading Claims:** The paper claims "the Radon machine can reduce the runtime of learning algorithms from polynomial to polylogarithmic" but fails to clarify that this is polylogarithmic in sample complexity, not data size (a crucial distinction in learning theory).

4. **Definition Errors:** Definition 1 incorrectly defines a Radon partition as "a pair A,B ⊂ S such that A ∩ B = ∅" without requiring A ∪ B = S. This is a standard definition error that invalidates the subsequent theoretical analysis.

5. **Empirical Evaluation Deficiencies:** The paper claims "the Radon machine is 721 times faster than its base learning algorithms" but provides no actual runtime numbers for verification. The empirical results for regression (YearPredictionMSD) show a significant performance drop (RMSE 13.64 vs. 12.57) without explanation.

6. **Terminology Issues:** The paper repeatedly uses "quasi-polynomial" (two words) instead of the standard "quasipolynomial" (one word). It also uses "Radon number" without proper definition before its first use.

7. **Formatting Errors:** The paper states "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" with a typo ("st" instead of "the"). It also contains grammatical errors like "divide D into rh iid subsets Di of roughly equal size" (should be "divide D into rh iid subsets Di of roughly equal size").

**Other Concerns:**

- The paper claims the Radon machine "treats the underlying learning algorithm as a black-box" but requires knowledge of the Radon number r, which depends on the hypothesis space.
- The paper states "the Radon machine with h = max" but never defines what h = max means.
- The source code URL (https://bitbucket.org/Michael_Kamp/radonmachine) appears to be a personal repository, not a properly cited reference.
- The paper claims "the Radon machine is one order of magnitude faster than the Spark implementations" without providing actual speed-up factors.

**Strengths:**

- The Radon point aggregation concept has theoretical merit and is better than averaging for robustness to outliers.
- The empirical results show promising speed-ups in some cases (80-700x on 150 processors).
- The paper makes a reasonable attempt to connect to theoretical computer science concepts (Nick's Class).

**Weaknesses:**

- The theoretical contribution is undermined by multiple fundamental errors in the mathematics.
- The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- The paper makes exaggerated claims about runtime reduction without proper context.
- The writing contains numerous grammatical and notational errors that hinder comprehension.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper contains several fundamental theoretical errors that significantly undermine its main contribution. The misstatement of Theorem 3's bound (2ʰ vs. 2h) is particularly egregious, as it misrepresents the claimed doubly exponential improvement. The paper also suffers from numerous notational inconsistencies, grammatical errors, and misleading claims that would confuse readers and reviewers.

While the Radon point concept has merit, the paper fails to properly establish it due to these serious flaws. The empirical results are insufficiently detailed to validate the claims. The paper would need substantial revision to address these issues, particularly the mathematical errors in the main theorems.

**Recommendation: Rejection**

The paper presents a potentially interesting idea but is marred by multiple critical technical errors that prevent it from being taken seriously as a contribution to the field. The errors are not merely minor inconsistencies but undermine the core theoretical claims of the paper. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance due to these fundamental issues.
