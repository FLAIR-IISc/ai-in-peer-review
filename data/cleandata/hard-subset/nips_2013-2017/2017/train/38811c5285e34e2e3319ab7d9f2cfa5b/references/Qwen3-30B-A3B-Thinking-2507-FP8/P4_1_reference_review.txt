

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the Radon Machine, a parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime while maintaining the same error bounds as sequential execution. The key insight is using Radon points (from convex geometry) for aggregating hypotheses from parallel learning runs, which provides doubly-exponential confidence improvement with each aggregation level. The authors prove that with h levels of aggregation, the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis decreases as (rδ)^(2^h), where r is the Radon number and δ is the base algorithm's failure probability.

The paper relates to previous work in parallel learning, particularly the averaging-based approaches [32, 45, 46] that lack provable polylogarithmic runtime guarantees. It connects to Long and Servedio's open question about efficient parallel learning of half-spaces [21], positioning their work as a step toward answering this fundamental question in parallel learning theory. The authors also contrast their approach with stochastic gradient descent methods [34] and empirical risk minimization approaches [6], noting that these typically cannot achieve polylogarithmic runtime.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical contributions are well-motivated and rigorously proven (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3). The proofs correctly leverage Radon's theorem and properties of convex functions, with appropriate mathematical formalism. The authors carefully consider the trade-offs between runtime, data requirements, and confidence guarantees.

2. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide strong evidence of the approach's practical value. The Radon Machine achieves 80-700x speedups over base learners while maintaining comparable predictive performance (AUC) across multiple datasets. The comparison to Spark MLlib implementations and the averaging-at-the-end baseline demonstrates clear advantages.

3. **Practical Utility:** The black-box nature of the parallelization scheme is a significant practical advantage, as it can be applied to a broad range of learning algorithms without modification. The paper also addresses limitations and potential extensions (e.g., for high-dimensional data using random projections).

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the Radon Machine algorithm (Algorithm 1) and its theoretical properties. The connection to Nick's Class [1] provides a meaningful theoretical context for the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Applicability:** The approach assumes a hypothesis space with finite Radon number (r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces), which restricts its applicability to non-Euclidean spaces. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how to handle spaces with infinite Radon numbers or non-convex spaces.

2. **Empirical Limitations:** The experiments are limited to moderately sized datasets due to scalability constraints of base learners. While the authors acknowledge this, the lack of experiments on very large datasets (beyond 5 million instances) makes it difficult to assess the approach's performance at scale.

3. **Communication Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions that communication complexity is low compared to periodic communication schemes, but doesn't provide concrete analysis or comparisons to other parallel approaches. This is an important consideration for distributed systems.

4. **Intuition for Radon Points:** While the paper proves Radon points outperform averaging, it lacks intuitive explanation for why this is the case. A more accessible explanation would strengthen the contribution.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Novel theoretical contribution addressing a fundamental open problem in parallel learning
- Strong empirical validation showing significant speedups with comparable performance
- Practical black-box parallelization scheme applicable to a broad class of algorithms
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proofs of key guarantees

*Con:*
- Limited applicability to Euclidean spaces with finite Radon number
- Experimental evaluation restricted to moderate-sized datasets
- Insufficient discussion of communication complexity
- Limited intuitive explanation for the superiority of Radon points

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning, providing a provably effective parallelization scheme that achieves polylogarithmic runtime for a broad class of algorithms. The theoretical foundation is strong, with clear proofs demonstrating the doubly-exponential confidence improvement. The empirical results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate substantial practical advantages over existing approaches.

The paper is well within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental theoretical question with practical implications for large-scale machine learning. While the scope is somewhat limited to Euclidean spaces with finite Radon number, this is a common starting point for theoretical work in this area, and the authors appropriately discuss extensions for non-Euclidean spaces.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a novel contribution to the field. The limitations identified are well-acknowledged and do not undermine the core contribution. This work represents a meaningful step toward answering the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be performed in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors.

Given the paper's theoretical novelty, strong empirical validation, and practical significance, I recommend acceptance.
