This paper proposes a parallelization scheme, the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms, aiming to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic by increasing data size and processing units. The core idea leverages Radon points for aggregating hypotheses computed on data subsets. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the runtime reduction and empirical validation demonstrating speedups on several datasets.

While the theoretical contribution of achieving polylogarithmic runtime is noteworthy *in principle*, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to significant methodological concerns that, collectively, render the work unsuitable for NIPS. The level of detail, while extensive, often obscures rather than clarifies, leading to a convoluted presentation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty (Potential):** The application of Radon points to parallel learning is a novel idea, though the connection to existing averaging-based approaches isn't sufficiently highlighted.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The attempt to formally analyze the runtime complexity and confidence guarantees is commendable.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate substantial speedups in certain settings, suggesting practical potential.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Overly Complex Presentation:** The paper is remarkably dense and difficult to follow. The constant interplay between theoretical statements and practical considerations creates a disjointed narrative. Numerous sentences are overly long and convoluted.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:**  `N0` is defined as a function of `ε` and `∆`, yet in subsequent equations, it's treated as a value, leading to confusion. The notation for probability measures (P) and distributions is also inconsistent.  The meaning of 'h' fluctuates between representing a parameter and the number of iterations.
*   **Unrealistic Assumptions:** The assumption of abundant data is acknowledged but not adequately addressed in the experimental section. Real-world datasets often have constraints on size. The claim of “black-box” parallelization is misleading; parameters like the Radon number 'r' need careful tuning, making it less of a plug-and-play solution.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons to Spark MLlib are presented, the choice of algorithms isn't fully justified. Why specifically these Spark implementations? A broader comparison with state-of-the-art parallel learning frameworks (e.g., Horovod, Dask) would be more informative.
*   **Communication Complexity Ignored:** The paper neglects a critical aspect of parallel computing: communication overhead.  Aggregating hypotheses, even with Radon points, involves data transfer, which can be a bottleneck, especially with a large number of processors.
*   **VC-Dimension/Rademacher Complexity Derivation:** The provided derivations for α and β are incomplete and lack sufficient explanation. They simply state "details in Appendix C.2" without providing any actual details in that appendix.
*   **Theorem 4 Caveats:** The conditions for achieving polylogarithmic runtime as stated in Theorem 4 are extremely restrictive. The requirement that the Radon number be polylogarithmic in sample complexity is unlikely to hold for many practical hypothesis spaces.
*   **Missing Baseline:** A simple, well-optimized sequential implementation of the base learner is missing. Showing the speedup *relative* to a highly optimized sequential version is essential to demonstrate the effectiveness of the parallelization.
*   **Formatting Issues:** The text occasionally contains awkward phrasing and grammatical errors, impacting readability. Appendices are referenced before their definition.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper’s complexity obscures its contribution.
*   Numerous methodological flaws and inconsistencies.
*   Overly restrictive assumptions limit practical applicability.
*   Lack of a complete and fair comparison to existing parallel learning methods.
*   The derivation of key parameters is missing from the Appendix.
*   Communication costs are ignored.

**For Acceptance:**

*   The core idea of using Radon points for parallel learning is novel.
*   The theoretical analysis attempts to provide rigorous guarantees.
*   The empirical results demonstrate potential speedups.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential novelty of the idea, the numerous flaws and the impracticality of the assumptions outweigh the contributions. The paper requires substantial revisions to address the identified weaknesses. The presentation needs to be significantly streamlined and clarified, the assumptions need to be more realistically justified, and a more comprehensive evaluation against relevant baselines is crucial. As it stands, the paper is not suitable for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend **rejection**.
