This paper introduces the “Radon machine,” a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to run the base learning algorithm on random subsets of the data, then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points. The authors claim that this approach achieves the same confidence and error bounds as sequential execution, but with a runtime reduced from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity, at the cost of increased data size and processing units (quasi-polynomial).

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction of the problem and contributions, followed by a detailed description of the Radon machine and its theoretical analysis. The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups compared to the base learner and competitive performance with Spark’s MLlib. The presentation of the theoretical results is strong, with clear definitions and proofs (detailed in the appendix). The inclusion of a discussion section acknowledging limitations and future work is also commendable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The use of Radon points for aggregating hypotheses in a parallel learning setting appears novel. While averaging-based parallelization is known, the Radon point aggregation offers a faster convergence rate (doubly exponential increase in confidence) and potentially better robustness.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating the effectiveness of the Radon machine in reducing runtime while maintaining confidence guarantees. The proofs are detailed and appear correct.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts, algorithms, and theoretical results. Algorithm 1 is particularly helpful in understanding the parallelization process.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results confirm the theoretical benefits, showing significant speedups in practice and comparable performance to existing methods.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The Radon machine is presented as a "black-box" parallelization scheme, meaning it can be applied to a wide range of learning algorithms without requiring specific modifications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Radon Number Dependence:** The runtime reduction relies on the Radon number of the hypothesis space being relatively small. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but it's a crucial assumption that might not hold for all practical problems, especially those with high-dimensional or complex hypothesis spaces. While the paper suggests workarounds like random projections, their effectiveness isn't fully explored.
*   **Quasi-Polynomial Processing Units:** The theoretical guarantees require a quasi-polynomial number of processing units. While the authors mention de-parallelization, the practical implications of this requirement are not thoroughly discussed. Real-world parallel computing resources have limitations.
*   **Limited Dataset Size in Empirical Evaluation:** The comparison with Spark's MLlib is performed on large datasets, but the comparison with the base learners is done on “moderately sized” datasets. This raises the question of whether the observed speedups would persist as the dataset size increases for the base learners.
*   **Communication Complexity:** Although briefly mentioned, a detailed analysis of the communication complexity of the Radon machine, especially in distributed environments, would be valuable. Communication overhead can be a significant bottleneck in parallel algorithms.
*   **Comparison to Other Parallelization Techniques:** While the paper compares to averaging, a more extensive comparison to other state-of-the-art parallelization techniques for specific learning algorithms (e.g., distributed stochastic gradient descent) would strengthen the evaluation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound parallelization scheme with the potential to significantly reduce the runtime of regularized risk minimization algorithms.
*   The empirical results are promising and support the theoretical claims.
*   The "black-box" nature of the scheme makes it broadly applicable.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and results.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units and a relatively small Radon number may limit the practical applicability of the scheme.
*   The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, particularly with respect to larger datasets and comparisons to other state-of-the-art parallelization techniques.
*   The communication complexity of the scheme is not fully analyzed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and theoretically well-founded contribution. The empirical results are encouraging. While there are some limitations, the potential benefits of the Radon machine are significant. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding the Radon number dependence, the practical implications of the quasi-polynomial processing unit requirement, and the communication complexity. A more thorough empirical evaluation on larger datasets and comparisons to additional parallelization techniques would further strengthen the paper.
