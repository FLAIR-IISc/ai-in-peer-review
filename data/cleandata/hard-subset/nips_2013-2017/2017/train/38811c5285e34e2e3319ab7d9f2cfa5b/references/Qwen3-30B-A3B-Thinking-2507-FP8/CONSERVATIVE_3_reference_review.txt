

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper proposes the "Radon machine" as a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms, claiming to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic time while maintaining the same error guarantees. The approach uses Radon points instead of averaging for aggregating hypotheses from parallel learning processes, with the theoretical claim that error probability decreases doubly exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree.

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by connecting convex geometry (Radon points) to parallel learning. The authors provide a rigorous proof of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3, which establish the error probability reduction claim. However, the theoretical foundation rests on several restrictive assumptions that significantly limit the practical applicability of the approach:

1. The Radon number (r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces) grows linearly with dimensionality, making the approach impractical for high-dimensional problems. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, it only suggests low-rank approximations as a potential solution, without providing evidence that these would work effectively in practice.

2. The analysis assumes a specific form for sample complexity (NL0(ε, δ) = (αε + βε ld 1/δ)^k), which may not hold for many real-world learning algorithms.

3. The claimed "polylogarithmic" runtime depends on the Radon number being small, which contradicts the paper's own statement that "for high-dimensional models, the basic version of the parallelisation scheme cannot directly be applied."

The empirical evaluation shows promising speedups (80-700x) on moderate-sized linear models with 150 processors. However, the evaluation is limited in scope: it only tests linear models, uses a small number of datasets, and doesn't adequately address the high-dimensional case where the Radon number becomes problematic. The comparison with Spark MLlib is superficial, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how the speedup scales with dataset size or dimensionality.

A critical weakness is the lack of thorough validation against established baselines. While the paper compares with "averaging-at-the-end" and Spark MLlib, it doesn't compare with other state-of-the-art parallel learning approaches that specifically address the challenges of distributed learning. The paper also fails to provide a detailed analysis of communication costs, which are crucial for distributed systems.

The paper's claim of solving "a fundamental open problem" in parallel machine learning is overstated. The authors themselves acknowledge that their approach requires the hypothesis space to have a small Radon number, which is not generally true for many real-world machine learning problems. The paper does not provide evidence that their approach can solve the open problem for the general case.

While the paper's black-box nature is a strength (it doesn't require modifying base learning algorithms), the theoretical limitations significantly undermine the practical significance of the contribution. The approach appears to be most applicable to very low-dimensional problems, which are not the typical use cases for modern machine learning.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with proper citation of related work. The theoretical claims are mathematically sound under the specified assumptions, but the assumptions themselves are too restrictive to support the broad claims made in the paper.

**Summary of strengths and weaknesses:**

*Strengths:*
- Clear theoretical foundation for the specific assumptions made
- Well-structured presentation of the Radon machine algorithm
- Demonstrates significant speedups in the limited experimental setting
- Black-box parallelization approach that doesn't require modifying base algorithms

*Weaknesses:*
- Theoretical results depend on restrictive assumptions that limit practical applicability
- Empirical validation is insufficiently comprehensive (limited to low-dimensional linear models)
- Fails to adequately address the high-dimensional case where the Radon number becomes problematic
- Overstates the significance of the contribution by claiming to solve a fundamental open problem
- Lacks thorough comparison with relevant state-of-the-art parallel learning approaches

**Recommendation:** Reject.

The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution but fails to provide sufficient evidence that the approach is practically valuable for the broad class of machine learning problems it claims to address. The theoretical claims are too dependent on specific assumptions that don't hold in typical machine learning scenarios, and the empirical validation is too limited to support the broad claims made. The paper would need to substantially expand its empirical evaluation to high-dimensional problems and provide a more rigorous analysis of the practical limitations before it could be considered for publication.
