- Summary: Proposes the "Radon machine," a parallelization scheme using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses, claiming polylogarithmic runtime reduction for regularized risk minimization at the cost of more data and processors; empirical results show strong speedups and competitive performance.

- Strength — Strong Theoretical Foundation: Provides rigorous analysis with confidence and error bounds and a claimed polylogarithmic runtime supported by proofs.

- Strength — Generality: Presented as a black-box method applicable to many consistent, efficient learning algorithms without algorithm-specific changes.

- Strength — Empirical Validation: Experiments show substantial speedups versus sequential learning and competitive results against Spark MLlib; outperforms simple averaging aggregation.

- Strength — Clear Presentation: Well-written and organized; Algorithm 1 concisely summarizes the method.

- Strength — Fundamental Question: Directly addresses the open problem of efficient parallelization for polynomial-time learning algorithms.

- Weakness — Practical Limitations: Polylogarithmic runtime requires a quasi-polynomial number of processors, raising concerns about real-world feasibility and dependence on manageable Radon number r.

- Weakness — Scalability Concerns: Basic scheme may not scale to high-dimensional models without random projections or low-rank approximations; impact on guarantees and performance is under-discussed.

- Weakness — Communication Complexity: Paper mentions but lacks a thorough analysis of communication costs for transmitting hypotheses in distributed settings.

- Weakness — Limited Empirical Scope: Evaluation uses a small set of datasets and algorithms; broader benchmarking would strengthen claims.

- Weakness — Missing Fault Tolerance Discussion: No treatment of processor failures or data corruption in distributed environments.

- Quality: Technically sound with convincing sample and runtime complexity derivations, but practical implications of processor requirements are concerning.

- Clarity: Generally clear and well-defined; Radon point computation (Appendix C.1) could use more explanation for unfamiliar readers.

- Originality: Novel use of Radon points for parallel learning, improving over averaging-based schemes in confidence bounds.

- Significance: Potentially significant if practical limitations are addressed, offering a theoretical route to polylogarithmic runtime for broad algorithm classes.

- Arguments For Acceptance: Novel, theoretically solid approach; strong empirical speedups; tackles a fundamental problem; high potential impact on ML scalability.

- Arguments Against Acceptance: Dependence on quasi-polynomial processors may limit practicality; scalability to high-dimensional models unclear; empirical evaluation is limited.

- Overall Assessment: Significant and compelling contribution with strong theory and promising experiments, but practical resource and scalability issues need addressing.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

- Revision 1 — Practicality: Expand discussion of deparallelization and effects of limited processor resources on runtime.

- Revision 2 — Communication Complexity: Provide a more detailed analysis of communication costs in distributed settings.

- Revision 3 — Scalability Analysis: Elaborate how random projections or low-rank approximations affect guarantees and practical performance for high-dimensional models.

- Revision 4 — Fault Tolerance: Briefly discuss strategies for handling processor failures and data corruption in distributed deployments.
