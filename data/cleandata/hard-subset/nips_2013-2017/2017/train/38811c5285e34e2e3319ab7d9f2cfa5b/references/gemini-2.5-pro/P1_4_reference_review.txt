### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms, which the authors name the "Radon machine." The core idea is to partition the data, train a base learning algorithm on each subset in parallel to obtain a set of initial hypotheses, and then aggregate these hypotheses hierarchically using a tree structure. The key innovation lies in the aggregation step: instead of averaging, each inner node of the tree computes a "Radon point" of its children's hypotheses.

The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this aggregation method reduces the probability of generating a poor hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree (Theorem 3). This powerful guarantee allows the authors to show that their scheme can reduce the runtime of a consistent and efficient learning algorithm from polynomial to polylogarithmic. This speedup, however, comes at the cost of requiring a quasi-polynomial number of processors and a larger dataset. This result provides a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning, connecting it to complexity theory concepts like Nick's Class (NC) and addressing a long-standing open question about the parallelizability of learning.

On the practical side, the Radon machine is presented as a black-box scheme that can be applied to existing learning algorithms without modification. The empirical evaluation is extensive, comparing the Radon machine against sequential base learners, an averaging-based parallel baseline, and state-of-the-art parallel algorithms from Spark MLlib. The results demonstrate substantial speedups (80-700x) over sequential learners and superior performance in terms of both runtime and accuracy compared to the baselines on several large datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **High Originality and Novelty:** The application of Radon points from convex geometry to aggregate hypotheses in a parallel machine learning context is a highly original and elegant idea. It provides a principled alternative to the common practice of averaging, which lacks similarly strong theoretical guarantees for confidence amplification.

2.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper presents a strong and well-formalized theoretical result. The doubly-exponential reduction in the failure probability (Theorem 3) is a powerful finding. The subsequent claim of achieving polylogarithmic runtime (Theorem 4) is a major theoretical breakthrough that directly addresses a fundamental open problem in learning theory concerning the parallel complexity of learning half-spaces and other concepts. This places the work firmly within the scope of foundational research presented at NIPS.

3.  **Practical Relevance and "Black-Box" Nature:** The proposed method is not just a theoretical curiosity. Its ability to parallelize existing learning algorithms without requiring internal modifications is a huge practical advantage. This "black-box" nature makes it broadly applicable. The paper includes a thorough empirical evaluation that demonstrates significant practical benefits, showing that the Radon machine can be much faster than both sequential and existing parallel implementations (like Spark MLlib) while maintaining or even improving predictive performance.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and structured. The motivation is clear, Algorithm 1 is easy to follow, and the theoretical claims are stated precisely. The authors are also transparent about the limitations and assumptions of their work, dedicating a discussion section to challenges like high-dimensional data and a limited number of processors.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Dimensionality:** The Radon number `r` for a `d`-dimensional Euclidean space is `d+2`. The number of processors (`r^h`) and the required data partitioning thus grow exponentially with the dimension `d` of the hypothesis space. This is a severe limitation for high-dimensional models (e.g., models with many features, or kernel methods where `d` can be very large). While the authors acknowledge this and suggest workarounds like dimensionality reduction, the core method presented is not directly applicable to such important high-dimensional settings, which limits its immediate practical impact in those areas.

2.  **Resource Requirements for Asymptotic Guarantees:** The headline theoretical result of polylogarithmic runtime relies on a quasi-polynomial number of processors. This is a very strong assumption that is unlikely to be met in practice for large problems, making the asymptotic guarantee primarily of theoretical interest. The experiments, while impressive, use a small and fixed number of aggregation levels (`h`), which demonstrates a practical speed-up but does not directly validate the asymptotic polylogarithmic scaling.

3.  **Clarity of Proof Sketch:** The proof of Proposition 2 in Section 7 is quite brief and could be made more precise. The claim is that `P(Q(r) > ε) ≤ (r * P(Q(F) > ε))^2`. The argument states that if `Q(r) > ε`, at least two input points `a, b` must have `Q(a), Q(b) > ε`. The proof concludes by invoking a union bound. A standard union bound over all `(r choose 2)` pairs of points would seem to yield a bound proportional to `r^2 * (P(Q(F) > ε))^2`, which matches the stated bound's order but might differ in the constant factor. A more detailed derivation would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation.

4.  **Assumption on Base Learner:** The theory requires the base learners to achieve a failure probability `δ ≤ 1/2r`. For high-dimensional problems where `r` is large, this requires the base learners (trained on small data subsets) to be highly accurate already. This could restrict the applicability of the theoretical guarantees to scenarios where a reasonably good hypothesis can be learned from a very small dataset.

### Detailed Comments

-   The connection made to Nick's Class and the P-completeness of some problems is excellent context and highlights the significance of the theoretical work.
-   The empirical comparison to averaging is very effective. Demonstrating a clear AUC advantage for the Radon machine (Fig 1b) strongly supports the claim that the proposed aggregation method is superior.
-   The discussion section is a strong point, proactively addressing many potential questions a reviewer might have about high dimensionality, non-linear models, communication costs, and limited processors.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper with a highly novel core idea, significant theoretical contributions that advance a fundamental area of learning theory, and a compelling set of experiments that demonstrate practical value. The weaknesses, primarily related to the curse of dimensionality and the high resource requirements for the asymptotic guarantees, are substantial but are also typical for this kind of foundational work. The authors are upfront about these limitations. The paper is well-written, impactful, and a perfect fit for NIPS. It is likely to inspire follow-up work on both the theoretical and practical fronts.
