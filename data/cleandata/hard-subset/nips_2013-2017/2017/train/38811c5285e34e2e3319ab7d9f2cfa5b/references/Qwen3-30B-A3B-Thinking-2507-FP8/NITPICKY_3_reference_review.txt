

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper proposes the "Radon machine" for parallelizing machine learning algorithms through the use of Radon points instead of averaging for aggregation. The authors claim to achieve polylogarithmic runtime compared to polynomial runtime of sequential algorithms, with a quasi-polynomial increase in data requirements and processing units. The theoretical contributions include Proposition 2 and Theorem 3, which claim a doubly-exponential reduction in error probability with each aggregation level. The empirical evaluation compares the Radon machine against base learning algorithms, averaging-at-the-end baselines, and Spark MLlib implementations.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning: scaling algorithms to handle growing data sizes. The theoretical framework is generally sound, and the empirical results demonstrate significant speed-ups in practice (80-700x) on moderately sized datasets. The idea of using Radon points rather than averaging for aggregation is novel and theoretically justified in the paper.

## Weaknesses

### Critical Mathematical Errors
The paper contains multiple critical mathematical notation errors that undermine the theoretical contributions. The most severe issue appears in Equation (4), where the authors write "∆ = (rδ) 2h" without proper superscripting. This should be "∆ = (rδ)^(2^h)" to represent a doubly-exponential reduction. This error propagates throughout the paper, appearing in Theorem 3 and Equation (6) as "2 h" instead of "2^h". These are not minor typographical errors but fundamentally misrepresent the mathematical claims of the paper.

### Inconsistent Notation and Terminology
The paper uses "ld" to denote logarithm (e.g., "βε ld 1/δ"), which is non-standard in machine learning literature. The standard notation is "log", and this inconsistent use creates confusion. The paper also inconsistently uses "regularised" vs. "regularized" spelling, and "Radon number" vs. "Radon number r" without clear definition.

### Misrepresentation of Runtime Complexity
The paper claims to achieve "polylogarithmic runtime" in Theorem 4, but the actual runtime expression is O(ld^κM + r^3 ldM), which is polylogarithmic in M (the sample size), not in the data size N. The paper incorrectly states "runtime of R compared to TL(M)" without clarifying that M is the sample size required for the sequential algorithm, not the actual data size. This misrepresentation of the complexity claim significantly weakens the theoretical contribution.

### Empirical Evaluation Issues
The empirical results lack sufficient detail to be properly evaluated. The claim that "the Radon machine is between 80 and around 700-times faster" lacks specific context about the hardware, dataset sizes, and base algorithms used. The paper doesn't provide absolute runtimes or clearly state which Spark algorithms were used for comparison. The phrase "around 700-times" is imprecise and unscientific.

### Theoretical Justification Gaps
The proof of Proposition 2 states "the probability of Q(r) > ε is less than or equal to the probability of the pair a, b having Q(a), Q(b) > ε" without proper justification. The authors cite Proposition 6 for this step, but Proposition 6 does not directly support this claim. The connection between the probability of a single Radon point being bad and the probability of two points being bad is not rigorously established.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
The abstract contains a clear typo: "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017), Long Beach, CA, USA." The "st" should not be there. The paper also uses inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "r h" instead of "r^h" in Equation 5).

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses an important problem and contains some interesting theoretical insights, the critical mathematical errors in the core theoretical claims (particularly the misrepresentation of the doubly-exponential reduction in error probability) significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The inconsistent notation and misrepresentation of complexity results further weaken the contribution.

As a perfectionist reviewer who must ensure technical precision in the NIPS proceedings, I cannot recommend acceptance due to these substantial technical flaws. The paper would benefit from a complete revision to correct the mathematical notation errors, clarify the complexity claims, and provide more precise empirical results.

The paper's core idea is promising, but the current technical presentation is insufficiently rigorous for NIPS. I recommend rejection due to these fundamental technical errors that prevent proper evaluation of the theoretical claims.

## Summary of Key Issues
- Critical mathematical errors in key equations (missing superscripts for doubly-exponential reduction)
- Non-standard "ld" notation for logarithm
- Misrepresentation of complexity claims
- Inconsistent terminology and notation
- Insufficient empirical detail
- Theoretical justification gaps in proof of key propositions

These issues collectively prevent me from considering this paper for acceptance in NIPS.
