This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea – leveraging Radon points for aggregation in a tree-like structure – is strikingly clever and offers a compelling theoretical guarantee: reducing runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic, albeit at the cost of increased data and processing units. This is a bold approach targeting a fundamental bottleneck in modern machine learning – scalability – and the potential impact is significant, particularly in high-stakes applications. The connection to Nick’s Class is also a particularly interesting framing of the contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The Radon machine presents a genuinely novel parallelization strategy. While averaging-based parallelization is common, the use of Radon points for aggregation and the resulting doubly-exponential confidence increase is a fresh perspective.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a clear and mathematically sound analysis, demonstrating the polylogarithmic runtime reduction. The theoretical results regarding sample and runtime complexity are well-defined and clearly stated.
*   **Black-box Approach:** The scheme's generality – treating the underlying learning algorithm as a black box – is a major strength. This allows broad applicability without requiring algorithm-specific modifications.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results, while conducted on a limited scale, strongly support the theoretical claims, showcasing substantial speedups compared to both the base learner and Spark’s MLlib.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the Radon machine and its theoretical underpinnings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Quasi-Polynomial Cost:** The dependence on a quasi-polynomial number of processing units is a significant practical limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, the implications aren’t fully explored in terms of hardware requirements or the cost of communication.
*   **Radon Number Dependency:** The performance hinges on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. The paper notes the dependence on this number but doesn’t fully address scenarios where the Radon number is prohibitively large. The discussion of using projections or low-rank approximations is promising, but remains somewhat superficial.
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation, while encouraging, is relatively limited in scope. Testing on a wider range of datasets, particularly high-dimensional datasets, would strengthen the findings.
*   **Communication Complexity:** While briefly mentioned, the communication complexity of the aggregation step deserves more detailed analysis, especially in distributed environments. The statement that it’s low is relative and needs further justification.
*   **Practicality of 'h':** The discussion on choosing 'h' is brief. How sensitive are the results to the choice of 'h'? Is there a practical guideline for choosing 'h' in real-world applications?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is profoundly novel and addresses a critical challenge in machine learning – scalability.
*   The theoretical analysis is robust and provides a compelling justification for the proposed scheme.
*   The empirical results, despite their limitations, validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate significant practical speedups.
*   The paper’s clarity and well-defined mathematical framework make it accessible to a broad audience.
*   Even if practical limitations exist, the paper opens up a new avenue for research in parallel machine learning, and the concept of using Radon points for aggregation is likely to inspire future work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The quasi-polynomial requirement for processing units presents a significant practical barrier.
*   The limited empirical evaluation prevents a thorough assessment of the scheme’s performance on real-world datasets.
*   The paper doesn’t fully address the challenges associated with high-dimensional data or communication complexity in distributed settings.
*   The theoretical results are contingent on the Radon number, and the paper doesn't adequately discuss scenarios where this number is large.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a highly promising paper that introduces a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms. While practical limitations exist, the potential impact of this work is significant, and the core idea is likely to inspire further research. I believe the originality and boldness of the approach outweigh the current limitations. I therefore **recommend acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above in a future revision or as directions for future work. Specifically, exploring the communication complexity in more detail, providing a more comprehensive empirical evaluation with high-dimensional data, and discussing strategies for mitigating the dependence on the Radon number would significantly strengthen the paper.
