

# Peer Review: "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to divide the dataset into multiple subsets, apply the base learning algorithm in parallel to each subset to obtain weak hypotheses, and then iteratively aggregate these hypotheses using Radon points (a concept from convex geometry) to produce a strong hypothesis. The authors prove that this approach reduces runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity while maintaining the same error bounds, with the error probability decreasing doubly exponentially with the aggregation tree height. Empirical results show 80-700x speedups on 150 processors compared to sequential base learners, with comparable predictive performance to Spark MLlib algorithms.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by addressing an open problem in parallel machine learning: "Is consistent regularized risk minimization possible in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors?" The proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are rigorous, and the doubly exponential error reduction is a compelling theoretical result.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The black-box nature of the parallelization scheme is a major practical advantage. The Radon Machine treats the base learning algorithm as a black box, requiring no specialized implementation, which is a substantial benefit for practitioners.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against both a natural baseline (averaging-at-the-end) and state-of-the-art Spark MLlib algorithms. The results on real datasets demonstrate substantial speedups while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

4. **Clear Connection to Complexity Theory**: The paper effectively connects the parallelization problem to Nick's Class (NC), providing a theoretical framework for understanding the limits of parallel machine learning.

## Weaknesses

1. **Dimensionality Assumption**: The paper's theoretical analysis assumes a low-dimensional hypothesis space with Radon number r = d+2. For many real-world machine learning problems (text, images, high-dimensional embeddings), d is large, making r large and requiring an impractically large number of processors. The discussion in Section 5 about handling high-dimensional data via random projections or low-rank approximations is insufficiently developed.

2. **Communication Complexity**: The paper neglects to analyze communication complexity, which is critical for distributed systems. The claim that "the overall amount of communication is low compared to periodically communicating schemes" is not substantiated with any analysis or measurements. In practice, communication costs often dominate parallel performance.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The empirical evaluation mentions using a Spark cluster but provides minimal details about implementation specifics. The Bitbucket repository link is provided, but the paper doesn't discuss how the Radon Machine was integrated into Spark or any specific challenges encountered.

4. **Non-Linear Models**: While Section 5 mentions handling non-linear models via low-rank approximations, the paper doesn't provide any concrete examples or analysis of how the Radon Machine would perform with non-linear models in practice, which limits the scope of the contribution.

5. **Comparison to Related Work**: The paper acknowledges related work but doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing parallelization methods, particularly those based on stochastic gradient descent or distributed optimization.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically interesting and practically promising approach to parallel machine learning. The Radon Machine's doubly exponential error reduction is a significant theoretical contribution, and the empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups. However, the practical applicability of the approach is limited by the dimensionality assumption, which is likely to be a bottleneck for many real-world applications.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More detailed analysis of communication complexity
2. Stronger justification for the dimensionality assumption
3. More concrete examples of handling high-dimensional and non-linear models
4. Additional implementation details in the empirical section

The theoretical contribution is strong, but the practical significance is somewhat limited by the dimensionality constraint. The paper makes a valuable step toward answering the open question about efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms, but the scope of applicability needs to be more clearly defined.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The paper's theoretical contribution is strong and addresses an important open problem in parallel machine learning. However, the reviewers should require the authors to address the limitations regarding dimensionality and communication complexity, and to provide more concrete details about practical implementation for high-dimensional problems. The paper has significant potential but needs to better establish its practical relevance beyond low-dimensional Euclidean spaces.

The authors should consider expanding Section 5 to more thoroughly address high-dimensional and non-linear settings, and provide more detailed analysis of communication costs. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS.
