This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, termed the Radon machine, for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to leverage Radon points for aggregation, achieving a runtime reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic by increasing data size and processing units (quasi-polynomially). The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the scheme’s effectiveness, maintaining confidence and error bounds, and support their claims with empirical evaluation demonstrating significant speedups over sequential learning and comparison with Spark’s MLlib.

This work builds upon established concepts in parallel computing and machine learning. Aggregation-based parallelization approaches ([32, 45, 46]) are acknowledged, but the Radon machine distinguishes itself through its doubling-exponential confidence increase during aggregation, compared to averaging. The theoretical foundation draws from Radon’s theorem [30] and concepts from convex optimization, aligning with previous work on PAC learning [5, 39] and parallel learning for specific classes like half-spaces [21]. The application to regularized risk minimization ties into the broader field of statistical learning theory [2, 3, 41].

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Radon points for parallelizing learning algorithms is a novel approach. While Radon points themselves are not new [30], their use in this context, providing a theoretical guarantee of polylogarithmic runtime, is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical analysis, demonstrating the effectiveness of the parallelization scheme with well-defined guarantees on runtime and confidence levels. The proof of Theorem 3 is particularly commendable.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The empirical evaluation convincingly demonstrates the potential of the Radon machine in practical settings, achieving substantial speedups compared to both sequential learning and existing parallel implementations (Spark MLlib). The comparison against averaging-at-the-end is also insightful.
*   **Black-Box Approach:** The scheme is designed as a “black box,” applicable to various learning algorithms without requiring algorithm-specific modifications, enhancing its versatility.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the Radon machine algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Radon Number Dependency:** The theoretical guarantees and practical performance are heavily reliant on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. The paper acknowledges limitations for high-dimensional models where the data size might be insufficient, but further discussion on mitigation strategies (e.g., random projections [15], low-rank approximations [2, 28]) could be more detailed.  The dependency on the Radon number being polylogarithmic in sample complexity is a strong requirement, and it isn't fully clear how frequently this holds in practice.
*   **Scalability Concerns (Beyond Experiments):** While the experiments demonstrate significant speedups, the dataset sizes used are moderate. The scalability to truly massive datasets, especially with highly complex models, requires further investigation. The impact of communication costs in a distributed setting is touched upon but could benefit from more in-depth analysis.
*   **Limited Exploration of Parameter *h*:** The choice of the parameter *h* (number of iterations) significantly impacts performance.  The paper justifies its selection for the experiments, but a more systematic exploration of the optimal *h* for different datasets and algorithm configurations could be beneficial.  The scaling of runtime with *h* should be further clarified.
*   **Connection to Nick's Class:** While mentioning Nick's Class [9] is relevant, the discussion feels somewhat cursory. A more nuanced explanation of its limitations and the broader context of efficient parallel computation would strengthen the paper. The critique of using only Nick’s Class could be expanded with citations.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper could benefit from citing more recent work on distributed optimization techniques beyond those explicitly mentioned, particularly those focused on communication efficiency (e.g., [34, 48]).



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant practical benefits.
*   The black-box nature of the scheme enhances its broad applicability.
*   The work addresses a fundamental question about the limits of parallelization in machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The dependence on the Radon number of the hypothesis space may limit the applicability of the scheme to certain problem domains.
*   The scalability to truly massive datasets and complex models requires further investigation.
*   The discussion of the theoretical foundations could be more nuanced and connect more deeply with existing work in parallel computation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the rigorous theoretical analysis and compelling empirical results, makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While the limitations regarding the Radon number and scalability are valid concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

The authors should address the following points in a revised version:

1.  **Expand discussion on the Radon number dependency:** Provide more detailed mitigation strategies for cases where the data size is smaller than the Radon number.  Clarify how often the polylogarithmic assumption on the Radon number holds in practice.
2.  **Address scalability concerns:** Discuss potential communication bottlenecks and strategies for optimizing communication in distributed settings. Consider simulations or experiments with larger datasets.
3.  **Investigate parameter *h*:** Perform a more systematic analysis of the optimal *h* for different datasets and algorithms.
4.  **Strengthen connection to Nick’s Class:** Provide a more nuanced discussion of Nick’s Class and its limitations.
5.  **Expand related work:** Include more recent and relevant work on distributed optimization and communication-efficient algorithms.
