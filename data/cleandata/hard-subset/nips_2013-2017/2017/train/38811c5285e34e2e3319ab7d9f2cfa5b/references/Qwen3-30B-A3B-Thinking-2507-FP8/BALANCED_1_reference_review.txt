

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The authors propose to divide the dataset into random subsets, apply the base learning algorithm in parallel to each subset, and then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points (a geometric concept from convex geometry). The key theoretical contribution is proving that the Radon machine can achieve the same (ε,Δ)-guarantees as the sequential algorithm in polylogarithmic time, with a quasi-polynomial increase in data size and number of processing units. The paper connects to Nick's Class (NC) in parallel computing theory and addresses an open question posed by Long and Servedio regarding efficient parallel learning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with Proposition 2 and Theorem 3, demonstrating that the Radon machine achieves doubly-exponential improvement in confidence bounds. This is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of parallel machine learning.

2. **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing the Radon machine to both base learning algorithms and Spark MLlib implementations across multiple datasets and tasks (classification, regression, multi-class). The results show substantial speedups (80-700x) while maintaining comparable predictive performance.

3. **Practical Implementation:** The paper provides a clear description of the Radon machine (Algorithm 1) and demonstrates its applicability as a "black-box" parallelization scheme that doesn't require modifications to the base learning algorithm.

4. **Connection to Open Questions:** The paper directly addresses the open question about whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be achieved in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors, which was called a "fundamental open problem" by Long and Servedio.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Applicability:** The theoretical guarantees require the hypothesis space to have a finite Radon number, which is only guaranteed for Euclidean spaces (with Radon number d+2). The paper acknowledges this limitation in Section 5 but doesn't adequately address how to handle the most common machine learning models (e.g., kernel methods, neural networks) where the hypothesis space is not Euclidean.

2. **Practical Constraints:** The proposed scheme requires a quasi-polynomial number of processors (rh), which may not be feasible for many practical applications. The paper mentions "deparallelization" for cases with fewer processors, but this would significantly reduce the theoretical benefits.

3. **Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is limited to moderately sized datasets (the largest has 5 million instances) and linear models. The paper claims the approach can handle non-linear models via low-rank approximations, but this is not validated in the experiments.

4. **Communication Overhead:** The paper briefly mentions communication complexity as "low," but doesn't provide concrete analysis of communication costs, which can be a significant bottleneck in distributed systems.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. **Expand the Empirical Evaluation:** Include experiments on larger datasets and more complex models (e.g., kernel SVMs, deep neural networks) to better demonstrate the practical scalability of the approach.

2. **Address Non-Euclidean Hypothesis Spaces:** Provide a more rigorous analysis of how the Radon machine can be applied to non-Euclidean spaces, including concrete examples of how low-rank approximations or other techniques can be integrated into the parallelization scheme.

3. **Quantify Communication Costs:** Include a detailed analysis of communication complexity, including how it scales with the number of processors and dataset size.

4. **Discuss Practical Implementation Challenges:** Address potential practical issues like load balancing, fault tolerance, and how to handle datasets that aren't a perfect multiple of the Radon number.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of parallel machine learning with a novel and provably effective parallelization scheme. The Radon machine represents a significant step toward answering the open question about whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be parallelized efficiently. However, the practical applicability of the approach is currently limited to specific types of hypothesis spaces (Euclidean spaces with finite Radon number), which excludes many important machine learning models.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations in applicability to non-Euclidean hypothesis spaces and providing more concrete evidence of scalability to larger datasets and more complex models. The current empirical evaluation is promising but limited in scope.

Given the strong theoretical contribution and promising empirical results, I believe this paper has significant potential. With the suggested improvements to address the applicability limitations, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the paper is technically sound and makes a meaningful theoretical contribution, but the limited scope of the practical applicability somewhat diminishes its significance for the broader machine learning community.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the applicability concerns and expand the empirical evaluation. The paper is well-written, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are compelling, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
