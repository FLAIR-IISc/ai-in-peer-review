This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, dubbed the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to leverage Radon points for aggregating hypotheses obtained from parallel runs on data subsets, aiming to achieve polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial increase in data size and processing units. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the consistency and efficiency of the scheme, alongside empirical validation demonstrating significant speed-ups compared to baseline algorithms and Spark’s MLlib.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents its contributions. It builds upon existing work in parallel computation and machine learning, specifically referencing averaging-based parallelization schemes and the theoretical limitations of parallelizing polynomial-time algorithms. The connection to Radon’s theorem and its application to hypothesis aggregation is innovative and provides a strong theoretical foundation. The inclusion of both theoretical analysis and empirical results strengthens the work’s credibility. The authors correctly identify the trade-off between runtime, data size, and the number of processing units.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The use of Radon points for hypothesis aggregation in a parallel learning context appears novel.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a formal analysis of the parallelization scheme, establishing consistency and efficiency guarantees. The proof of Theorem 3, linking the probability of bad hypotheses to the Radon number and iteration count, is particularly noteworthy.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm is clearly presented (Algorithm 1), and the theoretical concepts are explained in a reasonably accessible manner.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate significant speed-ups compared to both the base learners and existing parallel learning frameworks (Spark MLlib), supporting the practical viability of the proposed scheme.
*   **Black-box approach:** The algorithm treats the underlying learning algorithm as a black box which simplifies its use.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Limitations:** The requirement for a quasi-polynomial number of processing units may limit the scalability of the scheme in practice, particularly for high-dimensional data or complex models. While acknowledged, the discussion of handling scenarios with fewer processors (rh/c factor) could be more detailed.
*   **Radon Number Dependence:** The performance relies on the Radon number of the hypothesis space. While Euclidean spaces have a well-defined Radon number, its extension to more complex hypothesis spaces or non-Euclidean settings isn't thoroughly addressed beyond mentioning low-dimensional embeddings.
*   **Communication Complexity:** The communication complexity is briefly mentioned but not quantified or analyzed in detail. In a distributed setting, communication costs can be a significant bottleneck, and a more thorough analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Dataset Specificity:** The empirical results, while compelling, are limited to specific datasets. Further evaluation on a wider range of datasets, especially those with different characteristics (e.g., high dimensionality, different data distributions), would enhance the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Comparison to state-of-the-art:** The comparison to spark's MLlib is not fully comprehensive. It would have been interesting to see results against more recent and/or tailored parallel algorithms.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms with strong theoretical foundations.
*   The empirical results provide strong evidence of the scheme’s practical effectiveness and potential for significant speed-ups.
*   The paper addresses an important open question in the field of parallel learning and contributes to a deeper understanding of the trade-offs involved.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The practical limitations related to the number of processing units and Radon number dependence could restrict the applicability of the scheme in certain scenarios.
*   The lack of detailed analysis of communication complexity is a concern, particularly for distributed deployments.
*   The scope of empirical evaluation could be expanded to demonstrate broader generalizability.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. While there are some practical limitations and areas for further investigation, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the compelling empirical results make it a worthy candidate for acceptance at NIPS. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
