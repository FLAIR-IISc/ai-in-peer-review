Summary
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization over convex, finite-dimensional hypothesis spaces. The algorithm trains a base learner on multiple iid data chunks in parallel and aggregates the resulting hypotheses up a tree using Radon points instead of averaging. The key theoretical claim is that, if the base learner achieves an ε-regret with failure probability δ, then one level of Radon aggregation reduces the failure probability to roughly (r δ)² (r is the Radon number, r=d+2 in Rd), and h levels reduce it to (r δ)^(2^h). Combining this with standard sample-complexity assumptions, the authors argue one can achieve the same (ε, Δ)-guarantee as the base learner but in polylogarithmic time in the target sample complexity, at the expense of quasi-polynomially many processors (∼ r^h) and somewhat larger total data. Empirically, they report 80–700× speedups on 150 processors with comparable AUC to strong Spark baselines and clear improvements over simple averaging-at-the-end.

Relation to prior work
The paper is framed relative to: (i) model averaging and distributed ERM/SGD (where confidence or optimization error typically decays only linearly with the number of rounds and/or does not yield polylogarithmic time), and (ii) complexity-theoretic notions of NC-parallelizability. Using Radon points for robust aggregation appears novel in ML parallelization, and the theoretical confidence amplification contrasts with the known limitations of averaging (which the authors cite). The connection to centerpoint/Tukey depth literature and iterative Radon aggregation is appropriate.

Quality
- Theory: Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are clear and (at least at a high level) correct: a Radon point cannot have higher value under a quasiconvex objective than at least two inputs, yielding a bound P(bad) ≤ O(r^2)·δ² per aggregation level, and thus double-exponential decay across levels. The union-bound-based constants are loose but acceptable. The runtime analysis is transparent: TR,h = TL(n) + h r^3 with n the per-chunk sample size, and choosing h so that n ≈ log M yields polylogarithmic time in the target sample size M when using quasi-polynomial processors. The assumptions are explicit: convex parametrization in Rd, finite Radon number, convex (or quasi-convex) regret functional, base δ ≤ 1/(2r), sample complexity form (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k.
- Limitations: The polylogarithmic-time claim requires quasi-polynomial processors and assumes r = d+2 is polylogarithmic in the target sample complexity; for high-dimensional models, r^3 aggregation and r^h processors become prohibitive. The need for δ ≤ 1/(2r) at the leaves can be strong in practice unless h is small or d is small. The analysis abstracts away communication/scheduling overheads and assumes fully parallel leaf training; the discussion acknowledges de-parallelization with a linear slow-down factor.
- Experiments: The empirical section shows substantial wall-clock speedups and comparable or better AUC than Spark baselines and consistent improvements over averaging. Code availability is a plus. However, the experiments do not validate the high-confidence regime central to the theory (no calibration of δ at leaves nor Δ at the root), and h is small (often h=1 or 3), which is realistic but means the asymptotic double-exponential effect is only modestly exercised. Some details are deferred to appendices (e.g., dataset splits, hyperparameter selection), and statistical significance of AUC differences is not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The algorithm and assumptions are clearly stated; the intuition for Radon aggregation is accessible. A few places could be tightened:
- Be explicit earlier that Q needs to be quasi-convex and explain when regret is quasi-convex in common models.
- Clarify the sample-complexity assumption and how αε, βε are instantiated (the appendix is referenced).
- State more clearly the gap to NC: the scheme uses quasi-polynomial processors; NC requires polynomial.
- Provide more implementation details of Radon point computation and numerical stability in high d.

Originality
Using Radon points for robust hypothesis aggregation in distributed learning—and proving a doubly-exponential confidence amplification—appears novel. The black-box nature for convex ERM contrasts with algorithm-specific distributed solvers. The complexity-theoretic framing is also a fresh angle.

Significance
The work is interesting both theoretically and practically:
- Theoretically, it identifies a principled, robust aggregator with provable confidence amplification and articulates precise conditions under which polylogarithmic time is achievable (albeit with many processors).
- Practically, the scheme is simple to deploy with existing learners and demonstrates large speedups with comparable accuracy on real datasets.
The main constraints (small-to-moderate d, quasi-polynomial processors, requirement on δ) limit universal applicability, but the approach could influence robust aggregation methods and inspire extensions to broader settings.

Pros
- Clear, novel theoretical result on confidence amplification via Radon aggregation.
- Black-box, simple parallelization applicable to many convex learners.
- Strong empirical speedups with competitive predictive performance.
- Good discussion of limitations and connections to NC.

Cons
- Polylogarithmic-time guarantee needs quasi-polynomial processors and small r; not NC in the strict sense.
- Theoretical assumptions (δ ≤ 1/(2r), quasi-convex regret) are not validated empirically.
- Limited exploration of high-dimensional or non-linear settings; reliance on sketching/projections is mentioned but not evaluated.
- Some experimental details and statistical analyses are sparse.

Questions for authors
- For common losses (logistic, hinge, squared), under what conditions is the regret quasi-convex in parameters? Please clarify.
- How sensitive is performance to numerical issues in computing Radon points when d is in the hundreds or thousands?
- Can you report leaf-level δ estimates and resulting Δ to empirically illustrate the confidence amplification?
- How does the method perform with c < r^h processors in practice (de-parallelized setting), including communication overheads?

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper advances a novel, well-justified idea with both theoretical and practical value, is clearly presented, and will likely spur follow-up work on robust aggregation and parallel learning. The limitations are acknowledged and reasonable for a first step; strengthening empirical validation of the confidence claims and exploring higher-dimensional regimes would further enhance the paper.
