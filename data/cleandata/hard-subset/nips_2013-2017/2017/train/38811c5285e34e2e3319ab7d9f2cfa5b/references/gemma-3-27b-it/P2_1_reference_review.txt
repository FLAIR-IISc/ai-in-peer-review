## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially impactful approach to parallelizing regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea of using Radon points for aggregation offers a compelling path toward polylogarithmic runtime, albeit with increased data and processing unit requirements. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the empirical evaluation, while positive, feels somewhat limited in scope. I believe the paper is a solid contribution to NIPS, leaning towards acceptance, but with requests for clarification and expansion in the experimental section.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper tackles the challenge of efficiently parallelizing machine learning algorithms, particularly in the context of growing datasets. It proposes the "Radon Machine," a parallelization scheme built upon applying the base learning algorithm to random subsets of the data and then aggregating the resulting hypotheses using Radon points.  Radon points, by construction, provide a faster confidence boost than simple averaging. The authors prove that this scheme can achieve polylogarithmic runtime (in terms of the sample complexity of the base learner) using a quasi-polynomial number of processing units, at the cost of increased data size. The work connects to prior research in parallel computing, averaging-based parallelization, and geometric concepts like Radon points.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical analysis is rigorous and clearly presented.  The proof of the polylogarithmic runtime reduction (Theorem 4) is a significant result. The connection to the Radon number and its implications for the scalability of the scheme are well articulated.
* **Novelty:**  The application of Radon points to parallel machine learning is a novel idea. While averaging-based schemes exist, the use of Radon points for accelerated confidence boosting provides a distinct advantage, theoretically.
* **Black-Box Applicability:** A key strength is the claim that the Radon Machine can be applied as a “black box” to existing learning algorithms, minimizing the need for algorithm-specific modifications. This is highly desirable for practical adoption.
* **Positive Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate significant speedups compared to the base learners and comparable performance to Spark MLlib, providing initial evidence of the practical benefit.
* **Clear Writing (for the most part):** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Scope:** The empirical evaluation feels somewhat limited.  The datasets used are not extensively described and seem focused on demonstrating feasibility rather than comprehensive performance across a wider range of problem types and data characteristics (e.g., higher dimensionality, different data distributions). The experiments primarily focus on comparing against the *sequential* base learner. While this establishes the speedup, a deeper comparison with more sophisticated parallel learning approaches, beyond Spark MLlib, would be valuable.
* **Practical Considerations of the Radon Number:** The Radon number 'r' depends on the dimensionality of the hypothesis space. While the authors mention low-dimensional embeddings, a more detailed discussion of how to handle high-dimensional data *in practice* (beyond simply mentioning embeddings) is needed. What happens when the Radon number becomes very large?  How does this impact the computational cost of finding Radon points?
* **Communication Complexity:** Although mentioned briefly, the communication complexity of the scheme could be a significant bottleneck in distributed environments. A more detailed discussion of this aspect, perhaps with an analysis of the communication cost as a function of the number of processors and data size, is warranted.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the parameter 'h' (number of iterations).  More insight into how to choose 'h' effectively for different problems and datasets would be beneficial.
* **Clarification Needed:** The statement that the scheme can be applied "without writing dedicated code" feels overly optimistic. Implementing the Radon point calculation itself likely requires some coding effort, even if it's a library function. This should be clarified.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is strong.  The work feels complete within its defined scope, but expanding the experimental section would increase its quality.
* **Clarity:** The paper is mostly clear, but some sections (e.g., regarding the practical implications of the Radon number) could benefit from further elaboration.
* **Originality:** The application of Radon points to parallel machine learning is a novel idea. The work builds on existing concepts (parallelization, averaging, Radon points) but combines them in a unique and theoretically grounded way.
* **Significance:** The potential to reduce runtime to polylogarithmic complexity is significant.  If the practical limitations (data size, processing units, communication cost) can be addressed, this work could have a substantial impact on the scalability of machine learning algorithms.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  Strong theoretical guarantees of polylogarithmic runtime.
*  Novel application of Radon points.
*  Clear potential for significant speedups.
*  Black-box applicability makes the scheme widely adaptable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Limited scope of the empirical evaluation.
*  Lack of detailed discussion regarding the practical challenges of high-dimensional data and communication costs.
*  Some claims about "no dedicated code" are potentially misleading.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by:

1.  **Expanding the empirical evaluation:** Include experiments with higher-dimensional datasets, different data distributions, and a more thorough comparison to a wider range of parallel learning algorithms.
2.  **Providing a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the Radon number:** Explain how to handle high-dimensional data and the computational cost of finding Radon points in such cases.
3.  **Elaborating on the communication complexity:**  Provide an analysis of the communication cost as a function of key parameters.
4.  **Clarifying the claim about "no dedicated code"**: Be more precise about the coding effort required to implement the Radon point calculation.
5.  **Providing guidance on choosing the parameter 'h'.**



I believe that addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field.
