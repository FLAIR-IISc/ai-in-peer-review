

**Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"**

This paper introduces the Radon Machine, a parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime in the sample complexity at the cost of using a quasi-polynomial number of processing units. The key innovation is using Radon points (from convex geometry) for aggregating hypotheses from parallel runs on data subsets, rather than averaging. The authors prove that this approach leads to doubly exponential improvement in confidence bounds with each aggregation level, providing theoretical guarantees that the Radon Machine maintains the same error bounds as sequential execution while significantly reducing runtime. The work relates to previous theoretical treatments of parallel learning in the context of Nick's Class [1, 9, 21] and addresses an open question posed by Long and Servedio [21] about efficient parallel learning of half-spaces.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation with clear proofs of the error bounds (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3) and runtime complexity (Theorem 4). The doubly exponential improvement in confidence bounds with each aggregation level represents a significant theoretical insight that advances the understanding of parallel machine learning.

2. **Empirical validation:** The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating substantial speedups (80-700x) on moderately sized datasets while maintaining comparable predictive performance to sequential base algorithms and Spark's MLlib implementations. The comparison with Spark's algorithms is relevant and shows the Radon Machine's practical advantages.

3. **Black-box applicability:** The scheme treats the underlying learning algorithm as a black box, making it broadly applicable to many machine learning algorithms without requiring modifications to the base algorithm. This is a significant practical advantage over approaches that require algorithm-specific parallelization.

4. **Clarity and organization:** The paper is well-structured, clearly explains the Radon Machine, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The theoretical results are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **High-dimensional limitations:** The paper acknowledges that the Radon number r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces (where d is the dimension), which becomes large for high-dimensional problems (e.g., text data). The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the Radon number scales with dimensionality or how this impacts the practical applicability of the scheme for real-world high-dimensional problems.

2. **Limited empirical scope:** The empirical evaluation is restricted to linear models and moderately sized datasets. While the paper mentions applicability to non-linear models via low-rank approximations, this is not demonstrated empirically. The evaluation doesn't include more challenging high-dimensional datasets or non-linear models.

3. **Processor requirements:** The theoretical results require a quasi-polynomial number of processors (rh), but the paper doesn't clearly quantify how the number of processors scales with problem size or provide a detailed discussion of the practical limitations of this requirement for large-scale applications.

4. **Communication complexity:** While the paper briefly mentions that communication is low compared to periodically communicating schemes, it lacks a detailed analysis of communication costs, which is crucial for distributed systems.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the question of efficient parallel machine learning. The Radon Machine provides a novel and provably effective parallelization scheme that outperforms existing methods in terms of speedup while maintaining predictive performance. The theoretical results are rigorous and well-supported, and the empirical evaluation is convincing for the datasets and algorithms tested.

The primary limitation is the practical applicability for high-dimensional problems due to the growing Radon number. However, this limitation is acknowledged by the authors, and the paper provides reasonable suggestions for addressing it (low-rank approximations, random projections). The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial, and it represents a valuable step toward answering an important open question in parallel machine learning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Strong theoretical contribution addressing an important open question in parallel machine learning.
- Novel approach with rigorous guarantees and convincing empirical results.
- Broad applicability as a black-box parallelization scheme.
- Clear presentation of both theoretical and practical aspects.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Practical limitations due to high processor requirements for large-scale problems.
- Limited empirical evaluation on high-dimensional data and non-linear models.

**Recommendation:**
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of parallel machine learning. While the practical applicability has limitations for high-dimensional problems, these are acknowledged by the authors and are inherent to the theoretical approach. The Radon Machine provides a novel and provably effective parallelization scheme that outperforms existing methods in speedup while maintaining predictive performance. I recommend acceptance.
