This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, dubbed the "Radon machine," for regularized risk minimization algorithms, aiming to achieve polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial increase in data size and processing units. The core idea revolves around applying the base learning algorithm to random subsets of the data and iteratively aggregating the resulting hypotheses using Radon points, which provide a doubly-exponential increase in confidence compared to averaging. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the performance and demonstrate substantial speedups in empirical evaluations, comparing favorably with Spark MLlib and averaging-based approaches.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a critical need for efficient parallelization of machine learning algorithms to handle increasingly large datasets. The connection to Radon geometry is interesting and offers a compelling theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The authors clearly articulate the trade-offs between runtime, data size, and the number of processing units, and convincingly argue for the practical benefits of their approach. The experimental section is comprehensive, showcasing the Radon machine’s performance on various datasets and comparing it against established baselines. The writing is generally clear and concise, and the paper is well-organized. The inclusion of proofs in the appendix adds to the rigor of the work.

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. First, while the theoretical analysis is impressive, the assumptions regarding the Radon number of the hypothesis space (finite Radon number) and the availability of a sufficient number of processors (quasi-polynomial) may limit the applicability of the scheme in certain scenarios. The discussion of these limitations could be expanded.  Specifically, the relationship between the Radon number and the dimensionality of the data (and how to handle high-dimensional data) deserves more attention. While the authors mention embeddings and block coordinate descent, a more detailed discussion of practical strategies for dealing with these challenges would be valuable.

Second, the empirical evaluation, while thorough, primarily focuses on datasets where the number of features is significantly smaller than the number of instances. Exploring the performance of the Radon machine on high-dimensional datasets (e.g., text data) would strengthen the evaluation and provide a more complete picture of its scalability. It’s also unclear how sensitive the performance is to the choice of the parameter *h*. A sensitivity analysis of this parameter is warranted.

Third, the comparison to Spark MLlib could be more nuanced. The authors state that their scheme achieves hypotheses of similar quality, but a more detailed analysis of the performance differences, beyond just runtime and AUC, would be beneficial.  Specifically, examining differences in model parameters or specific error types could provide further insights.

Finally, the references are extensive, but some citations appear somewhat dated. Recent advancements in parallel and distributed machine learning algorithms, particularly those focused on communication efficiency and asynchronous methods, could be discussed to better position the proposed approach within the current research landscape.  For example, the work of McMahan et al. (2017) on federated learning is relevant.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound parallelization scheme.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating significant speedups.
*   Clear articulation of trade-offs and limitations.
*   Well-written and organized presentation.
*   Addresses an important problem in machine learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Assumptions about Radon number and processor availability may limit applicability.
*   Empirical evaluation lacks focus on high-dimensional datasets.
*   Limited sensitivity analysis of the parameter *h*.
*   Comparison to Spark MLlib could be more detailed.
*   Some citations are dated; recent related work is not fully addressed.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. While some weaknesses exist, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the promising empirical results warrant serious consideration. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly a more detailed discussion of the practical limitations and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation.  Specifically, the authors should expand on techniques for handling high-dimensional data and perform a sensitivity analysis on the parameter *h*. Incorporating recent advancements in distributed learning and providing a more granular comparison with state-of-the-art frameworks like Spark would further strengthen the contribution.
