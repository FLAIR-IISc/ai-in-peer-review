

# Review: Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning

## Summary
This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms that achieves polylogarithmic runtime compared to the sequential polynomial runtime. The key insight is using Radon points (from convex geometry) for aggregating hypotheses from parallel learning runs on random data subsets, rather than simple averaging. The authors prove that this approach reduces the probability of an ε-bad hypothesis doubly-exponentially with each aggregation level, enabling significant speedups. The empirical evaluation shows 80-700x speedups over sequential implementations while maintaining comparable predictive performance to Spark MLlib algorithms.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by establishing a provably effective parallelization scheme. The core results (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3) are well-motivated and rigorously proven, showing how the Radon point aggregation reduces error probability doubly-exponentially. This provides a clean theoretical foundation for understanding parallel learning.

2. **Practical Applicability**: The black-box nature of the approach (no need for algorithm-specific parallelization) is a significant practical advantage. The empirical results demonstrate substantial speedups (80-700x) while maintaining comparable predictive performance to state-of-the-art Spark implementations.

3. **Clear Differentiation from Prior Work**: The paper thoroughly contrasts with related approaches (averaging-based parallelization, stochastic gradient descent methods), clearly articulating the theoretical advantages of the Radon point approach.

4. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, algorithms, and a logical progression from theory to experiments.

## Weaknesses

1. **Practical Scalability Concerns**: The theoretical runtime improvement assumes a quasi-polynomial number of processors (rh). For real-world applications with limited hardware resources, this could be a significant constraint. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't explore how the approach performs with practical processor counts (e.g., c << rh), where the runtime would increase by a factor of max{1, rh/c}. The authors should provide more concrete examples of how this limitation affects practical implementations.

2. **Limited Empirical Scope**: The experiments focus on moderately sized datasets where base learning algorithms are still feasible sequentially. The paper doesn't demonstrate the approach on truly massive datasets where parallelization would be most beneficial. It would be valuable to see how the Radon Machine performs on datasets where the base algorithm would be infeasible to run sequentially.

3. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The approach relies on the hypothesis space having a finite Radon number (r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces). While the paper discusses workarounds for high-dimensional data, the theoretical guarantees don't directly apply to many practical machine learning problems without additional assumptions. The authors should clarify the scope of applicability more precisely.

4. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The empirical evaluation compares to Spark MLlib algorithms but doesn't include more recent parallel learning approaches (e.g., those using communication-efficient optimization techniques). Including such comparisons would strengthen the significance claim.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of parallel learning algorithms and presents a practically useful approach. The core idea of using Radon points for aggregation is novel and well-justified. The empirical results are promising, though the scope of the evaluation could be expanded.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the practical scalability concerns and expand the empirical comparisons. The paper makes a valuable contribution to both the theoretical and practical aspects of parallel machine learning.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand the empirical evaluation** to include larger datasets where sequential learning would be infeasible, demonstrating the true value of the approach in the most relevant scenarios.

2. **Clarify the practical implications** of the quasi-polynomial processor requirement. Provide concrete examples of how the approach performs when the number of processors is limited (c << rh), including runtime analysis for such cases.

3. **Strengthen the comparison** with more recent parallel learning algorithms beyond Spark MLlib, particularly those with communication-efficient designs.

4. **Clarify the theoretical scope** by explicitly stating the limitations of the approach (e.g., for high-dimensional data without dimensionality reduction) and how these limitations might be addressed in practice.

5. **Include a discussion** of how the Radon Machine would perform with non-Euclidean hypothesis spaces, as mentioned in the future work section.

The paper addresses an important problem with a novel solution that has both theoretical and practical merit. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent contribution to NeurIPS.
