This paper presents a parallelization scheme, the "Radon machine," for regularized risk minimization algorithms, aiming to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic at the cost of increased data and processing units. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm to random data subsets, then iteratively aggregate hypotheses using Radon points. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the effectiveness of this parallelization, demonstrating a doubly exponential increase in confidence with each aggregation step. Empirical evaluation confirms a substantial speedup compared to sequential execution and competitive performance against Spark MLlib.

While the idea of leveraging Radon points for aggregation is intriguing and potentially impactful, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet collectively substantial, issues that detract from its overall quality. My assessment is leaning towards rejection due to these cumulative flaws, despite acknowledging the novelty and theoretical soundness of the central concept.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of Radon points to accelerate parallel learning is a genuinely novel contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis, including the proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3, appears sound and provides a solid foundation for the proposed scheme.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate a significant speedup in practical settings, lending credence to the theoretical claims.
* **Black-box Applicability:** The scheme’s generality—treating the base learning algorithm as a black box—is a significant advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Precision in Notation:** The notation is inconsistent and sometimes ambiguous. For instance, the use of 'D' to represent both the dataset and a distribution is confusing.  The definition of N0(ε,∆) is introduced but rarely used in the subsequent derivations.
* **Overly Assertive Claims:** The opening statement claiming a reduction in runtime "from polynomial to polylogarithmic" is an oversimplification. Theorem 4’s conditions—specifically, the Radon number being polylogarithmic in sample complexity—are crucial and not sufficiently emphasized. This makes the initial claim misleading.
* **Inconsistent Terminology:**  The term “effective parallelisation” is not clearly defined beyond the polylogarithmic runtime guarantee. This definition lacks nuance and fails to acknowledge the practical limitations of increased data and processing unit requirements.
* **Minor Mathematical Errors/Imprecisions:**  In the derivation of sample complexity (Section 3), the handling of logarithmic terms and constants seems imprecise. More careful treatment of these terms is required.  The statement about "ignored logarithmic and constant terms" feels sloppy.
* **Insufficient Discussion of Practical Limitations:** While acknowledging the need for abundant data, the paper doesn’t adequately address scenarios where the Radon number 'r' is large relative to the dataset size. The suggestion of using low-rank approximations is brief and lacks concrete guidance.
* **Experimental Setup Details:** The experimental section lacks crucial details. The precise configurations of the Spark cluster, the specific versions of WEKA and scikit-learn used, and the parameter tuning strategies are not fully described. The justification for choosing specific datasets is also weak. The statement regarding optimization of parameters "on an independent split of the datasets" is vague.
* **Figures and Tables:** Figure 1(c) is difficult to interpret due to the lack of clear labels and a consistent scale. The figures lack sufficient detail and should have error bars. Table captions are also missing.
* **Writing Quality:** The writing style is often verbose and repetitive. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased. Many paragraphs could be more concise.
* **Related Work Coverage:** While the paper cites relevant work, the discussion of parallel learning algorithms in Spark MLlib is superficial.  A more thorough comparison of the Radon machine with state-of-the-art distributed learning frameworks is needed.
* **Appendix Organization:** The appendices are somewhat disorganized.  For example, the proof of Theorem 4 is in Appendix A.1, but the theorem itself is stated in Section 3.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The novel application of Radon points offers a promising avenue for accelerating machine learning algorithms.
* The theoretical analysis provides a strong foundation for the proposed scheme.
* The empirical results demonstrate a significant speedup in practical settings.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies detract significantly from the paper's overall quality.
* The overly assertive claims and imprecise notation may mislead readers.
* The insufficient discussion of practical limitations and the lack of detail in the experimental setup raise concerns about the reproducibility and generalizability of the results.
* The writing quality and organization of the paper are subpar.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the innovative idea and theoretical soundness, the paper is marred by a large number of minor flaws. While individually these flaws may not be fatal, their cumulative effect significantly diminishes the paper's quality and readability.  The lack of rigor in presentation undermines the credibility of the research. I recommend rejection in its current form. The authors should address the issues raised above, focusing on clarity, precision, and completeness, before resubmitting. A more thorough experimental evaluation and a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of the scheme are also essential.
