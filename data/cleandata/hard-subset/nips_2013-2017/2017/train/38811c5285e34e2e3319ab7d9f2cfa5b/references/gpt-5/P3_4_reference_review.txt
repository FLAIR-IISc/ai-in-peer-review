Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for convex regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean parameter spaces. The scheme trains base learners on rh i.i.d. subsets in parallel and aggregates hypotheses via iterated Radon points along a tree of height h. The core theoretical result (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3) shows that if a base hypothesis drawn from the base learner’s induced distribution is ε-bad with probability δ, then one level of Radon aggregation reduces the error probability to at most (r δ)2, and h levels yield (r δ)2^h. Coupled with standard sample complexity forms NL0(ε, δ) ≈ (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k and TL(n) ∈ O(n^κ), they choose h so that the subproblem size n becomes polylogarithmic in M = NL0(ε, ∆), achieving total runtime TR,h = TL(n) + h r^3 that is polylog(M), at the cost of quasi-polynomial data and processors (c = r^h). Empirically, using linear models (SVM, logistic regression, least squares), they report 80–700× speedups over single-processor baselines on up to 150 processors and competitive AUC versus Spark MLlib.

This builds on: (i) Radon points and centerpoint approximations (Clarkson et al.; Radon’s theorem), (ii) averaging-based distributed ERM (e.g., Zinkevich et al., McDonald et al.), (iii) communication-efficient distributed optimization (Boyd et al.; Shamir et al.; Zhang et al.), and (iv) the NC framework and P-completeness discussions (Cook; Greenlaw et al.). The novelty is using Radon point aggregation to obtain a doubly-exponential reduction in error probability, enabling polylog time under stated conditions.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A simple, model-agnostic aggregator (Radon points) with a clean probabilistic contraction property for any quasi-/convex “badness” functional Q, yielding a doubly-exponential confidence boost.
- Theoretical clarity: The reduction P(Q(rh)>ε) ≤ (r δ)2^h is elegant, robust to a single bad child (contrast to averaging), and the runtime decomposition is transparent. The r^3 aggregation cost and total O(h r^3) overhead are reasonable.
- Black-box applicability: Applies to many convex ERM learners without algorithm-specific derivations; the model class is preserved by convex combinations in parameter space.
- Empirical promise: Substantial wall-clock speedups with comparable AUC to strong distributed baselines (Spark MLlib), and small aggregation overhead in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Independence and data assumptions: The analysis assumes rh i.i.d. hypotheses, which requires rh i.i.d. datasets (or at least independent draws for each leaf). In practice, the paper partitions a single finite dataset “into iid subsets,” which is not strictly correct. The theoretical guarantees depend on independence; please clarify whether subsets are sampled with replacement or from separate data streams, and discuss the effect of dependence from partitioning without replacement.
- Sample and processor blowup: Achieving polylog time relies on making the leaf subproblems as small as n ≈ log M by choosing h ≈ log M. This entails c = r^h processors and total data N = r^h n that are quasi-polynomial in 1/ε and log(1/∆). While polynomial in M, this is a substantial resource assumption; Theorem 4 requires that the Radon number r be polylog in sample complexity, effectively limiting the scope to moderate or slowly growing dimensions. The paper should discuss the practical regime where d is large (e.g., sparse high-dimensional text), and quantify when the approach remains competitive after dimensionality reduction.
- Relation to other robust aggregators: There is related work on robust distributed aggregation (e.g., geometric median-of-means, coordinate-wise median) that can also amplify confidence and is robust to outliers/Byzantine workers. A comparison, at least theoretically (rates vs. constants, assumptions on Q), would help position the contribution.
- Bounds are distribution-free but loose: Proposition 2 uses a union bound over pairs, giving ≤ (r p)^2 rather than the tighter C(r,2) p^2; the stated form is fine but leads to a requirement δ ≤ 1/(2r) for contraction. The paper notes this but does not quantify constants in end-to-end sample complexity; a more explicit comparison to the sequential learner’s sample complexity would help practitioners choose h.
- Practical comparisons: The base learners seem single-threaded (WEKA/scikit-learn) while Spark baselines are distributed; please ensure fairness by (i) reporting strong multi-threaded baselines where possible, and (ii) showing scaling with processors and h beyond 150 CPUs. The superlinear speedups reported (up to 721× with 150 processors) suggest confounds (e.g., different numerical routines or caching effects). Ablations on aggregation cost versus learning time would help.
- Empirical focus on AUC versus claimed confidence: The theory guarantees (ε, ∆) calibration; experiments report AUC without confidence calibration or coverage estimates. While AUC is standard, including an evaluation aligned with the theoretical claims (e.g., empirical failure probabilities for a target ε) would strengthen the empirical story.
- Numerical stability and implementation details: Computing Radon points requires solving r×r systems; stability in higher dimensions and conditioning issues are not discussed. Please comment on regularization, normalization, and whether you used robust solvers.

Questions for the authors
- How exactly are the leaf datasets generated in experiments (with/without replacement)? Any empirical check on independence effects?
- Can you report scaling of runtime and accuracy as a function of h and d, and the breakdown of aggregation time?
- How sensitive are results to nonlinearity handled via random features or Nyström? Any experiments beyond linear models?
- Could you compare to geometric median or trimmed-mean aggregation?

Clarity and reproducibility
The paper is generally well written. A few points could be clearer: define ld as log base 2 on first use; make explicit the independence assumption; clarify when δ ≤ 1/(2r) is required; and summarize Appendix C.1’s Radon system in the main text. Code availability is a plus.

Overall significance
The idea of Radon aggregation for black-box parallel ERM is novel and potentially impactful for high-confidence learning under abundant data and moderate dimensionality. The theoretical contraction is elegant; the empirical results are promising. The main caveats are the strong independence and resource assumptions and limited robustness/comparison analysis.

Pros
- Simple, general method with strong, intuitive theory
- Doubly-exponential confidence amplification via Radon points
- Black-box applicability; low communication
- Solid empirical speedups with competitive accuracy

Cons
- Requires large data and many processors; scope limited by dimension
- Independence assumption not fully matched by experimental setup
- Missing comparisons to robust aggregators; limited analysis of stability
- Empirical metrics not directly aligned with theoretical guarantees

Recommendation: Weak accept. This is an interesting and well-executed contribution that advances our understanding of effective parallelization for convex ERM and offers a practical approach with clear benefits in appropriate regimes. Addressing the independence assumption, adding robust aggregator baselines, and clarifying numerical aspects would strengthen the final version.

Reviewer confidence: High.
