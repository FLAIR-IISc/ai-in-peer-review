

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the Radon Machine, a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The authors propose using Radon points for aggregating hypotheses from parallel runs of base learning algorithms, claiming a reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic runtime while maintaining the same confidence and error bounds. The theoretical contribution establishes that the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis decreases doubly exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. The paper relates to previous work on parallel learning, noting that averaging-based approaches [32, 45, 46] do not achieve polylogarithmic runtime guarantees, and positions their work as addressing the open question: "Is consistent regularized risk minimization possible in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors?" [21].

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with well-constructed proofs for Proposition 2 and Theorem 3. The demonstration of the doubly exponential reduction in error probability is mathematically sound and clearly presented. The connection to Nick's Class [9] and P-completeness provides important theoretical context.

2. **Practical Significance:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates substantial speedups (80-700x) across multiple datasets while maintaining comparable predictive performance to base learners and Spark MLlib implementations. The Radon Machine outperforms averaging-at-the-end baselines by at least 10% in AUC across all datasets.

3. **Novel Approach:** The use of Radon points instead of averaging for hypothesis aggregation is a significant theoretical innovation. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from existing approaches and why it provides better confidence guarantees.

4. **Black-Box Applicability:** The scheme treats the underlying learning algorithm as a black box, which is a major practical advantage over other parallelization methods requiring algorithm-specific modifications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Theoretical Results:** The theoretical guarantees assume the hypothesis space has a finite Radon number, which for Euclidean spaces requires r = d+2 (where d is the dimensionality). For high-dimensional problems, this creates a practical limitation that the authors acknowledge but don't fully address in the theoretical framework. The empirical evaluation is limited to datasets where dimensionality is much smaller than instance count.

2. **Empirical Evaluation Scope:** The empirical results are limited to moderately sized datasets due to scalability limitations of the base learners. The authors note they reserved larger datasets for comparison with Spark algorithms, which limits the ability to fully assess the scalability claims.

3. **Implementation Details:** While the paper provides a Spark implementation, the specific choices for the Radon number r and parameter h require manual tuning for different datasets. The paper doesn't provide guidance on how to optimally select these parameters for new applications.

4. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The comparison with Spark MLlib is good, but the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate why the Radon Machine would be preferred over other parallel approaches in scenarios where the authors' theoretical guarantees don't apply (e.g., when the hypothesis space doesn't have a finite Radon number).

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by addressing a fundamental question in parallel machine learning with a provably effective parallelization scheme. The Radon Machine provides a meaningful reduction in runtime complexity while maintaining theoretical guarantees, which is a substantial advancement over existing approaches. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate practical value.

The main limitation is the theoretical restriction to hypothesis spaces with finite Radon numbers, though the authors appropriately discuss potential mitigations (random projections, low-rank approximations) in Section 5. The empirical evaluation, while limited in dataset size, provides strong evidence of the practical benefits.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a valuable contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in parallel machine learning by providing a provably effective method that achieves polylogarithmic runtime with quasi-polynomially many processors, addressing an important open problem in the field.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications, addresses a significant open problem in parallel learning, and provides thorough empirical validation. While the scope is limited to certain hypothesis spaces, the approach is general enough to be applicable to many important machine learning algorithms, and the authors provide reasonable discussion of limitations and potential extensions. The work represents a meaningful advancement in the field of parallel machine learning.
