## Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically sound and potentially significant contribution to the challenging problem of parallelizing machine learning algorithms. The core idea of using Radon points for aggregation offers a novel approach to achieving polylogarithmic runtime with a quasipolynomial overhead in data and processing units. While the theoretical results are strong, the presentation could be improved for clarity, and the practical implications require further scrutiny. I believe this paper warrants acceptance, leaning towards a strong accept, but with a request for minor revisions to improve clarity and address some questions regarding practical applicability.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper introduces the “Radon machine,” a parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. It leverages the properties of Radon points – points within the convex hull of subsets – to aggregate hypotheses computed in parallel on different data subsets. The key contribution is a theoretical demonstration that this approach can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity, at the cost of increased data usage and processing units. The authors position this work within the context of parallel computation theory, specifically relating it to Nick’s Class and the challenge of efficiently parallelizing polynomial-time algorithms.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical analysis is rigorous and provides clear bounds on the sample complexity and runtime of the Radon machine. The proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are well-structured and demonstrate a solid understanding of convex analysis.
*   **Novelty:** The application of Radon points to aggregate hypotheses in a parallel learning setting is a novel idea. This distinguishes the work from traditional averaging-based parallelization schemes.
*   **Potential Significance:** Achieving polylogarithmic runtime for learning algorithms is a highly desirable goal, and this work presents a promising step in that direction. The scheme's black-box nature (applicable to various base learners) is a practical advantage.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate a significant speedup compared to sequential learning and competitive performance against Spark’s MLlib, validating the potential of the Radon machine in practice.
* **Comprehensive Discussion:** The paper engages with the broader theoretical landscape of parallel computation and discusses the limitations and future directions of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Accessibility:**  The paper is dense and requires a strong background in convex optimization and parallel computation. The initial sections, defining the problem setting, could benefit from more intuitive explanations and examples. The notation is occasionally heavy.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn't fully address the challenges of applying the scheme to very high-dimensional data or non-linear models. While acknowledging these limitations, a more detailed discussion of mitigation strategies (e.g., low-rank approximations) would be beneficial.
* **Radon Number Dependence:** The runtime complexity is directly tied to the Radon number. While the authors mention it's polylogarithmic in sample complexity for many common spaces, more discussion on how this impacts performance across diverse hypothesis spaces would be helpful.
* **Communication Cost:** Although the paper acknowledges communication costs, a more quantitative assessment of the communication overhead would strengthen the analysis, particularly in the context of distributed environments.
* **Empirical Setup:** While the results are promising, more details on the experimental setup, dataset characteristics, and hyperparameter tuning would enhance reproducibility and allow for a more thorough evaluation.

**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

*   **Quality:** Technically sound, with well-supported claims. The paper is a complete piece of work, though some aspects could be elaborated upon. The authors acknowledge limitations.
*   **Clarity:** Could be improved. The writing is dense and assumes a high level of expertise. More intuitive explanations and examples would be helpful.
*   **Originality:** The application of Radon points to parallel learning is novel and offers a unique approach to the problem. Related work is adequately referenced.
*   **Significance:** The potential for polylogarithmic runtime is significant. The black-box nature of the scheme increases its applicability and potential impact.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

*   Strong theoretical guarantees.
*   Novel and potentially impactful approach.
*   Promising empirical results.
*   Thorough discussion of related work and limitations.

**Against:**

*   Density and lack of clarity in some sections.
*   Limited discussion of practical challenges in high-dimensional and non-linear settings.

**Final Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept** with minor revisions. Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1.  **Improve Clarity:**  Revise the introductory sections to provide more intuitive explanations of the problem setting and the Radon machine's core ideas.
2.  **Expand Discussion of Practical Challenges:** Provide a more detailed discussion of how the scheme can be adapted to high-dimensional data and non-linear models (e.g., low-rank approximations, feature embeddings).
3.  **Elaborate on Communication Costs:**  Include a more quantitative assessment of the communication overhead, especially in distributed settings.
4. **Provide a more detailed discussion of the Radon number.** Discuss how the Radon number will affect the runtime in various scenarios.



I believe addressing these points will significantly enhance the paper's accessibility and impact, making it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
