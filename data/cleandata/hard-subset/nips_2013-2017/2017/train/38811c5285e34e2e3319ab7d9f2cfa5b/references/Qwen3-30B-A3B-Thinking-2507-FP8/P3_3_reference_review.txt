

# Peer Review: "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The approach divides data into random subsets, runs a base learner on each subset in parallel, and iteratively aggregates results using Radon points (geometric points in the intersection of convex hulls of partitions). The authors prove that error probability decreases doubly exponentially with each aggregation step, enabling polylogarithmic runtime in sample size with quasi-polynomial processors. Empirical results show 80-700x speedups on moderate datasets with comparable predictive performance to base learners and Spark MLlib.

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically significant contribution by establishing a novel connection between convex geometry (Radon points) and machine learning parallelization. The proof of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 is elegant and demonstrates a clear advantage over simple averaging methods. The authors correctly identify that averaging does not provide the same error reduction guarantees as their approach, which is a crucial theoretical distinction.

The empirical evaluation is well-conducted, with careful comparison to both baseline averaging and Spark MLlib implementations. The results on moderate datasets (up to 5 million instances) are convincing, showing substantial speedups with comparable predictive performance. The "black-box" nature of the approach—requiring no modifications to base learners—is a major practical advantage.

## Weaknesses

The most significant limitation is the impractical processor requirement. While the paper correctly states the runtime is polylogarithmic in N, the number of processors needed is quasi-polynomial in 1/ε and log(1/δ). For typical settings (ε=0.01, δ=0.01, r=100), this requires ~10^40 processors—far beyond any foreseeable hardware. This undermines the practical significance of the claimed speedup.

The paper's applicability is severely limited to low-dimensional data due to the Radon number requirement (r=d+2 for Euclidean space). For high-dimensional data (e.g., text with 10,000+ features), the approach becomes infeasible without dimensionality reduction. While the authors mention random projections as a potential solution, they provide no empirical evidence that this works effectively in practice for machine learning tasks.

The comparison with state-of-the-art distributed learning methods is insufficient. The paper doesn't compare with recent communication-efficient approaches like those of Zhang et al. (2015) or Li et al. (2020), which achieve good speedups with more practical processor requirements. The claim that the Radon Machine is "substantially faster" than Spark MLlib is not sufficiently contextualized against these alternatives.

The theoretical analysis assumes the base learning algorithm is consistent and efficient with polynomial sample complexity. The paper doesn't discuss how the Radon Machine would perform with non-consistent algorithms or when the base learner has poor generalization properties.

## Originality and Significance

The Radon point approach is novel in machine learning parallelization, though it builds on geometric concepts from computational geometry. The theoretical guarantee of doubly exponential error reduction is a clear advancement over existing averaging-based approaches. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the impractical processor requirements.

The paper correctly positions itself against prior work on parallel learning (e.g., Vitter and Lin [42], Long and Servedio [21]), but fails to fully engage with more recent distributed learning literature that addresses similar challenges with more practical assumptions.

## Recommendations

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More explicit discussion of practical limitations and when the approach is applicable
2. Empirical results on high-dimensional data demonstrating the effectiveness of dimensionality reduction
3. A more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art distributed learning methods
4. Analysis of the trade-off between error reduction and practical processor requirements

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of parallel machine learning. The Radon point approach provides a novel geometric perspective on aggregation that leads to stronger theoretical guarantees than simple averaging. However, the practical limitations—particularly the quasi-polynomial processor requirement—significantly reduce the paper's impact for real-world applications. The empirical results are strong for the datasets tested, but don't demonstrate viability for high-dimensional or extremely large-scale problems.

The paper is technically sound within its theoretical framework, but the claimed "effective parallelisation" is misleading without acknowledging the impractical processor requirements. The theoretical contribution is significant, but the practical significance is limited to specific low-dimensional use cases.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (theoretical contribution is strong but practical limitations require careful consideration by readers)
