Summary
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization with convex losses and convex hypothesis classes embedded in Rd. The method trains the base learner independently on rh iid subsets (leaves) and aggregates models in a balanced r-ary tree via Radon points (r = Radon number, d + 2 in Rd). A key theoretical result shows that the probability an aggregate model is ε-bad contracts doubly exponentially in the tree height h: if a base hypothesis is ε-bad with probability δ, the top-level Radon aggregate is ε-bad with probability at most (r δ)2^h. Under an assumed sample complexity form NL0(ε, δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k and base runtime TL(n) ∈ O(nκ), the authors select h to make leaf sample size n ≈ log M (where M is the sequential sample size needed for (ε, Δ)) and show the Radon machine runs in polylogarithmic time in M with quasi-polynomially many processors, provided r is polylogarithmic in M. Empirically, on Spark clusters, the scheme yields large wall-clock speedups over single-node base learners and is competitive with (and often faster than) Spark MLlib implementations, with similar AUC. Compared to averaging-at-the-end, Radon aggregation yields consistently better accuracy at comparable runtime.

Relation to prior work
The scheme builds on Radon’s theorem and the iterated Radon point method used to approximate center points (Clarkson et al.). It contrasts with averaging-based distributed learning (parameter averaging, model averaging), for which strong confidence amplification and polylog-time guarantees are not known and sometimes provably limited. It also differs from distributed SGD/ADMM approaches whose iteration counts scale with accuracy and/or number of workers. The paper ties the question of polylog-time learning to NC and P-completeness discussions and connects to sample complexity via VC/Rademacher bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, black-box scheme: applies to many convex ERM learners without modifying solvers.
- Clean, general confidence amplification guarantee via Radon aggregation; the double-exponential reduction in failure probability with height is elegant and new in the ML aggregation context.
- Clear complexity comparison between Radon aggregation and the base learner to reach identical (ε, Δ).
- Empirical evidence on several large datasets indicates substantial speedups and competitive accuracy; systematic comparisons with Spark and with averaging.
- Communication light: aggregation-at-the-end pattern, with small, parameter-sized messages.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions/independence: The theory assumes rh iid hypotheses at the leaves (each trained on an iid sample from the data distribution). The algorithm section says “divide D into rh iid subsets,” which is impossible for a fixed finite D without replacement; partitioning yields dependence. Please clarify the data access model: Is the method designed for the regime where total data N = r^h n is freshly sampled (with replacement) from the underlying distribution? If, in practice, leaves are formed by partitioning a fixed dataset, discuss whether (and how) the proofs adapt (e.g., exchangeability, without-replacement sampling).
- Processor count and feasibility: Achieving polylog runtime requires quasi-polynomially many processors: c = r^h, with r = d + 2. This can be very large when d is not tiny. The main theorem requires r to be bounded by a polylogarithmic function of the sample complexity. This is a strong condition that excludes many high-dimensional regimes; the paper mentions random projections/low-rank embeddings as a remedy but does not quantify the induced bias or its effect on guarantees. A more explicit treatment of the trade-off between dimension reduction error and overall guarantees would strengthen the work.
- Leaf accuracy requirement: The base learner must achieve δ ≤ 1/(2r). Since r = d + 2, this constant can be small in high dimensions. The analysis chooses h so that n ≈ log M, but it is not obvious that with such small n the base learner will achieve δ ≤ 1/(2r) for realistic d. A numerical example or lemma showing that this constant-level δ is attainable with n = O(log M) under standard VC/Rademacher bounds (as promised in the appendix) would help.
- Tightness of bounds: Proposition 2 uses a union bound over pairs and the property that at least two children are worse than the Radon point. This seems quite loose; any chance to sharpen constants or use dependent rounding arguments to reduce the r factor?
- Computation cost of Radon points: Each aggregation step solves O(r^3) linear systems; with r = d + 2, this is O(d^3) per group per level. For larger d, this becomes the bottleneck. The experiments focus on relatively modest d; it would be useful to include timing breakdowns and scaling with d, and compare to lighter robust aggregators (e.g., geometric median of models) as an additional baseline.
- Empirical fairness and scope: Some comparisons use different solvers (Weka/scikit vs Spark implementations with different regularization/optimizers). Please clarify hyperparameter tuning parity, convergence criteria, and whether Spark baselines used the same training time/data budget. The reported speedups (e.g., 700x with ~150 processors) are plausible due to smaller per-leaf problems but would benefit from strong-scaling/weak-scaling plots vs h and c, and ablations showing the effect of h on accuracy/Δ.

Quality
- Theory is sound under stated assumptions and is clearly presented. However, the independence and data access assumptions should be stated crisply, and the dimensionality constraint behind Theorem 4 should be emphasized as a key condition.
- Experiments are extensive and encouraging, but adding scaling analyses with h, c, d, and explicit communication/aggregation breakdowns would increase credibility.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. Suggestions: explicitly separate the oracle data model (iid draws from D) from the practical partitioning scenario; restate the required conditions (finite r, δ ≤ 1/(2r), sample complexity form) at the start of Section 3; provide a small running example illustrating parameter choices (ε, Δ, r, h, n, N).

Originality
- Using iterated Radon points to aggregate models for confidence amplification appears novel and interesting. The connection to NC-style polylog time (under nontrivial conditions) is also fresh.

Significance
- The idea could influence practical black-box parallelization of convex learners and stimulate work on robust aggregators beyond averaging. The theoretical framing is valuable, though practical impact will depend on dimensionality and data availability.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, elegant aggregation with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Black-box applicability to many convex learners.
- Solid empirical speedups and competitive accuracy; low communication.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumptions on iid leaf data and dimensionality; limited guidance on high-d regimes.
- Practical independence vs partitioning gap not addressed theoretically.
- Aggregation cost O(d^3) may limit applicability; no comparison to other robust aggregators.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the data model and independence assumptions; discuss without-replacement theory.
- Quantify the range of d and N where Theorem 4 is practically achievable; include experiments with random projections and report accuracy/runtime trade-offs.
- Provide scaling studies vs h, c, d; include breakdown of learning vs aggregation vs communication.
- Add a baseline using robust aggregators (geometric median/median-of-means) and report robustness to a fraction of bad leaves.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a neat and potentially influential idea with both theory and practice; addressing the noted assumptions and adding scaling evidence would strengthen it further.
