

# Peer Review: "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Radon Machine, a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The key insight is using Radon points (from convex geometry) to aggregate hypotheses from parallel learning on random data subsets. The authors prove that with h levels of aggregation, the error probability decreases doubly exponentially (Δ = (rδ)^2^h), enabling runtime reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity. The scheme treats the base learning algorithm as a black box, requiring no modifications to the underlying algorithm. Empirical results demonstrate 80-700x speedup over sequential execution on 150 processors while maintaining comparable predictive performance to Spark's MLlib implementations.

## Quality
The paper presents technically sound theoretical contributions with rigorous proofs for Proposition 2 and Theorem 3. The connection between the Radon point aggregation and the doubly exponential error reduction is well-established. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, with thorough comparisons to both a baseline (averaging-at-the-end) and state-of-the-art Spark implementations across multiple datasets. The authors properly acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for datasets to be at least a multiple of r^h, applicability to linear models) and provide thoughtful discussion of extensions to high-dimensional and non-linear models.

However, I have some concerns about the practical applicability of the quasi-polynomial processor requirement. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't sufficiently address how to handle cases where r^h exceeds available processors (beyond the brief mention of "deparallelisation"). The dependence on the Radon number (r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces) could be problematic when d is large, as it would require exponentially more processors with respect to dimensionality.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical validation. Algorithm 1 provides a precise description of the Radon Machine. The connection to Nick's Class and P-completeness theory is well-explained, though some readers might need background on Radon points (which is appropriately defined).

Minor improvements could be made to the presentation of Theorem 4, where the relationship between the Radon number and sample complexity could be made more explicit. The empirical results are presented clearly with appropriate figures and tables.

## Originality
The core contribution of using Radon points for hypothesis aggregation is novel. While averaging-based parallelizations have been explored (citing [32, 45, 46]), the Radon point approach provides a theoretically grounded alternative with stronger error bounds. The doubly exponential reduction in error probability is a significant theoretical advance over previous approaches, which typically do not provide such strong guarantees.

The paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work, particularly in Section 6 where it discusses PAC learning in parallel, distributed SGD approaches, and other parallel learning methods. The black-box nature of the approach is also a novel practical contribution.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. As datasets grow larger, efficient parallelization becomes increasingly important, particularly for high-stakes applications where error bounds are critical. The theoretical result of reducing runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic is a major step toward solving the open question of whether consistent regularized risk minimization can be done in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors.

The empirical results are compelling: 80-700x speedup with comparable accuracy on real datasets. The black-box nature makes this approach widely applicable to many machine learning algorithms without requiring algorithm-specific parallelization. The connection to theoretical computer science (Nick's Class, P-completeness) adds theoretical depth to an important practical problem.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical contribution with doubly exponential error reduction
- Strong empirical results demonstrating practical speedups
- Black-box parallelization approach applicable to many algorithms
- Comprehensive evaluation against multiple baselines
- Clear connection to theoretical computer science

**Weaknesses:**
- Quasi-polynomial processor requirement may limit practical applicability
- Limited exploration of non-linear models despite discussion
- The dependence on Radon number (r = d+2) could be problematic for high-dimensional data
- The paper doesn't fully address how to handle cases where dataset size isn't a multiple of r^h

## Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The theoretical results are rigorous, the empirical evaluation is thorough, and the approach has clear practical value for large-scale machine learning. The main weakness is the quasi-polynomial processor requirement, but the authors properly acknowledge this limitation and discuss practical workarounds.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of parallel machine learning, addressing a fundamental open question about the theoretical limits of efficient parallel learning. It connects machine learning to theoretical computer science in a productive way, and the empirical results demonstrate real-world value.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level, and the minor weaknesses do not detract from the substantial contributions.

## Specific Suggestions
1. Consider adding a brief discussion of how to handle cases where the dataset size isn't a multiple of r^h (beyond the "deparallelisation" note).
2. Clarify the relationship between the Radon number r and the actual dimensionality d in the context of real-world datasets.
3. Include a more detailed discussion of how the quasi-polynomial processor requirement affects the practical trade-offs in different application scenarios.

Overall, this is an excellent contribution that advances both the theory and practice of parallel machine learning.
