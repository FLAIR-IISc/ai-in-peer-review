This paper presents a parallelization scheme, dubbed the Radon machine, for regularized risk minimization algorithms, aiming for polylogarithmic runtime with a quasi-polynomial increase in data and processing units. The core idea involves parallel application of the base learner to data subsets and iterative aggregation using Radon points. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the resulting confidence and empirical validation demonstrating speed-ups.

While the theoretical results are intriguing, and the potential for speedup is significant, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet accumulating, flaws that collectively detract from its overall quality and impact. My assessment is leaning towards rejection, despite acknowledging the novelty of the approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of Radon points for parallel aggregation in machine learning is a novel idea.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical bounds on the sample complexity and runtime, establishing a connection to the underlying learning algorithm’s efficiency.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate a tangible speed-up compared to sequential execution and some Spark implementations.
*   **Black-box Approach:** The algorithm’s ability to treat the base learner as a black box is a practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The notation is occasionally inconsistent. For example, `N0` is used both as a function and as a sample size.  The subtle difference between 'D' representing a dataset and the distribution 'D' is not always clearly delineated.
*   **Overly Assertive Language:** The introduction makes overly strong claims ("reduce the runtime…from polynomial to polylogarithmic") without sufficient qualification. The “quasi-polynomial” caveat appears almost as an afterthought.
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper glosses over practical challenges. The requirement for a ‘finite dimensional Euclidean space Rd’ for direct application is restrictive. While low-dimensional embeddings are mentioned, the discussion lacks detail on their impact on performance and the associated complexities.
*   **Vague Parameter Choices:** The choice of parameter *h* in the Radon machine seems rather arbitrary. The justification for selecting *h* based on `ldM − ld ldM` feels ad-hoc and lacks a more principled derivation.
*   **Unclear Connection to Nick’s Class:** The connection to Nick’s Class is invoked but not fully explored. The discussion remains superficial, lacking a deeper dive into the implications for parallel algorithm design.
*   **Insufficient Analysis of Communication Costs:** The paper acknowledges communication complexity but dismisses it too quickly. In realistic distributed settings, communication can be a dominant bottleneck, and a more detailed analysis is warranted.
*   **Minor Mathematical Errors/Imprecisions:** The proof of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 requires careful scrutiny. While the overall direction is likely correct, the justification for some steps feels hand-wavy and could benefit from more rigorous mathematical exposition. Specifically, the application of the union bound seems overly simplistic.
*   **Presentation of Empirical Results:** The figures and tables are not consistently labeled, and the captions lack sufficient detail. Figure 2, for instance, lacks a clear y-axis label.
*    **Incomplete citations:** Several citations are missing page numbers (e.g., [27], [36]).
*   **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The paper is somewhat verbose and repetitive. Several concepts are explained multiple times without adding substantial new information.
*   **The handling of the Radon number 'r':** The treatment of the Radon number feels somewhat glossed over. The authors state its finiteness is required, but there is little discussion of how this impacts the applicability of the scheme to various hypothesis spaces.


**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms with theoretical guarantees.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a significant speed-up in practice.
*   The black-box nature of the algorithm makes it potentially applicable to a wide range of learning algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from numerous minor flaws in notation, presentation, and mathematical rigor.
*   The practical limitations and challenges are not adequately addressed.
*   The connection to existing work in parallel computing is superficial.
*   The overly assertive claims in the introduction are not fully justified by the results.
*   The redundancy and verbosity of the writing hinder clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the Radon machine presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning, the numerous minor flaws significantly detract from its overall quality. The lack of mathematical precision, the somewhat hand-wavy justifications, and the insufficient discussion of practical considerations raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach. The paper, in its current form, feels incomplete and insufficiently polished for acceptance to NIPS. A major revision addressing these concerns is necessary before it could be reconsidered.



**Recommendation:** Reject.
