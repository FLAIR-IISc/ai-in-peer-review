Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for convex regularized risk minimization. The algorithm trains a base learner on many iid subsets in parallel, then aggregates hypotheses via iterated Radon points in a tree. Theoretically, the probability that the final hypothesis is ε-bad shrinks doubly exponentially with the tree height h, i.e., P(Q > ε) ≤ (r·δ)^(2^h), where δ is the base learner’s ε-bad probability and r is the Radon number (d+2 in Rd). Under assumptions on the base learner’s sample complexity and on r, the authors show the Radon machine attains polylogarithmic runtime (in the target sample complexity) using a quasi-polynomial number of processors. Empirically, on a Spark cluster the method is 80–700× faster than the base learners with comparable AUC, competitive with Spark MLlib baselines, and better than an averaging-at-the-end baseline in accuracy.

The work relates to: (i) model averaging on disjoint subsets, which often yields weaker guarantees and typically cannot achieve polylogarithmic runtime; (ii) distributed optimization (e.g., SGD variants and ADMM), which either optimize expected risk directly or empirical risk with communication-heavy or iteration-dependent complexity; and (iii) theoretical treatments of NC-learnability (Vitter & Lin) and efficient parallel learning of halfspaces (Long & Servedio). The use of Radon points to aggregate hypotheses draws from geometric concepts (Radon’s theorem, Tukey depth) and appears novel in this ML parallelization context.

Strengths
- Clear, general black-box scheme: applicable to a broad class of convex ERM algorithms without modifying their internals.
- Theoretical contribution: a simple yet rigorous argument shows Radon aggregation improves confidence doubly exponentially in h, yielding an (ε,Δ)-guarantee with dramatically fewer serial steps. The robustness argument (Radon point remains ε-good if ≤1 child is ε-bad) is compelling.
- Complexity framing: the paper connects parallel ERM to questions around Nick’s Class, carefully distinguishing sample, runtime, and processor costs, and states conditions under which polylogarithmic time is achieved.
- Empirical results: strong speedups with modest processor counts and comparable predictive performance to competitive Spark implementations; consistent superiority over averaging-at-the-end in AUC.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Assumptions for the main runtime claim are strong. Theorem 4 requires the Radon number to be upper-bounded by a function polylogarithmic in the sample complexity. Since r = d + 2 in Rd, this effectively assumes low or polylog-dimensional parameterizations (or successful dimensionality reduction). The practicality of this condition should be discussed more prominently, including its implications for high-dimensional models.
- Processor complexity: achieving polylogarithmic runtime uses c = r^h processors; with h ≈ log M, this can be quasi-polynomial in the target sample complexity. While the paper discusses deparallelization, the formal bounds and empirical exploration under limited processors are limited.
- Sample complexity modeling: the base learner’s sample complexity is assumed to be of the form (α_ε + β_ε log(1/δ))^k. The derivation of these constants is deferred and the conditions under which this form holds (losses, capacity measures, regularization) could be clarified in the main text. The requirement δ ≤ 1/2^r is nontrivial for small subset sizes and should be examined empirically.
- Independence and aggregation: at each aggregation level, Radon points are computed from disjoint iid subsets. Independence across groups is necessary to iterate the bound cleanly; this seems to hold given the construction, but a brief explicit justification would improve clarity.
- Practical scope: experiments focus on linear models and moderate to large datasets where d ≪ N. The paper notes strategies (random features, low-rank approximations) for nonlinear/high-dimensional settings, but does not quantify how such preprocessing affects r, constants in the bounds, or empirical performance.
- Communication and memory: while the scheme is communication-light relative to iterative methods, a more explicit accounting of communication costs and memory footprints, including the r^3 aggregation cost and serialization overhead in real systems, would help practitioners.

Quality
- The theoretical core (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) is sound and clearly proved; the use of quasi-convexity is appropriate for regret. The union bound constants are somewhat loose ((r p)^2 instead of ~r^2 p^2/2), but this does not affect asymptotic conclusions.
- The complexity comparison is carefully set up, but the key assumption on r limits the breadth of the strongest claim. Empirical methodology is reasonable, although the link from finite-sample confidence bounds to observed AUC is indirect.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. Algorithm description is clear. Some crucial assumptions (on r, δ, and the sample complexity form) could be surfaced earlier and emphasized. Small editorial issues (e.g., “iid subsets” in line 5 of Algorithm 1 is an odd phrasing for a partition) can be corrected.

Originality
- Aggregating hypotheses via Radon points for parallel learning appears novel. The doubly-exponential reduction in failure probability through iterated Radon aggregation is an elegant and, to my knowledge, new angle in this literature.

Significance
- The work advances understanding of when and how convex ERM can be effectively parallelized, and provides a practical scheme with strong speedups in realistic settings. The theoretical conditions restrict universal applicability, but the approach is likely to be useful in many low- to moderate-dimensional scenarios and can inspire further work on robust aggregations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and simple aggregation method with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Black-box parallelization applicable to many convex learners.
- Solid empirical speedups with comparable accuracy to strong baselines.
- Clear connections to foundational questions in parallel complexity.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong runtime claims hinge on restrictive assumptions about dimensionality (Radon number) and processor availability.
- Limited exploration of high-dimensional or nonlinear settings and of performance under processor constraints.
- Practical confidence guarantees not demonstrated empirically; reliance on AUC without calibration to (ε,Δ).

Suggestions for improvement
- Make explicit and discuss the practical range where r is polylog in sample complexity; include experiments with random features/low-rank embeddings quantifying the effect on r and accuracy.
- Provide a systematic study of performance vs. number of processors c < r^h and vs. h, including end-to-end wall-clock and communication costs.
- Clarify the base learner sample complexity assumptions and report subset sizes and empirical δ to support the theoretical regime.
- Detail the computational model and memory/communication costs of aggregation in distributed systems.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a novel, theoretically grounded, and practically useful parallelization scheme with compelling empirical results, despite assumptions that limit the strongest theoretical claim’s scope.
