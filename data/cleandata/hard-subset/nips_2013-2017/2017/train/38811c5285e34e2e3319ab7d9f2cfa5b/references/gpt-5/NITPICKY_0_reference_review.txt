Summary and contributions
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for convex, regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean hypothesis spaces. The scheme trains base learners on rh i.i.d. subsets in parallel and aggregates them via iterated Radon points on a tree of height h. A key bound shows that if a single aggregation step maps r i.i.d. base models to their Radon point, the tail probability that the aggregate is ε-bad squares at each level, so after h levels the failure probability becomes roughly (rδ)^{2^h} for base failure probability δ. Under an assumption on the sample complexity of the base learner and sufficiently many processors, the authors claim a reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic runtime (in the sample size needed to achieve a target (ε,Δ)), at the cost of quasi-polynomially many processors and increased total data consumption. Empirical results on several large datasets suggest substantial wall-clock speedups versus single-machine base learners and Spark MLlib baselines, with comparable AUC.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself against averaging-based parallelization and distributed SGD. It leverages properties of Radon points to obtain stronger confidence amplification than averaging. It relates to the longstanding question of efficient parallel learning in the NC sense and cites work on PAC, P-completeness, and NC-learnability. While aggregation by geometric/robust statistics has appeared in distributed learning, the Radon-point-based confidence squaring is novel in this context.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, implementation-agnostic scheme; the “black-box” property is attractive.
- Clear, interpretable probabilistic amplification: per-level squaring of failure probability is elegant and practically relevant for high-confidence regimes.
- Theoretical development is self-contained for Proposition 2 and Theorem 3; the robustness argument versus averaging (one bad child cannot spoil the Radon aggregate) is compelling.
- Empirical results indicate large wall-clock improvements and competitive accuracy on several datasets; code is provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical assumptions and realism:
- The central parallel time claim (polylog runtime) relies on c = r^h processors and r (Radon number) bounded by a function polylogarithmic in the required sample size. For linear models r = d + 2, while the sample complexity typically scales linearly in d; thus r is generally not polylog in the sample complexity. As stated, Theorem 4’s precondition seems rarely satisfied for common regimes, making the NC connection less compelling in practice.
- The scheme requires δ ≤ 1/(2r) for the base learners, potentially demanding large per-processor sample sizes in high dimensions (r = d + 2), which conflicts with the desire to use small shards. This trade-off is not quantified empirically (e.g., how large must n be on each leaf to meet δ ≤ 1/(2r) for given datasets?).
- The analysis treats leaf hypotheses as i.i.d. samples from P; but Algorithm 1 “divides D into rh iid subsets,” which is incorrect: disjoint splits are not i.i.d. draws. While typical learning-to-generalization arguments can tolerate partitioning, the paper’s statements and proofs repeatedly assume i.i.d. draws of hypotheses; the gap should be addressed formally (e.g., sampling with replacement, or a coupling argument).
- Communication complexity is described as O(r^{h+1}) messages of size O(r). Message size should scale with the hypothesis dimension d (not r), so O(d), unless the hypothesis is of dimension r by design (it is not).
- Numerical stability and cost of computing Radon points via solving r×r linear systems are not discussed. For large d (hence r), O(r^3) may be substantial and ill-conditioning likely.

Notation, clarity, and correctness issues:
- Doubly-exponential reduction is inconsistently notated. The text alternates between “2h” and “2 h”; from the argument, the failure probability should be δ^{2^h}. Please make this explicit throughout (including Eq. (4), Theorem 3).
- Overloading of symbols decreases readability: F denotes both the hypothesis space and a random variable; r denotes both Radon number and Radon point map r(·). Consider disambiguation.
- The definition “divide D into rh iid subsets” should be “disjoint subsets” (or draw i.i.d. subsets with replacement if that is intended).
- Sample complexity model NL0(ε,δ) = (αε + βε ld 1/δ)^k is unusual and unexplained (what is “ld”? base-2 logarithm?). Appendix C.2 is referenced for αε,βε but not summarized in the main text; clearer assumptions/examples (e.g., VC/Rademacher bounds with explicit ε,δ,d dependencies) would help.
- Several typographical/grammatical errors (e.g., “We proof,” “st Conference…,” missing spaces) and inconsistent log notation (ld, log) throughout.
- References contain many formatting errors and missing metadata; some author names are misspelled or duplicated.

Empirical evaluation and fairness:
- Speedups exceeding the number of processors (e.g., 721× with 150 processors) likely reflect that leaf models train on far smaller shards. This is a valid algorithmic effect but complicates fairness: the base learner’s runtime on the full dataset is not a realistic parallelizable baseline. Consider adding baselines that: (i) train each leaf on the same shard size and aggregate by averaging or geometric median; (ii) simulate with fewer than rh processors to reflect realistic hardware; (iii) equalize total data usage across methods when comparing accuracy/time.
- Cluster description says 5 nodes × 25 processors = 125, but results cite 150 processors; please reconcile.
- Details on hyperparameter tuning parity across Weka/scikit-learn/Spark are insufficient. Report search spaces, seeds, and whether per-leaf tuning is performed (risk of overfitting shards).
- Figures referenced (1–4) drive key claims; the text should summarize numeric values and confidence intervals. No variance/error bars are discussed.

Positioning relative to robust aggregation:
- The paper contrasts with averaging, but omits discussion/experiments with robust aggregators such as geometric median or median-of-means, which also yield improved concentration in distributed settings. Including such baselines would strengthen the empirical and conceptual case.

Pros
- Simple, general scheme with a clean confidence-amplification mechanism.
- Theoretical analysis of Radon aggregation and robustness vs averaging.
- Promising empirical speedups and competitive accuracy; code available.

Cons
- Central NC-style runtime claim hinges on restrictive assumptions (r polylog in sample complexity; many processors).
- i.i.d. and independence assumptions for hypotheses from data partitions are not justified rigorously.
- Limited and potentially unfair empirical baselines; superlinear speedups reported without careful normalization.
- Numerous clarity, notation, and reference issues that hinder reproducibility and readability.

Questions for authors
1) Can you restate the main runtime claim with explicit dependence on d and h without the “r polylog” assumption, and quantify the processor and data requirements for typical d, ε, Δ? 
2) How sensitive are results to the δ ≤ 1/(2r) condition? Please report per-leaf δ estimates as h varies. 
3) Can you add baselines with robust aggregation (geometric median, MoM) and with constrained processor counts c < r^h? 
4) Please clarify the discrepancy in processor counts (125 vs 150) and hyperparameter tuning protocols across toolkits.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The core idea is elegant and potentially impactful, but the main NC-style claim rests on assumptions that seem rarely met; the empirical evaluation, while encouraging, lacks key baselines and careful normalization; and the paper contains many clarity/formatting issues. With tightened theory (clear dependence on d, realistic processor models), stronger baselines, and improved presentation, this could become a solid contribution.
