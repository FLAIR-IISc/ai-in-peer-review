

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a novel parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The authors propose a method that uses Radon points to aggregate hypotheses from parallel learning on random data subsets. The key theoretical contribution is Theorem 3, which demonstrates that the Radon machine reduces the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis doubly exponentially with each aggregation level. The paper claims the approach can reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic in sample complexity, with the cost being a quasi-polynomial increase in data size and number of processing units. The authors provide empirical evidence of 80-700x speedups on moderate-sized datasets compared to base learning algorithms and Spark MLlib implementations.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a connection between machine learning parallelization and Nick's Class (NC) from theoretical computer science. The Radon point aggregation approach is novel and provides a provable theoretical guarantee for parallel learning, addressing an open question in the field.

2. **Practical Impact**: The black-box nature of the Radon Machine is a major practical advantage, as it requires no modifications to the base learning algorithm. This makes the approach widely applicable across different ML algorithms, as demonstrated in the empirical evaluation.

3. **Rigorous Analysis**: The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis with clear proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3. The connection between the Radon machine's performance and the Radon number (r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces) is well-established.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The empirical results are impressive, showing substantial speedups (80-700x) while maintaining comparable predictive performance to base learning algorithms and Spark implementations. The comparison to averaging-at-the-end (a common baseline) demonstrates the superiority of the Radon point approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Practical Applicability Concerns**: While the theoretical results are compelling, the requirement for rh processing units (where h is the aggregation depth) may be impractical for large-scale applications. The paper acknowledges this limitation in Section 5, but the discussion of how to handle cases with fewer processors (c < rh) is insufficient. The runtime complexity becomes TR,h(N) = TL(n) + hr³ + max{1, rh/c}·TL(n), which could severely impact performance when c << rh.

2. **Limited Scope of Experiments**: The empirical evaluation is restricted to moderately sized datasets and a small number of base algorithms (SVM, logistic regression). The paper claims applicability to "a broad class of learning algorithms," but the experiments don't sufficiently demonstrate this breadth. More diverse datasets and algorithms would strengthen the practical claims.

3. **High-Dimensional and Non-Linear Models**: The paper's discussion of high-dimensional and non-linear models (Section 5) is somewhat superficial. While the authors mention random projections and low-rank approximations, they don't provide concrete evidence of how well these techniques work with the Radon Machine in practice. The claim that "using explicitly constructed feature spaces, Radon machines can directly be applied to non-linear models" lacks supporting evidence.

4. **Comparison to Communication-Efficient Approaches**: The paper doesn't adequately address the communication complexity of the Radon Machine. While it mentions that the scheme "requires O(rh+1) messages," it doesn't compare this to other communication-efficient parallel learning approaches (e.g., distributed SGD with compression), which is a critical factor in real-world distributed systems.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of parallel machine learning and provides a promising approach with strong empirical results. The Radon Machine concept is novel, well-motivated, and addresses an important practical problem. However, the practical applicability of the approach is limited by the quasi-polynomial processor requirement, and the empirical validation could be strengthened with more diverse experiments.

## Recommendations

1. **Expand Empirical Evaluation**: Include experiments with larger datasets and a wider range of base algorithms (including non-linear models) to better demonstrate the breadth of applicability.

2. **Address Practical Processor Limitations**: Provide a more detailed analysis of how the scheme performs when c < rh, including concrete runtime measurements with varying numbers of processors.

3. **Strengthen Discussion of High-Dimensional Models**: Include experiments showing how the Radon Machine performs with high-dimensional data (e.g., text classification) and with the suggested low-rank approximation techniques.

4. **Compare with Communication-Efficient Approaches**: Add a comparison to communication-efficient distributed learning methods (e.g., DSGD with compression) to better position the Radon Machine in the landscape of distributed learning.

5. **Clarify Practical Implications**: The paper makes strong claims about "polylogarithmic runtime," but it would be helpful to clarify the practical significance of this theoretical result given the quasi-polynomial processor requirement.

## Conclusion

The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically promising approach to parallel machine learning. While the theoretical contribution is strong and the empirical results are impressive, the practical limitations related to processor requirements and the scope of the empirical evaluation prevent it from being a definitive solution to the problem of parallel machine learning. With the suggested improvements, particularly in expanding the empirical validation and addressing practical processor constraints, this paper could make a significant contribution to the field. As it stands, it's a solid theoretical contribution with good empirical support, but the practical applicability requires further validation.
