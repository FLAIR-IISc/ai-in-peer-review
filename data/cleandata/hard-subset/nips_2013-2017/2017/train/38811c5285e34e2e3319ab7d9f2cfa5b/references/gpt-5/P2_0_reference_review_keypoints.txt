- Proposes the Radon machine: train base learners on iid subsets and aggregate hypotheses up a balanced tree via Radon points instead of averaging.
- Theoretically, failure probability of an ε-bad hypothesis decreases doubly exponentially with tree height, yielding (ε,Δ) guarantees in polylog wall-clock time at the cost of more data and quasi-polynomial processors.
- Empirically, achieves large wall-clock speedups (80–700x on ~150 processors) and competitive AUC vs Spark baselines, and consistently beats averaging-at-the-end.

- Closest to chunk-and-aggregate methods (usually averaging); Radon aggregation argued to be strictly more robust with exponential confidence gains per level.
- Sits between distributed ERM and distributed/mini-batch SGD as a non-iterative, low-communication alternative.
- Missing discussion and empirical comparison to robust/median-of-means-style aggregators (e.g., geometric median).

- Theory is precise: Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 follow from Radon point dominance giving squared tail bounds per aggregation level.
- Assumptions explicit: convex loss/regularizer, Euclidean hypothesis space with Radon number r=d+2, base learner sample complexity polynomial in 1/ε and log(1/δ), and δ ≤ 1/2r.
- Polylog-time claim hinges on r being upper-bounded by a polylog function of sample complexity, a substantive restriction.

- Trade-offs are clearly acknowledged: total data N and processor count c scale as r^h (quasi-polynomial), and per-level aggregation cost is O(r^3)=O(d^3).
- In high dimensions aggregation cost can dominate when subset sizes are small; empirical section notes weak observed dependence on d but deeper sensitivity analysis is needed.

- Experiments show strong speedups and comparable AUC with proper hyperparameter tuning and 10-fold CV.
- Unclear experimental fairness: Spark baseline configuration, per-processor utilization, I/O, and data partitioning not detailed.
- Missing baselines: no comparisons to robust aggregators like geometric median or median-of-means.
- Reporting should include confidence intervals and per-dataset values of d, r, h, n to interpret aggregation overheads.

- Paper is well organized; algorithm and Radon computation are clearly explained and assumptions are explicit.
- Suggested clarifications: emphasize r=d+2 and dependence on d, provide an asymptotic summary table in (ε,Δ,d,h), clarify exact cluster size used, and expand on when low-d embeddings preserve the learning target.

- Novel idea: using Radon points for distributed aggregation with doubly-exponential confidence amplification is conceptually interesting and contrasts with iterative distributed methods.
- Novelty would be strengthened by situating and comparing against robust estimators/median-of-means approaches.

- Conceptually significant: simple, low-communication black-box parallelization with provable confidence amplification and strong practical speedups.
- Practical limitations: needs abundant data and moderate r/d; lack of robust-aggregator comparisons reduces empirical impact.

- Key questions for authors: comparisons (theoretical/empirical) to geometric median/median-of-means; sensitivity to h for large d and guidance under processor budgets; quantification of communication and memory vs Spark; efficacy of random projections preserving target risk in high d.

- Pros: clear, elegant theory on confidence amplification; black-box low-communication parallelization with substantial speedups; well-scoped assumptions and complexity-theoretic discussion.
- Cons: requires quasi-polynomial processors and r being polylog-bounded; aggregation cost O(d^3) with limited high-d analysis; missing robust-aggregator comparisons and some experimental fairness/details.

- Recommendation: Weak accept — novel and well-argued with solid theory and promising experiments, but needs robust-baseline comparisons and clearer high-dimensional and experimental analysis.
