

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces a novel parallelization scheme called the Radon Machine, which achieves polylogarithmic runtime for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms. The key insight is using Radon points (from convex geometry) to aggregate hypotheses generated by parallel runs of a base learning algorithm on random data subsets. The authors prove that the Radon Machine reduces the probability of obtaining an ε-bad hypothesis doubly exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree, enabling runtime reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample size.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting machine learning to computational complexity theory (Nick's Class). It addresses a fundamental open problem: "Is consistent regularized risk minimization possible in polylogarithmic time on polynomially many processors?" The authors provide a step toward answering this question, showing that for hypothesis spaces with finite Radon number (e.g., r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces), their scheme achieves polylogarithmic runtime with quasi-polynomial processing units.

The Radon Machine's practical appeal lies in its black-box nature—it parallelizes any base learning algorithm without requiring mathematical derivations or code modifications. Empirical results demonstrate impressive speedups (80-700x) on multiple datasets with comparable predictive performance to base learners and Spark MLlib implementations. The paper also thoroughly evaluates the scheme's limitations and potential extensions to high-dimensional and non-linear models.

**Strengths:**
- **High novelty:** The use of Radon points for parallel learning is a bold, unexpected approach that connects machine learning to convex geometry and computational complexity theory. The doubly exponential confidence improvement is theoretically elegant and practically significant.
- **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides rigorous proofs for the key theoretical claims (Proposition 2 and Theorem 3), establishing a clear connection to Nick's Class and the P-completeness question.
- **Practical relevance:** The empirical results demonstrate substantial real-world speedups while maintaining predictive performance, making the theoretical contribution immediately applicable.
- **Comprehensive comparison:** The paper thoroughly compares the Radon Machine to state-of-the-art Spark implementations and the averaging-at-the-end baseline, demonstrating clear advantages.

**Weaknesses:**
- The assumption of a finite Radon number (r = d+2) limits applicability to Euclidean spaces, though the authors acknowledge this and suggest workarounds for non-Euclidean spaces.
- The quasi-polynomial number of processing units required might be impractical for some applications, though the paper shows how to "deparallelize" when fewer processors are available.
- The theoretical runtime improvement (from polynomial to polylogarithmic) depends on constants that could be large in practice, though the empirical results suggest these constants are manageable.

**Significance and Originality:**
This paper stands out for its bold theoretical contribution to a fundamental question in parallel machine learning. While many papers focus on incremental improvements to existing parallelization schemes, this work introduces a completely novel approach with profound theoretical implications. The connection to Nick's Class and the P-completeness question elevates the paper beyond a mere engineering contribution. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous approaches (e.g., averaging-based parallelizations, stochastic gradient descent), and the empirical results validate the theoretical claims.

**Comparison to Related Work:**
The paper effectively situates itself within the literature on parallel learning, contrasting with previous approaches that fail to achieve polylogarithmic runtime. It builds on but significantly advances the work of Long and Servedio [21] who posed the fundamental open problem, and connects to Vitter and Lin's [42] NC-learnable concept classes. The Radon Machine's theoretical guarantees go beyond what was previously possible with averaging-based methods, which the authors correctly identify as having limitations in achieving polylogarithmic runtime.

**Recommendation:**
This paper represents a significant advance in parallel machine learning with both theoretical novelty and practical value. The authors have identified a fundamental question in the field and made a compelling contribution toward answering it. While the requirement for quasi-polynomial processing units is a practical limitation, the paper demonstrates that the scheme can be applied with fewer processors (with a modest slowdown), and the empirical results show substantial real-world speedups. The theoretical insight about doubly exponential confidence improvement is particularly elegant and valuable.

As a reviewer who values bold, novel approaches over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field. The theoretical results are clean and well-supported, and the empirical validation confirms the practical utility of the approach. The paper clearly addresses the significance of its contribution to a fundamental question in parallel learning.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Introduces a novel theoretical approach using Radon points to achieve polylogarithmic runtime
- Solves a fundamental open problem in parallel learning (posed by Long and Servedio)
- Provides both rigorous theoretical guarantees and strong empirical validation
- Black-box parallelization scheme applicable to a broad class of learning algorithms
- Demonstrates substantial speedups (80-700x) while maintaining predictive performance

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Requires quasi-polynomial processing units (though the paper shows how to handle fewer processors)
- Limited to hypothesis spaces with finite Radon number (though this is a common assumption)
- Theoretical runtime improvement depends on constants that might be large in practice (though empirically validated)

This paper represents a significant advance in parallel machine learning that deserves publication at NIPS. The novelty of the approach and its theoretical elegance, combined with strong empirical validation, make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
