This paper proposes a novel parallelization scheme for a broad class of regularized risk minimization algorithms, termed the "Radon Machine." The core idea is to partition the data, train a base learning algorithm on each subset in parallel to obtain a set of initial hypotheses, and then hierarchically aggregate these hypotheses. The key novelty lies in the aggregation step, which uses the concept of a Radon point—a point in the convex hull intersection of a partition of the hypothesis set—instead of a simple average. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this aggregation method reduces the probability of generating a poor hypothesis doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. This allows them, under certain assumptions, to claim a reduction in runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic, at the cost of a quasi-polynomial increase in processors and data. This work relates to the fundamental theoretical computer science question of whether learning problems are in Nick's Class (NC).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Theoretical Framework:** The paper introduces a novel approach to parallelization by connecting it to the geometric concept of Radon points. The theoretical result (Theorem 3) demonstrating a doubly-exponential improvement in the confidence bound is elegant and provides a strong motivation for the proposed aggregation scheme over simple averaging.
2.  **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** The work attempts to make progress on the important and challenging open question of whether efficient parallel learning (i.e., in polylogarithmic time on a polynomial number of processors) is possible for broad classes of learning algorithms. This is a significant theoretical ambition.
3.  **Black-Box Approach:** A major practical advantage of the proposed scheme is that it treats the base learning algorithm as a black box. This makes it broadly applicable without requiring modifications to existing, well-established learning algorithms, a significant benefit over methods that require specialized distributed implementations.
4.  **Strong Empirical Speed-ups:** The experimental results demonstrate substantial runtime improvements (80-700x) over sequential base learners and show competitive, and sometimes superior, performance against highly optimized parallel learning algorithms in Spark's MLlib on the tested datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Critical Dependency on Dimensionality:** The primary concern with this work is its practicality in high-dimensional settings, which are prevalent in modern machine learning. The method's complexity is heavily dependent on the Radon number `r`, which for a `d`-dimensional Euclidean space is `r = d + 2`.
    *   The runtime of the aggregation step is given as `r^3`, meaning it is `O(d^3)`.
    *   The number of required processors/data subsets is `r^h`, which is `O(d^h)`.
    *   The crucial condition for the confidence bound to improve is `δ < 1/(2r)`, where `δ` is the failure probability of the base learner. This means for a high-dimensional problem (large `d`), the base learner trained on a small data subset must already be exceptionally accurate, which may be an unrealistic requirement.
    The authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion, suggesting dimensionality reduction as a pre-processing step. However, this feels like an external patch rather than a fundamental solution. The experiments are conducted exclusively on datasets with low dimensionality (e.g., 18 features for SUSY), which conveniently sidesteps this critical weakness. The paper's claims of general effectiveness are not well-supported without a thorough analysis and empirical evaluation of this `d`-dependency. The condition in Theorem 4 that the Radon number be polylogarithmic in the sample complexity seems highly restrictive and unlikely to hold for many important problem classes.

2.  **Insufficient Validation Against Baselines:** The comparison against averaging, the most natural and established baseline for this type of ensemble method, is not entirely convincing. The paper reports that the Radon machine outperforms averaging by at least 10% in AUC across all datasets. This is a surprisingly large margin. Simple averaging is a theoretically sound and often empirically strong baseline. Such a dramatic and consistent failure of averaging warrants deeper investigation. Without further analysis, one might suspect that this result is an artifact of the specific experimental setup rather than a generalizable advantage of the Radon point aggregation. Given the much higher computational cost of finding a Radon point compared to an average, the evidence against averaging needs to be more robust.

3.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** There is a notable disconnect between the ambitious theoretical claims and the practical experiments. The theory promises polylogarithmic runtime by setting the aggregation tree height `h` to be logarithmic in the sample size (`h ≈ ldM`). In the experiments, however, `h` is a small constant (e.g., 1 or 3). The doubly-exponential error reduction is a powerful asymptotic result, but its relevance is diminished when `h` is small. The impressive speed-ups shown empirically seem to derive more from the standard "embarrassingly parallel" training on data subsets rather than the novel multi-level aggregation, which is the paper's main theoretical contribution.

4.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The empirical study is confined to linear models for classification and regression. Claims of applicability to non-linear models via explicit feature maps or kernel approximations remain speculative. To be convincing, the paper should have included at least one experiment demonstrating the method's feasibility and performance with a non-linear kernel method, especially since the dimensionality of the feature space is the method's Achilles' heel.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and theoretically interesting idea connecting parallel learning to Radon's theorem. It addresses a fundamental question in learning theory and demonstrates significant empirical speedups in a black-box fashion on the problems tested.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The method's practical utility is severely undermined by its strong, and likely prohibitive, dependency on the hypothesis space dimension, a weakness that is not adequately addressed theoretically or empirically. The experimental validation against the crucial baseline of averaging is not fully convincing, and the overall evaluation is limited to low-dimensional, linear settings, failing to stress-test the method's primary limitation.

In conclusion, while the paper introduces an original and thought-provoking theoretical concept, its claims of being an "effective parallelisation scheme for a broad class of learning algorithms" are overstated. The methodological soundness is questionable in high-dimensional scenarios, which constitute a large portion of the problems of interest to the NIPS community. The work is an interesting theoretical contribution, but it lacks the rigorous and comprehensive validation required to endorse it as a practical and broadly applicable method.
