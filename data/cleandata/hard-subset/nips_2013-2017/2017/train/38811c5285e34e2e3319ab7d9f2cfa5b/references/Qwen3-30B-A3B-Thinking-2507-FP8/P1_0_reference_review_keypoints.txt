- Summary: Proposes the "Radon Machine," using Radon-point aggregation to parallelize regularized risk minimization, claiming polylogarithmic runtime in sample complexity with preserved error bounds and doubly exponential confidence gains per aggregation level; empirical results show 80–700× speedups with comparable performance to Spark MLlib.

- Theoretical contribution: Rigorous proofs (Proposition 2, Theorem 3) demonstrating doubly exponential confidence improvement and polylog runtime potential, connected to Nick's Class and Long & Servedio’s open problem.

- Practical impact: Thorough empirical evaluation across classification, regression, and multi-class tasks showing large speedups and comparable predictive accuracy.

- Novelty: Novel application of Radon points from convex geometry for hypothesis aggregation, distinct from averaging-based and SGD approaches.

- Clarity and organization: Well-written, clearly defined algorithm and theory, with detailed proofs in the appendix and effective figures.

- Dimensionality limitation: Requires finite Radon number (r = d+2), restricting applicability to low-dimensional problems and potentially needing many processors in high dimensions; low-rank approximations only suggested.

- Scalability to very large datasets: Empirical tests use moderately sized datasets (e.g., 5M instances); lacks analysis or experiments for extreme scales (e.g., 100M+ instances) where communication could dominate.

- Communication costs: Claims low communication complexity but provides no concrete measurements or scaling analysis for distributed systems where communication is often the bottleneck.

- Overall assessment: Strong theoretical and empirical contribution that advances parallel learning and is broadly applicable, meriting acceptance.

- Arguments against acceptance: Main concerns are dimensionality restriction, insufficient communication-cost analysis, and limited extreme-scale empirical validation.

- Recommendation: Accept.
