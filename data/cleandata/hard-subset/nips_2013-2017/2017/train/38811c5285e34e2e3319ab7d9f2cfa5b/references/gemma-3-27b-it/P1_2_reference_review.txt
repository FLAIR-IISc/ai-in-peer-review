## NIPS 2017 Review: Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel parallelization scheme, dubbed the “Radon machine,” for regularized risk minimization algorithms. The core idea is to apply the base learning algorithm to random subsets of the data, and then iteratively aggregate the resulting hypotheses using Radon points. The authors prove that this scheme achieves the same confidence and error bounds as sequential execution, but with a runtime reduced from polynomial to polylogarithmic in the sample complexity – at the cost of increased data size and processing units. The work positions itself within the context of limitations of parallelizing polynomial-time algorithms and relates to Nick’s Class of parallelizable problems. Empirical evaluation demonstrates significant speedups compared to sequential execution and competitive performance against Spark MLlib.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon existing work in parallelizing machine learning, particularly averaging-based approaches [32, 45, 46]. However, it distinguishes itself by utilizing Radon points for aggregation, offering a doubly-exponential improvement in confidence compared to simple averaging. The work acknowledges connections to parallel computing theory [7, 9, 13, 26] and highlights its contribution to the understanding of efficient parallelization of polynomial-time learning algorithms.  It correctly positions itself relative to the challenges of achieving polylogarithmic time complexity in parallel algorithms, a long-standing open question in theoretical computer science. The authors also cite related work on dimensionality reduction and low-rank approximation techniques [2, 28] to address high-dimensional data scenarios. 

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving a runtime reduction from polynomial to polylogarithmic under stated conditions. The proof of confidence guarantees is a significant contribution.
* **Generality:** The scheme is presented as a "black box" that can be applied to a wide range of consistent and efficient regularized risk minimization algorithms without requiring specific modifications to their implementation. This broad applicability is a major strength.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the speedup achieved by the Radon machine in practice, both compared to sequential execution and existing Spark implementations. The results are substantial and consistent across multiple datasets.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The core concepts (Radon points, Radon machine) are explained clearly and the algorithm is well-defined (Algorithm 1). The connection to Radon's theorem is well-established.
* **Addresses Important Problem:** The work tackles a fundamental challenge in machine learning: scaling algorithms to handle ever-increasing datasets, and does so with a compelling theoretical and empirical approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Quasi-Polynomial Cost:** While the runtime is reduced to polylogarithmic *in the sample complexity*, the scheme requires a *quasi-polynomial* number of processing units and increased data size. The practical implications of this cost – especially for very large-scale problems – are not fully explored.  It's not clear how easily this scales beyond the cluster size used in the experiments.
* **Radon Number Limitation:**  The requirement for a finite Radon number in the hypothesis space is a limitation. The paper acknowledges this and proposes solutions like dimensionality reduction, but these add complexity and potentially introduce approximations.  The robustness of the scheme when applied after dimensionality reduction is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Communication Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions communication complexity but doesn't quantify it. In a distributed environment, communication can become a major bottleneck, and it would be valuable to have a better understanding of the communication overhead of the Radon machine.
* **Limited Exploration of Parameter 'h':** While the paper mentions the parameter 'h' controlling the number of iterations, its impact on performance (trade-off between runtime and accuracy) isn't deeply investigated in the experiments.
* **Discussion of Practical Challenges:** The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of practical challenges related to implementation and deployment in real-world scenarios, such as fault tolerance and data partitioning.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to parallelizing machine learning algorithms.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant speedups.
* The "black box" nature of the scheme makes it widely applicable.
* The work addresses an important problem in machine learning and contributes to the ongoing discussion about efficient parallelization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The quasi-polynomial cost in terms of processing units and data size may limit practical applicability.
* The Radon number requirement could be a limitation for certain hypothesis spaces.
* The lack of detailed analysis of communication complexity is a concern.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of parallel machine learning. While the quasi-polynomial cost is a limitation, the theoretical guarantees, empirical results, and generality of the scheme are impressive. The paper is well-written and clearly presented. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Expand the discussion of the quasi-polynomial cost:**  Provide a more thorough analysis of the practical implications of the increased processing unit and data size requirements.
2. **Explore the impact of 'h' more deeply:** Conduct experiments to investigate the trade-off between the parameter 'h' and performance.
3. **Address communication complexity:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of the communication overhead of the Radon machine.
4. **Discuss practical considerations:**  Include a section on practical challenges related to implementation and deployment.
