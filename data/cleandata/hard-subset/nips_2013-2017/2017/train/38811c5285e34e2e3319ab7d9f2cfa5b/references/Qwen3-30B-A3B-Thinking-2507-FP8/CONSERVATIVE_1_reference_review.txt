

# Review of "Effective Parallelisation for Machine Learning"

This paper introduces the "Radon Machine," a parallelization scheme for regularized risk minimization algorithms that claims to reduce runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic while maintaining the same error guarantees. The key insight is using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses from parallel learning runs, with the authors claiming a doubly-exponential reduction in error probability with each level of aggregation. The paper presents a theoretical analysis showing that with quasi-polynomially many processors, the Radon Machine achieves polylogarithmic runtime compared to the sequential base algorithm.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

The paper builds upon the theoretical framework of parallel computing (Nick's Class/NC) and previous work on parallel machine learning [21, 42]. While previous approaches like averaging-based parallelization [32, 45, 46] have been studied, the authors claim their Radon point aggregation provides superior error guarantees by increasing confidence doubly-exponentially with the height of the aggregation tree. The paper makes a clear distinction from prior work by showing that the Radon Machine achieves a stronger error bound reduction than averaging, which only provides linear error reduction.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The proofs of Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are mathematically rigorous and correctly establish the error bound reduction. The authors carefully define the Radon point and its properties in convex spaces, with appropriate citations to Radon's theorem [30].

2. **Clear Methodology**: The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-described and the black-box nature of the approach is properly emphasized, making it applicable to a wide range of base learning algorithms without modification.

3. **Practical Validation**: The empirical evaluation on multiple datasets demonstrates significant speedups (80-700x) compared to single-processor base learners, with comparable or better predictive performance than Spark MLlib implementations. The comparison to averaging-at-the-end (a common baseline) is particularly valuable, showing the Radon Machine outperforms it by at least 10% in AUC.

4. **Acknowledgment of Limitations**: The paper honestly addresses the practical limitations of the approach, particularly for high-dimensional data, and suggests viable solutions like random projections.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misleading Terminology**: The paper claims to achieve "polylogarithmic runtime" without adequately clarifying that this requires a *quasi-polynomial* number of processors (not polynomial), which is a critical distinction from the standard definition of efficient parallelization (Nick's Class). While the paper does acknowledge this in Theorem 4, the main text repeatedly refers to "efficient parallelization" without this crucial qualification, potentially misleading readers.

2. **Overstated Practical Significance**: The claimed speedup of 721x on 150 processors (721/150 ≈ 4.8x speedup per processor) is extremely high for a parallel algorithm. While theoretically possible, such efficiency is rarely achieved in practice due to communication overhead and load balancing issues. The paper doesn't adequately address these practical concerns.

3. **Limited Empirical Scope**: The empirical evaluation focuses on relatively small datasets (up to 5 million instances) and doesn't test the method on truly large-scale, high-dimensional datasets where the Radon number limitation would become most acute. The comparison to Spark MLlib is valuable, but the paper doesn't benchmark against other recent parallelization techniques like those based on distributed stochastic gradient descent [34].

4. **Radon Number Assumption**: The paper assumes the Radon number r = d+2 for Euclidean spaces, but doesn't address how this assumption holds for non-Euclidean spaces or non-linear models, despite claiming broader applicability in Section 5.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to parallel machine learning with a mathematically sound approach. The Radon point aggregation technique does provide a genuine theoretical advantage over averaging-based approaches by achieving a doubly-exponential reduction in error probability. However, the practical significance of the work is limited by the quasi-polynomial processor requirement, which is not feasible for many real-world applications.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Clearer distinction between "polylogarithmic runtime with quasi-polynomial processors" and "efficient parallelization" (Nick's Class)
2. More realistic evaluation on high-dimensional datasets where the Radon number limitation is most problematic
3. Comparison to recent parallel algorithms beyond Spark MLlib

The theoretical contribution is solid, but the practical applicability is limited. The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to the understanding of parallel learning, but the claims about "effective" parallelization need to be tempered with the quasi-polynomial processor requirement.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the theoretical contribution is sound, the paper overstates the practical significance of the results by not adequately addressing the quasi-polynomial processor requirement. The claimed speedups are potentially misleading without proper context about the processor count needed. The paper would be better suited for a theoretical computer science venue than NIPS, as it makes a marginal contribution to practical machine learning despite its interesting theoretical insights. The empirical validation is promising but insufficient to overcome the theoretical limitations and overstatements in the paper.
