Summary
The paper proposes the Radon machine, a black-box parallelization scheme for consistent, convex regularized risk minimization in finite-dimensional Euclidean parameter spaces. The algorithm partitions data into rh independent subsets, trains the base learner L on each subset in parallel, and aggregates hypotheses up a tree by repeatedly computing Radon points of r hypotheses at each internal node. The core theoretical result shows a doubly-exponential reduction in the probability of producing an ε-bad hypothesis with tree height h: if the base learner has failure probability δ, the aggregated model’s failure probability is bounded by (rδ)^(2^h). Under a stylized sample-complexity model NL0(ε,δ) = (αε + βε log(1/δ))^k and polynomial runtime TL(n) = O(n^κ), choosing h ≈ log M − log log M yields polylogarithmic runtime in M (the sample size needed to achieve the same (ε,Δ) guarantee sequentially), assuming quasi-polynomially many processors (c = r^h) and Radon number r = d+2. Empirically, with up to 150 processors on Spark, the method obtains 80–700× speedups over the base learners and competitive AUC vs MLlib baselines, with runtimes 15–85% of MLlib and better robustness than simple model averaging.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to classic NC/parallel complexity (Kruskal et al.; Vitter & Lin) and the open question of polylog-time parallel learning (Long & Servedio). It contrasts with averaging-at-the-end approaches (e.g., Zhang et al., Rosenblatt & Nadler) by using Radon point aggregation, and with distributed optimization/SGD (e.g., CoCoA, ADMM, distributed minibatch SGD) which typically require many synchronization rounds and cannot achieve polylog time. The use of Radon points builds on computational geometry (Radon’s theorem; Clarkson et al.) and centerpoint depth ideas.

Quality
– Theory: Proposition 2 and Theorem 3 are clear and technically sound. The key insight—that a Radon point must be dominated by at least two points in the set for any quasiconvex Q—yields the (rδ)^2 bound via a union bound and composes cleanly across levels. The runtime discussion decomposes base learning time and aggregation cost O(hr^3), and the choice of h to equalize guarantees with the sequential learner is well motivated. Assumptions (finite-dim convex F with finite Radon number; base sample complexity form; δ ≤ 1/2r) are explicit.
– Empirics: Experiments on several large datasets show substantial wall-clock gains and competitive AUC relative to Spark, and superiority to simple model averaging. Code availability is a plus. However, the evaluation lacks error bars/variance, and details on hyperparameter tuning and system configuration that would allow reproduction and fairness checks (e.g., whether base learners are multithreaded, degree of data skew, I/O effects, cluster heterogeneity).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The algorithm is simple to understand; the theoretical statements are easy to follow. A few clarifications would help:
- Message size in the communication complexity section is stated as O(r); for linear models it should scale with parameter dimension d (≈ r−2).
- The assumption NL0(ε,δ) form and derivations for VC/Rademacher classes are deferred; a succinct inline explanation would help.
- Precisely state independence assumptions across levels (the partitioning at each aggregation stage uses disjoint and thus independent subsets, but a sentence making this explicit would strengthen Theorem 3’s applicability).

Originality
Using iterated Radon points as an aggregation operator to amplify confidence for black-box parallel learning is, to my knowledge, novel in ML. The double-exponential confidence amplification is a striking property that averaging lacks, and the connection to centerpoint depth is interesting. The positioning relative to model averaging and distributed optimization is well referenced and distinguishes the contribution.

Significance
The work advances our understanding of when polylogarithmic-time parallelization (in the NC spirit) is feasible for learning, given abundant data and many processors. Practically, the scheme is attractive as a black-box approach with low communication and strong robustness to outliers among weak models. That said, the reliance on quasi-polynomial processors and a small Radon number (hence modest d) limits direct applicability in high-dimensional settings; the discussion of random features/projections is reasonable but not analyzed theoretically. Empirical gains over strong, tuned distributed solvers in truly large-scale or high-d regimes remain to be demonstrated.

Strengths
- Clean, general, black-box parallelization scheme for convex ERM.
- Provable double-exponential reduction in failure probability via Radon aggregation; simple proofs.
- Clear runtime/sample complexity trade-offs and explicit dependence on r and h.
- Solid empirical speedups and competitive accuracy; code released.
- Good contextualization within NC and distributed learning literature.

Weaknesses
- Requires c = r^h processors; with r = d+2 this explodes in moderate/high dimensions.
- δ ≤ 1/2r assumption may force large subset sizes; practical guidance on achieving this is limited.
- Aggregation cost scales as O(h r^3); for d in the hundreds/thousands this may dominate.
- Empirical evaluation lacks error bars and stronger baselines (e.g., well-tuned CoCoA/ADMM or parameter-server-style model averaging with proximal updates).
- The effect of dimensionality reduction on guarantees is not analyzed; the message size/dimension point is confusing.
- Comparisons sometimes pit single-threaded base learners vs a parallel cluster; fairness caveats should be explicit.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to violations of δ ≤ 1/2r? Can you provide empirical curves of Δ vs h for realistic base learner δ on your datasets?
2) What is the largest d for which you found the aggregation overhead acceptable? Any experiments with d in the 10^3–10^4 range?
3) Can the Radon aggregation be replaced with approximate centerpoint methods to reduce r-dependence?
4) Could you report confidence intervals for AUC and provide per-fold runtimes to assess variability and fairness?

Suggestions
- Clarify communication/message size scaling with d, and include an end-to-end communication and serialization cost in experiments.
- Add experiments with random features/Nyström to show feasibility at higher d, and discuss how the embedding affects αε,βε and r.
- Include error bars and ablation on h (e.g., h = 0,1,2,3) to show the robustness/accuracy trade-off.
- Compare against stronger distributed ERM baselines (e.g., CoCoA variants) with tuned parameters.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a novel, theoretically elegant aggregation mechanism that yields unusually strong confidence amplification and demonstrates promising practical speedups. The main concern is practicality in higher dimensions and the reliance on many processors; expanding the empirical evaluation and clarifying system-level details would strengthen the case. Overall, this is a meaningful step toward principled, black-box parallel learning.
