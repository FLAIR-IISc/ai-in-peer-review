- Proposes the Radon machine: train base learners on iid subsets in parallel and aggregate via iterated Radon points along a tree.
- Main theoretical claim: one Radon aggregation gives P(Q(r)>ε) ≤ (rδ)²; h levels give P(Q(r_h)>ε) ≤ (rδ)^(2^h).
- Under polynomial sample/runtime base-learner model, picking h ≈ log M − log log M yields polylog runtime in M using r^h parallel workers.
- Empirically reports 80–700× speedups over single-core base learners and comparable or better AUC and runtime than Spark MLlib on several datasets.
- Builds on geometric Radon-theorem ideas and differs from averaging/distributed SGD by achieving doubly-exponential confidence amplification.
- Key probabilistic bound and composition across levels are simple, sound, and compose cleanly.
- Runtime accounting separates base learning TL(n) and aggregation O(hr³); choosing h makes n ≈ log M explicit for polylog M dependence when r=O(polylog M).
- Analysis assumes hypotheses from disjoint iid subsets behave as iid draws from a common P; this independence/uniform-generalization assumption should be stated explicitly.
- Requires δ ≤ 1/(2r) for base learners, which may demand large n and leads to substantial total sample growth since total samples scale as r^h·n.
- Processor requirement c = r^h is quasi-polynomial in M (not polynomial); the paper is transparent but should temper NC-related claims.
- Aggregation cost per node is O(r³)=O((d+2)³); high dimensionality sharply increases computational, sample, and processor costs.
- Suggested remedies for large d (random projections, low-rank embeddings, block-coordinate variants) are plausible but unevaluated.
- Empirical comparisons strongest vs single-core; comparisons to well-optimized multi-core/distributed solvers are limited and would strengthen the paper.
- Experiments mostly used h=1 (or limited by ≥100 samples per shard); need to show how accuracy/runtime scale with h and whether confidence amplification appears empirically for h>1.
- Paper is generally well-written; minor clarity fixes: define “ld”, state independence assumption, show where δ≤1/(2r) is used, and summarize Appendix A.1 conditions in main text.
- Using iterated Radon points for model aggregation and achieving double-exponential confidence reduction is a novel, original contribution.
- Theoretically interesting framing that sidesteps some averaging/SGD limits; practically simple with low communication but strongest gains require small d and large processor/data budgets.
- Pros: novel aggregation with clean theory, black-box applicability, low communication, practical speedups. Cons: needs quasi-polynomial processors for polylog runtime, scales poorly with d, and empirical baselines and h exploration are limited.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — promising theoretical and practical contribution but needs more empirical comparison and analysis for high-dimensional regimes.
