- Proposes the "Radon machine," a novel parallelization scheme using Radon points to aggregate hypotheses from random subsets of data.  
- Claims theoretical reduction of runtime from polynomial to polylogarithmic with maintained confidence and error bounds.  
- Builds on averaging-based and iterative Radon-point methods, distinguishing itself by faster (doubly exponential) aggregation convergence.  
- Positions work within parallel computing literature (e.g., NC) and contrasts with SGD and distributed optimization approaches.  
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous analysis and Theorem 4 showing potential for polylogarithmic runtime.  
- Black-box applicability: can be applied to many consistent, efficient learning algorithms without modifying them.  
- Empirical validation: experiments show significant speedups over sequential baselines and Spark MLlib while retaining comparable performance.  
- Clear presentation and well-organized exposition, with a concise Algorithm 1 and helpful roadmap.  
- Authors explicitly discuss limitations and propose mitigation strategies (dimensionality reduction, block coordinate descent).  
- Practical concern: requires a quasi-polynomial number of processing units, which may be a major barrier in real deployments.  
- Performance depends on the Radon number of the hypothesis space; large Radon numbers could hurt aggregation efficiency.  
- Experimental scope limited: mainly simple datasets and linear models; needs more complex models and higher-dimensional experiments.  
- Communication complexity is under-analyzed; more detail on communication overhead in distributed settings is needed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, broadly applicable black-box method with strong empirical speedups.  
- Arguments against acceptance: high processor requirements, dependency on Radon number, and limited experimental breadth.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant potential despite practical limitations; merits acceptance.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: analyze runtime vs. processor-count trade-offs in more detail.  
- Requested revisions: further discuss Radon-number implications across hypothesis spaces and mitigation strategies.  
- Requested revisions: expand experiments to include more complex models and higher-dimensional datasets.  
- Requested revisions: provide a more detailed analysis of communication complexity for parallel aggregation.
