

# Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

This paper proposes a method for identifying the "intrinsic group additive structure" in nonparametric regression, building on the work of [11]. The authors define a minimal group structure that can represent a function as a sum of group-specific nonparametric functions, and propose a method based on RKHS complexity measures to identify this structure. The approach involves minimizing a penalized risk that combines fitting error, RKHS norm, and a structural penalty based on covering numbers.

The paper has several strengths. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear definitions of group additive structures, intrinsic structures, and the complexity measure derived from covering numbers. The consistency results (Theorem 4) provide theoretical justification for the approach. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation and a logical progression from problem definition to theoretical results to experimental validation.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's novelty, practicality, and experimental validation that prevent me from recommending acceptance.

**Novelty concerns:** The paper's central claim of addressing "two important issues not addressed" in [11] is questionable. The concept of minimal additive structures has been explored in the literature (e.g., in functional ANOVA models [4,13] and sparse additive models [5,6]). The authors' "intrinsic" structure is essentially the minimal additive representation, which is a standard concept in additive model theory. The use of covering numbers as a complexity measure is not novel in RKHS theory (see [18, 14, 1]) and the paper fails to distinguish its approach from existing work that uses similar complexity measures.

**Practicality concerns:** The computational approach is severely limited. The exhaustive search method (Algorithm 1) is computationally infeasible for even moderately large p (the paper's simulations use p=5-6, where the curse of dimensionality is not a serious issue). The stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is described as "greedy" with no guarantees of finding the optimal structure. The authors acknowledge this limitation but provide no meaningful solution for high-dimensional settings, which is the main motivation for the paper. In contrast, established methods like sparse additive models [5] use L1 regularization that scales efficiently to high dimensions.

**Experimental validation concerns:** The simulation studies are limited to very low-dimensional settings (p=5-6), where the curse of dimensionality is not a significant concern. The paper doesn't demonstrate the method's advantage over simpler approaches like standard additive models or sparse additive models. The real data application on Boston Housing (p=13) is superficial and doesn't provide clear evidence that the method outperforms established alternatives. The paper fails to compare with relevant baselines such as [5] or [6], which are directly related to the problem.

**Theoretical concerns:** The consistency results rely on strong assumptions (universal kernels, specific properties of covering numbers) that may not hold in practice. The paper doesn't provide practical guidance for selecting the tuning parameters (µ and α), which are critical for the method's performance. The theoretical results don't translate to clear practical advantages over existing methods.

The claim that "the group additive model can maintain the estimation efficiency and interpretability of the original additive model while avoiding the problem of a high order model" is not sufficiently supported. The paper doesn't demonstrate that the proposed method achieves better performance than standard additive models in high-dimensional settings, which is the main motivation for the approach.

The most concerning aspect is the lack of thorough experimental validation. The paper presents results for only five synthetic models with small dimensionality, without comparing with established methods that solve similar problems. The absence of a clear advantage over simpler, more established approaches undermines the paper's contribution.

I appreciate the theoretical rigor of the paper, but the lack of practical justification, limited experimental validation, and questionable novelty make it difficult to recommend acceptance. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:

1. A thorough comparison with established methods for identifying additive structures (e.g., [5, 6])
2. Experiments on larger datasets where the curse of dimensionality is a real concern
3. A clearer justification for why the proposed complexity measure is superior to simpler alternatives
4. A discussion of computational limitations and potential extensions for high-dimensional settings

Given the current state of the paper, I recommend rejection. While the theoretical framework is sound, the proposed method does not offer sufficient practical advantages over established approaches to justify its novelty and complexity. The paper presents an interesting theoretical extension but fails to demonstrate its practical utility in the high-dimensional settings it aims to address.
