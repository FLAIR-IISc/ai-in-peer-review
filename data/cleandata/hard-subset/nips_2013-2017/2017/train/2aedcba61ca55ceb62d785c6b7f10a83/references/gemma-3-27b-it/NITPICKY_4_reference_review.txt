This paper addresses the problem of nonparametric regression in high dimensions by leveraging group additive structures. The core idea is to identify the “intrinsic” group structure, minimizing complexity while maintaining representational power. The authors propose a penalty term based on covering numbers of RKHSs to achieve this, and develop both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms for structure identification. While the motivation is sound and the problem is relevant, the paper suffers from numerous issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to potentially flawed theoretical justifications and a lack of rigorous experimental validation.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Addressing the curse of dimensionality in nonparametric regression is a crucial research area.
*   **Sound Motivation:** The concept of group additive structures offers a balance between the flexibility of nonparametric methods and the interpretability of additive models.
*   **Novelty (Potential):** The use of covering numbers as a complexity measure for group structures is a potentially interesting idea, although its practical benefit is unclear (see below).

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Lack of Rigor:** Several notational choices are perplexing and hinder readability. For instance, the notation "f̂ , Ĝ" for the joint optimization target in the introduction is unconventional and confusing. The definition of intrinsic group additive structure, while attempting rigor, feels unnecessarily complex and its connection to practical identifiability isn’t fully articulated.
*   **Theoretical Justification Questionable:** The connection between the covering number complexity and the practical performance of the algorithm is tenuous. The theoretical consistency results rely on assumptions (e.g., universal kernels) that are not thoroughly justified or explored in the experimental section. The statement that a universal kernel “allows f̂λ,G∗ to not only achieve unbiasedness but also recover the group additive structure” is a sweeping generalization and requires more detailed justification.
*   **Complexity Measure Concerns:** The proposed complexity measure, while mathematically well-defined, is computationally expensive and potentially insensitive to crucial structural differences. The practical impact of the α parameter, governing the covering number calculation, isn’t clearly explained. How sensitive are the results to its choice?
*   **Experimental Evaluation Insufficient:** The simulation studies are limited in scope and don't demonstrate robust performance. The reported frequencies of identifying the true structure aren’t consistently high, and the comparison to other methods (e.g., functional ANOVA, ESP-KRR) is missing. The real data example, while providing some intuition, lacks a quantitative evaluation. The claim about patterns observed in the plots is subjective and lacks statistical support.
*   **Algorithm 2 – Stepwise Approach Concerns:** The backward stepwise algorithm has a high probability of getting stuck in local optima, especially when the number of variables is large. The paper doesn’t discuss strategies to mitigate this issue.
*   **Minor Issues:** Numerous minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent spacing, missing citations) detract from the overall polish. The writing is often verbose and could benefit from more concise phrasing.  The citation style appears inconsistent.

**Detailed Comments & Specific Points:**

*   **Section 2.1:** The definition of the partial order relation "≤" for group additive structures is unnecessarily abstract. It’s difficult to grasp its practical implications.
*   **Equation (6):** The dependence of f̂ on µ and α is not explicitly stated in the notation, leading to potential confusion.
*   **Section 3 (Theory):** The proof sketches lack sufficient detail. Key steps are glossed over, making it difficult to assess the validity of the claims. The assumptions underlying the consistency results need to be clearly stated and justified.
*   **Section 4 (Simulation):** The choice of models is limited. More diverse scenarios, including those with stronger interactions, should be considered. A comparison with existing methods is crucial to demonstrate the advantages of GASI. The selection of λ and its influence on the results are not discussed.
*   **Section 5 (Real Data):** The choice of the Boston Housing dataset is arbitrary. The interpretation of the results is subjective and lacks quantitative validation.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The problem is relevant and the motivation is strong.
*   The idea of using complexity measures for group structure selection is novel.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification is questionable and lacks rigor.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The writing is often unclear and the paper contains numerous minor errors.
*   The algorithm may suffer from the pitfalls of a greedy stepwise search.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite addressing a relevant problem and presenting a potentially novel approach, the paper is riddled with technical and presentational flaws. The theoretical results are not fully justified, the experimental evaluation is limited, and the writing is often unclear. While the idea of using complexity measures for group structure selection is intriguing, its practical benefits remain unconvincing. I recommend **rejection**. Significant revisions addressing the points outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors should focus on strengthening the theoretical foundation, conducting more comprehensive experiments, and improving the clarity of the presentation.
