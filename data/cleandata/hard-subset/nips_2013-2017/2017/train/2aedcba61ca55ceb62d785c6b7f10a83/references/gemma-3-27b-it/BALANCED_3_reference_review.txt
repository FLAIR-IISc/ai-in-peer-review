This paper addresses the problem of group additive structure identification for kernel nonparametric regression, a valuable extension to standard additive models that allows for interactions between variables while avoiding the curse of dimensionality associated with fully nonparametric or high-order interaction models. The authors identify a non-uniqueness issue in prior work and propose a penalized kernel method to identify the "intrinsic" group additive structure, along with consistency guarantees and algorithms for practical implementation.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important problem. The connection to graphical models is appropriately discussed, highlighting the differences in scope. The theoretical contributions – the definition of the intrinsic group additive structure and the consistency result – are significant. The inclusion of both an exhaustive search and a stepwise algorithm acknowledges the computational challenges associated with the problem. The simulation studies and real data application demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed method. 

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods (standard additive models, functional ANOVA, high-order interactions) and positions the group additive model as a desirable compromise.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** The definition of the intrinsic group additive structure is a valuable contribution, addressing a key limitation of earlier work. The consistency result provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
* **Methodological Soundness:** The penalized kernel method seems well-motivated, and the use of covering numbers to define a complexity measure for group structures is a reasonable approach.
* **Practical Algorithms:** Providing both an exhaustive search and a stepwise algorithm demonstrates consideration for the computational challenges associated with the problem.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies and real data application provide evidence that the method performs well in practice.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity Measure:** While using covering numbers is a principled approach, the resulting complexity measure (and the associated tuning parameter α) might be difficult to interpret and tune in practice. The sensitivity of the results to the choice of α warrants further investigation.
* **Algorithm Scalability:** The stepwise algorithm is mentioned, but its performance in high-dimensional settings (beyond the simulations presented) isn’t thoroughly evaluated. The statement that it's suitable for “large p scenarios” feels optimistic without more empirical evidence.
* **Choice of Kernel:** The paper mentions using universal kernels, but doesn’t provide specific guidance on how to choose the most appropriate kernel for a given dataset. The impact of kernel choice on performance isn't investigated.
* **Limited Comparison:**  The comparison with existing methods (functional ANOVA, higher-order interactions) is somewhat limited. While the authors acknowledge the computational cost of these methods, a more direct comparison – even on smaller datasets – would strengthen the paper.
* **Real Data Analysis:** The real data analysis focuses primarily on visualization of the estimated functions. A quantitative evaluation of the model's predictive performance (e.g., using cross-validation) would be beneficial.  Also, the choice of the Boston Housing dataset is somewhat standard; exploring more challenging datasets could further demonstrate the method's utility.
* **Mathematical Detail in Proofs:** While theorems are stated, the proofs are relegated to the supplementary material. This is common, but reviewers may have to expend extra effort to verify the results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
* The theoretical contributions (intrinsic group structure, consistency result) are significant and well-justified.
* The proposed method is well-motivated and provides a practical approach to group additive structure identification.
* The empirical results demonstrate the method’s effectiveness in simulated and real-world data.
* The paper is clearly written and logically organized.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The complexity measure may be difficult to interpret and tune in practice.
* The scalability of the algorithms, particularly the stepwise algorithm, is not fully demonstrated.
* The comparison with existing methods is limited.
* The real data analysis lacks quantitative evaluation of predictive performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The theoretical and methodological contributions are significant, and the empirical results are encouraging. While there are some weaknesses, they are not critical. I believe the paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, with the understanding that the authors may be asked to address the concerns raised above (particularly regarding algorithm scalability and the complexity measure) in a post-review revision.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* **Elaborate on the tuning of α:**  Provide more guidance on how to select an appropriate value for α and investigate the sensitivity of the results to this parameter.
* **Assess algorithm scalability:** Conduct more extensive experiments with the stepwise algorithm on higher-dimensional datasets.
* **Expand the comparison with existing methods:**  Include a more direct comparison with functional ANOVA or higher-order interaction models, even if it's limited to smaller datasets.
* **Add quantitative evaluation to the real data analysis:**  Report cross-validation results or other metrics to assess the model's predictive performance.
* **Consider alternative complexity measures:** Explore alternative complexity measures for group structures and compare their performance to the proposed covering number-based measure.
* **Investigate kernel choice:**  Provide recommendations for choosing an appropriate kernel and explore the impact of kernel choice on performance.
