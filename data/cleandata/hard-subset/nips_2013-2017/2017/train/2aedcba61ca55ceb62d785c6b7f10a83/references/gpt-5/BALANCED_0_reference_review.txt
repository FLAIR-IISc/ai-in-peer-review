Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies nonparametric regression under a group additive structure: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of variables into groups. The authors define a partial order on group structures and formalize an intrinsic group additive structure G* as the unique minimal element (i.e., no group in G* can be further split without losing correctness). They then propose to jointly estimate f and identify G* by minimizing squared loss with an RKHS norm penalty and an additional structural penalty C(G) that measures the capacity of the direct-sum RKHS induced by G. Leveraging covering number bounds for RKHSs (e.g., for Gaussian/convolution kernels), they upper-bound the covering number of the direct-sum space by the sum of log-covering numbers of component spaces and propose a practical penalty that increases with group size. They prove that, for fixed p and with appropriate tuning, the selected Ĝ is consistent for G*. Algorithms include exhaustive search (small p) and a backward stepwise splitting heuristic (larger p). Simulations demonstrate recovery of the true structure on several synthetic models; a brief application to Boston Housing provides qualitative insights.

The work relates to: additive models [Hastie & Tibshirani], smoothing spline ANOVA [Gu], additive/higher-order interaction models with ESP kernels [Kandasamy & Yu], and earlier OKGT [Pan et al.]. It also connects (implicitly) to kernel additive models and multiple kernel learning (direct-sum RKHSs), as well as sparsity/structure selection in additive models (e.g., SpAM, COSSO) and hierarchical interaction selection; citing and contrasting with these literatures would strengthen positioning.

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Clear formalization of a partial order over group partitions and the intrinsic (minimal) group structure. The observation that L2 and RKHS spaces respect this order is useful.
- Structural penalty rationale: Using capacity measures (covering numbers) of direct-sum RKHSs to quantify the complexity of a group structure is principled and connects structure selection with learning-theoretic capacity.
- Theory: For fixed p, the paper separates “amiable” (correct) from non-amiable structures and shows that empirical risk under any amiable structure converges to the optimal population risk, whereas non-amiable structures incur asymptotic bias. Combining these with the structural penalty yields selection consistency for Ĝ.
- Practical motivation: When interactions are present but sparse and confined to small groups, the approach offers a middle ground between fully additive and high-order ANOVA/ESP models.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Theory–practice gap in the structural penalty. The covering-number-derived complexity involves ln(r/ε) and group dimension, but in experiments α is treated as an integer hyperparameter without grounding in kernel/bandwidth or ε, and µ, α are tuned by validation. The selection-consistency theorem presumes “proper” tuning tied to the capacity terms; provide explicit conditions on µ (and α) and discuss how the practical tuning aligns with theory. Consider data-driven estimates of entropy numbers (e.g., via empirical Rademacher complexity) to calibrate the penalty.
- Limited algorithmic development. The backward stepwise algorithm is underspecified (whether to split or reassign; single pass vs iterative; stopping criteria) and can easily miss the true structure, as seen in Model 4. Provide a complete procedure (split/merge, best-first or beam search, multiple restarts), complexity analysis, and practical heuristics (screening, warm starts). Discuss computational scaling (n, p) and employ kernel approximations (Nyström, random features) if applicable.
- Experiments are thin. There are no baselines (e.g., GAM with pairwise interactions, SpAM/COSSO, SALSA/ESP kernel KRR, hierarchical interaction selection, MKL-based additive kernels) and no predictive metrics. Only reporting recovery frequency under a small suite of toy models and a single real dataset (Boston Housing) is insufficient for NeurIPS. Add:
  - Comparisons on structure recovery and predictive performance.
  - Sensitivity to noise, kernel bandwidth, λ, µ, α.
  - Scalability studies with larger p, n.
  - Robustness under correlated covariates (theory uses independence in Prop. 1’s strict inclusion).
- Assumptions/clarity in theory. Several results assume fixed p and universal kernels; clarify to what extent results extend to p growing with n. Specify how |Ga| is controlled in bounds. Some conditions (λn n → 0) and constants in the tail bounds are stated but not interpreted; give guidance for practitioners.

Clarity
- Notation and presentation need polishing. There are typos, inconsistent subscripts (HG vs HK,G), and occasional ambiguous expressions (e.g., the transition from ∑ α(ε)|u|+1 to µ∑ α|u|). Define “amiable” early and relate to identifiability. Specify kernels used per group, bandwidth selection, and how λ is tuned. Provide precise pseudocode for Algorithm 2.

Originality
- The notion of intrinsic minimal group structure and the capacity-based structural penalty for direct-sum RKHSs are interesting. However, connections to prior structured kernel learning (e.g., MKL with group norms, hierarchical kernels) and sparse additive/interaction selection literatures are largely missing; situating the novelty more sharply is needed.

Significance
- If made robust and scalable with stronger empirical evidence and clearer theory–practice links, this could be a useful tool for discovering interaction groups in nonparametric regression. In its current form, the limited experiments and algorithmic maturity make impact uncertain.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled formulation of group structure identification with a novel capacity-based penalty.
- Nontrivial theoretical separation of amiable vs non-amiable structures and selection consistency (fixed p).
- Addresses a practically relevant middle ground between purely additive models and fully general interactions.

Cons against acceptance
- Insufficient experimental validation and lack of baselines; limited real data evidence.
- Algorithmic heuristic is underspecified and sometimes fails; no scalability analysis.
- Gap between theoretical penalty and the tuned hyperparameters used in practice.
- Clarity and related-work coverage need significant improvement.

Recommendation
Reject.

Constructive suggestions
- Tighten the link between the covering-number penalty and the implemented selection (derive α from kernel bandwidths and ε, or propose an empirical capacity surrogate).
- Develop and evaluate robust search procedures (split–merge, stochastic search, multiple restarts) with complexity analysis; exploit kernel approximations.
- Expand experiments with strong baselines, predictive metrics, ablations, and correlated designs; include higher-dimensional settings.
- Improve exposition (notation, algorithms, tuning), and broaden related work to MKL, sparse additive/interaction selection, and hierarchical kernels, clarifying the unique contributions.
