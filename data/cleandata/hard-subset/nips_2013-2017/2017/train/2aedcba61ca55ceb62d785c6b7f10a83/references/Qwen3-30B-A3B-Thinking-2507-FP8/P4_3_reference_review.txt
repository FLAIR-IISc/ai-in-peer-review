

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper proposes a method for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure in nonparametric regression, addressing two key limitations in previous work on group additive models. The authors define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group structure that can represent the target function, resolving the non-uniqueness problem of group additive structures. They develop a structural penalty method using a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers to identify this intrinsic structure, and propose two algorithms for implementation. The work connects to previous literature on additive models [5, 13], functional ANOVA [4, 6], and the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) method [11], positioning their approach as a middle ground between simple additive models and complex interaction models.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound theoretical framework. The authors rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure and prove its uniqueness (Theorem 1). They derive a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers and prove consistency of their method (Theorems 2-4). The theoretical development builds appropriately on existing literature (e.g., RKHS theory, covering numbers) and is well-supported. The simulation studies and real data application on the Boston Housing dataset provide empirical validation of the theoretical claims. The authors demonstrate that their method successfully identifies the true group structure in various model settings, with the backward stepwise algorithm showing promising performance for larger problems. The paper appropriately acknowledges the limitations of the approach, including the potential for the stepwise algorithm to get stuck in local optima.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively establishes the problem and motivation, and the theoretical development is logically presented. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the connection to previous work is well-explained. The algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) are clearly described, though the stepwise algorithm's greedy nature could benefit from more explicit discussion of potential limitations. Some technical details in the theoretical section may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with RKHS theory, but this is to be expected in a technical contribution. The real data analysis is well-presented with visualizations of the estimated nonparametric functions.

**Originality:** The concept of the intrinsic group additive structure is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The proposed structural penalty method using RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure represents a substantial contribution to the field. While previous work has considered group structures [11, 9], this paper provides the first systematic approach to identifying the intrinsic structure, which is a major advancement. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work on graphical models, noting that their method directly targets the group additive structure rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure.

**Significance:** The method addresses a fundamental challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression—the curse of dimensionality—by identifying an optimal group structure that balances estimation efficiency and interpretability. The approach provides a middle ground between simple additive models (which assume no interactions) and higher-order interaction models (which assume all possible interactions), making it particularly valuable for practical applications. The real data application on the Boston Housing dataset demonstrates practical utility, and the method could be applied to a wide range of high-dimensional regression problems. The work advances the state of the art in nonparametric regression by providing a principled approach to structure identification.

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous definition of the intrinsic group additive structure and proof of its uniqueness
- Novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers
- Theoretical consistency results for the proposed method
- Empirical validation through simulation studies and real data application
- Practical algorithms for implementation (exhaustive search and stepwise approach)

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of computational complexity, particularly for very high-dimensional problems
- Simulation studies are limited to relatively small-scale problems (5 models with unspecified dimensions)
- No thorough comparison with alternative approaches for group structure identification (e.g., graphical models)
- Assumption of universal kernels (Gaussian/Laplace) without exploring how kernel choice affects performance
- The stepwise algorithm's potential to get stuck in local optima is acknowledged but not fully addressed

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution to the problem of group additive structure identification.
- The proposed method addresses a significant gap in the literature.
- The theoretical development is sound and well-supported.
- The empirical results validate the theoretical claims.
- The method has clear practical applications in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited scalability analysis for very high-dimensional problems.
- The simulation studies could be strengthened with more complex scenarios.
- The paper could better differentiate from related work on graphical models and group structure identification.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression by providing a principled approach to identifying the intrinsic group additive structure. The theoretical development is sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the method has clear practical utility. While there are some limitations in the scalability analysis and comparison with related work, these do not detract from the core contribution of the paper. The work advances the state of the art in nonparametric regression and should be accepted for presentation at NeurIPS.
