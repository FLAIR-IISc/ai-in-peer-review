

# Peer Review: "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical gap in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: the identification of group additive structures that balance model flexibility, interpretability, and computational feasibility. The authors define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group partition that can represent a function as a sum of group-specific nonparametric functions, proving its uniqueness. They propose a structural penalty method based on RKHS covering numbers to identify this structure, theoretically establishing consistency and providing two algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) for implementation. The work builds on the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) method [11] but addresses two key limitations: non-uniqueness of group structures and lack of systematic identification methods. The paper relates to previous NIPS work on additive models (e.g., [5], [6]) and functional ANOVA models [4, 13], but offers a novel middle ground between traditional additive models and higher-order interaction models.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, establishing the uniqueness of the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and proving consistency of the proposed method (Theorem 4). The use of covering numbers as a complexity measure for group structures is well-justified and theoretically grounded.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively motivates the problem by clearly articulating the limitations of both standard additive models (which cannot capture interactions) and higher-order interaction models (which suffer from the curse of dimensionality). The definition of "intrinsic group additive structure" precisely addresses the non-uniqueness issue.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The simulation studies across five different models demonstrate the method's ability to correctly identify the true group structure. The Boston Housing data application provides a compelling real-world example, with interpretable visualizations of the estimated group functions.

4. **Practical Algorithms**: The authors provide two implementable algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) that address the computational challenges of structure identification. The stepwise algorithm is particularly valuable for high-dimensional settings.

5. **Clear Connection to Related Work**: The paper properly situates itself within the literature, acknowledging previous work on additive models [5, 6, 11] and functional ANOVA [4, 13], while clearly differentiating its approach from graphical models [2, 7, 9].

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Scalability**: While the backward stepwise algorithm addresses computational challenges for larger p, the exhaustive search method is computationally prohibitive for even moderate p (e.g., p > 20). The paper does not adequately discuss the practical limits of the method or provide guidance on when the stepwise approach might fail (as seen in Model 4 in Figure 2).

2. **Parameter Selection**: The paper uses a grid search for tuning parameters (µ, α) but does not provide sufficient guidance on how to select these parameters in practice. The use of a separate validation set (Algorithm 1) is reasonable, but the paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the results are to these choices.

3. **Limited Comparison to Alternatives**: The paper does not compare its method to other approaches for identifying interaction structures, such as those based on graphical models [2, 7, 9] or other structure selection methods for nonparametric regression. A comparative analysis would strengthen the argument for the proposed method's advantages.

4. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The theoretical results (Theorems 2-4) rely on specific assumptions about the kernel functions and the relationship between the intrinsic structure and amiable structures. The paper could better discuss the practical implications of these assumptions and their potential violations in real-world data.

5. **Connection to Graphical Models**: The brief discussion of the relationship to graphical models (Section 1) feels underdeveloped. The authors note that their approach targets the group additive structure directly rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure, but could elaborate on how these concepts relate and why their approach might be preferable in certain contexts.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression by providing a theoretically sound and practically implementable method for identifying group additive structures. The problem addressed is important, as high-dimensional nonparametric regression remains a challenging problem in statistics and machine learning. The theoretical results are rigorous and well-supported, and the experimental results demonstrate the method's effectiveness across various scenarios.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for the value of the proposed approach. While the computational limitations of the exhaustive search method are acknowledged, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations and comparisons to alternative methods.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound solution, and the experimental results are compelling. The limitations identified are relatively minor compared to the paper's overall contribution. The work represents a meaningful advance over previous approaches to nonparametric regression in high dimensions.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel concept of "intrinsic group additive structure" with rigorous uniqueness proof
- Theoretical consistency results for structure identification
- Practical algorithms addressing computational challenges
- Strong empirical validation across simulations and real data
- Clear motivation and connection to relevant literature

**Con Acceptance:**
- Computational limitations of exhaustive search method for large p
- Limited comparison to alternative structure identification methods
- Parameter selection guidance could be more detailed
- Connection to graphical models could be more thoroughly developed

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of nonparametric regression, and the theoretical and empirical results are compelling. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
