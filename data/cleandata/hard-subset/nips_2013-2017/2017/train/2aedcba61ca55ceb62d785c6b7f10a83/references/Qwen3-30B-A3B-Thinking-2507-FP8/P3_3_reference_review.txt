

# Peer Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a critical limitation of standard additive models in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: their inability to capture interactions between predictors. The authors propose identifying a "group additive structure" where predictors are partitioned into groups such that the response function is additive across these groups. The key contribution is the definition of an "intrinsic group additive structure" (the minimal such structure) and a method to identify it using a structural penalty based on RKHS capacity measures. The paper provides theoretical consistency results, two algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise), and empirical validation on synthetic and real data.

The work builds on important prior contributions including the additive model [5], functional ANOVA [4, 13], and the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) method [11]. The authors correctly position their work as a middle ground between standard additive models and full functional ANOVA, addressing the curse of dimensionality while allowing for interactions.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Conceptual Contribution**: The definition of the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal amiable structure is theoretically sound and addresses a fundamental issue of non-uniqueness in group additive models. This is a significant conceptual advance over prior work.

2. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides strong theoretical results (Theorems 2-4) demonstrating consistency of the proposed method. The connection between the structural penalty and RKHS capacity measures is well-motivated and theoretically grounded.

3. **Practical Algorithms**: The two proposed algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) address the computational challenges of structure identification. The stepwise algorithm is particularly valuable for high-dimensional settings.

4. **Clear Empirical Validation**: The simulation study with five different models and the Boston Housing application demonstrate the method's practical utility. The results show successful identification of true group structures in most cases.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Theoretical Assumptions and Practical Sensitivity**: The theoretical results rely on universal kernels and specific properties of the covering number. The paper doesn't explore how sensitive the method is to kernel choice or whether the theoretical guarantees hold for practical kernel choices (e.g., Gaussian kernel with finite bandwidth). The authors should provide more discussion on the practical implications of the theoretical assumptions.

2. **Computational Complexity**: While the backward stepwise algorithm is proposed for large p, the paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity or scalability of the method. For very high-dimensional problems (p > 100), the stepwise approach could still be computationally challenging, and the authors should discuss potential improvements.

3. **Tuning Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper uses a grid search for tuning parameters (µ, α) but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how sensitive the method is to these choices. The simulation results show some instability (e.g., Model 1 performance deterioration in the middle panel of Table 2), which suggests the method might require careful tuning. The authors should discuss principled ways to select these parameters beyond grid search.

4. **Comparison with Related Work**: The connection to graphical models is mentioned, but the paper lacks a thorough comparison with other methods for interaction structure identification, such as sparse additive models [6], interaction screening methods [19], or sparse group lasso approaches [20]. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's positioning.

5. **Complexity Measure Justification**: While covering numbers are used as a capacity measure, the paper doesn't provide a clear theoretical justification for why covering numbers are superior to other capacity measures (e.g., Rademacher complexity or VC dimension) for this specific problem. The authors should address this to strengthen their methodological foundation.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The concept of the intrinsic group additive structure is novel and addresses a real problem in the field. The theoretical results are rigorous, and the empirical validation is convincing for the models tested.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the concerns above, particularly regarding the practical sensitivity of the method and a more thorough comparison with related approaches. The theoretical assumptions could also be better contextualized for practical implementation.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper presents a valuable methodological contribution that addresses an important gap in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The theoretical foundation is solid, the empirical results are promising, and the approach has clear potential for practical application. While the weaknesses noted are important, they don't undermine the core contribution of the paper, which is novel, well-motivated, and well-executed.

The paper advances the state of the art by providing a principled approach to identify the minimal group additive structure, which balances model flexibility with interpretability and computational feasibility. This work is relevant to the NIPS community as it addresses a fundamental challenge in high-dimensional statistical modeling that is increasingly important in modern data science applications.

I recommend this paper for acceptance at NIPS, with the understanding that the authors should address the concerns raised in the review in the camera-ready version.
