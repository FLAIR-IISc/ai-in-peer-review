

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper proposes a method for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure in nonparametric regression, where the regression function can be expressed as a sum of functions over groups of predictor variables. The authors define the intrinsic group structure as the minimal partition of predictors that can represent the true function, and propose a penalized kernel method that uses a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers to identify this structure.

The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the literature on nonparametric regression by formally defining the intrinsic group additive structure and proving its consistency under certain conditions. The mathematical development is clear and well-organized, with appropriate connections to existing literature on additive models (e.g., [5, 13]) and functional ANOVA models. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including consistency proofs for their method, which is a strength of the paper.

However, as a reviewer who values established methods and rigorous empirical validation, I have significant concerns about the paper's practical contribution and the novelty of its approach. The proposed complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers is mathematically elegant but lacks practical justification. The paper fails to demonstrate that this measure provides meaningful advantages over simpler, more established complexity measures (e.g., number of groups, group sizes, or standard model selection criteria like AIC/BIC) that would make their method preferable to practitioners.

The experimental validation is notably limited. The simulation studies use only five synthetic models with small predictor sets (p ≤ 10), which doesn't adequately test the method's performance in high-dimensional settings where the authors claim their method is most valuable. The real data application on the Boston Housing dataset (p=13) is too small to demonstrate the method's utility in high dimensions. Crucially, the paper lacks thorough comparisons with established approaches like additive models [5], functional ANOVA [4, 13], or even simpler variable selection methods such as LASSO for interaction detection. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed method actually outperforms existing techniques.

The computational complexity of the proposed method is also concerning. The exhaustive search approach (Algorithm 1) has exponential complexity in p, making it impractical for even moderately large p. The backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is presented as a solution, but the paper acknowledges its limitations ("it is possible that the true group structures were never visited") and provides no evidence of its reliability beyond the limited simulations. The paper doesn't address how the method scales with p in practice, which is a major concern for high-dimensional applications.

The paper's claim of "novelty" is questionable. The concept of group additive structures has been previously explored in [11], and the authors' main contribution is a method to identify the "intrinsic" structure. However, they don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from related work in graphical models [2, 7, 9] or other structure learning methods. The connection to graphical models is mentioned but not explored in depth, and the authors don't demonstrate how their method provides advantages over existing graphical model approaches for this specific problem.

The theoretical results, while mathematically sound, are somewhat disconnected from practical implementation. The consistency results assume the intrinsic structure is identifiable and that the tuning parameters are chosen appropriately, but the paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to select these parameters in practice, especially for large p. The validation set approach (Algorithm 1) is standard, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that it reliably identifies the true structure.

In summary, while the paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the formalization of group additive structures, it lacks sufficient empirical validation, practical justification for the proposed complexity measure, and meaningful comparisons with established methods. The method appears to be primarily of theoretical interest rather than offering a practical improvement over existing approaches for high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of the intrinsic group additive structure
- Sound theoretical development with consistency proofs
- Well-organized presentation of mathematical concepts

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical validation on synthetic and real data
- No thorough comparison with established methods
- Questionable practical utility of the proposed complexity measure
- Computational inefficiency for large p without sufficient justification
- Unclear advantages over existing approaches in practice

This paper would benefit from more extensive empirical evaluation against established methods, clearer demonstration of practical advantages, and a more convincing argument for why the RKHS-based complexity measure is preferable to simpler alternatives. Without these, the paper makes only a marginal theoretical contribution to a well-studied area of nonparametric regression, which falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS.
