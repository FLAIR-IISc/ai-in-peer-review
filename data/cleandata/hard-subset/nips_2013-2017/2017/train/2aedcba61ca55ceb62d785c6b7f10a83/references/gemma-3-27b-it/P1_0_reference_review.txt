## NIPS 2017 Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of identifying the underlying group additive structure in nonparametric regression, a crucial step towards improving interpretability and efficiency in high-dimensional settings. Building on the work of [11], the authors address the non-identifiability of group structures by formally defining the “intrinsic group additive structure” – the minimal structure capturing the true relationships.  They then propose a penalty-based kernel method, minimizing a combination of empirical risk, RKHS norm, and a novel complexity measure for group structures based on covering numbers. They provide theoretical guarantees of consistency and demonstrate empirical effectiveness through simulations and a real-data application.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself well within the existing literature. It acknowledges the limitations of traditional nonparametric regression and additive models, citing key works like [16], [5], [4], [13], and [6]. It builds directly on the OKGT method of [11], addressing its limitations regarding structure identification. The connection to graphical models ([2], [7], [9]) is also acknowledged, highlighting the distinct focus on directly identifying the group additive structure rather than conditional dependencies. The use of covering numbers for RKHS complexity ([14], [18]) is standard and appropriately referenced.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Identifying structure in high-dimensional nonparametric regression is critical for both statistical efficiency and interpretability, and this paper offers a principled approach.
* **Formal Definition of Intrinsic Structure:**  The formalization of the “intrinsic group additive structure” is a significant theoretical contribution, providing a well-defined target for identification.
* **Novel Complexity Measure:** The proposed complexity measure, based on covering numbers, is well-motivated and potentially provides a tractable way to penalize overly complex group structures.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The consistency proof is a strong point, demonstrating that the proposed method can reliably recover the true structure under certain conditions.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies and real-data application provide evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The visualization of the estimated functions in the Boston Housing dataset is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:**  The exhaustive search algorithm (described in Algorithm 1) is computationally prohibitive for high-dimensional problems. While the stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is proposed as a solution, its performance appears less robust (as suggested by the simulation results with Model 4).
* **Sensitivity to Tuning Parameters:** Like many penalized methods, the performance relies on proper tuning of λ and (µ, α).  The paper doesn't provide extensive discussion on practical strategies for selecting these parameters beyond cross-validation.  A more detailed investigation of the sensitivity to these parameters would be valuable.
* **RKHS Choice:** The choice of kernel and its associated RKHS significantly impacts performance. The paper mentions universal kernels but doesn’t delve into a comparative analysis of different kernel options and their impact on structure identification.
* **Stepwise Algorithm Limitations:** The discussion acknowledges the potential limitations of the greedy stepwise approach, specifically that it might miss the true structure if not visited during the search. This is a significant concern and warrants further investigation.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The consistency proof relies on several assumptions. A more thorough discussion of the robustness of the results to violations of these assumptions would be beneficial.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles a challenging and important problem in nonparametric regression.
* The theoretical contributions (intrinsic structure, complexity measure, consistency proof) are significant.
* The empirical results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the methodology and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm limits its applicability to higher dimensions.
* The stepwise algorithm’s limitations raise concerns about its robustness.
* The sensitivity to tuning parameters requires careful consideration and might be a practical hurdle.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. While the computational limitations and sensitivity to tuning parameters are weaknesses, they are not fatal. The clarity of presentation and the thoroughness of the analysis outweigh these concerns.  The paper addresses a relevant problem with a novel and well-motivated approach, and the consistency guarantees provide a solid foundation for its use. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10.  I believe this paper warrants acceptance to NIPS, but further refinement of the algorithms and a more detailed discussion of practical considerations could significantly enhance its impact.
