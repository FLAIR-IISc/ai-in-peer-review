Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a group additive model: f(X) is assumed to decompose as a sum of unknown functions of disjoint groups of predictors. The authors (i) formalize the notion of a “group additive structure,” (ii) define an intrinsic (minimal/finer) group structure G* for any square-integrable f, (iii) propose identifying G* jointly with f via RKHS-regularized risk minimization penalized by a complexity term C(G) derived from covering number bounds of direct-sum RKHSs, and (iv) provide consistency guarantees for recovering G*. Two algorithms (exhaustive search with validation and a backward stepwise heuristic) are presented, with simulations and a Boston Housing example.

Relation to prior work
- Additive models and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models are classic; SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16) proposes additive approximations up to order D estimated via KRR. The proposed group additive model is intermediate: it allows interactions within learned blocks but not across them, reducing the number of nonparametric components.
- OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15) also motivates grouped transformations but did not address identifiability or provide a structure-selection method; this paper fills that gap.
- The paper mentions graphical models and block structure learning, but does not compare to methods that learn interaction structure in nonparametric regression via sparsity (e.g., sparse additive models with interactions, functional ANOVA with sparsity penalties). A more thorough discussion/empirical comparison would clarify novelty and practical value.

Quality
- Theory: The formalization of amiable structures, the partial order, and the existence/uniqueness of an intrinsic minimal group structure are sound (set partitions form a lattice under refinement). The use of direct-sum RKHSs is standard. Proposition 3 (covering number for sums) and the idea to penalize via capacity are plausible (covering number of a sum bounded by product leads to sum of logs). The high-level selection consistency (Theorem 4) is reasonable under fixed p and “proper” tuning of µ, λn. However:
  - The complexity penalty C(G): the path from covering number bounds ln N(ε, IG) ≲ Σ α(ε)^{|u|+1} to the practical penalty µ Σ α^{|u|} is heuristic. α depends on ε and kernel radius r; in practice it is treated as a freely tuned scalar in [1,10]. This weakens the claimed theoretical grounding of the penalty used in (6).
  - Tuning: Theorems require λn, µ to satisfy asymptotic conditions, but guidance is not provided; finite-sample selection via validation is mentioned but not tied to theory.
  - Assumptions: Several results rely on conditions (e.g., independence in Prop. 1 for strict inclusion; universality of kernels) that are not always discussed where used. The impact of dependent covariates on identifiability and selection consistency is not analyzed.
- Algorithms: Exhaustive search is exponential in p; the backward stepwise algorithm is only sketched (how to choose which split/merge to try, how many candidates per iteration, stopping criteria). No complexity or convergence discussion.
- Experiments: Simulations show some recovery of true structures, but details are sparse (kernels, bandwidths, λ selection, sample sizes). No comparisons to baselines (e.g., SALSA with D=2/3, sparsity-regularized functional ANOVA, OKGT variants, or interaction-detection methods). The real-data example is exploratory without predictive metrics or baseline comparisons. These gaps limit empirical support.

Clarity
- The paper is generally understandable and well-organized (intro → definitions → method → theory → experiments), with explicit equations (2), (3), (6).
- Some notation is heavy and occasionally imprecise (e.g., mixing R̂g and Rg; missing years in references; typographical issues). The stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) needs clearer specification. The role and interpretation of α should be clarified (currently introduced via covering numbers but then treated as a tuning scalar).

Originality
- Conceptually, defining and selecting an intrinsic finest group additive structure and tying the penalty to RKHS capacity is novel relative to OKGT and generic interaction models. Using covering numbers for a structure penalty is interesting.
- However, learning interaction structure via penalized nonparametric regression has related strands (sparse functional ANOVA, structured sparsity over interaction hypergraphs). The paper would benefit from deeper positioning and empirical comparisons to demonstrate distinctiveness.

Significance
- If robustly instantiated, learning block interactions can greatly reduce complexity versus all-pairs interactions and aid interpretability. The theoretical guarantee that the proposed penalization recovers G* (under assumptions) is valuable.
- The current empirical evaluation is too limited to establish practical impact. Lack of baselines and scalability analysis weakens significance for NeurIPS’ broad audience.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and identification of non-uniqueness via an intrinsic minimal group structure.
- Principled linkage between group complexity and RKHS capacity via covering numbers; plausible monotone penalty favoring finer partitions consistent with G*.
- Selection consistency result under fixed p and suitable tuning.
- Practical algorithms (exhaustive, stepwise) and some empirical evidence that structures can be recovered.

Cons
- Complexity penalty derivation is heuristic in its final usable form; key quantity α is ad hoc and lacks guidance.
- Experimental section lacks baselines, ablations, and scalability analyses; details of kernel/tuning choices are missing.
- Algorithmic description of the stepwise procedure is underspecified.
- Assumptions (e.g., independence, universality, tuning regimes) are not fully discussed; theory does not address high-dimensional regimes (p large, sparse groups).
- Writing/typos and incomplete references detract from clarity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a more rigorous bridge from covering number bounds to the concrete penalty, or justify the chosen penalty empirically via ablations; offer guidance for µ, α, λ selection.
- Add strong baselines (SALSA, sparse functional ANOVA, group-sparse interaction models, OKGT variants), report structure recovery rates and predictive performance across sample sizes and p, and include runtime/scalability.
- Specify the stepwise algorithm in detail (candidate generation, split/merge criteria, stopping), and analyze its computational complexity.
- Discuss identifiability under dependent covariates; state conditions under which G* is recoverable; extend theory to high-dimensional sparsity if possible.
- Improve exposition and fix references.

Recommendation
Given the promising idea and initial theory but insufficient empirical validation and clarity around the practical penalty/algorithm, my assessment is borderline negative at this stage. With stronger experiments and clarified methodology, this work could make a solid contribution.
