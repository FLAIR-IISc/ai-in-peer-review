### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of identifying interaction structures among predictor variables in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The authors propose a "group additive model" of the form f(X) = ∑_j f_{u_j}(X_{u_j}), where {u_j} is a partition of the variable indices. This model serves as an intermediate ground between restrictive additive models (which assume no interactions) and fully nonparametric models (which suffer from the curse of dimensionality).

The paper's main contributions are threefold:
1.  **Formalization of the Problem:** It rigorously defines the "intrinsic group additive structure" (G*) as the unique, minimal partition that can represent the true underlying function. This provides a well-defined target for structure identification and resolves the non-uniqueness issue present in prior work.
2.  **A Principled Method (GASI):** The authors propose a method, Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI), that simultaneously identifies the group structure and estimates the component functions. This is framed as a regularized optimization problem within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The key novelty is a structural penalty term on the group partition, `C(G)`, which is derived from the capacity (specifically, an upper bound on the covering number) of the corresponding direct sum RKHS.
3.  **Theoretical and Empirical Validation:** The authors prove the consistency of their method, showing that under certain conditions, the estimated group structure converges in probability to the true intrinsic structure as the sample size increases. They also provide algorithms (exhaustive search for small `p` and a backward stepwise heuristic for larger `p`) and demonstrate the method's effectiveness on synthetic and real-world data (Boston Housing).

This work builds upon the literature of additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1986) and functional ANOVA models (Gu, 2013), offering a more structured approach to capturing interactions. It directly addresses key limitations of the earlier Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) method by Pan et al. (2015), namely the lack of a formal definition for the target structure and a systematic identification procedure.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Problem Formulation:** The introduction of the "intrinsic group additive structure" is a significant conceptual contribution. By defining a partial order on group structures and proving the existence of a unique minimal structure (Theorem 1), the authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for the problem they aim to solve. This clarity elevates the work beyond a purely heuristic approach.

2.  **Novel and Principled Penalty Function:** The derivation of the structural penalty `C(G)` from the covering numbers of the associated RKHS is elegant and well-motivated. Instead of using an ad-hoc penalty (e.g., based only on the number of groups or their sizes), this approach connects the complexity of a given group partition directly to the richness of the function space it induces. This is a strong and novel aspect of the paper.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a consistency proof (Theorem 4) for the identification of the intrinsic group structure. This is a non-trivial result that provides strong theoretical backing for the proposed method and demonstrates its statistical soundness.

4.  **Interpretability and Practical Relevance:** The proposed model strikes a compelling balance between flexibility and interpretability. By identifying which groups of variables interact, the model can provide valuable scientific insights, as demonstrated in the Boston Housing data analysis. This makes the method potentially very useful for practitioners in various fields.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

1.  **Algorithmic Scalability and Clarity:** The proposed algorithms have limitations. The exhaustive search is only feasible for very small `p` due to the super-exponential growth of the number of partitions (Bell numbers). The backward stepwise algorithm is a reasonable heuristic, but it is greedy and not guaranteed to find the global optimum, as evidenced by its failure on Model 4 in the simulations.
    *   **Suggestion:** The description of Algorithm 2 ("Basic Backward Stepwise") is ambiguous. A backward algorithm should start with the single all-encompassing group and propose splits. The description "split j as a new group or add to an existing group" is unclear and seems to describe a different type of search. Please clarify the exact steps of the algorithm. Moreover, it would strengthen the paper to discuss alternative, potentially more robust search strategies, such as simulated annealing or genetic algorithms, to mitigate the issue of local minima.

2.  **Details of the Penalty Term:** The final penalty used in Equation (6), `µ ∑ α^|uj|`, is a simplification of the form derived from the covering number upper bound (`∑ α^(|uj|+1)`). The justification for this simplification ("combining the common factor 1 in the exponent with µ") is brief.
    *   **Suggestion:** A more detailed justification for this simplification would be helpful. Furthermore, the hyperparameter `α` plays a crucial role in penalizing larger groups relative to smaller ones. The paper would benefit from a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of `α` and a more intuitive explanation of its role beyond being a tunable parameter.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments demonstrate the method's potential but could be more comprehensive.
    *   **Suggestion:** The most significant omission is the lack of comparison with relevant baseline methods. How does GASI compare, in terms of both structure recovery and predictive accuracy, to other methods that can capture interactions? Potential competitors include MARS, sparse functional ANOVA models (e.g., using COSSO), Random Forests (which can provide variable importance measures for interactions), and particularly the OKGT method, which is the most direct predecessor. A comparative study would provide crucial context for the performance of GASI.
    *   **Suggestion:** More detail on the hyperparameter tuning procedure would improve reproducibility. Specifically, how is `λ` tuned? Is it selected jointly with `µ` and `α` in the outer loop, or is it tuned for each candidate structure `G` in the inner loop? The latter could be computationally prohibitive.

4.  **Clarity of Theoretical Exposition:** While the theoretical results are strong, the presentation in the main text is very dense. The expressions in Proposition 4 and Theorem 2 are complex.
    *   **Suggestion:** While the full proofs belong in the supplement, the main paper would be more accessible if the authors provided a more high-level, intuitive explanation of these results and the key dependencies (e.g., how the bounds depend on `n`, `λ`, and the properties of the RKHS).

### Decision

**Accept.**

The paper tackles an important and practical problem in nonparametric regression with a novel and theoretically sound approach. The formalization of the intrinsic group additive structure and the principled derivation of the structural penalty from RKHS capacity measures are significant contributions. The consistency guarantee provides strong theoretical validation. While the practical algorithm and experimental comparisons could be strengthened, the core ideas are excellent and represent a clear advance in the field. The weaknesses identified are addressable and I encourage the authors to incorporate the suggestions to improve the final version of the paper.
