Summary
The paper tackles nonparametric regression by introducing and identifying a “group additive” structure: a partition of predictors into groups such that the regression function decomposes as a sum of multivariate functions over groups. This sits between classical additive models (no interactions) and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models (many interactions), potentially improving interpretability and mitigating the curse of dimensionality when groups are small. The authors:
- Formalize a partial order over group partitions and define “amiable” group structures for a target f, proving the existence of a unique intrinsic (minimal) group structure G*.
- Propose a structural-penalized kernel method: fit a KRR estimator in the direct-sum RKHS induced by a candidate partition G, with an additional penalty C(G) measuring the capacity (complexity) of HG via covering numbers; select G by minimizing empirical risk + RKHS norm penalty + µ C(G).
- Provide concentration-based bounds and a selection-consistency theorem showing P(Ĝ=G*)→1 under certain conditions.
- Propose exhaustive and backward stepwise algorithms, and present simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

Relation to prior work
The proposal relates to generalized additive models and to functional ANOVA (Gu, SS-ANOVA) and SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu), but aims to constrain interactions to occur only within learned groups. It is also connected to RKHS additive kernels and multiple kernel learning (MKL) with decomposable kernels, sparse additive models (SpAM) and interaction selection (e.g., hierarchical lasso/HierNet), and to graphical models insofar as both target structure. Many of these connections are mentioned, though some important related lines (SpAM; additive/multiple kernel selection in RKHS; hierarchical interaction selection) deserve deeper discussion and experimental comparison.

Strengths
- Problem formulation is interesting and practically relevant: learning groups of interacting features yields a good trade-off between flexibility and interpretability.
- The lattice/partial order view of partitions and the “amiable vs intrinsic” distinction are conceptually clean; the intrinsic structure target is well-motivated.
- The use of direct-sum RKHS and universal kernels provides a natural framework; the idea to tie structure selection to an RKHS capacity penalty is appealing.
- Theory provides a route to structure selection consistency and separates amiable vs non-amiable structures via risk bounds.
- Algorithms are simple and the visualizations on real data illustrate interpretability.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical soundness and assumptions:
- Existence/uniqueness of G*: Theorem 1 depends on Ga (set of amiable partitions) being a complete lattice and closed under meets so that a unique minimal element exists. This closure is not proved. In general, the meet of amiable partitions need not be amiable unless additional assumptions (e.g., independence, orthogonality constraints) are imposed. Clarify conditions (distributional assumptions on X and function spaces) under which uniqueness holds.
- Identifiability constraints: Additive decompositions usually impose marginal constraints (e.g., E[f_u(X_u)]=0) to ensure identifiability across components. These are not stated; without them, component functions are non-unique and can drift across groups in estimation. This also affects the arguments in Proposition 1 and the RKHS decomposition.
- Dependence among covariates: Strict inclusion in Proposition 1 requires independence; most real data exhibit correlation. The results and G* uniqueness/interpretation under dependence need to be clarified.
- Capacity penalty derivation: The transition from covering-number bounds ln N(ε, I(HK,r), ∞) ≲ (ln(r/ε))p+1 to a penalty µ Σ α|u| (i.e., dropping the +1, absorbing constants, and treating α=ln(r/ε) as a hyperparameter) is heuristic. The choice of ε is left implicit and α is tuned over integers, which weakens the theoretical linkage. Additionally, (4) assumes convolution kernels on compact domains; the paper uses Gaussian kernels (fine) but the input domain is sometimes unbounded (Gaussian X), which complicates ∞-norm covering numbers unless restricted to compact sets.
- Selection consistency (Theorem 4) is for the global minimizer of the penalized objective with prescribed λn, µn; the practical backward stepwise heuristic is not covered. The theory also does not handle cross-validation choices of λ, µ, α.
- Rates: The bounds are relatively loose and do not yield explicit scaling of µ with n, p, or group sizes. Guidance for tuning is absent.

Algorithmic and empirical:
- Search over partitions is combinatorial; the paper lacks complexity analysis and principled scalable solvers. The stepwise algorithm is single-pass, greedy, and can get stuck (as acknowledged); no multi-pass or merge-split heuristics are explored.
- Baselines are missing. Comparisons to SALSA (D up to 2 or 3), sparse additive models (and their interaction extensions), MKL/additive kernels, HSIC-based interaction discovery, or OKGT would position the contribution better.
- Simulations are under-specified in the text (n, p, true group sizes, kernel/bandwidth choices, λ selection); evaluation emphasizes exact recovery frequency but not predictive performance nor robustness to correlation/noise. Results show sensitivity to hyperparameters and degradation after validation in some settings, suggesting instability.
- Real-data analysis is anecdotal; no competitive baselines, no statistical tests, and the selected groups are constrained (mostly size 2), which may reflect the greedy search rather than inherent structure.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally readable but contains notational inconsistencies and informal steps in key derivations. Specific issues:
- Define and enforce identifiability constraints for component functions.
- State assumptions for all theorems (boundedness/sub-Gaussian noise, kernel boundedness, compact domains, independence vs dependence).
- Make the derivation of Proposition 3 rigorous (how the HG-ball decomposes into Hu-balls and the role of |G|/ε).
- Clarify the choice of kernels per group and bandwidth selection.

Originality and significance
The high-level idea—learning partitions to confine interactions—is novel relative to standard additive and ANOVA models, and interesting. However, the penalty and selection framework leans on generic RKHS capacity bounds and is somewhat heuristic in practice. The current algorithmic and empirical development is preliminary, limiting immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen theory: formalize identifiability constraints; prove closure of Ga under meets (or state conditions ensuring uniqueness), and provide explicit scaling for µn guaranteeing consistency. Consider L2(PX)-covering numbers to avoid compact-domain issues.
- Develop scalable search: e.g., merge-split MCMC over partitions, hierarchical agglomerative/divisive procedures with guaranteed descent, or convex relaxations via overlapping group RKHS norms/MKL with group sparsity.
- Replace heuristic penalty with data-driven model complexity (e.g., BIC-type penalty using effective degrees of freedom for KRR per group), or directly penalize group counts and sizes with theoretically justified weights.
- Expand experiments: include baselines (SALSA, SpAM+interactions, MKL/additive kernels, HSIC-Lasso), higher-dimensional settings, correlated covariates, predictive metrics, and ablations on µ, α, λ.

Pros
- Addresses an important gap between additive and full interaction models.
- Clear conceptual target (intrinsic group structure) and interpretable outputs.
- Leverages RKHS machinery; provides some selection consistency.

Cons
- Gaps/ambiguities in identifiability, assumptions, and the uniqueness claim.
- Heuristic complexity penalty and limited guidance for tuning.
- Lack of scalable, principled optimization and weak empirical comparisons.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising, but the theoretical foundations and empirical validation need strengthening, and the algorithmic approach requires more robust development to meet NeurIPS standards.
