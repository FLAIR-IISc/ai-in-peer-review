

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper proposes a method for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, addressing a gap in the literature regarding the non-uniqueness of such structures. The authors introduce the concept of "intrinsic group additive structure" and propose a penalized kernel method to identify it. The paper presents theoretical results on consistency and two algorithms for structure identification, supported by simulation studies and a real data application.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The concept of intrinsic group additive structure is well-motivated and provides a theoretically sound framework for structure identification. The connection to RKHS theory and the proposed complexity measure based on covering numbers is novel and well-developed. The simulation studies and real data application demonstrate practical utility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous minor technical errors that undermine confidence in the work. For example, the complexity measure is defined as C(G) = ∑_{j=1}^d α^{|u_j|}, but α is never properly defined or justified. The paper states "α = ln(r/ )" but this appears to be a misinterpretation of the covering number bound (Equation 4). The use of α as a constant in the penalty term is inconsistent with its definition.

2. **Theoretical Gaps**: The paper claims "the number of amiable group additive structures is finite" (Section 2.3.1) without justification. For a constant function f(X), every partition is amiable, meaning there are infinitely many amiable structures. This invalidates the theoretical arguments in Theorems 2 and 3.

3. **Citation and Reference Issues**: The paper references [18] for the covering number bound for convolution kernels, but Zhou's paper (cited as [18]) does not contain this specific result. The authors should verify their citation and provide the correct reference.

4. **Formatting and Notational Errors**: 
   - "O ( n−d/(2d+p) )" should be "O(n^{-d/(2d+p)})"
   - "sub group additive structure" should be "subgroup"
   - "unif(0, 2)" should specify the interval as [0,2] or (0,2)
   - The paper references "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" which is inconsistent with standard conference naming conventions

5. **Algorithmic Ambiguities**: The backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is poorly specified. It states "G' ← either split j as a new group or add to an existing group" without clarifying how to determine which option is better. The algorithm lacks a clear criterion for group splitting.

6. **Theoretical Omissions**: The proof of Theorem 1 (existence of minimal group additive structure) is omitted, which is a significant theoretical gap. The paper states the theorem without proof, which is unacceptable for a theoretical contribution.

7. **Experimental Ambiguity**: The simulation study claims "the intrinsic group additive structures can be successfully identified" but provides no statistical evidence (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) to support this claim. The frequency metrics in Table 2 lack error bars or significance testing.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to nonparametric regression, the numerous technical inconsistencies, theoretical gaps, and formatting errors significantly undermine its credibility. The paper's claims about the finiteness of amiable structures and the definition of the complexity measure are particularly problematic. The theoretical foundation is weakened by omitted proofs and incorrect citations.

The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues. The authors should verify all mathematical statements, provide complete proofs for theoretical claims, clarify the definition and motivation for α, and correct the numerous formatting inconsistencies. The experimental results should be presented with appropriate statistical rigor.

As a reviewer, I find the paper's technical shortcomings too numerous to overlook. While the core idea is interesting, the execution lacks the precision expected at NIPS. The paper's errors suggest insufficient attention to detail in the writing and proofreading process, which is unacceptable for a scientific contribution.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but significant technical and theoretical flaws that collectively prevent the paper from meeting NIPS's standards for technical soundness and clarity.
