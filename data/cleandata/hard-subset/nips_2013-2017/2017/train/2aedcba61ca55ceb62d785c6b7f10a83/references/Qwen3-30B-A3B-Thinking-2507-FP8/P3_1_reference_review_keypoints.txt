- Summary: Proposes a method to identify the minimal (intrinsic) group additive structure in nonparametric regression using an RKHS-based complexity penalty, positioned between additive and full-interaction models.

- Strength: Provides rigorous theoretical results including uniqueness of intrinsic structure (Theorem 1) and consistency guarantees (Theorems 2–4).

- Strength: Introduces a novel RKHS-derived complexity measure C(G) based on covering-number upper bounds, linking to learning theory.

- Strength: Supplies practical algorithms (exhaustive and stepwise) and simulation studies showing effectiveness in various settings.

- Strength: Clearly motivates the problem by highlighting limitations of standard additive and full-interaction models.

- Weakness: Empirical validation is limited to small p (e.g., p=13); scalability and performance for truly high-dimensional cases (p>>100) are not demonstrated.

- Weakness: Complexity measure C(G)=∑_j α^{|u_j|} is derived from an upper bound that may be loose; choice of α∈[1,10] lacks theoretical or empirical justification.

- Weakness: The uniqueness claim for the intrinsic structure (Theorem 1) lacks clear conditions; multiple equally minimal decompositions may exist in practice.

- Weakness: Insufficient comparison and clarification of relationship to related work (e.g., Kandasamy & Yu 2016).

- Weakness: Practical tuning guidance is incomplete—selection of λ and computational cost of tuning µ and α via validation are not well addressed.

- Overall assessment: Technically strong and well-written with important theoretical contributions, but empirical evidence for high-dimensional utility is insufficient.

- Recommendation: Weak accept.

- Requested revisions: (1) Provide simulations with p>100 to show scalability, (2) justify the choice and form of α and C(G) more thoroughly, (3) clarify relation to Kandasamy & Yu (2016), (4) specify conditions guaranteeing uniqueness of the intrinsic structure.
