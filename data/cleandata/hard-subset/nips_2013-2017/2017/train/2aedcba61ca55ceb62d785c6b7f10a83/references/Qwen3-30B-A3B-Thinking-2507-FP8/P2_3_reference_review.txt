

# Review: "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, which represents an important middle ground between the standard additive model (which assumes no interactions) and high-order interaction models (which suffer from the curse of dimensionality). The authors define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group partition that can represent a function, and propose a method to identify this structure using kernel methods with a penalty based on RKHS covering numbers. They provide theoretical consistency results and demonstrate their approach through simulation studies and a real data application on the Boston Housing dataset.

## Strengths

**Quality (High):** The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors rigorously define the intrinsic group additive structure and prove its uniqueness (Theorem 1). Their theoretical analysis (Theorems 2-4) establishes the consistency of their method, which is a significant contribution to the field. The complexity measure derived from covering numbers (Equation 6) is well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The experimental section provides thorough validation through both simulations (Table 2) and a real-world application (Figure 1), demonstrating that their method successfully identifies the true group structure in most cases.

**Clarity (High):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly define their problem, motivate their approach, and present their theoretical results with appropriate mathematical rigor. The distinction between amiable and non-amiable group structures (Section 2.1) is particularly well-explained, and the connection between the group additive structure and the RKHS capacity measure is logically developed. The two algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) are clearly described and their computational trade-offs are well-articulated.

**Originality (High):** This work makes a novel contribution by introducing the concept of "intrinsic group additive structure" and developing a systematic method to identify it. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work: functional ANOVA models (which consider all possible interactions), the OKGT method (which assumes a known group structure), and graphical models (which focus on conditional dependence rather than group additive structure). The key insight that the intrinsic structure minimizes the function space while maintaining model fit is both novel and well-justified.

**Significance (High):** High-dimensional nonparametric regression is a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning, and the paper makes a meaningful contribution to addressing the curse of dimensionality. By identifying the intrinsic group structure, the method achieves both dimension reduction and interpretability, which are critical for practical applications. The approach bridges the gap between the standard additive model and high-order interaction models, offering a more flexible and efficient alternative. The real data application on Boston Housing demonstrates the practical utility of the method.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Scope (Moderate):** While the consistency proof (Theorem 4) is strong, the authors assume that the kernel is universal (e.g., Gaussian or Laplace). It would be valuable to discuss how the method might behave with non-universal kernels, which might be relevant for specific applications. Additionally, the paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of the method to the choice of the complexity measure C(G), though they do use covering numbers as a practical choice.

**Computational Scalability (Moderate):** The exhaustive search algorithm (Algorithm 1) becomes computationally infeasible as the number of predictors increases, and the stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) has limitations due to its greedy nature (as noted in Section 4). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, a more thorough discussion of computational complexity and potential improvements to the stepwise approach would strengthen the paper.

**Connection to Related Work (Minor):** While the paper references graphical models, it could better connect to more recent work on interaction structures in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. For example, the work by Kandasamy and Yu (ICML 2016) on "Additive Approximations in High Dimensional Nonparametric Regression" might provide useful context for how their approach differs from existing methods.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical and methodological contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The problem of identifying group additive structures has been a gap in the literature since the OKGT method was proposed, and this paper successfully addresses that gap with a theoretically grounded and practically validated approach. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a novel contribution that advances the state of the art.

The paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses. The theoretical results are strong, the experimental validation is thorough, and the practical significance of the method is clear. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the literature on nonparametric regression that will be of interest to both theoreticians and practitioners.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper would be a strong addition to NIPS 2017, providing a meaningful methodological advance with clear theoretical foundations and practical utility. The work is sufficiently novel compared to previous NIPS papers, and it makes a significant contribution to the field of high-dimensional nonparametric statistics.
