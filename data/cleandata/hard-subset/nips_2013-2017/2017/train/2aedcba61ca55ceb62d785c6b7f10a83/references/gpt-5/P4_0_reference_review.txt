Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonparametric regression under group additive structure: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of variables into groups. It addresses two open issues in OKGT [11]: (i) non-uniqueness/identifiability of group structures, and (ii) lack of a principled identification method. The authors formalize a partial order over group structures (refinement/coarsening), define amiable group structures for a target function f, and prove existence and uniqueness of a minimal element G* in the set of amiable structures (the intrinsic group additive structure). They then propose a selection criterion that jointly estimates f in an RKHS and penalizes the structural complexity of G via a capacity-based penalty derived from covering number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs. They provide a consistency result: the global minimizer Ĝ of the penalized empirical risk converges in probability to G*. Algorithms include exhaustive search with validation (small p) and a backward stepwise heuristic (larger p). Simulations and a Boston Housing application illustrate structure recovery.

This work connects to additive models [5], functional ANOVA and spline-ANOVA [4,13], SALSA [6] (additive approximations up to order D via KRR with ESP kernels), and to OKGT [11]. Unlike general interaction models where the number of components grows combinatorially, this paper focuses on partition-based grouping, which sits between univariate additivity and high-order interactions. The structural penalty leverages RKHS capacity measures (e.g., covering numbers) [18] and universality of kernels [15].

Strengths
- Clear conceptualization of intrinsic group additive structure with a rigorous partial order and minimality result.
- A principled complexity measure for direct-sum RKHSs based on covering-number subadditivity, leading to an interpretable penalty that prefers smaller groups.
- Consistency theorem showing Ĝ → G* under the penalized criterion, distinguishing amiable and non-amiable structures via risk behavior.
- Practical algorithms and initial empirical evidence that true group structures can be recovered in synthetic settings and a real dataset.

Weaknesses
- The penalty form and its hyperparameterization (α) are not fully specified or justified; the transition from covering number upper bounds to the concrete penalty µ∑j α|uj| includes unclarified choices (e.g., the role of ε and r, constants, and why the exponent reduces to |u| rather than |u|+1).
- Consistency is shown for the global minimizer over all partitions, but the proposed stepwise algorithm is heuristic with no guarantees; scalability and computational complexity are not analyzed.
- Assumptions needed for identifiability and bounds (e.g., kernel class, data distribution, independence of covariates in Proposition 1) are not consistently carried through to the main selection result; dependence among X can affect inclusion relations and amiability.
- Empirics lack comparisons to strong baselines (GAMs [5], spline-ANOVA [4,13], SALSA [6], pairwise-interaction KRR, and OKGT [11]) in both prediction and structure recovery, and do not test correlated designs or higher p.
- Sensitivity to λ, µ, and α is evident; no guidance is provided for theoretically grounded tuning.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The partial order and minimality definition are sensible; however, the proof sketch for the existence of a unique minimal G* relies on completeness of the partially ordered set Ga, which warrants clearer conditions (e.g., closure under meets). The capacity-based penalty is motivated by covering numbers, and Proposition 3 (covering numbers for direct sums) is plausible; a proof or citation would help. The consistency result depends on global optimization and on upper bounds that involve |Ga| and covering numbers; while correct in spirit, practical instantiation (choice of λn, µn, α, and kernel assumptions) is not fully specified. The stepwise algorithm has no theoretical support.
- Empirical evaluation: Simulations show recovery frequencies but omit predictive metrics and comparisons. Real-data analysis is exploratory; interpretability is illustrated but lacks baseline comparisons.
- Reproducibility: The optimization for (7) is standard KRR; more details on kernel choice, norm in the direct-sum RKHS, and search space over partitions would improve clarity.

Clarity
- Writing is generally clear, with a coherent narrative from identifiability to penalization to theory. Some notational and definitional points need refinement:
  - Precisely define the direct-sum RKHS norm used in (2)/(7) (e.g., ‖f‖2HG = ∑u‖fu‖2Hu).
  - Clarify assumptions for Proposition 1, Theorem 1 (conditions ensuring a unique minimal G*), and Theorem 4 (kernel class, universality, noise).
  - Make the derivation from ln N(ε, I(HK,r), d∞) bounds to C(G) explicit; specify ε, r, constants, and whether the exponent is |u| or |u|+1; reconcile C(G)=∑ α|u| versus earlier α(ε)|u|+1.
  - Specify how λ is tuned relative to theory (λn n → 0) and how α is chosen in practice.
  - Provide computational complexity of exhaustive search and of one pass of the stepwise method.

Originality
- The notion of an intrinsic group additive structure and a capacity-based structural penalty for partitions appears novel relative to OKGT [11], GAMs [5], spline-ANOVA [4,13], and SALSA [6]. Using covering-number subadditivity for direct-sum RKHSs to design a penalty is a useful idea.

Significance
- If scalable and with stronger empirical validation, the approach could impact interpretable nonparametric modeling by learning partitions that capture interactions without combinatorial explosion. Current scalability and lack of baseline comparisons limit immediate practical significance.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a rigorous proof or citation for the existence and uniqueness of G* (Theorem 1) under explicit assumptions on PX and function classes; discuss dependence among X.
- Clarify the penalty: derive C(G) from covering number bounds with explicit constants, specify ε and r, and justify the final simplified form. Provide theoretical guidance for µ and α as functions of n, p, and kernel parameters to guarantee selection consistency.
- Analyze computational complexity and propose a more principled search (e.g., submodular surrogates, branch-and-bound, or convex relaxations via group sparsity) with approximation guarantees.
- Extend experiments: include correlated covariates, larger p, varying group sizes; compare against GAM, spline-ANOVA, SALSA, OKGT, and pairwise-interaction KRR in both structure recovery and prediction; report runtime.
- Discuss robustness to kernel choice and mis-specification; study sensitivity to λ, µ, α.
- Clarify the role of universality for consistency and whether non-universal kernels affect identifiability.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean formalization of intrinsic group structure with a consistency result.
- Novel penalty construction linking RKHS capacity to structural complexity.
- Addresses a gap in OKGT and offers an interpretable middle ground between GAMs and high-order interaction models.

Arguments against acceptance
- The penalty’s final form and tuning lack theoretical specificity; some derivations are informal.
- No guarantees for the proposed search algorithm; limited scalability analysis.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks comparisons to strong baselines.
- Dependence assumptions and conditions for identifiability/consistency are not fully articulated.

Overall: promising idea with solid theoretical direction but needs stronger formalization of the penalty, clearer assumptions, scalable algorithms with analysis, and comprehensive empirical comparisons.
