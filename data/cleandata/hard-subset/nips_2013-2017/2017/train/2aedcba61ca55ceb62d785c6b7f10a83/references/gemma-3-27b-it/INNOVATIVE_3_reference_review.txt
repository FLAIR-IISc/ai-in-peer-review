This paper tackles the challenging problem of nonparametric regression in high dimensions, specifically addressing the limitations of both fully nonparametric approaches and traditional additive models. The core idea – identifying a “group additive structure” – is genuinely novel and represents a compelling middle ground between purely additive models and more complex interaction-based methods like ANOVA. The authors correctly identify the non-uniqueness problem and propose an intrinsic group additive structure as a solution, a conceptually strong contribution. The proposed method, utilizing a complexity penalty based on covering numbers, is a bold attempt to formalize and solve the structure identification problem, and the theoretical results guaranteeing consistency are impressive.

The strengths of this work lie primarily in its novelty and theoretical grounding. The concept of a group additive structure provides a valuable framework for thinking about interactions and dimensionality reduction in nonparametric regression. The attempt to define a complexity measure for these structures and use it within an optimization framework is ambitious and, if successful, could have a significant impact. The theoretical guarantees, even under assumptions, are a strong signal of rigor. The connection to graphical models is also appropriately acknowledged and contrasted.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the theoretical results are strong, the practical implications and computational challenges aren't fully addressed. The simulation results, while demonstrating the method *can* recover the true structure, lack sufficient depth and breadth. Specifically, the reported frequencies of correct identification need to be higher and accompanied by standard deviations. The parameter sensitivity analysis seems limited. The real data example is interesting but feels somewhat cursory; a more detailed discussion of the biological or domain-specific interpretability of the identified groups would strengthen the paper. The algorithms described (exhaustive search and stepwise) seem potentially intractable for very high-dimensional problems. A discussion of scalability and computational complexity is missing. The use of KRR as the underlying estimator, while reasonable, feels somewhat conservative; exploring other kernel methods or even non-kernel approaches could be beneficial. The link between the complexity measure (based on covering numbers) and the practical effectiveness of the algorithm is not entirely clear. It remains to be seen if this theoretical complexity accurately reflects the real-world difficulty of identifying the optimal structure.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The group additive structure concept is genuinely new and addresses a significant limitation of existing methods.
*   **Strong theoretical foundation:** Consistency guarantees provide confidence in the method's performance under certain conditions.
*   **Potential significance:** Successful structure identification could lead to more interpretable and efficient nonparametric regression models.
*   **Bold approach:** The complexity penalty and optimization framework represent a courageous attempt to tackle a difficult problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited empirical validation:** Simulation results are not sufficiently comprehensive and lack error bars.
*   **Scalability concerns:** The proposed algorithms may not scale well to very high-dimensional datasets.
*   **Parameter sensitivity:** The influence of tuning parameters (µ, α) is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Practical impact unclear:**  It's not immediately apparent how easily this method can be applied in real-world scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I lean towards **accepting** this paper. The novelty and theoretical contributions are significant enough to outweigh the current limitations. The paper presents a genuinely new idea with strong mathematical backing. While the empirical validation needs strengthening, the fundamental concept is compelling and warrants further investigation. The authors have identified a real problem and proposed a promising solution.  I would recommend the authors focus on expanding the empirical evaluation, providing a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability, and clarifying the link between the theoretical complexity measure and practical performance in a revised version. This work has the potential to open up a new direction in nonparametric regression.
