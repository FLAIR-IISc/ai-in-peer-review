This paper proposes a method for identifying a group additive structure in nonparametric regression, addressing the limitations of both standard additive models and higher-order interaction models. The core idea – finding a balance between interpretability and flexibility by grouping interacting variables – is conceptually sound and builds upon existing work in additive modeling and functional ANOVA. The authors aim to resolve the non-identifiability of group structures by defining an “intrinsic” structure and propose a penalized kernel method for its identification.

While the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, its execution and presentation suffer from a multitude of minor, yet cumulatively significant, flaws. These range from imprecise definitions and lack of rigor in theoretical justifications to inconsistencies in notation and a somewhat disorganized presentation. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly identifying a group additive structure, coupled with a complexity-penalized estimation procedure, represents a novel approach to nonparametric regression.
* **Potential Significance:** If successful, the method could offer a valuable compromise between the simplicity of additive models and the complexity of functional ANOVA, leading to more interpretable and efficient models in high-dimensional settings.
* **Connection to Graphical Models:** The authors acknowledge the relationship to graphical models, providing useful context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Imprecise Definitions:** The definition of the "intrinsic" group additive structure (Theorem 1) feels somewhat hand-wavy. While the idea of a minimal structure is intuitive, the formalization lacks sufficient clarity. The criteria for defining “subgroup” relationships in Definition 1 is too restrictive. A more natural definition would rely on set inclusion, not requiring *every* group to have a counterpart in the supergroup.
* **Theoretical Gaps:** The theoretical justification for the chosen complexity measure (based on covering numbers) feels underdeveloped. The connection between covering numbers and the "complexity" of a group structure is not fully explained.  The conditions under which the penalty term effectively promotes sparsity in the group structure are not thoroughly explored. Equations (4) and (5) feel presented without sufficient motivation or discussion regarding their applicability to this specific problem. The assumption of universal kernels being crucial for consistency is mentioned but not rigorously justified. 
* **Notational Inconsistencies:** There are inconsistencies in notation throughout the paper. For example, the use of *u<sub>j</sub>* to represent both an index in a partition and a set of variables is confusing. The notation *HG* for the direct sum RKHS appears only after its introduction in the paragraph before Equation (3), forcing readers to backtrack to understand its meaning.
* **Algorithm Details:** The description of the stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is incomplete. It is unclear how the algorithm handles ties or how it determines the optimal split of a group.  There’s no discussion of computational complexity of either the exhaustive search or stepwise algorithms.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation results, while encouraging, are not presented with sufficient detail. The precise values of λ and µ used in the experiments are not consistently reported.  The justification for the choice of models (M1-M5) is weak. The fact that Model 4’s results are less satisfying is mentioned, but not investigated further.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper suffers from a lack of narrative flow.  Sections feel disjointed, and the transition between theoretical derivations and algorithmic descriptions is abrupt. The writing style is often dense and difficult to follow. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased.
* **Minor Errors:** Numerous minor typographical errors and grammatical mistakes detract from the paper’s professionalism (e.g., "st Conference" in the header, incorrect use of "stimes", etc.).

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:** The numerous, albeit minor, flaws in theoretical justification, presentation, and experimental validation raise serious concerns about the paper’s overall rigor and clarity. The lack of a compelling theoretical argument for the chosen complexity measure, coupled with the imprecise definitions, weakens the foundation of the proposed method. The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient detail to fully assess its performance.

**For:** The core idea is novel and potentially impactful. The authors identify an important gap in the existing literature and propose a reasonable approach to address it. The simulation results, though not entirely convincing, suggest that the method can perform well in certain scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper addresses an interesting problem and presents a potentially valuable approach, the numerous flaws in its execution and presentation outweigh its strengths. I recommend **rejection**. The authors need to address the theoretical gaps, improve the clarity and rigor of their definitions, provide a more thorough empirical evaluation, and carefully revise the manuscript to eliminate inconsistencies and errors before resubmission. A significant rewrite is needed to meet the standards of NIPS.
