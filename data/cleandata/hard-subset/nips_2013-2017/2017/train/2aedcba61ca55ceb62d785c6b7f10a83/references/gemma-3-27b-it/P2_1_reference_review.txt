## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying the underlying group additive structure in nonparametric regression models. While traditional nonparametric methods suffer from the curse of dimensionality, and purely additive models may be overly restrictive, identifying the correct grouping of predictors can offer a sweet spot – improved interpretability and efficiency without sacrificing the ability to model interactions. The authors propose a penalized kernel ridge regression approach that simultaneously identifies the group structure and estimates the nonparametric functions within each group, using a novel complexity measure based on covering numbers. They provide theoretical guarantees for consistency and demonstrate empirical success on simulated and real data.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** The paper tackles a relevant and practical issue in nonparametric regression: balancing model flexibility and interpretability in high dimensions. Identifying underlying group structure can be highly valuable.
* **Sound theoretical foundation:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis, including a proof of consistency for the proposed method. The use of covering numbers to define a complexity measure for group structures is a novel and well-motivated idea.
* **Clear formulation & connection to existing work:** The authors clearly define the problem of Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI) and position their work within the broader landscape of nonparametric regression, additive models, and graphical models. The connection to graphical models is discussed thoughtfully.
* **Empirical validation:** The simulation studies demonstrate that the proposed method can successfully recover the true group structure in various settings. The application to a real dataset provides further support for the approach.
* **Algorithm development:** Development of both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms provides practical tools for applying the method to datasets of varying sizes.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the theoretical analysis:** While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the presentation is quite dense and may be challenging for readers not deeply familiar with covering numbers and related concepts. More intuition behind the complexity measure and its impact on the method would be helpful.
* **Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity:** While the authors present results with different parameter settings (µ, α) in the simulations, the discussion of the sensitivity of the method to these parameters is limited. A more in-depth analysis of the impact of parameter choices would be beneficial.
* **Stepwise algorithm performance:** The stepwise algorithm doesn’t perform as well as expected (specifically Model 4), raising concerns about its robustness in certain scenarios. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but further investigation into the reasons behind the performance difference is needed.
* **Computational cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm is computationally expensive and limited to small problems. While the stepwise approach addresses this, its performance is not consistently good. Exploring more efficient algorithms for GASI would be valuable.
* **Real-world applicability of the complexity penalty:** The theoretical justification for the complexity penalty is strong, but it's not immediately clear how well it translates to practical performance in real-world scenarios.

**Quality:** 8/10 – The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. It represents a complete piece of work.

**Clarity:** 7/10 – The paper is generally well-written, but the theoretical sections are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.

**Originality:** 8/10 – The combination of kernel methods, group additive structures, and a complexity penalty based on covering numbers is a novel contribution.

**Significance:** 8/10 – The proposed method has the potential to be valuable for practitioners and researchers working with high-dimensional nonparametric regression problems. Identifying underlying group structures can lead to more interpretable and efficient models.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and practical problem.
* Provides a theoretically sound and novel approach.
* Demonstrates promising empirical results on both simulated and real data.
* Offers a useful contribution to the field of nonparametric regression.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Theoretical analysis is quite dense and potentially inaccessible to some readers.
* Stepwise algorithm performance is not consistently reliable.
* Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The theoretical work is rigorous, the method is well-motivated, and the empirical results are promising. While the theoretical complexity and limitations of the stepwise algorithm are concerns, they do not outweigh the overall strengths of the paper. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions). I recommend the authors focus on providing more intuition behind the complexity measure and exploring the sensitivity of the method to parameter choices. Further investigation into the performance of the stepwise algorithm, particularly in challenging scenarios, would also be beneficial.
