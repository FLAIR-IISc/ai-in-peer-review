This paper proposes a method for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, aiming to bridge the gap between fully additive models and higher-order interaction models like ANOVA. The core idea is to define a complexity measure for group structures based on the capacity of the corresponding reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHS) and then formulate a penalized kernel ridge regression problem to simultaneously identify the group structure and estimate the nonparametric functions. The authors present theoretical results guaranteeing consistency and demonstrate the method's performance through simulations and a real-data application.

While the problem addressed – identifying interaction structure in nonparametric regression – is relevant, and the attempt to formalize a complexity measure for group structures is interesting, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing due to several key weaknesses. 

Firstly, the theoretical contributions, while present, feel somewhat disconnected from the practical aspects. The consistency proof relies on fairly strong assumptions and the derived complexity measure, while mathematically well-defined, isn’t immediately intuitive or demonstrably superior to existing model selection criteria (e.g., AIC, BIC) in the simulation results. The theoretical justification for the chosen penalty term (µC(G)) feels somewhat ad-hoc, lacking a clear connection to, for example, Bayesian model averaging principles.

Secondly, the simulation results, while showing promising performance, lack sufficient baselines. The comparison is largely against variations of the proposed method itself (different µ and α values). A crucial comparison is missing: how does this method perform against standard nonparametric regression techniques (e.g., KRR with a standard kernel) *without* any explicit group structure assumption? If the true underlying function is close to additive, the proposed method *should* outperform, but this isn't rigorously demonstrated. Similarly, comparing to functional ANOVA models or other established interaction detection techniques would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation. The simulations also seem limited in scope; varying the dimensionality of the input space (p) and the signal-to-noise ratio would provide a more robust assessment.

Thirdly, the practical implementation, while described with algorithms, appears computationally demanding, especially the exhaustive search approach. The backward stepwise algorithm is a reasonable mitigation, but its performance (as reported in the paper) on Model 4 raises concerns about its scalability and robustness. The parameter tuning process (grid search for µ and α) is also computationally expensive and not clearly justified.

Finally, the connection to graphical models, while acknowledged, feels superficial. While the authors correctly point out the relationship to sparse block structures, they don’t delve into how the proposed method could benefit from or contribute to the broader literature on graphical models.

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of imposing a group structure as a middle ground between purely additive and fully interactive models is reasonable.
*   The attempt to formalize a complexity measure for group structures is a novel contribution.
*   Theoretical consistency guarantees are presented.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Theoretical results lack clear practical implications.
*   Insufficient comparison to standard nonparametric regression methods and other interaction detection techniques.
*   Limited scope of simulation experiments.
*   Potential computational cost and scalability issues.
*   Superficial connection to related work in graphical models.



**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The paper does not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing nonparametric regression methods.
*   The theoretical contributions are not sufficiently grounded in practical considerations.
*   The empirical evaluation is inadequate and lacks robust baselines.

**Arguments for acceptance (weak):**

*   The problem is relevant and the proposed approach is novel.
*   The paper presents some theoretical guarantees and preliminary experimental results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper explores an interesting idea, it lacks the rigor, thoroughness, and comparative evaluation necessary for publication at NIPS. The claims of novelty are diminished by the lack of comprehensive comparisons to established methods. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a fully developed contribution. Significant revisions, including a more comprehensive empirical evaluation and a clearer justification for the proposed penalty term, would be required for reconsideration.
