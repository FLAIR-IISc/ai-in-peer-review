

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper proposes a method for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, addressing two limitations of the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) method: non-uniqueness of group structures and lack of systematic identification approach. The authors define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal amiable structure and propose a penalized kernel method to identify it using a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by rigorously defining the intrinsic group additive structure and proving its uniqueness (Theorem 1). The mathematical framework is well-developed, and the theoretical consistency results (Theorems 2-4) are technically sound. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with proper mathematical definitions and careful treatment of the problem space.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of empirical validation against established baselines:** The paper claims to address a "key drawback" of additive models (lack of interactions), yet fails to compare with established methods for handling interactions in nonparametric regression. The simulation studies (Section 4) only evaluate whether the method identifies the true group structure, not whether it outperforms existing methods in predictive accuracy. The authors should have compared with methods like sparse additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1990), functional ANOVA (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1990), or graphical models (Koller & Friedman, 2009), which are standard approaches for handling interactions in high-dimensional settings.

2. **Computational intractability:** The proposed exhaustive search approach (Algorithm 1) is computationally infeasible for even moderately large p. For p=20, the number of possible group structures is the Bell number B(20) = 51.7 billion, which is completely impractical. The paper acknowledges this limitation and proposes a stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2), but this greedy approach is not guaranteed to find the optimal structure (as the authors themselves note for Model 4). The paper provides no evidence that the stepwise approach reliably identifies the true structure in high-dimensional settings.

3. **Arbitrary complexity measure:** The complexity measure C(G) = ∑ α^{|u_j|} is not well-justified. The paper cites covering numbers as a standard capacity measure, but doesn't explain why this specific parameterization is superior to other possible measures (e.g., the number of groups, group sizes, or more standard model selection criteria like AIC/BIC). The paper's claim that "G* achieves the greatest dimension reduction" is not substantiated by empirical evidence.

4. **Limited scope of validation:** The simulation studies use only 5 models with p=3-5 predictors, which is far too small to demonstrate the method's utility in high-dimensional settings. The real data application on Boston Housing (p=13) is superficial and doesn't compare predictive performance to standard methods like standard additive models or kernel ridge regression.

5. **Theoretical conditions are unclear:** The consistency result (Theorem 4) requires λ_n * n → 0, but the paper doesn't specify how to choose λ_n in practice. The tuning parameters µ and α are selected via validation, but the paper provides no guidance on how to choose these in practice, which is crucial for real-world application.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a technically sound theoretical contribution to the problem of group additive structure identification. However, it falls short on the most critical criteria for a NIPS paper: empirical validation against established baselines and demonstration of practical utility in high-dimensional settings. The proposed method is not shown to provide significant advantages over simpler, more established approaches for high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

The authors' claim that their method "can successfully recover the true additive group structures in a variety of model settings" is misleading. The paper demonstrates recovery of the true structure in small-scale simulations but doesn't show that this leads to better predictive performance. In high-dimensional settings, the method's computational intractability and lack of comparison to existing methods make it difficult to justify as a practical contribution.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend rejection. While the theoretical framework is sound, the paper lacks the empirical evidence and practical validation necessary to establish its significance. The method does not convincingly address the "curse of dimensionality" issue it claims to solve, and the computational limitations severely restrict its applicability to real-world problems. The paper would need substantially more empirical validation against established baselines in high-dimensional settings to be considered for publication at NIPS.
