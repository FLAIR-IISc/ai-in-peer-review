- Paper studies nonparametric regression with a group additive model, defines a refinement partial order on groupings, and introduces the intrinsic minimal amiable group structure G*.

- Authors propose estimating G* and f via RKHS-based penalized least squares: KRR over groupwise direct-sum RKHSs with an added structural penalty µ sum_j α^{|u_j|}.

- They prove existence/uniqueness of G*, give non-asymptotic bounds separating amiable from non-amiable structures, and show selection consistency for the exhaustive penalized estimator under fixed p and other assumptions.

- Two search strategies are proposed: exhaustive search with validation and a backward stepwise heuristic; simulations and a Boston Housing example illustrate recovery and visualization.

- Strength: formal lattice-based definition of intrinsic group additive structure and its minimal element is conceptually novel and useful.

- Strength: RKHS framework with universal kernels and derived covering-number complexity for direct-sum RKHSs provides a principled route to consistency.

- Strength: Non-asymptotic bounds and an aggregation bound for RKHS sums are sensible theoretical contributions.

- Strength: Practical algorithms and interpretable real-data visualizations add applied relevance.

- Weakness: Structural penalty is loosely tied to capacity bounds; constants are absorbed into α and µ, making α effectively ad hoc and weakening theoretical motivation.

- Weakness: Theory assumes fixed p (finite partitions) and does not address high-dimensional regimes where p grows with n, despite claims about “large p”.

- Weakness: Independence assumption underlies strict inclusion results; it is unclear whether existence/uniqueness of G* or selection consistency hold under correlated covariates or collinearity.

- Weakness: Algorithmic details are sparse (representer theorem usage, kernel construction per group, computational cost, tuning of λ/µ/α, search complexity, stepwise rules and tie-breaking).

- Weakness: Empirical evaluation lacks baselines (e.g., SALSA/ESP-kernel, SpAM, sparse additive models with interactions, HSIC methods) and predictive comparisons.

- Weakness: Simulations are small-scale, mostly with independent covariates, one model poorly handled by stepwise search, and there is no runtime or sensitivity analysis.

- Weakness: Real-data results are descriptive without predictive evaluation or comparisons.

- Clarity issue: Notational inconsistencies and insufficient restatement of assumptions before theorems; transition from covering numbers to penalty needs tightening.

- Originality note: Intrinsic group additive structure and lattice view appear novel; penalty-from-covering-numbers idea is interesting but not strongly novel.

- Significance concern: Potentially interpretable and useful if reliably identified, but current empirical and scalability evidence limits practical impact.

- Question: Under what dependence conditions on X do existence/uniqueness and Theorem 1 hold—does uniqueness fail with collinearity?

- Question: How sensitive is recovery to kernel choice and λ; are Gaussian kernels used with per-group length-scale tuning?

- Question: Can the structural penalty be derived more directly as a function of |u|, kernel bandwidth, and n (BIC-like) to avoid ad hoc α?

- Question: What is the computational complexity of the stepwise Algorithm 2 per iteration and scaling behavior up to p ≈ 50–100?

- Suggestion: Add baseline comparisons for both recovery and prediction (SALSA/ESP-KRR, SpAM, sparse interaction methods).

- Suggestion: Include correlated-covariate simulations, larger p experiments, runtime reporting, and hyperparameter sensitivity analyses.

- Suggestion: Strengthen theoretical grounding of the penalty (data-dependent complexities, local Rademacher bounds, or BIC/MDL derivation) or justify α more precisely.

- Suggestion: Provide (even partial) theory for the stepwise procedure under separability or strong-signal conditions and expand algorithmic implementation details.

- Overall rating: 5 (weak reject); reviewer confidence 4/5.
