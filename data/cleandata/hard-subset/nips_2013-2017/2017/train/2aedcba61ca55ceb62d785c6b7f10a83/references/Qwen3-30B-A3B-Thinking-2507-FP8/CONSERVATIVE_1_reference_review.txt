

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper proposes a method for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, where the regression function can be decomposed as a sum of functions each depending on a group of predictor variables. The authors introduce the concept of "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group structure that can represent the true function, and propose a penalized kernel method to identify this structure using a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers.

The paper builds on existing work in additive models and functional ANOVA, acknowledging the connection to graphical models. The authors claim their approach lies between the standard additive model (no interactions) and higher-order interaction models (which suffer from the curse of dimensionality). The main technical contribution is the proposed complexity measure C(G) = ∑_j α^{|u_j|}, which they claim can consistently identify the intrinsic group structure.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the problem and makes an important distinction between amiable and non-amiable group structures. The theoretical results on consistency, while limited to a specific setting, are well-motivated. The paper is well-organized and the mathematical definitions are precise.

**Weaknesses:** The paper suffers from significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution:

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison to established methods:** The paper fails to compare against standard high-dimensional nonparametric regression approaches like sparse additive models (e.g., [1], [2]), functional ANOVA (e.g., [3], [4]), or other group-based methods. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers any real advantage over existing approaches. The paper claims the method "lies between" additive models and functional ANOVA but doesn't demonstrate how it compares to either in terms of predictive accuracy, computational efficiency, or interpretability.

2. **Unnecessarily complex theoretical framework:** The authors develop a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers, which they claim provides a tractable measure. However, this approach appears unnecessarily complicated. The paper doesn't demonstrate why this specific complexity measure is better than simpler alternatives that have been successfully used in the literature (e.g., the number of groups, group sizes, or other standard model selection criteria).

3. **Limited empirical validation:** The experiments are limited to five synthetic models and one real dataset (Boston Housing). The synthetic models are simple and don't adequately test the method's performance in high-dimensional settings. The paper doesn't report standard metrics like prediction error, computational time, or scalability. The validation procedure (using a separate validation set for tuning) is standard, but the results don't show clear advantages over simpler methods.

4. **Theoretical assumptions and gaps:** The consistency proof relies on assumptions that may not hold in practice, particularly the requirement that λ_n * n → 0. The paper doesn't discuss how this tuning parameter should be chosen in practice, which is a significant practical limitation.

5. **Lack of interpretation:** The paper claims improved interpretability, but the Boston Housing example only shows simple two-variable plots without meaningful interpretation of how the group structure improves understanding of the relationship between predictors and response.

**Critical Assessment:** The paper presents a theoretically interesting but practically unconvincing approach. The authors have introduced a new concept (intrinsic group structure) but haven't demonstrated why it's useful in practice. The proposed method appears to be a more complicated version of existing approaches without clear advantages. The paper doesn't address the key question: Why should practitioners use this method over simpler, well-established alternatives?

**Recommendation:** The paper should be rejected. While the problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression is important, the proposed solution lacks sufficient theoretical grounding and empirical validation against established baselines. The authors have not demonstrated that their method offers any meaningful advantage over existing approaches, which is a fundamental requirement for a NIPS paper.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Compare extensively against established methods like sparse additive models and functional ANOVA.
2. Simplify the complexity measure or provide strong justification for the current formulation.
3. Conduct more comprehensive experiments on high-dimensional synthetic data and additional real-world datasets.
4. Provide a clear explanation of why the intrinsic group structure is practically useful compared to other group structures.
5. Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms in relation to existing methods.

Without these improvements, the paper does not make a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The authors have not provided sufficient evidence to support the claim that their method is superior to existing approaches.
