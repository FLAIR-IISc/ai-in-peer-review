Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces the notion of a group additive structure for nonparametric regression, where predictors are partitioned into disjoint groups and the regression function is additive across groups but fully nonparametric within each group. The authors formalize a partial order on partitions (refinement) and define the intrinsic group additive structure as the unique minimal (most refined) partition among all partitions that can represent the true regression function (amiable structures). They propose to estimate both the structure and the functions in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) by minimizing an empirical squared loss with an RKHS norm penalty plus a structural penalty C(G) depending on the partition G. They derive C(G) from covering number bounds of direct-sum RKHSs, resulting in a penalty that increases (roughly exponentially) with group size, and propose two algorithms: exhaustive search and a backward stepwise splitting heuristic. They provide consistency results showing that, under suitable conditions and tuning, the selected structure converges to the intrinsic structure. Simulations and a Boston Housing case study illustrate recovery of groupings.

This work connects to (i) classical additive models and smoothing spline ANOVA (Hastie & Tibshirani; Gu), which restrict interactions by design; (ii) kernel ridge regression and universality (Steinwart & Christmann) for function approximation; and (iii) capacity control via covering numbers, VC dimensions, or Rademacher complexities (Zhou; Bartlett & Mendelson). It is related to SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu), which constructs additive approximations including higher-order interactions via kernel sums, and to hierarchical kernel learning and multiple kernel learning approaches that select variable subsets and interactions via sparsity-inducing norms. The authors also discuss connections to graphical models, although their target (functional grouping) differs from conditional dependence.

Quality (technical soundness)
Strengths:
- Formalization of amiable structures, a partial order on partitions, and the intrinsic (minimal) structure is clear and conceptually useful.
- The structural penalty is motivated from capacity control of direct-sum RKHSs. Proposition 3 relating covering numbers of direct sums to component spaces is natural, and leads to a tractable additive penalty over groups.
- Theoretical results sketch structure-selection consistency by separating amiable from non-amiable partitions via approximation error and using capacity-based uniform convergence for amiable ones.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Assumptions for the theorems are under-specified in the main text. High-probability bounds in sup-norm typically require bounded kernels, bounded outputs or sub-Gaussian noise, and control of RKHS radii; these are not clearly stated. The choice and scaling of λn and µ are only qualitatively described.
- The derivation of C(G) is heuristic: the dependence on kernel, input domain, and ε is collapsed into a tunable α. The practical penalty C(G)=∑j α|uj| (or variants) ignores kernel-specific constants and ε, so the link between the theoretical capacity and the implemented penalty is loose. No oracle inequality connects the chosen penalty to excess risk.
- Existence/uniqueness of the intrinsic structure hinges on completeness of the poset of amiable partitions and closure under meets; details are deferred. Proposition 1’s strict inclusion result requires independence of X, but it is unclear whether independence is needed elsewhere (e.g., identifiability of minimal structure).
- Optimization is combinatorial; the backward stepwise heuristic has no guarantees and can miss the true partition, as observed in simulations. Complexity and scalability (number of KRR fits) are not analyzed.
- Implementation details (kernel choices, bandwidth selection per group, centering constraints) are sparse. The behavior under misspecified kernels is not discussed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and well organized. The definitions of amiable and intrinsic structures, and the two-step estimation procedure, are clear.
- Some notation is inconsistent (e.g., switching between HG and HK,G). The transition from ln N(·) bounds to C(G)=∑ α|uj| would benefit from a more explicit derivation and discussion of units/scaling.
- Algorithm 2 is high-level; specifying precisely how splits/merges are considered and how λ is tuned would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The explicit notion of an intrinsic group additive structure and identifying it via a capacity-based structural penalty appears novel.
- However, the general idea of selecting variable groups/subsets and interactions in kernel spaces has significant precedent (e.g., hierarchical kernel learning, multiple kernel learning with structured sparsity, SALSA, ANOVA kernels). A deeper comparison (conceptual and empirical) to these is needed to position the contribution.

Significance
- If robust and scalable, recovering small interacting groups while retaining nonparametric flexibility is valuable for both interpretability and sample efficiency.
- Current empirical evidence is limited: simulations on a few synthetic models without comparisons to baselines, and a qualitative real-data example. No quantitative gains vs. additive models, SALSA, hierarchical kernel learning, or interaction screening methods are shown.
- The algorithmic scalability limits applicability to moderate p unless better search strategies are developed.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear and interesting problem formulation (intrinsic group additive structure).
- Principled link between RKHS capacity and a structural penalty for partitions.
- Consistency results separating amiable vs non-amiable structures.

Cons:
- Loose connection between the theoretical penalty and the implemented α-penalty; tuning lacks guidance.
- Missing assumptions and rates in theory; no guarantees for the heuristic search.
- Limited empirical evaluation and lack of baselines; scalability concerns.
- Incomplete discussion of related structured kernel learning literature.

Questions for the authors
- Under what precise assumptions (on kernels, noise, boundedness, distribution of X) do Theorems 2–4 hold? Is independence of predictors required beyond Proposition 1?
- Can you provide guidance or theory for choosing α and µ (e.g., dependence on n, p, kernel bandwidths)?
- How sensitive is structure recovery to kernel bandwidth selection? How are bandwidths tuned across groups of varying sizes?
- Can the backward stepwise algorithm be endowed with guarantees (e.g., under strong signal-to-noise and separability conditions)?
- How does GASI compare empirically to SALSA and hierarchical kernel learning on structure recovery and prediction?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add detailed assumptions and a more rigorous derivation tying the penalty to covering/Rademacher complexity, ideally with an oracle inequality or finite-sample selection guarantee.
- Include comprehensive comparisons to strong baselines (SALSA, hierarchical kernel learning, additive models with interaction selection), reporting both structure recovery and predictive performance.
- Analyze computational complexity and propose more scalable search (e.g., submodular surrogates, beam search, MCMC over partitions, or convex relaxations via kernel combinations).
- Provide implementation details (kernels, bandwidths, constraints) and ablations on α, µ, λ sensitivity.
- Clarify whether the intrinsic structure depends on the distribution of X and discuss identifiability when predictors are dependent.

Overall, the paper addresses a worthwhile problem with a principled framework, but requires stronger theoretical specification, algorithmic development, and empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards.
