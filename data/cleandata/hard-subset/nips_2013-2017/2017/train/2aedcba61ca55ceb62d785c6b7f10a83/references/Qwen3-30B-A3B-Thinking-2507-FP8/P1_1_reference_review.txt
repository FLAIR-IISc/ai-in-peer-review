

# Peer Review: "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical gap in group additive models for nonparametric regression. While previous work (notably Pan et al., 2015) introduced group additive structures, it did not provide a method to identify the correct grouping. The authors define the *intrinsic group additive structure* as the minimal group structure that can represent the target function, proving its uniqueness. They propose a structural penalty based on RKHS covering numbers to identify this structure through a penalized kernel method. The paper develops theoretical consistency guarantees and two algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) for structure identification. The approach bridges the gap between traditional additive models (which don't allow interactions) and functional ANOVA models (which suffer from combinatorial complexity).

The work relates to several NIPS threads: it builds on nonparametric regression literature (Stone, 1982; Hastie & Tibshirani, 1990) while addressing a practical limitation in high-dimensional settings. It also connects to recent work on structured nonparametric models (e.g., Kandasamy & Yu, 2016) but distinguishes itself by focusing on structure identification rather than just estimation.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Novelty**: The definition of intrinsic group additive structure is a significant theoretical contribution, addressing a fundamental problem of non-identifiability in group additive models. The paper clearly demonstrates why this minimal structure is desirable for both efficiency and interpretability.

2. **Theoretical Rigor**: The consistency proofs (Theorems 2-4) are well-constructed and address a key challenge in structure selection. The use of covering numbers as a complexity measure is well-justified and provides a practical penalty function.

3. **Practical Algorithms**: The two-step procedure (Algorithm 1) and backward stepwise approach (Algorithm 2) provide implementable solutions. The experimental results on synthetic data demonstrate the method's ability to correctly identify group structures.

4. **Clear Distinction from Related Work**: The paper effectively differentiates its approach from graphical models (Section 1), clarifying that it directly targets group additive structure rather than conditional dependence.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Scalability**: While Algorithm 2 addresses the computational burden of exhaustive search, the paper doesn't adequately evaluate its performance for large p (e.g., p > 50). The "greedy" nature of the stepwise approach (mentioned in Section 4) could lead to suboptimal solutions, but this limitation isn't thoroughly explored.

2. **Parameter Selection**: The choice of α (which appears to be an input parameter to the covering number) is not well-justified. The paper uses α as an integer in [1,10] without explaining its relationship to the data or the structure of the problem. This makes the method's practical implementation somewhat opaque.

3. **High-Dimensional Assumption**: The theoretical analysis assumes fixed p (number of predictors), yet the paper claims to address high-dimensional settings. This tension isn't adequately resolved—Theorem 4 states "for a fixed p," which contradicts the paper's high-dimensional focus.

4. **Limited Real-World Validation**: The Boston Housing application is interesting but limited in scope. More substantial real-world validation (e.g., with larger datasets or more complex relationships) would strengthen the claims about practical utility.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical results. The consistency proofs are rigorous, and the empirical results align with the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., computational complexity, greedy nature of stepwise algorithm). However, the theoretical analysis assumes fixed dimensionality, which conflicts with the paper's stated goal of addressing high-dimensional settings.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the connection between the RKHS theory and the practical algorithm is well-explained. The distinction between amiable and non-amiable structures is particularly clear. Some minor improvements could be made to the description of Algorithm 2 and the parameter selection process.

## Originality

This work makes a clear conceptual contribution by defining and identifying the intrinsic group additive structure. While group-based nonparametric models have been explored (Pan et al., 2015), the identification problem has not been addressed. The use of RKHS covering numbers for structural complexity is novel in this context. The paper properly situates itself relative to related work on functional ANOVA (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1990; Gu, 2002) and sparse graphical models (Koller & Friedman, 2009).

## Significance

The problem addressed is significant for high-dimensional nonparametric regression, where interpretability and computational efficiency are major challenges. The intrinsic group structure provides a meaningful middle ground between traditional additive models and full interaction models. The method could be particularly valuable in scientific domains where interpretable nonparametric models are needed. However, the computational limitations for large p may constrain its immediate applicability.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper with clear theoretical contributions and practical relevance. The main weaknesses—computational scalability and parameter selection—are significant but not deal-breakers. With minor revisions addressing these points, the paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**:
- Clear theoretical contribution addressing a fundamental gap
- Rigorous consistency guarantees
- Well-motivated complexity measure using RKHS covering numbers
- Demonstrated performance on synthetic data
- Clear distinction from related work

**Con Acceptance Arguments**:
- Computational limitations for large p not fully addressed
- Parameter selection (α) lacks justification
- Theoretical analysis assumes fixed dimensionality
- Limited real-world validation beyond Boston Housing

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the discussion of computational complexity for large p, perhaps with a brief analysis of the stepwise algorithm's behavior as p increases.

2. Provide more justification for the choice of α (e.g., how it relates to the data or the expected group sizes).

3. Clarify the relationship between the theoretical analysis (which assumes fixed p) and the high-dimensional claims.

4. Consider adding a second real-world application to strengthen the practical significance.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. With minor revisions addressing the computational and parameter selection concerns, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
