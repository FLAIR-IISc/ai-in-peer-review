- Studies nonparametric regression with group-additive structure f(x)=∑u∈G fu(xu), formalizes non-uniqueness of partitions, defines an “intrinsic” minimal amiable structure G*, and proposes a structure-penalized kernel estimator with selection consistency under fixed p.
- Connects to additive models, functional ANOVA/ESP kernels, OKGT, SALSA, lattice-of-partitions ideas, and structural risk minimization via covering numbers.
- The partial-order formalization and intrinsic structure concept are well-motivated and conceptually sound.
- Consistency proof strategy: amiable partitions share population risk, uniform convergence via covering numbers, non-amiable partitions have positive risk gap, and a complexity penalty C(G) biases toward G* with suitable µ.
- Derives a practical penalty C(G) that scales with group sizes C(G) ≍ ∑u α|u| using covering-number bounds, which is intuitive and convenient.
- Theoretical results assume fixed p and a finite set of amiable partitions; no theory is provided for large-p settings where greedy Algorithm 2 is used.
- Selection consistency depends on a “proper” µ but lacks a data-driven prescription; µ and α are tuned by validation on prediction error, which may not ensure structural correctness.
- The step from covering-number bounds to treating α as a tunable integer is heuristic; the theoretical-practical link for α is unclear.
- Proposition 3 and Theorem 1 need clearer conditions and proofs—uniqueness of the minimal amiable partition and strict subspace inclusions under dependence are nontrivial and should be explicit.
- No computational complexity analysis is provided; exhaustive search scales with Bell numbers and KRR per candidate is O(n^3) without approximations.
- Presentation is generally clear, but assumptions (independence, kernel class, boundedness), precise conditions on λn and µn, and representer/computational details for the direct-sum RKHS should be stated where used.
- Algorithm 2’s behavior (single pass vs iterative, handling ties/multi-splits) and complexity should be specified.
- The idea of targeting an intrinsic group-additive structure in RKHSs and using capacity-based penalties is novel and interesting.
- Practical impact is limited by scalability, lack of principled hyperparameter selection, and sparse empirical validation.
- Empirical evaluation is weak: small synthetic experiments with low noise, no comparisons to SALSA/ESP/OKGT/sparse-interaction baselines, and a single illustrative Boston Housing case without ground truth.
- Backward stepwise sometimes fails (Model 4) and remedies (multi-start, annealing, beam search) are not explored.
- Pros: clear formulation, sensible RKHS-based consistency strategy, interpretable structural penalty, and algorithms suitable for small/moderate p.
- Cons: limited empirical validation, no rigorous scalability/complexity analysis, absent theory for greedy large-p search, and unclear tuning guidance for µ and α.
- Suggestions: compare to SALSA, ESP/ANOVA kernels, OKGT, and sparse interaction models; clarify and prove Theorem 1 rigorously; provide data-driven or theoretical guidance for µn/α; strengthen Algorithm 2 (iterative/multi-start/beam search) and discuss kernel approximations for scalability.
- Also suggest discussing robustness to dependent covariates and misspecification, and expanding simulations to higher noise, larger p, and mis-specified kernels.
- Verdict: Weak reject — interesting and promising but requires stronger experiments, clearer assumptions/proofs, and scalable search strategies to meet conference standards.
