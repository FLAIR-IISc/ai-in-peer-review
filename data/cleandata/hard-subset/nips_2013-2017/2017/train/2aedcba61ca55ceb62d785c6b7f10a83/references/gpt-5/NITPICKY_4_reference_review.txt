Summary
This paper studies nonparametric regression under a “group additive” assumption: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of variables into groups. The authors (i) formalize a partial order over group structures and define an “intrinsic” group additive structure G* as the minimal element among all structures that can represent f, (ii) propose to estimate f and identify G* jointly via kernel ridge regression (KRR) on the direct-sum RKHS HG with an additional structural penalty C(G) motivated by covering-number bounds, and (iii) provide selection-consistency results and two structure-search algorithms (exhaustive and a backward stepwise splitting heuristic). Simulations and a Boston Housing case study are used to illustrate the approach.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned between standard additive models and higher-order ANOVA/additive interaction models (e.g., Gu, 2002; Kandasamy & Yu, 2016). It connects to OKGT (Pan et al., 2015) and to RKHS theory (Steinwart & Christmann; Zhou; Smola & Schölkopf). However, important related areas appear undertreated: structured sparsity with kernels and hierarchical kernel learning (e.g., Bach, 2009), multiple kernel learning for additive/grouped structures, sparse additive models (SpAM; Ravikumar et al., 2009), kernel ANOVA decompositions, HSIC-based dependence measures for grouping, and recent pairwise-interaction GAMs.

Strengths
- Addresses an interesting intermediate modeling regime between additive and fully-interactive models; the formalization of a minimal (“intrinsic”) group structure is appealing.
- Uses RKHS machinery to tie structure complexity to capacity (covering numbers), aiming for a principled penalty and a selection consistency result.
- The direct-sum RKHS construction is natural for grouped additive models.
- The empirical risk/decomposition arguments suggest a plausible route to structure selection.

Major weaknesses and concerns
1) Penalty derivation and internal inconsistencies
- The complexity penalty is derived from bounds ln N(ε, I(HK,r), d∞) ≤ C (ln(r/ε))p+1, yielding C(G) ≈ ∑u α(ε)|u|+1 with α(ε)=ln(r/ε). Later, this morphs into µ ∑j α|uj| (Eq. (6) even shows µ∑ α|uj| with no exponent; elsewhere α|uj| vs α|uj|+1 vs α|uj| appear). This is inconsistent and materially affects selection. The role of ε and r vanishes, constants Ck,p are dropped, kernel dependence disappears, and α is then tuned on a discrete grid [1,10]. This undermines the theoretical tie between the penalty and the covering numbers.
- The bound for direct sums: Proposition 3 states ln N(ε, IG) ≤ ∑ ln N(ε/|G|, Iu). No proof or citation is given. For sums of function classes under sup-norm, one typically uses N(ε, F1+…+Fd) ≤ ∏i N(ε/d, Fi); a correct constant matters for the penalty form. Please provide a rigorous proof or a standard reference.

2) Assumptions and correctness of the “intrinsic” structure
- Theorem 1 claims existence and uniqueness of a minimal G* over the partially ordered set of amiable structures. For a finite poset, minimal elements exist, but uniqueness is not guaranteed unless extra conditions hold. The paper does not state sufficient conditions. The example discussion hints at non-uniqueness, yet Theorem 1 asserts uniqueness. This needs a precise statement and proof (e.g., conditions on PX, independence, or a functional identifiability condition).
- Proposition 1 invokes independence of Xj to get strict inclusion L2G1 ⊂ L2G2. Later results seem to rely on this hierarchy to justify penalties. Clarify where independence is required and whether the main consistency result holds without it.

3) Theory clarity and rigor
- Proposition 4/Theorem 2 bounds mix empirical/process constants with λn and ‖f*G*‖. The terms “R̂g”, “Rg” vs “R̂”, “R” switch, and there are multiple typographical glitches (e.g., λn* n → 0). The dependence of µ and α on n for selection consistency (Theorem 4) is not specified; “choose a proper µ” is not actionable.
- The RKHS-to-C(X) embedding and covering-number bounds require compact domains and continuity; assumptions are not clearly stated. If Gaussian kernels are used, quote the precise bounds you rely on (Kühn, 2011).
- The representer theorem and the precise norm on HG (sum of group norms? squared sum?) are not stated; this matters for optimization and theory.

4) Algorithmic issues
- Algorithm 1 tunes µ and α via validation but treats λ as “tuned separately” with no protocol; kernel hyperparameters (e.g., bandwidths per group) are unspecified. Without these, results are not reproducible.
- Algorithm 2 (backward stepwise): the pseudo-code “solve (6)” while G is fixed is incorrect; it should solve (7). The procedure does only one pass over variables; no iteration to convergence or backtracking; splitting choice “either split j as a new group or add to an existing group” is ambiguous. Complexity and stopping criteria are unclear.
- The search over partitions is combinatorial (Bell number); no scalable relaxation (e.g., structured sparsity MKL) is considered.

5) Experiments
- Critical details are missing: explicit model definitions (Table 1 is not provided), p and n, kernels and bandwidth selection, λ selection, and how fu are estimated per group. Only frequencies of “recovering the true structure” are reported, without comparisons to baselines (SpAM, SALSA, pairwise GAMs, hierarchical kernel learning, graphical-model-based heuristics).
- The Boston Housing example reports a partition but no predictive comparison or stability analysis; without baselines and accuracy metrics, the practical value is unclear.

6) Writing and formatting
- Numerous typos, inconsistent notation (e.g., µ ∑ α|u| vs µ ∑ α|u|+1), undefined symbols, inconsistent subscripts, and missing years in references. Several references are incomplete, and some central related work is missing.

Pros
- Interesting problem formulation (group additive structure) with a clear motivation.
- Attempt to ground structure selection in RKHS capacity/control.
- Selection consistency claim (if formalized properly) would be valuable.

Cons
- Penalty derivation inconsistencies and weak connection between theory and the actual tuned objective.
- Questionable uniqueness of the “intrinsic” structure under stated assumptions.
- Incomplete theory exposition and assumptions; typographical errors in key theorems.
- Algorithmic design and pseudo-code are underspecified.
- Experimental evaluation lacks essential details and baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide rigorous conditions for existence/uniqueness of G* (or acknowledge possible multiple minimals and adapt selection accordingly).
- Derive a coherent penalty with explicit constants or a BIC-like surrogate tied to effective degrees of freedom; fix the α/ε/r story and keep the same form throughout.
- State all assumptions (kernel continuity, compactness, independence if used), and give complete proofs for Proposition 3 and Theorems 1–4.
- Specify optimization (representer theorem, per-group kernels, λ selection) and provide scalable alternatives (e.g., structured sparsity/MKL relaxations, HSIC-based pre-clustering).
- Expand experiments with full details and comparisons to strong baselines; report predictive accuracy and structure recovery rates with confidence intervals; release code.

Ratings
- Originality: Moderate
- Quality: Low to moderate (due to theoretical/algorithmic inconsistencies)
- Clarity: Low (many inconsistencies/omissions)
- Significance: Potentially moderate if corrected

Recommendation: Reject (as is). The idea is interesting, but the current manuscript has substantial technical, clarity, and experimental shortcomings that preclude acceptance.
