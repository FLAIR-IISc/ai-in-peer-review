This paper tackles the problem of identifying an additive structure in nonparametric regression, moving beyond the limitations of purely additive models and the combinatorial explosion of full interaction models. The core idea – identifying a “group” additive structure and developing a penalty based on the complexity of that structure – is genuinely novel and represents a potentially valuable contribution. The connection to graphical models is appropriately acknowledged, and the authors clearly articulate the distinct focus on group additive structures versus conditional dependence. The theoretical work, particularly the consistency proof, is ambitious and adds substantial weight to the contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of identifying a group additive structure and formalizing it with an intrinsic definition is a significant contribution. This provides a middle ground between purely additive models and high-order interaction models.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The consistency proof is a major strength. Establishing theoretical guarantees, even with assumptions, is critical for establishing the validity of the proposed method.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The authors clearly define the problem of Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI) and motivate its importance, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
*   **Complexity Measure:** The proposed complexity measure based on covering numbers offers a principled way to penalize model complexity and guide structure selection.
*   **Algorithmic Development:** The development of both an exhaustive search and a stepwise algorithm provides practical tools for applying the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm is likely to be impractical for high-dimensional problems, even with modest numbers of predictors. While the stepwise algorithm is proposed to address this, its performance isn't rigorously demonstrated in all scenarios.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of µ and α, requiring careful tuning (as shown in simulations). The sensitivity analysis could be more detailed.
*   **Kernel Choice:** The choice of kernel (and the associated RKHS) is crucial. The paper emphasizes universal kernels, but doesn’t thoroughly explore the impact of different kernel choices on performance, particularly in the real data application.
*   **Simulation Limitations:** While the simulations are comprehensive, they primarily focus on relatively simple scenarios. More complex, real-world-inspired datasets would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Real Data Interpretation:** The interpretation of the discovered group structure in the Boston Housing data is somewhat limited. More in-depth analysis of the identified groups and their implications would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel concept – the group additive structure – with a strong theoretical foundation.
*   The proposed method offers a promising approach to address a significant challenge in nonparametric regression: balancing model flexibility and interpretability.
*   The consistency proof provides strong theoretical support for the method's validity.
*   Even if computationally demanding, the algorithmic framework provides a starting point for further optimization and practical application.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm limits the method's scalability.
*   The sensitivity to parameter tuning (µ and α) may require significant effort in practice.
*   The empirical evaluation, while solid, could be strengthened with more complex datasets and a more thorough analysis of the impact of kernel choice.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that proposes a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem. The originality of the concept and the theoretical guarantees significantly outweigh the current limitations. While the computational cost and parameter sensitivity are concerns, they do not detract substantially from the overall contribution. I believe this paper has the potential to significantly impact the field and warrants acceptance, despite not being a completely polished or exhaustively validated method. The authors have introduced a compelling new direction for research.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I would encourage the authors to address the computational limitations and provide a more in-depth analysis of parameter sensitivity in a future work.
