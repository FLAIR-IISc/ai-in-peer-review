Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a “group additive” assumption: f(X) decomposes as a sum of functions over disjoint variable groups. The authors (i) formalize the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure G*, defined as the minimal partition for which such a decomposition exists; (ii) propose to identify G* jointly with the component functions via a penalized kernel regression over the direct-sum RKHS induced by a candidate partition G; (iii) construct a structural penalty C(G) from covering-number bounds of the constituent RKHSs; (iv) prove selection consistency under capacity bounds; and (v) provide exhaustive-search and backward stepwise algorithms, with simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

This sits between classical additive models and functional ANOVA/interaction models (Hastie & Tibshirani; Gu; Kandasamy & Yu’s SALSA with ESP kernels). It is related to OKGT (Pan et al.) and to (block) graphical models in spirit. The use of RKHS machinery (KRR, universality, covering numbers) follows standard learning-theory tools (e.g., Steinwart & Christmann; Zhou), and the formulation resembles MKL/hierarchical kernel learning in spirit, though that literature is not cited.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: group-level interactions can offer a compromise between purely additive and high-order interaction models.
- Formalization: definitions of amiable vs. intrinsic group structures are interesting; the partial order over partitions is natural.
- RKHS-based framework: direct-sum RKHS for a given grouping and representer-theorem-based estimation are standard and sound.
- Theory: consistency claim for selecting G* using a structural complexity penalty is appealing at a high level.
- Practicality: provides an exhaustive and a simple backward stepwise heuristic; initial synthetic experiments and a real-data illustration.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Structural penalty design and practicality. The penalty C(G) is derived from an upper bound on covering numbers that depends on ε via ln(r/ε) and on dimension. In practice, ε and constants are unknown, and the paper treats α as a tunable integer hyperparameter (1–10). This divorces the implemented penalty from the theoretical quantity. Moreover, Eq. (6) appears inconsistent: earlier C(G)=∑ α(ε)^{|u|+1}, but (6) uses µ∑ α|u| (is that α^{|u|} or α·|u|?). This ambiguity weakens both clarity and the connection between the estimator and the theory.
- Identifiability assumptions. Proposition 1’s strict inclusion relies on independence of Xj’s. Theorems on existence/uniqueness of the intrinsic structure and selection consistency appear to hinge on properties (closure/completeness) of the set of amiable structures that are not transparent without stronger conditions (e.g., independence or orthogonality in L2). If covariates are correlated, decompositions can be non-unique, and the minimal element may be ill-defined. The paper should state explicit conditions (e.g., independence, universal kernels, centering constraints) under which G* exists, is unique, and is recoverable.
- Theoretical bounds have limited operational guidance. The selection consistency relies on capacity bounds with large, unknown constants; “choose proper µ” is not constructive. There is no data-driven rule that links µ, α, λ, n, and p to guarantee selection. In practice the paper uses validation to pick µ, α, which is not shown to be consistent.
- Algorithms and scalability. Exhaustive search over partitions is infeasible beyond small p (Bell numbers). The backward stepwise heuristic has no guarantees and indeed fails on Model 4; no attempts are made to escape local minima. There is no complexity analysis (per iteration cost in terms of n, p, number of groups), and no kernel hyperparameter selection details (e.g., bandwidths) are given.
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient. The simulations report only “frequency of recovery” over a grid of µ, α, without:
  - comparisons to established baselines (GAM; GAM+pairwise with hierarchical sparsity such as hierNet; SALSA/ESP-KRR; OKGT; additive GPs with ARD/product kernels; group-sparse additive models),
  - sensitivity to correlated covariates,
  - predictive performance (RMSE) and sample-size/p-dimension scaling,
  - ablations (effect of λ, kernel choice).
  The real-data section is purely qualitative; no held-out accuracy or baseline comparison is provided. This makes it hard to assess significance relative to established methods.
- Clarity and notation. Several notational inconsistencies and missing details:
  - Eq. (6) penalty form inconsistent with preceding derivation.
  - The capacity penalty aggregates over groups using a uniform α; yet groups have different |u| and potentially different kernels—how are kernel bandwidths chosen per group?
  - The proofs rely on lnN(·,H,∥·∥∞) for each group; but in practice the direct-sum norm and empirical design-dependent degrees of freedom matter. A more transparent, data-dependent complexity (e.g., sum of effective degrees of freedom per group via trace of the smoother) would be more interpretable and practical.

Quality
The high-level framework is reasonable, but key methodological choices (penalty design, identifiability assumptions, tuning) are not fully justified, and the experiments do not convincingly validate the approach against established baselines.

Clarity
Generally readable, but the penalty inconsistency and missing implementation details hinder reproducibility. More careful exposition of assumptions and conditions for the main theorems is needed.

Originality
Targeting group-level additive structure is a useful perspective, but related literature on structured kernel learning and sparse/interacting additive models is under-cited, and the main novelty—the covering-number-based penalty—is theoretically motivated yet practically ad hoc.

Significance
Without strong empirical evidence or clear scalability, impact is limited. With rigorous comparisons and a more principled, data-driven structural penalty, the contribution could be strengthened.

Questions for the authors
1. Precisely state the assumptions under which G* exists and is unique. Do you require independence of Xj? What happens with correlated covariates?
2. Clarify Eq. (6): is the penalty µ∑ α^{|u|} or µ∑ α·|u|? How are α, ε, and r connected in practice?
3. Provide guidance (or a theorem) for data-driven tuning of µ, α, λ that yields consistent selection.
4. How are kernel types and hyperparameters chosen per group? Gaussian bandwidths fixed or tuned?
5. Can you report predictive performance and structure recovery against GAM, GAM+pairwise (with hierarchy), SALSA/ESP-KRR, OKGT, and additive GPs, across varying n, p, and correlation structures?
6. Any theoretical or empirical insight into the backward stepwise algorithm’s convergence or approximation quality?

Pros
- Well-motivated problem and formalization of “intrinsic” group structure.
- Use of direct-sum RKHS with selection consistency result (under capacity bounds).
- Initial algorithms and demonstrations.

Cons
- Structural penalty is loosely tied to the theory and ambiguous in the text.
- Identifiability conditions unclear; independence likely required but not stated.
- Weak experimental validation; no baselines; limited scalability evidence.
- Stepwise heuristic lacks guarantees and can fail.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is interesting, but the current version lacks methodological rigor in the penalty design/assumptions and convincing empirical validation against established baselines. Strengthening theory (clear assumptions, constructive tuning) and experiments (comparisons, scalability, robustness) would substantially improve the paper.
