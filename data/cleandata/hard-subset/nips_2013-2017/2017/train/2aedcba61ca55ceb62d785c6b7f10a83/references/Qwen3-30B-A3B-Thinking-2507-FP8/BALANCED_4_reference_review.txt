

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of identifying the intrinsic group additive structure for nonparametric regression, extending the work of Pan et al. (2011) who assumed the group structure was known. The authors formally define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal structure among all amiable group additive structures (those that can represent the true function), and propose a structural penalty method based on RKHS capacity measures to identify this structure. The paper makes a clear connection to previous work on additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1990) and functional ANOVA models (Gu, 2002), positioning their approach as a middle ground between standard additive models and higher-order interaction models. The method is theoretically supported with consistency results and demonstrated through simulation studies and a real data application on Boston Housing data.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by formally defining the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and proving its consistency under the proposed method (Theorem 4). This addresses a critical gap in previous work on group additive models.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively motivates the problem by carefully explaining the limitations of standard additive models and functional ANOVA approaches, and clearly positioning their approach as a middle ground.

3. **Well-Structured Methodology**: The authors provide a clear mathematical framework for the problem, with well-defined concepts (amiable structures, intrinsic structure, complexity measure) and a sound theoretical foundation based on RKHS theory.

4. **Practical Algorithms**: The paper offers two practical algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) for implementation, with the latter addressing computational challenges for large p. The real data application demonstrates the interpretability and usefulness of the method.

5. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The simulation study covers multiple model specifications, and the Boston Housing application provides concrete evidence of the method's interpretability and practical utility.

## Weaknesses
1. **Computational Complexity**: While the paper proposes a stepwise algorithm for large p, it doesn't provide sufficient analysis of its computational complexity or theoretical guarantees for the stepwise approach. The exhaustive search algorithm is exponential in the number of predictors, and the paper doesn't adequately discuss how this limitation might affect practical applications with many predictors.

2. **Limited Comparison to Alternatives**: The paper doesn't compare its method to alternative approaches for identifying group structures, such as those based on graphical models or other structure learning techniques. This weakens the demonstration of the method's superiority.

3. **Narrow Simulation Scope**: The simulation study only uses five models with relatively small numbers of predictors (p=5-6 in most cases). A more comprehensive simulation study with larger p and more complex structures would strengthen the empirical claims.

4. **Lack of Discussion on Edge Cases**: The paper claims the intrinsic structure is unique (Theorem 1), but doesn't discuss potential edge cases where this might not hold or how the method would behave in such scenarios.

5. **Complexity Measure Practicality**: The complexity measure based on covering numbers (C(G) = Σα^|uj|+1) is theoretically sound but may be computationally challenging to implement for large group structures. The paper doesn't discuss practical considerations for selecting α or how sensitive the method is to this choice.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a theoretically sound and well-motivated approach to group additive structure identification in nonparametric regression. The main contribution—defining and identifying the intrinsic group additive structure—is significant and addresses a clear gap in the literature. The method is well-justified theoretically and demonstrates promising empirical performance.

However, the paper has notable limitations in its empirical evaluation and discussion of practical implementation challenges. The computational complexity of the method for large p is not sufficiently addressed, and the comparison to alternative methods is limited. The simulation study is also somewhat narrow in scope.

## Recommendation
**Weak Accept** (borderline for acceptance)

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution with clear practical implications. The main weakness is the limited empirical evaluation and insufficient discussion of computational challenges for large-scale applications. These limitations could be addressed with a more comprehensive simulation study, additional comparison to alternative methods, and a more thorough discussion of computational considerations.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Expand the simulation study to include larger numbers of predictors (p > 10) and more complex group structures to better demonstrate scalability.

2. Add a comparison to alternative methods for identifying group structures, such as those based on graphical models or other structure learning techniques.

3. Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of both algorithms, particularly the stepwise approach.

4. Include a discussion of potential edge cases where the intrinsic structure might not be unique or where the method might fail.

5. Consider simplifying the complexity measure or providing practical guidelines for selecting α and µ in real-world applications.

6. In the experimental section, provide more detailed analysis of why Model 4 performed poorly with the stepwise algorithm (as noted in the paper) and potential remedies.

The paper has strong theoretical foundations and addresses an important problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. With the suggested improvements, it would make a significant contribution to the field.
