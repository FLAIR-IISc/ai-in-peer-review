This paper addresses the problem of identifying the group additive structure (GAS) in nonparametric regression, a middle ground between fully additive models and higher-order interaction models. The authors aim to resolve issues of non-identifiability and lack of systematic identification methods present in previous work, specifically citing [11]. They propose a penalized kernel method minimizing a loss function incorporating a novel complexity measure for GAS, and provide theoretical guarantees for consistency. The approach is evaluated through simulations and a real-data application.

**Summary of Contributions:**

The core contribution is a principled method for GAS identification leveraging RKHS theory and a complexity penalty. The authors rigorously define the "intrinsic" GAS, providing a theoretical foundation.  They derive a tractable capacity measure for GAS, used within a minimization framework to estimate both the structure and the nonparametric functions. The theoretical analysis establishes consistency of the proposed estimator. The empirical evaluation demonstrates practical performance on synthetic and real datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, rigorously defining the intrinsic GAS and proving consistency of the proposed estimator. The use of covering numbers to define the complexity measure is well-motivated and mathematically sound. The proofs, while not included in the main paper, are referenced as being available in the supplementary material.
* **Novelty:** The proposed complexity measure for GAS is a novel contribution, offering a principled way to penalize model complexity during structure identification. The combination of RKHS theory with structural penalty is a significant step forward.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the contribution relative to existing literature. Mathematical notation is generally clear.
* **Completeness:** The paper addresses both the theoretical and empirical aspects of the problem. It presents algorithms for implementation (exhaustive search and stepwise) and provides simulation results and a real-data application.
* **Connection to Related Work:** The paper appropriately connects the GAS approach to related concepts like graphical models, highlighting the differences and advantages of their proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm is computationally expensive for high-dimensional problems, as acknowledged by the authors. While a stepwise approach is proposed, its performance on more complex datasets (like Model 4 in the simulations) appears limited. Further exploration of scalable algorithms could enhance the practical applicability of the method.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The performance of the method appears sensitive to the choice of tuning parameters (λ, µ, α). While the authors mention using cross-validation, more details on the parameter tuning procedure and its impact on results would be beneficial.
* **Simulation Design:** While the simulation study demonstrates the method’s ability to recover the true structure in several settings, the simulated models are relatively simple. Evaluating the method's performance on more complex and realistic scenarios, including those with stronger interactions and non-Gaussian noise, would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Real Data Analysis:** The interpretation of the identified group structure in the Boston Housing dataset is somewhat limited. A more detailed discussion of the implications of the identified groups and their relationship to housing prices would enhance the practical relevance of the results.
* **Limited Empirical Comparison:** The paper lacks a direct comparison to existing GAS identification methods (other than [11], which has its own limitations), making it difficult to assess the relative performance of the proposed approach.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to a challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
* The theoretical results provide strong guarantees for the consistency of the proposed estimator.
* The empirical evaluation demonstrates the practical feasibility of the method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm may limit its scalability.
* The sensitivity to parameter tuning and the relatively simple simulation settings raise concerns about the robustness of the method.
* The lack of a comprehensive empirical comparison to existing techniques hinders a thorough assessment of its performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The theoretical foundations are solid, the proposed method is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates its practical potential. While some limitations exist regarding computational cost and parameter sensitivity, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding the exploration of scalable algorithms and a more thorough empirical comparison to existing methods.
