## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of identifying the underlying group additive structure in nonparametric regression models, a critical issue when dealing with high-dimensional data. While standard nonparametric regression suffers from the curse of dimensionality, imposing an additive structure helps.  Existing methods either assume interactions between all variables (like ANOVA models) or impose a fully additive structure, which may be restrictive. The authors propose a method to identify the *intrinsic* group additive structure—the most concise one—using kernel methods and a novel complexity measure for group structures based on covering numbers. They formulate an optimization problem balancing model fit and structural complexity, and provide theoretical guarantees for consistency.  They also present two algorithms – exhaustive search and a stepwise approach – for practical identification.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Identifying meaningful structure in high-dimensional nonparametric data is crucial for both statistical efficiency and interpretability.
* **Novelty:** The concept of an "intrinsic" group additive structure and the corresponding complexity measure are novel contributions.  The use of covering numbers to quantify the complexity of group structures is a clever approach.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the consistency of the proposed method, a significant strength. The theorems are clearly stated and appear well-motivated.
* **Practical Algorithms:**  Offering both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms demonstrates attention to practical application, acknowledging the computational challenges.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The simulation studies and real data application provide evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method. The visualizations from the Boston Housing data are insightful.
* **Connection to Related Work:** The paper appropriately connects its work to existing approaches like functional ANOVA, graphical models, and optimal kernel group transformation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Theoretical Results:** While the theoretical results are strong, they are quite dense and may be difficult for readers not deeply familiar with covering number theory to fully grasp. More intuitive explanations would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm is likely to be computationally expensive for high-dimensional problems, even with the stepwise approach. The paper acknowledges this but could explore further optimization techniques.
* **Limited Scope of Real Data Application:**  The Boston Housing dataset is a relatively standard benchmark. Applying the method to more complex and higher-dimensional real-world datasets would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the choice of the regularization parameter `µ` (penalty for structural complexity). A more thorough analysis of parameter tuning strategies would be helpful.
* **Algorithm 2’s Greedy Nature:** The authors acknowledge the potential issues with the greedy stepwise approach. It might be valuable to investigate alternative stepwise procedures or explore methods to mitigate the risk of getting stuck in local optima.



**Review of Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with strong theoretical justification and well-designed experiments. The analysis appears rigorous and the claims are supported by evidence. (Strong)
* **Clarity:** The writing is generally clear, though the theoretical sections are dense. More intuitive explanations of the mathematical concepts would improve readability. (Good)
* **Originality:** The concept of an intrinsic group additive structure and the associated complexity measure are genuinely novel. The approach represents a significant step forward in addressing the challenges of nonparametric regression in high dimensions. (Excellent)
* **Significance:** The results are likely to be significant to researchers working on nonparametric regression, dimensionality reduction, and statistical learning. The method has the potential to improve the efficiency and interpretability of models in various applications. (Good)

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem.
* Strong theoretical guarantees for consistency.
* Practical algorithms and promising empirical results.
* Clear connection to relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Theoretical results are dense and potentially inaccessible to a broader audience.
* Computational cost could be a limitation in high dimensions.
* Real data application could be more extensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS. It presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically relevant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. While there are areas for improvement in terms of clarity and computational efficiency, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The work addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and innovative approach, and the theoretical guarantees provide strong confidence in the method's validity.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and potentially expand the empirical evaluation.
