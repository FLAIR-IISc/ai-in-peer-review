## NIPS 2017 Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying a group additive structure in nonparametric regression, aiming to bridge the gap between fully additive models (prone to oversimplification) and high-order interaction models (prone to dimensionality issues). The authors propose a penalized kernel regression approach that simultaneously identifies the optimal grouping of predictors and estimates the underlying functions. They introduce a novel complexity measure for group structures based on covering numbers and demonstrate consistency of their method. They also provide both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms for practical implementation, supported by simulation studies and a real-data application.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges the limitations of standard nonparametric regression and the benefits of additive models. The authors appropriately cite functional ANOVA and higher-order interaction models (e.g., ESP kernel methods) while highlighting their scalability issues. The connection to Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) is made, and the paper clearly articulates its contribution in addressing the non-identifiability and lack of systematic identification methods present in the prior work. The link to graphical models is also acknowledged, with a clear distinction made between the focus on group additivity versus conditional dependence structures.

**Strengths:**

* **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper tackles a relevant and important problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression – balancing model complexity, interpretability, and accuracy.
* **Novel Complexity Measure:** The introduction of a complexity measure for group additive structures, based on covering numbers of RKHSs, is a significant contribution. This provides a theoretically grounded basis for penalization and promotes parsimony in the identified structures.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The consistency proof for the proposed method is a strong point. Establishing theoretical guarantees for group structure identification is challenging and valuable.
* **Practical Algorithms:** Providing both an exhaustive search and a stepwise algorithm broadens the applicability of the method to varying data sizes and computational constraints.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies and real-data application demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in recovering true structures and achieving reasonable predictive performance.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and explanations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the stepwise algorithm addresses scalability, the exhaustive search remains computationally prohibitive for large *p*. The paper doesn't fully explore alternative efficient search strategies.
* **Choice of Kernel & Bandwidth:** The performance of the method is sensitive to the choice of kernel and bandwidth. The paper doesn’t provide much guidance on how to select these parameters beyond mentioning universal kernels. A more detailed discussion on practical kernel selection and bandwidth tuning would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of the Complexity Measure:** The covering number-based complexity measure, while theoretically sound, is not easily interpretable. A more intuitive understanding of what the complexity penalty represents would aid in practical implementation.
* **Limited Real-Data Analysis:** The real-data application is somewhat limited.  A more comprehensive analysis, potentially including comparisons with other relevant methods (e.g., sparse partial least squares), would strengthen the practical validation.
* **Assumptions of Independence:** The theory relies on the assumption of independent predictors.  The robustness of the method to correlated predictors is not discussed, which is a common scenario in many applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a challenging problem with a novel and theoretically well-grounded approach. The proposed complexity measure and consistency proof are significant contributions. While some practical considerations (computational cost, kernel selection) remain, the method offers a promising direction for addressing high-dimensional nonparametric regression with interpretable structures.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to group structure identification.
* Rigorous consistency proof.
* Well-motivated problem with practical relevance.
* Clear presentation and organization.
* Demonstrated effectiveness through simulation and real-data application.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost of exhaustive search may limit scalability.
* Complexity of the proposed complexity measure.
* Limited exploration of kernel selection and bandwidth tuning.
* Assumption of independent predictors might limit applicability.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions.  The authors should address the following points:

1. **Discuss computational complexity more explicitly.** Provide a more detailed analysis of the runtime of both algorithms, and potentially explore more efficient search strategies for large *p*.
2. **Elaborate on kernel selection and bandwidth tuning.** Provide practical guidance and potentially explore adaptive methods for these parameters.
3. **Provide a more intuitive explanation of the complexity measure.** While the theoretical justification is sound, a more accessible interpretation would be beneficial.
4. **Expand the real-data analysis.** Compare the method to other relevant techniques and explore the robustness of the results.
5. **Briefly discuss the limitations related to correlated predictors.**



This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression and has the potential to influence future research in this area.  Addressing the minor revisions outlined above will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
