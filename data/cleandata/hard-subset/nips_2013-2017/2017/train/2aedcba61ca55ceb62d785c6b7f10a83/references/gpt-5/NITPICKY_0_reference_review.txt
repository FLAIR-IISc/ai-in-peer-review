Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression with a group-additive structure: f(X) = sum_{u∈G} f_u(X_u) where G is a partition of predictors. It (i) formalizes a notion of “intrinsic” group additive structure as the minimal element among all partitions G for which f admits such a decomposition (“amiable” structures), (ii) proposes to identify G while estimating f by minimizing empirical square loss plus an RKHS norm penalty and a structural penalty C(G) derived from covering number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs, and (iii) provides selection-consistency arguments and simple search algorithms (exhaustive; a backward stepwise heuristic). Simulations and a Boston Housing case study illustrate the method.

Relation to prior work
The paper sits between classical additive models and full functional ANOVA/ESP-kernel KRR models by allowing small groups to capture interactions without exploding the number of terms. It is related to OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15) but targets the structure identification problem, and to SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16), though SALSA considers sums of low-dimensional components up to a fixed order. It also connects to graphical models but aims for a simpler structural target. Missing in the discussion are connections to multiple kernel learning for additive structures, structured sparsity for ANOVA components (e.g., hierarchical/overlapping group lasso kernels), and interaction screening/sparsity-inducing approaches.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: group-level interactions can mitigate curse-of-dimensionality while staying interpretable.
- Formalization of a partial order over partitions and the idea of an “intrinsic” minimal structure is conceptually appealing.
- Using capacity measures to penalize structure is interesting; the covering-number-based decomposition for direct sums is plausible.
- Consistency target (recover G*) is relevant; the high-level separation between amiable vs non-amiable structures is meaningful.
- Empirical section attempts both exhaustive and greedy search and includes a real dataset.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Quality/technical soundness
- Existence/uniqueness of G*. The paper asserts Ga (the set of amiable structures) is “partially ordered and complete,” hence has a unique minimal element (Theorem 1). This is nontrivial. For partitions, the meet operation exists, but it is not shown that the meet of two amiable partitions remains amiable (i.e., f is representable on the refined partition), which is essential for the existence of a unique minimal element within Ga. The proof sketch/conditions (e.g., independence, centering constraints) are missing.
- Identifiability constraints. Standard additive/ANOVA decompositions require marginal constraints (e.g., zero-mean per component) to ensure uniqueness. Here, no such constraints are specified for the f_u, nor is the impact on identifiability of G analyzed. RKHS norm regularization alone generally does not ensure a unique partition-level decomposition.
- Proposition 1 assumes independence for strict inclusion L2_G1 ⊂ L2_G2 but this assumption is not stated globally. Later consistency results appear to rely on separability properties; assumptions are underspecified.
- Structural penalty derivation. The transition from ln N(ε, I(H_K,r), d_∞) ≤ C (ln(r/ε))^{p+1} to C(G) = sum_u α^{|u|} is ad hoc:
  - α is defined as ln(r/ε) but then treated as a tunable integer in [1,10]; r and ε vanish from the theory, and kernel-dependent constants C_{k,p} are ignored.
  - The exponent changes from |u|+1 in the bound to |u| in Eq. (6), justified by “combining the common factor with μ,” which is not mathematically valid. This weakens the claimed theoretical link between capacity and the chosen penalty.
  - The ε appearing in covering numbers is not tied to sample size n or estimation error; yet consistency (Theorem 4) would require a regime for μ_n, λ_n, and α_n compatible with concentration rates.
- Proposition 3 (covering numbers for direct sums) is stated without proof or precise conditions (norms, radii, embeddings). The ε/|G| scaling and additive log-covering bound need justification; constants likely depend on group sizes and norms (e.g., via Lipschitz constants of the sum map).
- Theorems 2–4 use nonstandard constants (e.g., “12n,” “1/24,” etc.) and mixed notations (Rg, R̂g), and require λ_n n → 0, but do not specify μ_n, α_n scaling needed for selection consistency. The gap C̃ in Theorem 3 depends on approximation error with wrong partitions; conditions for its strict positivity are not given.
- Algorithmic details are insufficient. Solving (7) in HG requires a representer theorem for direct-sum RKHSs and a kernel matrix construction; neither is presented. Hyperparameters (kernel types, bandwidths) and identifiability constraints in estimation are unspecified. Algorithm 2’s “backward stepwise” description is vague (what is the stopping criterion? one pass? beam width? how are candidate splits/merges chosen?).

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous typos/notation slips: “an square integrable,” “amiable” (nonstandard term), inconsistent use of subscripts (Rg vs R), missing definitions (IG vs IK), and stray “g” in R̂g. Equations (9)–(10) include undefined symbols and inconsistent notation.
- Several references lack years and complete bibliographic data. The conference header says “st Conference on NIPS 2017,” which appears to be a placeholder artifact.
- Figures/Tables are referred to but not present; crucial simulation details (n, p, true G, kernels, λ tuning) are missing.
- The penalty parameters μ and α: the paper alternates between validation and CV; selection protocols and data splits are not clearly specified.

Originality
- The notion of an intrinsic group partition and attempting to penalize structure via RKHS capacity is novel. However, related literatures on structured kernel learning/ANOVA sparsity and MKL are not adequately compared, making it hard to place the contribution.

Significance
- If the theory were rigorous and the penalty well-founded, an estimator that consistently recovers minimal group structure would be impactful. As it stands, the structural penalty is heuristic, the theory has gaps, and the empirical section is preliminary (no baselines, limited diagnostics). Practical scalability is not demonstrated.

Pros
- Interesting problem and useful modeling sweet spot between additive and full interactions.
- Conceptual framework for “intrinsic” partition.
- Attempt to connect structure selection to RKHS capacity.
- Some empirical evidence of structure recovery on synthetic data.

Cons
- Theoretical gaps (existence/uniqueness of G*, penalty derivation, covering number composition, missing assumptions).
- Heuristic penalty (α) breaks the claimed capacity connection.
- Insufficient algorithmic specification and complexity analysis.
- Sparse and incomplete experiments; no comparisons to baselines (e.g., SALSA, ANOVA kernels, OKGT, interaction lasso).
- Numerous writing, notation, and reference issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide precise assumptions and proofs for Theorem 1 (closure of Ga under meet; identifiability constraints).
- Derive a principled structural penalty with explicit dependence on ε_n and kernel constants, or position the penalty as heuristic and adjust claims accordingly.
- Prove Proposition 3 rigorously with norms/radii and constants.
- Specify kernels, hyperparameter selection (λ, bandwidths), identifiability constraints (e.g., centering of f_u), and give the representer theorem and resulting linear system.
- Replace the stepwise heuristic with a well-specified search (e.g., merge-split with beam search) and provide complexity/runtime.
- Expand experiments with baselines, predictive performance, and robustness; report n, p, true groups, and calibration of μ, α.
- Fix typos and complete references.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper has a promising idea but currently suffers from significant theoretical, methodological, and presentation issues. With rigorous proofs, a principled structural penalty, clearer algorithms, and stronger experiments, it could be a solid contribution.
