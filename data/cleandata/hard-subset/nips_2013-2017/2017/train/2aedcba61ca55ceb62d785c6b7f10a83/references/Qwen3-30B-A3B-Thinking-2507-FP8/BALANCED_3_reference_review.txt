

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses an important problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: identifying the group additive structure that minimizes dimensionality while maintaining model interpretability. The authors define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group structure that can represent the target function, solving the non-uniqueness problem that plagues previous group additive models [11]. They propose a structural penalty method using RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure, leading to a tractable optimization problem for structure identification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by rigorously defining the intrinsic group additive structure and proving its consistency under the proposed method. The formalization of the partial order on group structures and the demonstration of the uniqueness of the intrinsic structure are particularly strong.

2. **Novel complexity measure:** The use of RKHS covering numbers to define a complexity measure for group structures is well-motivated and theoretically sound. The derivation of the explicit complexity measure C(G) = ∑α|uj| is a key technical contribution that enables practical implementation.

3. **Empirical validation:** The simulation studies demonstrate the method's ability to successfully identify the true group structure across multiple model settings. The real data application on the Boston Housing dataset provides a meaningful illustration of the method's practical utility.

4. **Clear connection to existing work:** The paper thoughtfully positions itself relative to previous work on additive models [5, 13], functional ANOVA [4, 6], and graphical models [2, 7, 9], clearly articulating how their approach differs from these alternatives.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational scalability:** The exhaustive search algorithm (Algorithm 1) is computationally infeasible for large p, and while the stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) addresses this, it's described as "greedy" with potential for suboptimal solutions. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the computational trade-offs between the two algorithms and doesn't provide runtime complexity analysis.

2. **Limited comparison with related methods:** The paper doesn't compare its approach with alternative structure identification methods, particularly those from the graphical models literature (e.g., [9] on sparse block structures). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the argument for the proposed method's advantages.

3. **Practical implementation details:** The tuning parameter selection procedure (using validation sets) is described but not thoroughly evaluated. The paper doesn't address how sensitive the results are to the choice of α and µ, or provide guidance for practitioners on selecting these parameters.

4. **Assumptions in theory:** The theoretical consistency results rely on strong assumptions about the kernel function (exponential Fourier transform decay) and the relationship between λn and n. The paper would benefit from a discussion of how these assumptions might be relaxed or whether they are reasonable in practice.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**

1. Include a comparison with alternative structure identification methods, particularly those from the graphical models literature, to better establish the method's relative advantages.

2. Add a section discussing computational complexity for both algorithms, including runtime analysis for different values of p, to help readers understand practical limitations.

3. Provide more detailed guidance on tuning parameter selection, including sensitivity analysis for α and µ, and potentially exploring cross-validation strategies beyond the current 10-fold CV.

4. Discuss potential extensions to handle cases where the intrinsic group additive structure is not well-defined or when multiple structures have similar complexity.

5. Consider including a brief discussion of how the method might be extended to handle dependent predictor variables, as the current approach assumes independence (as noted in Proposition 1).

**Overall assessment:**

This is a well-written paper with a clear theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the group additive model literature. The proposed method for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure is theoretically sound and empirically validated. While the computational limitations of the exhaustive search approach are acknowledged, the paper could strengthen its contribution by providing a more thorough comparison with related methods and better practical implementation guidance.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of nonparametric regression, particularly for high-dimensional settings where interpretability and estimation efficiency are both critical. The method has clear potential for practical application in exploratory data analysis and model building.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel theoretical contribution that is well-supported by empirical results. While there are some limitations in computational scalability and comparative analysis, these do not undermine the core contribution, and the paper provides a solid foundation for future work in this area. The authors have made a valuable addition to the literature on structured nonparametric regression.
