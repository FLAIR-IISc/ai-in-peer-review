Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a group additive structure: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu) where G is a partition of predictors. The authors (i) formalize a partial order over partitions and define the intrinsic group additive structure G* as the unique minimal amiable partition under which f is representable; (ii) propose a structure-penalized kernel regression estimator that searches over partitions and penalizes structural complexity via a covering-number-based measure derived from RKHS capacity; (iii) prove selection consistency of the estimated partition under fixed p; and (iv) present an exhaustive-search algorithm for small p and a backward stepwise heuristic for larger p, with simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

Relation to prior work
This builds on additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), functional ANOVA/SS-ANOVA (Gu), higher-order interaction RKHS methods such as SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu), and OKGT (Pan et al.), which motivated grouping predictors. It also connects to graphical models (conditional dependence) but targets a different object (GA structure). Relevant related lines that should be discussed include: additive Gaussian processes and kernel structure discovery (e.g., Duvenaud et al., NIPS 2011; automatic compositional kernel learning), multiple kernel learning and hierarchical kernel learning (e.g., Bach, 2008–2009), and sparse/additive nonparametric models with group structure (e.g., SpAM variants, group-lasso-penalized additive models). These works also learn interactions/partitions via kernel composition or sparsity-inducing penalties and provide useful algorithmic and theoretical perspectives.

Strengths
- A clear formalization of group additive structures, a partial order over partitions, and the notion of an intrinsic (minimal) partition G*; the existence/uniqueness result is conceptually useful.
- The use of RKHS direct sums to parameterize group-wise components is natural; coupling this with a structural penalty targeting model capacity is a principled route to structure selection.
- The complexity penalty is linked to covering numbers of Gaussian RKHSs, yielding a dimension-aware measure increasing with group size; this aligns with the intuition that larger groups have higher capacity.
- Theoretical results separate amiable from non-amiable structures and establish selection consistency under fixed p, tying together empirical risk, capacity bounds, and a structural penalty.
- The work addresses a practical need: discovering low-order interactions without fully enumerating all pairwise/higher-order terms.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- The derivation and use of the complexity penalty are not fully coherent. Starting from log covering number bounds of the form O((log(r/ε))p+1) for a |u|-dimensional Gaussian RKHS, the paper proposes C(G)=∑u α(ε)|u|+1, then “simplifies” to ∑u α|u| by folding constants into μ. Changing the exponent from |u|+1 to |u| is not justified; absorbing multiplicative constants into μ cannot remove the +1 in the exponent. This gap matters because the penalty’s relative growth across group sizes governs consistency and practical selection.
- The step from Proposition 3 to the final penalty deserves a precise statement with constants: ln N(ε, ⊕j Hj) ≤ ∑j ln N(ε/|G|, Hj) is plausible by triangle inequality, but constants and norms should be handled carefully. Please provide a proof or citation and verify dependence on |G|.
- Assumptions needed for identifiability/separation are under-specified. Proposition 1’s strict inclusion L2G1 ⊂ L2G2 requires independence of Xj’s; similar conditions likely underpin Theorem 3 (positive approximation gap for non-amiable G). In realistic settings with correlated covariates, L2-approximation properties depend on P_X. The theorems should state distributional conditions (e.g., product measures or ANOVA/Hoeffding decomposability) explicitly.
- The generalization bounds in Proposition 4/Theorem 2 use uniform covering number bounds for squared loss but do not incorporate kernel bandwidths, eigenvalue decay, or data-dependent complexities (e.g., effective dimension). The presence of λ in the bound is unusual and constants are opaque; references or a sketch derivation would help establish credibility.
- The consistency theorem (Theorem 4) requires a “proper” μ but gives no scaling guidance (as a function of n, λ, p). Without rates or a data-driven choice, the result is of limited practical use.

Originality
- The intrinsic partition formalization is a nice conceptual contribution. Penalizing structure via RKHS capacity is interesting, though related ideas exist in MKL/hierarchical kernel learning and additive GP structure discovery. The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning relative to these.

Significance
- If made robust and computationally tractable, learning group partitions has clear practical value. However, the empirical evidence is limited: small simulations with partial success, no comparisons to strong baselines (SALSA, SS-ANOVA, additive GP/kernel learning, SpAM/grouped additive models), and a single real dataset without predictive benchmarks. This limits claims of impact.

Clarity
- Notation occasionally drifts (e.g., R̂ vs R̂g; mixing 1/n factors). The two-step procedure is clear, but Algorithm 2 is ambiguous: starting from the single-group partition, “split j as a new group or add to an existing group” needs precise definition; do you iterate until convergence, allow merging, or multiple passes? As written, it seems to do at most one pass of single-split moves, which is unlikely to locate good partitions.
- The choice of kernels per group (e.g., Gaussian with ARD length-scales) and bandwidth selection are not discussed; these choices strongly affect both fit and covering-number constants.

Experiments
- No baselines, no ablations on λ, no robustness to covariate correlation, and limited reporting (tables/figures referenced but not shown). Treating α as an integer hyperparameter 1–10 is ad hoc given its theoretical role as ln(r/ε); its interaction with μ and λ is unclear. The stepwise method fails on one model; no analysis of failure modes or improved heuristics (e.g., repeated passes, beam search, stochastic local search) is provided.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the penalty derivation: either use the theoretically justified ∑u (log(r/ε))|u|+1 form (with consistent constants) or provide a new derivation for the simplified penalty. Consider a data-dependent penalty (e.g., Rademacher/empirical entropy) to reduce sensitivity to unknown constants.
- State and justify the distributional assumptions (independence or product measure) under which Theorems 2–4 hold; discuss extensions to correlated X.
- Provide guidance or theory on choosing μ and λ (rates), and an automatic procedure (nested CV or structural risk minimization).
- Clarify and strengthen Algorithm 2 (clear move set, multiple iterations until no improvement, possibility of merges/splits, complexity analysis), and, if possible, give conditions under which it recovers G*.
- Compare against strong baselines on both synthetic and real data, and report predictive performance and structure recovery accuracy. Include scenarios with correlated predictors.
- Discuss kernel selection per group (e.g., ARD) and how it interacts with the penalty.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean formalization of intrinsic group additive structure.
- Principled attempt to tie structure selection to RKHS capacity.
- Consistency result (under fixed p) aligning with the proposed penalty.
- Addresses an important problem between fully additive and full-interaction models.

Cons for acceptance
- Gaps/inconsistencies in the penalty derivation; unclear constants and exponents.
- Under-specified assumptions for identifiability and separation; limited generality to correlated covariates.
- Weak empirical validation and no baselines; algorithmic component is heuristic and underspecified.
- Limited guidance on tuning and computational scalability.

Overall evaluation
Interesting idea with a promising theoretical direction, but the current form has technical gaps in the penalty construction, unclear assumptions, and insufficient empirical support. I recommend rejection at this stage, with the above revisions likely to substantially strengthen the contribution.
