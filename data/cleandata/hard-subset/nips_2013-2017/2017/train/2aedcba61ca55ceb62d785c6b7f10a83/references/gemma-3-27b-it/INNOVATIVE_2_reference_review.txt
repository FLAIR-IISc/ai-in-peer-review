This paper tackles the important problem of nonparametric regression in high dimensions, specifically addressing the limitations of both purely additive models and higher-order interaction models like ANOVA. The core idea – identifying a group additive structure (GAS) – is a compelling middle ground, and the proposed approach of penalizing group complexity via covering numbers is genuinely novel. This isn't just another incremental improvement; it's a fundamentally different way to approach interaction modeling, shifting focus from *all* possible interactions to identifying meaningful groupings. The theoretical guarantees, particularly the consistency proof, are a significant strength.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed GAS identification method, coupled with the RKHS-based complexity penalty, is a genuinely new contribution. It addresses a real gap in the existing literature.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The consistency proof provides strong theoretical support for the approach, a rarity in this area.
*   **Conceptual Clarity:** The introduction clearly articulates the motivation and positioning of the work within the broader landscape of nonparametric regression. The definition of the "intrinsic" group structure is insightful.
*   **Potential Significance:** Successfully identifying GAS could lead to more interpretable and efficient models, especially in applications where underlying group structures are expected.
*   **Connection to Graphical Models:** The authors acknowledge the relationship to graphical models, intelligently clarifying the difference in approach (targeting GAS directly vs. conditional dependence).

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While Algorithms 1 & 2 are presented, the exhaustive search (Algorithm 1) is clearly impractical for high-dimensional data. The stepwise approach (Algorithm 2) is an improvement but still carries the risk of getting stuck in local optima – the simulation results hint at this limitation with Model 4.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of µ and α, while tunable, could be crucial and require careful consideration. The paper acknowledges this, but more guidance on sensible ranges or automated selection strategies would be beneficial.
*   **Simulation Scope:** The simulation studies, while helpful, primarily focus on relatively simple models. Exploring more complex, realistic scenarios (e.g., with varying group sizes, stronger interactions) would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Real Data Analysis – Limited Depth:** The real data analysis provides a proof-of-concept, but lacks in-depth exploration of the identified groups and their implications. Deeper insights into the biological or domain-specific relevance of the groupings would be valuable.
*   **Penalty Justification:** While the theoretical justification for using covering numbers is present, a more intuitive explanation of *why* this particular complexity measure is well-suited for GAS identification would be helpful.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Highly novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   Strong theoretical backing with a consistency guarantee.
*   Potential for significant impact in applications requiring interpretable models.
*   Clear and well-written paper.

**Con:**

*   Computational scalability is a concern.
*   Parameter tuning could be challenging in practice.
*   Simulation studies could be more comprehensive.
*   Real-world application lacks extensive analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling and novel idea. While the computational limitations and simulation scope are concerns, the theoretical rigor and potential significance outweigh these weaknesses. The approach is a welcome departure from standard methods, and I believe it has the potential to spark further research in this area. I am willing to overlook the minor flaws and incomplete validations, given the boldness and novelty of the approach.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I would encourage the authors to address the computational scalability issue (perhaps exploring approximate search strategies) and expand the simulation studies in future work. Furthermore, more detailed insights for real-world applications would significantly strengthen the paper.
