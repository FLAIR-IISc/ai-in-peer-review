**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying interaction structures in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. It proposes a "group additive model," where the regression function is a sum of several lower-dimensional functions, each acting on a disjoint subset (group) of the predictor variables. The authors' main contributions are twofold. First, they formalize the concept of a unique, minimal "intrinsic group additive structure" (G*) for any square integrable function, providing a well-defined target for their method. Second, they propose a penalized estimation framework to simultaneously identify this structure and estimate the component functions. The method minimizes a kernel ridge regression objective plus a novel penalty term that penalizes the complexity of the group structure. This complexity measure is derived from the covering number of the associated reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). The authors provide a theoretical proof of consistency, showing that their method recovers the true intrinsic structure with probability approaching one as the sample size increases. They demonstrate the method's performance on synthetic data and a real-world dataset (Boston Housing).

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper provides a solid motivation for group additive models, positioning them as a sensible intermediate between overly simplistic additive models and overly complex fully nonparametric or functional ANOVA models. The formalization of the problem, particularly the definition of the partial order on group structures and the resulting unique "intrinsic group additive structure" (G*), is a definite strength. It provides a clear theoretical foundation for the work.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The main theoretical result—the consistency of the structure selection procedure (Theorem 4)—is significant. Proving that the proposed penalized estimator can correctly identify the true underlying group structure in the asymptotic limit lends considerable credibility to the approach. The theoretical development appears sound.

3.  **Interpretability:** The proposed method, when successful, yields an interpretable model. The Boston Housing data example effectively demonstrates how identifying small interacting groups of variables can lead to insightful visualizations and conclusions, which is a key advantage of this class of models.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** This is the most significant weakness of the paper. The authors propose a new method for structure identification in an additive modeling context, but the experiments are conducted in a vacuum. There are several well-established methods that tackle similar problems, such as sparse additive models (e.g., SpAM), methods that use group Lasso penalties to select interactions, or component selection methods like COSSO. A comparison against these or other relevant baselines is essential to demonstrate that the proposed method offers a tangible improvement over the state of the art. Without such a comparison, the practical utility and significance of the proposed approach remain unproven.

2.  **Ad-hoc Nature of the Complexity Penalty:** While motivated by the covering number of RKHSs, the final form of the penalty, `µ * Σ α^|uj|`, is a significant simplification of a theoretical upper bound. This simplification introduces two new tuning parameters, `µ` and `α`, whose selection seems non-trivial. The justification for this specific functional form over other potential capacity measures or simpler penalties (e.g., based on the number of groups or their sizes) is not thoroughly argued. This makes the penalty feel somewhat heuristic and raises questions about the sensitivity of the method to this specific choice and the tuning of its parameters.

3.  **Practical Algorithm vs. Theory:** The theoretical consistency result implicitly assumes that the minimization over all possible group structures `G` is feasible. However, for any non-trivial number of predictors, this is computationally intractable. The paper proposes a backward stepwise greedy algorithm as a practical solution. This is a standard heuristic, but it comes with no guarantee of finding the global optimum. The authors' own simulation results show this algorithm fails to find the true structure for one of the five models (M4). This creates a significant gap between what is proven in theory and what is done in practice. The limitations of this greedy approach should be more thoroughly discussed.

4.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation study is useful for demonstrating that the method *can* work under certain conditions, but it is not extensive. The failure on model M4 is acknowledged but not analyzed, which would have been instructive. The real-data application on the Boston Housing dataset is illustrative, but as there is no ground truth, it serves more as a case study than a rigorous validation of the method's accuracy.

**Conclusion**

The paper presents an interesting and theoretically well-founded approach to an important problem in nonparametric regression. The formalization of the intrinsic group additive structure is a valuable conceptual contribution. However, the work in its current form is let down by a critical lack of empirical comparison to existing methods. The novelty of the proposed penalty function is also tempered by its heuristic nature. While the consistency theorem is a strong point, the reliance on a greedy search algorithm in practice weakens the overall story. To be a compelling contribution for NIPS, the paper would need to be substantially strengthened with a rigorous experimental comparison against relevant and established baselines to demonstrate its practical significance.
