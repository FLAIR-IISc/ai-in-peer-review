This paper addresses the problem of identifying interaction structures in nonparametric regression. It proposes a middle ground between purely additive models, which assume no interactions, and fully nonparametric models, which suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The proposed "group additive model" posits that the regression function is a sum of functions, each of which depends on a disjoint subset (or group) of the predictor variables. Interactions are allowed within groups but not between them.

The main contributions are threefold. First, the paper formalizes the problem by defining an "intrinsic group additive structure," which is shown to be the unique, minimal correct structure for a given function. This provides a clear theoretical target for estimation. Second, it proposes a method, Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI), to simultaneously identify this structure and estimate the component functions. This is framed as a penalized optimization problem within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The novelty lies in a new penalty term on the group structure itself, which is motivated by the covering number of the associated RKHS. Third, the authors provide a proof of consistency, showing that their method recovers the true intrinsic structure asymptotically. The method is evaluated on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper provides a rigorous and well-motivated formalization of the group additive structure problem. The definition of a partial ordering on group structures and the subsequent proof of existence and uniqueness of a minimal "intrinsic" structure (Theorem 1) is a solid theoretical contribution that elegantly addresses the identifiability issue mentioned.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The primary strength of this work is its theoretical depth. Grounding the estimation procedure in RKHS theory is a standard and powerful approach. More importantly, the authors provide a consistency proof (Theorem 4) for the selection of the group structure. For a novel structure selection method, this is a crucial result that demonstrates the procedure is not merely a heuristic but is principled and asymptotically correct.

3.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The goal of finding a model that balances the interpretability of additive models with the flexibility to capture key interactions is a significant and long-standing problem in statistics and machine learning. This work places itself clearly within this research program and proposes a plausible solution.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The Complexity Penalty:** The central novel component of the proposed method is the complexity penalty `µ * sum(α^|uj|)`. While the authors provide a theoretical motivation based on upper bounds of RKHS covering numbers (Proposition 3 and Eq. 4), the final form feels somewhat disconnected and heuristic. The derivation relies on an upper bound, and the constants and other factors are absorbed into tunable hyperparameters (`µ` and `α`). It is not clear why this specific functional form is optimal or how sensitive the results are to this choice versus other penalties that also favor smaller, less complex groups. The work would be stronger if it provided more justification for this specific penalty or demonstrated its robustness.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation and Baselines:** This is the most significant weakness of the paper. The experiments focus exclusively on whether GASI can recover the true group structure on synthetic datasets where the ground truth is known. While this is a necessary sanity check, it is not sufficient. The paper fails to compare its method against any established baselines. A comprehensive evaluation should include comparisons in terms of both predictive accuracy and structure identification against alternative methods, such as:
    *   Standard interaction-capable models like Gradient Boosting Machines or Random Forests, from which variable importance and interaction strengths can be extracted.
    *   Sparse additive models or methods that use sparsity-inducing penalties to select interactions (e.g., variants of group Lasso for hierarchical interactions).
    *   Methods like COSSO (Component Selection and Smoothing Operator), which are also designed for variable selection in additive models.
    Without these comparisons, it is impossible to assess whether this new, complex method offers any practical advantage over existing, well-understood techniques.

3.  **Scalability and Algorithmic Concerns:** The paper proposes two algorithms: an exhaustive search, which is computationally infeasible for even a moderate number of variables, and a backward stepwise algorithm. Stepwise procedures are known to be greedy and can easily get stuck in poor local optima. Indeed, the authors' own results show that the backward algorithm fails to find the true structure for one of their five models. This significantly undermines the claim that the method is suitable for "large p scenarios." The practical utility of the proposed method seems limited given the high computational cost and the heuristic nature of the search algorithm for non-trivial problems.

4.  **Practical Complexity:** The proposed method involves tuning multiple hyperparameters: the RKHS regularization `λ`, and the two new structure penalty parameters `µ` and `α`, in addition to the kernel parameters themselves. This high tuning complexity, combined with the expensive search over partitions, makes the method difficult to use in practice and raises concerns about its stability.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   The paper presents a novel and theoretically rigorous framework for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression.
    *   The proof of selection consistency is a strong theoretical result that lends credibility to the approach.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   The experimental evaluation is critically lacking. The absence of any comparison to established baseline methods makes it impossible to judge the practical significance or utility of the proposed technique.
    *   The core complexity penalty, while theoretically motivated, appears ad-hoc in its final parameterization.
    *   The proposed algorithm for scaling the method is a simple greedy heuristic that is shown to fail, limiting the method's applicability to the small-p settings where exhaustive search is possible.

In conclusion, while the theoretical ideas presented in this paper are interesting and the formalization is sound, the work in its current form is incomplete. The lack of a thorough empirical comparison against relevant baselines is a major flaw. A new method must demonstrate not only that it works in principle on idealized problems, but also that it provides a tangible benefit over existing approaches. This paper has not yet met that standard.
