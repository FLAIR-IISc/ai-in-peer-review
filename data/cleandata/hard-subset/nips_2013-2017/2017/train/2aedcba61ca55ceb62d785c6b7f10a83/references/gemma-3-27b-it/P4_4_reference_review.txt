This paper tackles the problem of identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, aiming to bridge the gap between fully additive models and higher-order interaction models like functional ANOVA. The core idea is to define an “intrinsic” group additive structure – a minimal partitioning of predictors – and then develop a method to estimate this structure alongside the nonparametric functions themselves. The approach leverages kernel methods, RKHS theory, and introduces a complexity penalty on the group structure to guide the selection process. Two algorithms, exhaustive search and a stepwise approach, are proposed for identifying the structure.

The paper is well-motivated. High-dimensional nonparametric regression suffers from the curse of dimensionality, and imposing structure is a common strategy. Group additive models offer a middle ground between the simplicity of additive models and the complexity of full interaction models. The problem of identifying the *right* group structure is a natural and important one. The theoretical contribution – defining the intrinsic group additive structure and establishing consistency of the proposed estimator – is the strongest aspect of the work. The authors connect their work to graphical models, acknowledging similarities and highlighting the distinct focus on group additive structures.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis, particularly the consistency result (Theorem 4), is a significant contribution. The derivation of the complexity measure for group structures, based on covering numbers, is well-justified.
*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the problem of group additive structure identification and the concept of the “intrinsic” structure.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of RKHS theory, complexity penalties for group structure, and the specific focus on identifying the *intrinsic* structure is novel.
*   **Algorithmic Proposals:** The provision of both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms provides practical approaches for implementing the method.
*   **Connections to Related Work:** The authors appropriately position their work with respect to existing literature on additive models, functional ANOVA, graphical models, and kernel methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:**  The exhaustive search algorithm is impractical for high-dimensional problems. The backward stepwise algorithm is presented but its performance limitations are acknowledged (e.g., getting stuck in local optima, as demonstrated by the simulation results on Model 4). Further discussion on scalability and more efficient algorithms would be beneficial.
*   **Choice of Parameters:** The performance of the method appears sensitive to the choice of the tuning parameters μ and α. The paper describes a grid search for these parameters, but a more principled approach to parameter selection (e.g., based on theoretical considerations or cross-validation strategies tailored to model selection) would strengthen the work.
*   **Simulation Design:** While the simulation study demonstrates the method's ability to recover the true structure in some cases, the range of scenarios explored feels somewhat limited. Including more complex interactions, different noise distributions, and higher-dimensional problems would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
*   **Real Data Analysis:** The real data analysis is somewhat cursory. While the results are presented, a more in-depth discussion of the biological/domain-specific implications of the identified group structure would add value. The lack of a baseline comparison against other model selection methods is a drawback.
*   **Clarity in some sections:** The mathematical notation, particularly concerning covering numbers and RKHS capacities, could benefit from more detailed explanation.  Readers unfamiliar with these concepts might struggle to fully grasp the theoretical contributions. The discussion of Proposition 3, specifically the interplay between the covering numbers of individual RKHSs and the direct sum space, could be clarified.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
*   The consistency result is a significant contribution to the field.
*   The proposed method offers a potentially useful tool for exploratory data analysis and model interpretability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the proposed algorithms limits their applicability to high-dimensional problems.
*   The sensitivity to tuning parameters and the limited scope of the simulation study raise concerns about the robustness of the method.
*   The real data analysis lacks sufficient depth and comparison to other methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a clear contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the proposed method addresses an important problem. However, the practical limitations related to computational complexity and parameter tuning, as well as the somewhat limited empirical evaluation, warrant careful consideration.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by: (1) discussing strategies for improving the scalability of the algorithms, (2) exploring more principled approaches to parameter selection, (3) conducting a more comprehensive simulation study, and (4) providing a more in-depth analysis of the real data applications, including comparison to baseline methods. Improving the clarity of the mathematical notation would also be helpful.
