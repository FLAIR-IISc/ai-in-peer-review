This paper tackles the problem of identifying a group additive structure in nonparametric regression, aiming to balance the flexibility of nonparametric methods with the interpretability and efficiency of additive models. The core idea – finding a grouping of predictors that reflects underlying interactions – is a valuable one, situated well within the existing literature on additive models, functional ANOVA, and kernel methods. The authors attempt to formalize the notion of an "intrinsic" group structure and propose a penalized kernel ridge regression approach for its identification. 

While the motivation and high-level concepts are promising, the paper suffers from a remarkable number of flaws, ranging from superficial theoretical justification to inconsistencies in notation and algorithmic description. These issues, while individually minor, accumulate to create a deeply problematic presentation. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** The problem of identifying structure in high-dimensional nonparametric regression is important.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and positions the group additive model as a reasonable compromise.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors adequately situate their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant papers on additive models, ANOVA, and kernel methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Gaps & Imprecision:** The definition of the "intrinsic" group additive structure relies on a partial order. While mathematically sound, the practical implications and identifiability guarantees beyond the theoretical definition are unclear. Proposition 1, stating L2G1 ⊆ L2G2 if G1 ≤ G2, is trivially true and doesn't offer much insight. Proposition 2, stating that if G ≤ G′, then G′ is amiable, is also straightforward. The theoretical contribution feels minimal.
*   **Complexity Measure Issues:** The choice of covering number as a complexity measure, while standard, is poorly justified in the context of group structures. The connection between the covering number and the *structural* complexity (i.e., the number of groups) seems tenuous. The parameter α, representing the “radius” of the covering, is introduced without sufficient explanation of its impact on the penalty and its practical tuning. The connection between α and the choice of kernel is not well described.
*   **Algorithmic Ambiguity:** Algorithm 1 (exhaustive search) lacks detail. How is the grid for (µ, α) defined? What stopping criterion is used? Algorithm 2 (backward stepwise) is equally vague. The phrase "add to an existing group" is ambiguous – is this merging groups, splitting a group, or something else? The choice of which variable to split or merge is not specified.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** There are multiple instances of inconsistent notation. For example, 'd' is used to denote both the number of groups *and* the dimension of the input space. `Hu1 ⊕···⊕Hud` uses `Hu` instead of `Huj` in the summation. `f̂λ,µ, Ĝ` introduces subscripts in a way that isn't consistently followed throughout.
*   **Empirical Evaluation Concerns:** The simulation results, while demonstrating the method *can* work, lack a thorough comparative analysis. The primary metric is “frequency of correct identification”, which doesn’t tell us anything about the quality of the estimated functions *given* a potentially incorrect structure. The real data example is descriptive but doesn’t quantify the improvement over standard nonparametric methods.
*   **Minor Errors & Typos:** Numerous minor grammatical and typographical errors exist throughout the manuscript, detracting from its readability and professionalism.
*   **Overclaiming:** The authors state "GASI has the power to identify true group structures." This claim is too strong, given the limited empirical evidence and the potential for local optima in the optimization problem.
* **Lack of Discussion on Computational Cost:** The computational cost of the proposed approach, especially the exhaustive search algorithm, is not adequately addressed. The authors acknowledge this issue, but there’s no concrete discussion of scalability.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The theoretical contributions are incremental and lack depth.
*   The algorithmic descriptions are vague and incomplete.
*   The empirical evaluation is insufficient and lacks comparative analysis.
*   Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies detract from the paper’s quality.
*   The paper feels incomplete and rushed.

**For:**

*   The problem addressed is relevant and important.
*   The idea of a group additive structure is a reasonable compromise between flexibility and interpretability.
*   The connection to existing work is well-established.

**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents an interesting idea, the numerous flaws in its theoretical justification, algorithmic description, empirical evaluation, and presentation significantly outweigh its strengths. The paper feels like a preliminary exploration of the idea rather than a complete and rigorous scientific contribution. Significant revisions, addressing all the points raised above, would be required for reconsideration. Specifically, the theoretical claims need to be strengthened, the algorithms need to be clearly specified, a more comprehensive empirical evaluation is needed, and the manuscript needs a thorough proofreading. Even with these changes, the paper’s novelty may remain questionable given the existing literature.
