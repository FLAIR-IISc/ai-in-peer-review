

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses a significant limitation in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: the non-uniqueness of group additive structures, which prevents consistent identification of the optimal grouping of predictor variables. The authors define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group structure that can represent the true function, resolving the non-identifiability issue. They propose a structural penalty method using RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure for group structures, enabling simultaneous identification of the intrinsic structure and nonparametric function estimation.

The paper builds on additive models [5] and functional ANOVA models [4, 13] while extending the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) method [11]. Unlike previous work, the authors directly target group additive structure rather than conditional dependence structure, as clarified in their discussion of graphical models [2, 7, 9]. The theoretical framework is well-grounded in RKHS theory, with clear definitions of group additive structures, amiable structures, and the intrinsic structure.

**Quality:** The theoretical contributions are strong, with rigorous proofs of consistency (Theorems 2-4) that build on established RKHS capacity theory. The authors derive a tractable complexity measure based on covering numbers that directly addresses the group structure identification problem. The simulation studies (Section 4) demonstrate the method's ability to identify true group structures across multiple model settings, and the Boston Housing data application (Section 5) provides a practical illustration of the method's interpretability benefits. The approach is technically sound and well-supported by both theory and experiments.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Key concepts are defined precisely (e.g., Definitions 1-2, Theorem 1), and the relationship between group structures and function spaces is clearly established (Proposition 1). The two-step estimation procedure (Section 2.4) and algorithms (Algorithms 1-2) are described with sufficient detail for replication. The connection to prior work is thoroughly discussed, and the distinction between amiable and non-amiable group structures is particularly well-explained.

**Originality:** The concept of the intrinsic group additive structure is novel and addresses a fundamental problem in the literature. The complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers (C(G) = ∑ⱼ α^|uⱼ|) represents a significant methodological contribution. While group additive models [11] and graphical models [2, 7, 9] have been studied, the authors clearly distinguish their approach by focusing directly on group additive structure identification rather than conditional dependence structure. The paper demonstrates sufficient novelty compared to previous NIPS work.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to high-dimensional nonparametric regression, where efficiency and interpretability are critical challenges. The group additive model provides a middle ground between fully additive models (no interactions) and full interaction models (curse of dimensionality). The proposed method offers a practical approach to identifying interpretable group structures, with clear potential for applications in exploratory data analysis and high-dimensional regression. The real data application demonstrates the method's utility in producing interpretable models.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by defining and identifying the intrinsic group additive structure. The complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers is well-motivated and effective. The experimental results strongly support the theoretical claims, with consistent identification of true group structures across multiple simulation scenarios. The connection to graphical models is well-justified, and the paper provides a thorough comparison with related work.

**Weaknesses:** The real data application is limited to a single dataset (Boston Housing), which somewhat restricts the demonstration of broad practical utility. The paper does not compare with alternative approaches for group structure identification, though this may be due to the novelty of the method. The computational complexity for very large p is not fully addressed beyond the stepwise algorithm, though the authors acknowledge this as a future direction.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to high-dimensional nonparametric regression by resolving the non-uniqueness problem in group additive structures. The proposed method is theoretically sound, empirically validated, and addresses a clearly defined gap in the literature. The work has clear potential for both practical applications and theoretical extensions.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The limited real data application and lack of comparison with alternative methods are minor limitations, but they do not undermine the paper's core contributions.

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of nonparametric regression, with clear theoretical advances and practical implications. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a significant problem in high-dimensional statistical modeling. I recommend acceptance.
