Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses nonparametric regression when f(X) admits a group-additive structure: f(X) = ∑j fu_j(X_u_j), where groups u_j form a partition of variables. This sits between univariate additive models (GAM) and high-order interaction models (functional ANOVA, kernel ESP/ANOVA kernels), aiming to capture within-group interactions while avoiding combinatorial explosion. The authors (i) formalize a notion of intrinsic group additive structure G* as the minimal (w.r.t. a refinement partial order) amiable partition for which f belongs to ⊕u L2_u, (ii) propose to learn both G and f via RKHS regression with a structural penalty based on a covering-number capacity bound for the direct-sum RKHS, and (iii) prove selection consistency under their penalty and provide two search procedures (exhaustive and a backward stepwise heuristic). Simulations and a Boston Housing example illustrate feasibility.

This builds on classic additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), SS-ANOVA (Gu), and recent interaction kernels (e.g., additive/interaction KRR including ESP kernels; cf. SALSA, Kandasamy & Yu). It is related to structured sparsity/MKL and hierarchical kernel learning that select additive/interaction components in RKHSs, and to sparse additive models (e.g., Ravikumar et al., Meier et al.)—though the present work focuses specifically on learning a partition of variables.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for partition-based structures, which trade off flexibility and interpretability compared to GAMs and full ANOVA.
- Formalization of the intrinsic (minimal) group structure and the refinement partial order is conceptually useful.
- Use of direct-sum RKHS with universal kernels gives a flexible modeling class for group-wise multivariate functions.
- Structural penalty derived from covering-number bounds of direct-sum RKHSs yields a principled (if coarse) complexity control and enables a selection-consistency result.
- The framework is modular: any kernel per group; algorithms for small p (exhaustive) and larger p (greedy).
- Empirical results show some success at recovering planted structures.

Weaknesses
Quality
- The capacity penalty C(G) ultimately reduces to a sum over groups of α|u|, with α treated as a tuning parameter; this ignores kernel- and data-dependent constants, and collapses capacity differences to group sizes. The gap between the theory (covering-number bounds) and the practical penalty is large.
- Assumptions required for uniqueness/existence of G* and the key inclusions (e.g., independence of covariates used in Proposition 1) are not stated prominently with sufficient clarity in the main text. It is unclear under what conditions Ga forms a set with a unique minimal element; counterexamples without independence or with overlapping interactions seem possible.
- The selection-consistency theorem is non-constructive: “choose a proper µ” and λ_n n → 0, but no scaling guidance in terms of n, p, kernel bandwidth, or signal-to-noise is provided. Finite-sample rates are not quantified.
- Algorithmic scalability is limited. The backward stepwise search has no guarantees and fails on some settings; no stronger search (split-merge, beam search, stochastic search) or relaxation is provided.
- Empirical evaluation lacks baselines. No comparisons to SALSA, kernel ANOVA/ESP kernels, sparse additive models, MKL/structured sparsity, or hierarchical kernel learning. Predictive performance and runtime are not reported comprehensively.

Clarity
- Some notation is overloaded (fG, fG*, f∗G∗). The presentation of α = ln(r/ε) vs. tuning α ∈ {1,…,10} is confusing; the dependence on ε and r disappears in practice without justification.
- Proof conditions and key assumptions (e.g., independence, distributional conditions on X, universality choice, regularization scaling) are deferred to the supplement; the main text should summarize them.
- Minor typos/grammar; a few equations mix subscripts/superscripts inconsistently.

Originality
- Framing structure discovery as learning a partition with an RKHS direct-sum penalty is interesting. However, related strands (structured sparsity in RKHS, hierarchical kernel learning for interactions, sparse additive models) are not fully discussed, making it harder to position novelty precisely.

Significance
- Potentially useful for discovering interacting blocks of variables with interpretable groups. Current empirical scope and scalability limits reduce impact. Without stronger algorithms and comparisons, it’s hard to assess practical advantage.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptual contribution: intrinsic group structure and refinement order.
- A theoretically motivated structural penalty linked to RKHS capacity and a selection-consistency result.
- A promising middle ground between GAMs and full interaction models with interpretability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficiently constructive theory (no data-dependent tuning or finite-sample rates).
- Fragile identifiability/uniqueness story; assumptions unclear in the main text.
- Algorithmic approach does not scale and lacks guarantees; empirical performance mixed.
- No comparisons to strong, closely related baselines; limited real-data evidence.

Suggestions for improvement
- State explicit assumptions ensuring uniqueness of G* (e.g., independence, non-degeneracy) in the main text; discuss when uniqueness can fail.
- Bridge theory and practice: derive a data-dependent penalty (e.g., Rademacher/local Rademacher-based, or BIC-like degrees-of-freedom for KRR) and provide guidance for λ, µ, α scaling with n, p, |u|.
- Add strong baselines: SALSA/higher-order ANOVA kernels, sparse additive models (SpAM), hierarchical kernel learning/structured sparsity in RKHS, and graphical-model-based interaction discovery. Report both structure recovery and prediction metrics with statistical significance.
- Improve search: split-merge or beam search over partitions; stochastic local search; Bayesian partition models; or differentiable relaxations for partitions. Provide runtime analyses and ablations.
- Discuss and, if possible, extend beyond disjoint partitions to allow overlapping interactions, or justify the restriction and its implications.
- Use scalable kernel approximations (Nyström, random features) and report computational complexity.
- Clarify the role of universal kernels and bandwidth selection; analyze sensitivity to kernel choices.

Recommendation: Weak Reject
The ideas are interesting and the goal is important, but the current version lacks sufficiently constructive theory, robust algorithms, and empirical validation against relevant baselines to meet the bar for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to strengthen assumptions/guarantees, tighten the penalty–theory connection, and expand experiments; this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: 0.7.
