### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of identifying variable interactions in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. Standard additive models are interpretable and avoid the curse of dimensionality but assume no interactions between predictors. Conversely, models that include all possible interactions, like functional ANOVA models, quickly become computationally intractable and statistically inefficient. The authors propose a middle ground using a "group additive model," where the regression function is a sum of nonparametric functions, each of which depends on a distinct group of interacting variables: f(X) = Σ_j f_{u_j}(X_{u_j}). This structure allows for interactions within groups while maintaining additivity between them.

The primary contributions of this work are threefold. First, it formalizes the notion of an "intrinsic group additive structure" (G*), proving it is the unique, minimal representation among all valid group structures for a given function, thereby addressing a key non-identifiability issue. Second, it proposes a method, Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI), to simultaneously estimate the regression function and identify G*. This is achieved by minimizing a penalized objective function that includes the empirical risk, a standard RKHS norm penalty, and a novel penalty term for the complexity of the group structure G itself. Third, this structural complexity penalty is principled, derived from the capacity of the corresponding direct sum RKHS, specifically using its covering number. The authors prove that their method is consistent for selecting the intrinsic group structure. They propose both an exhaustive search algorithm for small numbers of predictors and a backward stepwise algorithm for larger problems, demonstrating the method's effectiveness on synthetic and real-world data. This work builds upon prior notions of group additive models (e.g., Pan et al., 2011) by providing a rigorous theoretical foundation and a systematic method for structure identification.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formalization of the problem through the definition of a partial order on group structures and the proof of existence and uniqueness of an "intrinsic group additive structure" (Theorem 1) is a strong theoretical contribution. It provides a clear target for the estimation procedure. The main theoretical result, the consistency of the structure selection (Theorem 4), provides strong justification for the proposed penalized objective function. The derivation of the penalty term from the covering number of an RKHS gives the method a principled, non-heuristic foundation. The experimental results on synthetic data generally support the claim that the method can recover the true underlying structure.

However, there are some weaknesses. The performance of the backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2), which is necessary for practical application beyond a very small number of variables, is a potential concern. The authors honestly report that it fails on one of their five simulation models (Model 4), suggesting that the greedy nature of the search can be a significant limitation. The practical applicability of the method also depends heavily on the selection of three tuning parameters (λ, μ, α), which can be a challenging optimization problem in its own right.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the proposed method between existing approaches. The progression from the theoretical definition of the intrinsic structure to the development of the penalized estimation framework is easy to follow.

A point of minor confusion arises in the derivation of the final complexity penalty in Section 2.3.2. The paper starts with a bound proportional to `(ln(r/epsilon))^(|u|+1)`, reparameterizes this as `α(|u|+1)`, and then presents the final penalty in Equation (6) as `Σ α^|uj|`. The justification provided for this final form ("By combining the common factor 1 in the exponent with µ, we could further simplify...") is not entirely transparent and could benefit from a more detailed explanation. Additionally, the description of Algorithm 2 is high-level; a more detailed specification of the "split" and "add to an existing group" steps would improve reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper's core ideas are novel and represent a significant contribution. While group additive models and kernel methods are established concepts, the formulation of a principled penalty term for the group partition *itself*, derived from RKHS capacity measures, is a novel approach to structure learning in this context. The formalization of the intrinsic group structure is also an original contribution that provides a solid theoretical basis for the work. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from prior work, particularly [11], by addressing the identifiability problem and proposing a concrete, theoretically-backed identification method.

**Significance:** The problem of discovering interaction structures in high-dimensional data is of fundamental importance in many scientific fields. This work provides a valuable tool that balances model flexibility, statistical efficiency, and interpretability. The ability to automatically partition variables into interacting groups can lead to more insightful and accurate models. The application to the Boston Housing data, for instance, yields interpretable bivariate functions that reveal non-linear interaction effects. Researchers and practitioners in fields requiring interpretable predictive models could find this method highly useful for exploratory data analysis and model building. The primary limitation to its widespread adoption may be the computational cost and the robustness of the heuristic search algorithm for high-dimensional settings (large p).

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper rigorously defines the "intrinsic group additive structure" and proves consistency for its identification method. This is a complete piece of work with solid theoretical backing.
2.  **Novel and Principled Penalty:** The proposed penalty on group structures is novel and derived from the sound theoretical concept of RKHS covering numbers, avoiding ad-hoc choices.
3.  **Addresses an Important Problem:** The method provides an elegant solution to the fundamental trade-off between simple additive models and complex, fully nonparametric models, enabling interpretable interaction discovery.
4.  **Clear Contribution over Prior Art:** The work substantially advances the state of group additive modeling by solving key identifiability and estimation problems left open by previous research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The exhaustive search is computationally infeasible for more than a handful of variables. The proposed backward stepwise heuristic is not guaranteed to find the optimal solution and failed in one of the reported experiments, raising questions about its reliability.
2.  **Tuning Parameter Sensitivity:** The method's performance depends on the selection of three hyperparameters (λ, μ, α), which can be difficult to tune effectively in practice.
3.  **Minor Lack of Clarity in Penalty Derivation:** The final form of the complexity penalty is a simplification of the theoretically derived bound, and the justification for this step could be clearer.
