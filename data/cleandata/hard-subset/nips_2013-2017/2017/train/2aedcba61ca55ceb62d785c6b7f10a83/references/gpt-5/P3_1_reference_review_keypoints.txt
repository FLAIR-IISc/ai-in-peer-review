- Paper proposes "group additive" nonparametric regression: decompose f as sum of multivariate functions over a partition of predictors to balance interactions and interpretability.  
- Formalizes a partial order over partitions, defines "amiable" structures, and claims a unique minimal intrinsic group structure G*.  
- Introduces a structural-penalized KRR: direct-sum RKHS for a candidate partition plus a capacity penalty C(G) based on covering numbers; selects G by minimizing empirical risk + RKHS norm + µ C(G).  
- Provides concentration bounds and a selection-consistency theorem stating P(Ĝ = G*) → 1 under certain conditions.  
- Offers exhaustive and backward stepwise algorithms and presents simulations plus a Boston Housing case study.  
- Relates to additive models, functional ANOVA/SS-ANOVA, SALSA, MKL/additive kernels, SpAM, hierarchical interaction selection, and graphical models, but some prior work comparisons are insufficient.  
- Strength: novel, practically relevant formulation that interpolates between no-interaction and full-interaction models.  
- Strength: clean lattice/partial-order conceptualization and well-motivated intrinsic structure target.  
- Strength: natural RKHS framework and appealing idea to tie structure selection to RKHS capacity.  
- Strength: theory offers a pathway to selection consistency and distinguishes amiable vs non-amiable structures.  
- Strength: simple algorithms and interpretable visualizations on real data.  
- Major technical concern: uniqueness of G* relies on Ga being closed under meets, but closure is not proved and may fail without extra assumptions.  
- Major technical concern: identifiability constraints for additive components (e.g., zero-mean) are not stated, undermining uniqueness and decomposition arguments.  
- Major technical concern: Proposition 1 and uniqueness claims assume independence or orthogonality; real covariate dependence undermines strict inclusion and interpretation.  
- Major technical concern: capacity penalty derivation is heuristic—dropping +1, treating α as a tunable integer, and implicit ε weaken theoretical linkage; compact-domain assumptions conflict with Gaussian X.  
- Major technical concern: selection consistency applies to the global minimizer with prescribed λn, µn; practical greedy algorithm and CV-chosen hyperparameters lack theoretical coverage.  
- Major technical concern: bounds are loose; no explicit scaling of µ with n, p, or group sizes and no tuning guidance.  
- Algorithmic/empirical concern: partition search is combinatorial; no complexity analysis or scalable solvers and the greedy stepwise method can get stuck.  
- Empirical concern: missing baselines (SALSA, SpAM+interactions, MKL, HSIC-based methods, OKGT) and insufficient experimental details (n, p, group sizes, kernel/bandwidth, λ selection).  
- Empirical concern: simulations focus on exact recovery frequency, omit predictive performance and robustness to correlation/noise; hyperparameter sensitivity and instability observed.  
- Real-data concern: analysis is anecdotal, lacks competitive baselines, statistical tests, and may reflect greedy search bias toward small groups.  
- Clarity issue: notational inconsistencies, informal derivations; needs explicit assumptions (noise, kernel boundedness, domain compactness, independence).  
- Clarity issue: make Proposition 3 rigorous (HG-ball decomposition) and clarify kernel/bandwidth choices per group.  
- Originality/significance: idea is novel and interesting, but penalty/selection framework is heuristic and algorithmic/empirical work is preliminary.  
- Suggestions: strengthen theory (identifiability, closure conditions, explicit µn scaling, use L2-covering numbers), develop scalable search (merge-split MCMC, agglomerative/divisive, convex relaxations), adopt data-driven penalties (BIC/effective df), and expand experiments with strong baselines and ablations.  
- Pros summary: addresses gap between additive and full-interaction models, clear intrinsic target, leverages RKHS with some selection consistency.  
- Cons summary: gaps in identifiability and uniqueness claims, heuristic complexity penalty and tuning guidance, lack of scalable optimization and weak empirical comparisons.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject — promising idea but requires stronger theory, better empirical validation, and more robust algorithms to meet conference standards.
