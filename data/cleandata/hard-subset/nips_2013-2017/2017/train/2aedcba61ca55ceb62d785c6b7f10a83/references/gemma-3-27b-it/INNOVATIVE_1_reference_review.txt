This paper tackles the important problem of nonparametric regression in high dimensions, specifically addressing the limitations of both purely additive models and higher-order interaction models like functional ANOVA. The core idea – identifying a *group* additive structure – is a compelling and relatively novel approach that sits in a sweet spot between overly restrictive additivity and computationally expensive full interaction modeling. The authors correctly identify the non-uniqueness of group structures and propose a complexity-penalized kernel method to find an “intrinsic” structure. The theoretical results establishing consistency are a significant strength, and the empirical validation, while not exhaustive, is promising.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly identifying group additive structures, coupled with a complexity penalty, is genuinely novel. It offers a principled way to balance model complexity and interpretability, a crucial aspect often overlooked in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The mathematical formulation, including the definition of the intrinsic group additive structure and the consistency proof, demonstrates a strong theoretical understanding of the problem. The use of covering numbers as a complexity measure is a reasonable choice.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and motivate the need for their method.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper correctly situates the work within the broader context of additive models, functional ANOVA, and graphical models.
*   **Algorithm Development:** Providing both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms provides practical implementations of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** Despite the stepwise algorithm, identifying the optimal group structure remains computationally intensive, particularly with a large number of predictors. This is acknowledged, but more discussion on scalability limitations would be valuable.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of λ, µ, and α. While the simulations show a range of successful parameters, a more robust sensitivity analysis, perhaps with guidance on parameter selection based on data characteristics, is needed.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation studies, while informative, are somewhat limited in scope. More diverse data generating processes and higher-dimensional settings would strengthen the empirical validation. The real data application is a good start, but a comparison with existing methods on publicly available datasets would be beneficial.
*   **RKHS Choice:** The paper states that universal kernels are important for consistency, but doesn’t deeply explore the impact of *different* universal kernels.
*   **Algorithm 2 limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of the backward stepwise algorithm.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to group additive structure identification for kernel nonparametric regression. The consistency guarantee is a strong result. While there are some limitations in the empirical validation and computational scalability, the potential impact of this work is significant. The authors identify a crucial gap between purely additive models and full interaction models and provide a principled method for bridging that gap. The potential for interpretable, efficient nonparametric regression in high dimensions is substantial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is highly novel and addresses an important problem.
*   The theoretical results are rigorous and provide a strong foundation for the method.
*   The proposed approach offers a potentially significant improvement over existing techniques.
*   The algorithms are well-defined and provide a starting point for practical implementation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Computational cost could limit applicability to very high-dimensional datasets.
*   Empirical evaluation is not exhaustive and could be strengthened.
*   The sensitivity to tuning parameters requires further investigation.



**Recommendation:**

**Accept.** Despite some limitations, the novelty, theoretical strength, and potential impact of this work outweigh its shortcomings. I believe it represents a valuable contribution to the field and is well-suited for presentation at NIPS. The minor flaws can be addressed in future work, but the core idea is strong enough to warrant acceptance. I would encourage the authors to address the scalability concerns and conduct more comprehensive empirical evaluations in subsequent revisions.
