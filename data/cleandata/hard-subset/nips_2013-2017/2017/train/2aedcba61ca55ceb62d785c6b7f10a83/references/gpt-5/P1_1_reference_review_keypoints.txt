- Paper studies nonparametric regression with a group additive structure f(X)=Σ_j f_{u_j}(X_{u_j}) and defines a partial order on partitions to identify a unique minimal "intrinsic" partition.  
- Method: fit direct-sum RKHS HG = ⊕_{u∈G} H_u with kernel ridge regression plus a structural penalty µ C(G) motivated by covering number bounds.  
- Derive proxy penalty C(G) ∝ Σ_u α^{|u|} from covering number inequalities for kernels with exponentially decaying Fourier spectra (e.g., Gaussian).  
- Propose two algorithms: exhaustive search over partitions for small p and a greedy backward stepwise splitting heuristic for larger p.  
- Provide selection consistency results (for fixed p) showing the selected partition converges to the intrinsic structure under appropriate regularization.  
- Empirical evaluation: small synthetic experiments and one real-data (Boston Housing) example showing some recovery and interpretability.  
- Strength: clear formalization of group additive structures, sensible partial order, and notion of intrinsic minimal structure.  
- Strength: principled use of direct-sum RKHS and covering-number-based monotone complexity proxy across partitions.  
- Strength: theoretical uniform deviation bounds distinguishing amiable vs non-amiable structures and a final consistency theorem (fixed p).  
- Weakness: experiments are limited—small-scale, no comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., SS-ANOVA, SALSA, hierarchical kernel learning), no runtime/complexity analysis, and only modest p in real data.  
- Weakness: proofs rely on kernel-specific covering bounds (e.g., Gaussian-like kernels); dependence of constants on kernel and dimension and robustness to kernel misspecification are not discussed.  
- Weakness: consistency is proved only for fixed p; stepwise greedy algorithm (aimed at larger p) has no theoretical guarantees and can fail on some models.  
- Weakness: path from ln N bounds to practical penalty C(G)=Σ α^{|u|} is heuristic; interpretation and tuning of α and µ and their scaling with n are not justified.  
- Weakness: clarity gaps on identifiability—whether independence is required for uniqueness versus strict inclusion, and lacking a constructive definition of G* (e.g., meet of amiable partitions).  
- Weakness: algorithmic details are insufficient—partition search complexity, how splits are generated in the greedy routine, number of passes, stopping criteria, and KRR implementation for direct-sum kernels.  
- Weakness: missing related work on structured kernel learning, hierarchical kernel learning, structured sparsity for interactions, and multiple kernel learning over subsets.  
- Quality assessment: theoretical development is mostly sound within stated setting; penalty is a reasonable monotone proxy but practical instantiation is ad hoc; empirical evidence insufficient.  
- Clarity assessment: generally well-written but needs clearer exposition of α, relation to ε/r, explicit kernel/input distribution assumptions, and a more detailed Algorithm 2.  
- Originality: novel notion of intrinsic group additive structure and RKHS-capacity-based identification, but connections to interaction-selection literature underexplored.  
- Significance: potentially important if scalable and robust to moderate/high p, but current experiments do not demonstrate practical impact.  
- Recommendations: add empirical comparisons to relevant baselines, evaluate robustness to correlated covariates and kernel misspecification, provide guidance/theory for µ and α as functions of n, and detail/improve the stepwise search and complexity analysis.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak reject—interesting theory and principled idea but needs stronger empirical validation, clearer positioning, and more detailed algorithms to meet NeurIPS standards.
