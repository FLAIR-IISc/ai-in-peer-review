**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying the group additive structure in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. Additive models are a popular way to circumvent the curse of dimensionality, but standard additive models assume no interactions between predictors. This work generalizes this by assuming the regression function is a sum of functions, where each function can depend on a group of variables, i.e., $f(X) = \sum_{j=1}^{d} f_{u_j}(X_{u_j})$. The central challenge is to identify the unknown partition of variables into groups, $G = \{u_1, ..., u_d\}$.

The authors make two main contributions. First, they formalize the problem by defining a partial order on group structures and proving the existence and uniqueness of a minimal, "intrinsic group additive structure" for any square-integrable function. This provides a clear theoretical target for identification. Second, they propose a method to simultaneously estimate the regression function and identify this intrinsic structure. The method, termed Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI), is based on kernel regression in a direct sum of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) corresponding to a given group structure G. To select the best structure G, they introduce a novel penalty term on the complexity of the group structure itself. This penalty is derived from the capacity of the associated RKHS, specifically an upper bound on its covering number. The authors prove that, under certain conditions, their method is consistent in identifying the true intrinsic group structure. They propose an exhaustive search algorithm for problems with a small number of predictors and a backward stepwise algorithm for larger problems. The method's performance is demonstrated on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development in Section 2.1, which establishes the concept of a unique intrinsic group additive structure, is sound and provides a solid foundation for the work. The main theoretical result, the consistency of the group structure selection (Theorem 4), is a significant strength and lends strong support to the proposed approach. The derivation of the structural penalty from RKHS covering numbers (Section 2.3.2) is principled and well-motivated.

However, there are weaknesses that detract from the overall quality.
1.  **Algorithms:** There is a significant disconnect between the theoretical objective function (Equation 6) and the practical algorithms for optimizing it. The exhaustive search (Algorithm 1) is computationally infeasible for all but very small numbers of predictors `p`, as the number of partitions (Bell numbers) grows super-exponentially. The paper acknowledges this and proposes a backward stepwise heuristic (Algorithm 2). This algorithm is greedy, lacks theoretical guarantees, and its empirical performance is mixed (it fails to identify the correct structure for "Model 4" in the simulations). This practical limitation is a major weakness, as it calls into question the method's utility for the high-dimensional settings it aims to address.
2.  **Theoretical Assumptions:** The conditions for the consistency proof could be stated more clearly. For instance, in Theorem 4, the condition "$\lambda_n \cdot n \to 0$" is unusual in kernel regression literature, where conditions like $\lambda_n \to 0$ and $n\lambda_n \to \infty$ are more common. Clarification on this assumption and its implications would be beneficial.
3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental validation is limited. The simulations are conducted on low-dimensional problems ($p \le 6$), which does not adequately test the method's performance in the high-dimensional scenarios mentioned in the introduction. Crucially, the paper lacks any comparison to alternative methods for interaction detection or structure discovery (e.g., sparse functional ANOVA methods like COSSO, or interaction detection via random forests). Without such baselines, it is difficult to assess the practical advantages and state-of-the-art performance of GASI.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The motivation for group additive models is clear, and the authors do a good job of logically developing their method, starting from the problem definition and leading to the proposed penalized objective function. The definitions and propositions are stated clearly. The algorithms are presented in a straightforward manner.

A minor point for improvement would be to provide more intuition or practical guidance regarding the tuning parameter $\alpha$. Its theoretical motivation as $\ln(r/\epsilon)$ is understood, but its practical effect and the reason for choosing an integer grid `[1, 10]` are not well explained.

**Originality**

The paper is highly original. While group additive models have been considered before (e.g., Pan et al., 2015), this work is the first to provide a rigorous definition of the identification problem through the concept of an intrinsic structure and to propose a systematic, theoretically-backed method for its discovery. The core novel contribution is the design of a penalty on the group structure's complexity based on RKHS capacity measures. This is a novel and elegant way to regularize the model space at the structural level, moving beyond standard parameter-level regularization.

**Significance**

The problem of learning interaction structures in a nonparametric context is of great importance to machine learning and statistics. A method that can reliably identify such structures would be a significant contribution, improving model interpretability and predictive performance while mitigating the curse of dimensionality.

The theoretical ideas presented in this paper are significant and could inspire future work in structural learning. However, the practical significance of the proposed GASI method is currently limited by its computational cost and the reliance on a heuristic search algorithm with mixed performance. For the method to be widely adopted by practitioners, more scalable and robust optimization algorithms are needed, along with a more thorough empirical comparison demonstrating its advantages over existing approaches.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Strong theoretical contribution:** The paper introduces and formalizes the concept of an "intrinsic group additive structure" and provides a consistency proof for its identification.
*   **Novel methodology:** The idea of penalizing structural complexity using a measure derived from RKHS capacity (covering numbers) is new and principled.
*   **Important problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental and challenging problem at the intersection of nonparametric regression and model interpretability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Computational feasibility:** The proposed exhaustive search algorithm is intractable for even moderately sized problems, and the heuristic alternative lacks guarantees and shows mixed results in experiments.
*   **Limited experimental validation:** The simulations are restricted to low dimensions, and there is no comparison against competing methods, making it hard to judge the practical utility and performance of the proposed approach.
*   **Practicality of tuning:** The method introduces two new tuning parameters ($\mu, \alpha$) in addition to the standard kernel and regularization parameters, which may be difficult to tune robustly in practice.
