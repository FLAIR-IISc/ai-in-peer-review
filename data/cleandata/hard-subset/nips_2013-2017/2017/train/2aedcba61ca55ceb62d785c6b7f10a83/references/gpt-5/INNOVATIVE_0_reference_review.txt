Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a “group additive” structure: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where variables are partitioned into groups u and each fu is a multivariate nonparametric function on its group. The authors formalize the notion that such a structure may be non-unique and introduce a partial order over groupings, leading to the definition and existence/uniqueness of an intrinsic (minimal) group additive structure G*. They propose to identify G* and estimate f by kernel methods in a direct-sum RKHS, with a structural penalty derived from RKHS capacity: they upper bound covering numbers for each group’s RKHS and combine them to produce a tractable complexity measure C(G) ≈ ∑u α|u|. They then minimize empirical risk with an RKHS norm penalty and µC(G), prove selection consistency (P(Ĝ = G*) → 1) under appropriate choices of λn and µ, and provide two algorithms: exhaustive search with validation, and a backward stepwise heuristic. Simulations indicate recovery of true structures across several synthetic models (with some failures), and a Boston Housing case study yields interpretable 2D group functions.

Relation to prior work
This paper sits between classical additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), functional ANOVA/smoothing spline ANOVA (Gu), and higher-order interaction models estimated via KRR with ESP kernels (Kandasamy & Yu, SALSA). It connects to OKGT (Pan et al.) but resolves identifiability by defining an intrinsic structure and proposes a principled capacity-based penalty to select it. The approach also relates to graphical models but targets a simpler object (grouped functional decomposition rather than conditional dependencies). The RKHS capacity angle draws on covering number bounds (Zhou; Kühn) and universality of kernels (Steinwart & Christmann).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The intrinsic group additive structure and its partial order are clean and compelling, providing an identifiability target between purely additive and fully interaction-rich models.
- Theoretical contribution: Consistency of structure selection using a capacity-based penalty on a direct-sum RKHS is interesting and, to my knowledge, new in this precise form. The link from group structure to covering-number-based complexity is principled.
- Interpretability: Grouping supports low-dimensional component visualization while allowing within-group interactions.
- Methodological scope: The framework is general across universal kernels and group sizes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality of the penalty: The derivation from covering numbers (ln(r/ε))|u|+1 to a tunable α|u| surrogate is heuristic. Constants, the role of ε and r, and dependence on kernel hyperparameters are abstracted away. Ultimately α is selected from a small grid, which undercuts the “principled” nature of the penalty in practice. A data-dependent capacity measure (e.g., empirical Rademacher complexity) or an information criterion could be more grounded.
- Statistical assumptions and scope: Independence is invoked to sharpen Proposition 1; sensitivity to correlated covariates is not theoretically addressed, yet is common in practice. Theorems rely on covering-number bounds with unspecified constants, and finite-sample rates or oracle inequalities aren’t provided. The requirement λn n → 0 is stated without practical guidance on scaling.
- Algorithms: Exhaustive search over partitions is combinatorial; the backward stepwise heuristic is under-specified (Step 4 is ambiguous: how is the destination group chosen? merge/split policies? termination?). No guarantees, complexity analysis, or exploration strategies (restarts/beam search) are discussed. The failure on Model 4 suggests the greedy strategy can easily miss G*.
- Experiments: Limited and missing key baselines. There’s no comparison to SALSA (ESP kernel KRR), smoothing spline ANOVA, OKGT, or sparse additive/interaction learners (e.g., SpAM) or HSIC-based interaction discovery. Only one real dataset is shown, with no predictive performance comparison. No stress tests on correlated X, misspecified kernels, or scalability. Hyperparameter interactions (λ, µ, α) are not analyzed; the noted deterioration on Model 1 after validation is left unresolved.
- Clarity/presentation: Some notation is imprecise and several editorial issues remain (missing years, typos, ambiguous symbols). The transition from the theoretical capacity bound to the final penalty ∑ α|u| is abrupt and should be better justified.

Questions for the authors
- How does identifiability and selection consistency behave under correlated predictors? Can you extend the theory (or at least experiments) to this case?
- Why is ∑ α|u| preferable to a penalty that also directly penalizes the number of groups d, e.g., µ1 d + µ2 ∑ |u|? As written, many singleton groups could be favored depending on α.
- Can you provide empirical ablations on µ and α and guidance for setting them? Is α dimension-dependent?
- Could you compare to SALSA, smoothing spline ANOVA, and OKGT on the synthetic and Boston data (both structure recovery and prediction)?
- Can the stepwise search include merge/split moves with multiple passes, restarts, or beam search? Any heuristics (e.g., HSIC screening) to initialize groups?
- Runtime and scalability: How does the method fare for p=50–200?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen experiments with competitive baselines, correlated covariate settings, larger p, runtime and stability analyses, and predictive metrics.
- Clarify and, if possible, tighten the link from covering-number bounds to the chosen penalty; consider data-dependent capacities or mixed penalties that penalize both group sizes and count.
- Provide a more detailed algorithmic description (move set, scoring, termination), plus practical guidance on tuning λ, µ, α.
- Clean up notation, fix editorial issues, and unify the theoretical and practical sections.

Decision
I value the conceptual novelty (intrinsic group additive structure) and the bold, capacity-driven selection framework with a consistency result. Despite the current experimental and algorithmic limitations, this paper opens a promising direction between additive and high-order interaction models. I recommend Weak Accept, contingent on substantially improving empirical evaluation and clarifying the penalty and search procedures.

Confidence: Medium.
