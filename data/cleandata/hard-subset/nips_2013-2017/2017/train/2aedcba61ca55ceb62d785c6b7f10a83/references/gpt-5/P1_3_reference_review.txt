Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonparametric regression when the regression function admits a group additive structure: f(X) = Σu∈G fu(Xu) where G is a partition of variables. The authors (i) formalize a partial order on group structures, define “amiable” group structures (those that can represent f), and prove the existence of a unique minimal element among amiable structures, termed the intrinsic group additive structure G*. (ii) Propose an RKHS-based estimation procedure that jointly fits fu via kernel ridge regression (KRR) and selects G by penalizing a complexity measure C(G) derived from covering numbers of the induced direct-sum RKHS. They show a consistency result: with suitable tuning, the selected G converges in probability to G*. (iii) Provide two algorithms (exhaustive search with validation and a backward stepwise heuristic) and demonstrate performance on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset.

This sits between additive models (no interactions) and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models (many interactions), most closely related to: OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15), SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16), smoothing spline ANOVA (Gu), and kernel methods in RKHS (Schölkopf & Smola; Steinwart & Christmann). The paper’s distinctive angle is using RKHS capacity (covering numbers) to define a penalty over partitions and proving structure selection consistency.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation: grouping variables captures interactions without exploding complexity as in full ANOVA.
- Conceptual contribution: formal partial order over partitions, “amiable” group structures, and the existence/uniqueness of an intrinsic structure; this is useful for identifiability.
- Methodological novelty: deriving a tractable structural penalty from covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs (via a subadditivity inequality on log-covering numbers).
- Theoretical guarantees: separation between amiable and non-amiable structures and a structure-selection consistency theorem (Theorem 4), contingent on tuning.
- Practical algorithms and initial empirical evidence; the Boston example shows interpretable bivariate effects.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality/technical soundness
- Penalty derivation and use: The covering-number upper bound ln N(ε, I(HK,r), d∞) ≤ C (ln(r/ε))p+1 for convolution kernels is invoked to motivate C(G) ≈ Σu α|u|+1, then simplified to Σu α|u| (absorbing constants into μ). This makes C(G) depend only on group sizes and an arbitrary α, largely decoupled from the actual kernels, domains, and constants in the bound. The gap between the theoretical capacity control and the final practical penalty is substantial; the paper should articulate conditions under which the simplified penalty preserves the ordering needed for consistency and specify how μ, α should scale with n.
- Theorem 4 states consistency “by choosing a proper tuning parameter μ,” but the paper does not provide a principled schedule (μn, λn, potentially α) nor verify that CV/validation-based tuning meets the conditions. Similarly, the condition “λn ∗ n → 0” is unclear (typo?) and needs precise asymptotics.
- Identifiability constraints: In additive/RKHS decompositions it is standard to impose centering constraints (e.g., E fu(Xu)=0 for each u) to avoid non-uniqueness across components. The paper does not mention such constraints, which are important for the claimed uniqueness of the “intrinsic” structure and for estimation.
- Assumptions: Proposition 1’s strict inclusion requires independent covariates; many real datasets violate this. How do dependence structures affect amiability and uniqueness? Some assumptions (compact domain [0,1]p) are used for covering numbers, while simulations use Gaussian/unbounded inputs; the implications should be discussed.
- Algorithms: Exhaustive search is exponential in p (Bell numbers). The backward stepwise heuristic is greedy and may miss the true structure (as seen in Model 4); there is no analysis of its objective landscape, convergence, or use of restarts. Solving (7) efficiently with multiple groups (via representer theorem and block kernels) should be described; current algorithmic details are sparse.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally readable but contains notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., “st Conference…”, “λn ∗ n → 0”). The role and interpretation of α are not clear: originally α(ε)=ln(r/ε), later treated as an integer grid in [1,10].
- Algorithm 2 is underspecified: how are splits performed (creating a new singleton vs. merging into an existing group)? Is there a selection criterion when multiple improving splits exist? Are multiple passes/restarts used?

Originality
- The focus on partition-based additive structures and the connection to RKHS capacity to guide structure selection are novel to my knowledge. However, prior related work on sparse additive models and kernels with interactions (e.g., SpAM, hierarchical kernel learning, ANOVA kernels, OKGT, SALSA) should be more comprehensively discussed, and empirical comparisons are currently missing.

Significance
- If scalable and robust, identifying intrinsic groupings could meaningfully improve interpretability and efficiency in high-dimensional nonparametrics. The current empirical section is limited: small synthetic studies, no comparisons to baselines (e.g., ANOVA-KRR, SALSA, OKGT, hierarchical kernel learning), and a qualitative single real dataset. Scalability to p≥50 and sensitivity to μ, α are not explored. As it stands, impact is promising but not yet demonstrated.

Questions and suggestions to improve the paper
- Provide precise conditions and scaling for λn, μn (and whether α depends on n) that guarantee Theorem 4; explain how practical tuning (CV/validation) respects these conditions.
- Clarify identifiability: impose and state centering constraints for each component fu, and explain how they are handled in optimization.
- Expand related work and add empirical baselines: compare to SALSA, OKGT, additive models with pairwise ANOVA kernels, hierarchical kernel learning (HKL), and SpAM-style methods.
- Strengthen algorithms: describe an efficient solver for (7) leveraging block structure; add a more powerful search over partitions (e.g., beam search, stochastic local search with restarts, or submodular relaxations) and report runtimes and scalability.
- Study robustness: cases where the true structure is a single large group (penalty strongly disfavors it), correlated covariates, misspecified kernels, and different sample sizes. Provide ablations for μ and α.
- Clean up typos, notation, and the proofs’ accessibility (move key lemmas into the main text or provide a more detailed sketch).

Reasons to accept
- Interesting and relevant problem; solid conceptual framing (intrinsic structure).
- Novel RKHS-capacity-driven structural penalty and selection-consistency guarantees.
- Interpretable models demonstrated on real data.

Reasons to reject
- Theory-to-practice gap in the penalty and tuning; lack of precise conditions and clarity.
- Limited, small-scale experiments with no baselines; weak evidence of superiority or scalability.
- Algorithmic section underdeveloped; stepwise search may be unreliable.

Overall, I find the ideas promising and the theoretical direction interesting, but the paper needs stronger empirical validation, clearer assumptions and tuning guidance, and more thorough algorithmic development to meet NeurIPS standards. I encourage the authors to address the above points.
