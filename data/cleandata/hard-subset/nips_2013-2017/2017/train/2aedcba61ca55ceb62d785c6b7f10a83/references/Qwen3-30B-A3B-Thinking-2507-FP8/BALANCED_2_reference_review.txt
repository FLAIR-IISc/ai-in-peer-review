

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses an important gap in nonparametric regression by proposing a method to identify the intrinsic group additive structure (the minimal group structure that can represent the true function) while estimating the nonparametric functions. The authors build on the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) framework but resolve two key limitations: (1) non-uniqueness of group structures and (2) lack of systematic identification method. They define the intrinsic group additive structure and propose a complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs to enable structure identification through penalized estimation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation, clearly defining the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and proving its uniqueness. This is a significant theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental issue in group additive modeling.

2. **Novel Complexity Measure:** The authors derive a tractable complexity measure for group structures based on covering numbers of RKHSs, which is well-motivated by the relationship between group structure order and function space complexity (Proposition 3). This measure is directly incorporated into the penalized estimation framework.

3. **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The consistency proof (Theorem 4) is well-executed and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The authors carefully distinguish between amiable and non-amiable group structures and provide appropriate bounds for both cases.

4. **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies (Table 2) demonstrate the method's ability to identify true group structures across multiple models. The real data application on Boston Housing provides a concrete example of the method's practical utility.

5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, logical flow, and appropriate mathematical notation. The algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) are clearly described and implementable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison with Existing Methods:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison with alternative approaches for identifying group structures. How does this method compare to sparse graphical model approaches (cited in Section 1) or other group structure identification methods? A comparative analysis would strengthen the significance claim.

2. **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper doesn't provide sufficient guidance on tuning parameter selection in practice. The validation set approach (Algorithm 1) is described, but the paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the results are to the choice of µ and α, or how to select them systematically without cross-validation.

3. **Computational Scalability:** While Algorithm 2 addresses computational concerns for large p, the paper doesn't provide empirical evidence of its performance compared to the exhaustive search. How much does the stepwise approach trade off accuracy for computational efficiency?

4. **Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper doesn't explore how the method performs under different kernel choices (beyond noting that universal kernels like Gaussian are preferable) or when the true function doesn't perfectly match the group additive assumption.

5. **Theoretical Assumptions:** The consistency proof (Theorem 4) depends on λₙn → 0, but the paper doesn't discuss how this constraint affects practical implementation or whether the method remains robust to moderate violations of this assumption.

**Significance and Originality:**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on high-dimensional nonparametric regression by providing a theoretically sound approach to group structure identification. While related to additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1990) and functional ANOVA (Friedman, 1991), the focus on identifying the *intrinsic* group structure rather than assuming it is novel. The paper is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS work (e.g., Kandasamy et al., 2016) by addressing the structure identification problem rather than just estimation under a known structure.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and practically useful approach to group additive structure identification in nonparametric regression. The core contribution of defining the intrinsic group additive structure and developing a complexity measure for structure identification is significant and addresses an important gap in the literature.

However, the paper would be strengthened by:
1. Adding a comparative analysis with existing structure identification methods
2. Providing more detailed guidance on practical implementation and tuning parameter selection
3. Including a sensitivity analysis of the method's performance to different kernels and model misspecification
4. Providing empirical evidence on the trade-off between accuracy and computational efficiency for the stepwise algorithm

Given the strong theoretical foundation, clear presentation, and practical relevance, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these points. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of nonparametric regression and aligns well with NIPS' focus on principled statistical learning methods.
