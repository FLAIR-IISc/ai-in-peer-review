This paper addresses the problem of identifying the group additive structure (GAS) in nonparametric regression, a problem arising when one suspects relationships between predictors exist within groups but not necessarily across groups. The authors acknowledge the non-uniqueness of GAS and propose a method to identify the “intrinsic” GAS – a minimal structure satisfying the additive property. They formulate a penalized kernel ridge regression problem, introducing a novel complexity measure for GAS based on covering numbers of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs), and provide theoretical guarantees for consistency. They also develop two algorithms, an exhaustive search and a stepwise approach, for GAS identification, demonstrating their effectiveness through simulations and a real-data application.

The paper is well-motivated, building logically from the drawbacks of high-dimensional nonparametric regression and existing approaches like functional ANOVA. The introduction clearly explains the benefits of GAS, its relationship to other methods (additive models, functional ANOVA, graphical models), and the specific challenges addressed in this work (non-identifiability, lack of a systematic identification approach). The theoretical contributions, particularly the definition of the intrinsic GAS and the consistency proof, are significant. The complexity measure based on covering numbers is a novel contribution and appears well-justified. The empirical evaluation through simulations and a real-data example provides further support for the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed approach to GAS identification, especially the complexity measure for group structures and the associated theoretical guarantees, is novel and contributes to the field.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation, defining the intrinsic GAS, deriving a complexity measure, and proving consistency.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation for the work and the key concepts are clearly explained.
* **Completeness:** The paper includes both theoretical analysis and empirical validation, demonstrating the practical applicability of the proposed method. The inclusion of two different algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) addresses potential computational limitations.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors appropriately relate their work to existing literature on nonparametric regression, functional ANOVA, graphical models, and kernel methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the stepwise algorithm is proposed to address computational cost for high-dimensional settings, the exhaustive search remains impractical for larger problems. A more detailed discussion of the computational scaling of both algorithms would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The method relies on several tuning parameters (λ, µ, α). The sensitivity of the results to these parameters and practical guidance for their selection (beyond the CV approach) could be expanded.
* **Choice of Kernels:** The performance is linked to the choice of kernel. The sensitivity to different kernels is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Limited Scope of Real-Data Application:** The real-data analysis is somewhat limited. Expanding the real-data application to more complex datasets or performing a more thorough comparison with alternative methods would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Overly Dense Derivations:** While the theoretical contributions are significant, some of the derivations appear dense and might benefit from more careful explanations.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
* The proposed complexity measure for GAS is a valuable contribution to the field.
* The consistency guarantees provide strong support for the method's reliability.
* The empirical evaluation, although limited, demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written and logically organized.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* The computational complexity of the exhaustive search algorithm limits its applicability to larger problems.
* The sensitivity of the method to tuning parameters and kernel choice needs further investigation.
* The real-data analysis could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions to the field of nonparametric regression. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and clarity of the paper outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, contingent on minor revisions addressing the comments above regarding computational complexity, parameter tuning, and kernel choice. Specifically, I would like to see a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the algorithms and potential strategies for scaling them to higher dimensions.
