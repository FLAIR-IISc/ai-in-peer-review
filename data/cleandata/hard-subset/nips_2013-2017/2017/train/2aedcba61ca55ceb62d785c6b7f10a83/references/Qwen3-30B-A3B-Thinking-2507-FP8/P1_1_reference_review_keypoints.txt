- Problem & contribution: Defines the intrinsic group additive structure (unique minimal grouping) and proposes a penalized kernel method to identify it.

- Method: Introduces a structural penalty based on RKHS covering numbers and develops consistency guarantees for structure identification.

- Algorithms: Provides two practical algorithms — exhaustive search and a backward stepwise (greedy) procedure — for recovering group structure.

- Theoretical results: Proves consistency (Theorems 2–4) and justifies the covering-number-based complexity penalty.

- Novelty: The identification of the intrinsic group additive structure and use of RKHS covering numbers for structure selection are new.

- Positioning: Clearly distinguishes the approach from graphical/conditional-dependence models and from prior group-based nonparametric work.

- Empirical validation: Synthetic experiments show the method can recover group structures; one real-data example (Boston Housing) is included.

- Strength: Conceptual novelty — addresses non-identifiability and provides an interpretable minimal representation.

- Strength: Theoretical rigor — well-constructed consistency proofs and justified complexity measure.

- Strength: Practical algorithms — implementable procedures with demonstrated synthetic performance.

- Weakness: Computational scalability — stepwise/greedy algorithm performance and scalability for large p (e.g., p > 50) are not thoroughly evaluated.

- Weakness: Parameter selection — choice of α in covering-number penalty is opaque and lacks justification.

- Weakness: High-dimensional claims vs. theory — theoretical analysis assumes fixed p, conflicting with stated high-dimensional goals.

- Weakness: Limited real-world validation — only one modest dataset (Boston Housing) limits evidence of practical utility.

- Quality & clarity: Paper is technically sound, well-organized, and clearly written, with consistent notation and good explanation of RKHS links.

- Recommendation: Strong accept pending minor revisions to address computational scalability, α selection, and the fixed-p vs. high-dimensional tension.

- Suggested revisions: analyze stepwise complexity as p grows, justify or guide α selection relative to data/group sizes, clarify fixed-p theoretical assumptions versus high-dimensional claims, and add another real-world application.
