Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression with group-additive structure: f(X) = Σu∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of predictors. The authors formalize a partial order on group structures, define amiable structures, and claim existence/uniqueness of a minimal “intrinsic” structure G*. They propose identifying G* by penalized kernel regression over direct-sum RKHSs HG with a structural penalty derived from covering number bounds, leading to minimization of empirical risk plus λ||f||^2HG plus µC(G), with C(G) intended to reflect RKHS capacity growth with group size. They show selection consistency under assumptions, and present (i) an exhaustive-search-with-validation procedure for small p and (ii) a backward stepwise heuristic for larger p. Simulations and a Boston Housing application are reported.

Strengths
- The problem (recovering block interactions rather than all pairwise/higher-order) is worthwhile and sits between additive models and full ANOVA/sparse-inteaction models.
- The notion of an intrinsic, minimal partition under refinement is conceptually appealing.
- The RKHS framing and attempt to tie structure selection to capacity control via covering numbers is interesting.
- Provides consistency statements and some algorithms for practice.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Penalty derivation is inconsistent. Starting from ln N(ε, I(HK,r), d∞) ≲ (ln(r/ε))p+1, the paper sets C(HK) ∝ (ln(r/ε))p+1 and then proposes C(G) = Σu α(ε)|u|+1. It subsequently “simplifies” to µ Σu α|u| in (6), claiming to “combine the common factor 1 in the exponent with µ.” Exponents cannot be absorbed into µ; dropping the +1 and replacing α(ε) by a tunable integer α severs the link to the capacity bound. As written, the implemented penalty is size-based, not theory-based.
- The theory compares the penalized empirical minimizer f̂λ,G to the unpenalized population risk minimizer f*G* (Proposition 4), mixing objectives. The bounds involve terms like (ε/24 − λn||f*G*||^2/12)^2, which appear dimensionally inconsistent and require λn choices that are not specified rigorously. Later Theorem 4 states λn·n → 0 but gives no conditions on µn (or α) scaling needed for structure recovery.
- The claimed lattice/completeness properties of amiable structures (Theorem 1) are nontrivial and not justified. If G and G′ are amiable, it is not immediate that their meet (common refinement) is amiable; this needs proof that L2G ∩ L2G′ = L2G∧G′ (or an appropriate closure property). Without this, existence/uniqueness of G* is not established.
- Covering-number inequality for direct sums (Proposition 3), ln N(ε, IG, d∞) ≤ Σ ln N(ε/|G|, Iuj, d∞), is stated without proof. The bound and the ε allocation across components require justification (e.g., via triangle inequality in sup-norm and optimal εj splitting). Equal splitting ε/|G| is suboptimal and may be loose; the implications for the penalty are not discussed.
- Assumptions and domains are inconsistent. The capacity bounds the paper uses require compact domains (embedding into C(X)). The theory sets X = [0,1]^p, but simulations use Gaussian covariates with unbounded support. The universality discussion is high level; bandwidth selection and its effect on capacity/penalty are ignored.
- Algorithmic details are under-specified. Algorithm 2 (backward stepwise) is ambiguous: “split j as a new group or add to an existing group” is not well-defined; only a single pass over variables is performed; no explicit stopping criterion or re-iteration until convergence; and the “return G′” appears to return the last tried, not the best, structure. Moreover, (6) requires µ, α; Algorithm 2 provides no tuning protocol, yet results are reported.
- Computational complexity is not analyzed. Exhaustive search over partitions scales with the Bell number; feasibility limits are not discussed.
- Several notational inconsistencies and possible typos in the theory (e.g., R̂g vs R̂; subscript/superscript confusion; missing 1/n in (6) vs present in (3) and (7); inconsistent use of HK vs Hk; I(HK,r) vs I (HK,r)) hinder verification.

Clarity
- The paper is difficult to follow due to notation drift and editing artifacts (e.g., “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)” in the middle of the introduction; inconsistent spacing and symbols; missing equation references). Several references lack years/complete bibliographic info.

Originality
- The grouping perspective differs from classical additive or ANOVA kernels and related SALSA/OKGT approaches. The attempt to tie a structure penalty to RKHS capacity is novel, though the execution is currently problematic.

Significance and empirical evaluation
- Empirically, there are no baselines (e.g., additive models, KRR with ANOVA kernels, SALSA, OKGT, hierarchical interaction selection) for structure recovery or prediction. Reporting only “frequency of identifying the true group structure” without standard errors and without predictive metrics limits significance claims. The Boston Housing example offers anecdotal interpretations but no test-set performance or comparisons.

Minor issues/formatting
- Many references lack years and proper formatting.
- Equation (6) uses sum of squares, (3) uses mean square; be consistent.
- α is tuned as an integer in [1,10], but theoretically α = ln(r/ε); this mismatch should be acknowledged and discussed.
- The discussion of connections to graphical models is superficial; formal contrasts (e.g., Markov blankets vs additive blocks) could be sharper.

Questions for the authors
- Provide a rigorous proof (or precise conditions) for Theorem 1 that the meet of amiable partitions is amiable.
- Reconcile the penalty: either keep a theoretically grounded C(G) with explicit ε (and show how to set εn, µn for consistency), or present a principled surrogate and revise the theory accordingly.
- Clarify the comparison between penalized empirical and population risks in Proposition 4; provide a consistent objective and precise scaling for λn, µn (and α).
- Specify kernel choices and bandwidth selection per group, and discuss how they affect C(G) and consistency.
- Detail Algorithm 2, including candidate moves (split/merge), acceptance criteria, stopping rule, and complexity. Consider local search with multiple passes and hill-climbing or beam search.
- Add competitive baselines and predictive metrics (RMSE, structure F1), with statistical uncertainty and ablations.

Pros
- Addresses an important middle ground between additive and full interaction models.
- Concept of intrinsic group structure is appealing.
- Attempts to ground structure selection in capacity control and provides consistency claims.

Cons
- Theoretical gaps/inconsistencies in penalty derivation and selection consistency.
- Algorithmic description and tuning are underspecified.
- Empirical evaluation lacks baselines and rigor.
- Numerous notation/formatting issues impede clarity.

Recommendation: Reject (confidence: high). The idea is promising, but theoretical foundations, algorithmic clarity, and experimental validation need substantial revision before publication.
