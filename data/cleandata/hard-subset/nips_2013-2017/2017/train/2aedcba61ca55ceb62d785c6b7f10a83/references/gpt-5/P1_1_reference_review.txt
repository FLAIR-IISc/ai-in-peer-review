Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a group additive structure: f(X) = sum_j f_u_j(X_u_j), where variables are partitioned into groups u_j and each f_u_j is an unknown multivariate function. The authors define a partial order on group structures (refinements), show that among all “amiable” (compatible) group structures there exists a unique minimal one—the intrinsic group additive structure—and propose a method to recover it jointly with the functions using RKHS machinery. Concretely, for a candidate partition G they consider a direct-sum RKHS HG = ⊕_u∈G H_u with universal kernels on each group, and fit via kernel ridge regression with an additional structural penalty µ C(G). They motivate C(G) via covering number bounds for RKHS balls, deriving ln N(ε, I_HG, ∞) ≤ Σ_u ln N(ε/|G|, I_Hu, ∞), which under Gaussian-like kernels leads to a proxy C(G) ∝ Σ_u α^{|u|} (with α related to ln(1/ε)). They propose two algorithms: exhaustive search over partitions (small p) with validation, and a greedy backward stepwise splitting heuristic (larger p). They provide consistency results showing that with appropriate regularization the selected partition equals the intrinsic structure with probability tending to one, and present simulations and an application to Boston Housing.

The work builds on additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), SS-ANOVA (Gu), higher-order additive/ANOVA decompositions (e.g., SALSA), and OKGT which introduced groupwise additive structure but without identification. The structural selection via RKHS capacity connects loosely to learning-theoretic capacity measures (covering numbers, Rademacher complexity). The paper does not discuss related lines in structured kernel learning and sparse interaction selection (e.g., hierarchical kernel learning and structured sparsity for interactions), which are closely related in spirit.

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of group additive structures, with a sensible partial order and the concept of an intrinsic (minimal) structure.
- Use of direct-sum RKHS and covering number arguments to derive a principled, monotone complexity proxy across candidate partitions.
- Selection consistency results distinguishing amiable vs non-amiable structures, with uniform deviation bounds based on covering numbers, and a final consistency theorem (under fixed p).
- Practical algorithms (exhaustive and greedy) and some empirical evidence that the method can recover true groupings and produce interpretable fits on a real dataset.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:
- The experimental section is limited: small-scale synthetic examples, no comparisons against strong baselines (e.g., SS-ANOVA with interaction selection, SALSA, hierarchical kernel learning/structured sparsity approaches), no runtime/complexity analysis, and only one real dataset with modest p. NeurIPS standards typically expect stronger empirical validation and baselines.
- Assumptions and scope: proofs leverage covering number bounds that hold for convolution kernels with exponentially decaying Fourier transform (e.g., Gaussian). The dependence of constants on kernel and dimension is not discussed, nor is robustness to misspecified kernels. The selection consistency holds for fixed p (|Ga| finite). The stepwise algorithm, intended for larger p, has no theoretical guarantees and degrades on some models.
- The penalty C(G): the path from ln N bounds to C(G) = Σ α^{|u|} is heuristic (absorbing +1 in the exponent and ε/r into µ). While mathematically Σ α^{|u|+1} = α Σ α^{|u|}, the practical meaning of α and its tuning are not well justified. Treating α as an integer hyperparameter in [1,10] divorces the penalty from the underlying capacity interpretation. A discussion of how µ, α should scale with n for the selection consistency (akin to model selection penalties) is missing.
- Identifiability: Proposition 1 notes strict inclusion of L2 spaces with independence; theorems about existence/uniqueness of the intrinsic structure rely on the poset of amiable partitions being complete. The paper should clarify whether independence is required anywhere for uniqueness or only for strict inclusion claims, and provide a constructive definition of G* (e.g., the meet of amiable partitions).
- Algorithmic details: The search space of partitions grows as the Bell number; the greedy stepwise routine is only sketched. It is unclear how candidates are generated in Step 4 (split j as singleton vs. move to existing groups), how many passes are made, and how stopping is determined. Complexity of solving (7) for each G, and how KRR is implemented for direct-sum kernels, should be detailed.
- Missing related work: There is substantial literature on selecting interactions with kernels and structured sparsity (e.g., hierarchical kernel learning and related convex surrogates), sparse additive models with interactions, and multiple kernel learning over subsets. Positioning relative to these would strengthen originality claims and guide baselines.

Quality:
- Theoretical development is mostly sound within the stated setting (fixed p, appropriate kernels). The capacity-based penalty is a reasonable monotone proxy, though the practical instantiation via α-tuning is ad hoc. Proof-level details are deferred; the high-level logic (amiable vs non-amiable, uniform bounds, penalty favoring minimal structure) is convincing.
- Empirical evidence is insufficient to establish practical advantages or robustness.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and organized. However, the role and interpretation of α and its relation to ε/r deserve clearer exposition. The assumptions on kernels and input distributions should be stated explicitly where used. Algorithm 2 needs more detail.

Originality:
- The notion of an intrinsic group additive structure and its identification via a structural penalty derived from RKHS capacity is novel. However, connections to structured kernel learning/interaction selection are underexplored.

Significance:
- Potentially significant if it reliably identifies meaningful variable groupings and scales to moderate/high p. Current experiments do not yet demonstrate strong practical impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear and principled formulation of intrinsic group structure; nontrivial consistency results.
- Conceptually appealing capacity-based complexity measure for partitions.
- Addresses an important gap between additive models and full interaction models.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited experiments and no comparisons to relevant baselines.
- Scalability and algorithmic guarantees for the stepwise method are unclear.
- Some theoretical–practical mismatch in the penalty instantiation and hyperparameter selection.

Constructive suggestions:
- Add empirical comparisons to SALSA, SS-ANOVA with interaction selection, and hierarchical kernel learning/structured sparsity methods on synthetic and real datasets; report recovery accuracy and predictive performance.
- Evaluate robustness to correlated covariates and kernel misspecification; clarify if independence is required anywhere.
- Provide guidance or theory for selecting µ (and α) as a function of n; consider a data-driven surrogate derived from empirical Rademacher complexities.
- Detail and improve the stepwise search (e.g., best-split selection, multiple passes, beam search), and discuss computational complexity.
- Expand related work to include structured kernel learning and interaction selection literature.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper has interesting theory and a principled idea, but needs stronger empirical validation, clearer positioning, and more detailed algorithms to meet NeurIPS standards.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
