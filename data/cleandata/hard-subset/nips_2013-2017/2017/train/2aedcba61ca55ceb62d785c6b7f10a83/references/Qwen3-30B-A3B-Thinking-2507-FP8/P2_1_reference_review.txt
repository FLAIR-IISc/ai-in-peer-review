

# Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: balancing model flexibility with interpretability and computational feasibility. The authors propose identifying the "intrinsic group additive structure" for nonparametric regression, where the target function f(X) can be expressed as a sum of functions over disjoint groups of predictors. This approach lies between the standard additive model (which assumes no interactions) and functional ANOVA models (which capture all possible interactions but suffer from the curse of dimensionality when the number of predictors is large).

The work builds on [11] (OKGT), which introduced group additive models but did not address the non-uniqueness of group structures or provide a systematic identification method. The authors make two key contributions: (1) rigorously defining the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group structure that can represent f(X), and (2) proposing a complexity-based penalty method to identify this structure using kernel methods and RKHS theory.

## Strengths

**Technical Quality and Theoretical Contributions:** The paper is exceptionally strong in theoretical rigor. The authors provide a careful mathematical foundation for the problem, including a well-defined partial order on group structures, a proof of existence and uniqueness of the intrinsic group additive structure, and a novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers. The consistency proof (Theorem 4) is particularly impressive, demonstrating that the proposed method correctly identifies the intrinsic structure as sample size increases.

**Methodological Innovation:** The complexity measure C(G) = ∑α^{|u_j|+1} is a clever application of learning theory to the group structure identification problem. The authors justify this choice through a thorough comparison of capacity measures (entropy, VC dimensions, Rademacher complexity, covering numbers), demonstrating why covering numbers are the most practical choice for their problem.

**Experimental Validation:** The simulation studies and real data application (Boston Housing) provide convincing empirical support for the method. The authors show that their method successfully recovers true group structures across multiple models, and the real data application yields interpretable results that align with domain knowledge (e.g., the relationship between house value, crime rate, and number of rooms).

## Weaknesses

**Practical Implementation Concerns:** While the exhaustive search algorithm (Algorithm 1) is theoretically sound, it becomes computationally intractable as the number of predictors increases (p > 20). The backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is a practical solution, but the paper acknowledges its limitations (e.g., Model 4 results were unsatisfactory due to the greedy nature of the approach). The authors should provide more concrete guidance on when to use each algorithm or suggest hybrid approaches.

**Interpretation of Results:** The real data application (Section 5) presents interesting patterns in the estimated functions, but the paper lacks deeper interpretation of what these patterns mean for housing markets. A more detailed analysis of how the identified group structure informs the relationship between predictors and house value would strengthen the practical significance.

**Connection to Related Work:** While the paper correctly differentiates itself from graphical models, it could better position its approach relative to other methods for high-dimensional nonparametric regression. For example, how does it compare to methods like Salsa [6] or other sparse additive models?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric regression by providing a rigorous framework for group structure identification. The problem is well-motivated, the methodology is sound, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper advances the state of the art by addressing two critical limitations of prior work on group additive models.

**Quality:** The paper is technically excellent with a solid theoretical foundation, careful proofs, and comprehensive experimental validation. The authors are appropriately cautious about limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical definitions and logical flow. The algorithms are described in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The concept of intrinsic group additive structure is novel, and the proposed complexity measure represents a creative application of learning theory to structure identification.

**Significance:** The method provides a practical solution to a meaningful problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. It enables interpretable modeling while avoiding the curse of dimensionality, which has direct applications in fields like economics, healthcare, and social sciences.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of nonparametric regression with clear theoretical and practical implications. The work is well-executed, addresses a meaningful problem, and advances the state of the art. While the computational limitations of the stepwise algorithm warrant further investigation, the method shows significant promise and is likely to be adopted by practitioners in high-dimensional nonparametric modeling.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper meets all NIPS criteria at a high level. It is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field that will likely inspire future work on structured nonparametric regression.
