This paper addresses the problem of identifying a group additive structure in nonparametric regression, a topic relevant to NIPS as it tackles high-dimensional data and model interpretability. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of traditional additive models (lack of interaction) and functional ANOVA models (curse of dimensionality with increasing interactions). They propose a novel approach based on kernel methods and a complexity penalty to simultaneously identify the group structure and estimate the nonparametric functions.

The core idea—using a complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs to penalize the group structure—is a significant contribution. This provides a principled way to balance model fit and parsimony, addressing the non-identifiability issue inherent in group additive models. The theoretical results, particularly the consistency proof for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure, are strong and well-presented. The algorithms proposed—exhaustive search and a stepwise approach—offer practical solutions for different data scales. The simulation studies and real data application provide empirical support for the method's effectiveness.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The notation is dense and often lacks clear explanation. For instance, the meaning of α and its relationship to the covering number requires careful parsing. While the theoretical derivations are rigorous, they are presented in a somewhat opaque manner, making it difficult for a broader audience to fully grasp the underlying principles. The connection to graphical models, while mentioned, is not thoroughly explored; a more detailed comparison would be beneficial. The simulations are somewhat limited in scope. While multiple models are tested, the parameter ranges explored, and the number of repetitions could be increased to provide more robust evidence of performance. The choice of kernel functions and the impact of their parameters are not discussed in detail. The practical implications of the algorithm's computational cost, particularly for the exhaustive search, are not adequately addressed.  The performance on the Boston Housing dataset is presented, but a comparison with existing methods (e.g., standard additive models, functional ANOVA) is missing. This makes it hard to assess the added value of the proposed approach.  Finally, there’s a recurring use of phrases like "can be" and "is expected" without strong justification, which weakens the claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach of using RKHS complexity to identify group structure is original and offers a principled solution to a challenging problem.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The consistency proof provides a strong theoretical foundation for the method.
*   **Potential Impact:** The method could be valuable in applications where interpretability and efficiency are crucial in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
*   **Well-structured:** The paper follows a logical flow, with clear sections for method, theory, simulations, and real data analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper suffers from dense notation and a lack of intuitive explanations, making it difficult to follow.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations are somewhat limited in scope, and a comparison with existing methods is missing.
*   **Computational Considerations:** The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm is not adequately addressed.
*   **Lack of Detailed Discussion:** Key aspects like kernel selection and parameter tuning are not discussed in sufficient detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field, but it falls short of being truly outstanding. The theoretical contributions are strong, but the lack of clarity and limited empirical validation hinder its impact. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement for the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, they should:

1.  **Improve Clarity:** Revise the paper with a focus on clarity and intuitive explanations. Define all notation explicitly and provide more context for the theoretical derivations.
2.  **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct more extensive simulations, explore a wider range of parameter settings, and compare the performance of the proposed method with existing techniques.
3.  **Address Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational complexity of the algorithms and provide guidance on how to scale them to larger datasets.
4.  **Detail Kernel Selection:** Explain the choice of kernel functions and provide recommendations for parameter tuning.
5.  **Strengthen Claims:** Replace vague statements like "can be" with more precise and justified assertions.



Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact on the NIPS community.
