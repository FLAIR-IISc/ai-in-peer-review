This paper addresses the challenging problem of identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, a middle ground between fully additive models and higher-order interaction models like ANOVA. The core idea – leveraging the complexity of group structures within a kernel-based regression framework – is novel and well-motivated. The authors aim to tackle both the non-uniqueness of group additive structures and the lack of systematic identification methods, proposing a penalized RKHS approach and consistency guarantees.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The problem itself is well-defined and important. Bridging the gap between additive models and more complex interaction models is a valuable contribution, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The intuition connecting group structure to variable interactions is sound.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a strong theoretical underpinning for their approach. The definition of the "intrinsic group additive structure" is rigorous and addresses the non-uniqueness issue. The consistency proof (Theorem 4) is a significant achievement.
* **Complexity Measure:** The proposed complexity measure for group structures, based on covering numbers of RKHSs, is a creative idea. While technically demanding, it provides a quantifiable way to penalize more complex groupings.
* **Algorithm Development:** The authors propose both an exhaustive search and a stepwise algorithm for identifying the group structure, catering to different dimensionality scenarios.
* **Experimental Validation:** The simulation studies demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, and the real data application provides a concrete example of its utility. The visualization of the estimated functions is insightful.
* **Connections to Related Work:** The authors appropriately position their work relative to graphical models and other relevant approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The paper is very technically dense, particularly the theoretical sections. While rigor is appreciated, it may make the paper inaccessible to a broader audience. More intuitive explanations of some concepts (e.g., covering numbers) could improve readability.
* **Algorithm Scalability:** While the stepwise algorithm addresses scalability to some extent, it is still not clear how well the method performs on very high-dimensional data (p >> n).  The simulation studies, while helpful, don’t fully explore this aspect. A discussion of computational limitations is needed.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of parameters (µ and α). The tuning procedure based on validation sets mitigates this, but a more robust parameter selection strategy (perhaps based on information criteria) could be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** The simulation study compares against methods not always directly addressing the same problem (i.e., identifying group structures). A more direct comparison with existing group structure learning algorithms would be valuable.
* **Choice of Kernel:** The authors mention universal kernels but don’t provide much guidance on kernel selection. A discussion of kernel sensitivity or recommendations for specific applications would be helpful.
* **Real Data Interpretation:** While the visualization of the estimated functions in the real data example is interesting, the interpretation of the identified group structure lacks depth. A more detailed discussion of the domain-specific meaning of the groupings would strengthen the application.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important and novel problem in nonparametric regression.
* The theoretical results are rigorous and provide strong guarantees for the proposed method.
* The proposed approach is well-motivated and offers a practical solution for identifying group additive structures.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in various settings.
* The paper is well-written (despite its technical density) and clearly presents the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical density may limit the paper's accessibility.
* The algorithm’s scalability to extremely high-dimensional data is not fully addressed.
* The simulation studies lack a direct comparison with competing group structure learning algorithms.
* The real data application could benefit from a more in-depth interpretation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The authors have developed a theoretically sound and practically effective method for identifying group additive structures. While the paper has some weaknesses (primarily related to technical density, scalability, and limited comparison), the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.  I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance, but with minor revisions to address the points mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should consider adding more intuitive explanations of the key theoretical concepts, discussing the computational limitations of their approach, and providing a more thorough comparison with existing methods.



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
