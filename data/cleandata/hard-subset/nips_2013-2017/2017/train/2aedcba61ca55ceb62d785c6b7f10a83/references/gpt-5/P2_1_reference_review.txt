Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonparametric regression with a group-additive structure: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of variables. It (i) formalizes the notion that many partitions can represent the same f, (ii) defines an “intrinsic” group additive structure G* as the minimal (most refined) amiable structure under a refinement partial order, (iii) proposes a structure-penalized kernel estimator that jointly estimates f and G, using a complexity penalty derived from RKHS covering numbers, and (iv) proves selection consistency (P(Ĝ=G*)→1) under fixed p and suitable tuning. It provides an exhaustive-search algorithm (small p) and a backward splitting heuristic (larger p), and demonstrates results on small synthetic models and Boston Housing.

This connects to additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), functional ANOVA (e.g., smoothing spline ANOVA; ANOVA/ESP kernels), and structured RKHS learning. It is related to OKGT (Pan et al.) and to interaction discovery methods like SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu, cited), as well as the lattice of partitions (refinement order) and structural risk minimization via capacity control (e.g., covering numbers).

Quality (technical correctness and support)
- Theory: The partial-order formalization and the “intrinsic” structure are well motivated. The consistency argument is based on: (a) amiable structures achieve the same population risk and empirical risk converges uniformly (via covering numbers), (b) non-amiable structures incur a positive risk gap, and (c) a penalty C(G) that prefers smaller structures tips selection to G* with suitable µ. The use of universal kernels to ensure approximation density is standard.
- Derivation of C(G): Starting from covering number bounds for convolution kernels and bounds for direct sums, the paper arrives at a penalty that depends essentially on group sizes: C(G) ≍ ∑u α|u|. This is intuitive and practically convenient.
- Gaps/concerns:
  - The theoretical results appear to assume fixed p and a finite set of amiable partitions Ga. The large-p setting (where Algorithm 2 is intended) lacks theory.
  - The selection-consistency result relies on “proper” µ but does not provide a data-driven prescription; µ and α are tuned by validation on prediction error, which need not target structural correctness.
  - The step from covering-number bounds to the specific penalty substitutes α=ln(r/ε) and then treats α as a tunable integer; the link between the theoretical α and the practical grid is heuristic.
  - Proposition 3 (covering number of the direct sum space) and Theorem 1 (existence/uniqueness of the intrinsic structure) are plausible but would benefit from clearer conditions. In particular, uniqueness of a smallest element in Ga under refinement is nontrivial; the lattice-of-partitions perspective suggests the meet of all amiable partitions exists, but the argument should be explicit. Also, the strict inclusion L2G1 ⊂ L2G2 under independence is assumed; dependence weakens this.
  - No computational complexity analysis is provided; exhaustive search scales with Bell numbers, and KRR per candidate is O(n^3) without approximations.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The motivation for group-additive vs. full ANOVA is clear. Some notation and assumptions could be clarified:
- State assumptions (e.g., independence, kernel class, boundedness) precisely where used.
- Specify conditions on λn and µn for consistency (Theorem 4 currently has λn n → 0 and informal “proper µ”).
- Provide the representer form and computational details for (7) in the direct-sum RKHS, and the complexity of Algorithms 1–2. Algorithm 2 seems to do only a single pass; state whether it iterates to convergence and how ties/multi-splits are handled.

Originality
Defining and targeting the intrinsic group-additive structure in RKHSs and designing a capacity-based structural penalty for direct-sum RKHSs is a novel and interesting angle. The overall approach combines known pieces (universal kernels, covering numbers, SRM-style penalties) in a new structure-identification context. Related directions (OKGT, SALSA, ANOVA kernels, hierarchical kernel learning) are cited, but the empirical section does not compare against them.

Significance
If scalable and robust, the ability to discover low-order interacting groups directly (between additive and full ANOVA extremes) is valuable. The theory provides a foundation for structure identification. However, practical impact is limited by:
- Scalability (search over partitions; per-candidate KRR).
- Lack of strong empirical validation and baselines.
- Sensitivity of structure recovery to hyperparameter selection not theoretically grounded.

Empirical evaluation
Synthetic experiments are small, with very low noise and no comparisons to alternatives (e.g., SALSA, ANOVA-KRR/ESP kernel, OKGT, sparse additive models with pairwise interactions, hierarchical kernel learning). The backward stepwise sometimes fails (Model 4), and no remedies (multi-start, annealing, beam search) are explored. The Boston Housing case study is illustrative but lacks ground truth and baseline comparisons; interpretability comments are nice but anecdotal.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and motivation; “intrinsic” structure is a useful target.
- Sound use of RKHS tools; consistency proof strategy is sensible.
- A simple, interpretable structural penalty tied to group sizes via capacity arguments.
- Practical algorithms for small and moderate p.

Cons
- Limited empirical evaluation; no baselines; small-scale simulations.
- Scalability and computational complexity not addressed rigorously.
- Theory for large p/greedy search absent; tuning of µ, α lacks principled guidance.
- Some theoretical claims need clearer statements of assumptions and proofs.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide comparisons to SALSA (cited), functional ANOVA with ESP kernel, OKGT, and sparse additive interaction models; include runtime and structure recovery metrics.
- Clarify and prove Theorem 1’s uniqueness (intrinsic structure) carefully; state assumptions (e.g., independence) where needed.
- Give a data-driven prescription or theory for µn (and α) that yields consistency, or at least discuss stability selection for structure.
- Strengthen Algorithm 2 (iterative splitting/merging, multi-start, beam search) and discuss complexity; consider kernel approximations (Nyström, random features) for scalability.
- Discuss robustness to dependent X and model misspecification.
- Expand simulations with higher noise, larger p, and mis-specified kernels.

Verdict
Weak reject. The problem is interesting and the conceptual/theoretical contributions are promising, but the current empirical validation and scalability story are not yet at NeurIPS standards. With stronger experiments, baselines, clarified assumptions/proofs, and a more scalable search strategy, this work could have significant impact.
