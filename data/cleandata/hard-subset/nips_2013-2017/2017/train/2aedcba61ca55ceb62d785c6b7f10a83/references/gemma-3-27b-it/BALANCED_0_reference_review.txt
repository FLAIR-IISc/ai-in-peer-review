This paper addresses the problem of identifying the group additive structure (GAS) in nonparametric regression, a problem arising from the desire to balance the flexibility of nonparametric methods with the interpretability and efficiency of additive models. The authors propose a penalized kernel method that simultaneously identifies the GAS and estimates the underlying nonparametric functions. The penalty term is based on a novel complexity measure for GASs, derived from covering numbers of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). The paper includes theoretical guarantees for the consistency of the proposed method and demonstrates its effectiveness through simulations and a real-data application.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Relevance:** The problem of GAS identification is well-motivated.  Additive models with potential interactions provide a valuable compromise between full nonparametric flexibility and the curse of dimensionality.
* **Theoretical Contribution:** The definition of the “intrinsic” GAS and the development of a complexity measure for GASs are significant theoretical contributions. The consistency results (Theorem 4) provide a strong foundation for the proposed method. The mathematical analysis appears rigorous, though detailed verification would require expert knowledge in RKHS theory (addressed as a weakness).
* **Methodological Innovation:**  The proposed penalized kernel method provides a principled approach to GAS identification. The use of covering numbers to define the complexity penalty is a novel and potentially powerful idea.
* **Experimental Validation:** The simulation studies demonstrate the ability of the method to recover the true GAS under various settings. The real-data application provides further support for the method’s practical utility, and the visualizations of the estimated functions are insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.  The motivation and intuition behind the key ideas are clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Theory:** While the theoretical results are a strength, their accessibility is limited. The arguments rely heavily on concepts from RKHS theory and covering numbers, which may be unfamiliar to a broad audience.  More intuitive explanations or a more gradual introduction to these concepts would be beneficial.  A thorough review by an expert in RKHS theory is warranted to ensure the correctness and tightness of the theoretical results.
* **Computational Cost:**  The exhaustive search algorithm for GAS identification can be computationally expensive, especially for high-dimensional problems. The backward stepwise algorithm is presented as a solution, but its performance is not always satisfactory (as acknowledged in the paper for Model 4).  Further investigation of more efficient algorithms (e.g., those based on greedy approaches or metaheuristics) is needed. The authors do not provide runtime comparisons.
* **Choice of Kernels:** The paper mentions the importance of using universal kernels, but the specific choice of kernel is not thoroughly discussed. The performance of the method may be sensitive to the kernel parameters, and a more systematic investigation of different kernels and bandwidth selection strategies would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper primarily focuses on establishing the performance of the proposed method in isolation. A more comprehensive comparison to existing methods for variable selection and interaction detection (e.g., penalized regression with interaction terms, graphical models) would provide a clearer picture of the method’s advantages and disadvantages.  While the connection to graphical models is briefly mentioned, a more in-depth discussion of the differences and potential synergies would be helpful.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The details of the parameter tuning procedure (especially for µ and α) could be more explicit. The paper mentions using a validation set, but the specific validation strategy (e.g., k-fold cross-validation) is not described.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important and challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
* The theoretical contributions are significant and provide a strong foundation for the proposed method.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in recovering the true GAS.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* The theoretical complexity may limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
* The computational cost of the method can be a barrier to its application in high-dimensional problems.
* The lack of a comprehensive comparison to existing methods makes it difficult to assess the method’s relative merits.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and methodological contributions. The proposed method for GAS identification is novel and promising. However, the theoretical complexity and computational cost are potential drawbacks.  While the paper demonstrates encouraging results, further investigation of more efficient algorithms and a more thorough comparison to existing methods are needed.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend *acceptance* with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide more intuitive explanations of the key theoretical concepts.
2.  Investigate more efficient algorithms for GAS identification.
3.  Conduct a more comprehensive comparison to existing methods.
4.  Provide more details on the parameter tuning procedure.
5.  Address the potential sensitivity to kernel choice.
6.  Ensure a thorough review of the theoretical results by an expert in RKHS theory.
