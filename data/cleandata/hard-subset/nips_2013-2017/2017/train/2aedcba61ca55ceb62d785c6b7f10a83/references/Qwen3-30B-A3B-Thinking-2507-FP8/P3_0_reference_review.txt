

# Peer Review: "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical gap in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: the identification of group additive structures rather than assuming a fixed structure. The authors define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group structure that correctly represents a function, and propose a method (GASI) to identify this structure using a penalized kernel approach with a complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers. The paper makes significant contributions by rigorously defining the intrinsic structure, providing theoretical consistency results, and proposing practical algorithms.

## Strengths

The paper's most significant strength is its rigorous theoretical foundation. The definition of the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and the proof of its uniqueness provide a crucial conceptual advance over previous work (like [11]). The authors properly address the non-identifiability issue that plagues previous group additive approaches.

The theoretical contribution is particularly strong. Theorem 4 proves the consistency of the proposed method, and the supporting lemmas (Propositions 4-3) provide a solid foundation for understanding the behavior of the empirical risk. The connection between group structure complexity and RKHS covering numbers is well-motivated and theoretically sound.

The paper also demonstrates practical utility through comprehensive simulations (Table 2) and a real data application on Boston Housing. The results show that GASI can successfully identify true group structures, with the backward stepwise algorithm providing a computationally feasible approach for larger problems.

The paper's connection to graphical models is well-articulated, and the authors correctly distinguish their approach (targeting group additive structure directly) from methods that target conditional dependence structures. This distinction is important for practitioners who want to model interactions without the full complexity of graphical models.

## Weaknesses

While the theoretical foundation is strong, the complexity measure C(G) = Σ α^|uj| appears somewhat arbitrary. The paper derives this from covering number bounds but doesn't sufficiently justify why this specific form is optimal for structure identification. A more principled derivation or comparison with alternative complexity measures would strengthen this contribution.

The paper's claim about computational feasibility for large p is somewhat overstated. The exhaustive search algorithm (Algorithm 1) is computationally prohibitive for even moderate p (the number of possible group structures grows super-exponentially with p). The backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is practical but, as the authors note, is greedy and may not find the optimal structure. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity of both algorithms and a comparison with alternative structure learning methods.

The paper does not adequately compare with related approaches for structure identification. While the connection to graphical models is mentioned, the authors don't provide a detailed comparison with methods that might also identify group structures (e.g., sparse graphical models or other structure learning techniques). A comparison with methods like those in [9] or [2] would strengthen the paper's contribution.

The theoretical analysis assumes fixed p (number of predictors), which is inconsistent with the paper's focus on high-dimensional settings. While the authors acknowledge the "small p" and "large p" scenarios, they don't provide theoretical guarantees for the case where p grows with n, which is crucial for high-dimensional applications.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a meaningful gap in nonparametric regression methodology. The paper provides a rigorous foundation for group additive structure identification, with solid theoretical results and empirical validation. The conceptual contribution of the "intrinsic group additive structure" is particularly valuable.

The main limitations are the somewhat ad hoc complexity measure, the lack of detailed comparison with related approaches, and the insufficient discussion of computational complexity for large-scale problems. However, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's strong theoretical foundation and practical utility.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contributions are significant and well-executed, and the method has clear practical value for high-dimensional nonparametric regression. While there are some limitations in the complexity measure and computational discussion, these do not undermine the paper's core contributions. The paper makes a valuable addition to the literature on nonparametric regression and structure learning, and would be of interest to the NIPS community.

The paper's strength in theoretical rigor and clear conceptual contribution outweighs the limitations in computational discussion and comparison with related methods. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
