### Review

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying interaction structures among predictor variables in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. Standard additive models are interpretable but ignore interactions, while fully nonparametric or high-order interaction models suffer from the curse of dimensionality and are difficult to interpret. The authors propose a middle ground: the group additive model, where the regression function is a sum of nonparametric functions, each defined over a disjoint subset (group) of predictors. This allows for interactions within groups but not between them.

The primary contributions of this work are twofold. First, it formalizes the problem of structure identification by defining the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the unique, minimal partition of variables that can represent the true regression function. This addresses the non-uniqueness issue present in prior work. Second, it proposes a novel method, Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI), to simultaneously identify this intrinsic structure and estimate the component functions. The method is framed as a penalized optimization problem within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. A key novelty is the introduction of a structural penalty term `C(G)` for a given group structure `G`. This penalty is derived in a principled manner from the capacity of the corresponding RKHS, specifically using bounds on its covering number. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that their proposed estimator for the group structure is consistent. They also propose an exhaustive search algorithm for small numbers of predictors and a backward stepwise algorithm for larger problems. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound. The formalization of the problem through the definition of amiable and intrinsic group structures (Definitions 1 & 2, Theorem 1) provides a solid theoretical foundation for the work. The proposed objective function (Eq. 6) is well-motivated, combining empirical risk, a standard RKHS norm penalty for regularization, and a novel penalty on the complexity of the group structure itself.

The theoretical analysis is a major strength. The authors provide a consistency proof (Theorem 4) for their structure identification procedure, showing that under certain conditions, the probability of recovering the true intrinsic structure converges to one as the sample size increases. The proof sketch, based on the convergence properties of the empirical risk for amiable versus non-amiable structures (Theorems 2 & 3), is logical and lends strong credibility to the proposed penalty.

The experimental validation supports the paper's claims, showing that the method can recover the true structure in several synthetic examples. The application to the Boston Housing data demonstrates the potential for generating interpretable, data-driven hypotheses about variable interactions.

A potential weakness lies in the practical implementation. The backward stepwise algorithm is a heuristic, and as the authors acknowledge, its greedy nature can prevent it from finding the global optimum, which may explain the poor performance on one of the simulation models (M4). The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the limitations of this heuristic approach. Furthermore, the theoretical results hold for "a proper tuning parameter μ," but guidance on how μ should scale with the sample size `n` to guarantee consistency would be valuable.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the group additive model relative to existing approaches. The progression in Section 2 from the definition of group structures, to kernel methods with a known structure, and finally to the proposed method for identifying an unknown structure is clear and easy to follow.

However, there are two areas where clarity could be improved:
1.  **Derivation of the Penalty Term:** The transition in Section 2.3.2 from the covering number bound in Eq. (4) to the final penalty `µ Σ α^|uj|` in Eq. (6) is abrupt. The paper mentions that the upper bound is dominated by `(ln(r/ε))^(p+1)`, which is reparameterized as `α^(p+1)`. The subsequent step to the final penalty form, where the `+1` in the exponent is dropped by "combining the common factor 1 in the exponent with µ," is not clearly justified and seems somewhat ad-hoc. A more detailed explanation of this simplification would be beneficial.
2.  **Algorithm Description:** The description of the backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is too high-level. The step "G' ← either split j as a new group or add to an existing group" is ambiguous. A more precise pseudocode or description detailing the set of moves considered at each step is necessary for reproducibility.

**Originality:**

The paper makes a significant and original contribution. While group additive models have been discussed previously (e.g., [11]), this work is the first to provide a rigorous framework for identifying the underlying structure from data. The core novelty lies in designing a penalty term based on the geometric complexity (via covering numbers) of the function space induced by a given group structure. This is a departure from standard regularization techniques like L1/L2 penalties on coefficients and represents a creative application of concepts from learning theory to a model selection problem. The formalization of the "intrinsic group additive structure" is also a novel conceptual contribution that clarifies the estimation target.

**Significance:**

The research addresses a fundamental problem in modern machine learning and statistics: how to build models that are flexible enough to capture complex relationships in high-dimensional data while remaining interpretable and avoiding the curse of dimensionality. The proposed GASI method provides a practical tool for data-driven discovery of interaction structures, which is valuable in many scientific fields where understanding the relationships between variables is as important as predictive accuracy. The approach could inspire new methods for structure discovery in other contexts (e.g., graphical models, deep learning).

The primary limitation on the work's immediate impact is computational scalability. The exhaustive search is limited to a very small number of predictors (`p`). The backward stepwise algorithm is more practical, but its computational cost and heuristic nature may limit its application to problems with very high dimensionality. Despite this, the paper presents a strong foundational framework that could spur further research into more efficient and scalable algorithms for this important problem.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and challenging problem of balancing flexibility and interpretability in nonparametric regression.
*   Introduces a novel and principled method for identifying group additive structures based on a well-motivated penalty derived from RKHS capacity.
*   Provides strong theoretical support with a consistency proof for the structure identification.
*   The work is a complete contribution, including theory, algorithms, and empirical validation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical scalability is a major concern, as the proposed algorithms are either computationally infeasible or heuristic for large `p`.
*   Key details regarding the derivation of the penalty term and the implementation of the stepwise algorithm lack clarity, hindering full understanding and reproducibility.
