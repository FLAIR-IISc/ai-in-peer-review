This paper addresses the problem of identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, a crucial step towards interpretability and efficiency in high-dimensional settings. The authors aim to tackle the non-uniqueness of such structures and propose a method based on kernel methods and a novel complexity measure for group additive structures. The paper is well-motivated, building upon existing work in additive models, functional ANOVA, and optimal kernel group transformations. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and positions the contribution within the existing literature.

The core idea—penalizing the complexity of the group structure—is insightful. The proposed complexity measure, based on covering numbers of the associated RKHSs, is well-justified and appears theoretically sound. The formulation of the optimization problem (6) and the proposed algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise approach) are clear and reasonable. The theoretical results, particularly the consistency proof (Theorem 4), are a significant contribution and provide a strong foundation for the method.  The authors appropriately acknowledge the connections to graphical models and highlight the differences in their approach.

The experimental evaluation is reasonably thorough, with simulations covering different model settings and a real-data application to the Boston Housing dataset. The simulation results demonstrate the ability of the method to recover the true group structure under various conditions. The real-data example offers a glimpse into the interpretability gains achieved by the group additive structure.  However, the simulations could benefit from more detailed comparisons with existing methods (e.g., OKGT) beyond simply showing the proposed method *can* recover the true structure. The discussion around the stepwise algorithm’s performance on Model 4 raises a valid point about the limitations of greedy approaches, and acknowledging this proactively is good scientific practice.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper is built on a strong theoretical foundation, with a rigorous proof of consistency.
*   **Novel Complexity Measure:** The proposed complexity measure for group additive structures is a significant contribution.
*   **Clear Formulation:** The optimization problem and algorithms are clearly presented and easy to understand.
*   **Well-Motivated:** The paper effectively motivates the problem and positions the contribution within the existing literature.
*   **Potential for Interpretability:** The method offers the potential for increased interpretability in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods:** The simulation results lack a detailed comparison to existing methods like OKGT. A benchmark comparison would strengthen the claims of novelty and improvement.
*   **Stepwise Algorithm Limitations:**  The authors acknowledge limitations of the stepwise algorithm, but further investigation into alternative algorithms might be valuable.
*   **Computational Cost:** The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm is not thoroughly discussed, potentially limiting its applicability to very high-dimensional problems.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The sensitivity of the method to the choice of tuning parameters (λ, μ, α) could be explored further.  A more systematic analysis of parameter selection would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
*   The proposed complexity measure and consistency proof are significant contributions to the field.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in recovering the true group structure.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a comprehensive comparison to existing methods weakens the claims of novelty and improvement.
*   The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm may limit the practical applicability of the method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and methodological contributions. The novelty of the complexity measure and the consistency proof are particularly commendable. While the lack of a comprehensive comparison to existing methods is a minor weakness, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the comparison point and potentially elaborate on the computational cost of the exhaustive search.
