This paper addresses the problem of identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, motivated by the limitations of purely additive models and the computational burden of higher-order interaction models. The authors propose a penalized kernel method to simultaneously identify the intrinsic group additive structure and estimate the nonparametric functions. The penalty term is based on a novel complexity measure for group additive structures derived from covering numbers of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs). Theoretical consistency results are established, and the method is evaluated through simulations and a real-data application.

The core idea of identifying a ‘group’ structure, effectively mediating between fully additive and fully interactive models, is a valuable contribution. The introduction clearly explains the motivation and positions the work within the existing literature on nonparametric regression, functional ANOVA, and optimal kernel group transformations. The formulation of the problem and the proposed solution are mathematically sound. The use of RKHS theory to define a complexity measure for group structures is insightful and provides a solid theoretical foundation. The consistency proof is a significant achievement, demonstrating the theoretical validity of the approach. The two algorithmic approaches, exhaustive search and a stepwise method, provide practical options for implementation, addressing different computational scales. The real-data application demonstrates the potential usefulness of the method in a practical setting.

However, there are some weaknesses. The extensive use of asymptotic results and theoretical guarantees, while rigorous, makes the paper somewhat dense and difficult to follow for readers less familiar with the underlying mathematical machinery.  The practical impact of the proposed complexity measure, while theoretically sound, isn’t fully elucidated. It’s unclear *why* this specific measure is better than other possible complexity measures for group structures. The simulations, while comprehensive in terms of model variations, lack a detailed comparison against existing methods for nonparametric regression or interaction detection. The impact of the choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., bandwidth) isn’t thoroughly discussed; it is noted in passing that universal kernels should be used, but a more detailed analysis of sensitivity to these choices would be helpful. The real-data analysis is somewhat limited; a more in-depth discussion of the interpretability of the identified groups and their implications for the housing data would strengthen the application.  The explanation of Algorithms 1 and 2, while present, could benefit from more detailed pseudocode or illustrative examples to improve clarity.  The connection to graphical models is briefly mentioned but not fully explored – expanding on this could highlight potential synergies.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed group additive structure identification method and the associated complexity measure are novel contributions to the field of nonparametric regression.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is grounded in solid mathematical foundations, with a rigorous consistency proof.
*   **Potential Significance:** The method addresses a relevant problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression, offering a balance between interpretability and flexibility.
*   **Clear Presentation of Core Ideas:** The core concepts and mathematical framework are well-defined.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Density and Technicality:** The paper's mathematical rigor may make it inaccessible to some readers.
*   **Limited Empirical Comparison:** The simulation results lack a detailed comparison with existing methods.
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:**  The sensitivity to kernel choice and parameter tuning is not fully addressed.
*   **Real-Data Application Could Be Stronger:** The real-data analysis lacks in-depth interpretation of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. While the density of the mathematical presentation and the limited empirical comparison are weaknesses, the theoretical rigor and potential significance of the work outweigh these concerns.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider: 1) Providing more intuitive explanations of the key mathematical concepts; 2) Including a more thorough comparison against existing methods in the simulations; and 3) Expanding the discussion of practical considerations, such as the choice of kernel and parameter tuning.  A more detailed interpretation of the real-data results would also be beneficial.
