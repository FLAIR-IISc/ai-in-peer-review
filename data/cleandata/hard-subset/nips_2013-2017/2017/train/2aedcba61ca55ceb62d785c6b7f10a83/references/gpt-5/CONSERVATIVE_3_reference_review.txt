Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a “group additive” assumption: f(X) can be written as a sum of unknown functions, each depending on a group of variables. The authors (i) formalize a partial order over group partitions and define the “intrinsic” group additive structure as the minimal element among all partitions that can represent f, (ii) propose to identify this structure by minimizing a kernel ridge regression objective augmented with a penalty on the complexity of the group structure, where the penalty is derived from covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs, and (iii) provide consistency results for identifying the intrinsic group structure when globally minimizing the penalized objective. They give two algorithms (exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic) and present small-scale simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

Relation to prior work
This work sits between additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), functional ANOVA/smoothing spline ANOVA (Gu), and higher-order interaction KRR (e.g., SALSA with ESP kernels, Kandasamy & Yu). The idea of selecting interaction structure in kernel spaces relates to hierarchical multiple kernel learning (e.g., Bach, NIPS/JMLR 2008–2011), sparse additive models (Ravikumar et al., Annals 2009), and kernel methods with ANOVA kernels. The proposed “group” perspective is close to OKGT (Pan et al., KDD), which the authors extend by introducing identifiability via an intrinsic structure and a structural capacity penalty.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual target: formalizing an intrinsic group partition that balances expressiveness and interpretability.
- A principled use of RKHS capacity notions to motivate a structure penalty, together with covering-number control for direct sums.
- Asymptotic selection consistency for the global optimizer under the proposed penalization.
- The framework unifies estimation and structure selection and is compatible with universal kernels.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological soundness and theory
- Identifiability assumptions: Several results (e.g., strict inclusion of function spaces under refinement) invoke independence among covariates; however, identifiability of the “intrinsic” structure under general dependence is not made precise. In realistic settings (e.g., Boston Housing), X’s are correlated; how does dependence affect uniqueness/identifiability of G* and the selection guarantees?
- Completeness of Ga: The existence/uniqueness of a minimal element (Theorem 1) relies on Ga being partially ordered and “complete,” but the conditions ensuring completeness are not specified; the argument is sketched rather than rigorously established.
- Penalty derivation: The transition from covering-number bounds of the form ln N(ε, HK,r, ||·||∞) ≤ C (ln(r/ε))d+1 to a practical penalty µ Σj α|uj| (with α tuned over integers 1–10) is ad hoc. ε and r are abstract parameters; treating α as a free scalar severs the penalty from the theoretical bound. A principled, data-dependent complexity control (e.g., local Rademacher complexity) or a BIC-style penalty scaling with n would be more defensible.
- The selection consistency (Theorem 4) appears to hold for the global minimizer of the nonconvex search over partitions. No guarantees are provided for the stepwise heuristic used in all nontrivial settings.

Algorithmic practicality
- Exhaustive search over partitions is combinatorial and infeasible beyond very small p; the stepwise algorithm is greedy with no approximation or recovery guarantees. There is no discussion of computational complexity, scalability, or strategies (e.g., convex relaxations, hierarchical constraints) that have been studied in HKL.

Empirical validation
- Baselines: There is no comparison to strong, established alternatives, such as SALSA/ESP kernels, smoothing spline ANOVA, HKL, SpAM/GAMs with pairwise/low-order interactions (including kernelized group lasso or hierarchical sparsity). Without such baselines, it is difficult to assess practical benefits.
- Metrics and rigor: Simulation results emphasize “frequency of recovering the true group structure” under multiple tuned (µ,α) grids, but do not report predictive performance, calibration, or sensitivity to λ. The deterioration noted for Model 1 suggests instability. The stepwise method succeeds on 4/5 toy models; failure on Model 4 is attributed to greediness, underscoring the need for algorithmic improvements.
- Real data: The Boston Housing study reports an interpretable partition but no out-of-sample comparison versus standard GAMs, ANOVA kernels, SALSA, or tree-based models. Given correlated features and no ground truth, it is hard to judge whether the discovered groups are robust or superior.

Clarity and presentation
- Several notational/typographical issues and missing years in references. The role of kernel choice, hyperparameter selection (bandwidths), and λ is under-specified. The dependence of constants on group dimension (|u|) versus total p should be clarified in capacity bounds.
- The term “amiable” is nonstandard; consider “admissible” or “compatible” to align with prior literature.

Originality and significance
- The notion of an intrinsic group additive structure and linking structure selection to RKHS capacity is interesting. However, related lines (HKL, SALSA, sparse additive/ANOVA models) already address interaction selection with stronger algorithmic and empirical grounding. The incremental theoretical contribution is limited by the imprecise identifiability assumptions and the gap between the penalty derivation and its practical instantiation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide precise conditions (on PX, dependence among X, and kernels) under which G* exists and is identifiable; discuss failure modes under dependence.
- Replace or justify the α-penalty with a theoretically grounded, data-dependent penalty; consider BIC/Rademacher-based penalties with explicit n-scaling.
- Develop an algorithm with guarantees (e.g., hierarchical multiple kernel learning / convex relaxations) or prove that the stepwise procedure is consistent under conditions (e.g., restricted submodularity).
- Add comprehensive experiments with strong baselines (SALSA, SS-ANOVA, HKL, SpAM/GAM+pairwise), report predictive and structural metrics, ablations on λ, µ, α, and scalability to larger p.
- Release code and detail kernel choices and tuning.

Pros
- Conceptually appealing framework; uses RKHS theory.
- Provides asymptotic selection consistency for the idealized global optimizer.
- Interpretability motivation is clear.

Cons
- Identifiability under dependence unclear; theoretical gaps.
- Penalty instantiation is heuristic relative to the derivation.
- No scalable, provably good algorithm; heuristic used in practice.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks strong baselines.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper presents an interesting idea but currently lacks the methodological rigor (on identifiability and penalty design), algorithmic development, and empirical validation against established baselines necessary for NeurIPS. Strengthening the theoretical assumptions, providing a principled penalty and a scalable algorithm with guarantees, and adding thorough comparisons could make this a solid contribution.
