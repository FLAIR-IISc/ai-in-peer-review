

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper introduces the concept of "intrinsic group additive structure" for nonparametric regression and proposes a method to identify it using RKHS complexity measures. The paper builds upon additive models and functional ANOVA models by introducing a middle ground that accommodates interactions while maintaining dimensionality benefits. The key contribution is defining the minimal group additive structure and developing a structural penalty approach for identification.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to an important problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The concept of intrinsic group additive structure is well-defined and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The methodological development is logical, with clear connections between group structure, RKHS capacity, and model complexity. The experimental results demonstrate practical utility on both synthetic and real data.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical Missing Reference:** The paper claims "the optimal asymptotic error rate for estimating a d-times differentiable function is O(n^{-d/(2d+p)})" without citing the source (cited as [16] in the text). This is a fundamental claim about nonparametric regression theory that requires proper attribution.

2. **Inconsistent Notation:** The complexity measure uses "α( )" without defining what " " represents. This is a critical ambiguity as the entire complexity measure depends on this parameter. The paper should explicitly define this as the covering radius or similar.

3. **Formatting Inconsistencies:** 
   - The line break in "f(X) = f1(X1) + · · ·+fp(Xp)" (with a space before the plus sign) is inconsistent with standard mathematical notation.
   - References contain formatting errors, such as "Rademacher and gaussian complexities: Risk bounds and structural results, P.L. Bartlett, S. Mendelson, The Journal of Machine Learning Research, 3:463–482, , " with an extra comma.

4. **Unjustified Methodological Choices:** The paper states "after investigating and comparing different measures, we use covering number" but provides no justification for why covering numbers were preferred over other capacity measures (VC dimension, Rademacher complexity). This is a significant omission for a theoretical paper.

5. **Ambiguous Parameter Specification:** The paper states "α is an integer in [1, 10]" but doesn't explain why these specific values were chosen. The relationship between α and the covering number parameter needs clarification.

6. **Theoretical Gaps:** The claim that "for a fixed p, the number of amiable group additive structures is finite" is stated but not proven or referenced. This is important for the consistency proof but presented as an obvious fact.

7. **Algorithmic Detail Missing:** In Algorithm 1, step 9 uses "e2Ĝµ,α" notation without defining what "e2" represents. This should be explicitly defined as the validation error or mean squared error.

8. **Supplementary Material Reference:** The paper states "The proof and supporting lemmas are included in the supplementary material" but doesn't provide a specific reference to the supplementary material in the main text, which is required for reproducibility.

**Additional Concerns:**

The paper claims the intrinsic group structure "can help much mitigate the curse of dimensionality" but fails to quantify this advantage compared to standard additive models. The connection to graphical models is mentioned but not elaborated, making it unclear how this approach differs from existing work in that domain.

The simulation results in Table 2 show "Model 1 deteriorated" when using Algorithm 1, but the paper doesn't investigate why this occurred, which is a significant weakness in the experimental evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to nonparametric regression, with a novel concept of intrinsic group additive structure. The methodological approach is sound, and the experimental results are promising. However, the paper contains multiple technical and formatting inconsistencies that undermine its quality as a scientific contribution. The missing reference for a fundamental claim, inconsistent notation, and lack of justification for methodological choices are particularly concerning for a NIPS submission.

While the core idea is novel and potentially impactful, the paper's current form contains too many minor but significant flaws that would make it difficult for other researchers to reproduce the work or fully understand the theoretical contributions. These issues are not merely stylistic but affect the paper's scientific rigor.

**Recommendation:** Reject

The paper's theoretical contribution is valuable, but the numerous technical and presentation flaws significantly diminish its quality as a scientific contribution to NIPS. The paper would benefit from substantial revision to address these issues before being considered for publication.
