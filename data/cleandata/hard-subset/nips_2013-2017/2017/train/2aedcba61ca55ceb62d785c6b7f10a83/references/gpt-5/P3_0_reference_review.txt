Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression under an additive structure over groups of variables. Instead of classic additive models (sum over individual coordinates) or high-order functional ANOVA, the authors consider partitions G of {1,…,p} and models f(X)=∑u∈G fu(Xu). They formalize a partial order on group structures (refinement), define amiable structures for a target f, and introduce the intrinsic group additive structure G* as the “minimal” element among amiable structures. Estimation is done in RKHS via a direct-sum space HG=⊕u∈G Hu with a penalized objective that trades off fit, RKHS norm, and a structural penalty C(G). C(G) is derived from covering number bounds of direct-sum RKHSs and simplified to C(G)=∑u∈G α|u| (α tuned). The authors show selection consistency of Ĝ for G* under their penalty, propose exhaustive and greedy (backward stepwise) search algorithms, and present simulations and a Boston Housing application.

Relation to prior work
- Additive models and GAMs: classic [Hastie & Tibshirani]. Functional ANOVA and spline ANOVA (e.g., Gu) and the ESP kernel approach (SALSA) consider interactions up to a specified order. The present work lies between pure additivity and full interaction by allowing multivariate components over groups.
- RKHS-based model selection: ties to structural capacity control (covering numbers, Rademacher complexity) and kernel ridge regression. Related to hierarchical/structured kernel learning and group-sparsity over ANOVA decompositions (e.g., Bach 2009; not cited) and sparse additive models (SpAM; Ravikumar et al., not cited).
- OKGT (Pan et al.) motivated group additivity but did not address identifiability or provide a scalable selection mechanism; this paper attempts both.

Strengths
- Interesting and relevant problem formulation that targets interactions implicitly via grouping without incurring the combinatorial blow-up of all pairwise/higher-order terms.
- Clear link between model selection over partitions and RKHS capacity; using covering numbers to define a structure penalty is principled in spirit.
- Theoretical results aiming at selection consistency for the (population) intrinsic group structure, with a uniform risk control for amiable vs. non-amiable structures.
- Algorithms for small p (exhaustive) and a basic greedy heuristic for larger p; empirical illustrations.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
1) Intrinsic structure existence/uniqueness: Theorem 1 claims a unique minimal G* such that G* ≤ G for all amiable G. That is a least element, not just a minimal one. Existence/uniqueness is nontrivial. The set of partitions is a lattice under refinement, but the subset of partitions for which f is representable (amiable) need not have a least element unless additional assumptions hold. Counterexamples arise when variables are collinear or deterministically related (e.g., X2=X1 and f depends only on X1), yielding multiple incomparable minimal partitions. Independence is invoked later to obtain strict inclusions for L2 spaces (Prop. 1), but Theorem 1 is stated without such assumptions. Please state precise conditions on PX (e.g., product measure with full support) under which G* exists and is unique, or relax Theorem 1 to allow multiple minimal elements.

2) Structural penalty design and its link to theory: The derivation starts from covering numbers ln N(ε, I(HK,r), ∞) ≤ C(ln(r/ε))p+1 for convolution kernels with exponentially decaying Fourier transform (e.g., Gaussian). For direct sums, you upper bound the covering number by a product/union-type inequality (Prop. 3), then propose C(G)=∑ α|u|. However:
- The transition from (ln(r/ε))|u|+1 to α|u| (absorbing constants into μ) is ad hoc; α is defined as ln(r/ε) but is treated as an integer tuning parameter in experiments. This disconnect weakens the conceptual justification of the practical penalty.
- The penalty does not address incomparability of partitions beyond dimensionality; two structurally different partitions with the same multiset of group sizes receive the same C(G). Is this sufficient for consistency? The proofs rely on risk separation between non-amiable and amiable plus a penalty preference for the smallest RKHS capacity. Please clarify how the chosen C(G) (with tuned α) ensures G* is the unique minimizer among all amiable partitions, especially when multiple partitions have identical size profiles.
- The theory assumes kernels with stated capacity bounds; the empirical section uses Gaussian/Laplace? Please state precisely and verify compatibility.

3) Statistical identifiability: Without ANOVA-style marginal constraints, additive component functions are identifiable only up to constants, which is fine, but group partitions themselves may be unidentifiable under correlated PX. Your consistency results consider population risks but do not discuss how correlation structure can induce multiple amiable minimal partitions. This ties back to point (1): either impose independence/product structure or discuss identifiability under dependence.

4) Algorithms and scalability: 
- Exhaustive search scales with the Bell number of partitions; not feasible beyond small p.
- The backward stepwise algorithm is under-specified (how are candidate splits/merges enumerated? how many passes? stopping rules?) and lacks any approximation guarantees or consistency. The negative result on Model 4 highlights instability.
- There is a rich literature on structured sparsity/partition search (graph cuts, dynamic programming over partitions, submodular relaxations) that could be leveraged for more reliable and scalable selection.

5) Experiments:
- The simulation study measures frequency of recovering the true partition but tunes μ and α to maximize this frequency, which is optimistic. The validation-based selection results are mixed and suggest sensitivity to λ and the KRR solver.
- No comparisons to baselines such as SALSA (order-2/3 interactions), hierarchical kernel learning/structured sparsity, SpAM with interaction groups, or OKGT. Predictive performance, stability, and runtime comparisons are necessary to establish significance.
- Real data (Boston Housing): the discovered partition is plausible, but there is no evaluation against baselines, no assessment of predictive gains, and no stability across CV folds. Interpretations are anecdotal.

6) Clarity and presentation:
- Several statements are informal or imprecise (e.g., “Ga is complete” and uniqueness of G*). Proof sketches and assumptions should be explicit. Distinguish “least element” vs. “minimal elements.”
- Notation: L2u(X) defined via PX; ensure conditions for density of universal kernels in L2(Xu) wrt PXu are stated.
- References are incomplete (years missing) and some relevant works are not cited (structured/hierarchical kernel learning; sparse additive models; interaction detection via HSIC).

Pros
- Addresses a practically relevant trade-off: allowing interactions while controlling model complexity via groups.
- Attempts a principled capacity-based penalty and provides a selection consistency result.
- Bridges kernel methods with partition selection.

Cons
- Key identifiability/uniqueness claim lacks sufficient assumptions.
- Structural penalty used in practice is weakly tied to the theoretical derivation and may not distinguish incomparable partitions.
- Algorithmic framework is not scalable or theoretically justified; empirical evaluation is limited and lacks baselines.

Suggestions
- Clarify and correct Theorem 1; add sufficient conditions (e.g., independence, richness of PX support) or allow multiple minimal partitions and adapt consistency claims accordingly (e.g., convergence to the set of minimal partitions).
- Tighten the link between covering-number bounds and the implemented penalty. Consider a data-dependent capacity metric (localized Rademacher/empirical complexity) or a convex structured sparsity penalty over group kernels with theoretical selection guarantees.
- Provide a more robust search strategy (e.g., beam search, branch-and-bound with admissible bounds from your penalty, or convex relaxations).
- Expand experiments with strong baselines, predictive metrics, runtime, and stability analyses; avoid oracle tuning on recovery frequency.
- Improve clarity of assumptions and complete references.

Overall: The problem and direction are interesting, but the current version has theoretical and empirical gaps that make it premature for acceptance. With strengthened identifiability assumptions/theory, a better-justified structural penalty, scalable selection, and thorough comparisons, this could be a solid contribution.
