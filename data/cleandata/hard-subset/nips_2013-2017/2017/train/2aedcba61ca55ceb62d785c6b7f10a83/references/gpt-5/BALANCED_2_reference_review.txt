Summary
This paper studies nonparametric regression under a group additive structure: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of variables and each fu is multivariate within its group. The authors (i) formalize a partial order on group structures and define the intrinsic group additive structure G*, the minimal element among all partitions for which f is representable; (ii) propose to identify G* jointly with estimating f via RKHSs corresponding to each group and a structural penalty derived from covering-number capacity bounds; (iii) provide selection consistency for recovering G* under appropriate tuning; and (iv) develop an exhaustive and a backward stepwise algorithm, with simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

Relation to prior work: The paper sits between additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1990) and functional ANOVA/symmetric polynomial/kernel interaction models (e.g., smoothing spline ANOVA; Kandasamy & Yu, 2016), offering more flexibility than additive models while avoiding the combinatorial explosion of unrestricted interactions. It connects to structural risk minimization in RKHS (Vapnik, 1998; Schölkopf & Smola, 2002) by penalizing capacity, and to universality results (Steinwart & Christmann, 2008). The “grouping” idea echoes OKGT (Pan et al., 2015), but here the authors introduce an identifiability notion (intrinsic structure) and a penalty-based estimator. There are also links to sparse additive models and interaction selection (e.g., Ravikumar et al., 2010; Bien et al., 2013), though these are not discussed.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: formal definition of an intrinsic group additive structure and consistency results for identifying it.
- Sound use of RKHS machinery: direct-sum RKHS construction aligns naturally with grouped variables; universality ensures approximation capability.
- Theoretical guarantees: uniform risk bounds using covering numbers and a selection consistency theorem under suitable tuning.
- Practical motivation: when small groups capture interactions, the approach balances flexibility and interpretability better than pure additive or high-order interaction models.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Structural penalty derivation vs implementation: The covering-number bound leads to C(G) ∝ ∑u α(ε)^{|u|+1}. The paper then “simplifies” to µ∑u α^{|u|} and tunes α on a grid. This disconnects the implemented penalty from the theoretical capacity measure (kernel- and ε-dependent constants are dropped; exponent changes from |u|+1 to |u|). The selection consistency proof appears to rely on the principled penalty, but experiments use a heuristic. Please clarify which penalty is covered by Theorem 4 and provide conditions/tuning schedules µn (and any αn) that ensure consistency.
- Loose bounds and unknown constants: Covering-number bounds carry kernel- and distribution-dependent constants, making the penalty hard to calibrate. Consider replacing capacity with data-driven surrogates (e.g., per-group effective degrees of freedom for KRR: tr(Ku(Ku+λI)^{-1})) to yield BIC-like penalties with clearer interpretation and potential finite-sample gains.
- Statistical assumptions: Strict inclusion of function spaces uses independent covariates. Real data often have correlated X. Discuss how identifiability and Theorem 1 behave under dependence (e.g., Hilbert-space ANOVA requires product measures). What assumptions on PX are required for selection consistency?
- Algorithmic scalability: Exhaustive search over partitions is Bell-number growth; the basic backward stepwise procedure is greedy and may miss the true partition (acknowledged). No approximation guarantees or heuristics (e.g., beam search, stochastic search/MCMC over partitions, hierarchical divisive clustering, or relaxations via group-sparsity) are provided.
- Empirics: No comparisons to relevant baselines (GAM/GAM with interactions, SpAM, SALSA/ESP kernel, functional ANOVA with selection, hierarchy-enforcing methods like hierNet). Experiments use small p and do not study p>>n, runtime, or robustness to correlation and kernel misspecification. The Boston Housing example is illustrative but not diagnostic.

Clarity
- Several notation and editorial issues: α’s definition drifts; sometimes |u|+1 vs |u|; occasional inconsistencies (R̂g vs R̂, λn conditions, subscript conventions). The stepwise algorithm description (step 4) is under-specified. References lack years in many entries. Tighten exposition and ensure the implemented penalty matches theory.
- Specify kernel choice and bandwidth selection; universality alone does not guarantee good finite-sample performance.

Originality
- The intrinsic structure notion and RKHS-based structure selection are original in this form. However, connections to and differentiation from existing structured nonparametric methods (SpAM; group-sparse ANOVA; interaction pursuit) should be expanded and empirically validated.

Significance
- Potentially impactful if it robustly identifies small interacting groups. Current empirical evidence is preliminary; lack of baselines and scalability limits make it hard to assess practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and interesting formalization of group additive structure and its identifiability.
- A principled capacity-based approach to structure selection with consistency guarantees.
- Addresses an important middle ground between additive and high-order interaction models.

Cons for acceptance
- Gap between theoretical penalty and practical implementation; tuning guidance is unclear.
- Limited, small-scale empirical validation, no baselines, and weak large-p story.
- Algorithmic approach is heuristic with no guarantees and mixed performance on some models.
- Assumptions (independence) and conditions for consistency not fully aligned with practical settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Align the penalty used in practice with the theory, or re-derive guarantees for the implemented penalty. Provide explicit schedules for µn (and αn if needed) and discuss sensitivity.
- Consider data-driven penalties (effective degrees of freedom per group) and compare against covering-number-based penalties.
- Add strong baselines and broader experiments: compare to GAM/SpAM, SALSA/ESP-kernel, functional ANOVA with selection, and hierarchy-respecting interaction models; include correlated covariates, p up to 50–200, p>>n regimes, runtime, and ablations on α, µ, kernel bandwidths.
- Improve search: beam search, stochastic local search, annealing/MCMC over partitions; or relax to a continuous proxy with group-sparsity/overlapping-group penalties and derive guarantees under restricted conditions.
- Clarify assumptions on PX (independence vs dependence), kernel choices, and give constructive examples of when G* is unique under dependence.
- Release code and provide reproducibility details.

Decision
Reject (encouraged to resubmit). The paper presents a thoughtful and potentially useful framework with nontrivial theory, but the mismatch between the theoretical penalty and the implemented method, the lack of strong empirical validation and baselines, and limited scalability currently fall short of NeurIPS standards. Strengthening these aspects could make this a compelling contribution.
