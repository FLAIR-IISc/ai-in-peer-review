

# Peer Review: "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical gap in nonparametric regression: the identification of group additive structures, which lies between standard additive models (no interactions) and higher-order interaction models (which suffer from the curse of dimensionality). The authors define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group structure that can represent a target function, resolving the non-uniqueness issue present in previous work (e.g., [11]). They develop a structural penalty approach using RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure for group structures, proving theoretical consistency and proposing efficient algorithms for structure identification. The paper connects to prior work on additive models ([5], [13]), functional ANOVA ([4], [6]), and graphical models ([2], [7], [9]), while clearly distinguishing its focus on group additive structure rather than conditional dependence.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by rigorously defining the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and proving the consistency of the proposed method (Theorem 4). The derivation of the complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers (Section 2.3.2) is novel and well-justified.

2. **Practical Algorithms**: The paper develops two practical algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) that can handle both small and large predictor dimensions, with clear implementation details (Algorithms 1 and 2). The empirical results in Sections 4 and 5 demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both synthetic and real data.

3. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively frames the problem of group additive structure identification as a trade-off between model fit and structural complexity, which is a natural and well-motivated approach.

4. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The simulation study (Table 2, Figure 2) demonstrates successful identification of true group structures across multiple models. The Boston Housing application (Section 5) provides a compelling real-world example with interpretable visualizations of the estimated functions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Algorithmic Limitations**: The backward stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is greedy and may fail to identify the true structure if the optimal path isn't traversed (as noted in Section 4). The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't provide a clear path forward for improving the algorithm.

2. **Computational Complexity**: While the paper acknowledges the computational burden of exhaustive search for large p, the theoretical justification for why the backward stepwise approach works (beyond empirical results) is limited. The authors should discuss how the computational complexity scales with p.

3. **Parameter Selection**: The tuning parameter selection (using validation sets) seems somewhat ad hoc. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore how sensitive the results are to the choice of µ and α, or whether there are principled ways to select them.

4. **Comparison with Related Work**: While the paper distinguishes its approach from graphical models, it would strengthen the contribution to more directly compare with methods that also aim to identify interaction structures, such as those in [6] or [11].

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical foundations and well-designed empirical validation. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the greedy nature of Algorithm 2) and provide a complete picture of their method's strengths and weaknesses. The paper is a complete piece of work that advances the field of nonparametric regression.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem definition to theoretical development to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The algorithms are described precisely, and the supplementary material appears to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The real data application (Section 5) is particularly well-presented with informative visualizations.

## Originality Assessment

The concept of "intrinsic group additive structure" is novel and addresses a fundamental issue in the field (the non-uniqueness of group structures). The use of RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure for group structures is innovative. While building on previous work on additive models and OKGT, the paper makes a clear and significant contribution that goes beyond incremental improvements. The approach is distinct from related work on graphical models and functional ANOVA.

## Significance Assessment

This work addresses an important practical problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The ability to identify interpretable group structures while maintaining estimation efficiency fills a critical gap between standard additive models and more complex interaction models. The method has clear potential applications in fields requiring interpretable nonparametric models. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded, practical solution to a previously overlooked problem.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression with a novel theoretical framework, practical algorithms, and strong empirical validation. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful problem that has practical implications for high-dimensional data analysis. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding algorithmic improvements), these do not undermine the paper's substantial contribution.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- First rigorous definition of intrinsic group additive structure
- Novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers
- Theoretical consistency guarantees
- Practical algorithms with strong empirical performance
- Clear distinction from related work on graphical models

**Con:**
- Backward stepwise algorithm is greedy and may miss optimal structure
- Computational complexity analysis for large p is limited
- Parameter selection strategy could be more principled

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances our understanding of nonparametric regression in high dimensions and provides a valuable tool for practitioners. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field.
