This paper tackles the important problem of nonparametric regression in high dimensions by proposing a method to identify a group additive structure. The core idea – finding a “sweet spot” between fully additive models and higher-order interaction models – is compelling and potentially impactful. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches and propose a principled solution based on RKHS theory and a novel complexity measure for group structures. The connection to graphical models is also well-articulated. This is a conceptually strong paper that takes a bold approach to a challenging problem.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly identifying a group additive structure, along with the proposed complexity measure and associated minimization problem, is genuinely novel. This goes beyond incremental improvements and proposes a new framework for tackling high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is built on a solid theoretical foundation, leveraging RKHS theory to derive a tractable complexity measure for group structures and providing consistency guarantees for the proposed method.
*   **Potential Significance:** If successful, this approach could lead to more interpretable and efficient nonparametric models in high-dimensional settings. The proposed method balances model complexity and goodness-of-fit in a principled manner.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors clearly articulate the shortcomings of existing methods and motivate the need for a new approach. The definition of the "intrinsic group additive structure" is particularly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The exhaustive search algorithm, while conceptually straightforward, is computationally expensive and limits the applicability to small p. The stepwise algorithm is an improvement, but its greedy nature raises concerns about finding the optimal structure.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the choice of λ, μ, and α, as suggested by the simulation results. A more robust method for parameter selection would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies are reasonably comprehensive, but the real data application is somewhat limited. More extensive validation on diverse datasets would be beneficial.
*   **Assumptions:** The theory relies on certain assumptions regarding the kernel and data distribution. A more thorough discussion of the robustness of the method to violations of these assumptions would be helpful.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially significant contribution to the field. While the computational complexity and parameter sensitivity are concerns, they do not overshadow the originality and theoretical soundness of the work. The authors have identified a crucial gap in existing methods and proposed a compelling solution. I am willing to overlook the minor flaws in validation and computational efficiency given the boldness and potential impact of the idea.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and conceptually strong approach to a challenging problem.
*   Solid theoretical foundation with consistency guarantees.
*   Potential to significantly improve the interpretability and efficiency of nonparametric regression in high dimensions.
*   Clear problem definition and motivation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity limits scalability.
*   Parameter selection appears sensitive.
*   Real-data validation is somewhat limited.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept.**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field, and the novelty of the approach outweighs the minor weaknesses. I encourage the authors to address the computational complexity and parameter sensitivity issues in future work. This paper has the potential to open up new avenues of research in nonparametric regression and inspire further investigation into group structure identification.
