Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies nonparametric regression when the regression function admits an unknown “group additive” structure: the predictors are partitioned into disjoint groups and the regression function is the sum of multivariate functions over groups. This generalizes classical additive models (univariate components) while controlling the combinatorial explosion of full ANOVA or higher-order interaction models. The authors (i) formalize non-uniqueness of group structures and define a partial order over partitions; (ii) introduce the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure (the minimal amiable partition under this order) and prove existence/uniqueness; (iii) propose a kernel-RKHS framework for estimation when the group structure is known; and (iv) when it is unknown, propose selecting G via penalized empirical risk that couples a KRR fit within each direct-sum RKHS with a novel penalty on G derived from covering-number bounds. They give consistency results for recovering the intrinsic structure and present an exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic, with small-scale simulations and one real-data example.

The work connects to additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), SS-ANOVA (Gu), and kernel methods (KRR, universal kernels). Unlike functional ANOVA and ESP-kernel work (e.g., Kandasamy & Yu’s SALSA), which entertains all interactions up to order D, this paper promotes disjoint groups to capture a subset of interactions compactly. It relates to OKGT (Pan et al.), providing a principled identification procedure and consistency guarantees. It is also related to structured sparsity and interaction discovery (e.g., SpAM; Bien et al.’s hierNet), additive GPs (Duvenaud et al., NIPS 2011), and multi-kernel learning, although direct empirical comparisons are not provided.

Quality
- Theory: The intrinsic structure concept is well-motivated. The use of covering-number upper bounds for RKHS balls to induce a structure penalty is interesting; via a bound on the covering number of a direct-sum space, the proposed complexity becomes sum over groups of α|u| (after simplifications). The high-level selection consistency results for G are appealing: amiable structures achieve the same population risk, non-amiable ones incur a fixed bias, and the penalty differentiates G* from larger amiable partitions. However:
  - The path from the bound ln N(ε, H, ∞) ≲ (ln(r/ε))p+1 to the practical penalty µ Σ α|u| involves several heuristic steps: reparameterizing ln(r/ε) by a tunable α and then effectively dropping the +1 in the exponent (absorbing it “into µ”). This weakens the formal link between the generalization bound and the implemented penalty, and raises questions about calibration and robustness.
  - The partial-order and existence/uniqueness of G* rely on completeness of the poset of amiable structures; details are deferred, and assumptions ensuring uniqueness beyond toy cases (e.g., independence) are not fully explicit. Proposition 1 requires feature independence for strict inclusion of function spaces; the role of dependence in uniqueness of G* and identifiability in RKHSs is not clarified.
  - The analysis assumes universal kernels to avoid approximation bias. In practice, ridge penalties and overlapping representer decompositions across groups may induce different RKHS norms for the same target function under different partitions; the paper does not discuss whether the norm penalty might inadvertently favor a super-structure even among amiable G.
- Algorithms: Exhaustive search scales exponentially with p; the backward stepwise heuristic is simple but can get stuck and has no guarantees. No complexity analysis or acceleration (e.g., submodular relaxations, dynamic programming under constraints, or screening) is provided.
- Experiments: Simulations indicate the method can recover true structures for small p. However, there are few baselines (none reported), limited sensitivity analyses (e.g., effect of λ, µ, α), and no larger p regimes. The Boston Housing case study is illustrative but not conclusive. The mismatch between theory-driven parameter choice and practice (α treated as integer 1–10) deserves discussion.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-structured, though there are several notation/terminology issues:
- The definition and use of α from ln(r/ε) is confusing; the transition to the final penalty µ Σ α|u| should be made precise.
- Some proofs and assumptions (e.g., poset completeness; independence vs dependence) are deferred; a clearer statement of assumptions in main text would help.
- Algorithm 2’s step 4 (“split j as a new group or add to an existing group”) is ambiguous; specify the search neighborhood and stopping criterion.

Originality
The intrinsic group additive structure formulation and the covering-number–based penalty tailored to direct-sum RKHSs are novel to my knowledge. The conceptual middle ground between additive and full ANOVA models is not new, but the identification consistency result for group partitions in an RKHS setting is original relative to OKGT and SALSA. That said, the penalty’s practical instantiation is somewhat ad hoc.

Significance
Identifying low-order interacting groups can materially improve interpretability and sample efficiency. If scalable and robust, this could impact nonparametric modeling practice. As presented, limited scalability, heuristic tuning, and modest empirical validation constrain immediate impact.

Pros
- Clear problem motivation; formalization of intrinsic group structure.
- Principled connection between RKHS capacity and structure selection; selection consistency results.
- Simple algorithms; positive small-scale empirical indications; interpretable real-data example.

Cons
- Penalty derivation-to-implementation gap (α and exponent handling).
- Assumptions for uniqueness/identifiability not fully explicit; dependence not treated.
- Computational scalability limited; stepwise lacks guarantees.
- Sparse empirical evaluation and no comparisons to strong baselines (SALSA, additive GPs, hierNet/SpAM-like methods, OKGT).

Suggestions for improvement
- Tighten the theoretical bridge from covering-number bounds to the exact penalty used. Either keep the exponent |u|+1 and justify absorbing constants, or present an information criteria/MDL-style derivation yielding a comparable additive penalty over group sizes.
- Clarify assumptions for Theorem 1 (existence/uniqueness) and discuss the role of covariate dependence; provide examples where dependence leads to non-uniqueness and how the method behaves.
- Analyze the effect of λ and the RKHS norm on selection among amiable structures; consider adding an explicit structural regularizer independent of λ (e.g., a penalty proportional to group count/size) and study identifiability in the direct-sum RKHS (orthogonality/centering constraints).
- Provide stronger experiments: compare to SALSA, additive GPs with learned additive structure, OKGT, and interaction-selection methods; include higher-dimensional settings; report recovery rates, prediction error, and runtime; perform ablations over α, µ, λ.
- Improve scalability: consider greedy merges/splits with lookahead, stochastic local search, submodular relaxations, or graph-based pre-screening to propose candidate groups; kernel approximations (random features, Nyström) to reduce KRR cost.
- Clarify Algorithm 2’s neighborhood, tie-breaking, and stopping rules; add cross-validation strategies for µ and α with justification.
- Minor: fix typos; standardize notation; provide proofs or stronger references for Proposition 3 (covering numbers of direct sums).

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper tackles an important problem with an interesting theoretical angle and offers a potentially useful framework, but the current version has gaps between theory and practice, limited scalability, and insufficient empirical validation. Addressing the concerns above would substantially strengthen the contribution.
