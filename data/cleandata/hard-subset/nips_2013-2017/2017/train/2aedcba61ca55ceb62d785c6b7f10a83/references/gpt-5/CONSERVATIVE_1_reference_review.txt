Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a “group additive” structure: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of predictors into groups. The authors (i) formalize a notion of intrinsic (minimal) group additive structure G*, (ii) propose to identify G jointly with fu via kernel ridge regression on a direct-sum RKHS with an additional structural penalty C(G), and (iii) prove a selection-consistency result under a covering-number-based complexity measure. They provide two algorithms (exhaustive search over partitions and a backward stepwise splitting heuristic) and present small-scale simulations and one real-data example (Boston Housing).

Relation to prior work
The work extends additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), spline ANOVA (Gu), and kernel interaction models (e.g., Kandasamy & Yu’s SALSA) by promoting blocks of variables rather than all pairwise or higher-order interactions. It is related to additive GPs (Duvenaud et al., NIPS’11), sparse additive models (SpAM; Ravikumar et al., NIPS’09), hierarchical/structured sparsity for interactions, and kernel ANOVA decompositions. OKGT (Pan et al.) is directly related. Compared to these, the paper’s main novelty is the “intrinsic group” formalization and the RKHS covering-number penalty to select partitions.

Strengths
- Problem motivation: Learning groups that capture within-group interactions while preserving interpretability and mitigating curse of dimensionality is useful and timely.
- Clear formulation of direct-sum RKHS for a given partition and use of universal kernels to approximate L2 components.
- Theory: The paper gives a selection-consistency claim for recovering G* with a structural penalty; the use of covering numbers to control capacity of direct-sum RKHS is technically reasonable, and the decomposition inequality (sum of logs of covering numbers) is intuitive.
- Algorithms: The paper presents both exhaustive and stepwise procedures; the latter is necessary for scalability.
- Experiments: Simulations demonstrate that the method can recover the true grouping in several settings; the Boston Housing example offers qualitative interpretability.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness
- Identifiability and existence of G*: The paper asserts the existence and uniqueness of a minimal “intrinsic” partition for any square-integrable f, but the conditions are not fully specified. Proposition 1 uses independence to derive strict inclusions; however, Theorem 1’s uniqueness claim appears to hold without assumptions. In practice, uniqueness typically requires orthogonality constraints and/or independence of inputs; otherwise multiple incomparable partitions may be minimal. This needs precise assumptions and proofs.
- Structural penalty derivation: The penalty C(G) is motivated by an upper bound on covering numbers for convolution kernels (Gaussian), but key constants are unknown and the dependence on ε and r is rolled into α, which is then treated as a tunable integer 1–10. This is ad hoc; the theoretical selection consistency depends on unknown constants and a suitable µn schedule, but practical tuning is via validation. A clearer bridge between theory (nonconstructive constants) and practice (CV) is needed.
- Learning-theoretic bounds: The bounds in Proposition 4/Theorem 2 hinge on covering numbers of HG and involve ‖f*‖ and λn; the requirements (e.g., λn n → 0) and separation assumptions ensuring non-amiable structures incur non-vanishing risk are plausible but not verified on realistic distributions. The result feels asymptotic and indirect; no finite-sample oracle inequality or gap condition is provided to calibrate µ with n and p.
- Algorithms: Exhaustive search over partitions scales with Bell numbers and is infeasible for moderate p. The backward stepwise heuristic lacks guarantees and empirically fails on some models. No complexity analysis or pruning strategy (e.g., submodular surrogates, MCMC over partitions, beam search) is provided.
- Kernel choice and hyperparameterization: Universality ensures density in L2 but does not ensure good finite-sample estimation for all components; there is no discussion of kernel bandwidth selection per group, nor of how mis-specified kernels affect structure recovery.

Empirical evaluation
- Baselines: There are no comparisons against established methods that capture interactions or additive structure: SALSA (ICML’16), additive GPs (NIPS’11), SpAM/structured sparsity with interactions (e.g., hierarchical lasso), kernel ANOVA decompositions, or tree/boosting models designed to reveal interaction structure (e.g., GA^2M). The current experiments mainly report “frequency of identifying true groups,” with no predictive performance comparisons or calibration against simpler penalties (e.g., BIC-like penalties on group size/number).
- Scale: Experiments are small (p ≤ 13). There are no large-p settings, no runtime/memory benchmarks, and limited sensitivity analyses to µ, α, λ. The stepwise method’s instability is acknowledged but not addressed.
- Metrics: Focus on recovery frequency without reporting estimation error, test risk, or robustness under correlated covariates. Since identifiability rests on assumptions (often independence), experiments with correlated X are important.

Clarity
- The manuscript has typos, incomplete/broken references (years missing), and ambiguous notation (e.g., α arising from ln(r/ε) but then discretized). The presentation of the partial order, completeness of Ga, and Theorem 1 would benefit from a rigorous, self-contained proof and clear conditions.
- Practical guidance on choosing kernels per group, setting µ and α, and implementing the stepwise procedure is limited.

Originality and significance
- The formal notion of “intrinsic group additive structure” and the use of a capacity-based penalty for partitions are interesting. However, given the absence of strong empirical validation, lack of comparisons with close baselines, and scalability concerns, the current contribution feels premature for NeurIPS.

Pros
- Well-motivated problem and potentially useful modeling bias (groups).
- Theoretical attempt at structure selection consistency.
- Direct-sum RKHS perspective is clean and potentially extensible.

Cons
- Identifiability assumptions and uniqueness of G* insufficiently justified.
- Structural penalty is loosely connected to practice; constants unknown.
- No comparisons to strong, relevant baselines; small-scale experiments only.
- Algorithmic scalability and stability not addressed; heuristic often fails.
- Clarity and references need significant polishing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely state assumptions (e.g., independence or orthogonality of components) under which G* exists and is unique; include proofs or counterexamples.
- Provide finite-sample guarantees (oracle inequality) with explicit, data-dependent penalties (e.g., via local Rademacher complexities) rather than an α proxy.
- Compare against SALSA, additive GPs, SpAM/structured-sparsity interaction models, and kernel ANOVA on predictive accuracy and structure recovery, including correlated covariates and larger p (e.g., p ~ 50–200).
- Develop a scalable search over partitions (e.g., MCMC/SMC over partitions, branch-and-bound with bounds from the penalty, submodular relaxations) with some guarantees.
- Clarify kernel selection, bandwidth tuning, and sensitivity; report runtime.

Recommendation: Reject (leaning)
While the problem is interesting and the theoretical angle is promising, the paper lacks rigorous empirical validation against established baselines, relies on a penalty whose practical instantiation is ad hoc, and does not convincingly address identifiability or scalability. I encourage the authors to strengthen theory/practice alignment and provide comprehensive experiments. Confidence: High.
