Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonparametric regression under group-additive structure. Instead of standard additive models f(X)=∑i fi(Xi) or high-order functional ANOVA with many interaction terms, the authors posit that f admits a partition G={u1,…,ud} of predictors such that f(X)=∑j fuj(Xuj). This mid-ground captures within-group interactions while keeping groups small for interpretability and rate benefits.

The main conceptual contribution is the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure G*, defined as the minimal (w.r.t. refinement) group structure among all “amiable” structures (i.e., partitions under which f lies in the corresponding group-additive function space). The paper argues existence and uniqueness of G* and uses RKHS machinery to estimate f and select G simultaneously.

Methodologically, the authors:
- Use a direct-sum RKHS HG=⊕u∈G Hu, with universal kernels per group to allow dense approximation in L2.
- Propose a structure-penalized estimator minimizing empirical squared loss plus a standard RKHS norm penalty λ||f||^2_HG and an additional penalty µ C(G).
- Derive C(G) from covering-number bounds for RKHSs. Using an inequality for the covering number of sums of function classes, they propose C(G)≈∑u α|u| (after a simplification from a bound of order (ln(r/ε))|u|+1).
- Provide two algorithms: exhaustive search with validation (small p) and a greedy backward stepwise splitter (larger p).

Theoretical results include: (i) for any amiable G, the empirical risk under HG converges (in probability) to the optimal population risk under G*; (ii) for any non-amiable G, a positive gap persists; (iii) combining these with the structural penalty yields selection consistency P(Ĝ=G*)→1 for fixed p (under conditions on λn and µ). Experiments on synthetic data show correct recovery in many cases, and a Boston Housing example provides qualitative interpretations.

Related work: Additive models [Hastie & Tibshirani], smoothing spline ANOVA [Gu], and kernelized higher-order interactions such as ESP kernels (SALSA; Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16) address flexible structures with interactions. Multiple kernel learning with hierarchical/group sparsity (e.g., Hierarchical Kernel Learning; Bach 2009/2011) and sparse additive models (e.g., SpAM; Ravikumar et al. 2009) are closely related in spirit to selecting interaction structures via complexity penalties. OKGT [Pan et al., KDD’15] considered grouped transformations but did not address identifiability or selection.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation of group-additive structure and the need to select a minimal (intrinsic) grouping to balance flexibility and interpretability.
- Use of direct-sum RKHSs with universal kernels is standard and appropriate; the optimization with λ||f||^2_H is well-posed.
- Consistency argument separating amiable vs non-amiable structures is conceptually sound for fixed p: non-amiable models incur irreducible bias; among amiable models, a complexity penalty can favor the minimal structure.
- Provision of both exhaustive and scalable (greedy) procedures, with empirical demonstrations, including real-data visualization of 2D interactions.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality/technical soundness
- Complexity penalty derivation: The covering-number bound used suggests log N(ε,Hu) scales like (ln(r/ε))|u|+1. The paper then sets C(G)=∑u α(ε)|u|+1 and finally simplifies to ∑u α|u| by “absorbing” factors into µ. Dropping the +1 in the exponent cannot be absorbed into a scalar µ; this change alters the relative penalties across different group sizes. This step needs a precise justification or the original form should be retained in theory and practice.
- The inequality for the covering number of the direct sum class (Eq. (5)) is plausible (product of covers ⇒ sum of logs) with ε allocation via triangle inequality, but no proof or conditions are provided (e.g., boundedness, Lipschitz norms). Assumptions should be stated precisely.
- Existence/uniqueness of G*: The claim that the poset of amiable partitions is “complete” and thus admits a unique minimal element is nontrivial. A constructive characterization (e.g., via variable-interaction connectivity) and required assumptions would strengthen this claim. The strict inclusion in Proposition 1 requires independence of Xi; reliance on this assumption (and where it is needed) should be made explicit.
- Consistency is proved for fixed p with |Ga| finite. No results for p growing with n are provided, while the algorithmic section targets larger p. This limits theoretical significance for high-dimensional regimes common at NeurIPS.
- Tuning α: In the final algorithm α is a free hyperparameter (1–10). This decouples the penalty from the kernel-dependent capacity measure that motivated it, and the selected structure can vary qualitatively with α. The conditions under which selection consistency holds with data-driven α (e.g., via validation) are not discussed.

Clarity
- Presentation has several typographical/notation issues (missing years in references, inconsistent subscripts/superscripts, minor grammar). Some definitions (e.g., HG vs HG∗, r-ball, inclusion map) are invoked without explicit assumptions on domains and kernel boundedness.
- Algorithm 2 (backward stepwise) is under-specified: “split j as a new group or add to an existing group” does not enumerate the candidate moves for multi-variable groups; convergence and tie-breaking are not described.

Originality
- Defining intrinsic group additive structure and linking selection to RKHS capacity is a novel synthesis. However, related strands (hierarchical kernel learning, sparse additive/interactions, structured MKL) are not cited or empirically compared, making it difficult to position novelty precisely.

Significance
- Potentially useful framework for discovering low-order interacting groups. However, empirical evaluation is limited: no comparisons against strong baselines (e.g., SALSA/ESP kernels, HKL, SpAM with interactions, pairwise/fANOVA KRR), limited metrics (mainly recovery frequency), and no scalability or runtime analysis. The real-data illustration is qualitative; predictive performance and stability across folds are not reported.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros
- Addresses an important modeling gap between purely additive and full interaction models.
- Concept of intrinsic group structure and fixed-p selection consistency are interesting and potentially useful.
- Method builds on well-established RKHS/KRR foundations; practical algorithms provided.

Cons
- Gaps in the complexity-penalty derivation (notably the exponent simplification) and incomplete assumptions weaken theoretical rigor.
- No high-dimensional theory; independence assumptions appear in key propositions.
- Empirical validation is limited and lacks comparisons to closely related methods.
- Algorithmic details and scalability analysis are insufficiently developed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous derivation and implementation of C(G) consistent with covering-number theory, or justify the simplified form and specify kernel/ε-dependent constants. Clarify the role/selection of α and its impact on preferring finer vs coarser partitions.
- State assumptions clearly (kernel boundedness, data distribution, independence) and delineate where they are used. Strengthen the existence/uniqueness argument for G*.
- Extend theory to regimes with p growing with n or provide conditions under which the greedy search is consistent.
- Compare empirically against SALSA (ESP kernels), hierarchical kernel learning/structured MKL, sparse additive models with interactions, and functional ANOVA KRR, reporting structure recovery, prediction error, and runtime on synthetic and real datasets.
- Flesh out Algorithm 2 (candidate moves, stopping criteria, multiple passes) and consider more robust search (e.g., beam search, agglomerative/divisive clustering guided by validation risk).

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a relevant problem with an appealing formulation and some theoretical guarantees for fixed p. However, issues in the penalty derivation, missing assumptions, and limited empirical validation reduce confidence in the current form. Strengthening theory and experiments and situating the work among related methods would be necessary for a strong NeurIPS contribution.
