This paper tackles the problem of nonparametric regression in high dimensions by proposing a method for identifying a group additive structure within the data. The core idea – finding a partitioning of predictors that allows for additive modeling while capturing some interaction effects – builds on existing work in additive models and functional ANOVA, offering a compromise between their limitations. The authors formulate the problem as an optimization problem involving a kernel method and a novel complexity penalty for the group structure, and provide theoretical guarantees for consistency.

The paper is generally well-written and the motivation for the work is clear. The mathematical formulation is relatively precise, and the attempt to provide theoretical justification (consistency proof) is commendable. However, my overall assessment is skeptical. While the idea is reasonable, the presentation lacks sufficient evidence of practical advantage and relies heavily on theoretical results without demonstrating compelling empirical benefits beyond what simpler methods might achieve.

**Strengths:**

*   **Well-motivated problem:** Addressing the curse of dimensionality in nonparametric regression is a valuable goal. The group additive structure offers a potentially effective way to balance flexibility and interpretability.
*   **Theoretical foundation:** The consistency proof provides some assurance of the method’s validity, although the assumptions required for this proof are not clearly articulated in terms of practical applicability.
*   **Clear formulation:** The optimization problem is clearly defined, and the introduction of a complexity penalty for the group structure is a logical step.
*   **Connection to existing work:** The authors appropriately contextualize their work within the existing literature on additive models, functional ANOVA, and graphical models.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies, while present, feel somewhat limited in scope. The models considered are relatively simple, and the comparison to strong baselines (e.g., standard kernel ridge regression with feature selection, sparse additive models) is insufficient.  The figures presented are not very informative.  A more thorough empirical evaluation is needed to demonstrate the real-world utility of the proposed method. Showing a strong benefit over a simple additive model with feature selection would be a good starting point.
*   **Complexity of the Method:** The proposed method involves several hyperparameters (λ, µ, α) that require tuning. The sensitivity of the results to these parameters is not fully explored, and the practical guidance for selecting appropriate values is limited.  The proposed algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) seem computationally expensive, especially for high-dimensional data.
*   **Novelty of the Complexity Measure:** While the complexity measure based on covering numbers is mathematically sound, its practical relevance is not immediately apparent. It's unclear how this measure compares to other commonly used model complexity measures (e.g., AIC, BIC). It would be helpful to discuss the intuition behind this measure and its potential limitations.
*   **Real Data Application:** The real data application is brief and doesn’t provide strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The interpretation of the identified group structure is somewhat superficial. The housing data is a standard benchmark, and showing superior performance compared to established methods would be crucial.
* **Lack of a Clear Benefit:** The paper doesn’t clearly articulate *why* finding the “intrinsic” group structure is crucial. The argument about interpretability is weak without demonstrating that the discovered groups actually correspond to meaningful relationships in the data.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contributions are solid.
*   The idea of identifying group structure is potentially valuable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is weak and fails to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The method is complex and computationally expensive.
*   The practical relevance of the proposed complexity measure is unclear.
*   The paper lacks a convincing demonstration of the benefits of identifying the “intrinsic” group structure.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a mathematically sound approach to a relevant problem, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and the complexity of the method outweigh its theoretical contributions. The authors need to significantly strengthen the empirical validation, provide a more detailed discussion of the method’s practical limitations, and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing techniques before this work would be suitable for NIPS. The current version feels more like a theoretical exploration than a practical contribution.
