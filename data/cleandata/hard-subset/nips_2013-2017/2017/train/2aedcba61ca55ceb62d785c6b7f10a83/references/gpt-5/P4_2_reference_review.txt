Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a structured hypothesis that the regression function decomposes additively over disjoint groups of predictors (group additive model). The authors (i) formalize a partial order over group structures and define the intrinsic group additive structure as the unique minimal element among all “amiable” (compatible) groupings for a square-integrable target function; (ii) propose estimation in reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs) using a direct-sum RKHS associated with a candidate partition; (iii) introduce a structure-penalized empirical risk minimization that trades off data fit, RKHS norm, and a complexity penalty on the group structure derived from covering number bounds; (iv) prove selection consistency for recovering the intrinsic structure (fixed p) under universal kernels; and (v) provide two algorithms (exhaustive search and a simple backward stepwise heuristic), with simulations and a Boston Housing example.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on additive models and functional ANOVA (e.g., Hastie & Tibshirani; Gu), kernel ridge regression and RKHS theory (Smola & Schölkopf; Steinwart & Christmann), and covering-number-based capacity control (Zhou; Kühn; Bartlett & Mendelson). Compared to higher-order interaction models such as SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16) and ESP-kernel-based KRR, this paper restricts interactions to disjoint groups, yielding a partition rather than general subsets. It is closely related to OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15), which motivated group structure but lacked a formal identification procedure; here the authors define an intrinsic structure and provide a selection-consistent estimator. The work is also related to sparse additive models (e.g., SpAM/Ravikumar et al., NIPS’09) and hierarchical kernel learning/structured MKL (e.g., Bach), which are not cited but are relevant baselines and context.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of group additive structure, including a refinement order and the notion of amiable vs. non-amiable structures, leading to a target “intrinsic” minimal partition.
- Conceptually appealing use of direct-sum RKHSs and a capacity-based structural penalty drawn from covering number bounds, with a transparent inequality for covering numbers of sums translating into an additive penalty over groups.
- Consistency claim for recovering the intrinsic structure (fixed p), separating amiable from non-amiable partitions via bias arguments, and controlling empirical-population risk gaps via capacity bounds.
- The approach targets a middle ground between univariate additive models and fully general interaction models, potentially improving interpretability and sample efficiency when interactions are localized within small groups.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical assumptions and some steps are under-specified or possibly problematic:
  - Uniqueness of the intrinsic structure (Theorem 1) is asserted via completeness of the partially ordered set but conditions ensuring uniqueness are not clearly stated. In particular, uniqueness can be delicate without distributional assumptions (e.g., independence) guaranteeing orthogonality/decomposability; Proposition 1 mentions independence only for strict inclusion but Theorem 1 does not.
  - The transition from the upper bound ln N(ε, IK,r) ≲ (ln(r/ε))p+1 to the penalty µ∑ α|u| appears to drop the +1 in the exponent (from |u|+1 to |u|) by “absorbing constants” into µ. Because the number of groups varies across partitions, this is not a constant shift and can alter relative penalties, potentially affecting selection consistency.
  - The selection consistency result (Theorem 4) requires λn · n → 0 (as written), which is unusual for KRR (typical regimes require λn → 0 with nλn → ∞ or similar). The required scaling of µ and the relationship between the tuning parameter α and the theoretical ε are also not specified.
  - Capacity constants depend on kernel and domain dimension; the proposed penalty ignores kernel-dependent constants, which may matter in finite samples and for incomparable partitions.

- Algorithmic aspects are weak:
  - Exhaustive search scales with the Bell number in p and is impractical beyond small p.
  - The backward stepwise heuristic is unspecified in key steps (how to choose among “split/add” options), has no guarantees, and empirically misses true structures in some models.

- Empirical evaluation is limited:
  - Simulations are modest and lack comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., SALSA, functional ANOVA, SpAM, hierarchical kernel learning).
  - The real-data example gives qualitative patterns but no predictive or structural validation against alternatives or external knowledge.

- Missing or incomplete references to closely related literature (e.g., sparse additive models, structured MKL, hierarchical kernel learning).

Quality
- The high-level methodology is reasonable, and the capacity-based argument for penalizing group structures is principled. However, the theoretical development contains ambiguities (uniqueness of intrinsic structure, penalty exponent change, tuning parameter scaling). Algorithmic proposals are basic and lack analysis. Empirical support is insufficient to establish robustness or comparative advantage.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, with a clear motivation and structure. Some notation choices and jumps (e.g., the α reparameterization) are confusing. Algorithm 2 needs more precise specification. Important assumptions (e.g., independence or conditions for uniqueness) should be stated explicitly.

Originality
- The notion of an intrinsic minimal partition and the specific penalty derived from RKHS covering numbers constitute a novel combination. Grouped additive decomposition itself is known, but formalizing identification and proving selection consistency under RKHS modeling is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
- If strengthened, the approach could be useful for interpretable nonparametric modeling with interactions localized to small disjoint groups. As presented, the limited scalability, theoretical gaps, and lack of empirical comparisons constrain impact.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and target notion (intrinsic group structure).
- Principled structural penalty derived from capacity control.
- Consistency guarantee (fixed p) under universal kernels.
- Potentially interpretable models with localized interactions.

Cons
- Theoretical ambiguities (uniqueness conditions, penalty simplification, tuning regimes).
- Limited, heuristic algorithms without guarantees; scalability concerns.
- Sparse empirical validation and no comparisons to relevant baselines.
- Missing references to closely related NeurIPS/ML literature.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify conditions ensuring existence and uniqueness of the intrinsic structure; specify when independence of predictors is required.
- Revisit the penalty derivation to retain the |u|+1 exponent or explicitly justify any modification that preserves ordering across partitions; specify how µ and α should scale with n.
- Provide representer theorem details and computational formulations for the direct-sum RKHS KRR.
- Develop stronger search procedures (e.g., merge–split, beam search, stochastic local search) with some approximation guarantees, and report computational complexity.
- Extend theory beyond fixed p, or explicitly state the scope; include sample complexity or identifiability conditions.
- Add comprehensive experiments with comparisons to SALSA, functional ANOVA KRR, SpAM, and hierarchical kernel learning; report both structure recovery and prediction metrics on synthetic and real datasets.
- Cite and discuss closely related works (e.g., Ravikumar et al. on SpAM; Bach on hierarchical kernel learning/structured MKL).

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and potentially useful direction, but it requires stronger theoretical clarity, algorithmic development, and empirical validation to meet the NeurIPS bar.
