This paper addresses the problem of nonparametric regression in high dimensions, specifically focusing on identifying an additive group structure within the data. The motivation is clear: standard nonparametric methods suffer in high dimensions, while additive models are efficient but may miss important interactions. The authors propose a method to find a balance by identifying groups of variables that interact, and introduce a complexity penalty to select the optimal grouping. The approach leverages kernel methods and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), and presents theoretical results guaranteeing consistency of the identified group structure.

The core idea – finding a group structure that balances dimensionality reduction with interaction modeling – is reasonable and addresses a relevant challenge. The theoretical foundation, particularly the definition of the “intrinsic group additive structure” and the accompanying consistency proof, represents a substantial effort. The proposed complexity measure, based on covering numbers, is a thoughtful attempt to quantify the structural complexity of the grouping.

However, despite the theoretical rigor, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that raise significant concerns. First, the practical relevance of the proposed method is not convincingly demonstrated. The simulation studies, while extensive, feel somewhat isolated and don't adequately compare against strong, established baselines. The comparison is mainly to the original OKGT method [11], but there’s no comparison to, say, a carefully tuned functional ANOVA model, or even a random forest which would provide a readily available, non-parametric benchmark. The results show improvement over OKGT, but this doesn't necessarily translate to a strong recommendation for acceptance. The performance of the backward stepwise algorithm, particularly in Model 4, is also worrying, raising doubts about the scalability and robustness of the approach.

Second, the formulation and justification of the complexity penalty seem overly complex. While mathematically sound, the reliance on covering numbers feels computationally burdensome and potentially sensitive to kernel choice and parameter settings. The practical tuning of both λ and (µ, α) is not discussed in sufficient detail. The use of a validation set is mentioned, but the sensitivity of the results to the size and representativeness of this validation set is not analyzed. 

Third, the connection to graphical models, though acknowledged, is glossed over. The authors state their approach directly targets group additive structures rather than conditional dependence, but a more thorough discussion of the relationship – and the potential benefits of incorporating insights from graphical model selection – would be valuable.

Fourth, the clarity suffers from a large amount of mathematical notation and a somewhat dense writing style. While mathematical precision is appreciated, streamlining the presentation and providing more intuitive explanations of key concepts would improve accessibility. For instance, the motivation for choosing covering numbers as the complexity measure could be further elaborated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical foundation is strong and the consistency proof is a significant achievement.
*   The concept of identifying an intrinsic group additive structure is a novel contribution.
*   The method offers a potential middle ground between purely additive models and complex interaction models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical relevance is not convincingly demonstrated due to the lack of comparison to established baselines.
*   The complexity penalty is computationally burdensome and its practical tuning is unclear.
*   The connection to related work (e.g., graphical models) is not sufficiently explored.
*   The clarity of the presentation could be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and theoretically rigorous contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. However, the lack of compelling empirical evidence, the computational complexity of the proposed method, and the relatively weak comparison to existing techniques significantly diminish its practical impact. While the theoretical results are interesting, I am not convinced that this work represents a significant advance over existing approaches that would warrant acceptance to NIPS at this time. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the concerns regarding empirical validation, computational scalability, and clarity before resubmitting. Specifically, a comprehensive comparison against relevant baselines (random forests, functional ANOVA) and a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed method are crucial.
