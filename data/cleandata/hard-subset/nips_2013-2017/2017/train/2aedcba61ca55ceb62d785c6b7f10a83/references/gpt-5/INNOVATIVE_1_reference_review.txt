Summary
This paper introduces Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI) for kernel nonparametric regression. The core idea is to model f(X) as a sum of unknown nonparametric components over groups of variables rather than individual variables (additive GAM) or all pairs/higher-order interactions (functional ANOVA, ESP/SALSA). The authors formalize the notion that a function may admit multiple “amiable” group additive structures and define a unique minimal one—the intrinsic group additive structure—under a refinement partial order on partitions. They then propose to jointly identify the group structure and estimate the component functions via kernel regression in a direct-sum RKHS, adding a penalty on the group structure derived from covering-number bounds for RKHSs. Concretely, they define a complexity C(G) that scales with group sizes via upper bounds ln N(ε, IK) ≲ (ln(r/ε))dim+1, leading to a penalty of the form μ∑u∈G α|u| (α is a hyperparameter). They provide selection consistency results showing that, with proper tuning and universal kernels, the estimator recovers the intrinsic structure with probability → 1. Two algorithms are offered: exhaustive search (small p) and a backward stepwise heuristic (large p). Simulations and a Boston Housing case study demonstrate feasibility.

Relation to prior work
- Extends additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani) by allowing interactions within learned groups, but avoids the combinatorial explosion of fully general functional ANOVA (Gu) and SALSA/ESP kernels (Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16).
- Related to OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15) but addresses identifiability and provides a principled structure-selection mechanism with consistency.
- Touches on ideas from sparse additive models (e.g., SpAM), hierarchical kernel learning, and structure learning in graphical models; the authors position their target as simpler than full CD structure.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: formalization of an intrinsic group additive structure (minimal partition) is clean and useful; it bridges interpretability, interaction discovery, and nonparametric flexibility.
- A principled structural penalty tied to RKHS capacity via covering numbers is an original angle, giving a theoretically motivated surrogate for “group complexity” beyond ad-hoc group size penalties.
- Nontrivial theory: consistency for structure selection, with separation of amiable vs. non-amiable structures and uniform risk bounds over amiable structures.
- Practical relevance: when interactions are localized to small groups, this can dramatically reduce complexity compared to general ANOVA/ESP models while retaining interpretability.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical evaluation is limited. There are no comparisons against strong baselines such as SALSA/ESP kernels, sparse additive/interaction models (SpAM and successors), OKGT, or hierarchical kernel learning. The Boston example is illustrative but not compelling as evidence.
- Scalability is a major gap. Exhaustive search is infeasible for moderate p, and the backward stepwise heuristic is very simple and often fails (as seen in Model 4). No complexity analysis, pruning strategies, submodular relaxations, or stochastic search over partitions are provided.
- The penalty instantiation is somewhat ad hoc in practice. The derivation moves from (ln(r/ε))|u|+1 to α|u|, folding an exponent shift into μ by treating α and μ as tunable. This weakens the theoretical tie between the covering-number bound and the actual penalty used in optimization; more discussion is needed on the conditions under which the chosen α, μ preserve the intended ordering and the selection-consistency proof.
- Assumptions and scope are not always clear. For instance:
  - Proposition 1 invokes independence for strict inclusion of function spaces; how essential is this for identifiability and selection consistency?
  - Universality of kernels is used to argue unbiasedness, but practical finite-sample behavior depends on λ, kernel bandwidths, and noise—these sensitivities aren’t analyzed.
  - Theorems assume a finite set of amiable structures; as p grows, the space of partitions grows super-exponentially, and the bounds become vacuous without stronger structural assumptions.
- Clarity and completeness: proofs are deferred to the supplement; important constants/conditions (e.g., dependence on kernel and distribution) are opaque. The exposition around the penalty and capacity bounds could be tightened.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide empirical comparisons to SALSA (ESP kernels), OKGT, and sparse additive/interaction baselines on your synthetic settings, and report structure-recovery and prediction metrics.
- Justify the practical penalty choice μ∑ α|u| more carefully. Can you give a data-dependent α (e.g., via Rademacher/localized complexities) or show that for a range of α the ordering of amiable structures matches the covering-number ordering with high probability?
- Improve scalability: consider greedy-with-lookahead, MCMC over partitions, split-merge proposals, submodular relaxations, or differentiable relaxations with group-sparsity/overlapping penalties to navigate the partition space more robustly.
- Clarify assumptions (independence, universality, kernel choices) required for Theorem 4, and discuss how violations affect identifiability.
- Add ablations on λ, α, μ; show sensitivity and a principled tuning strategy (e.g., nested CV).
- Discuss computational costs and memory for KRR per group and overall, and whether Nyström/CG/multi-kernel tricks can help.

Pros
- New and compelling problem formulation (intrinsic group additive structure) with identifiability.
- Theoretically principled structural penalty connecting capacity control and structure learning.
- Consistency guarantees for structure recovery.
- Interpretability and potential practical impact when interactions are localized.

Cons
- Weak experimental validation and lack of comparisons.
- Limited scalability; heuristic search often fails.
- Practical penalty deviates from the theoretical form; tuning is opaque.
- Some assumptions and proofs critical to practice are not fully transparent in the main text.

Recommendation
Weak accept (score: 6.5/10). The paper presents a fresh and ambitious direction—identifying groupwise interactions in a nonparametric kernel setting with a capacity-based penalty—and backs it with meaningful theory. Despite shortcomings in experiments and scalability, the conceptual contribution and the linkage to RKHS capacity are sufficiently novel to merit presentation at NeurIPS, provided the authors strengthen empirical comparisons and clarify the penalty/theory connection.

Confidence: 3/5
