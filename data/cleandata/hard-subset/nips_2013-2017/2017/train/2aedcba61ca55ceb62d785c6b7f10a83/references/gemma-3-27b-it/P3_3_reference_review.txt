## NIPS 2017 Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of nonparametric regression in high-dimensional spaces. Recognizing the limitations of both fully nonparametric methods (curse of dimensionality) and traditional functional ANOVA models (exponential growth in parameters), the authors propose a group additive model where interactions are implicitly captured by grouping interacting variables. The core contribution lies in developing a method to *identify* the intrinsic group additive structure directly from data, alongside estimating the nonparametric functions within each group.  They formulate this as a penalized kernel ridge regression problem, introducing a novel complexity measure for group structures based on covering numbers of RKHSs.  Theoretical consistency guarantees are provided, and empirical validation demonstrates the efficacy of their approach on simulated and real-world datasets.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a solid foundation of prior work in nonparametric regression, additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 2009), functional ANOVA (Gu, 2002), and kernel methods (Smola & Schölkopf, 2004). They appropriately position their work as a generalization of the simple additive model (Hastie & Tibshirani) and a more parsimonious alternative to functional ANOVA and higher-order interaction models (Kandasamy & Yu, 2016).  The connection to graphical models (Koller & Friedman, 2009) is acknowledged, but the authors correctly highlight the distinction – GASI directly targets group structure, whereas graphical models focus on conditional dependencies. The reference to Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) (Pan et al., 2011) is crucial, as this work extends it by addressing the non-identifiability and structure identification problems that were left open in the original OKGT paper.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Identifying the group structure *directly* from data is a significant contribution. Existing methods typically require the grouping to be pre-specified.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The consistency proof is a strong asset.  Deriving a tractable complexity measure for group structures is particularly impressive, and provides a clear justification for the penalty term.
* **Practicality:** The proposed algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise approach) offer flexibility for different problem sizes.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The simulation studies demonstrate the ability to recover the true structure across various scenarios. The real-data application on the Boston Housing dataset provides a compelling example.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Literature:** The authors meticulously position their work relative to established approaches.
* **Well-written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written, and the mathematical notation is clear.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm will be intractable for even moderately high-dimensional problems.  The stepwise algorithm offers a solution, but its greedy nature raises concerns about finding the globally optimal structure.  A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of both algorithms would be beneficial.
* **Choice of RKHS Capacity Measure:** While the covering number based complexity measure is theoretically justified, its practical computation can be challenging, and the sensitivity of the results to the choice of α (controlling the covering number) isn’t fully explored.  Are there practical heuristics for choosing a good value of α?
* **Parameter Tuning:** The paper mentions using cross-validation to tune λ, but doesn't delve into the details of how µ and α were selected, beyond stating they used a grid search.  A more rigorous discussion of parameter tuning strategies would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Comparison to Alternatives:** While the authors compare to the original OKGT, a more direct comparison with existing variable selection techniques for functional ANOVA models (e.g., sparse functional ANOVA) would be valuable.  Do they outperform these methods in terms of prediction accuracy and/or interpretability?
* **Real Data Interpretation:**  While the real data results are presented, a more in-depth discussion of the *meaning* of the identified groups (in the context of the housing market) would further enhance the impact of the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
* The proposed method is novel and theoretically well-founded.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm is a limitation.
* The practical implications of choosing the complexity measure parameter α require further investigation.
* A more comprehensive comparison to alternative methods is needed.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The core idea of learning the group structure is innovative, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are encouraging. While the computational concerns and parameter tuning details could be further addressed, these are not fatal flaws.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  The authors should consider:

* Providing a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithms.
* Exploring heuristics for choosing a good value for the parameter α in the complexity measure.
* Adding a more comprehensive comparison to alternative variable selection methods for functional ANOVA models.
* Expanding the interpretation of the identified groups in the real data example.



**Confidence:** 8/10
