

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses an important gap in nonparametric regression literature by proposing a method to identify the intrinsic group additive structure for high-dimensional regression problems. The authors build upon previous work on additive models (which assume f(X) = f1(X1) + ... + fp(Xp)) and functional ANOVA models (which allow for interactions between variables), introducing a group additive model where variables are partitioned into groups and the function is additive across these groups (f(X) = fu1(Xu1) + ... + fud(Xud)). The key contribution is identifying the "intrinsic" group additive structure (the minimal group structure that correctly represents the function), which the authors rigorously define and prove exists and is unique.

The paper makes several technical contributions: (1) it rigorously defines the intrinsic group additive structure and proves its existence; (2) it develops a tractable complexity measure for group structures based on RKHS covering numbers; (3) it proposes a structure-penalized kernel method for simultaneous structure identification and function estimation; (4) it proves consistency of the proposed method under appropriate conditions; and (5) it develops two algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) for practical implementation.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations with well-constructed proofs, including Theorem 1 establishing the existence and uniqueness of the intrinsic group additive structure, and Theorems 2-4 establishing consistency of the proposed method. The authors carefully distinguish their work from related approaches, particularly graphical models, noting that their method directly targets the group additive structure rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and well-written. The mathematical definitions and theorems are clearly stated, and the connection between the theoretical framework and the practical algorithms is well-maintained. The experimental section includes both simulation studies (with five different models) and a real data application (Boston Housing), providing empirical support for the theoretical claims.

The main strengths of the paper are its rigorous theoretical framework, clear distinction from related work, and the practical algorithms developed for implementation. The paper successfully addresses a significant limitation in previous group additive model approaches (non-uniqueness of group structures) and provides a systematic method for identifying the intrinsic structure.

The paper has several minor weaknesses. The discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method is limited, especially for large p (though the authors do propose a stepwise algorithm for this scenario). The justification for choosing covering numbers as the complexity measure over other capacity measures (like VC dimension or Rademacher complexity) could be strengthened. The real data application is somewhat limited to a single dataset (Boston Housing), and the interpretation of the results could be more detailed. Additionally, the paper could better discuss the sensitivity of the method to kernel choice and tuning parameters.

Regarding novelty, the paper makes a clear and significant contribution by introducing the concept of intrinsic group additive structure and developing a method to identify it. While group additive models have been studied previously (e.g., OKGT method), the authors correctly identify that previous work did not address the non-uniqueness of group structures or provide a systematic identification method. The comparison with related work (particularly graphical models) is thorough and well-justified.

The significance of the work is strong. High-dimensional nonparametric regression is a fundamental problem in statistics and machine learning, and the proposed method offers a principled approach to balance model flexibility with interpretability. The ability to identify meaningful variable groupings could have practical value in applications where understanding variable interactions is important, such as in medical or social sciences. The real data application on Boston Housing demonstrates practical utility, and the method could be applied to a wide range of high-dimensional regression problems.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses core statistical learning theory topics with a novel mathematical framework and practical algorithms. The quality of the theoretical development is strong, with careful attention to the assumptions and conditions required for the consistency results.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on nonparametric regression by introducing the concept of intrinsic group additive structure and developing a theoretically sound method to identify it. The theoretical framework is rigorous, the experimental results support the theoretical claims, and the method has clear potential for practical application in high-dimensional regression problems.

Pro: Rigorous theoretical framework with clear definitions and proofs; addresses a significant gap in the literature; well-organized and clearly written; strong empirical validation; meaningful contribution to the field of nonparametric regression.

Con: Limited discussion of computational complexity for large p; justification for choice of covering numbers as complexity measure could be strengthened; real data application limited to one dataset; sensitivity to kernel choice and tuning parameters not thoroughly discussed.

The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field that should be of interest to the NIPS community. The theoretical results are sound, the method is well-motivated, and the empirical results support the claims. I recommend acceptance.
