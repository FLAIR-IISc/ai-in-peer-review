This paper addresses the problem of group additive structure identification for kernel nonparametric regression, a crucial task for improving interpretability and mitigating the curse of dimensionality in high-dimensional settings. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches like functional ANOVA and higher-order interaction models, which can suffer from exponential growth in the number of parameters. Their proposed group additive model offers a compelling middle ground, and the introduction of a complexity measure for group structures, along with a corresponding minimization problem (3), is a significant contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of GASI and its relevance to existing nonparametric regression techniques. The motivation for seeking group structures – balancing interpretability, efficiency, and interaction modeling – is well-explained.
* **Theoretical Contributions:** Defining the “intrinsic group additive structure” is a valuable step toward addressing the non-identifiability issue. The derivation of a tractable capacity measure for the direct sum RKHS is a key technical achievement, providing a foundation for the structural penalty. The consistency theorem (Theorem 4) is a strong theoretical result.
* **Methodology:** The proposed penalized kernel method (3) appears well-motivated and offers a principled approach to simultaneously identifying the group structure and estimating the nonparametric functions. The provision of both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms caters to different dimensionality scenarios.
* **Experimental Validation:** The simulation studies demonstrate the method's ability to recover true group structures under various conditions. Applying the method to a real dataset (Boston Housing) and visualizing the resulting functions provides practical insight.
* **Connections to Related Work:**  The discussion relating the group additive model to graphical models provides valuable context and highlights the differences in their modeling approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Theory:** While the theoretical results are strong, the presentation is quite dense and may be difficult for readers not already familiar with RKHS theory and covering numbers to fully grasp.  More intuitive explanations or intermediate steps could improve accessibility.  The placement of proofs in the supplementary material is standard, but a more concise summary of the key ideas within the main text would be beneficial.
* **Algorithm Scalability:** The exhaustive search algorithm (Algorithm 1) is computationally expensive for high-dimensional problems. The stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is presented as a solution, but the simulation results suggest its performance is not consistently robust (specifically Model 4).  Further investigation into more scalable algorithms is needed.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method appears sensitive to the choice of the tuning parameters µ and α. The paper mentions using a grid search and cross-validation, but a more detailed discussion of how to select these parameters in practice would be helpful.  The connection between the regularization parameter λ and the proposed complexity penalty needs clarification.
* **Limited Real-Data Analysis:** While the Boston Housing example is a good starting point, a more comprehensive evaluation on multiple real-world datasets with varying characteristics would strengthen the claims of practical applicability.
* **Clarity of Notation:** Throughout the paper, the notation could be more consistent and clear. For example, the use of subscripts for different objects (e.g., f̂λ,G, f∗G∗) can sometimes be confusing.

**Originality:**

The paper presents a novel approach to GASI by introducing a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers. While the core idea of grouping variables to address dimensionality is not entirely new (see [11]), the theoretical framework and the proposed penalty term are original and represent a significant advancement.  The rigorous definition of the “intrinsic group additive structure” is also a valuable contribution.

**Significance:**

The work has the potential to be impactful in the field of nonparametric regression. By providing a principled method for identifying group additive structures, the authors address a critical limitation of traditional nonparametric approaches.  The proposed method could be applied to a wide range of problems where interpretability and efficiency are paramount.  The theoretical results provide a solid foundation for future research in this area.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation with a consistency guarantee.
* Novel approach to GASI with a well-motivated complexity measure.
* Promising experimental results demonstrating the method's effectiveness.
* Addresses a significant problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Algorithm scalability is a concern for very high-dimensional problems.
* Parameter tuning can be challenging.
* The theoretical presentation is dense and may be difficult for some readers to follow.
* The real-data analysis is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding algorithmic scalability and the complexity of the theoretical presentation, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance, contingent on addressing the reviewer's concerns regarding scalability, parameter tuning, and clarity in a revised version. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider:

1.  Investigating more scalable algorithms for GASI.
2.  Providing more guidance on parameter selection (µ, α, λ).
3.  Simplifying the theoretical presentation and adding more intuitive explanations.
4.  Evaluating the method on a more diverse set of real-world datasets.
5.  Improving the consistency of notation throughout the paper.
