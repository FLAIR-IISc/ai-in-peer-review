Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies nonparametric regression under a group additive assumption: the regression function f(X) admits a partition of variables G = {u1,…,ud} such that f(X) = Σj fuj(Xuj). The authors (i) formalize identifiability by introducing a partial order over group structures and defining an “intrinsic group additive structure” G∗ as the unique minimal (most refined) partition among all that can represent f; (ii) propose to identify G∗ jointly with f by solving a kernel ridge regression in a direct-sum RKHS HG, penalized by a complexity term C(G); (iii) derive C(G) from covering-number bounds of RKHS balls, leading to a penalty proportional to Σj α|uj| (with α tuned); (iv) prove a structure selection consistency result under fixed p; and (v) provide an exhaustive-search and a backward stepwise algorithm, evaluated on simulations and Boston Housing.

The work connects to additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), functional ANOVA (Gu), and the ESP-kernel SALSA model for higher-order interactions (Kandasamy & Yu), as well as the OKGT approach (Pan et al., KDD’15) which motivated groups. It also relates conceptually to structural risk minimization and multiple kernel/additive kernel learning, where complexity/regularization selects among additive decompositions. The authors briefly discuss ties to graphical models but argue their target (group additive structure) is simpler than conditional independence graphs.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and a useful notion of intrinsic group structure. The minimality concept under the refinement partial order is natural and addresses non-uniqueness in prior group-additive formulations (e.g., OKGT).
- RKHS-based framework with universal kernels allows representing a rich class of functions while tying identifiability to function space capacity.
- The penalty derived from covering number upper bounds provides a principled capacity-based regularizer that favors simpler partitions among amiable structures.
- Consistency guarantees for structure selection are provided (for fixed p) with a reasonable proof strategy that separates amiable vs non-amiable structures and leverages covering numbers.
- Practical algorithms (exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic) and initial empirical demonstrations.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation

Quality (technical soundness, empirical support)
- Theory is asymptotic in n for fixed p. There is no analysis of high-dimensional regimes (p growing with n), which is the setting where structure discovery is most compelling. The number of partitions grows super-exponentially (Bell numbers), and selection guarantees or computational strategies under p ≫ n are not addressed theoretically.
- The derivation of the penalty: C(G) transitions from Σ α(ε)|uj|+1 to Σ α|uj| by absorbing a factor into μ. While tuning (μ, α) on validation makes this practically harmless, the mapping from the covering-number parameters (r, ε, kernel bandwidth) to α is not operationally clarified; the role of kernel bandwidths per group and their effect on C(G) are not discussed. Data-dependent complexity measures (e.g., Rademacher/local complexities) might better align with practice.
- Assumptions behind identifiability: Proposition 1 notes strict inclusion of L2 spaces when Xj are independent. For dependent covariates, the relationship between partitions and function spaces is weaker, and identifiability of G∗ may be problematic. The consistency theorem does not state distributional assumptions on X beyond those needed for covering numbers and universality; clearer conditions are needed.
- Algorithms: Exhaustive search is intractable for moderate p. The backward stepwise heuristic has no guarantees and can miss true structures (as seen in Model 4). There is no complexity analysis or guidance on when the heuristic succeeds.
- Empirics are limited: small synthetic suite, no comparisons to strong baselines such as SALSA (ESP kernels), functional ANOVA with selection, hierarchical or sparse multiple kernel learning, HSIC-Lasso/interaction screening, or model-based interaction selection (hierNet). The single real dataset (Boston Housing) is too small to demonstrate robustness; predictive performance and stability vs baselines are not reported.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, but notation is occasionally inconsistent (e.g., mixing HG, HK,G, Rg, R) and several definitions/results are deferred to the supplement. The practical meaning of α and its tuning is under-explained. Kernel choices and hyperparameters (bandwidths) per group are not specified in experiments.
- References have missing years and incomplete bibliographic details; related MKL/additive kernel literature is not cited (e.g., Bach 2008 on hierarchical kernel learning; Koltchinskii & Yuan on MKL sparsity), which would help position the contribution.

Originality
- The intrinsic group additive structure notion and its use with a capacity-based penalty for structure identification are novel to my knowledge. The use of direct-sum RKHSs is standard, but tying covering numbers to a partition penalty is interesting. However, connections to adjacent fields (hierarchical kernel learning/MKL, sparse additive kernels, GP kernel structure discovery) should be more fully explored and cited.

Significance
- Discovering interaction groups can substantially improve interpretability and efficiency in nonparametric regression. If the approach scaled and was empirically validated against baselines, it could be impactful. As is, the limited experiments and lack of high-dimensional guarantees make it hard to assess practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean formalization of intrinsic group structures.
- A principled, theoretically supported approach using RKHS capacity control.
- Initial consistency result and practical algorithms.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation and no baseline comparisons.
- Scalability concerns; no guarantees or analysis for the heuristic search in larger p.
- Unclear operationalization of the complexity penalty and kernel hyperparameters.
- Assumptions for identifiability under dependence are not clearly spelled out.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines and metrics: compare structure recovery and prediction against SALSA, functional ANOVA with selection, sparse MKL/hierarchical kernel learning, HSIC-based screening, and interaction-focused methods (e.g., hierNet).
- Clarify and justify the penalty: specify how α relates to ε, kernel bandwidths, and group dimensionality; consider a simpler, monotone penalty c(|u|) with theoretical support or a data-dependent complexity proxy (e.g., empirical Rademacher complexities).
- Expand theory: provide assumptions on X (independence vs dependence), discuss identifiability under dependence, and explore high-dimensional regimes or screening steps with guarantees.
- Improve algorithms: consider merge-split search, stochastic/MCMC over partitions, or submodular relaxations to escape greedy traps; analyze computational complexity.
- Report implementation details: kernel choices and bandwidth selection per group, λ tuning, and sensitivity analyses. Provide code for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Reject for NeurIPS this year. The paper presents a promising idea with a clean formalization and an initial consistency result, but the empirical validation is too limited, scalability is unclear, and important connections and comparisons to existing methods are missing. With stronger experiments, clearer assumptions, and improved algorithms, this work could be competitive in a future submission. Reviewer confidence: moderate.
