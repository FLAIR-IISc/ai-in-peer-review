## NIPS 2017 Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying a group additive structure in nonparametric regression, aiming to bridge the gap between fully additive models (which may miss interactions) and higher-order interaction models (which can suffer from dimensionality issues). The authors propose a RKHS-based approach with a novel complexity penalty for group structures, and provide theoretical guarantees for consistent identification of the “intrinsic” group structure – the minimal structure sufficient to represent the underlying function. They also present algorithms for identifying this structure, including an exhaustive search and a stepwise approach, and demonstrate performance on both simulated and real-world data.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon the established field of additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 2009) and functional ANOVA (Gu, 1998). It acknowledges the limitations of higher-order interaction models (Kandasamy & Yu, 2016) and draws inspiration from Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) (Pan et al., 2011), but crucially addresses the critical issues of non-identifiability and lack of systematic identification methods. The use of RKHS and covering numbers for complexity control is well-grounded in learning theory (Smola & Schölkopf, 2004; Vapnik, 1998; Zhou, 2008) and statistical learning (Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Finding a balance between model flexibility and interpretability in high-dimensional nonparametric regression is a significant challenge. This paper offers a principled approach to achieving this balance.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The consistency result for identifying the intrinsic group structure is a major strength. The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
* **Novel Complexity Measure:** The use of covering numbers to quantify the complexity of group additive structures is a novel and insightful contribution.
* **Practical Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) offer practical solutions for identifying the group structure.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on both simulated data and a real-world dataset (Boston Housing) demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Clear Connection to Related Fields:** The paper clearly articulates the connection to graphical models, positioning the work within a broader context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm is computationally expensive and impractical for high-dimensional problems. While the stepwise approach addresses this, its performance is not consistently superior in the simulations.  More efficient algorithms for high dimensions would be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The performance appears sensitive to the tuning parameters μ and α. While the authors provide guidance for parameter selection, more robust methods for automatically determining these values would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Real-World Data:** The real-world application is limited to a single dataset.  Demonstrating performance on a wider range of datasets with different characteristics would strengthen the conclusions.
* **RKHS Assumption:** The method relies on the choice of appropriate kernels. The impact of different kernel choices on the performance of the algorithm is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on specific assumptions about the data and the kernel functions. Discussing the robustness of the method to violations of these assumptions would be helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The authors identify an important problem, propose a novel and theoretically sound solution, and demonstrate its effectiveness through simulations and a real-world application. While the computational cost and parameter sensitivity are potential limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation with consistency guarantees.
* Novel and insightful complexity measure for group structures.
* Addresses an important and practical problem in high-dimensional regression.
* Clear presentation and well-written manuscript.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational limitations of the exhaustive search algorithm.
* Parameter sensitivity requires careful tuning.
* Limited scope of real-world data analysis.




**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should:

*  Discuss the computational complexity of the algorithms more thoroughly and explore strategies for improving the efficiency of the stepwise approach.
*  Investigate methods for automatically selecting the tuning parameters μ and α.
*  Consider applying the method to a wider range of real-world datasets.
*  Provide a more in-depth discussion of the impact of kernel choice on the performance of the algorithm.
* Briefly address the robustness of the theoretical results to violations of the underlying assumptions.
