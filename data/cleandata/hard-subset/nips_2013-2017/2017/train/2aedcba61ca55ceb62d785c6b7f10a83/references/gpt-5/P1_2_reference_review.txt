Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a group additive model: f(X) is assumed to decompose as a sum of functions over disjoint groups of variables. The authors (i) formalize a partial order on group structures (refinement), (ii) define amiable group structures (those for which f can be represented), and (iii) prove the existence and uniqueness of a minimal element among all amiable group structures, called the intrinsic group additive structure G*. They then propose to identify G* while estimating f by minimizing squared error with an RKHS norm penalty plus a structural penalty on groupings derived from covering number bounds of direct-sum RKHSs. Concretely, for a given partition G with groups u, they solve (kernel ridge regression over HG) plus a penalty µ sum_j α^{|u_j|} (based on an upper bound scaling like sum_j α^{|u_j|+1}). They prove selection consistency of the exhaustive penalized estimator (under fixed p, universal kernels, and tuning choices). Two search strategies are given: exhaustive search with validation and a backward stepwise heuristic. Simulations on five small synthetic models show that the method can often recover the true grouping; a Boston Housing example yields a plausible partition and 2D response visualizations.

Relation to prior work: The paper positions itself between additive models and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models by grouping variables. It relates to OKGT (Pan et al.), SALSA/ESP kernel for bounded-order interactions, and to graphical models (block structure), but targets predictive decomposition rather than conditional independence. The use of RKHS direct sums is standard; the structural penalty constructed from covering numbers is the distinctive element.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of the “intrinsic” group additive structure and its partial order; the minimal element in the lattice of partitions is conceptually useful.
- RKHS-based estimation framework with universal kernels provides a principled route to consistency.
- Derivation of a complexity measure for direct-sum RKHSs using covering-number bounds; aggregation bound for sums is sensible.
- Non-asymptotic bounds that separate amiable from non-amiable structures and a selection consistency theorem (for the exhaustive search).
- Practical algorithms (exhaustive and a simple backward stepwise heuristic), and interpretable real-data visualization.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- The structural penalty is only loosely tied to the capacity bounds. The derivation moves from ln N(ε) = O(sum α^{|u|+1}) to a penalty µ sum α^{|u|} by absorbing constants, r, ε into α and µ. In practice, α is treated as an integer tuning parameter in [1,10]. This weakens the theoretical motivation and leaves the penalty function largely heuristic; it also ignores kernel-specific constants and smoothness.
- Theory assumes fixed p (finite number of partitions) and does not cover the high-dimensional regime where p grows with n. Yet the paper emphasizes “large p” and proposes a stepwise algorithm without guarantees.
- The independence assumption is used (Proposition 1 strict inclusion) to justify strict ordering of function spaces. Many real problems involve correlated covariates; it is unclear whether existence/uniqueness of G* or selection consistency survive under strong dependence.
- Algorithmic details are sparse: how to solve KRR on direct sums (representer theorem, kernel construction per group, computational cost), tuning of λ, µ, α, and search complexity. The stepwise procedure is under-specified (how to choose among multiple candidate splits/merges, stopping rules, tie-breaking), and has no theoretical analysis.
- Empirical evaluation is thin: no comparisons to baselines such as SALSA/ESP-kernel KRR, (sparse) additive models with pairwise interactions and hierarchy, SpAM and its variants, or HSIC-based interaction discovery. Without such comparisons, it is hard to assess practical gains in recovery or prediction.
- Simulations are small-scale and mostly with independent covariates; one model (M4) is admitted to be poorly handled by the stepwise search. No runtime, scalability, or sensitivity analyses (to λ, µ, α) are provided.
- Real-data results are descriptive; there is no predictive evaluation or comparison.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but there are notational inconsistencies (e.g., subscript g on risks, use of “α(ε)” vs α as a tuning constant). Explicitly restating the assumptions before each theorem (e.g., universality, independence, fixed p, choice of λ_n) would help.
- The transition from covering-number bounds to the final penalty should be tightened: clarify r, ε, and constants, and justify the simplification.
- Some references have missing years; a few typographical issues remain.

Originality
- The notion of an intrinsic group additive structure and its formal lattice-based characterization appears novel and valuable.
- Using RKHS covering-number bounds to induce a model-structure penalty is interesting, though reminiscent of capacity-based model selection/MDL ideas. The specific penalty depending only on group sizes is simple but arguably not strongly novel.

Significance
- If reliably identified, G* offers interpretable structure with manageable nonparametric estimation. However, the current empirical evidence and scalability story are limited. Absent stronger experiments and comparisons, it is hard to gauge impact beyond conceptual interest.

Questions for the authors
- Under what dependence conditions on X does Theorem 1 (existence/uniqueness of G*) hold? Does uniqueness fail with collinearity?
- How sensitive is recovery to kernel choice and λ? Are Gaussian kernels used for all groups? Are length-scales tuned per group?
- Can the structural penalty be derived more directly as a function of |u|, kernel bandwidth, and n (e.g., a BIC-like term), avoiding the ad hoc α?
- What is the computational complexity of Algorithm 2 per iteration, and how does it scale to p ~ 50–100?

Suggestions
- Add baselines (SALSA/ESP-KRR; sparse additive with pairwise interactions and hierarchy; SpAM) for both recovery and prediction.
- Include correlated-covariate simulations and larger p; report runtime and sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Strengthen the link between theory and penalty: either a principled data-dependent penalty (e.g., local Rademacher complexities) or a BIC/MDL-style derivation.
- Provide theory (even partial) for the stepwise procedure (e.g., consistency under separability or strong-signal conditions).

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and elegant notion of intrinsic grouping.
- Sound RKHS framework; selection consistency for exhaustive search.
- Interpretable modeling goal; promising qualitative real-data insights.

Cons
- Penalty derivation is loose; α is ad hoc.
- No theoretical or empirical guarantees for the proposed scalable (stepwise) algorithm.
- Limited experiments with no comparisons; small scale.
- Assumptions (fixed p, independence) restrict applicability.

Overall rating: 5 (weak reject)
Confidence: 4/5
