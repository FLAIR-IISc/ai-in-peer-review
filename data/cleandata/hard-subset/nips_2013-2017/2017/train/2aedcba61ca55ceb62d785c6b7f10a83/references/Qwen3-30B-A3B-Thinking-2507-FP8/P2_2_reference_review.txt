

# Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

## Summary
This paper addresses a key limitation in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: the trade-off between model flexibility (to capture interactions) and computational feasibility. The authors propose a "group additive structure" model, where predictors are grouped, and the response depends on the groups rather than individual predictors. They rigorously define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group structure that can represent the true function, and propose a kernel-based method with a structural penalty to identify this structure. The paper makes three main contributions: (1) a formal definition of intrinsic group additive structures, (2) a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers, and (3) theoretical consistency results for their identification method. The approach sits between the traditional additive model and functional ANOVA models, offering a middle ground for capturing interactions while maintaining estimation efficiency.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical foundation for the group additive structure, with clear definitions of key concepts (amiable structures, intrinsic structure) and a well-motivated complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers. The theoretical analysis is sound, with Theorem 4 proving the consistency of their method under appropriate conditions. The empirical evaluation includes both synthetic data (5 models) and a real-world application (Boston Housing data), with appropriate statistical validation (10-fold CV). The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., Model 1 performance issues, computational complexity of exhaustive search), demonstrating honesty about the method's strengths and weaknesses.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem within the literature on additive models and functional ANOVA. The mathematical formulations are precise and consistent, with appropriate references to supporting literature (e.g., RKHS theory, covering numbers). The algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) are described in sufficient detail for implementation. The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the intrinsic structure is the "best" choice (beyond just theoretical minimality), and a clearer visualization of how the group structures relate to the true underlying function in the simulations. The connection to graphical models (Section 1) is well-explained but could be more explicitly contrasted with their approach.

## Originality
The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on nonparametric regression. While group additive structures were previously considered in [11], this paper addresses two critical gaps: (1) the non-uniqueness of group structures (through their definition of the intrinsic structure) and (2) the lack of a systematic identification method. The authors' approach to complexity measurement using RKHS covering numbers is novel within this context. The connection to graphical models is interesting, but the paper clearly distinguishes its focus on direct identification of the group additive structure versus the more complex conditional dependence structure. Compared to recent NIPS papers on nonparametric regression (e.g., [6] on functional ANOVA), this work offers a more computationally feasible alternative for high-dimensional settings.

## Significance
The problem addressed is significant for high-dimensional nonparametric regression, where traditional additive models are too restrictive and higher-order interaction models become computationally intractable. The proposed method provides a practical middle ground that maintains estimation efficiency while capturing interactions. The empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in identifying true group structures across multiple simulation scenarios. The real data application to Boston Housing shows the method's practical utility, though the limited number of predictors (13) in this dataset somewhat limits the demonstration of high-dimensional scalability. The approach has potential to be applied in various domains where high-dimensional nonparametric modeling is needed, such as genomics or environmental science.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Rigorous theoretical foundation with a clear definition of the intrinsic group additive structure
2. Well-motivated complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers
3. Strong theoretical consistency results (Theorem 4)
4. Practical algorithms with both exhaustive and stepwise approaches
5. Comprehensive empirical evaluation with both simulations and real data

**Weaknesses:**
1. The explanation for why the intrinsic structure is the "best" choice beyond theoretical minimality is somewhat lacking
2. The performance deterioration in Model 1 (Figure 2, middle panel) is not thoroughly explained
3. The real data application uses a relatively small dataset (13 predictors) that doesn't fully demonstrate high-dimensional utility
4. The computational complexity of the exhaustive search method isn't explicitly discussed, making it hard to gauge scalability
5. The paper doesn't fully explore alternative complexity measures (e.g., VC dimension, Rademacher complexity) that might be more efficient

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric regression that addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the understanding and identification of group additive structures. While the real data application could be more extensive, the methodological contribution is significant and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community, addressing a problem with clear relevance to high-dimensional statistical learning.

I recommend acceptance. The paper advances the state of the art in nonparametric regression by providing a rigorous framework for identifying group additive structures, with both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. It offers a practical solution to a common problem in high-dimensional nonparametric modeling that balances flexibility with computational feasibility. The authors have clearly demonstrated the novelty and significance of their approach compared to previous work, and the paper meets all NIPS quality standards.
