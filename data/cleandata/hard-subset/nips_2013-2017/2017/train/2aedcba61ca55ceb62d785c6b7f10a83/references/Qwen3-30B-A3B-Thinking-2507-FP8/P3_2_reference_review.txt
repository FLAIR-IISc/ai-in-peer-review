

# Peer Review: "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses an important challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: balancing model flexibility with interpretability and computational efficiency. The authors propose a novel framework for identifying the intrinsic group additive structure (G*) in nonparametric regression, where the true function can be decomposed as f(X) = ∑ⱼ fᵤⱼ(Xᵤⱼ) with minimal grouping. This work extends the additive model framework by considering group structures rather than individual variables, bridging the gap between simple additive models and full interaction models. The paper builds on seminal works like [5, 6, 11] but introduces the key innovation of defining and identifying the *intrinsic* group structure rather than assuming it is known.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for the problem, defining the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and proving its uniqueness. The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is comprehensive, establishing consistency of the proposed method (Theorem 4).

2. **Novel Complexity Measure**: The derivation of the complexity measure C(G) based on RKHS covering numbers is elegant and well-motivated. The connection between the capacity of RKHSs and group structure complexity is insightful and provides a principled way to penalize model complexity.

3. **Practical Algorithms**: The two proposed algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) provide practical solutions for different scale scenarios. The implementation details are clear and well-structured.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The simulation studies (Section 4) and real data application (Section 5) demonstrate the method's ability to recover true group structures. The Boston Housing application provides concrete evidence of the method's interpretability benefits.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper mentions related work but doesn't sufficiently compare against existing methods for structure identification. For example, how does this approach compare to sparse additive models [6] or graphical model-based approaches [9]? A direct comparison on benchmark datasets would strengthen the empirical contribution.

2. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The theoretical results rely on assumptions about the covering numbers of RKHSs that might be restrictive in practice (e.g., exponential decay of Fourier transforms for Gaussian kernels). The paper would benefit from discussing how these assumptions hold in realistic nonparametric regression scenarios.

3. **Computational Scalability**: While the stepwise algorithm is proposed for large p, the paper doesn't provide concrete computational complexity analysis or empirical runtime comparisons. For high-dimensional problems (p > 100), the computational burden of even the stepwise approach could be prohibitive.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't address how to choose the tuning parameters λ, μ, and α in practice. The validation set approach (Algorithm 1) is reasonable but may not be optimal for small sample sizes.

5. **Interpretability Gap**: While the paper claims improved interpretability, it doesn't provide a clear demonstration of how the identified group structure leads to better domain-specific insights compared to other methods. The Boston Housing example is somewhat superficial in this regard.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the method addresses a real problem in the field. The main limitation is that the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this approach improves upon existing methods in terms of predictive performance or computational efficiency.

The paper's primary contribution is the formalization of the intrinsic group additive structure and the development of a principled method for its identification. However, the practical advantages over existing approaches (e.g., [6, 11]) are not clearly established through comprehensive comparisons.

## Recommendation

I recommend *acceptance* with minor revisions. The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and addresses an important problem. The main areas for improvement are:

1. Expand the empirical comparison to include a direct comparison with relevant state-of-the-art methods.
2. Provide more detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
3. Strengthen the interpretation of results, particularly in the Boston Housing example.

The paper is a solid contribution that advances the field of interpretable nonparametric regression. While the improvements over existing methods are somewhat incremental, the theoretical novelty and clean formulation of the intrinsic group structure make it a valuable addition to the literature.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with clear definitions and proofs
- Novel concept of intrinsic group additive structure
- Well-motivated complexity measure based on RKHS properties
- Practical algorithms with empirical validation

**Con:**
- Limited comparison with existing methods
- Theoretical assumptions may be restrictive in practice
- Insufficient discussion of computational scalability
- Interpretability claims need stronger empirical support

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field that could guide future work on interpretable high-dimensional nonparametric models. With the suggested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
