Summary
This paper studies nonparametric regression under an additive structure over groups of predictors. The authors (i) formalize a partial order over group partitions and define amiable group structures, (ii) prove the existence and uniqueness of a minimal “intrinsic” group additive structure G*, (iii) propose to estimate f and identify G* jointly by penalized kernel regression over a direct-sum RKHS with a structural penalty derived from RKHS covering numbers, (iv) prove that, with an appropriate penalty, the selected group structure is consistent, and (v) provide two algorithms (exhaustive search with validation and a backward stepwise heuristic), with simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

Relation to prior work
- Additive and ANOVA decompositions: builds on generalized additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani) and SS-ANOVA (Gu; Wahba’s lineage), and on SALSA for additive approximations (Kandasamy & Yu).
- Grouping and interactions: relates to OKGT (Pan et al.) by targeting groups rather than individual variables; contrasts with functional ANOVA that enumerates many interaction terms.
- RKHS capacity control: uses covering numbers (Zhou; Kühn) to define a tractable complexity measure for direct-sum RKHSs.
- Closely related streams not sufficiently discussed: COSSO/component selection in SS-ANOVA (Lin & Zhang, 2006), sparse additive models (Ravikumar et al., 2009), and especially hierarchical kernel learning / multiple kernel learning with ANOVA kernels and convex structure-inducing penalties (e.g., Bach 2008–2010). These methods also select among sets of RKHS components (including grouped or higher-order interactions), offering both algorithmic and theoretical tools.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual formulation of “intrinsic” group additive structure as the minimal amiable partition; the partial order over partitions is natural and useful.
- Sound use of RKHS tools: a direct-sum RKHS for a given group structure; covering-number-based capacity control to define a structural penalty; a product-covering argument (Proposition 3) relating covering numbers for the sum space to those of group RKHSs.
- Theoretical results: uniform risk bounds (via covering numbers) for amiable structures, a separation for non-amiable structures, and selection consistency under appropriate tuning.
- Practical motivation: group additivity balances interpretability and flexibility between purely additive and high-order ANOVA models; the Boston example illustrates interpretability.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical
- Structural penalty ambiguity: the derivation moves from ln N(ε, H) ≲ (ln(r/ε))p+1 to a penalty C(G) ≍ Σ α(ε)^{|u|+1}, but the final optimization in Eq. (6) uses µ Σ α|u| (notation unclear: α|u| or α^{|u|}?). The dependence on ε is then treated as a hyperparameter α, which is ad hoc. The paper should give a precise, consistent penalty, and explain how α relates to covering numbers and does not break the selection consistency proof.
- Selection consistency (Theorem 4) requires “proper µ” and λn with certain scaling, but no explicit guidance or rates are provided. The role of α in the theory is absent. It is not shown how to choose (λ, µ, α) to guarantee consistency in practice.
- Algorithmic details are underspecified. The backward stepwise procedure is vague (“split j as a new group or add to an existing group”)—does it try all placements of variable j across existing groups and singleton? What is the objective used at each step (full re-solve with tuned µ, α?) and stopping rule? No guarantees are given for this heuristic.
- Scalability is not addressed. Exhaustive search over partitions scales with Bell numbers; the heuristic may still be costly if each evaluation solves a KRR with group-specific kernels.
- Assumptions and identifiability: strict inclusion in Proposition 1 uses independence. More broadly, the uniqueness of G* for general PX and f deserves a clearer statement of assumptions and a proof sketch (the text asserts Ga is partially ordered and complete). Are there conditions under which multiple minimal partitions could arise? How does dependence among Xj affect identifiability?
- Kernel choices and tuning are under-specified (kernel type, bandwidth per group, λ selection), and the effect on both theory and practice is not explored.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally well written, but suffers from notational inconsistencies around the penalty (α exponent vs product), missing years in references, and some typographical issues. Provide a worked example connecting the covering-number bound to the final penalty used in optimization, with constants.
- Algorithms: present exact pseudocode with all options considered and computational complexity per iteration.

Originality
- The intrinsic group structure formalization and its consistency result for RKHS-based selection are interesting. However, the penalty-based selection of additive/ANOVA components in RKHS has significant prior art (COSSO; hierarchical kernel learning/MKL), which is not cited or contrasted. The novelty would be stronger if the authors clarified how their penalty and identification guarantees differ from those literatures, especially regarding groupings of variables rather than fixed ANOVA components, and whether convex formulations (as in HKL) could be leveraged.

Significance
- The problem—identifying interacting groups to balance flexibility and interpretability—is important. The theoretical framing is promising. However, empirical validation is limited:
  - No comparisons against strong baselines (e.g., SS-ANOVA with component selection, COSSO, SALSA, OKGT, HKL/MKL methods, sparse additive with interactions).
  - Synthetic experiments are small-scale; recovery frequencies are reported without sensitivity to n, p, SNR, or dependence structure; λ selection appears underexplored.
  - Real-data results are descriptive rather than comparative (no predictive benchmarks).
These shortcomings limit the paper’s impact in its current form.

Pros
- Clear motivation and formalization of group-based additivity; intrinsic structure is a useful concept.
- Principled RKHS-based complexity control and selection-consistency theory.
- Practical algorithms and interpretable examples.

Cons
- Ambiguity and ad hoc treatment in the structural penalty; incomplete guidance for tuning.
- Missing key related work (COSSO, hierarchical kernel learning/MKL), weakening the positioning.
- Limited, non-comparative experiments; scalability and algorithmic details underdeveloped.
- Theory does not connect concretely to the implemented penalty/tuning choices.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely define the penalty used in Eq. (6), reconcile with covering-number theory, and give explicit conditions on (λn, µn, αn) for consistency. If α is purely a hyperparameter, justify theoretically or remove it.
- Add and discuss baselines: COSSO, SS-ANOVA with component selection, SALSA, OKGT, HKL/MKL with ANOVA kernels (e.g., Bach), and sparse additive/interactions methods. Compare both structure recovery and prediction.
- Provide algorithmic details, complexity analysis, and, if possible, a convex or more scalable alternative (e.g., MKL-style formulation over a library of group kernels with structured sparsity).
- Clarify assumptions for uniqueness of G*, and give a proof sketch. Discuss impact of dependence among covariates.
- Expand experiments: vary n, p, group sizes, dependence, and SNR; include runtime; study sensitivity to kernel bandwidths and λ.
- Clean up references (complete citations) and notation.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents a well-motivated and potentially useful framework with appealing theory, but currently lacks clarity in the penalty/selection mechanism, omits closely related prior work, and provides limited empirical evidence and algorithmic detail. Strengthening these aspects could make it a solid contribution.
