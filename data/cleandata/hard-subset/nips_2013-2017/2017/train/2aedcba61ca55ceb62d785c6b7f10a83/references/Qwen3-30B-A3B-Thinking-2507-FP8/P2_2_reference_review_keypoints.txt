- Proposes a group additive structure for high-dimensional nonparametric regression where predictors are grouped and the response depends on groups rather than individual variables.  
- Introduces the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group representation of the true function and a kernel-based method with a structural penalty to identify it.  
- Main contributions: formal definition of intrinsic structure, an RKHS covering-number-based complexity measure, and theoretical consistency results for the identification method.  
- Strong technical quality with rigorous definitions (amiable, intrinsic), precise math, and Theorem 4 proving consistency under appropriate conditions.  
- Empirical evaluation includes five synthetic models and Boston Housing with 10-fold CV; authors acknowledge limitations like Model 1 issues and computational cost of exhaustive search.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; algorithms are implementable, but needs more intuitive justification of intrinsic structure and clearer visualizations of group-function relationships.  
- Novelty: addresses non-uniqueness of group structures and provides a systematic identification method; RKHS covering-number complexity measure is a novel angle.  
- Significance: offers a practical middle ground between additive models and full interaction (functional ANOVA) models, useful for high-dimensional nonparametric problems, though real-data demonstration is low-dimensional.  
- Strengths: rigorous theoretical foundation, motivated complexity measure, strong consistency results, practical exhaustive and stepwise algorithms, comprehensive experiments.  
- Weaknesses: limited intuitive rationale for intrinsic structure, unexplained degradation in Model 1, small-scale real-data example, scalability concerns for exhaustive search, and lack of exploration of alternative complexity measures.  
- Overall assessment: technically strong and novel contribution to nonparametric regression with empirical support; recommended for acceptance.
