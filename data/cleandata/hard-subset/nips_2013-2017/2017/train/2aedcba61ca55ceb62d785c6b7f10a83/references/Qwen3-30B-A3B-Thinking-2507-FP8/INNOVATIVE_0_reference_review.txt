

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper introduces a novel approach to nonparametric regression by identifying the intrinsic group additive structure of the underlying function. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while additive models (f(X) = Σf_i(X_i)) and functional ANOVA models (which include higher-order interactions) have been studied, the problem of identifying the minimal group structure that captures all necessary interactions has been overlooked. The authors define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal partition of predictor variables such that the regression function can be expressed as a sum of functions over these groups. This concept lies between the standard additive model (which assumes no interactions) and functional ANOVA models (which assume all possible interactions), offering a promising middle ground that balances interpretability, estimation efficiency, and the ability to capture relevant interactions.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a rigorous definition of the intrinsic group additive structure, (2) a structural penalty method using RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure to identify this structure, and (3) theoretical consistency results and practical algorithms for implementation. The authors derive a tractable complexity measure C(G) = Σα^{|u_j|} for group structures G, which enables them to formulate the identification problem as an optimization that balances fitting error and structural complexity. Theoretical results establish that the estimated structure is consistent for the intrinsic structure as sample size increases, while the empirical results demonstrate successful identification of true group structures across multiple synthetic models and the Boston Housing data.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While previous work (like [11]) considered group additive models, it did not address the critical issue of structure identification. The authors' definition of intrinsic group structure and their approach to identifying it using RKHS complexity measures represent a significant conceptual advance. The paper clearly positions its contribution relative to related work on additive models [5, 13], functional ANOVA [4, 6], and graphical models [2, 7, 9], making it clear how their approach differs and why it's valuable.

The theoretical foundation is strong, with careful development of the necessary mathematical machinery (including RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure) and rigorous consistency proofs. The experimental validation is thorough for the scope of the paper, with synthetic data experiments demonstrating the method's ability to recover true group structures and the Boston Housing application showing practical utility. The algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) are well-motivated and practical for different problem sizes.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression, particularly for high-dimensional settings where the curse of dimensionality is a major concern. The concept of intrinsic group structure is novel and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. The authors have successfully developed a principled approach to identifying this structure, with sound theoretical foundations and practical implementation. While the RKHS complexity measure might be somewhat technical, the authors provide a clear explanation of its motivation and derivation.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a compelling case for the importance of the problem it addresses. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the stepwise algorithm may not always find the global optimum, and the approach is limited to group structures with small group sizes), which demonstrates intellectual honesty.

In terms of the NIPS criteria:
- **Novelty**: High. The intrinsic group additive structure concept is original and fills an important gap.
- **Significance**: High. The approach has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency and interpretability of high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
- **Quality**: High. The theoretical results are sound, and the empirical validation is thorough for the scope of the paper.
- **Clarity**: High. The paper is well-organized and clearly written.

This paper represents a bold and novel direction in nonparametric regression that goes beyond incremental improvements. While some technical details (like the RKHS complexity measure) might be challenging for some readers, the core idea is conceptually clear and significant. The authors have successfully bridged a gap between additive models and functional ANOVA, offering a practical middle ground that addresses a real problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS. This paper introduces a significant new concept (intrinsic group additive structure) with clear theoretical and practical implications, making it an important contribution to the field. The authors have demonstrated both the novelty and significance of their approach, and the paper is well-written and technically sound. While the empirical validation could be expanded to more real-world applications, the current results are sufficient to support the method's promise, and the authors acknowledge this as a direction for future work.
