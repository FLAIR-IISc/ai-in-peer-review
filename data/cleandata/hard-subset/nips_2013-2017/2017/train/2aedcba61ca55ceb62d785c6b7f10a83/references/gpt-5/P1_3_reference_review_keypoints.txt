- Paper studies nonparametric regression with group additive structure, defines a partial order on partitions, amiable structures, and proves existence/uniqueness of an intrinsic minimal group structure G*.
- Proposes an RKHS/KRR-based estimator that jointly fits component functions and selects group partition G via a complexity penalty C(G) derived from covering-number bounds, with a consistency result for selecting G*.
- Presents two algorithms (exhaustive search with validation and a backward stepwise heuristic) and empirical results on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset.
- Situates work between additive models and full ANOVA/higher-order interactions; relates to OKGT, SALSA, smoothing spline ANOVA, and RKHS kernel methods.
- Strength: clear problem formulation and motivation—grouping variables captures interactions without full ANOVA complexity explosion.
- Strength: conceptual contribution of a formal partial order, amiability notion, and unique intrinsic structure aiding identifiability.
- Strength: methodological novelty in deriving a structural penalty from RKHS covering-number bounds using subadditivity of log-covering numbers.
- Strength: theoretical guarantees including separation of amiable/non-amiable structures and a structure-selection consistency theorem.
- Strength: practical algorithms and initial interpretable empirical results (e.g., bivariate effects in Boston dataset).
- Weakness: penalty derivation simplifies covering-number bounds to a group-size-only penalty, disconnecting it from kernels, domains, and constants—gap not well justified.
- Weakness: Theorem 4’s consistency relies on tuning (μ, λ) without providing principled schedules or showing CV/validation meets required conditions; asymptotic conditions are unclear/typoed.
- Weakness: identifiability issues—no mention of standard centering constraints (e.g., E[fu]=0), which are necessary for uniqueness and estimation.
- Weakness: key assumptions (e.g., independent covariates, compact [0,1]^p domain) are restrictive and inconsistent with simulations using unbounded/Gaussian inputs; effects of dependence not discussed.
- Weakness: algorithms scale poorly—exhaustive search is exponential and the backward stepwise heuristic is greedy, may miss true structure with no analysis of landscape, convergence, or restarts.
- Weakness: computational/implementation details sparse—solving the joint KRR problem for multiple groups and block kernels is not sufficiently described.
- Clarity issue: notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., “λn ∗ n → 0”), unclear role/interpretation of α, and ambiguous treatment of α in experiments.
- Clarity issue: Algorithm 2 underspecified—how splits are chosen, handling multiple improving splits, and whether restarts/multiple passes are used is unclear.
- Originality: partition-based grouping + RKHS-capacity-driven selection is novel, but related literature (SpAM, HKL, ANOVA kernels, OKGT, SALSA) should be discussed more and compared empirically.
- Significance concern: empirical section limited—small simulations, single real dataset, no comparisons to baselines, unclear scalability to p≥50, and sensitivity to μ/α not studied.
- Suggestions: provide precise scaling conditions for λn, μn (and α if relevant) for Theorem 4 and show CV/validation compatibility.
- Suggestions: impose and state centering/identifiability constraints and explain how they are enforced in optimization.
- Suggestions: expand related work and add empirical comparisons to SALSA, OKGT, HKL, ANOVA-KRR, and SpAM-style methods.
- Suggestions: strengthen algorithms—detail efficient solvers leveraging block structure and consider better partition search strategies (beam search, stochastic local search, restarts) and report runtimes/scalability.
- Suggestions: study robustness to true large-group structures, correlated covariates, kernel misspecification, varied sample sizes, and provide ablations for μ and α.
- Suggestions: fix typos/notation and improve proofs’ accessibility (move key lemmas or provide detailed sketches).
- Reasons to accept: interesting and relevant problem, solid conceptual framing, novel RKHS-capacity penalty, and interpretability on real data.
- Reasons to reject: gap between theory and practical penalty/tuning, limited experiments with no baselines, and underdeveloped algorithmic treatment.
- Overall: promising ideas and theory but needs stronger empirical validation, clearer assumptions/tuning guidance, and improved algorithmic development to be competitive for NeurIPS.
