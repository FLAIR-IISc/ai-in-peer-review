Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a group additive structure: f(X) = sum over groups u of fu(Xu), where each group consists of a subset of variables and functions are unknown and potentially nonlinear. The authors (i) formalize a notion of intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal (finest) group partition under which f is representable, (ii) propose an RKHS-based estimation procedure that simultaneously estimates f and selects the group structure by penalizing a complexity measure derived from covering number bounds, and (iii) provide selection consistency results. They propose exhaustive search for small p and a backward stepwise splitting heuristic for larger p, and present simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

This situates between additive models [Hastie & Tibshirani], functional ANOVA/smoothing spline ANOVA [Gu], higher-order interactions via ESP kernels (SALSA, Kandasamy & Yu), and OKGT (Pan et al.), with a different focus: discovering groups of interacting variables without enumerating all interactions. There are also conceptual links to block-structured graphical models.

Quality (technical soundness)
Strengths:
- The formalization of a partial order on group partitions and the existence/uniqueness of a minimal “intrinsic” group structure are conceptually clean and useful. The lattice viewpoint is natural and helps clarify identifiability.
- The penalized RKHS formulation is reasonable: representer theorem implies estimation reduces to KRR with a sum of group kernels; the complexity penalty encourages smaller groups and thus the intrinsic structure.
- Theoretical results distinguish amiable vs. non-amiable structures and provide probability bounds using covering numbers, culminating in a (high-level) selection consistency statement.

Weaknesses / gaps:
- Derivation-to-practice gap for the complexity penalty. The covering-number upper bound depends on the kernel class, radius r, metric epsilon, and input dimension. The paper collapses this into a scalar α and then treats α as a tuning parameter (even integer-valued 1–10), severing the claimed theoretical link. Moreover, there are inconsistencies in the penalty: C(G) is variously written as sum α(ε)^{|u|+1}, sum α|u|, and in Eq. (6) as sum α^{|u|}. This needs to be corrected and justified.
- Assumptions behind the bounds (e.g., convolution kernels with exponentially decaying Fourier transform, sup-norm entropy, independence conditions used in Proposition 1) are not cleanly reconciled with the practical algorithm (Gaussian kernels, finite-n KRR, cross-validated λ, µ, α). The final consistency result states “choose proper µ” and a vague condition “λn ∗ n → 0,” without concrete rates or guidance that align with the implemented selection of α and µ.
- Algorithmic details are underspecified. The norm on the direct sum RKHS HG and the exact kernel used for each group should be explicitly defined (and the representer form of the combined kernel K_G = sum K_u stated). The greedy backward splitting in Algorithm 2 is ambiguous (“either split j as a new group or add to an existing group”), appears to make a single pass, and is prone to local minima; no analysis of its computational complexity or guarantees is given.
- Empirical evaluation is limited. Simulations report frequencies of exact recovery but provide sparse details on the data-generating models and no comparisons to strong baselines such as SALSA/ESP-KRR, sparse/additive+pairwise models, OKGT, hierarchical kernel learning, or HSIC-based variable grouping. The real-data example is descriptive; no held-out predictive comparisons are provided. As a consequence, the practical advantage over existing methods is not convincingly demonstrated.

Clarity
- The paper is generally understandable but hampered by notational inconsistencies, typos, and missing definitions (e.g., inconsistent penalty forms; unclear λ condition in Theorem 4; incomplete reference years). Some proofs are deferred to the supplement; more intuition around key lemmas would help. Algorithm 2 needs a precise specification of candidate moves and stopping criteria. Please clearly define the RKHS norm on HG and the combined kernel used in (7).

Originality
- The notion of intrinsic group additive structure and using RKHS capacity measures to induce a structural penalty for group discovery appear novel. While related to OKGT and interaction modeling via ANOVA/ESP kernels, the explicit penalization over partitions and the consistency claim for recovering the minimal group structure contribute new perspective. That said, the step from covering number bounds to a practical fixed-form penalty is not fully justified.

Significance
- If strengthened, the approach could be useful: learning “who interacts with whom” at low orders is practically relevant and can mitigate the curse of dimensionality while retaining interpretability. However, current experimental evidence is insufficient to assess impact. Without comparisons and scalability analysis, it is unclear whether practitioners would adopt this over existing additive/interaction discovery methods.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and identifiability via minimal partition
- RKHS-based joint estimation/selection framework with theoretical consistency (at a high level)
- Conceptually appealing complexity-penalized search over group structures

Cons
- Inconsistent and weakly justified structural penalty; theory/practice mismatch (α as a hyperparameter)
- Limited and baseline-free empirical evaluation; stepwise algorithm unclear and likely brittle
- Missing algorithmic and computational details; unclear scalability
- Several notation/typo issues and reliance on supplement for key proofs

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix and justify the penalty form; connect α and µ to kernel, ε, and n, or propose a principled surrogate with clear properties.
- Provide stronger experiments with baselines (SALSA/ESP-KRR, pairwise additive KRR, OKGT, HKL, SpAM/Group-Lasso variants), report prediction accuracy and structure recovery, and analyze scalability.
- Clarify the direct sum RKHS construction, combined kernel, and optimization; detail Algorithm 2 (search moves, multiple passes, restart strategy).
- State precise assumptions and rates in Theorem 4 consistent with the implemented tuning.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The problem is interesting and the conceptual framework promising, but the current manuscript has theoretical-practical gaps, clarity issues, and insufficient empirical validation.
