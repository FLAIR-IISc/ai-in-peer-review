This paper proposes a method for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, aiming to balance the flexibility of nonparametric methods with the interpretability and efficiency of additive models. The authors address the non-identifiability issue of group additive structures by defining an "intrinsic" structure and propose a penalized kernel method for its identification. The core idea – leveraging a complexity penalty on the group structure – is reasonable, and the connection to graphical models is briefly discussed.

However, while the motivation is sound and the problem potentially relevant, the paper falls short of convincing me of its substantial contribution, primarily due to a lack of thorough empirical validation against well-established baselines and an over-reliance on synthetic data. My primary concerns relate to the novelty, significance, and clarity of the work.

The notion of grouping variables to achieve a trade-off between full nonparametric flexibility and additive model structure isn't entirely new (see [11] as the authors acknowledge, and also related work in sparse additive modeling). The main contribution appears to be the proposed complexity measure and the associated minimization problem. While the theoretical justification for consistency is presented, the practicality and advantage of this specific complexity measure remain unclear. It’s not demonstrated that this measure consistently outperforms simpler alternatives or provides genuinely insightful groupings.

The simulation studies are extensive but feel somewhat insular. The focus is almost entirely on demonstrating that the proposed method *can* recover the true structure when the data is generated according to the model assumptions. Critically, the paper lacks a comparison to established methods like functional ANOVA or even regularized additive models (e.g., those based on splines or GAMs) on tasks where the true underlying structure is unknown. How does this method perform when the data doesn’t perfectly conform to a group additive structure? Does it consistently identify meaningful groupings, or does it fall back to a fully nonparametric model, losing the benefits of structure?  The comparison to exhaustive search and a stepwise approach is useful for evaluating computational efficiency, but doesn't assess predictive performance relative to other methods. The real data example is limited and doesn’t offer strong evidence of practical benefit beyond what could be achieved with more standard techniques.

The clarity is also somewhat hampered by the dense mathematical notation. While the theoretical development is appreciated, the presentation could be more accessible. The connection between the capacity measure, the penalty term, and the practical effect on group structure identification isn’t sufficiently explained for a non-specialist. The algorithms described are relatively high-level and could benefit from pseudocode or more detailed explanations.

**Strengths:**

*   The problem of identifying group additive structures is relevant and potentially important.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a foundation for the proposed method.
*   The proposed penalty term attempts to address the non-identifiability issue.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty – the core idea builds on existing work without a clear demonstrable advantage.
*   Lack of comparison to established baseline methods (functional ANOVA, GAMs, etc.).
*   Over-reliance on synthetic data with ideal conditions.
*   The real data example is not particularly compelling.
*   Dense mathematical notation can hinder clarity.
*   The practical advantages of the proposed complexity measure are not convincingly demonstrated.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to a potentially valuable problem.
*   The consistency proof is a positive contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of empirical validation against strong baselines significantly weakens the claims.
*   The limited novelty and unclear practical benefits do not justify acceptance to NIPS.
*   The presentation could be more accessible.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper demonstrates a degree of technical sophistication, it fails to convince me of its practical significance or substantial contribution to the field. The absence of a thorough empirical evaluation against established methods is a critical flaw. The paper needs to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing techniques to warrant acceptance to NIPS. A major revision addressing these concerns, with a strong emphasis on comparative performance on real-world datasets, would be required for reconsideration.
