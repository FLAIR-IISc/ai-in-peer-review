

# Peer Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces a novel approach to identifying the intrinsic group additive structure for nonparametric regression, addressing a significant gap in the additive model literature. The authors define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal decomposition of a function into a sum of group-dependent functions (uniquely identifying the most concise representation), and propose a method to identify this structure using a RKHS-based complexity penalty. The paper connects to previous work on additive models (e.g., [5]), functional ANOVA [4, 13], and the OKGT method [11], positioning group additive models as a middle ground between standard additive models and full interaction models.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution with the definition of the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and its consistency properties (Theorems 2-4). The authors rigorously establish that their proposed method identifies the minimal group structure, which is crucial for dimensionality reduction in high-dimensional settings.

2. **Novel Complexity Measure**: The derivation of the complexity measure C(G) based on RKHS covering numbers is well-motivated and connects to established learning theory (e.g., [18]). The authors appropriately address the challenge of defining a complexity measure that properly reflects the structural differences between group additive structures.

3. **Practical Algorithms**: The paper provides two practical algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) for implementation, with thorough simulation studies demonstrating the method's effectiveness in identifying the correct group structure across various model settings.

4. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively establishes the problem's importance by clearly articulating the limitations of both standard additive models (no interactions) and full interaction models (curse of dimensionality).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Evidence for Large p**: While the paper claims to address high-dimensional settings, the simulation studies and real data application are limited to small p (13 predictors in Boston Housing). The stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is presented as a solution for "large p," but the paper doesn't provide strong evidence of its effectiveness on truly high-dimensional problems (e.g., p > 100). The theoretical consistency results also assume fixed p, which is inconsistent with the high-dimensional claim.

2. **Over-reliance on Upper Bounds**: The complexity measure C(G) = ∑_j α^{|u_j|} is derived from an upper bound on the covering number (equation 4), which may not be tight in practice. The authors don't provide empirical evidence that this specific form of the complexity measure works well in practice compared to other potential measures. The choice of α as an integer in [1, 10] seems arbitrary without theoretical justification.

3. **Theoretical Gaps**: The paper claims the intrinsic structure is unique (Theorem 1), but doesn't establish clear conditions under which this holds. In practice, functions can sometimes have multiple decompositions that appear equally minimal. The authors should clarify the conditions under which the intrinsic structure is uniquely defined.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites Kandasamy and Yu (2016) on "Additive approximations in high dimensional nonparametric regression," but doesn't adequately contrast their approach with the current work. The current method's relationship to this line of work needs clarification.

5. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper states that λ can be tuned separately, but doesn't provide guidance on how to select λ for the group structure identification problem. The tuning of µ and α via validation (Algorithm 1) is reasonable, but the paper doesn't address the computational cost of this tuning procedure.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, theoretically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the additive model literature. The core idea of identifying the intrinsic group additive structure is novel and important, and the authors provide strong theoretical guarantees for their method.

The primary limitations are the lack of evidence for the method's performance in truly high-dimensional settings (p >> 100), the somewhat ad-hoc nature of the complexity measure, and the limited empirical validation on large datasets. While the paper acknowledges these limitations (particularly in the discussion), the simulation studies and real data application don't sufficiently demonstrate the method's utility in the high-dimensional regime where it's most needed.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the literature, but falls short of demonstrating its significance for the most challenging high-dimensional problems that the method was designed to address.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution with clear implications for nonparametric regression, but the empirical evidence for the method's effectiveness in truly high-dimensional settings is insufficient. With stronger empirical validation on larger datasets and a more thorough justification of the complexity measure, this paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS.

The authors should address the following in revision:
1. Provide simulations with p > 100 to demonstrate the method's scalability
2. Justify the choice of α and the specific form of C(G) more thoroughly
3. Clarify the relationship between this work and Kandasamy & Yu (2016)
4. Discuss the conditions under which the intrinsic structure is uniquely defined

The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important problem, but the evidence for its practical utility in the most relevant setting (very high-dimensional problems) needs strengthening.
