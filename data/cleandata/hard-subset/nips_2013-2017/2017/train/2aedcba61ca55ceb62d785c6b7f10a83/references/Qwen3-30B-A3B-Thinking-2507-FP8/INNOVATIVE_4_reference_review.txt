

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper introduces the concept of "intrinsic group additive structure" for nonparametric regression, addressing a critical gap in the literature on high-dimensional nonparametric modeling. The authors recognize that existing approaches to group additive models either assume the group structure is known (e.g., [11]) or fail to address the non-uniqueness of group structures, leading to identifiability issues. Their key contribution is the rigorous definition of the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group structure that can represent the true function, and a novel method to identify this structure through a penalized kernel approach with a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers.

The paper's main novelty lies in its conceptual framework for group additive structure identification, which provides a middle ground between standard additive models (which assume no interactions) and functional ANOVA models (which assume all possible interactions). This is a bold approach that directly targets the group additive structure rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure considered in graphical models. The authors make a clear theoretical distinction between their approach and graphical models, noting that their method directly targets the group additive structure rather than inferring it from conditional dependencies.

The technical contribution includes a tractable complexity measure for group additive structures derived from RKHS covering numbers, which they use as a penalty in their optimization problem. They prove consistency of their estimator for the intrinsic group structure, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper develops two practical algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) for structure identification and demonstrates the approach on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset.

The paper's significance is substantial. High-dimensional nonparametric regression suffers from the curse of dimensionality, and the group additive structure offers a promising compromise between model flexibility and estimation efficiency. The authors' method provides a principled way to discover this structure without assuming it a priori, which is a major practical advantage. Their approach could be particularly valuable for practitioners dealing with high-dimensional data where interactions are suspected but full functional ANOVA is computationally prohibitive.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient theoretical and empirical evidence for its claims. The simulation results demonstrate that the method can successfully recover the true group structure across various model settings, and the real data application on Boston Housing provides interpretable results that align with domain knowledge.

While the paper is strong in its theoretical contributions and novel conceptual framework, I note a few minor weaknesses. The comparison with alternative methods for group structure identification is somewhat limited, though the authors do acknowledge this limitation. The computational complexity of the exhaustive search approach for large p is acknowledged but not deeply analyzed. However, the authors address this by proposing a stepwise algorithm, which is a practical solution.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual contributions, I find this paper particularly compelling. The introduction of the "intrinsic group additive structure" as a unique minimal representation is a significant theoretical advance that opens new research directions in nonparametric regression. The paper's approach is both conceptually novel and practically applicable, addressing a meaningful gap in the literature.

The paper's contribution stands out from previous work on additive models (e.g., [5], [13]) and functional ANOVA (e.g., [6], [10]) by providing a principled way to discover the optimal group structure rather than assuming it a priori or considering all possible interactions. This represents a meaningful step beyond incremental improvements in the field.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression and would be of interest to the NIPS community. The novelty of the intrinsic group additive structure concept, combined with the practical algorithms and solid theoretical foundation, makes it a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Strengths:**
- Novel concept of intrinsic group additive structure (unique minimal representation)
- Principled method to identify group structure without assuming it a priori
- Clear theoretical foundation with consistency proofs
- Practical algorithms for implementation
- Demonstrated effectiveness on synthetic and real data

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison with alternative structure identification methods
- Computational complexity analysis for large p could be deeper
- Minor methodological details in the theoretical proofs could be expanded

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper introduces a genuinely novel concept that addresses a fundamental gap in nonparametric regression literature. The authors' approach is bold, theoretically sound, and practically valuable, making it a strong contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's significant conceptual novelty and practical utility, which align well with NIPS's emphasis on innovative and impactful research.
