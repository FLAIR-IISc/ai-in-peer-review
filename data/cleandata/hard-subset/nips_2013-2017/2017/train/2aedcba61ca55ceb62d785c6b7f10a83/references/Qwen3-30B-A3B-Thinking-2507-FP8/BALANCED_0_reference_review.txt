

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses the important problem of identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, which generalizes the standard additive model by allowing interactions within groups while maintaining interpretability. The authors define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group partition that can represent the target function, and propose a penalized kernel method to identify this structure using a complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers. The paper makes a clear contribution by formally defining and addressing the nonidentifiability problem in group additive models and providing a theoretically grounded approach to structure identification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical framework for group additive structures, introducing key concepts like amiable structures and the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1). The proof of the uniqueness of the intrinsic structure is well-motivated and addresses a gap in prior work (e.g., [11]).

2. **Novel Complexity Measure:** The authors derive a tractable complexity measure for group structures based on RKHS covering numbers, which is a significant contribution. This measure is theoretically justified (Proposition 3) and leads to a practical penalty term in the optimization problem (Equation 6).

3. **Algorithmic Development:** The paper proposes two practical algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) for structure identification, with clear descriptions and implementation details. The empirical results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in recovering true structures across multiple synthetic models.

4. **Connection to Related Work:** The paper properly contextualizes its contribution relative to functional ANOVA models ([4], [13]), graphical models ([2], [7], [9]), and the OKGT method ([11]), clarifying the key distinction between group additive structures and conditional dependence structures.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper establishes consistency (Theorem 4), it lacks rates of convergence, which would be valuable for practitioners. The theoretical analysis also assumes fixed dimensionality (p), which is a limitation for modern high-dimensional settings where p >> n is common.

2. **Computational Scalability:** The paper mentions the backward stepwise algorithm for large p, but does not provide computational complexity analysis or empirical evidence of scalability (e.g., runtime as p increases). This is a significant omission for a method targeting high-dimensional problems.

3. **Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation study is limited to only 5 synthetic models and one real dataset (Boston Housing). More extensive experiments would strengthen the claims, particularly regarding the sensitivity to tuning parameters (µ, α) and the comparison to alternative methods (e.g., graphical models or other structure learning approaches).

4. **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper does not address how to select the tuning parameters λ and α in practice, beyond using validation sets. A more thorough discussion of parameter sensitivity would be helpful for practitioners.

5. **Connection to Modern High-Dimensional Methods:** The paper does not clearly position its method relative to recent advances in high-dimensional nonparametric regression (e.g., [6], [16]), which might provide useful comparisons or extensions.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound approach to group additive structure identification with clear practical implications. While the core contribution is novel and well-motivated, the paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above. Specifically, the authors should:

1. Expand the theoretical analysis to include convergence rates and considerations for high-dimensional settings (p >> n)
2. Provide more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including comparisons to relevant baselines and scalability tests
3. Include a more detailed discussion of practical implementation details, particularly regarding tuning parameter selection
4. Better contextualize the method within the landscape of high-dimensional nonparametric regression

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression by addressing the important problem of structure identification. While the current version has some limitations, with the suggested improvements, it would be a strong candidate for NIPS. The method has potential to impact both theoretical and applied work in high-dimensional nonparametric modeling.

**Pro and Con Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Rigorous theoretical foundation for group additive structure identification
- Novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers
- Clear distinction from related work (functional ANOVA, graphical models)
- Practical algorithms with empirical validation

*Con:*
- Limited theoretical analysis (no convergence rates)
- Insufficient empirical evaluation for high-dimensional settings
- Lack of computational scalability analysis
- Limited discussion of practical implementation details

Overall, this is a well-written paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the nonparametric regression literature. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the empirical and theoretical limitations noted above.
