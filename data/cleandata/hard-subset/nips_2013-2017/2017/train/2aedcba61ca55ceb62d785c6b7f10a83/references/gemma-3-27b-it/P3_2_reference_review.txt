## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression, tackling the challenging problem of identifying underlying group additive structures in high-dimensional data. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical framework, propose a penalty-based method for structure identification, and demonstrate promising results through simulations and a real-data application. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the practical algorithms and their performance analysis could be further refined. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper addresses the limitations of traditional nonparametric regression in high dimensions (curse of dimensionality, interpretability) and the shortcomings of existing extensions like functional ANOVA models (potential for overfitting). It builds upon the idea of group additive models (GAMs) – where predictors are grouped based on interaction – but crucially addresses the lack of systematic methods for *identifying* the optimal group structure. The authors propose a novel complexity measure for group structures based on the covering numbers of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs) and formulate a penalized regression problem that simultaneously estimates the group structure and the underlying nonparametric functions.  This allows for a data-driven approach to identifying intrinsic interactions between variables.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature. They clearly connect to the well-established fields of additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 2009), functional ANOVA (Gu, 1999), and higher-order interaction models (Kandasamy & Yu, 2016).  They also acknowledge connections to graphical models (Koller & Friedman, 2009) but highlight the distinction – their approach directly targets the group additive structure rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure.  The reference to Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) (Pan et al., 2011) is crucial, as this work forms the foundation upon which the authors build, addressing the limitations of identifying the group structure that OKGT left open.  The theory draws upon concepts from statistical learning theory, including covering numbers and Rademacher complexity (Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002; Zhou, 2006).

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:**  The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, including the definition of the “intrinsic group additive structure” and consistency guarantees for their proposed method.  The use of covering numbers to quantify the complexity of group structures is a novel and mathematically sound approach.
* **Novel Complexity Measure:** The proposed complexity measure, based on RKHS covering numbers, offers a principled way to penalize overly complex group structures and prevent overfitting.
* **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The identification of underlying interactions is a fundamental challenge in nonparametric regression. This work provides a valuable tool for addressing this problem in a data-driven manner.
* **Clear Presentation of Algorithms:** The proposed algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise approach) are clearly described and offer practical solutions for different data scenarios.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in recovering the true group structures under various settings. The application to the Boston Housing data provides a real-world example of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm is computationally expensive and may not scale well to high-dimensional datasets. While the stepwise algorithm addresses this, its performance (as acknowledged by the authors) may be suboptimal, particularly in complex scenarios.  More efficient search strategies could be explored.
* **Sensitivity to Tuning Parameters:** The performance of the method is sensitive to the choice of tuning parameters (λ, μ, α). While the authors suggest using a validation set for parameter selection, a more systematic approach for parameter tuning and a sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Limited Real-Data Analysis:** While the Boston Housing data application is illustrative, a more comprehensive evaluation on a diverse range of real-world datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Assumption of Universal Kernels:** The reliance on universal kernels for achieving consistency is mentioned, but the practical implications of kernel choice and its impact on performance are not thoroughly discussed.
* **Algorithm 1 is a bit unclear**: It describes a two-step process of choosing (µ,α) before applying the parameters, but it doesn't detail how to handle the fact that the "best" Ĝ is dependent on (µ,α). It implies the two-step nature happens independently.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem.
*  The proposed method is well-motivated and builds upon existing literature.
*  The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in recovering true group structures.
*  The clarity of the exposition is good, making the paper accessible to a wide audience.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm may limit its applicability.
*  The sensitivity to tuning parameters and lack of a robust parameter selection strategy are concerns.
*  The real-data analysis is somewhat limited.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should:

1.  **Expand the discussion on computational efficiency:**  Investigate and potentially incorporate more efficient search algorithms beyond the stepwise approach.
2.  **Provide a more detailed parameter tuning strategy:**  Discuss best practices for selecting λ, μ, and α, and potentially explore automated parameter tuning methods.
3.  **Conduct a more comprehensive real-data evaluation:** Apply the method to a wider range of datasets with different characteristics.
4. **Clarify Algorithm 1**: Explain the optimization between (µ, α) and Ĝ.
5. **Discuss Kernel Choice**: Discuss the impact and sensitivity to the kernel choice on performance.



This work represents a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression and has the potential to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners. Addressing the minor revisions suggested above will further enhance the quality and impact of the paper.
