Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies nonparametric regression when the regression function admits a group additive structure: f(X) = sum over groups u of fu(Xu), where groups partition the predictors. It (i) formalizes the notion that multiple groupings may represent the same function and defines an “intrinsic” (minimal under refinement) group structure; (ii) proposes a structure-penalized kernel method to jointly estimate f and identify the group structure using a complexity penalty derived from covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs; (iii) proves selection consistency under asymptotics; and (iv) presents exhaustive-search and backward stepwise algorithms, with simulations and one real-data case.

This sits between classical additive models and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models (Hastie & Tibshirani; Gu; Kandasamy & Yu SALSA). It connects loosely to graphical models but targets a different object (grouping w.r.t. the regression function, not conditional dependence). It builds on OKGT (Pan et al.) and RKHS capacity results (e.g., Steinwart & Christmann; Zhou; Kühn). Using capacity-based penalties to control model selection is known; applying covering-number bounds to select additive groupings in direct-sum RKHSs is a novel angle.

Quality:
- Theory: The high-level idea—penalize a capacity proxy that increases with group dimensionality, and show that the minimal (intrinsic) grouping wins asymptotically—is sensible. The direct-sum covering-number inequality (Prop. 3) and the union-bound based generalization bounds (Prop. 4, Thm. 2) are standard in spirit. The separation between amiable and non-amiable structures (Thm. 3) underwrites identifiability. However, there are several technical gaps and ambiguities that need attention:
  - Intrinsic structure existence/uniqueness (Thm. 1): The paper asserts Ga is “partially ordered and complete,” hence has a unique minimal element. The existence of a meet in the poset of partitions is standard, but it is nontrivial that the meet of amiable groupings is itself amiable for f; the manuscript does not state assumptions (e.g., independence, product measures) under which L2-direct-sum decomposability is closed under refinement. The independence condition appears in Prop. 1 for strict inclusion but not in Thm. 1. A proof or precise conditions are needed.
  - Penalty derivation: The complexity measure moves from ln N(ε, HG, ∞) ≲ sum over groups of (ln(r/ε))|u|+1 to defining C(G)=sum α(ε)^{|u|+1}, then “combines the common factor 1 in the exponent with µ” to arrive at µ sum α|u| (Eq. 6). This step is unclear and appears incorrect (exponent dropped from |u|+1 to |u|). If α is a tunable parameter unrelated to ε, the connection to the covering-number bound becomes tenuous. The paper should either keep the theoretically motivated exponent or explicitly justify the reparameterization and its effect on consistency.
  - Assumptions: Theorems depend on universal kernels and covering-number bounds requiring specific kernel/space conditions (e.g., Gaussian kernel on bounded domains, continuity). These assumptions are not fully enumerated (noise distribution, boundedness, input distribution, choice of λn). The consistency statement (Thm. 4) requires λn*n→0 but does not specify the scaling of µ with n or how α is chosen—yet these are critical for consistent model selection.
  - Computation: For (7), a representer-theorem-based finite-dimensional formulation for direct-sum RKHSs should be given to clarify how f is estimated in practice and the complexity. The exhaustive search is exponential in p, and the backward stepwise heuristic lacks guarantees.

- Empirics: Simulations show recovery frequencies but limited quantitative evaluation (e.g., MSE vs. baselines) and no comparisons to relevant methods (SALSA; sparse additive models with pairwise interactions; spline-ANOVA; BART; HSIC-based interaction detection). The real data example is illustrative but lacks predictive comparison and stability analysis. The authors note sensitivity to λ and shortcomings in stepwise search, which weakens practical support.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and well-structured, with reasonable motivation. However:
- Several references lack years and details; some typos remain.
- Notation can be tightened (e.g., consistent use of fG vs fG∗; use of |G| in covering-number splits).
- The penalty derivation needs clearer exposition and correction (see above).
- State all assumptions explicitly before theorems; provide proof sketches for key steps (intrinsic structure, Prop. 3).

Originality:
- The formalization of intrinsic group additive structure and aiming to identify it is a nice contribution beyond standard additive/ANOVA models.
- Using RKHS capacity via covering numbers to penalize groupings in a direct-sum setting appears novel. That said, the idea of structural penalties increasing with interaction order is related to SALSA and hierarchical model selection; the paper should better differentiate from these and cite recent NeurIPS/ICML work on interaction discovery and additive decompositions.

Significance:
- If made rigorous and computationally robust, the approach could be useful for interpretable nonparametric modeling with interactions at the group level, mitigating the curse of dimensionality. However, the current computational scaling, sensitivity to tuning, and limited empirical validation constrain near-term impact. Strengthening theoretical conditions, providing a consistent and scalable search (e.g., dynamic programming for bounded group size, mixed-integer formulations, or submodularity results), and thorough comparisons would increase significance.

Pros:
- Clear motivation for group-level interactions; interpretable target.
- Concept of intrinsic group structure is appealing.
- RKHS-based structural penalty with some consistency theory.
- Simple algorithms and some positive empirical indications.

Cons:
- Gaps/ambiguities in theory (intrinsic structure existence; penalty exponent change; missing assumptions).
- Heuristic tuning and sensitivity; no guidance on µ, α.
- Computational tractability not addressed beyond a greedy heuristic with no guarantees.
- Limited empirical comparisons and analysis.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify and prove Theorem 1 under explicit conditions; discuss necessity of independence or alternative conditions.
- Fix the penalty derivation; either retain the theoretically justified exponent or justify the reparameterization and specify how µ, α scale with n to ensure consistency.
- Provide a representer-theorem formulation and complexity analysis for (7).
- Add competitive baselines and predictive metrics; include higher-p scenarios and scalability experiments.
- Explore principled search strategies (e.g., bounded group sizes, regularized objective with convex relaxations, or information criteria calibrated via Rademacher complexity).

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The problem is timely and the direction is promising, but theoretical clarity and empirical validation need strengthening to meet NeurIPS standards.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
