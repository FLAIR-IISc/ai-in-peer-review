Summary
This paper studies nonparametric regression models that are additive over groups of variables: f(X) = Σu∈G fu(Xu) for a partition G of {1,…,p}. The authors (i) formalize a notion of “intrinsic” group additive structure G*, the minimal partition among all partitions for which f admits such a decomposition; (ii) propose to estimate both G and f via RKHS-based kernel ridge regression with a structural penalty C(G) motivated by covering number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs; (iii) give probability bounds using covering numbers and claim selection consistency (P(Ĝ=G*)→1) under suitable λ, μ; (iv) provide an exhaustive-search algorithm for small p and a backward stepwise heuristic for larger p; (v) present simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

Relation to prior work: The work sits between additive models [Hastie & Tibshirani] and low-order interaction models (functional ANOVA [Gu], SALSA/ESP kernel [Kandasamy & Yu]). It connects to OKGT [Pan et al.] and to graphical models but targets group structure directly. The complexity-penalty is inspired by classical capacity measures in RKHS learning theory (Zhou, Kühn, Bartlett & Mendelson).

Strengths
- Problem formulation: Identifying a minimal partition that reveals interactions while retaining interpretability is a worthwhile goal.
- Conceptual contribution: The “intrinsic group additive structure” idea, and the partial order via refinement, are natural and potentially useful.
- Kernelization: Using direct-sum RKHSs with universal kernels is a principled way to estimate grouped components.
- Consistency direction: The attempt to ground structure selection in capacity-based penalties rather than purely ad hoc criteria is appealing.

Weaknesses and concerns

Quality (technical soundness)
- Intrinsic structure existence/uniqueness (Theorem 1): The paper asserts Ga is “partially ordered and complete” and therefore admits a unique minimal element. A subset of a lattice is not necessarily complete; the meet of two amiable partitions need not be amiable. The conditions under which a unique minimal amiable partition exists are not stated or justified. This is foundational and must be clarified or restricted.
- Penalty derivation vs implementation: The penalty C(G) is derived from ln N(ε, IG, d∞) ≲ Σu α(ε)|u|+1, then “simplified” to μ Σ α|u|. This drops the +1, absorbs constants and ε-dependence into μ, and in experiments treats α as an integer grid hyperparameter. This disconnect undermines the theoretical motivation: the algorithm effectively penalizes group size with an ad hoc exponential weight; the dependence on ε, r, kernel constants, and d is ignored.
- Proposition 3 (covering number inequality): The bound ln N(ε, IG, d∞) ≤ Σ ln N(ε/|G|, Iu, d∞) is plausible but nontrivial; no proof or assumptions (e.g., norm constraints on each component, sup-norm vs L2 embedding, bounded domain) are given. The scaling ε/|G| appears without derivation.
- Generalization bounds: Results invoke sup-norm covering numbers on C(X) but the paper alternates between X compact ([0,1]^p) and unbounded distributions (Gaussian) in experiments. Conditions for boundedness of functions, noise (sub-Gaussian?), and kernel universality (on compact X) are not stated, yet are needed.
- Identifiability within a fixed partition: As with additive models, component functions are not unique without centering/marginal constraints (e.g., Ef_u(X_u)=0). These constraints are not specified, which affects both estimation and theoretical arguments.
- Consistency conditions: Theorem 4 assumes “λn ∗ n → 0” (unclear notation), but does not specify rates for μn, α, or how data-driven selection via validation preserves consistency. The results bound empirical risks but do not derive a criterion that the implemented penalty actually optimizes.
- Algorithms: Exhaustive search over partitions is infeasible beyond tiny p (Bell numbers). The backward stepwise algorithm is under-specified (single pass? iterate to convergence?), has no guarantees, and is prone to local minima. No computational complexity analysis or approximations (e.g., Nyström) are provided for KRR within the search.

Clarity
- Numerous notation/typographical issues impede readability: inconsistent subscripts (HG vs HK,G), R̂g vs R̂, missing norms/spaces, “an square integrable”, “st Conference…”, etc. Many references lack years; formulas have mismatched braces; some terms (e.g., “amiable”) are nonstandard and could be replaced by “admissible/compatible.”
- Key details omitted: how λ is tuned; kernel choices per group; whether components are centered; how f is represented (sum kernel K_G=Σ K_u?); exact simulation settings (n, p, true groups); missing tables/figures referenced in the text.

Originality
- The grouping notion relative to additive and low-order ANOVA is interesting, and the attempt at a capacity-derived penalty is novel. However, closely related literature (e.g., sparse additive models with interactions, kernel additive models with learned interactions, hierarchical testing for interactions) is not discussed, making it hard to position the contribution versus prior NeurIPS/ICML work.
- The final practical penalty reduces essentially to a size-based term Σ α|u|, which weakens the originality of the capacity-based argument.

Significance
- Without clear theoretical guarantees tied to the implemented criterion and without strong empirical validation, the potential impact is limited. No baselines (e.g., SALSA, OKGT, GAM+interactions, hierarchical/group lasso, kernel-ANOVA) are compared. The real-data result reports a partition but no predictive gains, stability analysis, or interpretation robustness.

Specific comments and questions
- Provide precise conditions under which Ga has a unique minimal element; otherwise, relax to “a” minimal element or characterize multiplicity.
- State and prove Proposition 3; clarify norm constraints and dependence on d and ε. Explain the ε → 0 limit and how it informs a finite-sample penalty.
- Align the implemented penalty with the theory or provide a new consistency proof for the actual criterion μ Σ α|u|.
- Impose and state identifiability constraints for component functions.
- Detail the representer theorem and implementation: is K_G(x,x′)=Σu K_u(x_u,x′_u) with a single λ? Are group-wise λ’s beneficial?
- Specify assumptions on X, Y, noise, kernels, and domains for the covering number bounds used in Propositions/Theorems.
- Provide comprehensive experiments with clear n, p, true G*, selection frequencies, runtime, and comparisons to strong baselines.

Pros
- Addresses an important middle ground between additive and fully interacting models.
- Attempts to tie structure learning to RKHS capacity control.
- Some theoretical development toward selection consistency.

Cons
- Foundational claims about intrinsic structure are under-justified.
- Gap between theory and the implemented penalty; unclear consistency.
- Algorithmic specification and scalability are weak; no baselines.
- Clarity and presentation issues; missing details and numerous typos.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising, but the current version has significant theoretical gaps, a misalignment between derivation and implementation of the penalty, under-specified and potentially non-identifiable estimation, and insufficient empirical validation. I encourage the authors to tighten the theory to match the implemented criterion, clarify assumptions and algorithms, add identifiability constraints, and provide thorough comparisons and ablations.
