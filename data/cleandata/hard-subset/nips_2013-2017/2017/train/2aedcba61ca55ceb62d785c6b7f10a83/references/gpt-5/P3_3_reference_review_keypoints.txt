- Paper studies nonparametric regression with group-additive structure, defines a partial order over partitions and the intrinsic minimal partition G*, proposes a structure-penalized RKHS estimator, proves selection consistency for fixed p, and gives algorithms plus experiments.

- Related work connections are missing/under-discussed: additive GPs, automatic compositional kernel learning (Duvenaud et al.), multiple/hierarchical kernel learning (Bach), SALSA, OKGT, SS-ANOVA, and group-sparse additive models.

- Strength: clear formalization of group-additive structures and the unique intrinsic partition G* (existence/uniqueness is conceptually useful).

- Strength: natural use of RKHS direct sums to model group-wise components and a principled idea to penalize structural capacity.

- Strength: complexity penalty linked to Gaussian RKHS covering numbers, producing group-size–dependent complexity measures.

- Strength: theoretical separation between amiable and non-amiable structures and a fixed-p selection consistency result.

- Weakness: penalty derivation is incoherent—changing coverage exponent from |u|+1 to |u| by absorbing constants into μ is unjustified and affects growth across group sizes.

- Weakness: step from Proposition 3 to the final penalty lacks precise constants and a correct handling of dependence on |G|; needs proof or citation.

- Weakness: identifiability/separation assumptions are under-specified—results likely require independence/product-measure assumptions on X and should state distributional conditions explicitly.

- Weakness: generalization bounds ignore kernel bandwidths, eigenvalue decay, and data-dependent complexities; the role of λ and constants in the bounds are unclear and need derivation or references.

- Weakness: consistency theorem gives no guidance on how μ should scale with n, λ, or p, limiting practical utility.

- Originality: intrinsic partition formalization is novel, but penalizing structure via RKHS capacity overlaps with MKL/hierarchical kernel and additive GP literature and needs clearer positioning.

- Significance: problem is important, but empirical validation is weak—limited simulations, no strong baselines, and a single real-data case without predictive benchmarks.

- Clarity: notation drifts (e.g., R̂ variants, 1/n factors) and Algorithm 2 is ambiguous about move definitions, iterations, merges/splits, and convergence.

- Experiments: missing baselines and ablations (λ, μ, α), no tests for correlated covariates, ad hoc treatment of α, and limited reporting of figures/tables and failure-mode analysis.

- Suggested fixes: rectify penalty derivation or adopt a data-dependent penalty (Rademacher/empirical entropy), state distributional assumptions, provide μ/λ selection guidance or rates, and strengthen Algorithm 2 (clear moves, multiple passes, complexity analysis).

- Suggested fixes: compare against strong baselines (SALSA, SS-ANOVA, additive GP/kernel learning, SpAM variants), test correlated predictors, and discuss per-group kernel choice and bandwidth selection.

- Pros for acceptance: neat conceptual formalization, principled RKHS-capacity-based approach, consistency under fixed p, and practical motivation for learning low-order interactions.

- Cons for acceptance: technical gaps in penalty construction, unspecified identifiability assumptions, weak empirical validation, heuristic/underspecified algorithm, and limited tuning/scalability guidance.

- Overall verdict: interesting and promising direction but current manuscript has technical gaps, unclear assumptions, and insufficient experiments—recommend rejection pending substantial revision.
