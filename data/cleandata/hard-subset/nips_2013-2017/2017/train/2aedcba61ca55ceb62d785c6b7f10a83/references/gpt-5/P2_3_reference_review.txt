Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles nonparametric regression when the regression function admits a group additive structure: f(X) = sum over groups u of fu(Xu), where groups partition the predictors. This sits between fully additive models (no interactions) and functional ANOVA/high-order interaction models (many interactions) by allowing interactions within small groups but not across groups. The authors (i) formalize a partial order over group structures and define the intrinsic group additive structure (the unique minimal amiable structure that represents f), (ii) propose to estimate f and identify its group structure simultaneously by kernel ridge regression in direct-sum RKHSs with an additional structural penalty based on capacity (covering numbers), and (iii) prove a selection consistency result for recovering the intrinsic structure under suitable conditions. They provide an exhaustive-search and a greedy backward splitting algorithm, and illustrate on synthetic data and Boston Housing.

This connects to several literatures: additive/nonparametric regression (Hastie & Tibshirani), functional ANOVA and ESP kernels (Kandasamy & Yu), kernel methods and covering numbers, OKGT (Pan et al.) which motivated the group notion, and graphical models (for conditional dependence vs. group additivity). There is also closely related work in sparse additive models and RKHS model selection (e.g., COSSO; SpAM; multiple kernel learning and group-sparsity for additive components) that should be discussed to situate novelty.

Quality
- Theory: The definition of intrinsic group structure via a partial order is clean and useful. The capacity-based penalty is motivated by covering number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs; Proposition 3 relating covering numbers of the sum space to those of components is plausible (proof deferred). Theorems 2–4 establish that, with universal kernels and appropriate tuning (λn, µ), the penalized estimator selects the intrinsic structure with probability → 1 for fixed p. However:
  - The penalty form is unclear/inconsistent. The derivation suggests C(G) ≍ sum_u α^{|u|+1} (or α(ε)^{|u|+1}), but (6) shows µ sum_j α|u_j| (is this α^{|u_j|} or α·|u_j|?). This ambiguity affects both implementation and theoretical interpretation.
  - The dependence on ε and r in α is non-operational; in practice α is tuned, but the consistency result relies on a “proper” µ without a constructive, data-driven choice or rates.
  - Proposition 1’s strict inclusion requires predictor independence; many practical settings have correlated X. It is not clear how identifiability or selection behaves under dependence, and experiments mostly consider independent X.
  - Assumptions for the covering number bounds (convolution kernels, exponential Fourier decay) and the universality claims are only partially specified; the theory would benefit from a consolidated assumptions section and a statement clarifying which kernels and distributions are covered.

- Algorithms/experiments: The exhaustive search is exponential (Bell numbers), so scalability hinges on the greedy algorithm. The greedy backward splitting is simple but lacks theoretical guarantees; empirical results show it can miss true structures (Model 4). Experiments are limited:
  - Synthetic: 5 models with n, p, noise described, but kernel choices, bandwidths, and λ tuning are not detailed. Evaluation focuses on recovery frequency rather than prediction or estimation error, and there are no comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., additive with pairwise interactions via KRR/ESP kernel, SALSA, COSSO/SpAM, hierarchical interaction selection).
  - Real data: Only Boston Housing, with no comparison or statistical robustness; the selected groups are plausible but anecdotal.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-organized, with a helpful conceptual build-up from definitions to penalty and algorithms. Notable clarity issues:
- The structural penalty is inconsistently notated (α exponent vs. multiplication). Please fix and precisely define α (what it depends on; how it’s set).
- Some notation/typos (e.g., subscripts/superscripts in f̂λ,G∗; inconsistent use of r and ε; missing constants) should be cleaned.
- A dedicated assumptions section for theorems would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- Positive: The explicit notion of intrinsic group additive structure and the use of covering-number-driven penalties to select among groupings of variables are novel and interesting. The direct-sum RKHS capacity control to encode structure complexity is a fresh angle compared to l1/group-lasso penalties.
- Needs positioning: There is substantial related work on selecting additive components/interactions in RKHS or spline spaces via sparsity norms (COSSO; SpAM; MKL with per-component RKHS norms; hierarchical interaction selection). The paper should situate how its penalty and identifiability notion differ and what advantages they confer. Also relate more deeply to SALSA/ESP-kernel methods in terms of complexity vs. flexibility.

Significance
The problem is important: discovering interacting blocks can balance flexibility and interpretability and mitigate dimensionality issues. The paper’s conceptual contribution (intrinsic group) and a path to consistent selection are valuable. However, practical significance is limited by:
- Scalability: Exhaustive search is intractable; the greedy method is heuristic without guarantees. No runtime/scale experiments (e.g., p ≥ 50–100).
- Hyperparameter dependence: Success hinges on µ and α tuning; guidance is minimal.
- Empirical validation: Lack of baselines and limited datasets make it hard to assess gains over established methods.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and unique minimal (intrinsic) structure concept.
- Principled penalty grounded in RKHS capacity and a selection consistency result.
- Bridges kernel methods and structural model selection in a nonparametric setting.
- Simple algorithms and some empirical support.

Cons
- Structural penalty definition and implementation unclear; theoretical assumptions scattered.
- No constructive selection of µ/α; dependence on ε undermines interpretability.
- Limited, mostly small-scale experiments without comparisons; weak evidence of practical advantage.
- Greedy algorithm lacks guarantees and can fail; scalability not demonstrated.
- Independence assumption plays a role; behavior under correlated covariates unclear.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify the exact form of C(G): is it µ sum_j α^{|u_j|+1}, µ sum_j α^{|u_j|}, or µ α sum_j |u_j|? What is α, concretely, in implementation?
2. What kernels and bandwidth selection procedures were used for each group? How is λ chosen in the reported experiments?
3. How does the method fare with correlated predictors? Can Proposition 1 and selection consistency be extended beyond independence?
4. Can you compare to baselines (SALSA, pairwise KRR with ESP kernel, COSSO/SpAM, hierarchical interaction selection) on recovery and prediction?
5. Do you have any theoretical or empirical guidance for µ, α scaling with n, p, and group sizes?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The conceptual and theoretical contributions are promising, but the penalty ambiguity, limited empirical validation, and lack of comparisons/scalability evidence reduce confidence in practical impact. With clarified penalty, stronger baselines, and more robust experiments (including correlated X and larger p), this could become a strong contribution.
