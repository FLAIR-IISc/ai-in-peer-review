## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying the group additive structure in nonparametric regression, a crucial step towards achieving both efficiency and interpretability in high-dimensional settings. The authors acknowledge the non-uniqueness of group additive structures and propose a method to identify the “intrinsic” structure – a minimal, well-defined structure. They formulate a penalized kernel ridge regression problem incorporating a novel complexity measure for group structures based on covering numbers of RKHSs, and develop both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms for identification.  Theoretical consistency results are presented, and the method is evaluated through simulations and a real-data application.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Identifying relevant variable groupings can drastically improve the performance and interpretability of nonparametric models in high dimensions.
* **Well-motivated approach:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches (fully additive models, functional ANOVA) and position their work as a valuable middle ground.  The concept of an "intrinsic" group structure is a useful theoretical construct.
* **Theoretical foundation:** The paper provides consistency guarantees for the proposed method, demonstrating a solid theoretical grounding. The use of covering numbers to define structural complexity is mathematically sound, although its practical implications require careful consideration (see weaknesses).
* **Algorithmic contributions:** Providing both an exhaustive search and a stepwise algorithm expands the applicability of the method to varying problem sizes.
* **Empirical validation:** Simulations and a real-data example demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the approach.  The Boston Housing dataset application provides a tangible illustration of the identified group structure.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the complexity measure:** While theoretically elegant, the proposed complexity measure (based on covering numbers) is computationally demanding and potentially difficult to interpret. The connection between the theoretical covering number and actual performance in practice is not fully explored. The tuning parameter α appears sensitive and its impact should be investigated further.
* **Computational cost:** The exhaustive search algorithm is only practical for small p. While the stepwise algorithm is offered as a solution for larger p, its performance (especially the potential for getting stuck in local optima) isn’t rigorously evaluated and the simulation results for the stepwise approach are less convincing than the exhaustive search.
* **Limited exploration of alternative complexity measures:** The paper focuses heavily on the covering number-based complexity. A brief discussion of the advantages and disadvantages of alternative complexity measures (e.g., based on model size or information criteria) would strengthen the work.
* **Lack of comparison to existing methods:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing variable selection/grouping methods, such as sparse partial correlations or blockwise sparse regression, particularly in the context of nonparametric regression. It’s not immediately clear how this approach outperforms other state-of-the-art methods.
* **Clarity in the real-data section:**  The discussion of the Boston Housing data results is somewhat brief.  Providing a more detailed explanation of the identified groups and their implications for housing price prediction would enhance the impact of the application.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and theoretically well-motivated approach to identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression. The concept of the "intrinsic" group structure and the RKHS-based complexity penalty are novel contributions.  However, the computational complexity of the proposed methods and the limited empirical comparison to existing techniques raise some concerns.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novelty: The proposed approach and the theoretical framework are original contributions.
* Theoretical soundness: The consistency results are a strong point.
* Potential impact: Identifying appropriate group structures can significantly improve the performance and interpretability of nonparametric models.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost: The methods may not be scalable to high-dimensional problems without further optimization.
* Limited empirical evaluation: The comparisons to existing methods are insufficient.
* Complexity measure: The practical utility of the covering number-based complexity measure requires further investigation.




**Rating:**  **Weak Accept.** 

I believe this paper has merit and represents a valuable contribution to the field. However, addressing the weaknesses related to computational cost, empirical evaluation, and the complexity measure would significantly strengthen the work and make it more likely to have a broader impact.  I would encourage the authors to consider these points in a revision.
