

# Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in nonparametric regression: the identification of group additive structures. While additive models (which assume no interactions between predictors) and functional ANOVA models (which allow all interactions) have been extensively studied, the authors propose a middle ground: group additive models where predictors are partitioned into groups such that interactions occur within groups but not across groups. The key theoretical contribution is the definition of the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group partition that can represent the true function, which they prove is unique. The authors then propose a penalized kernel method using a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers to identify this structure. The paper includes theoretical consistency results, two algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise), and empirical validation through simulations and a real data application on Boston Housing.

## Strengths

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by formally defining and proving the uniqueness of the intrinsic group additive structure, addressing a key limitation in previous work on group additive models (e.g., [11]). The use of RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure for group structures is innovative and well-motivated, with the authors carefully connecting this to the capacity of the function space. The theoretical consistency proofs (Theorems 2-4) are rigorous and well-justified, providing strong theoretical support for the proposed method.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed. The simulation studies across five different models demonstrate the method's ability to identify true group structures, with the authors carefully documenting both successes and limitations (e.g., Model 1's performance degradation). The real data application on Boston Housing effectively illustrates the practical utility of the method, with the group structure identified being interpretable and consistent with domain knowledge.

The paper also provides a clear connection to related work, particularly in distinguishing their approach from graphical models and functional ANOVA, while acknowledging the limitations of previous methods like OKGT [11]. The discussion of how the group additive model lies "in the middle" between the standard additive model and higher-order interaction models is insightful and well-articulated.

## Weaknesses

While the paper makes important contributions, several weaknesses limit its impact:

First, the paper does not adequately address the computational complexity of the exhaustive search algorithm for large p. The authors note that "when a model contains a large number of predictor variables, such exhaustive search suffers high computational cost," but provide no concrete analysis of how the computational burden scales with p. For a method targeting high-dimensional problems, this is a significant omission.

Second, the tuning parameter selection strategy is underdeveloped. The paper relies on grid search with a validation set (Algorithm 1) but does not provide guidance on how to choose the appropriate grid or how the method would scale to very high-dimensional problems. The authors acknowledge that "the middle panel shows that the performance of Model 1 deteriorated" but do not provide a thorough explanation for this, which undermines confidence in the method's robustness.

Third, the connection to graphical models is mentioned but not fully developed. The authors note a relationship but don't explore how their method might complement or differ from graphical model approaches to interaction identification, which could have strengthened the paper's significance.

Fourth, the paper doesn't adequately address the case where the true group structure has a large number of groups (e.g., p groups when p is large). The complexity measure C(G) = ∑ α^{|uj|} suggests that the method would favor smaller group sizes, but the paper doesn't discuss how this might affect performance when the true structure has many small groups.

## Quality and Significance Assessment

The paper is technically sound, with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough empirical validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the potential performance issues in Model 1 and the computational challenges of the exhaustive search. The method represents a meaningful advance over previous work by providing a principled approach to identifying group structures rather than assuming them.

The significance of the work is substantial. High-dimensional nonparametric regression remains a challenging problem, and the group additive model offers an attractive middle ground between the standard additive model (which assumes no interactions) and functional ANOVA (which becomes computationally intractable for large p). By providing a method to identify the group structure rather than assuming it, this work directly addresses a key limitation in previous approaches. The practical application to the Boston Housing dataset demonstrates the method's real-world utility.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to nonparametric regression with clear practical implications. While the computational considerations and tuning parameter selection could be strengthened, the core method is novel, well-justified, and effectively validated. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria:

- **Quality**: High - Theoretical foundation is rigorous, empirical results are thorough.
- **Clarity**: High - Well-organized, clearly written, with precise mathematical definitions.
- **Originality**: High - The intrinsic group additive structure concept and complexity measure are novel.
- **Significance**: High - Addresses a meaningful gap in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of computational complexity and tuning parameter selection, but these are minor issues that could be addressed in the camera-ready version. The contribution is significant enough to warrant publication in NIPS, and the method has clear potential for adoption by practitioners working with high-dimensional nonparametric regression problems.

## Pro/Con for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Rigorous definition of intrinsic group additive structure (novel theoretical contribution)
- Strong theoretical consistency results
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple models
- Clear connection to related work with thoughtful distinctions
- Practical application on real data with interpretable results

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of computational complexity for large p
- Underdeveloped tuning parameter selection strategy
- Incomplete exploration of the connection to graphical models
- Model 1 performance degradation not thoroughly explained

Overall, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and I believe it represents a meaningful contribution to the field of nonparametric regression.
