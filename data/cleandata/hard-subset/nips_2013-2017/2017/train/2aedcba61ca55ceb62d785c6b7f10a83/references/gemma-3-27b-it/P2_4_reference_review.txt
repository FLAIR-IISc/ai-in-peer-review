## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying the group additive structure in nonparametric regression, a crucial step towards improving interpretability and mitigating the curse of dimensionality. The authors build upon existing work on additive models and functional ANOVA, proposing a penalty-based approach that leverages RKHS theory to identify the "intrinsic" group additive structure – the most concise representation of the underlying function. They develop algorithms for identifying this structure (exhaustive search and a stepwise approach) and demonstrate their effectiveness through simulations and a real-data application.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Identifying structure in high-dimensional nonparametric regression is critical for both statistical efficiency and interpretability. The group additive model offers a compelling middle ground between fully nonparametric approaches and more restrictive global models.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical framework, including the definition of the "intrinsic" group additive structure and a consistency proof for their proposed method. The use of covering numbers to define a complexity penalty is a particularly strong aspect.
* **Well-Defined Methodology:** The penalized kernel regression approach is clearly formulated and builds logically upon established techniques.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations demonstrate the ability of the proposed method to recover the true group structure in various settings. The real-data application provides further evidence of its practical utility.
* **Connection to Graphical Models:** The paper acknowledges and clarifies the relationship between their work and graphical models, highlighting the specific advantage of directly targeting group additive structures.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** The theoretical framework, while rigorous, translates into a potentially complex implementation. The choice of kernels, tuning parameters (λ, µ, α), and potentially the computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm could present challenges in practice.
* **Limited Scalability:** While the stepwise algorithm is proposed for larger datasets, the simulations do not fully demonstrate its scalability to very high dimensions. The performance of the stepwise algorithm is also reported to be limited in one of the simulation settings (Model 4).
* **Sensitivity to Parameter Tuning:**  The simulation results show significant sensitivity to the choice of µ and α.  A more robust method for parameter selection or adaptive parameter tuning strategies could improve the practical applicability.
* **Assumptions:** The consistency proof relies on specific assumptions regarding the kernel and the smoothness of the underlying function.  A discussion of the robustness of the method to violations of these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods:** While the introduction mentions related work, the experimental section lacks a direct comparison to existing methods for nonparametric regression or structure identification (e.g., sparse estimation techniques, variable selection methods in functional ANOVA).

**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed simulations. However, the potential complexity of implementation and sensitivity to parameter tuning raise some concerns about its robustness.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the theoretical sections are dense and require careful reading.  More intuitive explanations of the covering number-based complexity measure would be helpful.
* **Originality:** The combination of RKHS theory, complexity penalization, and the focus on identifying the *intrinsic* group additive structure represents a novel contribution. 
* **Significance:** The proposed method addresses an important problem in nonparametric regression and has the potential to be useful for researchers and practitioners dealing with high-dimensional data. The improved interpretability offered by the group additive structure is a significant benefit.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* Rigorous mathematical framework and consistency proof.
* Promising empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the method.
* Potential for practical applications in various fields.

**Con:**

* Potential implementation complexity and sensitivity to parameter tuning.
* Limited demonstration of scalability to very high dimensions.
* Lack of direct comparison to existing methods.
* Assumptions underlying the theoretical guarantees might be restrictive.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The proposed method for identifying group additive structures is theoretically grounded and empirically validated. While there are some concerns regarding implementation complexity and scalability, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:

* Provide more detailed guidance on parameter tuning, potentially including adaptive strategies.
* Discuss the robustness of the method to violations of the underlying assumptions.
* Include a comparison to existing methods in the experimental section.
* Address the scalability concerns, possibly by exploring more efficient algorithms or providing further empirical evidence for the stepwise approach.
