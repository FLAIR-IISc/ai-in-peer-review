- Paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression f(x)=∑u∈G fu(xu), formalizes a partial order on partitions, defines an intrinsic minimal amiable partition, proposes KRR in a direct-sum RKHS with a structural penalty C(G), proves selection consistency, and gives exhaustive and backward stepwise algorithms with experiments.

- Relation to prior work: connects to additive models, functional ANOVA, ESP/SALSA, OKGT, and RKHS capacity literature but omits discussion of structured sparsity for kernels, HKL/MKL, SpAM, and MARS.

- Strength: clear conceptual contribution formalizing partial orders and targeting a minimal intrinsic structure bridging additive and full-interaction models.

- Strength: principled use of RKHS/direct-sum and representer theorem leading to a clean estimator tied to capacity measures.

- Strength: theoretical focus on identifiability and selection consistency rather than only prediction.

- Strength: interpretability—group-level small interactions balance complexity and interpretability.

- Weakness 1 (assumptions/uniqueness): Proposition 1’s strict inclusion may fail under dependent X; paper doesn’t discuss how dependence affects uniqueness/identifiability of G*.

- Weakness 1 (lattice/completeness): Claim that Ga is partially ordered and complete and that the meet of amiable partitions is amiable lacks justification/proof and is a key foundational gap.

- Weakness 2 (penalty derivation): Transition from covering-number bound ln N(ε,IG,d∞)≲∑u α(ε)|u|+1 to implemented penalty μ∑u α|u| is inconsistent; α’s dependence on ε/n and its tuning are not clearly defined or justified.

- Weakness 2 (consistency and tuning): Theorem 4 assumes choosing proper μ but gives no guidance on how μ (and α) should scale with n or an oracle inequality linking the penalty to risk bounds.

- Weakness 2 (λn regime): Required λn conditions (e.g., n·λn→0) differ from standard KRR theory, risking overfitting without justification.

- Weakness 3 (capacity bounds/domain): Covering-number bounds used require compact domains and specific kernels, yet experiments use unbounded inputs (Normal) without clarifying rescaling/truncation or applicability.

- Weakness 3 (technical detail): Proposition 3’s ε/|G| splitting in covering numbers needs a proof or reference and clarity on constants.

- Weakness 4 (algorithms/scalability): Exhaustive search is infeasible beyond small p; the backward stepwise heuristic is underspecified (split rules, passes, stopping, backtracking) and lacks theoretical guarantees.

- Weakness 4 (algorithm suggestions): Authors should consider more robust searches (best-split, repeated passes, merge-and-split, stochastic search) or convex relaxations via structured sparsity.

- Weakness 5 (empirical evaluation): Limited synthetic settings, missing comparisons to strong baselines (SpAM, SALSA, MARS, HKL/MKL, OKGT), no runtime analysis, and Boston Housing results lack predictive metrics or baselines.

- Clarity issues: Notational inconsistencies (α, ε), missing proof sketches for claims (Ga completeness), missing kernel/domain specifications and preprocessing details.

- Originality/significance: Identifying a minimal group-additive partition is novel and interesting; tying structure selection to RKHS capacity is principled, but theoretical foundations and empirical validation need strengthening.

- Questions for authors: Under what PX assumptions does the intrinsic structure exist and is unique; can you prove Ga is closed under meets/refinement?

- Question: How should μ and α scale with n; can you provide an oracle inequality showing the penalized criterion recovers G* among amiable structures?

- Question: Reconcile the λn condition with standard KRR theory and clarify the domain/kernels where covering-number bounds apply.

- Question: Can you empirically compare against SALSA, SpAM, MARS, MKL/HKL, and OKGT for both recovery and prediction?

- Question: Can you better specify and improve the stepwise algorithm (multiple passes, best-split selection) and provide guarantees (e.g., monotone improvement or consistency under separability)?

- Pros summary: conceptually clean formulation, RKHS-based estimation with a principled complexity view, and a consistency objective.

- Cons summary: foundational/uniqueness assumptions not justified, penalty derivation ad hoc, λn/penalty scaling unspecified, limited experiments and sparse algorithmic detail.

- Recommendation: Reject in current form; promising direction but needs stronger theoretical rigor, clarified assumptions and penalties, robust scalable algorithms, and more comprehensive empirical validation.
