- Proposes identifying the intrinsic group additive structure G* in high-dimensional nonparametric regression, extending additive models to grouped interactions.
- Theoretical rigor: defines intrinsic group structure (Thm.1), proves uniqueness, and establishes estimator consistency (Thm.4).
- Introduces a novel complexity measure C(G) based on RKHS covering numbers, linking RKHS capacity to group complexity.
- Provides practical algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) with clear implementation details for different scales.
- Empirical validation via simulations and a Boston Housing application shows the method can recover group structures and aids interpretability.
- Limited empirical comparison to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., sparse additive models, graphical approaches) and lacks benchmark comparisons.
- Theoretical assumptions on RKHS covering numbers (e.g., Gaussian kernel conditions) may be restrictive or unrealistic in practice.
- Computational scalability is insufficiently addressed: no complexity analysis or runtime experiments, raising concerns for p > 100.
- Practical tuning: paper does not give robust guidance for selecting λ, μ, and α, beyond a validation-set approach that may fail with small samples.
- Interpretability claims are under-supported; the Boston Housing example is superficial and doesn't show clear domain-specific insights.
- Overall assessment: technically strong and novel formalization of intrinsic group structure but practical advantages over existing methods are not fully demonstrated.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions focusing on expanded empirical comparisons, clearer scalability/complexity discussion, and stronger interpretation of results.
- Pros summary: rigorous theory, novel intrinsic group concept, principled complexity measure, and practical algorithms with empirical checks.
- Cons summary: limited comparisons, potentially restrictive theoretical assumptions, unclear scalability, and weak empirical interpretability evidence.
