- Paper formalizes group additive models f(X)=∑u∈G fu(Xu), defines a partial order on group structures, and proves existence/uniqueness of an intrinsic minimal group structure G*.
- Proposes a structural penalty for selecting G* based on covering-number capacity bounds for direct-sum RKHSs and proves selection consistency for the global minimizer under fixed p.
- Presents two algorithms: exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic, with simulations and a Boston Housing case study showing plausible grouping recovery.
- Situates work between fully additive and high-order interaction models and contrasts with OKGT by directly addressing identifiability and structure selection.
- Links the structural penalty to classical RKHS capacity results (covering numbers and learning theory).
- Distinguishes focus from sparse/additive and hierarchical interaction methods by targeting discovery of variable partitions (grouping) rather than sparsity over coordinates.
- Strength: clear formalization of group-additive identifiability and unique intrinsic minimal structure.
- Strength: principled connection of group complexity to RKHS covering numbers; Proposition 3 is useful.
- Strength: theoretical selection consistency for the global penalized estimator under suitable conditions.
- Strength: practical appeal in learning small interacting groups for interpretability and avoiding combinatorial explosion.
- Strength: empirical results suggest recoverability of true groupings on synthetic data and interpretable groupings on real data.
- Weakness: derivation of implemented penalty C(G)=∑u α|u| is loose—drops constants, ε-dependence, and kernel-specific terms; bridge between theory and implemented penalty is unclear.
- Weakness: assumptions (e.g., independence of Xj) are not clearly stated across results; need clarity on PX and kernel conditions.
- Weakness: algorithmic guarantees only for global minimizer; no consistency guarantees for the greedy stepwise heuristic, which can fail in practice.
- Weakness: experiments lack comparisons to relevant baselines (ESP-KRR, SALSA, OKGT, sparse additive/interaction methods), runtime/complexity measures, sensitivity analyses, and robustness to correlated predictors.
- Weakness: practical implementation details (kernel choices, bandwidths, tuning of λ/α/µ, CV procedure) are underspecified.
- Clarity: penalty derivation, constant tracking, and dependence on group dimension |u| need clearer presentation; Algorithm 2 moves are described too vaguely.
- Originality: novel identifiability notion and linking structural penalty to RKHS capacity are interesting and distinct from prior structure-selection work.
- Significance: approach is promising for interpretable nonparametric modeling but limited by fixed-p theory and heuristic search scalability.
- Questions for authors: provide consistency theorem for the implemented penalty or guidance on α; clarify assumptions on PX and extension to correlated predictors.
- Questions for authors: detail kernel and bandwidth selection per group and whether the same kernel is used across groups.
- Questions for authors: provide empirical comparisons to ESP-KRR, SALSA, OKGT, and sparse interaction models on synthetic setups.
- Questions for authors: discuss improvements to Algorithm 2 (random restarts, beam search, merge-split moves) and empirical scaling with p.
- Cons: theoretical–practical gap in penalty form, missing guarantees for heuristic search, limited experiments and unclear assumptions.
- Minor: fix typographical/formatting issues, define constants in bounds, and ensure equations track dependence on |u|.
- Recommendation: borderline—interesting conceptual contribution but needs clearer assumptions, stronger empirical comparisons, and tighter linkage between theory and implemented methods.
