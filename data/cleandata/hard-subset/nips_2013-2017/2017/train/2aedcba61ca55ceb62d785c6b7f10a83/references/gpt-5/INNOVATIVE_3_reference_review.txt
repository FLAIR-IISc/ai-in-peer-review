Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles nonparametric regression by introducing and formalizing “group additive structures” (GA): f(X) decomposes as a sum of functions each depending on a disjoint group of predictors. This sits between classical additive models (no interactions) and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models (potentially many interactions). The authors (i) formalize a partial order over group structures and define the intrinsic (minimal) group additive structure G*, prove its existence and uniqueness, (ii) propose a structure-penalized RKHS method to simultaneously estimate f and identify G*, and (iii) derive selection consistency under universal kernels and a covering-number-based structural penalty. They also provide an exhaustive-search algorithm for small p and a backward stepwise heuristic for larger p, with simulations and a Boston Housing demo.

This builds on OKGT (Pan et al., KDD’15), which suggested group-based transformations but did not address identifiability or systematic estimation of the grouping. It contrasts with functional ANOVA (Gu; Ramsay & Silverman) and SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu, ICML’16) that enumerate interactions up to order D (often leading to combinatorial explosion). The work is also related to graphical models, but targets GA directly rather than conditional dependence.

Strengths:
- Novel conceptual contribution: a clean formalization of the intrinsic group additive structure with a clear partial order over partitions. This is a compelling middle ground between additive and full interaction models and addresses an important interpretability–flexibility tradeoff.
- Methodological idea: a structural penalty derived from RKHS capacity (via covering numbers) mapped to the direct sum RKHS of a candidate partition. The bridge from statistical learning theory (covering numbers) to a practical structure selection criterion is creative.
- Theory: selection consistency for recovering G* with a structure penalty; clear separation of amiable vs non-amiable structures; use of universal kernels to argue density and unbiasedness. The uniform risk control over amiable structures is technically interesting.
- Practicality: two algorithms, an exhaustive enumeration for small p and a backward stepwise splitting heuristic for larger p; simple hyperparameterization (μ, α) and use of KRR.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Capacity penalty choice is ad hoc in places. The derivation starts from ln N(ε, I(HK,r)) bounds like C(ln(r/ε))^{p+1} for convolution kernels, then moves to a surrogate penalty μ∑ α^{|u|} (dropping the +1 and absorbing constants/ε into α). This leap, while pragmatic, is not fully justified: α is treated as a tunable integer unrelated to kernel, bandwidth, or ε. Consequences:
  - Different partitions with identical group-size profiles receive identical penalties regardless of data geometry or kernel specifics.
  - The penalty no longer reflects known sharper Gaussian RKHS covering bounds or dependence on kernel bandwidth.
  Please clarify the precise conditions under which Proposition 3 and the subsequent simplifications hold, and provide guidance or ablations on α’s effect.
- Assumptions and scope of theory. Several key results rely on fixed p (finiteness of Ga) and, for strict inclusion of function spaces under different partitions (Proposition 1), independence of Xj. Real data often have correlated covariates; discuss whether uniqueness/consistency still hold or what identifiability conditions on PX are needed when X is dependent.
- Computational scalability. Searching over partitions is super-exponential; the stepwise algorithm is greedy and has no guarantees. In simulations, Model 4 is not well recovered, suggesting local minima. There is no complexity analysis or stopping rule discussion. Consider alternative search strategies (e.g., split–merge MCMC over partitions, beam search, submodular/approximate greedy criteria, or relaxations via structured sparsity with random features).
- Empirical evaluation is limited. There are no comparisons to strong baselines such as:
  - Sparse functional ANOVA or pairwise-additive models with hierarchical sparsity,
  - SALSA (which also combats the combinatorial burden),
  - SpAM and variants, or tree-based models capturing limited-order interactions.
  Without comparative baselines, it is hard to assess practical gains. Also, sensitivity to μ, α, and λ is not explored beyond grid search; the deterioration in Model 1 after validation suggests instability.
- Clarity/presentation. Some references lack years; typos appear; Algorithm 2 is underspecified (how splits are chosen; when to add vs split; multiple passes?); and details on kernel choices/bandwidths are sparse. Figures/tables are referenced but not shown in the main text.

Quality: The paper is generally technically sound and the main claims are backed by reasonable arguments. However, some derivations around the penalty are heuristic, and the empirical validation is underpowered.

Clarity: Mostly clear with good intuition, but the penalty construction and algorithmic details need tighter exposition. Fix missing bibliographic details and improve the algorithms’ descriptions.

Originality: High. The formalization of intrinsic GA and the RKHS-capacity-driven structural penalty is a fresh angle relative to prior additive/interaction modeling and OKGT.

Significance: Potentially significant. If scalable, consistent recovery of meaningful groups would be valuable for interpretable nonparametric modeling. Current empirical support is modest; more comprehensive experiments would strengthen impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Introduces a compelling and novel modeling concept (intrinsic GA) and connects it to RKHS capacity for structure learning.
- Provides nontrivial theory for selection consistency.
- Offers a principled alternative to functional ANOVA approaches with potential interpretability benefits.

Cons for acceptance:
- Structural penalty simplified to a tunable size-based surrogate; theoretical–practical gap not fully bridged.
- Limited experimental evaluation, no baseline comparisons, and scalability concerns.
- Some assumptions (fixed p, independence) and algorithmic details limit immediate applicability.

Suggestions:
- Empirically compare against SALSA, sparse pairwise-additive ANOVA with hierarchical penalties, and tree ensembles on synthetic and real datasets; report both structure recovery and prediction error.
- Provide ablations on α and μ; investigate data-driven α tied to kernel bandwidth or local complexities (e.g., local Rademacher complexity).
- Clarify identifiability under dependent X; add conditions ensuring minimality is well-defined in practice.
- Strengthen Algorithm 2 (multiple passes, best-first search, split–merge operations) and discuss complexity.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a bold, original direction with a solid theoretical backbone. Despite empirical and practical gaps, the conceptual contribution and consistency results justify inclusion, and I expect this work to spur follow-up on scalable algorithms and data-driven penalties.
