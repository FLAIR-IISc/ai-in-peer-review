- Addresses non-uniqueness in group additive models by defining the intrinsic group additive structure and proving its existence and uniqueness.  
- Proposes a structure-penalized kernel method using RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure to identify the intrinsic structure.  
- Provides theoretical consistency results (existence/uniqueness: Theorem 1; estimator consistency: Theorem 4) with proofs in the supplement.  
- Offers two algorithms for implementation: exhaustive search and a greedy backward stepwise procedure.  
- Empirical evaluation includes five synthetic models and a Boston Housing real-data application showing successful structure identification and interpretable estimates.  
- Technically rigorous and mathematically well-founded; definitions and proofs are clear and complete.  
- Minor quality concern: abrupt transition from the complexity measure theory to practical implementation; justification for using covering numbers could be deeper.  
- Well-organized and clearly written; examples effectively illustrate concepts (e.g., amiable structures).  
- Suggested clarity improvements: more intuition for the covering-number complexity measure, discussion of computational complexity, and a figure illustrating group vs. intrinsic structures.  
- Novel contribution: introduces the intrinsic group additive structure and a new structure-penalized kernel approach, filling a gap overlooked by prior work (e.g., OKGT).  
- Adequately situates work relative to related areas (functional ANOVA, graphical models) and clarifies differences.  
- Significant for high-dimensional nonparametric regression—improves estimation efficiency and interpretability via principled dimensionality reduction.  
- Strengths: addresses an important limitation, strong theory, practical algorithms, comprehensive empirical results, clear distinction from prior work.  
- Weaknesses: limited discussion of computational cost, need stronger motivation for the complexity measure, limited simulation variety, no direct comparisons with other group-identification methods.  
- Recommendation: accept (NIPS); work is technically sound, novel, and practically useful despite minor empirical and exposition shortcomings.
