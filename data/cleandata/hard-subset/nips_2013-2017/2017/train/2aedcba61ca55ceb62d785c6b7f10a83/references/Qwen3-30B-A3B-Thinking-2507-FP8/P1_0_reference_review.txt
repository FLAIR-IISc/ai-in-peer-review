

# Peer Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: identifying group additive structures where predictor variables can be partitioned into groups such that the target function is additive across these groups (f(X) = f_u1(X_u1) + ... + f_ud(X_ud)). The authors make two key contributions: (1) they rigorously define the "intrinsic group additive structure" as the minimal group structure that can represent the true function, resolving the non-uniqueness issue of group additive structures; (2) they propose a method to identify this intrinsic structure using a structural penalty based on RKHS complexity. The paper builds on the additive model literature (e.g., [5]) and extends the group additive model proposed in [11] by addressing two key issues not addressed in that work: non-uniqueness of the group structure and lack of systematic identification approach.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical framework is well-developed with clear definitions (intrinsic group structure), rigorous proofs (Theorems 2-4), and appropriate use of RKHS theory. The authors carefully address the non-uniqueness issue by defining the intrinsic structure as the minimal amiable group structure, a significant theoretical contribution. The complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs is well-motivated and effectively used to penalize overly complex group structures. The empirical results are thorough, with comprehensive simulation studies across multiple models and a real data application. The theoretical consistency results (Theorem 4) are particularly strong, showing that the estimated group structure converges to the intrinsic structure as sample size increases. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., stepwise algorithm's greedy nature), demonstrating honesty about the work's scope.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to empirical validation. The definitions (e.g., amiable group structures, intrinsic group structure) are clearly articulated, and key contributions are well-motivated. The two-step estimation procedure and algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) are described in sufficient detail. However, some technical details in Section 2.3.2 could be more accessible to readers less familiar with RKHS theory. The derivation of the complexity measure C(G) = ∑ α^|uj| could benefit from more intuitive explanation. The simulation results (Table 2) are clear, but the supplementary material could provide more detailed discussion of why Model 1's performance deteriorates when parameters are tuned.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution to the nonparametric regression literature. While group additive models were proposed in [11], the authors correctly identify that two key issues (non-uniqueness and lack of systematic identification) were not addressed. The concept of "intrinsic group additive structure" is novel and provides a clear theoretical foundation for structure identification. The use of RKHS complexity as a structural penalty is also novel in this context. The connection to graphical models is interesting but the paper clearly distinguishes its approach from conditional dependence structure approaches, which is a valuable contribution. The paper is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS work on additive models (e.g., [5], [6], [13], [16]) and extends the group additive model framework in a meaningful way.

## Significance
The problem addressed is highly significant for high-dimensional nonparametric regression, where the curse of dimensionality makes standard nonparametric methods infeasible. By identifying group structures that represent interactions, the method achieves a better balance between model flexibility and computational tractability. The approach maintains interpretability of additive models while accommodating interactions, representing a substantial improvement over both standard additive models and higher-order interaction models. The empirical results demonstrate successful recovery of true group structures across various settings, with clear practical implications for real-world high-dimensional regression problems. The Boston Housing data application effectively demonstrates the method's utility in practice. The approach could potentially be extended to other nonparametric regression settings, making it broadly applicable.

## Strengths
1. Rigorous theoretical foundation for intrinsic group additive structure
2. Novel structural penalty based on RKHS complexity
3. Comprehensive theoretical consistency results
4. Thorough empirical validation through simulations and real data
5. Clear distinction from related work (e.g., graphical models)

## Weaknesses
1. The stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) has limitations (e.g., Model 4's poor performance), and the paper doesn't sufficiently address how to overcome these limitations
2. The explanation of the complexity measure C(G) could be more intuitive for readers unfamiliar with RKHS theory
3. The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity in detail, which is important for large-scale applications
4. The tuning parameter selection (via validation) might be sensitive to the choice of validation set

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical and methodological contribution to high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The problem is well-motivated, the theoretical framework is rigorous, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper addresses important gaps in the group additive model literature and provides a principled approach to structure identification. While there are some limitations in the algorithmic implementation, these are acknowledged by the authors and represent opportunities for future work rather than fundamental flaws.

I recommend acceptance. The paper advances the state of the art in high-dimensional nonparametric regression with clear theoretical grounding and empirical validation, making it an excellent fit for NIPS. The work has the potential to significantly impact both theoretical and applied aspects of nonparametric regression in high-dimensional settings.
