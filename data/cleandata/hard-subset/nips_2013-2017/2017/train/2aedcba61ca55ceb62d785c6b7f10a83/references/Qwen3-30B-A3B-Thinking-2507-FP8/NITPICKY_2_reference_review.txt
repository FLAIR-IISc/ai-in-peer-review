

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary

This paper proposes a method for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, addressing two key limitations of previous work: the non-uniqueness of group additive structures and the lack of systematic identification methods. The authors define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal amiable structure and propose a penalized kernel method that incorporates a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers. The approach is theoretically justified with consistency proofs and evaluated through simulations and a real data application.

## Strengths

The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature on additive models and makes a valuable contribution by formalizing the concept of intrinsic group additive structure. The theoretical foundation using RKHS covering numbers is novel, and the two-step algorithm for structure identification is well-motivated. The simulation studies demonstrate reasonable performance on synthetic data, and the real data application provides interpretable results.

## Weaknesses

Despite the promising contribution, the paper contains several critical methodological and presentation flaws that undermine its scientific rigor:

1. **Technical inaccuracies**: The paper states "Two examples of universal kernel are Gaussian and Laplace" (Section 2.2). This is factually incorrect. The Laplace kernel does not yield a universal RKHS (its RKHS is not dense in L2), while the Gaussian kernel does. This fundamental error suggests a lack of careful technical understanding.

2. **Inconsistent domain assumptions**: The paper defines "X := [0, 1]^p be the p-dimensional unit cube" (Section 2.1), but the simulation studies use X ~ N(0,1) or Unif(-1,1) (Section 4). The theoretical results cannot be applied to these non-unit-cube domains without additional justification.

3. **Formatting and presentation errors**: 
   - The reference list contains inconsistent formatting (e.g., "The Journal of Machine Learning Research, 3:463–482, ," with trailing commas)
   - Section 3's Theorem 2 contains a critical typo: "2n 144" should be "2n/144" or "2n/144^2"
   - The paper uses "f̂" with diacritics throughout, which is non-standard in academic writing and would cause LaTeX compilation issues

4. **Unjustified complexity measure**: The paper defines C(G) = ∑d j=1 α^|uj| as the complexity measure (Equation 6), but fails to justify why this specific form is appropriate. The connection between the covering number bound and this simplified form is unclear and lacks theoretical justification.

5. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses both "G" and "Ĝ" for group structures (Section 2.3.1), creating ambiguity. The notation for the intrinsic structure G* is also inconsistent with the standard notation for minimal elements in posets.

6. **Missing algorithm details**: The paper references "Algorithm 1" and "Algorithm 2" but provides neither in the main text (only mentioning they're in supplementary materials). This violates NIPS's clarity requirements for reproducibility.

7. **Unsubstantiated claims**: The paper states "The intrinsic group structure can help much mitigate the curse of dimensionality" without providing any quantitative evidence or comparison to standard additive models.

8. **Typographical errors**: "X22" and "X 2 3" in the example (Section 2.1) should be "X2^2" and "X3^2". The phrase "st Conference" (first page) should be "The Conference".

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in nonparametric regression, the cumulative effect of these technical inaccuracies, formatting inconsistencies, and theoretical gaps significantly undermines the paper's scientific contribution. The fundamental error regarding universal kernels alone is sufficient to question the paper's technical rigor. The theoretical results in Section 3 depend on correct assumptions about kernel properties that are not properly established.

The paper's claims about the complexity measure and its relationship to covering numbers are particularly concerning due to the lack of justification. The authors should have provided a more detailed theoretical foundation for their proposed complexity measure before proceeding to the algorithmic development.

Given these issues, I recommend rejection. The paper contains promising ideas but fails to meet the technical standards expected at NIPS due to these avoidable errors. The authors would need to substantially revise the theoretical foundation, correct the technical inaccuracies, and provide a more rigorous justification for their complexity measure before the paper could be considered for publication.
