Summary
This paper studies nonparametric regression under a group-additive structure: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of predictors. The authors (i) formalize a partial order over groupings and define an intrinsic (minimal) group structure G* under which f admits a group-additive representation; (ii) propose to learn both G and f by penalized kernel regression over direct-sum RKHSs, with a structural penalty intended to reflect the capacity of the direct-sum space; (iii) give consistency results showing that the selected Ĝ converges to G* under certain conditions; and (iv) present exhaustive-search and backward stepwise algorithms, with simulations and a small real-data example.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned between additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani) and functional ANOVA/kernel interaction models (e.g., smoothing spline ANOVA, KRR with ESP kernel; SALSA). It also relates to OKGT (Pan et al.), which motivated group-wise transformations but did not address identifiability or structure recovery. However, there is substantial related work in kernel-based structured sparsity for additive and interaction models that is not discussed, notably hierarchical/multiple kernel learning frameworks that select variable groups and interactions with group-sparsity penalties (e.g., hierarchical kernel learning, Bach 2009), sparse additive models (Ravikumar et al., SpAM), and kernel-based dependence/interaction selection (e.g., HSIC-Lasso). These offer directly comparable baselines and theoretical tools.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for group-wise additivity as an intermediate between full additivity and high-order interactions.
- Formalization of a partial order on groupings and the notion of an intrinsic minimal group structure is conceptually neat.
- Use of direct-sum RKHSs is natural; the observation that capacity of the sum can upper-bound via covering numbers of component spaces is reasonable and leads to a simple penalty depending on group sizes.
- Theoretical aim is appropriate (selection consistency of Ĝ), and the paper provides high-level results contrasting amiable vs non-amiable groupings.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological soundness
- The structural penalty C(G) is only loosely justified. It is derived from asymptotic covering-number bounds for convolution kernels and then simplified to µ∑j α|uj| (after dropping a “+1” in the exponent and absorbing constants). This step is ad hoc: α is not data-driven, depends on an arbitrary ε via ln(r/ε), and the change from |u|+1 to |u| is not mathematically clean. The resulting penalty does not reflect kernel bandwidth, regularization, or covariate distribution, all of which affect effective capacity.
- The capacity-based justification assumes convolution kernels with exponentially decaying Fourier transforms; yet the paper also emphasizes universality. The constants in the covering-number bounds are intractable, undermining the practical relevance of the proposed surrogate.
- Identifiability/uniqueness of the “intrinsic” structure: Theorem 1 asserts a unique minimal element without clearly stated assumptions on X’s distribution. Earlier, independence was required to obtain strict inclusion of L2 spaces. With dependent covariates, minimality and uniqueness can be subtle; this needs explicit conditions and counterexamples/clarifications.
- Selection consistency (Theorem 4) hinges on a nonvanishing risk gap for non-amiable structures (Theorem 3) but no margin condition is stated. In practice, a model may be arbitrarily close to amiable under a wrong grouping, making the finite-sample problem hard; the theory should specify separation assumptions and rates.
- Algorithmic specification is incomplete. The stepwise procedure (Algorithm 2) is underspecified: how are candidate splits/merges enumerated per variable? What is the stopping rule? There is no complexity analysis; exhaustive search scales with Bell numbers and is infeasible even for moderate p.

Empirical evaluation
- Very limited. Simulations are toy-sized; there is no comparison to strong baselines, such as SALSA, functional ANOVA KRR with ESP kernels, hierarchical kernel learning/structured MKL, sparse additive/interactions (e.g., SpAM variants, hierNet for a linear baseline), or OKGT. Without such comparisons, it is hard to assess whether the proposed penalty actually improves structure recovery or prediction.
- Metrics focus on frequency of recovering the true grouping, but there is little reporting on predictive performance, robustness to covariate dependence, sensitivity to kernel/bandwidth/λ, or scalability. The failure of the stepwise algorithm on one model is acknowledged but not analyzed.
- Real-data analysis (Boston Housing) is purely exploratory, with no out-of-sample comparison to baseline GAMs or interaction models.

Clarity
- The manuscript has numerous typos and incomplete references (missing years, formatting), which hampers readability.
- Several key assumptions are missing or scattered (boundedness, noise model, kernel choices, bandwidth selection). The role and selection of α is unclear.
- Proposition 3 proof sketch is absent; the inequality for covering numbers of direct sums should be stated with explicit norms and constants.

Originality and significance
- The group-additive viewpoint is not entirely new (OKGT), and kernel-based structured sparsity for selecting groups/interactions is an active area with established methods. The main novelty claimed is the specific complexity penalty tied to covering numbers and the intrinsic-structure formalization. Given the weak practical grounding of the penalty and the limited experiments, the contribution feels incremental at best at this stage.
- Potential significance could be higher with a principled, data-driven structural complexity (e.g., Rademacher complexities/localized complexities/BIC-type criteria) and stronger empirical validation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide precise assumptions for the existence/uniqueness of G*, especially under dependence among X, and discuss identifiability limits.
- Revisit the structural penalty: either derive a criterion with clear finite-sample meaning (e.g., PAC-Bayes/Rademacher-based, MDL/BIC-style penalties with data-dependent complexity) or provide empirical evidence that the current surrogate correlates with generalization and selection accuracy. Clarify and justify the α parameter and the exponent.
- Flesh out Algorithm 2 (split/merge proposals, search strategy, stopping, complexity). Consider more powerful search (beam search, MIO, learned proposals) and warm-started KRR solvers.
- Substantially expand experiments: larger p (50–200), correlated covariates, varied SNR; include strong baselines (SALSA, ESP-KRR, HKL/structured MKL, SpAM, OKGT). Report both structure recovery and prediction, with runtime.
- Clarify kernel choices, bandwidth selection, λ, µ, α tuning, and provide code-level details for reproducibility.
- Improve writing and references; include key related work in structured kernel learning.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper presents an interesting direction and some theoretical framing, but the structural penalty is weakly justified, the theory lacks clear, verifiable assumptions and rates, the algorithms do not scale, and the empirical validation is insufficient and lacks comparisons to established baselines.

Confidence
Moderate.
