## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying the underlying group additive structure in nonparametric regression models, a crucial step towards improving interpretability and mitigating the curse of dimensionality. The authors propose a penalty-based approach within a kernel ridge regression framework, leveraging covering numbers to quantify the complexity of different group structures. They establish theoretical consistency guarantees for their method and demonstrate its effectiveness through simulations and a real-world application on the Boston Housing dataset. The work builds upon the existing literature on additive models, functional ANOVA, and kernel methods, aiming to bridge the gap between fully nonparametric approaches and overly complex interaction models.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Identifying underlying structure in high-dimensional nonparametric data is a critical challenge, and the group additive model provides a compelling intermediate approach.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, including the definition of an "intrinsic group additive structure" and a consistency proof for the proposed method.  The use of covering numbers as a complexity measure is a rigorous and well-motivated choice.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The authors propose two algorithms for identifying the group structure: an exhaustive search and a backward stepwise approach, catering to different computational constraints.
* **Empirical Validation:** Simulation studies and a real-data application demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed approach in recovering the true group structures.
* **Connection to Graphical Models:** The authors thoughtfully discuss the relationship between their work and graphical models, highlighting the differences and advantages of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** The covering number calculation, while theoretically sound, may be computationally demanding in practice, potentially limiting the scalability of the method.  The practical impact of this on real-world applications isn’t fully addressed.
* **Tuning Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method appears sensitive to the choice of the regularization parameters (λ, µ, α). While the authors employ a validation set, a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning strategies and sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Algorithm 2 Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of the backward stepwise approach, specifically its potential to miss the optimal structure due to its greedy nature.  Exploring alternative optimization strategies (e.g., forward selection, simulated annealing) might improve performance.
* **Limited Scope of Real Data Application:** While the Boston Housing dataset application provides a proof-of-concept, it would be beneficial to showcase the method's performance on more complex and higher-dimensional datasets.
* **Clarity in Some Sections:**  Certain sections, particularly those detailing the theoretical analysis (e.g., Proposition 3, Theorem 2), could benefit from improved clarity and more intuitive explanations.



**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous theoretical foundation and well-supported empirical results.  However, the computational complexity of the complexity measure and potential sensitivity to tuning parameters slightly detract from the overall quality.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally well-written, but some sections (particularly the theoretical derivations) could benefit from more accessible explanations.  The notation is generally consistent but can be dense.
* **Originality (8/10):**  The combination of kernel methods, group additive modeling, and covering number-based complexity regularization is novel. While the individual components are established, their integration and theoretical analysis represent a significant contribution.
* **Significance (7/10):**  The proposed method has the potential to be impactful in applications where interpretability and dimensionality reduction are crucial.  However, the computational challenges and sensitivity to tuning parameters may limit its widespread adoption.  Further validation on diverse datasets is needed to fully assess its significance.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a challenging and relevant problem in nonparametric regression.
* Provides a rigorous theoretical framework with consistency guarantees.
* Offers a practical algorithm for identifying group structures.
* Demonstrates promising results through simulations and a real-world application.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity of the complexity measure.
* Sensitivity to tuning parameters.
* Limitations of the stepwise algorithm.
* Limited scope of the real-data application.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors have presented a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression, addressing an important problem with a theoretically sound and practically relevant approach.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly providing a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning strategies and exploring alternative optimization algorithms – would further strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.  I believe the strengths of the work outweigh its weaknesses, and it represents a worthwhile addition to the NIPS proceedings.  Specifically, I’d recommend the authors include discussion of computational cost in their results and address the limitations of the stepwise algorithm further.
