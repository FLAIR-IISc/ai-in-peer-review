

# Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

This paper introduces a novel concept of "intrinsic group additive structure" for nonparametric regression, addressing two important gaps in the existing literature on group additive models. The authors define the minimal group additive structure that can represent a function and propose a method to identify this structure using a structural penalty based on RKHS complexity measures. The key novelty lies in formalizing the "intrinsic" structure concept and developing a complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs, which enables consistent identification of the true group structure.

The paper builds on the group additive model framework introduced in [11] but makes a significant conceptual leap by defining and identifying the minimal (intrinsic) group structure. Previous work on group additive models either assumed the structure was known or did not address the non-uniqueness issue. The authors' approach of using RKHS complexity as a penalty for structural simplicity is particularly elegant and novel. This directly addresses the problem of choosing between different amiable group structures by penalizing more complex (less concise) structures.

The theoretical contributions are sound, with consistency proofs for the proposed method (Theorems 2-4). The authors derive a tractable complexity measure C(G) = ∑j α|uj| based on covering numbers of RKHSs, which is a clever adaptation of existing capacity measures for RKHSs. The two-step algorithm (exhaustive search and stepwise) provides practical implementation, and the simulation studies demonstrate reasonable performance in identifying the true structure across various models.

The paper's significance lies in its potential to bridge the gap between simple additive models (which suffer from missing interactions) and complex functional ANOVA models (which suffer from the curse of dimensionality). By identifying the intrinsic group structure, the method can maintain the interpretability and efficiency of additive models while accommodating meaningful interactions. This addresses a real pain point in high-dimensional nonparametric regression where practitioners must choose between simple but potentially misspecified models and complex but computationally intractable ones.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical definitions and proofs are rigorous, and the connection between the group structure and RKHS complexity is well-motivated. The real data application on Boston Housing provides a meaningful illustration of the method's interpretability advantages.

However, there are some limitations to note. The simulation study is limited to only five models, and the real data example is quite small (13 predictors). The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison to alternative methods for structure identification (e.g., graphical models or interaction detection methods). While the theoretical consistency is established, the paper doesn't provide strong empirical evidence that their method significantly outperforms existing approaches in practice, particularly when compared to simpler methods that might approximate the group structure.

The connection to graphical models mentioned in the paper is somewhat superficial and doesn't add substantial value to the main contribution. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly distinguished their approach from related work in high-dimensional regression structure identification.

Despite these limitations, this paper makes a genuinely novel contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The concept of intrinsic group additive structure is both theoretically clean and practically relevant. The method provides a principled way to identify the simplest structure that can represent the true function, which addresses a fundamental problem in high-dimensional modeling. The authors have made a significant step toward making group additive models more practical for real-world applications.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be an exciting contribution that opens up new directions in nonparametric regression. The core idea of identifying the intrinsic group structure using RKHS complexity is bold and well-executed. While the empirical validation could be strengthened, the theoretical foundation is solid and the approach represents a meaningful advance over previous work.

The paper's significance is substantial: it provides a new framework for high-dimensional nonparametric regression that balances interpretability and flexibility. This could be particularly valuable in domains where model interpretability is crucial (e.g., healthcare, social sciences), where practitioners need to understand not just the predictions but the underlying relationships.

I recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. The novelty of the intrinsic group additive structure concept and the elegant method for identifying it are significant contributions to the field. While the empirical results are somewhat limited, the theoretical foundation is strong and the approach is sufficiently novel to warrant publication. This paper represents a clear step forward in making group additive models more practical and theoretically grounded.
