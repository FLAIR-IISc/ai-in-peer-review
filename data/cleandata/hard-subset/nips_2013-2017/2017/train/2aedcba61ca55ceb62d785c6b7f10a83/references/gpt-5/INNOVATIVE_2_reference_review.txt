Summary
This paper introduces Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI) for kernel nonparametric regression. The core idea is to model E[Y|X] as a sum over groups of variables rather than singletons (additive) or all subsets (functional ANOVA), striking a middle ground between flexibility and tractability. The authors (i) formalize the notion of amiable group structures and define a unique minimal “intrinsic group additive structure” (the most parsimonious partition consistent with f), (ii) propose a structure-penalized kernel ridge framework that estimates f while selecting the group partition, and (iii) derive a group-structure complexity penalty from covering numbers of direct-sum RKHSs, leading to an objective that trades off fit, RKHS norm, and a complexity term that increases with group sizes. They prove selection consistency for the intrinsic structure under appropriate conditions, and provide two algorithms: exhaustive search (small p) and a backward stepwise heuristic (larger p). Simulations and a Boston Housing case study illustrate feasibility.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned between generalized additive models [Hastie & Tibshirani], functional ANOVA/smoothing spline ANOVA [Gu], and recent interaction-learning approaches such as ESP kernels estimated by KRR [Kandasamy & Yu]. It also relates to OKGT [Pan et al.], but differs by explicitly defining and selecting the intrinsic group partition. Compared to SALSA/ANOVA kernels that include all interactions up to order D, GASI restricts interactions to within learned groups, reducing the number of components to estimate. There is also a connection to graphical models and block-structured GGMs, although GASI targets a different object (group partition driving the regression function rather than full conditional dependence structure).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure (minimal partition) is crisp and useful. Establishing existence/uniqueness and tying it to function spaces is elegant.
- Principled complexity penalty: deriving a structural penalty from covering number bounds of direct-sum RKHSs is a creative way to translate functional capacity into a combinatorial penalty over partitions.
- Consistency: the selection consistency result for the group structure (with universal kernels and suitable regularization) is theoretically appealing.
- Practicality: the framework uses standard KRR machinery and can leverage universal kernels (Gaussian, Laplace). The direct-sum RKHS formulation is clean, and the approach is interpretable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practicality of the complexity penalty: the bridge from covering number bounds to the implemented penalty µ∑j α|uj| is heuristic. α is introduced as ln(r/ε) but is then treated as a small integer tuned by validation. The dependence on unknown constants and ε suggests that the penalty may not faithfully reflect true capacity. An ablation comparing this penalty to simpler surrogates (e.g., µ1·#groups + µ2·∑|uj|, or exponential-in-size without α tuning) would help.
- Scalability/algorithms: exhaustive search is intractable for even moderate p. The backward stepwise scheme is greedy and can miss the true partition (as seen in M4). There is no global optimization guarantee, and computational complexity is not analyzed. The paper would benefit from scalable search (e.g., constrained max group size, MCMC over partitions, submodular relaxations, or mixed-integer formulations) and low-rank kernel approximations (Nyström, random features).
- Assumptions and clarity in theory: strict inclusion of function spaces (Prop 1) uses independence of covariates; it is unclear which parts of the main consistency theory require independence or what happens under dependence. Guidance for choosing λn and µn is limited. The role of universal kernels is central; practical sensitivity to bandwidth choice is not explored.
- Empirical evaluation: synthetic experiments show promise but are limited in scope, and the real data example is small and purely illustrative. There are no comparisons to strong baselines: SALSA/ESP kernel, smoothing spline ANOVA, pairwise additive models with KRR, SpAM, or HSIC-based interaction selection. Structure recovery and prediction metrics versus these baselines would substantiate significance.
- Clarity/polish: several typos, missing years in references, and notational inconsistencies (e.g., dropping “+1” in the exponent, transitions from covering bound to α-penalty) hinder readability. Some details of Algorithms 1–2 (e.g., how kernel parameters are set per group, computational cost per iteration) are missing.

Questions for the authors
- Can you clarify precisely which assumptions (e.g., independence) are needed for Theorems 2–4 versus only for Proposition 1? Does intrinsic structure remain identifiable under general dependence?
- How sensitive is selection to α and µ? Can α be tied to data-driven quantities (e.g., kernel bandwidth, effective degrees of freedom) instead of grid-tuning an abstract constant?
- How does GASI compare empirically to SALSA (ESP kernel), smoothing spline ANOVA, pairwise KRR with sparsity, and HSIC-Lasso interaction discovery in both recovery and prediction?
- What is the computational complexity of Algorithm 2 per iteration, and can you use kernel approximations to scale to n>10^4 and p>50?
- Are you constraining max group size? If so, does theory extend to this constrained search?
- Will you release code?

Pros
- Novel and appealing problem formulation (intrinsic group additive structure)
- Principled bridge from RKHS capacity to structural penalty; selection consistency
- Interpretability and a promising middle ground between additive and full interaction models

Cons
- Penalty instantiation is heuristic; constants unknown; limited guidance
- Algorithmic scalability and optimality concerns; limited experiments; no baselines
- Some theoretical assumptions and practical choices insufficiently clarified
- Presentation issues

Recommendation: Weak accept (leaning accept on novelty/significance). The paper introduces a compelling new target (intrinsic group partitions) with a principled, kernel-based selection mechanism and selection consistency. While the empirical and algorithmic parts are not yet fully mature and the penalty’s practical instantiation is somewhat ad hoc, the conceptual contribution is strong and likely to stimulate follow-up work on scalable search and alternative penalties. Strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying assumptions would make it a clear accept.
