### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of identifying interaction structures in high-dimensional nonparametric regression. It positions itself as a middle ground between simple additive models, which are interpretable but ignore interactions, and fully nonparametric or higher-order interaction models (like functional ANOVA), which capture interactions but suffer from the curse of dimensionality and are difficult to interpret. The authors propose using a "group additive model," of the form `f(X) = f_u1(X_u1) + ... + f_ud(X_ud)`, where `{u1, ..., ud}` is a partition of the predictor indices. This model assumes that interactions only occur between variables within the same group.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it formalizes the problem by defining the "intrinsic group additive structure" (G*) as the unique, minimal partition that can represent the true underlying function. This concept elegantly resolves the non-uniqueness issue where multiple groupings could describe the same function. Second, it proposes a method to simultaneously identify this intrinsic structure and estimate the component functions. This is framed as a penalized optimization problem using kernel methods in a direct sum of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The central novelty is the penalty term on the group structure itself, `µC(G)`. Third, this complexity penalty `C(G)` is not ad-hoc but is theoretically motivated and derived from the capacity of the corresponding RKHS, specifically using its covering number. Finally, the authors provide a theoretical proof of consistency, showing that their method recovers the true intrinsic group structure G* with probability approaching one as the sample size increases. They also propose two practical algorithms: an exhaustive search for low-dimensional problems and a backward stepwise algorithm for higher dimensions. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset.

This work builds upon prior work like Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) by Pan et al. [11], but critically addresses the problems of non-identifiability of the group structure and the lack of a systematic method for its identification.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical contributions are the main strength. The formalization of the "intrinsic group additive structure" (G*) provides a solid foundation for the problem. The derivation of the structural complexity penalty from the covering number of the associated RKHS is principled and elegant. The main theoretical result, the consistency of the structure selection (Theorem 4), is significant and provides strong justification for the proposed method. The authors are also honest about the limitations of their proposed greedy algorithm, noting its failure on one of the simulation models.
*   **Weaknesses:** The proof of consistency relies on certain assumptions, such as `λn * n -> 0`, and the practical choice of the tuning parameters `λ`, `µ`, and `α` relies on cross-validation over a grid, which can be computationally intensive. The connection between the theoretical upper bound for the covering number (Eq. 4) and the final, simplified penalty term `µ * sum(α^|u_j|)` (Eq. 6) involves some simplifications (e.g., reparameterizing `ln(r/epsilon)` as `α`, combining constants into `µ`) that, while practical, could be elaborated upon for greater clarity.

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly well-written and clearly organized.

*   **Strengths:** The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature. The example in Section 2.1 is very effective at illustrating the difference between amiable and intrinsic group structures. The overall flow from problem definition to the proposed method and theoretical validation is logical.
*   **Weaknesses:** Section 2.3.2, which introduces the complexity measure, is quite dense and may be difficult to follow for readers not intimately familiar with learning theory concepts like covering numbers and RKHS embeddings. A more intuitive explanation or a slightly more detailed derivation of how the final penalty term is reached would be beneficial. The description of the Basic Backward Stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is very high-level; for instance, "either split j as a new group or add to an existing group" is ambiguous about the set of possible moves considered at each step, which hinders reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and well-motivated approach to a known problem.

*   **Strengths:** While group additive models have been considered before (e.g., [11]), the key novelty lies in the formulation of a principled, data-driven method for *identifying* the underlying group structure. The use of RKHS capacity as a structural penalty is, to my knowledge, a new and insightful contribution in this context. It connects the complexity of the statistical model directly to the geometric complexity of the function space it resides in. This is a significant departure from more heuristic approaches to model selection.
*   **Weaknesses:** The individual components used—kernel ridge regression and backward stepwise selection—are standard techniques. The originality lies in their novel combination and, most importantly, the new penalty term that guides the selection process.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important problem with a potentially high-impact solution.

*   **Strengths:** The problem of discovering interaction structures in a flexible and interpretable way is fundamental in many scientific and industrial applications. This method provides a practical tool for moving beyond simple additive models without jumping to a fully unstructured, black-box model. The interpretability of the results, as demonstrated in the Boston Housing example, is a major advantage. Other researchers are likely to build on these ideas, perhaps by developing more scalable search algorithms or exploring alternative complexity penalties. The work clearly advances the state of the art in interpretable nonparametric modeling.
*   **Weaknesses:** The primary limitation is scalability. The exhaustive search is only feasible for a small number of predictors (`p`). The proposed backward stepwise algorithm is a heuristic that is not guaranteed to find the global optimum and, as the authors show, can fail. For modern high-dimensional settings where `p` is large, the utility of the proposed algorithms may be limited without further development.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Method:** The paper introduces a novel method for a significant problem, guided by a theoretically-grounded penalty derived from RKHS capacity.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The consistency proof for recovering the true group structure is a strong theoretical result that validates the approach.
3.  **Addresses a Clear Gap:** The work provides a compelling solution that sits between overly simple additive models and overly complex fully nonparametric models, balancing flexibility and interpretability.
4.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is well-written, provides a complete piece of work from theory to experiments, and clearly situates itself within the literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

1.  **Practical Scalability:** The proposed algorithms have significant scalability limitations. The exhaustive search is intractable for even moderate `p`, and the stepwise alternative is a greedy heuristic that can fail.
2.  **Clarity in Key Sections:** The derivation of the penalty term and the description of the stepwise algorithm could be clearer to improve reader understanding and reproducibility.
3.  **Computational Cost:** The reliance on grid search and cross-validation for three tuning parameters (`λ`, `µ`, `α`) makes the method computationally expensive, especially in conjunction with the combinatorial search over group structures.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of interpretable machine learning and nonparametric statistics. The formalization of the intrinsic group additive structure and the development of a theoretically-grounded, consistent method for its identification are impressive. While the practical algorithms have scalability issues, this is a common challenge for structure learning problems, and the authors are transparent about these limitations. The theoretical contribution alone is substantial enough to warrant publication. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
