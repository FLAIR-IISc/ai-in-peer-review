

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical gap in group additive models for nonparametric regression: the non-uniqueness of group additive structures and the lack of systematic methods to identify the intrinsic (minimal) group additive structure. The authors define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal structure among all amiable group additive structures for a given function, prove its uniqueness, and propose a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers to identify this structure. They develop a penalized estimation approach that combines kernel methods with a structural penalty term, and propose two algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) for implementation. The paper connects their work to existing literature on additive models [5, 13], functional ANOVA [4, 6], and the OKGT method [11], while distinguishing their approach from graphical models [2, 7, 9] by directly targeting group additive structures rather than conditional dependence structures.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors rigorously define key concepts (intrinsic group additive structure, amiable structures), prove their uniqueness (Theorem 1), and establish the consistency of their proposed method (Theorem 4). The theoretical analysis is thorough, with supporting lemmas and propositions that build a solid foundation for the method. The complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers is well-motivated and properly derived (Proposition 3), and the connection between the capacity measure and the group structure complexity is logically established. The empirical validation through simulation studies (Section 4) and real data application (Section 5) provides reasonable support for the method's practical utility. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., Model 4 performance in simulations) and discuss future work, demonstrating careful evaluation.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem definition to theoretical development to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The algorithms (Algorithms 1 and 2) are described in sufficient detail for reproducibility. However, some concepts (particularly the connection between the covering number complexity measure and its practical interpretation) could be clarified for readers less familiar with RKHS theory. The discussion of how their method differs from graphical models (Section 1) is helpful but could be more explicit about the advantages of their direct approach.

## Originality
The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature. While group additive models [11] and functional ANOVA models [4, 6] have been studied, the problem of identifying the intrinsic group additive structure has not been systematically addressed. The authors' definition of the intrinsic structure (Theorem 1) and their novel complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers (Section 2.3.2) represent substantial theoretical advances. The paper distinguishes itself from previous work by focusing on the minimal structure rather than higher-order interactions, and by directly targeting group additive structures rather than inferring them from conditional dependence. This approach appears distinct from recent NIPS contributions on additive models and graphical models.

## Significance
The problem addressed is significant for high-dimensional nonparametric regression, where balancing model complexity, interpretability, and estimation efficiency is crucial. The proposed method directly addresses this balance by identifying the minimal group additive structure, which maintains estimation efficiency while improving interpretability. The empirical results demonstrate the method's ability to successfully recover true group structures in various settings (Table 2, Figure 2), with the real data application on the Boston Housing dataset providing concrete evidence of practical utility. The method has potential applications in various fields requiring interpretable high-dimensional nonparametric models.

## Strengths
- Clear identification and rigorous solution of a well-defined gap in the literature (non-uniqueness of group additive structures).
- Strong theoretical foundation with well-proven consistency results.
- Practical algorithms with empirical validation across multiple simulation scenarios and a real data application.
- Clear distinction from related work on functional ANOVA models and graphical models.
- Comprehensive treatment of the problem from both theoretical and empirical perspectives.

## Weaknesses
- The explanation of the complexity measure's practical interpretation (α|u|+1) could be strengthened to better connect the theoretical derivation to intuitive understanding.
- The limitations of the backward stepwise algorithm (greedy nature) are acknowledged but not thoroughly analyzed in the context of why it fails for Model 4.
- The connection to graphical models is mentioned but not fully explored in terms of comparative advantages beyond the brief discussion in Section 1.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- The paper addresses a significant, well-motivated problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
- The theoretical contribution (intrinsic structure definition, consistency proof) is novel and substantial.
- The empirical validation demonstrates practical utility across multiple scenarios.
- The paper provides clear algorithms and sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- The work advances the state of the art by providing a systematic approach to group additive structure identification.

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- The paper could better explain the practical implications of the complexity measure beyond the theoretical derivation.
- The failure of the stepwise algorithm on Model 4 warrants more thorough analysis to understand the limitations.
- The comparison with alternative structure identification approaches (beyond graphical models) could be expanded.

Overall, this paper makes a technically sound and significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The method is well-motivated, theoretically rigorous, and empirically validated. The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature and provides a novel solution with practical implications for high-dimensional nonparametric modeling. The minor weaknesses do not undermine the paper's substantial contribution, and it meets all four criteria for quality at NeurIPS.
