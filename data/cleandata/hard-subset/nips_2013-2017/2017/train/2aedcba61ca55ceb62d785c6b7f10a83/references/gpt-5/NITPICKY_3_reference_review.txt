Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression models that decompose the regression function into a sum over groups of variables, f(X)=∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of predictors. The authors (i) formalize a notion of “intrinsic” group additive structure as the minimal group partition among those that admit such a decomposition, (ii) propose a structure-penalized kernel regression estimator over direct-sum RKHSs that simultaneously estimates f and selects G, and (iii) derive a complexity penalty C(G) inspired by covering number bounds for RKHS balls, leading to a practical penalty proportional to ∑u α|u|. They provide probability bounds suggesting selection consistency (fixed p), propose an exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic, and present limited simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

Strengths
- Tackles an important problem between additive models and full interaction models: discovering groups that jointly interact while maintaining interpretability and avoiding exponential explosion in higher-order ANOVA.
- Using direct-sum RKHSs is natural; the representer theorem implies computationally tractable KRR once G is fixed.
- The idea to relate a structure penalty to capacity measures of RKHSs is interesting, offering a path to principled structural regularization.
- The paper is motivated with clear examples and attempts to provide a selection-consistency result.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Theory and identifiability
- Theorem 1 (existence/uniqueness of a minimal “intrinsic” group structure) is asserted with virtually no conditions. In general, the set of partitions that admit a decomposition f=∑u fu(Xu) need not be closed under refinement; thus minimal elements may not exist or may not be unique. Example: f(X1,X2,X3)=g(X1,X2)+h(X2,X3) with nonseparable interactions is amiable for G1={(1,2),(3)} and G2={(1),(2,3)} but not for the common refinement {(1),(2),(3)}. This contradicts the claim that Ga is “complete” and has a unique minimal element without strong assumptions. Please either add conditions under which the meet of amiable partitions remains amiable (and then prove uniqueness), or relax the claim to the existence of minimal elements (possibly non-unique).
- Proposition 1 states strict inclusion L2G1 ⊂ L2G2 under independence when G1<G2, but Theorem 1 does not reference distributional assumptions. Several later arguments implicitly assume compact domains and boundedness; simulations use unbounded Gaussian X, contradicting conditions for sup-norm covering number bounds and embeddings into C(X).
- Identifiability of component functions is not addressed (e.g., centering constraints per group). Without constraints, decompositions are not unique; only the partition is of interest, but proofs should reflect this.

Penalty derivation and mismatch
- The derivation starts from ln N(ε,I(HK,r),||·||∞) ≤ Ck,p (ln(r/ε))p+1 for convolution kernels on compact domains, then via a sum bound for direct sums yields ln N(HG) scaling like ∑u α(|u|+1) with α=ln(r/ε). The final penalty used in (6) is µ∑u α|u| with α treated as a free integer hyperparameter in [1,10]. This discards the dependence on ε,r,kernel, constants, and the +1 in the exponent. The jump from a theoretical covering bound to a tunable α is not principled; thus the “capacity-based” penalty reduces to an ad hoc exponential size penalty. Clarify this mismatch, justify the simplification, and discuss sensitivity.
- Proposition 3: the covering number inequality for sums of function classes typically yields N(ε, F1+…+Fd,||·||∞) ≤ ∏j N(ε/d, Fj), hence log covers sum of logs with ε/d. The paper uses |G| in the denominator but does not provide proof or constant factors; moreover domains and closures need to be handled carefully. Provide a rigorous proof with assumptions (compact X, bounded kernels, norm radii).

Learning bounds and consistency
- Probability bounds (Proposition 4, Theorems 2–4) rely on sup-norm covering numbers and bounded losses; squared loss is not Lipschitz without bounded Y,f. Assumptions on Y (bounded/sub-Gaussian) are missing. The statements contain unusual factors (e.g., “12n,” “n>2/ε^2,” “λn‖f*‖^2” inside exponents) with unclear derivation. Please clean up constants, specify assumptions (compact domain, bounded outputs), and state the regime for λn (the text “λn ∗ n → 0” is unclear).
- Consistency is shown only for fixed p with finite Ga; yet the paper claims applicability to “large p.” No high-dimensional guarantees (e.g., scaling in p, β-min conditions, sparsity of groups) are provided.

Algorithmic clarity and practicality
- Algorithm 1 (exhaustive search) scales with the Bell number of partitions; feasible only for very small p. No pruning strategies or relaxations are offered.
- Algorithm 2 (backward stepwise) is underspecified. Step 4 (“either split j as a new group or add to an existing group”) is ambiguous: which existing group? Are all candidate moves tried? How many passes? How are µ and α set during the search? A single pass is likely insufficient; the method may get stuck. Provide a precise procedure, complexity analysis, and heuristics to avoid local minima (e.g., repeated passes, best-improvement, merge-split moves).
- Kernel choices and hyperparameters (bandwidths per group) are not specified. Using “universal kernels” is not enough; performance is highly sensitive to bandwidths. How are they selected across many candidate partitions without overfitting?

Experiments
- Key experimental details are missing: actual Table 1/2/Figure 2 contents, sample sizes, noise levels, λ selection, kernel bandwidths, computational costs. The main reported metric is the frequency of recovering the true partition; no predictive performance, no ablation on α, µ, λ, no stability analysis.
- No baselines are compared: e.g., SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu, 2016), functional ANOVA with RKHS, kernel HSIC-based interaction discovery, OKGT [11], or graphical-model-inspired block discovery. Without baselines, significance is unclear.
- The real-data analysis lists one partition with qualitative plots; no comparison to additive/non-additive baselines, no statistical validation, and feature indices are not mapped to variables for interpretability.

Clarity and presentation
- Notation and typos abound: inconsistent subscripts (f̂λ,G vs f̂λ,G∗), missing kernel subscripts, “an square integrable,” “is the 0 mean error,” “st Conference…” stray line, etc. Many references are incomplete (missing years, page ranges, incorrect attribution of ESP kernel). The paper needs careful editing.
- Assumptions (compact X, boundedness, independence) appear inconsistently across sections; simulations contradict theoretical settings.

Arguments pro acceptance
- Interesting problem and a potentially useful modeling sweet spot.
- Conceptual link between RKHS capacity and structure complexity is promising.
- Attempt at selection consistency.

Arguments con acceptance
- Core identifiability result (unique minimal partition) appears incorrect without strong additional assumptions; currently unproven and likely false in general.
- Penalty derivation vs implementation mismatch undermines the claimed principled nature.
- Theory has unclear/incorrect assumptions and presentation; fixed-p results only.
- Algorithms are underspecified; scalability and robustness not demonstrated.
- Experimental evaluation is insufficient and lacks baselines; real-data analysis is anecdotal.
- Numerous clarity, notation, and citation issues.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper addresses an appealing problem, but the theoretical foundation (existence/uniqueness of intrinsic structure), penalty justification, algorithmic specification, and empirical validation require substantial strengthening. Suggestions: (1) precisely state assumptions under which a unique minimal partition exists (or weaken the claim), (2) provide a rigorous, kernel-dependent penalty that does not rely on a free α, or justify the approximation empirically and theoretically, (3) specify and analyze the algorithms, (4) add competitive baselines and predictive evaluations, and (5) thoroughly revise clarity and references.
