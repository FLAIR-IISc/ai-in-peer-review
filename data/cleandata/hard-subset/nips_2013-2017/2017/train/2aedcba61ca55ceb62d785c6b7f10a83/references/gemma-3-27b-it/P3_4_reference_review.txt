## NIPS 2017 Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of identifying a group additive structure in nonparametric regression, aiming to bridge the gap between fully additive models and more complex interaction models like functional ANOVA. Recognizing the non-uniqueness of group additive structures, the authors define an “intrinsic” structure and propose a penalty-based kernel method to simultaneously identify this structure and estimate the underlying nonparametric functions. A key contribution is a novel complexity measure for group additive structures based on covering numbers of RKHSs. The authors present both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms for identifying the structure, and validate their approach with simulations and a real-world dataset.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the existing literature on additive models [5, 11], functional ANOVA [4, 13], and higher-order interaction models [6]. It explicitly addresses the limitations of these approaches, particularly the curse of dimensionality and interpretability issues. The connection to graphical models [2, 7, 9] is acknowledged, but the authors correctly highlight the distinction – their work directly targets group additive structure rather than conditional dependence. The use of kernel methods and RKHS theory is well-grounded in the existing literature [10, 12, 14, 15, 18]. The novelty lies in the combination of these techniques to address the specific problem of *identifying* the group structure, alongside estimating the function.

**Strengths:**

* **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper addresses a practical and important problem in nonparametric regression – balancing model flexibility with interpretability and efficiency in high dimensions.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The definition of the intrinsic group additive structure is a significant contribution, providing a principled way to deal with non-uniqueness.  The theoretical guarantees of consistency (Theorem 4) are a strong positive aspect.
* **Novel Complexity Measure:**  The use of covering numbers to quantify the complexity of group additive structures is a clever idea, providing a theoretically sound basis for the penalty term.
* **Algorithmic Contributions:** Providing both exhaustive search and a more scalable stepwise algorithm demonstrates practical consideration.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies and real data application provide evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The visualizations in the real data example are insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas.  The mathematical notation is generally clear and consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Theory:** While the theoretical results are strong, they are also quite dense and require a substantial background in RKHS theory to fully appreciate. This might make the paper less accessible to some readers.  More intuition building around the complexity measure could be helpful.
* **Computational Cost:**  The exhaustive search algorithm is computationally prohibitive for high-dimensional problems. The stepwise algorithm is an improvement, but its performance on complex datasets (like Model 4 in the simulations) is limited, as acknowledged by the authors.  Further investigation of more efficient search strategies could be beneficial.
* **Choice of Kernels & Parameters:** The impact of kernel choice and the tuning parameter λ is not explored in sufficient detail. The paper mentions using universal kernels but doesn’t offer guidance on which kernels are most appropriate for different types of data.  The sensitivity of results to λ selection is also not thoroughly investigated.
* **Limited Comparison:** The empirical evaluation could be strengthened by comparison to alternative methods for group structure identification, even if those methods are not directly applicable to nonparametric regression. A comparison to sparse graphical models or other variable selection techniques might provide valuable context.
* **Real Data Interpretation:** The interpretation of the Boston Housing data results is somewhat limited. More in-depth analysis of the relationships between variables within each group would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and novel approach to a challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
* The definition of the intrinsic group additive structure and the proposed complexity measure are significant contributions.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in a variety of settings.
* The paper is well-written and addresses a relevant topic for the NIPS audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical complexity and computational cost might limit the practical applicability of the method.
* The empirical evaluation could be strengthened by more extensive comparisons to alternative approaches.
* The sensitivity analysis of key parameters (λ, kernel choice) is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. While the complexity of the theory and the computational cost of the algorithms are potential drawbacks, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical guarantees, and the promising empirical results outweigh these concerns.  I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program and recommend **acceptance**.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10.  While I strongly recommend acceptance, the computational limitations and the need for more extensive empirical evaluation give me some reservation.
