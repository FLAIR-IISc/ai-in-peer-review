

# Peer Review: "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary

This paper addresses an important limitation in nonparametric regression: the trade-off between model flexibility and interpretability in high-dimensional settings. The authors propose a method to identify the intrinsic group additive structure for nonparametric regression, which lies between the standard additive model (no interactions) and functional ANOVA (full interactions). The key contribution is defining the intrinsic group additive structure (the minimal group structure that can represent the true function) and developing a method to identify it using a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers. The paper introduces a penalized kernel method that simultaneously identifies the group structure and estimates the nonparametric functions, with theoretical consistency guarantees and empirical validation.

The work builds on the OKGT method by Pan et al. (2015) but addresses two critical gaps: non-uniqueness of group structures and the lack of a systematic identification approach. It also distinguishes itself from functional ANOVA approaches (e.g., by Gu, 2006) and graphical models (e.g., by Koller and Friedman, 2009) by focusing directly on the group additive structure rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by rigorously defining the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and proving its consistency (Theorem 4). The definition of "amiable" group structures and the partial order relationship provide a solid mathematical foundation for the problem.

2. **Novel Complexity Measure**: The use of RKHS covering numbers as a complexity measure for group structures (Equation 6) is innovative and well-motivated. The authors carefully justify their choice of covering number over other capacity measures (VC dimension, Rademacher complexity, etc.).

3. **Practical Algorithms**: The paper develops two practical algorithms (exhaustive search and backward stepwise) that can be applied to both small and large datasets. The stepwise algorithm is particularly valuable for high-dimensional problems.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The simulation studies (Section 4) and real data application (Section 5) provide strong empirical support for the method. The results demonstrate successful identification of true group structures in various model settings.

5. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches (additive models, functional ANOVA) and positions the group additive model as a middle ground that balances interpretability and flexibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Related Work**: While the paper mentions related work, it doesn't thoroughly compare with other methods for structure identification in nonparametric regression. For instance, how does the proposed method compare to approaches based on sparsity (e.g., by Kandasamy and Yu, 2016) or other structure learning techniques? A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper.

2. **Implementation Details**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient details about computational complexity, especially for the stepwise algorithm. How does the backward stepwise algorithm scale with the number of predictors? What are the practical limitations of the exhaustive search approach?

3. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The consistency proof (Theorem 4) relies on assumptions about the kernel and the true function that aren't fully explained. For example, the requirement that λ_n * n → 0 needs more justification in the context of the specific problem.

4. **Model Selection**: The paper uses a validation set for parameter tuning (Algorithm 1), but doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of results to the choice of validation set size or the range of hyperparameters. This could impact the practical utility of the method.

5. **Real Data Interpretation**: The real data analysis (Section 5) is interesting but lacks deeper interpretation of the identified groups. For instance, why do these particular groups emerge in the Boston Housing data? A more detailed discussion of the domain-specific insights would strengthen the contribution.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the assumptions required for Theorem 4, particularly regarding the choice of λ_n. How should λ_n be selected in practice to satisfy λ_n * n → 0?

2. **Comparison to Related Work**: Please expand the discussion to compare your method with other approaches to structure identification in nonparametric regression, such as the Salsa method by Kandasamy and Yu (2016) or sparse additive models.

3. **Algorithm Complexity**: Include a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the stepwise algorithm and how it compares to exhaustive search. This would help readers understand the practical applicability of the method.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity**: In Section 4, provide more information about how sensitive the results are to the choice of α and μ. A sensitivity analysis would be valuable.

5. **Real Data Interpretation**: In Section 5, provide more domain-specific insights about why the identified groups make sense for the Boston Housing data. This would strengthen the practical relevance of your method.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical results and strong empirical validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and provide a thorough analysis. (4/5)

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise mathematical definitions. However, some technical details (e.g., the exact form of the complexity measure) could be better explained. (4/5)

**Originality**: The concept of intrinsic group additive structure and its identification via RKHS covering numbers is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on additive models and functional ANOVA. (5/5)

**Significance**: The problem of structure identification in high-dimensional nonparametric regression is important, and the proposed method offers a practical solution that balances interpretability and flexibility. The approach has clear potential for application in various domains. (4/5)

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric regression with practical implications for high-dimensional data analysis. The paper is well-written, the methodology is sound, and the empirical results are convincing. While there are some areas for improvement (noted above), these do not detract from the overall quality of the work.

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in a novel way and provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The proposed method fills an important gap between standard additive models and more complex functional ANOVA approaches, offering practitioners a useful tool for high-dimensional nonparametric regression with interpretable structure.

The paper would be a valuable contribution to NIPS, advancing the state of the art in nonparametric regression methods. I believe it would be of interest to a broad audience of machine learning researchers and practitioners working with high-dimensional data.
