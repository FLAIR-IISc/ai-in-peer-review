## NIPS 2017 Review: Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of identifying a group additive structure in nonparametric regression, aiming to balance the flexibility of nonparametric methods with the interpretability and efficiency of additive models. The authors propose a novel approach based on kernel methods and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs), introducing a complexity measure for group additive structures and formulating a penalized minimization problem to simultaneously identify the structure and estimate the functions. They establish theoretical consistency guarantees and demonstrate the method’s effectiveness through simulations and a real-world dataset. The work builds on existing approaches like functional ANOVA and Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT), directly tackling the challenge of identifying the underlying group structure, a key limitation of previous work.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** Identifying a suitable structure in high-dimensional nonparametric regression is crucial for achieving both statistical efficiency and model interpretability. This work directly addresses this challenge.
* **Novel Complexity Measure:** The introduction of a complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHSs provides a principled way to penalize complex group structures, guiding the model towards simpler, more interpretable solutions.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:**  The consistency proof for the identified group structure is a significant contribution, providing strong theoretical backing for the proposed method.  The proof appears rigorous (though a detailed review of the supplementary material is required for full verification).
* **Practical Algorithms:** The proposal of both an exhaustive search and a stepwise algorithm offers practical solutions for identifying the group structure in varying dimensional settings.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation studies demonstrate the method's ability to recover the true group structure in different scenarios. The application to the Boston Housing dataset provides a realistic example.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper clearly positions its contribution relative to existing methods like functional ANOVA, higher-order interaction models, and OKGT, highlighting the advantages of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** Exhaustive search, while conceptually straightforward, is computationally expensive for large p.  The stepwise algorithm is a good alternative, but its greedy nature may not always find the optimal structure (as acknowledged by the authors).  Further discussion of computational scalability and potential approximations would be beneficial.
* **Choice of Hyperparameters:** The performance relies on appropriately tuning λ, µ, and α. While the paper mentions using a validation set, a more detailed discussion of sensitivity to these parameters and guidelines for their selection would strengthen the practical applicability.
* **Kernel Choice:** The paper mentions using universal kernels, but doesn't delve into the impact of different kernel choices on performance.  A comparison of different kernels (e.g., Gaussian, Laplace) could be insightful.
* **Real Data Analysis – Limited Insight:** While the real data application is a good demonstration, the analysis remains somewhat superficial. More detailed interpretation of the identified group structure in the context of housing prices and a comparison with existing knowledge in the field would increase the impact.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical guarantees rely on certain assumptions (e.g., smoothness of functions, independence of predictors). A discussion of the robustness of the method to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach. The theoretical analysis is a significant strength. The empirical validation is reasonable, but could be more extensive.
* **Clarity (7/10):**  The paper is generally well-written, but the theoretical sections can be dense and challenging. More intuitive explanations of the RKHS complexity measure would improve clarity. The algorithm descriptions are clear.
* **Originality (8/10):** The proposed approach, particularly the complexity measure for group structures and the penalized minimization framework, is novel and represents a significant contribution.
* **Significance (7/10):** The results are promising and have the potential to be valuable for practitioners and researchers dealing with high-dimensional nonparametric regression.  The impact will depend on the practical scalability and robustness of the method.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
* The proposed approach is novel and well-motivated.
* The theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees for the method.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in simulated data.
* The work is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the exhaustive search algorithm can be prohibitive for large datasets.
* The performance is sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters.
* The real data analysis could be more insightful.
* The assumptions underlying the theoretical guarantees need further discussion.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept (with minor revisions).** This is a solid contribution with a clear novelty and strong theoretical foundation. The authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above – particularly regarding computational scalability, hyperparameter tuning, and real data interpretation – through minor revisions.  A more detailed explanation of the RKHS complexity measure would also improve clarity.  I believe that addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and make it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
