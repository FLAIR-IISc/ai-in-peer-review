

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper introduces a novel framework for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The authors propose a method to identify the "intrinsic group additive structure" - the minimal group structure that can represent the underlying function, which lies between the standard additive model (no interactions) and functional ANOVA models (all possible interactions). The key innovation is the development of a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers to identify this minimal structure, rather than assuming group structures a priori as in previous work like OKGT.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it rigorously defines the intrinsic group additive structure as the unique minimal structure that can represent the target function. This is a significant theoretical advance over previous approaches that either assumed group structures or used functional ANOVA without structure identification. Second, the authors develop a complexity measure for group structures based on RKHS covering numbers, which they incorporate into a penalized risk framework for structure identification. Third, they provide theoretical consistency guarantees for their approach and develop two practical algorithms (exhaustive search and stepwise) for implementation.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While additive models and functional ANOVA have been extensively studied, the systematic identification of group structures as a principled middle ground between these approaches is new. The authors clearly articulate how their method differs from related work on graphical models and functional ANOVA, noting that they directly target the group additive structure rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure.

The technical quality is strong, with rigorous theoretical development and proofs of consistency. The experimental results demonstrate the method's effectiveness in both simulation studies and a real data application (Boston Housing), successfully recovering true group structures in most cases. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation.

The significance of this work is substantial for high-dimensional nonparametric regression. By identifying the minimal group structure, the approach mitigates the curse of dimensionality while maintaining interpretability - a critical challenge in modern data analysis. The method could enable more efficient and interpretable modeling in applications with high-dimensional predictors.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The stepwise algorithm's greedy nature may not always find the global optimum, as noted in the paper.
2. The choice of tuning parameters (µ and α) is somewhat ad-hoc, though the paper addresses this with validation approaches.
3. The real data application uses only 13 predictors, which doesn't fully demonstrate the method's advantage in high-dimensional settings.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find these minor limitations outweighed by the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The identification of the intrinsic group additive structure represents a genuinely new direction in nonparametric regression, moving beyond the standard additive model and functional ANOVA toward a more principled approach to handling interactions.

The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature and provides a theoretically sound, practically implementable solution. It advances the state of the art in nonparametric regression by introducing a novel framework for structure identification that balances flexibility with interpretability.

This work is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers on additive models and functional ANOVA. The authors properly contextualize their contribution relative to prior work (e.g., OKGT, functional ANOVA, graphical models), demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression and would be of interest to the NIPS community. It presents a novel conceptual framework with strong theoretical underpinnings and practical utility, addressing a meaningful challenge in high-dimensional data analysis. The paper's focus on structure identification rather than assuming group structures a priori represents a substantial step forward.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates both theoretical and empirical significance. While the stepwise algorithm could be improved, this is a minor issue for a paper that introduces such a novel concept. I strongly recommend acceptance.
