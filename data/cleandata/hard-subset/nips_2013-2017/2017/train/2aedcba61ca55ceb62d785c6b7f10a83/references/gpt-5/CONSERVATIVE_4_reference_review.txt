Summary
The paper studies nonparametric regression under a “group additive” structure: the regression function is assumed to decompose as a sum of unknown functions, each depending on a subset (group) of covariates. The authors (i) formalize identifiability by defining amiable group structures and a unique “intrinsic” minimal group additive structure; (ii) propose to identify this structure and estimate the component functions via kernel methods in direct-sum RKHSs; (iii) introduce a structural penalty on group partitions derived from covering-number bounds of RKHSs, leading to an objective combining squared loss, RKHS norm, and a complexity term C(G); (iv) provide consistency results asserting that their penalized procedure recovers the intrinsic group structure under suitable conditions; and (v) present two algorithms (exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic) with simulations on five synthetic models and an application to Boston Housing.

Relation to prior work
The work lies between classic additive models and functional ANOVA/interaction models (SS-ANOVA, KRR with ESP kernel). It also relates to structured sparsity and multiple kernel learning that select/additively combine variable subsets (e.g., Hierarchical Kernel Learning, Bach 2009; SALSA, Kandasamy & Yu 2016). Connections to graphical models are discussed. Compared to these, the paper enforces a partition constraint (interactions only within groups) and penalizes partitions via RKHS capacity.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: group additivity offers a middle ground between additive and full interaction models, potentially improving interpretability and sample efficiency.
- The RKHS formulation for a fixed partition is standard and sound, and the use of universal kernels to control approximation bias is appropriate.
- The attempt to tie the structural penalty to covering-number bounds is interesting and provides a principled lens beyond ad hoc penalties.
- Consistency results are claimed for selecting the intrinsic group structure, and the high-level proof strategy (separating amiable vs non-amiable structures via approximation error and penalizing capacity) is reasonable.
- Algorithms are simple to implement when p is small, and the stepwise heuristic is a practical baseline.

Weaknesses
Technical correctness and assumptions
- Intrinsic structure uniqueness: Theorem 1 claims existence/uniqueness of a minimal amiable partition. This is nontrivial: the set of amiable partitions is not obviously closed under refinement (splitting a group requires that a component function further decomposes additively). Without explicit assumptions (e.g., independence, orthogonality, or a functional decomposition property), uniqueness may fail. The current exposition does not clearly state sufficient conditions.
- The propositions/theorems hide key conditions: bounded kernels/outputs, noise assumptions, distributional assumptions (independence was invoked in Proposition 1 for strict inclusion), and requirements on universal kernels are not consistently stated alongside results. The consistency theorem (Theorem 4) assumes λn n → 0 but provides no concrete guidance on tuning sequences.
- The derivation from covering-number bounds to the penalty C(G) is loose. The final penalty simplifies to Σ α|u| (dropping the +1 and the dependence on |G| and ε), and then α is treated as a tunable integer in [1,10]. This undermines the purported principled nature: once α is tuned arbitrarily, the link to RKHS capacity is tenuous and the theory no longer justifies the implemented procedure. It remains unclear whether Theorem 4 applies to the algorithm that tunes α via validation.
- Proposition 3 inequality and the ε/|G| scaling are plausible, but the step from such bounds to a dimension-only penalty disregards kernel bandwidths and domain constants known to affect covering numbers for Gaussian RKHSs.

Algorithmic/scalability concerns
- Exhaustive search over partitions is infeasible beyond very small p (Bell numbers). The backward stepwise heuristic can get stuck and is not analyzed; no guarantees are provided for high-dimensional regimes.
- No complexity analysis (time/memory) is provided; kernel methods plus partition search become quickly prohibitive.

Empirical evaluation
- Limited and not compelling. “Extensive” simulations are five low-dimensional synthetic setups. There is no exploration of higher p (e.g., p ≥ 20–50), correlated covariates, varying SNRs, or group sizes > 3.
- No comparisons to strong baselines: SALSA (ICML’16) is cited but not compared; hierarchical/multiple kernel learning methods (e.g., Bach 2009, HKL), SS-ANOVA, additive models with two-way interactions, and structured sparsity (overlapping group lasso) are missing from experiments. Without such baselines, it is hard to assess significance.
- The real-data example (Boston Housing) is purely descriptive; there is no quantitative predictive comparison or stability analysis of the learned grouping. The claimed interpretability is not validated against domain knowledge beyond anecdotal observations.

Clarity and presentation
- Several definitions and statements are imprecise or contain typographical issues. Some key quantities (e.g., constants in covering-number bounds, domain assumptions) are not defined when used. Notation occasionally shifts (HG vs H_G), and some results (e.g., Theorem 2/3 bounds) are difficult to parse without the missing assumptions.
- The jump from theory to the implementable penalty should be clarified, with a careful discussion of what the theory guarantees when α and μ are tuned by validation.

Originality and significance
- The idea of learning group partitions in nonparametric regression is interesting, but related literatures on structured sparsity and kernel learning over variable subsets provide alternative, tested approaches. The paper’s main novelty—the penalty derived from RKHS covering numbers—ends up as a size-based heuristic once implemented. Without stronger theory tied to the actual procedure and competitive empirical results, the incremental contribution is limited.

Questions for the authors
1. Under what explicit conditions on f and PX does the intrinsic amiable partition exist and is it unique? Does uniqueness require independence of covariates? Can you provide counterexamples or a constructive characterization?
2. Do your consistency results apply when α and μ are tuned by cross-validation? If not, can you provide a practical, theoretically-justified recipe to set α from kernel parameters and data?
3. How does GASI compare empirically and theoretically to HKL (Bach 2009), SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu 2016), SS-ANOVA, and structured sparsity methods for interaction selection?
4. Can you handle correlated X and larger p (≥30) with groups of size up to 3? Please report recovery rates, precision/recall, and prediction error, with runtime.
5. What kernels and bandwidth selection strategies were used? How sensitive are results to λ?

Pros
- Interesting problem formulation with interpretability.
- RKHS framework for fixed partition is solid.
- Attempts a principled structural penalty and provides selection consistency claims.

Cons
- Key identifiability claim (unique intrinsic structure) not convincingly supported.
- Theory-practice mismatch for the penalty; tuned α weakens justification.
- No comparison to strong baselines; limited experiments; poor scalability story.
- Missing assumptions and clarity in statements.

Recommendation: Reject
While the direction is promising, the paper lacks rigorous support for its central identifiability claim, the theoretical guarantees do not clearly apply to the implemented procedure, and the empirical validation is insufficient against established baselines. Strengthening the theoretical assumptions and proofs, aligning the penalty with implementable tuning, providing scalable algorithms with guarantees, and adding comprehensive comparisons would substantially improve the work.

Confidence: High
