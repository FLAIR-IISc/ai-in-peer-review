## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper tackles a significant problem in nonparametric regression – dealing with high dimensionality and the desire for interpretability beyond purely additive models. The authors propose a principled approach to identifying group additive structures, offering a middle ground between fully additive models and more complex interaction-based models like ANOVA. The theoretical contributions (consistency guarantees) are a strong point, and the simulation results are promising. However, the clarity of the presentation could be improved, and the real-data application feels somewhat limited given the complexity of the method.  I lean towards **accept**, but with requests for major revisions to improve clarity and provide more comprehensive empirical evaluation.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the curse of dimensionality in nonparametric regression by exploring group additive models.  These models assume that the function *f(X)* can be decomposed into a sum of functions, each dependent on a group of predictors, instead of individual predictors. While the concept of group additive models is present in previous work (e.g., [11] which is correctly cited), this paper distinguishes itself by focusing on the crucial problem of *identifying* the optimal group structure. It introduces the notion of an "intrinsic group additive structure" – a minimal representation – and proposes a penalized kernel method for simultaneously identifying this structure and estimating the underlying functions.  The work builds on concepts from RKHS theory, capacity measures, and sparse modeling.  The connection to graphical models is well-noted, and the paper accurately highlights the differences in scope (focusing on group additivity rather than full conditional dependencies).

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's strongest asset is its theoretical foundation. The definition of the intrinsic group additive structure provides a solid foundation for the problem, and the consistency proof for the proposed method is a significant contribution.  The use of covering numbers as a complexity measure for group structures is novel and well-motivated.
* **Principled Approach:**  The penalized kernel method provides a statistically sound framework for simultaneously learning the group structure and the underlying functions. The incorporation of a complexity penalty is crucial for avoiding overfitting and selecting a parsimonious model.
* **Well-Defined Problem:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods (fully additive models lacking interaction, ANOVA models suffering from combinatorial explosion) and position their work as a valuable compromise.
* **Simulation Results:** The simulation studies provide initial evidence that the proposed method can successfully recover the true group structure, demonstrating its practical potential.
* **Connection to RKHS Theory:** The connection to RKHS and capacity measures is well done and adds theoretical depth.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Readability:**  The paper is dense and occasionally difficult to follow. The mathematical notation is heavy, and the explanations could be more intuitive.  For instance, the transition from defining the complexity measure C(G) to using it in the optimization problem (Equation 6) feels abrupt.  More detailed explanations and examples would improve understanding.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the simulation studies are a good start, they lack sufficient variety in model complexity and data characteristics.  Specifically, the simulations appear to focus on scenarios where the true group structure is relatively simple. Evaluating the method's performance on more challenging datasets (e.g., with more complex interactions, larger numbers of predictors) would be crucial.
* **Algorithm Scalability:** The authors mention two algorithms: exhaustive search and a stepwise approach. The discussion of the stepwise approach is brief, and its scalability for truly high-dimensional datasets (p > 100) is not clearly addressed.  The real-data example (Boston Housing) has only 13 predictors, limiting the demonstration of scalability.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (λ, µ, α) is critical. The paper provides a grid search approach but does not discuss the sensitivity of the results to these parameters in detail. More robust parameter tuning strategies would be beneficial.
* **Real Data Analysis Depth:** The real-data application is somewhat superficial. The authors identify a group structure and visualize the estimated functions, but they don't provide a deep interpretation of the results or compare the performance of their method to other relevant approaches (e.g., standard additive models, penalized regression).



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and consistency guarantees.
* Novel approach to identifying group additive structures.
* Well-defined problem with clear motivation.
* Promising simulation results.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Clarity and readability issues.
* Limited empirical evaluation and lack of scalability demonstration.
* Superficial real-data application.
* Parameter sensitivity concerns.

**5. Specific Suggestions & Questions:**

* **Clarity:**  Provide more intuitive explanations of key concepts (e.g., the complexity measure C(G)).  Consider adding diagrams or illustrations to help visualize the group structures.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** Conduct more extensive simulations with varying levels of noise, different data distributions, and more complex group structures.  Include experiments with a larger number of predictors.
* **Scalability:** Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms and explore techniques for improving scalability. Evaluate the stepwise algorithm on higher-dimensional datasets.
* **Parameter Tuning:** Investigate more robust parameter tuning methods, such as cross-validation with a wider range of parameter values.
* **Real Data:**  Provide a more in-depth analysis of the real-data application. Compare the performance of the proposed method to other relevant approaches. Discuss the interpretability of the identified group structure in the context of the Boston Housing dataset.  What domain knowledge supports the identified groupings?
* **Connection to Graphical Models:** Expand on the connection to graphical models, potentially discussing how the proposed method could be used to estimate sparse block structures.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly improving clarity and providing more comprehensive empirical evaluation, would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
