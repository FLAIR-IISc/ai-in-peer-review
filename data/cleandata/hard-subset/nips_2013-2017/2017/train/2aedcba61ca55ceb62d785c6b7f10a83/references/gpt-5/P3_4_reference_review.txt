Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies nonparametric regression when the regression function admits a group-additive structure: f(X)=∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of variables into groups. This positions itself between fully additive models (no interactions) and higher-order ANOVA/interaction models, aiming to capture within-group interactions while avoiding the combinatorial explosion of all pairwise/higher-order terms. The paper (i) formalizes a partial order on group structures and defines the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal (finest) amiable partition under which f is representable; (ii) proposes a kernel-based estimator in a direct-sum RKHS HG, and (iii) introduces a structural penalty intended to quantify the capacity of HG via covering number bounds, leading to an objective minimizing empirical risk + λ‖f‖²HG + μ∑u α|u|. The authors prove a selection consistency result (fixed p) under suitable tuning, and provide two algorithms (exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic), with simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

This work connects to additive models and functional ANOVA (Hastie & Tibshirani; Gu), sparse additive modeling (e.g., SpAM; group-lasso approaches), higher-order interaction models such as SALSA (Kandasamy & Yu), hierarchical kernel learning/ANOVA kernels, and OKGT (Pan et al.) which motivated group-wise transformations. It also relates to RKHS capacity control (Bartlett & Mendelson; Zhou; Kühn) and the lattice of partitions.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: balances flexibility/interpretability by allowing within-group interactions while avoiding the full interaction graph.
- Formalization of a partial order on group structures and the notion of an “intrinsic” (minimal) amiable structure is conceptually interesting and potentially useful beyond this paper.
- Use of direct-sum RKHSs to model group-wise functions is natural; the representer theorem and KRR lead to computable estimators once G is known.
- The attempt to tie structure selection to a capacity measure of HG is principled, and Proposition 3 giving a covering-number bound for direct sums is useful.
- Consistency result (selection of the intrinsic partition with probability → 1 for fixed p) helps anchor the proposal theoretically.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
1) Uniqueness/existence of the intrinsic structure. Theorem 1 asserts a unique minimal group additive structure in the subset Ga of partitions that are amiable for f. While the full lattice of partitions under refinement has meets/joins, it is not obvious that Ga is closed under meets. Without such closure, Ga need not contain a unique least element; multiple incomparable minimal elements can exist. The proof sketch is not provided here. Please clarify conditions (e.g., product measure/independence across variables) under which the minimal amiable partition exists and is unique, and whether the meet of amiable partitions is amiable.

2) Distributional assumptions and identifiability. Proposition 1 uses independence to obtain strict inclusion L2G1⊂L2G2. Functional ANOVA uniqueness typically requires a product measure and centering constraints. The paper does not state whether components fu are constrained (e.g., zero mean with respect to marginals) to ensure identifiability. Without constraints and/or independence, f may be representable under multiple incomparable partitions. Please state the required assumptions on PX and the normalization constraints used in estimation.

3) Structural penalty derivation and its mismatch with practice. The transition from covering-number bounds to C(G)=∑u α(ε)|u|+1 and then to the implemented penalty ∑u α|u| (with α tuned as an integer) is ad hoc. The constants in covering-number bounds depend on the kernels and input dimensions; replacing them by an arbitrary α breaks the claimed theoretical linkage. Moreover, the penalty monotonicity under refinement depends critically on α: for example, splitting a size-2 group into two singletons reduces the penalty iff α≥2 (since α^2≥2α). The theory should state an admissible range for α (and its dependence on ε, kernels) that guarantees the structural risk will prefer the minimal amiable partition.

4) Scope of consistency. The selection consistency (Theorem 4) is for fixed p and requires tuning (λn n→0 and a “proper” μ). There is no guarantee that data-driven tuning (CV/validation, as used in experiments) yields consistency. The high-dimensional setting (large p) is a key motivation, yet no theory is provided for p growing with n, nor are there sparsity assumptions or guarantees for the greedy algorithm.

5) Algorithmic design. The backward stepwise procedure is underspecified (single pass? order of splits? convergence criterion?) and lacks guarantees (e.g., local optimality, consistency under restricted conditions). Heuristics that allow both split and merge operations, or beam search, could improve robustness. Computational complexity and scaling behavior (e.g., Gram matrices per group) are not discussed.

6) Experimental evaluation. 
- Baselines are missing. Comparisons to sparse additive models (e.g., SpAM, Meier et al.), SALSA, hierarchical/ANOVA kernels, OKGT, additive Gaussian process models, or structured sparsity approaches would contextualize performance.
- Details for reproducibility are sparse: choice of kernels per group, bandwidth selection, centering/identifiability constraints, λ tuning, and how the RKHS norm is computed for the direct sum.
- Metrics focus on the frequency of exact recovery; consider adjusted Rand index or pairwise F1 for partitions. Also report predictive RMSE and uncertainty across seeds.
- Real data: only one dataset, no baselines, no statistical significance; the recovered structure is plausible but anecdotal.

7) Minor technical points.
- The direct-sum RKHS norm should be explicitly defined (sum of group norms).
- The requirement on λn in Proposition 4 is nontransparent; providing a readable sufficient choice and practical guidance would help.
- The link between non-amiable structures and a positive excess risk gap (Theorem 3) would benefit from intuition and conditions ensuring a uniform gap.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely state the assumptions (on PX, function class, kernels, identifiability constraints) under which G* exists and is unique; provide the proof or cite an appropriate lattice-theoretic argument ensuring Ga is closed under meet.
- Tighten the structural penalty: either keep the theoretically derived form (with kernel-dependent constants) or justify a simplified surrogate with provable monotonicity under refinement (e.g., choose α≥α0 with explicit α0). Alternatively, consider penalties proportional to information criteria (e.g., Rademacher complexity bounds) or structured sparsity norms with clearer behavior.
- Provide high-dimensional theory (p→∞) under sparsity of groups and a restricted isometry/incoherence-type condition, or clearly scope the method to moderate p.
- Strengthen the algorithm: iterative split/merge with validation, multiple restarts, or use of combinatorial optimization relaxations; report runtime and scaling.
- Expand experiments with competitive baselines and comprehensive metrics; detail all hyperparameters; include synthetic settings where dependence among variables challenges identifiability.

Pros
- Well-motivated problem and neat formalization of groupwise structure.
- Principled use of RKHS and capacity control; selection consistency (fixed p).
- Potentially useful in practice for interpretable modeling with interactions.

Cons
- Key assumptions for uniqueness/identifiability are unclear.
- Structural penalty derivation and practical instantiation are misaligned.
- Limited algorithmic guarantees and experimental comparison; scalability unclear.

Overall assessment
Interesting and potentially impactful direction with solid ideas, but important theoretical clarifications (existence/uniqueness, penalty design) and stronger empirical validation are needed. I view this as borderline; with clearer assumptions and more rigorous alignment between theory and practice, plus competitive experimental comparisons, it could be a strong contribution.
