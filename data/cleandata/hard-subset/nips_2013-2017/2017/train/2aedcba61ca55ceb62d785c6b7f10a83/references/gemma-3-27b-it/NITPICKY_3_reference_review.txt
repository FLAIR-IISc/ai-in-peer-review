This paper proposes a method for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression, addressing the limitations of both purely additive models and higher-order interaction models. The core idea – finding a balance between interpretability and flexibility by grouping interacting variables – is conceptually sound and builds upon existing work in additive models and functional ANOVA. The authors attempt to formalize the notion of an “intrinsic” group structure and develop a penalized kernel method for its estimation.

However, while the ideas are promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from superficial theoretical justification to inconsistencies in notation and a lack of rigorous experimental validation. My overall assessment is negative, and I recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Identifying meaningful structure in high-dimensional nonparametric regression is a crucial challenge. The proposed group additive model represents a reasonable intermediate approach between purely additive models and full interaction models.
*   **Formalization Attempt:** The attempt to define an “intrinsic” group additive structure is a step towards addressing the non-identifiability problem.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper correctly positions its contributions within the broader context of additive models, functional ANOVA, and graphical models.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Flaws and Lack of Rigor:** The theoretical development, particularly regarding the capacity measure *C(G)*, feels underdeveloped and lacks crucial justifications. The connection between the covering number and the “complexity” of a group structure is not clearly established. Proposition 1 states L2G1 ⊂ L2G2 if G1 ≤ G2, *only if* X1,…,Xp are independent, yet the statement is presented without this critical condition.  The proofs, referenced as being in the supplementary material, are essential for evaluating the validity of the theoretical claims. Without access to those proofs, assessing the technical soundness is impossible.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies and Ambiguity:** Numerous notational inconsistencies hinder comprehension. For example, the definition of *Hu* varies depending on the context. The meaning of α in the complexity measure is poorly explained, and its relation to the covering number is unclear. The symbol “d” is used to represent both the number of groups and a dimension – a source of potential confusion.
*   **Empirical Evaluation Concerns:** The simulation studies are insufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The results are presented as frequencies of identifying the *true* structure, but there’s no comparison to baseline methods or clear indication of statistical significance. The chosen models are relatively simple. The application to the Boston Housing data is also limited—showing estimated functions is insufficient; a comparison to standard regression techniques would be essential.  The claim that the backward stepwise algorithm works well for Model 1, 2, 3, and 5, but not for Model 4, requires explanation.
*   **Algorithm Implementation Details:** The paper lacks crucial implementation details. How are the parameters λ, µ, and α selected in practice? What kernel functions are used? These details are critical for reproducibility.
*   **Minor Issues:** Several minor typos and grammatical errors detract from the overall presentation. “st Conference” in the header should simply be “Conference”.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is important and relevant.
*   The proposed approach offers a potential compromise between model complexity and interpretability.

**Arguments Against Acceptance (and these are substantial):**

*   The theoretical foundation is weak and lacks rigor.
*   The empirical evaluation is inadequate and does not convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   The paper contains notational inconsistencies and ambiguities.
*   Lack of critical implementation details hinders reproducibility.
*   The presented material lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission.



**Overall:**

This paper presents an interesting idea, but its execution falls short of NIPS standards. The theoretical claims require more rigorous justification, the empirical evaluation needs to be substantially strengthened, and the presentation needs significant improvement. While the concept of identifying group additive structures is valuable, this paper does not provide a compelling demonstration of its feasibility or effectiveness. The numerous flaws outweigh the strengths, leading me to recommend rejection. Addressing the issues related to theoretical rigor, empirical validation, and presentation clarity would be necessary for a future revision to be considered.
