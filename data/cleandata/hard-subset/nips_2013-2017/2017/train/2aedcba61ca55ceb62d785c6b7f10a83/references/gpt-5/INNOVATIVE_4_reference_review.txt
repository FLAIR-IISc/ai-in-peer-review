Summary
This paper introduces Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI) for kernel nonparametric regression. The core idea is to model E[Y|X] as an additive sum of functions over disjoint groups of variables (a partition), interpolating between fully additive (no interactions) and full interaction models. The authors (i) formalize the notion of an intrinsic group additive structure: the minimal (w.r.t. a partial order on partitions) amiable partition that exactly represents the regression function; (ii) propose a structure-penalized kernel estimator in a direct-sum RKHS that jointly estimates the function and identifies the group partition; (iii) derive a complexity measure for a partition via covering-number bounds of the corresponding direct-sum RKHS and use it as a structural penalty; and (iv) prove selection consistency for recovering the intrinsic structure. They provide an exhaustive-search and a backward stepwise algorithm, with simulations and a Boston Housing case study.

Relation to prior work
The work sits between classic additive models and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models (e.g., SALSA with ESP kernels). It extends OKGT by addressing non-uniqueness/identifiability (via the intrinsic structure) and by proposing a principled structural penalty. It relates to graphical models conceptually but targets a simpler partition structure rather than a full conditional independence graph. Compared to sparse additive/interactions models, the distinctive choice here is to constrain interactions to within groups forming a partition.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: The intrinsic group additive structure is a clean and useful concept, giving a principled notion of the “minimal” interacting clusters needed to represent f. This is an appealing middle ground between additive and fully nonparametric models.
- Theory-driven penalty: The structural penalty is tied to RKHS capacity via covering numbers and the direct-sum structure, rather than ad hoc group sizes alone. The inequality relating covering numbers for direct sums gives a plausible route to penalize higher-dimensional groups.
- Consistency: The paper proves selection consistency under reasonable conditions (universal kernels, appropriate tuning), separating amiable vs. non-amiable structures, and showing why goodness-of-fit alone cannot distinguish among amiable supersets.
- Algorithms: An exhaustive-search variant for small p and a backward stepwise heuristic for larger p make the method usable in practice.
- Interpretability: The partition output is directly interpretable as clusters of interacting variables and is easier to visualize than general interaction models.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Empirical validation is limited. There are no comparisons to strong baselines such as SALSA (ESP kernel), sparse additive/interactions models (e.g., SpAM, hierarchical interaction selection), or OKGT. The Boston Housing experiment lacks predictive performance comparisons. Without these, it’s hard to assess practical advantage.
- Penalty instantiation lacks clarity. α is theoretically linked to ln(r/ε) but is treated as a tunable integer in experiments. This weakens the theory-to-practice bridge. Guidance on choosing α and µ as n grows (beyond grid/CV) would strengthen the story.
- Assumptions and scope: Independence is used to get strict inclusions (Prop 1), but practical X often exhibits correlation. The paper should clarify which parts of the theory require independence and how correlation affects identifiability and selection.
- Scalability and guarantees of the stepwise algorithm: The greedy backward splitting is intuitive but can miss the true structure (observed in Model 4). There is no complexity/running-time analysis or strategies to escape local minima (e.g., multiple restarts, beam search, or stochastic moves).
- Methodological details under-specified: Kernel choices and bandwidth selection per group, λ tuning protocols, and the handling of differing group sizes in KRR are not fully detailed. Reproducibility would benefit from more explicit procedures.
- Notational inconsistencies and minor clarity issues: The transition from C(G) ∝ Σ α(ε)^{|u|+1} to Σ α^{|u|} (Eq. 6) is abrupt; constants and exponents appear to shift between sections. Some typos and artifacts remain.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is selection to α and µ? Can you provide asymptotic guidance or data-driven rules that align with the theory (beyond validation)?
2. How does GASI perform versus SALSA, OKGT, SpAM, and HSIC-based interaction-screening in both accuracy and runtime?
3. What are the effects of correlated predictors on identifying the intrinsic structure? Any conditions under which the intrinsic structure remains unique/identifiable?
4. Can the stepwise search be strengthened (e.g., with local merges/splits, randomized restarts, or a global surrogate) while retaining scalability?
5. Which kernels and bandwidths were used per group, and how were they selected?

Pros
- Introduces a compelling, minimalistic notion of intrinsic group structure.
- Derives a principled RKHS capacity-based structural penalty and proves selection consistency.
- Offers a practical joint estimation/selection framework with interpretable outputs.

Cons
- Missing comparisons to key baselines; limited empirical evidence of superiority.
- Practical penalty specification (α) is decoupled from its theoretical origin.
- Scalability/search quality not fully addressed; at least one simulated failure case.
- Some assumptions and implementation details need clarification for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper proposes a bold and conceptually novel direction—identifying minimal interacting partitions via a theory-grounded RKHS penalty—and provides nontrivial consistency results. Despite limitations in empirical evaluation and some gaps in practical guidance, the central idea is strong and likely to inspire follow-up work on scalable structure learning in nonparametric regression. Strengthening experiments with competitive baselines and clarifying the penalty/assumptions would make this a solid accept.
