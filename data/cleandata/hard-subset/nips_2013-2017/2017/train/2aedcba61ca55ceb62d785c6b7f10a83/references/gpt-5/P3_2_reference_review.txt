Summary
This paper studies nonparametric regression under “group additive” structure: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of predictors into groups. The authors (i) formalize a partial order on group structures and define the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal amiable partition that admits such a decomposition; (ii) propose to estimate f and identify G jointly via kernel ridge regression in a direct-sum RKHS with a structural penalty C(G) derived from covering-number capacity bounds; (iii) prove selection consistency of the estimated structure; and (iv) provide two algorithms (exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic), with simulations and a Boston Housing example.

Relation to prior work
The motivation connects with additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani), functional ANOVA (Gu), and interaction modeling via ESP kernels (Kandasamy & Yu, SALSA). The grouping perspective is linked to OKGT (Pan et al.). The structural penalty draws on classical RKHS capacity measures (Bartlett & Mendelson, Zhou, Steinwart & Christmann) and covering numbers for Gaussian RKHS (Kühn). The paper would benefit from discussing related structured sparsity for kernels (e.g., hierarchical kernel learning and MKL), sparse additive models (Ravikumar et al., SpAM), and MARS, which also constrain structure for interpretability and estimation.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: formalizing a partial order on partitions and targeting the “intrinsic” minimal structure is appealing and bridges additive models and full interaction models.
- Sound use of RKHS machinery: direct-sum RKHS and representer theorem lead to a clean estimator; connecting structure complexity to capacity measures is principled.
- Consistency goal: the paper aims beyond prediction to identifiability, proving that penalized selection recovers the minimal amiable structure under assumptions.
- Interpretability value: group-level interactions of small size can capture interactions while keeping estimation feasible and models interpretable.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
1) Assumptions and uniqueness of the intrinsic structure:
- Proposition 1’s strict inclusion L2G1 ⊂ L2G2 requires independence of Xj; dependence is common in practice, and then order may not translate to strict inclusion. The implications for uniqueness and identifiability of G* under dependence are not discussed.
- Theorem 1 asserts existence/uniqueness of a minimal amiable partition because Ga is “partially ordered and complete.” Completeness and closure of Ga under meets/refinements are not established. In general, the set of partitions is a lattice, but it is not obvious that the meet of amiable structures is itself amiable for f without additional conditions. This is a key foundational point that needs a precise statement of assumptions and proof.

2) Structural penalty derivation and consistency:
- The penalty C(G) is motivated by covering number upper bounds for direct-sum RKHS. However, the transition from ln N(ε, IG, d∞) ≲ ∑u α(ε)|u|+1 to the implemented penalty μ∑u α|u| in (6) is inconsistent: α(ε)|u|+1 = α(ε)·α(ε)|u|; absorbing a factor of α into μ is fine only if α is fixed, but α is also tuned. The paper should cleanly define α, whether it depends on ε and n, and justify replacing the theoretical capacity term by the simpler power penalty.
- The selection consistency (Theorem 4) assumes “choose proper μ” but provides no guidance on how μ (and α) must scale with n to ensure that overfitting amiable superstructures are penalized while avoiding underfitting. Theorems 2–3 bound empirical vs population risks, but the link to the specific penalty in (6) is not made explicit via an oracle inequality.
- Condition on λn: Theorem 2/4 require λn so that n·λn → 0 and a positivity constraint; these differ from standard KRR regimes (often λn → 0 with nλn → ∞). This discrepancy needs justification; otherwise the estimator may overfit as n grows.

3) Capacity bounds and domain assumptions:
- The covering-number bound used (ln N ≤ C(ln(r/ε))p+1) assumes convolution kernels with exponentially decaying Fourier transforms and compact domains. The paper mixes [0,1]p and unbounded inputs (e.g., Normal in simulations) without clarifying rescaling/truncation and the conditions under which the bounds apply.
- Proposition 3 uses a decomposition with ε/|G| inside the covering numbers; a proof or reference is needed to justify the specific ε-splitting and constants.

4) Algorithms and practicality:
- Exhaustive search scales with the Bell number; infeasible beyond small p.
- The backward stepwise algorithm is underspecified: how splits are chosen (as a new singleton vs moving to an existing group), whether multiple passes/backtracking are allowed, and stopping criteria. No theoretical guarantees are offered, and empirically it fails on one model. Consider more robust search (e.g., best-split per iteration, repeated passes, merge-and-split, or stochastic local search), or convex relaxations via structured sparsity over group kernels.

5) Empirical evaluation:
- Limited scope: only five synthetic settings; tables/plots are missing in the provided excerpt; no comparisons to strong baselines (SpAM, SALSA, MARS, HKL/MKL, OKGT). No runtime analysis.
- On Boston Housing, only a qualitative visualization is given; no predictive metrics or comparison to baselines to demonstrate practical utility.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but has notational inconsistencies and imprecisions:
- Definitions of α, ε, and the jump from α|u|+1 to α|u| should be clarified.
- Several references lack years; some statements (“Ga is complete”) need proof sketches.
- Explicitly state kernel choices per group (e.g., Gaussian with product form) and preprocessing.

Originality and significance
The idea of identifying a minimal group-additive partition is interesting and, to my knowledge, novel in kernel regression. Tying structure selection to RKHS capacity is principled. However, the theoretical foundations (existence/uniqueness, penalty specification, λn regime) and the empirical support (comparisons, scalability) need strengthening to reach NeurIPS-level impact.

Questions for authors
- Under what assumptions on PX does the intrinsic structure exist and is unique? Can you prove that Ga is closed under refinement (meet) so that the minimal element is amiable?
- How should μ and α scale with n? Can you provide an oracle inequality that shows the penalized criterion prefers G* among amiable structures?
- Please reconcile the λn condition with standard KRR theory, and clarify the domain/kernels for which the covering-number bounds hold in your setting.
- Can you compare empirically against SALSA, SpAM, MARS, MKL/HKL, and OKGT, both in recovery accuracy and prediction?
- Can you improve and better specify the stepwise algorithm (multiple passes, selection of best split/merge), and add some guarantees (e.g., monotone improvement, consistency under separability)?

Pros
- Conceptually clean formulation of group-additive identification and intrinsic structure.
- RKHS-based estimation and principled complexity lens.
- Selection consistency target.

Cons
- Foundations and assumptions for uniqueness not fully justified.
- Structural penalty derivation to implementable form is ad hoc.
- λn condition unusual; scaling of μ, α unspecified.
- Limited, non-comparative experiments; algorithmic details sparse.

Recommendation: Reject in current form. The paper has promising ideas but requires stronger theoretical rigor on identifiability and penalty design, clearer assumptions, and substantially more empirical validation against competitive baselines, along with a more robust scalable algorithm. I encourage the authors to address these points; the direction is worthwhile. Confidence: high.
