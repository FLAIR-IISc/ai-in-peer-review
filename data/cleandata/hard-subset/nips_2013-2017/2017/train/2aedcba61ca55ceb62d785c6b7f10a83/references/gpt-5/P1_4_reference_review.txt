Summary
The paper tackles nonparametric regression in high dimensions by introducing and formalizing group additive models: f(X) = ∑u∈G fu(Xu), where G is a partition of predictors into groups and fu are multivariate nonparametric components. The authors (i) define a partial order on group structures and prove the existence and uniqueness of an intrinsic (minimal) group additive structure G*, (ii) propose a structural penalty for identifying G* when it is unknown, derived from covering-number capacity bounds for direct-sum RKHSs, and (iii) prove selection consistency of the globally optimal solution to the penalized problem. They give two algorithms: an exhaustive search and a backward stepwise heuristic, and present simulations and a Boston Housing case study suggesting the method can recover plausible groupings.

Relation to prior work
- Additive and functional ANOVA models are standard; this work sits between fully additive and high-order interaction expansions (e.g., smoothing spline ANOVA, ESP kernel KRR). Compared to OKGT (Pan et al.), this paper directly addresses identifiability and proposes a structure selection principle.
- The structural penalty is motivated by RKHS capacity via covering numbers (links to classical learning theory: Vapnik, Zhou, Steinwart & Christmann, Bartlett & Mendelson).
- Compared to sparse additive models and interaction selection (e.g., SpAM; high-dimensional additive modeling; hierarchical interaction selection like HierNet; SALSA), this work focuses specifically on discovering partitions of variables (grouping) rather than sparsity over coordinates or arbitrary interaction sets.

Strengths
- Clear and useful formalization of group additive structure, with a rigorous partial order and identification of a unique intrinsic structure. This is a valuable conceptual contribution for identifiability.
- Principled attempt to tie structural complexity of groupings to RKHS capacity measures. Proposition 3 on covering numbers for direct sums is natural and useful.
- Consistency results: the paper shows (under fixed p and suitable conditions) that the globally optimal penalized estimator selects G* with probability tending to 1.
- Practical interest: learning small interacting groups avoids full combinatorial explosion of high-order ANOVA while allowing interactions, with interpretability benefits.
- Empirical demonstrations indicate recoverability of true groupings on synthetic data and interpretable groupings on a real dataset.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (soundness and support)
- The derivation of the structural penalty C(G) is loose. The paper goes from ln N(ε, I(HG), ∥·∥∞) ≤ O(∑u α(ε)|u|+1) to using C(G) = ∑u α|u|, treating α as a tunable integer. This drops the +1, the ε dependence, and kernel-dependent constants. While a pragmatic simplification can work in practice, the selection consistency theorem relies on the capacity of HG, not necessarily on the particular simplified penalty used by the algorithm. A clearer bridge between the bound and the implemented penalty (including guidance on α scaling) is needed, or a theorem stated for the implemented penalty.
- Assumptions: Several results (e.g., strict inclusion L2G1 ⊂ L2G2) require independence of Xj. It is not clear which parts of the main selection consistency result rely on independence or other distributional assumptions. Clarify assumptions on PX (independence, boundedness/compactness, sub-Gaussianity) and kernels (universality, translation-invariance, bandwidth selection).
- Algorithmic guarantees: Theorems concern the global minimizer of the penalized problem. The stepwise heuristic (Algorithm 2) has no guarantee of reaching that minimizer; indeed, simulations show failures (Model 4). Discuss conditions under which the greedy procedure might be consistent or propose alternative search strategies (e.g., beam search, stochastic local search) with practical diagnostics.
- Experiments: There is no comparison to baselines (functional ANOVA with KRR/ESP kernel, SALSA, OKGT, sparse additive with pairwise interactions, HierNet/GA2M). Without these, it is hard to assess practical advantage. Also missing are runtime/complexity measurements, sensitivity to λ/α/µ, ablations on kernel choices, and robustness to correlated predictors.
- Practical details are underspecified: kernel choices per group, bandwidth selection, how λ is tuned, data splits, and how µ, α are searched or regularized. For Boston Housing, only one CV split is used; repeated CV and predictive performance relative to baselines would strengthen the case.

Clarity
- Overall well-organized; the identifiability section is clear. However:
  - The penalty derivation would benefit from a more careful presentation, keeping track of constants, the role of ε, and justifying the final simplified form used in code.
  - Notation sometimes shifts between p (total dimension) and |u| (group size) in capacity bounds; be explicit that constants depend on group dimension |u|.
  - Algorithm 2 description “either split j as a new group or add to an existing group” is too vague; define the candidate moves precisely and how ties are broken.

Originality
- The identifiability notion (intrinsic minimal group structure) and connecting structural penalty to RKHS capacity are novel and interesting. Prior work on additive/interaction models usually enforces sparsity or heredity but does not formalize minimal partitions in this way.
- That said, more discussion and comparison with related structure-selection methods (group-sparse additive models, ANOVA kernels with sparsity, interaction screening) is warranted.

Significance
- If scalable and robust, discovering intrinsic low-order groupings could be impactful for interpretable nonparametric modeling. The current theoretical results (fixed p) and heuristic search limit immediate applicability to large p; nonetheless, the conceptual and methodological direction is promising.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a theorem that selection consistency holds for the implemented penalty C(G) = ∑u α|u| (with α treated as a constant or function of n), or guidance on setting α from kernel/ε?
2. What assumptions on PX are needed for Theorems 2–4? Do results extend to correlated predictors?
3. How are kernels and bandwidths chosen per group in practice? Is the same kernel used across groups?
4. Can you report comparisons to ESP-KRR (up to order 2), SALSA, OKGT, and sparse additive models with pairwise interactions on your synthetic setups?
5. Any insights on improving Algorithm 2 (e.g., multiple random restarts, beam search, or merge-and-split moves), and its empirical scaling with p?

Pros
- Clear identifiability formalism and unique minimal group structure.
- Principled capacity-based structural penalty; selection consistency for global optimum.
- Practical algorithms and illustrative results; interpretable modeling.

Cons
- Theoretical–practical gap in the penalty form; missing algorithmic guarantees for the heuristic.
- Limited experiments, no baselines or runtime analysis; missing implementation details.
- Assumption clarity (independence, kernel conditions) needs improvement.

Minor
- Typographical/formatting issues throughout (e.g., missing years in references, inconsistent symbols).
- Define all constants in bounds; ensure equations (4)–(6) track dependence on |u| cleanly.

Recommendation
Borderline. The paper offers a thoughtful identifiability framework and an appealing capacity-based penalization idea, but the experimental evaluation and the theoretical justification of the implemented penalty/search are not yet strong enough. With clearer assumptions, stronger empirical comparisons, and tighter linkage between theory and practice, this could be a valuable contribution.
