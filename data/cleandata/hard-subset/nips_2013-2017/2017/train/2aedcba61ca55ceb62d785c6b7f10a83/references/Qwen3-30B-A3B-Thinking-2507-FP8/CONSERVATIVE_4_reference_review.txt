

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

## Summary
This paper proposes Group Additive Structure Identification (GASI), a method for identifying group additive structures in nonparametric regression. Building on the Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) method from [11], the authors claim to resolve two issues: nonidentifiability of group structures and lack of systematic structure identification. The approach uses a penalized kernel method with a complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers to identify the "intrinsic group additive structure" - defined as the minimal group structure that can represent the true regression function.

## Strengths
The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for the concept of an "intrinsic group additive structure," proving its existence and uniqueness. The derivation of the complexity measure based on RKHS covering numbers is mathematically sound, and the paper includes consistency proofs under appropriate conditions. The two-step estimation procedure (exhaustive search and stepwise algorithm) is clearly presented, and the empirical results on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset demonstrate the method's ability to identify group structures.

## Weaknesses
The paper suffers from several significant shortcomings that undermine its contribution:

1. **Lack of Novelty and Insufficient Comparison**: The core idea of group additive models is not new, as the paper itself references [11] (OKGT) which introduced group additive structures. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate how their method fundamentally differs from or improves upon [11] or other established approaches. Crucially, the paper lacks comprehensive comparisons with standard methods (e.g., standard additive models, functional ANOVA with regularization) that would demonstrate the practical advantages of their proposed complexity measure and identification approach.

2. **Unjustified Complexity Measure**: The authors propose a complexity measure C(G) = ∑α^{|uj|} based on RKHS covering numbers, but provide no evidence that this measure is superior to simpler alternatives (e.g., number of groups, group sizes, or standard model complexity metrics). The choice of using (ln(r/))^{|u|+1} appears arbitrary and lacks theoretical justification beyond the paper's own derivations. Without empirical validation showing this measure leads to better model selection, the penalty term seems ad hoc.

3. **Limited Empirical Evaluation**: The simulation studies cover only five synthetic models, and the real data application is limited to the Boston Housing dataset. The paper does not compare GASI's prediction accuracy, computational efficiency, or model interpretability against established methods. For instance, it's unclear how GASI compares to standard additive models (which are widely used and well-understood) or to functional ANOVA with appropriate regularization.

4. **Computational Scalability Concerns**: While the paper proposes a stepwise algorithm for large p, it fails to address the computational complexity of the method. The exhaustive search approach (Algorithm 1) is computationally intractable for high-dimensional data, and the stepwise approach (Algorithm 2) is described as "greedy" with potential to miss optimal solutions. The paper lacks analysis of how computational cost scales with p, which is critical for a method claiming to address high-dimensional regression.

5. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims the intrinsic group structure "achieves the greatest dimension reduction" and "mitigates the curse of dimensionality," but these claims are not substantiated with empirical evidence or detailed theoretical analysis. The consistency proofs are asymptotic and don't address finite-sample behavior, which is crucial for practical applications.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a technically sound extension of existing work, it fails to demonstrate meaningful advantages over established methods for high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The proposed complexity measure lacks justification, the empirical evaluation is insufficient, and the paper doesn't convincingly show why their approach should be preferred over simpler alternatives.

The contribution appears incremental rather than innovative, and the paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. Without stronger evidence of practical benefits and clearer differentiation from existing work, this paper does not meet NIPS standards for a high-impact contribution.

**Pro/Con Summary**
*Pros*: Rigorous theoretical foundation, clear derivation of complexity measure, consistency proofs, well-structured empirical validation
*Cons*: Lack of novelty, unjustified complexity measure, limited empirical evaluation, insufficient comparison to established methods, computational scalability concerns

**Final Recommendation: Reject**
