## Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of identifying the underlying group additive structure in nonparametric regression models, addressing a limitation of both standard additive models (which lack interaction modeling) and higher-order interaction models (which suffer from dimensionality issues). The authors propose a penalized kernel ridge regression approach to simultaneously identify the optimal grouping of predictors and estimate the corresponding nonparametric functions.  They introduce a complexity measure based on covering numbers of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs) to penalize more complex group structures, and demonstrate consistency of their method. They provide both exhaustive search and stepwise algorithms for identifying the group structure and validate their approach through simulations and a real-world dataset.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon existing work in additive models (Hastie & Tibshirani, 2009), functional ANOVA (Gu, 2002), and higher-order interaction models (Kandasamy & Yu, 2016). It acknowledges the work of Pan et al. (2011) on Optimal Kernel Group Transformation (OKGT) but addresses the issues of non-identifiability and lack of a systematic identification approach.  The connection to graphical models is also noted, highlighting the difference in focus – the paper targets group additive structure directly, rather than the more complex conditional dependence structure. The use of RKHS and covering numbers draws on established theory from functional data analysis (Ramsay & Silverman, 2005) and learning theory (Vapnik, 1998; Zhou, 2008).

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Identifying interactions in nonparametric regression is crucial for model accuracy and interpretability. This work provides a principled approach to this problem.
* **Theoretical foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical justification for their approach, including a consistency proof for the identified group structure.  The introduction of a complexity measure based on covering numbers is a novel contribution.
* **Practical algorithms:** The authors propose two algorithms – exhaustive search and a more scalable stepwise approach – enabling application to datasets of varying dimensionality.
* **Empirical validation:** The simulations demonstrate the method’s ability to recover the true group structure under different scenarios. The real-data application showcases the interpretability of the identified groups.
* **Clear formulation:** The problem is well-defined, and the mathematical notation is relatively clear (though some RKHS concepts might be challenging for readers not familiar with the field).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational complexity:** The exhaustive search algorithm is computationally expensive and may not scale to high-dimensional problems. While the stepwise algorithm is proposed to address this, its performance (especially compared to exhaustive search) needs further investigation and clear justification.
* **Choice of hyperparameters:** The method relies on tuning parameters λ, µ, and α.  The paper mentions using a validation set, but doesn't provide detailed guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice.  The sensitivity of the results to these parameters needs further analysis.
* **RKHS assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on the use of universal kernels. The impact of the choice of kernel on the performance of the method is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Limited real-data analysis:** The real-data application is limited to the Boston Housing dataset. Applying the method to other datasets with different characteristics would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Complexity of theoretical details:** While the consistency proof is a strength, the mathematical details (especially related to covering numbers) are relegated to the supplementary material, making it difficult to fully assess the rigor and generality of the theoretical results.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in nonparametric regression with a well-motivated and theoretically sound approach.
* The introduction of a complexity measure based on covering numbers is a novel contribution.
* The proposed algorithms provide practical tools for identifying group additive structures.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the exhaustive search algorithm may limit its practical applicability.
* The choice of hyperparameters requires careful tuning and its impact needs further investigation.
* The real-data analysis is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The paper proposes a principled and theoretically justified approach to identifying group additive structures, addressing a long-standing limitation of existing methods. While the computational complexity and hyperparameter tuning aspects require further attention, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

The authors should:

1.  Provide more detailed guidance on how to select the hyperparameters λ, µ, and α, perhaps through a sensitivity analysis.
2.  Further investigate the performance and scalability of the stepwise algorithm, comparing it more systematically with the exhaustive search algorithm.
3.  Consider applying the method to a wider range of real-world datasets to demonstrate its generalizability.
4.  Provide a more accessible summary of the key theoretical results in the main text, or at least a clearer motivation for the use of covering numbers.
