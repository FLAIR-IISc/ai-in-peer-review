

# Review of "Group Additive Structure Identification for Kernel Nonparametric Regression"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in high-dimensional nonparametric regression: balancing flexibility, interpretability, and computational feasibility. The authors propose a novel approach to identify the "intrinsic group additive structure" for nonparametric regression, which generalizes the standard additive model by allowing interactions within groups while preventing interactions across groups. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) rigorously defining the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group structure that can represent the true function, and (2) proposing a structural penalty based on RKHS covering numbers to identify this structure.

## Quality and Technical Soundness

The theoretical foundation of the paper is strong. The authors clearly define the intrinsic group additive structure (Theorem 1) and prove its existence and uniqueness. The connection between RKHS capacity measures and group structure complexity is well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis (Section 3) establishes consistency of the proposed method (Theorem 4). The derivation of the complexity measure C(G) = ∑α|uj|+1 in Section 2.3.2 is particularly insightful, as it provides a practical way to measure structural complexity that aligns with the RKHS capacity theory.

However, the paper would benefit from addressing several technical gaps. First, while the authors mention universal kernels (Gaussian, Laplace) as suitable choices, they don't provide guidance on kernel selection for practical applications or discuss how kernel choice affects the identified group structure. Second, the theoretical analysis assumes the true function has a group additive structure, but the paper doesn't explore performance when this assumption is violated. Third, the paper lacks comparison with state-of-the-art methods for high-dimensional nonparametric regression (e.g., [6] which uses ESP kernel for higher-order interactions, or [11] which inspired this work).

## Clarity and Organization

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The key concepts (group additive structure, intrinsic structure, complexity measure) are carefully defined with precise mathematical notation. The connection between the RKHS capacity measures and the group structure complexity is logically developed. The two-step estimation procedure (Algorithm 1) and the stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) are clearly described.

The connection to graphical models (Section 1) is somewhat confusing. The authors claim their approach directly targets the group additive structure rather than the conditional dependence structure, but they don't adequately explain why this is advantageous. A clearer distinction would strengthen the paper.

## Originality

The concept of the "intrinsic group additive structure" is novel and addresses a real limitation in the OKGT method [11]. The structural penalty based on RKHS covering numbers is also innovative. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from related work. The group additive model is similar to the "block structure" approach in [9] (Sparse Gaussian Graphical Models with Unknown Block Structure), though the authors acknowledge this. A more thorough discussion of how their method differs from these related approaches would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Significance

The problem addressed is highly significant in modern statistics and machine learning, where high-dimensional nonparametric regression is increasingly important. The group additive model provides a practical middle ground between the standard additive model (which ignores interactions) and functional ANOVA models (which become computationally intractable). The ability to automatically identify the group structure is a substantial practical contribution.

The empirical results (Section 4-5) are promising. The simulation studies show that the method successfully identifies the true group structure in various model settings, and the Boston Housing data application demonstrates practical utility. However, the paper could better highlight how the identified group structure improves interpretability and prediction compared to standard approaches.

## Major Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with related methods**: The paper doesn't compare performance with other high-dimensional nonparametric regression methods, making it difficult to assess the relative advantages of the proposed approach.

2. **Lack of practical implementation guidance**: The paper doesn't provide recommendations for kernel selection, tuning parameter selection (beyond the grid search), or how to handle categorical variables.

3. **Scalability concerns**: The paper doesn't address how the method scales to extremely high dimensions (p >> n), which is a key challenge in modern data analysis. The stepwise algorithm (Algorithm 2) is described but its computational complexity isn't analyzed.

4. **Theoretical limitations**: The theoretical analysis assumes the true function has a group additive structure, but the paper doesn't discuss the behavior when this assumption is violated.

## Strengths

- Clear theoretical foundation for the intrinsic group additive structure
- Solid theoretical analysis establishing consistency
- Practical algorithms for structure identification
- Good empirical validation through simulations and real data
- Addresses a real limitation in previous work [11]

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper with a well-motivated problem and sound theoretical contributions. The method addresses a genuine limitation in existing approaches to high-dimensional nonparametric regression. While the paper has some weaknesses, particularly in the lack of comparison with related methods and practical implementation guidance, these are not fatal flaws.

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should:

1. Add a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods for high-dimensional nonparametric regression
2. Provide more detailed discussion of the relationship to graphical models and other related approaches
3. Include practical guidance on kernel selection and parameter tuning
4. Discuss the limitations of the approach when the true function doesn't have a group additive structure

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, and with these minor revisions, it would be suitable for publication at NIPS. The theoretical foundation is solid, the empirical results are promising, and the method addresses a real problem in high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
