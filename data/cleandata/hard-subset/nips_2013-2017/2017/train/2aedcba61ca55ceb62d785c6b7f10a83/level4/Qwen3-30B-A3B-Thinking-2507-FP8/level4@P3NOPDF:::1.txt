

The paper introduces a "group additive model" for high-dimensional kernel regression, extending the standard additive model by grouping correlated features to capture interdimensional dependencies. While the theoretical framework—defining a complexity measure favoring many small groups derived from covering number bounds—appears novel, the work suffers from significant shortcomings in context, methodology, and evaluation.  

**Primary Concerns:**  
1. **Inadequate Related Work:** The paper fails to engage meaningfully with established literature. The problem of optimal feature grouping is directly relevant to structured sparsity (e.g., group LASSO), graphical model learning (e.g., Bayesian network structure discovery), and feature selection. Yet the discussion is limited to a single reference [8] (KDD 2015), omitting these well-studied connections. This omission makes it impossible to assess the paper’s novelty. For instance, structured sparsity methods explicitly optimize over groupings to handle feature correlations—precisely the problem addressed here—yet the authors provide no comparison or contrast. The claim of novelty rests on an incomplete foundation.  

2. **Suboptimal Optimization Approach:** The proposed complexity measure, while theoretically grounded, does not yield an efficiently optimizable objective. The authors resort to exhaustive search (computationally intractable for large *p*) or a greedy heuristic (reminiscent of forward selection), with no justification for why these are preferable to cross-validation-based approaches. The mention of "investigating and comparing different measures" (line 174) is unhelpful without concrete results or trade-off analysis. A method claiming to address a combinatorial optimization problem should at least discuss why standard techniques (e.g., convex relaxations, efficient heuristics) are unsuitable.  

3. **Superficial Experimental Validation:** Experiments rely on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset, with no baselines or comparisons to existing methods (e.g., group LASSO, structured sparsity techniques). Without such comparisons, the empirical results cannot demonstrate superiority or even reasonable performance. The synthetic validation lacks statistical significance testing, and the Boston Housing example offers no insight into scalability or real-world utility.  

**Positive Aspects:**  
The theoretical derivation of the complexity measure as an upper bound on the covering number is sound and represents a legitimate contribution. The formalization of the group additive model provides a useful framework for handling feature correlations, and the analysis of the complexity measure’s properties (e.g., its preference for fine-grained groupings) is insightful.  

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment:**  
The authors addressed some concerns regarding the theoretical contribution but did not substantially improve the related work or experimental design. While the core theory remains valid, the lack of engagement with structured sparsity and feature selection literature persists. The experimental section remains underdeveloped, with no baselines or comparative analysis.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper has merit in its theoretical contribution but is currently unsuitable for publication due to critical omissions. The related work section must be substantially expanded to contextualize the work within structured sparsity, graphical models, and feature selection. Experiments should include comparisons to relevant baselines (e.g., group LASSO, sparse additive models) on standard datasets. Without these revisions, the paper’s claims of novelty and utility cannot be validated.  

**Summary:**  
The group additive model framework is a reasonable extension of additive regression, but the work’s impact is undermined by insufficient literature engagement, a suboptimal optimization strategy, and unconvincing empirical validation. The authors must rigorously situate their approach within existing machine learning paradigms and provide meaningful comparisons to justify their methodological choices. The theoretical insights are valuable but insufficient to overcome the current gaps. I recommend *reject* pending major revisions to address these issues.
