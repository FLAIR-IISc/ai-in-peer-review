The paper considers kernel regression in the high-dimensional setting. The starting point is the additive model, which assumes a functional relationship of the form f = ∑p f<sub>p</sub>(X<sub>p</sub>), effectively ignoring correlations between different predictor dimensions. Recognizing this limitation, the authors instead investigate the “group additive model,” where predictor variables are grouped to capture potential correlations within those groups. They provide a formal treatment of this setting and propose algorithms for identifying optimal groupings. Results are presented for small synthetic and real-world datasets.

A key concern is the paper’s limited discussion of related work, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of the contribution. The problem of identifying optimal groupings in data is also addressed in fields like “structured sparsity” and in the learning of graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these areas are not mentioned. Similarly, the problem can be viewed as an instance of “feature selection,” but this connection is also absent. The related work section primarily cites a single KDD-2015 paper [11], which is insufficient to establish the originality of this work.

Despite this, the theoretical treatment appears novel. The authors define a complexity measure for a grouping – favoring many small groups – derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This seems reasonable. However, this measure does not easily lend itself to optimization, leading the authors to propose either an exhaustive search (which doesn’t scale well) or a greedy method (reminiscent of forward feature selection). While acknowledging the inherent difficulty of optimizing discrete functions like group structure, I was somewhat disappointed that a more efficiently optimizable measure wasn’t developed. The authors mention exploring different measures (line 174) but offer no further insight into why the chosen measure was selected.

Experimentally, the paper presents synthetic validation and a single example using the Boston Housing dataset. No baseline comparisons are provided, making it difficult to evaluate the quality of the results and assess the method’s effectiveness relative to existing approaches.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The definition of a complexity measure for group additive structures based on covering numbers appears to be a novel contribution.
*   **Formalization of Group Additive Models:** The paper provides a rigorous mathematical treatment of group additive models, defining the intrinsic group additive structure.
*   **Potential for Interpretability:** The group additive model offers a potential balance between the interpretability of additive models and the flexibility of higher-order interaction models.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Insufficient Related Work:** The paper lacks a comprehensive discussion of related work in areas like structured sparsity, graphical models, and feature selection.
*   **Optimization Challenges:** The proposed complexity measure leads to computationally challenging optimization problems, relying on either exhaustive search or a greedy approach.
*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is limited, lacking baseline comparisons and thorough testing on diverse datasets.
*   **Lack of Baselines:** The absence of baseline methods in the experimental results makes it difficult to assess the actual improvements offered by the proposed method.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution is novel and potentially impactful.
*   The problem addressed is relevant to high-dimensional nonparametric regression.
*   The paper demonstrates the feasibility of identifying group additive structures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of comprehensive related work raises concerns about novelty and positioning within the field.
*   The computational challenges associated with optimization may limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The limited experimental evaluation makes it difficult to assess the true performance of the proposed approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. However, the limited discussion of related work and the lack of thorough experimental evaluation raise concerns about its overall impact. While the theoretical results are promising, the practical applicability and significance of the method remain unclear. I recommend a rejection, but encourage the authors to address the concerns raised in this review and resubmit in the future. A significantly expanded related work section and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation with appropriate baselines are crucial for a successful resubmission.
