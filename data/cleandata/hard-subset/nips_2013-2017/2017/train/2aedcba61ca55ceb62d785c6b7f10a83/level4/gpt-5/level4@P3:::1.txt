Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies nonparametric regression in high dimensions by moving beyond additive models to a group additive model (GAM) in which variables are partitioned into groups, and the regression function is additive across groups. The key idea is that interactions are allowed within groups but not across groups, offering a middle ground between additive models (no interactions) and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models (many interactions). The paper formalizes the notion of group structures via a partial order on partitions, introduces “amiable” group structures that can represent the target function, and defines the “intrinsic” group additive structure as the minimal element (most parsimonious correct partition). 

Methodologically, the authors propose RKHS-based estimation for a given group structure and then address the unknown-structure case by penalizing the “complexity” of a group structure. The penalty is derived from covering-number capacity bounds of direct-sum RKHSs: a structure G incurs a penalty proportional to the sum over groups u in G of α|u|. They prove selection consistency of the structure-penalized estimator under appropriate conditions and provide two search procedures for the partition: exhaustive search and a greedy backward stepwise heuristic. Simulations and a Boston Housing example illustrate the approach.

This work is related to (i) additive and ANOVA models (e.g., smoothing spline ANOVA, SALSA), (ii) kernel methods and universal kernels, (iii) structured sparsity (e.g., group/overlapping group penalties for selecting interacting subsets), and (iv) graphical models (for conditional dependence). The paper cites functional ANOVA and SALSA and briefly discusses connections to graphical models. However, its relation to structured sparsity and feature selection methods is not sufficiently developed.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of partitions as group structures, with a precise partial order and existence/uniqueness of an intrinsic (minimal) structure.
- Conceptually appealing penalization that ties structural complexity to RKHS capacity via covering numbers for direct sums.
- Consistency results for structure recovery under the proposed penalty and tuning, with nontrivial uniform bounds over amiable structures.
- Practical algorithms for small p (exhaustive) and larger p (greedy stepwise), plus demonstrations on synthetic and real data.

Weaknesses
- Related work is too narrow. The paper underplays connections to structured sparsity/feature selection (e.g., group/overlapping group lasso, hierarchical interaction selection) and to alternative kernel constructions for low-order interactions.
- The complexity penalty depends on α, derived from bounds that involve an arbitrary covering precision ε; the choice and interpretation of α and its impact on identifiability and finite-sample behavior are not well justified.
- Search procedures are either combinatorially expensive (exhaustive) or heuristic (stepwise) without guarantees; no discussion of approximation quality or stopping criteria for the greedy method.
- Experimental evaluation is thin: no baselines, limited ablations, and no systematic sensitivity to λ, μ, and α. As a result, empirical significance is hard to gauge.
- Some assumptions (e.g., independence used in Proposition 1 for strict inclusion of function spaces) and conditions for consistency (choices of λn, μn) could be discussed more prominently with practical guidance.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The theoretical development appears careful, and the consistency result is interesting. The reliance on covering-number bounds is standard, though the translation into a scalar α parameter is somewhat opaque; clearer guidance on setting α would help.
- Empirical support: The simulations show recovery of true structures under favorable settings, but the absence of baselines (e.g., SALSA, functional ANOVA with KRR/ESP kernels, structured sparsity approaches) weakens the empirical case. The Boston Housing example is illustrative but not conclusive.

Clarity
- The core ideas are understandable, and the structure identification problem is well motivated. However, the exposition would benefit from:
  - A more extensive related work section situating the contribution within structured sparsity and interaction selection.
  - A clearer explanation of α (its origin from ε in covering numbers, scale, and practical tuning).
  - A more detailed presentation of the stepwise algorithm (search moves, stopping rule, complexity).
  - Minor editorial polishing to improve flow and reduce typographical errors.

Originality
- Framing structure identification via a covering-number–based penalty on direct-sum RKHSs, coupled with the intrinsic group structure notion and consistency proof, is novel to my knowledge. The overall problem of learning interacting groups is not new, but this specific kernel/capacity-based approach appears original.

Significance
- If robustly realized, identifying parsimonious interaction groups could be impactful for interpretable nonparametric modeling. The current empirical evidence is limited; broader and stronger comparisons would enhance the perceived significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand related work: Discuss structured sparsity (group/overlapping group lasso, hierarchical interaction selection), kernel-based interaction models, and additive GP models; clarify distinctions and possible synergies.
- Baselines and comparisons: Include SALSA, functional ANOVA with kernel ridge regression, and a structured sparsity baseline; compare structure recovery and predictive error across a range of p and noise levels.
- Practical tuning: Provide guidance or heuristics for selecting λ, μ, and α; show sensitivity analyses and learning curves.
- Algorithmic details: Specify the stepwise search space, scoring, and stopping; consider multiple random restarts or beam search; report runtime and scalability with p.
- Theory/exposition: Clarify the role of independence in function-space inclusions, state assumptions for Theorem 4 concisely in the main text, and interpret constants/probability bounds.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and principled penalty connecting RKHS capacity to structure selection.
- Clear formal definition of intrinsic group additive structure and consistency guarantees.
- Addresses an important gap between additive models and full interaction models with interpretability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient related-work coverage, especially regarding structured sparsity and interaction selection.
- Limited empirical evaluation with no baselines; unclear practical advantages.
- Heuristic search without guarantees and sparse guidance on tuning critical hyperparameters.

Post-rebuttal note
The rebuttal clarified parts of the theory and positioning. I remain concerned about the experimental depth and related-work coverage, but I acknowledge the technical contributions and have raised my evaluation accordingly.
