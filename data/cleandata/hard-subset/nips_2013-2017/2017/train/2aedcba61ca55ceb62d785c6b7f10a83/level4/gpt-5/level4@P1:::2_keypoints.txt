- Summary: Studies learning group-additive nonparametric regression by penalizing candidate groupings via covering numbers of direct-sum RKHSs, provides two optimization algorithms, and proves risk bounds and group-selection consistency; experiments validate approach.

- Novelty: Using covering numbers of direct-sum RKHSs to quantify grouping complexity is elegant and seemingly new.

- Clarity of setup: Formalizes intrinsic vs. amiable group structures and their partial order, clarifying the inference target.

- Theoretical support: Provides risk bounds and consistency results that align with the proposed structural penalty.

- Practical relevance: Offers a useful compromise between additive and high-order interaction models when true group sizes are small, improving interpretability.

- Computation weakness: Optimizing over group structures scales poorly with p; exhaustive search and simple backward stepwise heuristics are likely insufficient in high dimensions.

- Empirics weakness: Performance appears sensitive to tuning parameters (λ, µ, α) and the paper lacks practical guidance on selecting them.

- Writing weakness: Manuscript contains numerous typos and minor clarity issues that distract from contributions.

- Relation to prior work: Positions itself meaningfully between additive models and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction methods and connects to graphical models.

- Conceptual question: Request for a model-agnostic characterization of interaction between variables, not relying on explicit functional decomposition.

- Clarification needed (Line 169): Specify whether “almost surely” means equality PX-almost everywhere or is an asymptotic statement as n → ∞.

- Correction needed (Eq. 3): The empirical risk term is missing the summation over i.

- Terminology suggestion (Line 205): Prefer “translation-invariant kernel” over “convolution kernel.”

- Typos/minor edits: several specific fixes (e.g., “on RHS” → “on the RHS”; grammar fixes at Lines 152, 160, 247; Algorithm 2 Line 1 “Start with”; Line 251 “by comparing”).

- Overall evaluation: Technically interesting and well-motivated work with solid theory; main remaining issues are computational scalability and manuscript polishing.
