

The paper investigates kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, beginning with the additive model where the regression function is expressed as $ f = \sum_p f_p(X_p) $, effectively ignoring correlations between dimensions. Recognizing this limitation, the authors propose a "group additive model" that groups dimensions to capture correlations within groups. They provide a formal framework for this setting and introduce somewhat ad hoc algorithms for identifying optimal groupings, with results on small synthetic and real-world datasets.

The primary concern is the inadequate coverage of related work, making it difficult to assess the paper's novelty. The problem of optimal grouping overlaps significantly with structured sparsity, graphical model learning, and Bayesian networks, yet these connections are omitted. Similarly, the task resembles feature selection, but this perspective is not addressed. Consequently, the related work discussion reduces to a single reference to a KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient to evaluate the paper's contributions.

The theoretical contribution is novel: the authors define a complexity measure for groupings (favoring many small groups) derived as an upper bound on the covering number, which is conceptually sound. However, this measure does not enable efficient optimization, leading to an exhaustive search (which scales poorly) or a greedy method (analogous to forward feature selection). While discrete optimization challenges are acknowledged, I was disappointed the authors did not propose a more efficiently optimizable measure, particularly given their mention of investigating alternatives (line 174) without providing comparative insights.

Experimentally, the paper presents synthetic validation and a single example using the Boston Housing dataset, but provides no baselines or comparisons to alternative methods. Without such comparisons, it is impossible to evaluate the empirical results' quality.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors addressed some concerns in their rebuttal. I still consider the experimental evaluation insufficient and the related work discussion unacceptably poor. However, I acknowledge the paper's valid theoretical contributions and have adjusted my score upward. I strongly recommend the authors substantially improve the related work discussion in the final version.
