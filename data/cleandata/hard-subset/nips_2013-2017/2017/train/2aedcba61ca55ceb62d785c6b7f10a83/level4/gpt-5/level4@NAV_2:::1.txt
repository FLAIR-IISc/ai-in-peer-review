The paper addresses kernel-based regression in high-dimensional settings. It starts from the classical additive model, f(X) = ∑p i=1 fi(Xi), which ignores interactions across features. As this omission can be problematic, the authors consider a “group additive model,” in which variables are partitioned into groups so that within-group interactions can be modeled. They give a formal treatment of this framework and propose (somewhat) ad hoc procedures to select groupings. Empirical results are shown on small synthetic settings and a simple real-data example.

My main concern is that the discussion of related literature is too limited to assess novelty. Methods for learning groups of variables arise in structured sparsity and in learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these connections are not discussed. Likewise, the problem is closely related to feature selection, which is also not covered. As presented, the related work section effectively hinges on a single prior paper from KDD, which is insufficient for me (as a non-specialist in this subarea) to gauge the contribution’s originality.

That said, the theoretical development appears to be new. The paper proposes a straightforward structural complexity measure over groupings—one that tends to prefer many small groups—derived from an upper bound on covering numbers. This choice is reasonable. The drawback is that the resulting objective is not easy to optimize, so the paper relies either on exhaustive search (which does not scale) or on a greedy heuristic (akin to forward feature selection). While I appreciate that optimizing discrete structures is inherently difficult, I was disappointed that the authors did not arrive at a criterion enabling more efficient optimization than via cross-validation over groupings. The paper mentions having “investigated and compared different measures,” but offers no additional insight, making it hard to judge whether the selected measure is the most appropriate.

On the empirical side, the paper presents basic synthetic validations and a Boston Housing example. There are no baselines and no head-to-head comparisons with alternative approaches. As a result, it is hard to interpret the quality of the results in context.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors addressed part of my concerns in their response. I still find the experimental evaluation limited and the treatment of related work insufficiently connected to broader machine learning literature. Nonetheless, I acknowledge that the paper contains valid contributions, and I have raised my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to substantially improve the related work discussion for the camera-ready version.
