Summary:
The paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression, where the regression function is additive across groups of predictors. This setting interpolates between fully nonparametric regression (flexible but hard to fit in high dimensions) and the standard additive model (interpretable but often too restrictive). The group structure is assumed unknown and must be learned from data. The authors propose a structural penalty derived from covering numbers of RKHS balls; this penalty is added to a kernel ridge regression objective defined on the direct-sum RKHS induced by a candidate grouping. They present two algorithms for the resulting joint optimization over groupings and functions, and provide theory showing (a) the empirical risk of the fitted model approaches the population risk of the oracle model, and (b) consistency of the estimated group structure (the probability of recovering the intrinsic/true grouping tends to 1 as n → ∞). Experiments on synthetic and real data illustrate the approach.

Strengths:
- Novelty: The core idea—quantifying the complexity of a candidate group structure via covering numbers of the associated direct-sum RKHS—is elegant and, to my knowledge, new in this context.
- Clarity of setup: The paper carefully formalizes intrinsic vs. amiable group structures and their partial order, which helps define the target of inference.
- Theoretical support: The risk bounds and the group-structure selection consistency are well motivated and align with the proposed penalty.
- Practical relevance: When group sizes are small, the approach offers a useful middle ground between additive and high-order interaction models, with improved interpretability.

Weaknesses/Concerns:
- Computation: Optimizing over group structures scales poorly with p. The proposed exhaustive search and a basic backward stepwise heuristic may be insufficient in high dimensions; as acknowledged in the Discussion, this remains the main practical bottleneck.
- Empirics: While the simulations and Boston Housing example are informative, the sensitivity to tuning (e.g., λ, µ, α) appears nontrivial, and some performance degradation is reported depending on how λ is chosen. More guidance on tuning would strengthen the empirical section.
- Writing quality: The manuscript contains many typos and minor clarity issues (see below), which distract from the contributions.

Relation to prior work:
The paper situates itself between additive models and functional ANOVA/higher-order interaction models, and also discusses connections to graphical models. Assuming the discussion around Lines 31–50 is fair and reasonably complete, this appears to be a meaningful advance over prior approaches that either neglect interactions or consider an intractably large set of them.

Minor comments and questions:
- Conceptual: Is there a simple, model-agnostic characterization of “interaction” between two variables that does not rely on explicitly writing the function as in Equation (1)? A brief discussion could help readers connect the notion of grouping to standard concepts of interaction.
- Line 169: “almost surely” is used when comparing fG∗ and fG. Do you mean in the sense of equality under PX (i.e., function equivalence almost everywhere), or is this intended as a statement as n → ∞? Please clarify.
- Equation (3): The empirical risk term appears to be missing the summation over i.
- Line 205: “Translation-invariant kernel” is a more standard term than “convolution kernel” in this context.

Overall evaluation:
This is a well-motivated and technically interesting paper. The structural complexity based on covering numbers, paired with direct-sum RKHS modeling, is a compelling approach to learning group-additive structure with theoretical guarantees. The primary limitation is computational scalability in large p, where the current search procedures may not reliably find the true grouping. With improvements to the search/optimization component and polishing of the writing, this work would make a valuable contribution.
