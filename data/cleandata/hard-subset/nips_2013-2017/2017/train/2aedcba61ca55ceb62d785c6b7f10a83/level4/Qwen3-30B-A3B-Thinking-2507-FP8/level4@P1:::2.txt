

This paper introduces a method for identifying group-additive structures in nonparametric regression, where the regression function is additive across groups of predictor variables rather than individual variables. The approach addresses a key limitation of standard additive models (which ignore interactions) and fully nonparametric models (which suffer from the curse of dimensionality), offering a middle ground that can capture meaningful interactions while maintaining estimation efficiency and interpretability. The core contribution is a novel method for simultaneously identifying the optimal group structure and estimating the regression function, without requiring prior knowledge of the group partitioning.

The authors propose a structure-penalized kernel method that incorporates a complexity measure based on covering numbers of RKHS balls. This penalty term is added to the standard kernel ridge regression objective, creating a joint optimization problem over both the group structure and the regression estimates. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation, proving that the estimated group structure is consistent as the sample size increases. Two algorithms are presented for solving the optimization problem: one using exhaustive search and another employing a backward stepwise approach. The method is validated through extensive simulation studies and real data applications, demonstrating its ability to recover true group structures across various model settings.

The key innovation lies in the authors' use of covering numbers to quantify the complexity of group structures, enabling a principled approach to structure identification. The paper is comprehensive, with a well-motivated method, solid theoretical results, and thorough empirical validation. The discussion of related work (lines 31-50) adequately positions the contribution within the literature on additive models and nonparametric regression, suggesting this represents a meaningful advance in the field.

The main limitation of the approach is computational complexity when dealing with a large number of predictor variables, as noted in the discussion. While the stepwise algorithm offers a practical solution for larger problems, further work on scalable methods would be valuable. The paper is generally well-written, though it contains several typographical errors that need correction.

Strengths:
- Novel theoretical contribution using covering numbers for structure complexity
- Comprehensive theoretical results establishing consistency of the group structure estimator
- Well-designed empirical validation with both synthetic and real data
- Clear presentation of the method and its relationship to existing work
- Practical algorithms for implementation

Weaknesses:
- Computational complexity for high-dimensional problems remains a challenge
- Several typographical errors throughout the manuscript
- The connection to graphical models could be more thoroughly explored

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of nonparametric regression by providing a principled method for identifying group-additive structures. The theoretical results are sound, and the empirical validation is thorough. The method has clear potential for application in high-dimensional regression problems where interactions between variables are important but the full complexity of a nonparametric model is undesirable.

Minor comments:
- It would be helpful to provide a simpler characterization of variable interactions that doesn't require writing out the entire model in Equation (1).
- On line 169, the use of "almost surely" is unclear; does this refer to the limit as n approaches infinity?
- Equation (3) appears to be missing a summation over observations (i).
- Line 205: "convolutional kernel" might be more commonly referred to as "translation-invariant kernel."
- Typos: 
  - Line 145: "on RHS" should be "on the RHS"
  - Line 152: "reduces" should be "reduce"
  - Line 160: "G^ exists and unique" should be "G^ exists and is unique"
  - Line 247: "turning parameters" should be "tuning parameters"
  - Algorithm 2, line 1: "State with" should be "Start with"
  - Line 251: "by compare" should be "by comparing"

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated. With corrections to the typographical errors, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
