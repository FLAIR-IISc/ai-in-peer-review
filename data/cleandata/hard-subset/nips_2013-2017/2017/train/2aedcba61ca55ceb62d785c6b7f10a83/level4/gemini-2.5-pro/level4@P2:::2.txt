### Review

**Summary**

This paper investigates group-additive nonparametric regression models, which serve as a flexible intermediate between fully nonparametric models (which are difficult to estimate in high dimensions) and standard additive models (which can be overly restrictive by ignoring variable interactions). The central problem addressed is the identification of the unknown group structure from data. To solve this, the authors introduce a novel structure-penalized kernel method. The key contribution is a new penalty function for group structure complexity, derived from the covering numbers of the corresponding Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) balls. This penalty is incorporated into the kernel ridge regression objective, allowing for joint optimization over both the group partition and the regression function. The paper presents two algorithms—an exhaustive search and a backward stepwise approach—for approximately solving this challenging optimization problem. The methodology is supported by theoretical results demonstrating the consistency of the group structure estimate, ensuring that the probability of recovering the true structure approaches one with increasing sample size. The effectiveness of the proposed method is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary innovation is the principled derivation of a complexity penalty for group additive structures based on the capacity (specifically, covering numbers) of the induced function space. This is a creative and well-motivated approach to a challenging model selection problem. The work addresses an important gap between simple additive models and complex, fully nonparametric models, and assuming the authors' discussion of related work is complete, this represents a potentially significant advance in the field.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is comprehensive and technically sound. It provides a clear theoretical foundation by defining the "intrinsic group additive structure" and proving the consistency of its proposed estimator. This theoretical rigor adds substantial weight to the contribution.
*   **Completeness:** The paper presents a complete research contribution, including a novel method, theoretical justification, optimization algorithms, and empirical validation. The inclusion of both simulations and a real-data application effectively demonstrates the method's practical potential.
*   **Clarity:** For the most part, the paper is well-written and clearly organized, making a complex topic accessible to the reader.

**Weaknesses (Arguments Against Acceptance)**

*   **Computational Scalability:** The main limitation of the proposed method is the computational difficulty of optimizing over the combinatorial space of group structures, particularly as the number of predictor variables grows. The proposed backward stepwise algorithm is a reasonable heuristic but, being greedy, is not guaranteed to find the optimal structure. This scalability issue may limit the method's applicability in very high-dimensional settings.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** While generally clear, the paper is marred by a significant number of typographical and grammatical errors. These errors detract from the overall quality of the manuscript and should be carefully corrected in a final version.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

The following points are intended to help the authors improve the manuscript:

*   **On the concept of "interaction":** Is there a simpler characterization of when two variables "interact" that does not require defining the full group additive structure in Equation (1)? A more direct, pairwise definition might improve the reader's intuition.
*   **Line 169:** The phrase "almost surely (in population)" is slightly ambiguous. Does this refer to the asymptotic behavior as the sample size `n` approaches infinity, or is it with respect to the data-generating distribution for a fixed `n`? Please clarify.
*   **Equation (3):** This equation appears to be missing a summation over the sample index `i` inside the empirical risk term.
*   **Line 205:** The term "convolution kernel" is used. "Translation-invariant kernel" is a more common and perhaps more descriptive term in the machine learning literature.
*   **Suggested Edits/Typos:**
    *   Line 145: "on RHS" should be "on the RHS".
    *   Line 152: "not only can the bias of f̂λ,G reduces" should be "not only can the bias of f̂λ,G be reduced" or "not only does the bias of f̂λ,G reduce".
    *   Line 160: "G* exists and unique" should be "G* exists and is unique".
    *   Line 247: "turning parameters" should be "tuning parameters".
    *   Algorithm 2, Line 1: "State with" should be "Start with".
    *   Line 251: "by compare" should be "by comparing".
