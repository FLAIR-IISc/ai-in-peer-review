The paper investigates kernel regression within a high-dimensional framework, moving beyond the limitations of standard additive models – which assume independence between dimensions – to a “group additive model” that allows for correlation within defined groups. The authors present a formal analysis of this grouped structure and propose algorithms, albeit somewhat heuristically, for identifying effective groupings. Their evaluation includes results on both synthetic and real datasets of limited size.

A primary weakness of the paper lies in its insufficient contextualization within existing literature. The problem of optimal grouping bears a strong relationship to fields such as structured sparsity and the learning of graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these connections are not discussed. Similarly, the work could be positioned within the feature selection literature, but this is also overlooked. The related work section predominantly cites a single paper from KDD-2015 [8], which limits my ability to assess the true novelty of the contribution.

Despite this, the theoretical development appears original. The authors introduce a complexity measure for groupings – favoring numerous smaller groups – derived as an upper bound on the covering number, which seems logically sound. However, this measure presents optimization challenges. The proposed solutions – exhaustive search (computationally expensive) and a greedy approach (resembling forward feature selection) – are less than ideal. While optimizing discrete functions is inherently difficult, I expected a complexity measure more amenable to efficient optimization than reliance on cross-validation. The authors mention explorations of alternative measures (line 174), but offer no details to justify the chosen approach.

The experimental evaluation consists of basic synthetic validation and a single demonstration on the Boston Housing dataset, lacking comparison to baseline methods. This absence makes it difficult to interpret the significance of the observed results.

== Post rebuttal ==

The authors’ response to my review addressed some of my initial concerns. While I maintain that the experimental evaluation remains limited and the connection to relevant machine learning literature is inadequately established, I recognize that the paper does present valid contributions. Consequently, I have revised my score accordingly. I strongly recommend the authors significantly expand their discussion of related work prior to publication.
