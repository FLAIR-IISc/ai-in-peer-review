- Paper studies group-additive kernel regression allowing interactions within predefined variable groups; derives a covering-number complexity measure favoring many small groups and proposes exhaustive/greedy search; experiments on small synthetic data and Boston Housing.

- Strength: Clearly motivated — standard additive models can be too restrictive when interactions are localized.

- Strength: Theoretical treatment appears sound; complexity measure is intuitive and formalizes model/grouping trade-offs.

- Strength: Algorithms are simple and easy to implement, suitable for small-to-moderate dimensions or simple group structure.

- Weakness: Related work is inadequately covered; missing connections to structured sparsity (group/overlapping group lasso), kernelized nonlinear extensions, additive/ANOVA kernels, multiple kernel learning, graphical model structure learning, and interaction discovery methods.

- Weakness: Complexity measure lacks an accompanying optimization strategy beyond exhaustive/greedy search; no guarantees for the greedy procedure or discussion of properties (e.g., submodularity) that would justify the heuristic.

- Weakness: Computational scalability not analyzed; unclear how search scales with number of features, candidate groups, kernel evaluations, or performance in high-dim/low-sample regimes.

- Weakness: Experiments are thin — only small synthetic studies and one real dataset, with no baselines, ablations, sensitivity analyses, or measurements of computational cost.

- Weakness: Synthetic experiments do not sufficiently probe group recovery, robustness to misspecification, or effects of varying group sizes and overlap.

- Clarity: Paper is generally well written but several definitions/assumptions need precision (e.g., whether groups can overlap, how kernels are composed across groups, how regularization is applied per group).

- Clarity: Claim of having compared different measures is unhelpful without presenting alternatives or insights.

- Suggestion: Substantially expand related work to position contributions relative to structured sparsity, additive/ANOVA kernels, MKL, interaction discovery, and structure learning; clearly state what is new.

- Suggestion: Strengthen empirical evaluation with multiple real datasets, strong baselines (additive kernels, structured sparsity methods, MKL, random forests/GB), predictive/calibration/runtime metrics, ablations, and synthetic tests for group recovery and misspecification.

- Suggestion: Analyze computational complexity and provide practical guidance on candidate group generation, pruning, and scalability.

- Suggestion: Provide theoretical or empirical evidence supporting the greedy search (e.g., approximate submodularity or bounded guarantees) or propose a more principled surrogate objective for efficient optimization.

- Overall: Relevant problem with a coherent theoretical framework and a notable complexity measure, but insufficient positioning in related literature and weak empirical validation limit assessment of novelty and practical impact.

- Post-rebuttal: Authors addressed some concerns, but experiments remain too limited and related-work discussion still insufficient; reviewer raised score but urges substantial expansion of related work and empirical comparisons.
