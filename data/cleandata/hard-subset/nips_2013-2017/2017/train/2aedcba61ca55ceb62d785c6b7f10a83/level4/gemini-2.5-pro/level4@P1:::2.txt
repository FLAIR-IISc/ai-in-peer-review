### Summary

This paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models. These models assume the regression function is additive across distinct groups of predictor variables, offering a compromise between fully nonparametric models, which are difficult to fit in high dimensions, and standard additive models, which can be overly restrictive. The central problem addressed is learning this group structure from data when it is not known a priori. To this end, the authors propose a novel penalty on model complexity, derived from the covering numbers of RKHS balls, and incorporate it into the kernel ridge regression objective. The resulting objective function is optimized over both the group structure—which defines the function space as a direct sum of RKHSs over the variable groups—and the regression function itself. The paper presents two algorithms for this complex optimization task. Theoretical contributions include a convergence rate for the empirical risk and a proof of consistency for the group structure estimator, showing it recovers the true group structure with probability approaching one as the sample size increases. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.

### Main Comments

The paper's key innovation is its principled approach to quantifying the complexity of a group structure via the covering number of the corresponding direct sum RKHS. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed penalty. The paper is comprehensive, presenting a novel and well-motivated method supported by solid theoretical analysis and empirical results. Based on the provided review of related work (Lines 31-50), this method appears to be a significant advance for fitting models that interpolate between additive and fully nonparametric regression.

A primary limitation, acknowledged by the authors, is the computational challenge of optimizing over the combinatorial space of group structures, especially when the number of variables is large. The proposed backward stepwise algorithm is a practical heuristic, but it is not guaranteed to find the optimal solution. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, though it would benefit from careful proofreading.

### Minor Comments/Questions

-   Is there a simpler or more intuitive characterization of "interaction" between two variables than the one implied by Equation (1)? For example, could it be defined in terms of mixed partial derivatives or other functional properties?
-   Line 169: The use of "almost surely" is unclear. Does this refer to the limit as n -> infinity, or with respect to the data-generating distribution for a fixed n?
-   Equation (3) appears to be missing a summation over the data points (i=1 to n) in the empirical risk term.
-   Line 205: The term "convolution kernel" is used. In the machine learning literature, "translation-invariant kernel" is a more common term for this concept.
