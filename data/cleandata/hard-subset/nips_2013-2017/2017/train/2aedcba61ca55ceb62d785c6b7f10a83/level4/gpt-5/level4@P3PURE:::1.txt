This paper studies kernel regression in high-dimensional settings. It starts from the additive model, f = ∑p fp(Xp), which ignores correlations across dimensions. To address this limitation, the authors consider a group additive model, in which features are partitioned into groups so that within-group correlations can be modeled. The paper provides a formal treatment of this setting and proposes somewhat ad hoc algorithms for finding groupings. Results are reported on small synthetic datasets and a simple real-world example.

My main concern is the treatment of related work. As a non-expert, I find it difficult to assess the true novelty of the contributions because the paper does not situate its ideas clearly within the broader literature. Closely related goals—identifying groups of variables that act together—also arise in structured sparsity, as well as in learning graphical models and Bayesian networks. Likewise, the problem can be framed as feature selection. None of these connections are discussed. In practice, the related work section largely reduces to a single KDD 2015 paper [8], which is insufficient for establishing novelty.

On the positive side, the theoretical development appears novel. The authors define a simple complexity measure on a grouping, derived as an upper bound on the covering number, which tends to favor many small groups. This is a sensible direction. However, the resulting criterion is not easy to optimize. The paper offers either an exhaustive search (which does not scale) or a greedy approach akin to forward feature selection. While I appreciate that discrete optimization over groupings is generally hard, I was disappointed that the authors did not arrive at a measure that could be optimized more efficiently than by resorting to cross-validation. The paper notes they have been “investigating and comparing different measures” (line 174) but gives no further insight, making it hard to judge the suitability of the chosen measure.

Experimentally, the paper provides simple synthetic validation and a small-scale example on the Boston Housing dataset. There are no baselines and no comparisons to alternative methods. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to assess the practical value of the approach.

Post-rebuttal
The authors clarified some of my concerns in their rebuttal. I still find the experiments thin and the connections to established ideas in machine learning unacceptably underdeveloped. Nevertheless, I acknowledge that the paper contains valid contributions and have raised my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to substantially improve the discussion of related work in any final version.
