Summary:
The paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models, where, for some partition of the predictors, the regression function is additive across groups. This framework interpolates between fully nonparametric models, which are hard to fit, and additive models, which can be too restrictive. The key setting here is that the group structure is unknown and must be learned from data. To address this, the paper proposes a novel penalty based on covering numbers of RKHS balls, added to the kernel ridge regression objective. The resulting objective is optimized jointly over the group structure (which, together with the kernel, defines a function space via a direct sum of RKHSs over groups) and the within-group regression functions. Two algorithms are provided to approximately solve this coupled optimization. Theoretical results then establish (a) a rate at which the empirical risk of the estimator approaches the true risk of the true optimum, and (b) consistency of the estimated group structure, i.e., the probability it matches the true grouping tends to 1 as n → ∞. Experiments on synthetic and real data are also presented.

Main Comments:
The paper’s key innovation is to quantify the complexity of a given group structure via covering numbers of the corresponding direct-sum space. The work is comprehensive, with a well-motivated and novel method supported by reasonably solid theoretical and empirical results. I am not deeply familiar with other approaches to models between fully additive and fully nonparametric; assuming the discussion in Lines 31–50 is reasonably complete, this appears to be a potentially significant advance. As noted in the Discussion, the main practical limitation is the difficulty of optimizing over the group structure when the number of variables is large. The paper is generally clear, aside from numerous typos.
