- Proposal: Introduces a group-additive regression model allowing interactions within groups and defines an intrinsic group additive structure unique under a natural partial order.  
- Method: Proposes a structure-penalized kernel estimator using direct-sum RKHSs per group with a penalty motivated by covering numbers that depends on group sizes.  
- Algorithms: Offers two search strategies—exhaustive search with validation and a backward stepwise heuristic (scalability concerns noted).  
- Theory: Provides risk bounds, an amiable/non-amiable separation result, and consistency of group-structure selection under stated conditions.  
- Relation to prior work: Positions between GAMs and interaction-rich methods, builds on OKGT but adds identifiability and systematic structure selection; should relate more to hierarchical kernel learning and sparse-interaction models.  
- Theory critique: Formalization and uniqueness are well-motivated, but assumptions (e.g., independence, kernel regularity, universality) need to be stated explicitly and guidance on how µ and α scale with n is lacking.  
- Method critique: Complexity penalty is well-justified; practical choice of α is ad hoc and needs data-driven calibration; greedy search can miss true structures—stronger heuristics are needed.  
- Experimental critique: Synthetic experiments show recovery but are sensitive to tuning and solver; real-data example is interpretable but lacks baseline comparisons and predictive performance metrics.  
- Clarity: Paper is readable but has many typos and notational/terminology issues; needs clearer separation of L2 vs RKHS, clearer conditions for existence/uniqueness, and explanation of universal-kernel role.  
- Originality: Definition of intrinsic group additive structure and covering-number-based penalty appear novel and distinct from existing structured-sparsity approaches.  
- Significance: Framework is promising and interpretable; impact depends on improving scalability and empirical validation against baselines.  
- Pros: Clear formalization of intrinsic structure, novel RKHS-based structural penalty, supporting theory for consistency, and interpretability in real data.  
- Cons: Scalability of partition search, sensitivity to tuning parameters (λ, µ, α), limited empirical baselines, and unclear usage of probabilistic language (e.g., “almost surely”).  
- Suggested theoretical question: Provide a population-level characterization of “no interaction across groups” (e.g., via orthogonal decompositions or conditional expectations).  
- Suggested assumption clarity: State exactly where predictor independence is required and whether uniqueness results hold without it.  
- Kernel issues: Discuss sensitivity to non-universal kernels and mismatched bandwidths and the resulting approximation bias.  
- Penalty calibration: Provide guidance on choosing µ and α as functions of n, p, and kernel hyperparameters or propose data-driven estimation methods.  
- Algorithmic suggestions: Consider beam search, randomized restarts, MCMC over partitions, split–merge moves, or convex/structured-sparsity relaxations to improve robustness.  
- Ties handling: Explain how ties or near-ties in penalized risk are resolved at finite n when multiple amiable partitions have similar scores.  
- Typos/minor edits: Several concrete fixes needed (e.g., Eq.(3) missing ∑ over i, wording corrections like “on the RHS”, algorithm wording, and clarification of “almost surely”).  
- Overall recommendation: Solid and novel theoretical contribution; encourage acceptance if experiments are strengthened and assumptions/tuning guidance are clarified, otherwise still a promising direction.
