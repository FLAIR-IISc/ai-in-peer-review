The paper considers kernel regression in high-dimensional settings. It begins with the “additive model,” which represents regressors in the form of f = ∑ f<sub>p</sub>(X<sub>p</sub>), effectively ignoring correlations between different dimensions. Recognizing the limitations of this approach, the authors instead introduce the “group additive model,” where dimensions are grouped to allow for modeling correlations within those groups. They provide a formal treatment of this setting and propose several (somewhat) ad hoc algorithms for finding optimal groupings. Results are presented on small synthetic datasets and real-world cases.

My primary concern with the paper is its inadequate discussion of related work. This makes it difficult for me, as a non-expert, to assess the true novelty of the contribution. The task of finding optimal groupings in data is also addressed in fields like “structured sparsity” and when learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these areas receive no mention. Similarly, the problem can be viewed as a specific instance of “feature selection,” but this connection is also not made. As a result, the related work section largely consists of a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient for me to properly evaluate the paper’s originality.

That said, I find the theoretical treatment to be novel. The authors define a simple complexity measure for a grouping – one that favors many small groups – derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This seems reasonable. The drawback is that this measure is not easily optimized. Consequently, the authors propose either an exhaustive search (which doesn’t scale well) or a greedy method (similar to forward feature selection). While I understand that optimizing discrete functions – such as those describing optimal groupings – is generally challenging, I was somewhat disappointed that they didn’t arrive at a measure that could be optimized more efficiently than relying solely on cross-validation. The authors mention that they have been “investigating and comparing different measures” (line 174), but offer no further details, making it difficult to judge the suitability of their chosen measure.

Experimentally, the authors provide a straightforward synthetic validation of their framework, along with a single example using the Boston Housing dataset. They provide no baseline comparisons to other methods. Again, without a comparative analysis, it’s difficult to determine whether the presented results are meaningful.

== Post rebuttal ==

The authors addressed some of my concerns in their rebuttal. I still believe the experiments are limited, and the paper suffers from a significant lack of connection to relevant work in machine learning. However, I do acknowledge that the paper presents valid contributions and have adjusted my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to substantially improve their discussion of related work before publication.
