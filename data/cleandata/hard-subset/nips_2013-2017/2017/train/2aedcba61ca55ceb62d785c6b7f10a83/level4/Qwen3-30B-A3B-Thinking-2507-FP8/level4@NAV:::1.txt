

The paper addresses kernel regression in high-dimensional settings by proposing a "group additive model" as an extension to the standard additive model (f = ∑fₚ(Xₚ)), which ignores cross-dimensional correlations. The group additive model partitions features into groups to capture such correlations, offering a formal framework and ad hoc algorithms for optimal grouping. Results are demonstrated on small synthetic and real-world datasets (Boston Housing).

The primary concern is the inadequate coverage of related work. The paper fails to reference key areas addressing similar grouping problems, such as structured sparsity, graphical model learning, or feature selection. Instead, it solely cites a single KDD-2015 paper [8], making it impossible to assess the work's novelty. The theoretical contribution—defining a complexity measure favoring small groups as an upper bound on the covering number—appears novel and well-motivated. However, this measure does not enable efficient optimization; the authors resort to computationally expensive exhaustive search or a greedy approach (reminiscent of forward feature selection). While discrete optimization challenges are acknowledged, the absence of a more tractable measure (beyond cross-validation) or discussion of alternative measures (mentioned in line 174) is disappointing.

Experimentally, the validation lacks baselines and comparative methods, rendering the results on synthetic data and Boston Housing inconclusive. The absence of performance benchmarks prevents evaluation of the approach's efficacy.
