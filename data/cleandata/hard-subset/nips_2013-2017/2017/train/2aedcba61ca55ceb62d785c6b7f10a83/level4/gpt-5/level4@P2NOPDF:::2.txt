Summary:
The paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression, where the predictor variables are partitioned into groups and the regression function is additive across groups. This setup interpolates between fully nonparametric models (flexible but hard to fit) and classical additive models (often too restrictive). The group structure is unknown and must be learned from data. The authors introduce a new penalty, based on covering numbers of RKHS balls, and add it to the kernel ridge regression objective. This yields an optimization over both the group partition (which, with the kernel, defines a direct-sum RKHS) and the within-group regression functions. Two approximate algorithms are proposed to solve this joint problem. Theoretical results establish (a) rates at which the empirical risk of the estimator converges to the population risk of the true optimum, and (b) consistency of the estimated group structure, i.e., the probability of recovering the true partition tends to 1 as n → ∞. Experiments on synthetic and real data are provided.

Main Comments:
The central contribution is to quantify the complexity of a group partition via covering numbers of the associated direct-sum RKHS. The paper is comprehensive, with a well-motivated and novel method, along with solid theoretical and empirical support. I am not deeply familiar with alternative approaches that interpolate between additive and fully nonparametric models, but if the discussion in Lines 31–50 is accurate and complete, this appears to be a meaningful advance. As noted in the Discussion, the main practical limitation is the difficulty of optimizing over group structures when the number of variables is large. The paper is generally clear, though marred by numerous typos.
