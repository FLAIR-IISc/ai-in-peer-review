### Summary

This paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models. In this model class, the regression function is additive across groups of predictor variables, where the groups form a partition of the variable set. This approach interpolates between the fully nonparametric model, which is often difficult to fit, and the purely additive model, which can be overly restrictive. The paper focuses specifically on learning this group structure from data when it is not known a priori.

To this end, the authors propose a novel penalty function based on the covering numbers of RKHS balls, which is incorporated into the kernel ridge regression objective. The resulting objective function is optimized over both the group structure—which, together with the kernel, defines the function space as a direct sum of RKHSs over the variable groups—and the regression estimate itself. The authors present two algorithms for approximately solving this complex optimization problem. They also provide theoretical results establishing convergence rates for the proposed estimator and proving the consistency of the group structure selection (i.e., the probability of recovering the true group partition converges to 1 as n increases). The paper concludes with experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

### Main Comments

The key innovation of this paper is the proposal to quantify the complexity of a given group structure using the covering numbers of the corresponding direct sum RKHS. The paper is comprehensive, presenting a well-motivated and novel method supported by reasonably solid theoretical and empirical results.

While I am not an expert on methods that interpolate between additive and fully nonparametric models, assuming the authors' survey of related work (Lines 31-50) is comprehensive, this paper represents a potentially significant advance. The primary limitation, as the authors acknowledge in their discussion, is the computational challenge of optimizing over the group structure when the number of variables is large. Overall, the paper is clearly written, although it suffers from numerous typographical errors.
