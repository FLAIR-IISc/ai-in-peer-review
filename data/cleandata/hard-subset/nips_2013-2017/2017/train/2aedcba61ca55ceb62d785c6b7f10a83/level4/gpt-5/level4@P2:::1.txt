This paper studies nonparametric regression in high dimensions by moving beyond standard additive models. The authors formalize a group additive model in which predictors are partitioned into groups and the regression function decomposes additively across groups, thereby allowing interactions within a group while avoiding the combinatorial explosion of full interaction models. They introduce the notion of an intrinsic (minimal) group additive structure for a target function, show its existence and uniqueness, and connect group structures to direct-sum RKHSs. They propose a structure-penalized kernel regression objective whose penalty is derived from covering-number bounds for the induced RKHS, and prove selection consistency for recovering the intrinsic group structure. Two search strategies are offered (exhaustive and a backward stepwise heuristic), with experiments on synthetic data and the Boston Housing dataset.

Relation to prior work: The paper positions group additive models between classical additive models and higher-order interaction models (e.g., functional ANOVA, ESP kernels), and cites OKGT as a related idea. However, the related-work discussion is too narrow for NIPS standards. There are strong connections to sparse and structured sparsity (e.g., group/overlapping group penalties for interactions), sparse additive models and variants, hierarchical interaction modeling, and graphical models/conditional independence learning. These literatures also address grouping or interaction discovery and should be discussed and contrasted, including differences in identifiability, penalties, computational scaling, and guarantees.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of group additive structures and a well-motivated definition of the intrinsic (minimal) structure. The partial order and existence/uniqueness result are useful and intuitive.
- A principled complexity penalty derived from covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs, leading to a tractable surrogate that increases with group size.
- Consistency results that separate amiable vs. non-amiable structures and show selection consistency under appropriate tuning.
- Practical algorithms (exhaustive search for small p; backward stepwise for larger p) and an application demonstrating interpretability.

Weaknesses
- Related work is insufficient. The paper does not adequately situate contributions relative to structured sparsity for interactions, sparse additive models, hierarchical interaction learning, or graphical models/Bayesian networks. This makes novelty difficult to assess.
- The proposed structural penalty, while theoretically motivated, yields a challenging discrete optimization. The search procedures (exhaustive, greedy) do not scale and lack approximation or recovery guarantees; tuning relies on cross-validation without guidance on stability or sensitivity.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: small synthetic cases and Boston Housing, with no baselines. There is no comparison to functional ANOVA/RKHS ANOVA kernels, SALSA/additive approximations, sparse additive models, group-lasso-based interaction selection, or graphical-model approaches to interaction discovery.
- Practical aspects are underexplored: sensitivity to kernel choice and hyperparameters (λ, μ, α), robustness to noise/misspecification, runtime/complexity as p grows, and behavior when the true structure is only approximately group additive.
- Some theoretical pieces hinge on capacity upper bounds and universal kernels; implications for finite samples and non-universal kernels could be discussed more explicitly.

Quality
- The theoretical framework is carefully developed and the main claims (existence/uniqueness of the intrinsic structure, consistency under the proposed penalty) are plausible and valuable. However, algorithmic quality is limited by reliance on brute-force or greedy search without guarantees, and empirical validation is too thin to support strong claims about practical performance.

Clarity
- The technical exposition is largely clear. The paper would benefit from an expanded related-work section, a more explicit discussion of computational complexity, and clearer guidance on tuning (μ, α) and kernel selection.

Originality
- The intrinsic structure definition and the specific capacity-derived structural penalty for group additive RKHSs appear novel. The overall problem—discovering interaction groupings—is related to existing fields that are not adequately referenced, which obscures the precise novelty.

Significance
- If scalable and competitive, the approach could provide a useful middle ground between purely additive and fully nonparametric interaction models. Current experiments and algorithms do not yet demonstrate clear state-of-the-art impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand related work: discuss sparse/structured additive models, hierarchical interaction selection, group/overlapping group lasso for interactions, and connections/differences to graphical models/ANOVA kernels.
- Add baselines and ablations: compare to functional ANOVA kernels, SALSA/additive approximations, sparse additive models, group-lasso-based interaction discovery, and perhaps simple two-way interaction selection with hierarchical constraints.
- Provide computational analysis: complexity per evaluation of a structure, scaling of the stepwise method, and heuristics to prune the search.
- Analyze sensitivity: kernel choice, λ/μ/α tuning, and robustness to approximate group additivity.
- Consider alternative structural penalties (e.g., data-dependent complexities such as empirical Rademacher complexity) and discuss why covering-number-based penalties were preferred.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear and interesting formalization of intrinsic group structures.
- Theoretically principled structural penalty with consistency guarantees.
- Potentially interpretable models that capture interactions without full combinatorial blow-up.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient related-work coverage; novelty relative to several established areas is unclear.
- Limited, small-scale experiments with no baselines.
- Non-scalable search procedures and no guarantees for the greedy algorithm.
- Tuning and practical guidance are minimal.

Post-rebuttal
The rebuttal addressed some clarity concerns, but the empirical evaluation remains thin and the related-work discussion is still inadequate for NIPS. I acknowledge the theoretical contributions and have raised my score accordingly. I encourage the authors to substantially strengthen the related-work section and add competitive baselines in a camera-ready version.
