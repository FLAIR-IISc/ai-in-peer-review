The paper explores kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, introducing the "additive model" as its starting point. This model considers regressors of the form f = ∑ f_p(X_p), which ignores correlations between different dimensions. However, neglecting these correlations is problematic, prompting the authors to propose the "group additive model." This alternative approach groups different dimensions, enabling the modeling of correlations between certain dimensions. The authors provide a formal treatment of this setting and propose some algorithms for finding optimal groupings, albeit in a somewhat ad hoc manner. The results are presented for small synthetic and real-world cases.

My primary concern with the paper is its inadequate description of related work, making it challenging for a non-expert like myself to assess the true novelty of the work. The task of finding optimal groups in data is also explored in "structured sparsity" and when learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet the authors fail to mention these fields. Similarly, the problem can be viewed as an instance of "feature selection," which is not discussed. As a result, the related work described in the paper is limited to a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient for me to determine the actual novelty of the paper.

That being said, the theoretical treatment appears to be novel. The authors define a simple complexity measure on a grouping, which favors many small groups. This measure is derived as an upper bound on the covering number, making it a sensible approach. However, this measure does not lend itself to easy optimization, prompting the authors to propose either an exhaustive search (which scales poorly) or a greedy method (akin to forward feature selection). While I acknowledge that discrete functions, such as those describing optimal groupings, are generally difficult to optimize, I was somewhat disappointed that the authors did not arrive at a measure that could be optimized more efficiently than just using cross-validation. The authors mention that they have been "investigating and comparing different measures" (line 174), but they provide no further insights, making it hard for me to determine the suitability of the chosen measure.

Experimentally, the authors provide simple synthetic validation of their framework along with a basic example on the Boston Housing data. However, they do not provide a baseline or compare their method with other approaches, making it difficult for me to assess the quality of the presented results.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors addressed some of my concerns in their rebuttal. Although I still believe the experiments are limited, and the authors did an unacceptably poor job of relating their work to ideas in machine learning, I acknowledge that the paper has valid contributions. Consequently, I have improved my score somewhat. I strongly encourage the authors to enhance their discussion of related work when the paper is published.
