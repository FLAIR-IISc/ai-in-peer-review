**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of structure identification in group-additive nonparametric regression models. Such models serve as an effective compromise between restrictive additive models, which ignore variable interactions, and fully nonparametric models, which suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The central contribution is a novel, structure-penalized kernel method designed to simultaneously identify the unknown grouping of predictor variables (the "intrinsic group additive structure") and estimate the nonparametric regression function. The key to this method is a new complexity penalty for group structures, which the authors derive from the covering number of the associated Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The paper presents two algorithms for optimizing the proposed objective function, provides theoretical results establishing the consistency of the structure identification, and demonstrates the method's effectiveness through experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strengths of this paper are its originality and completeness. The core idea of using a complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers to penalize group additive structures is novel, theoretically sound, and well-motivated. This provides a principled approach to a challenging model selection problem. The paper is comprehensive, covering the theoretical formulation, optimization algorithms, formal consistency guarantees, and empirical validation. The theoretical results, in particular the proof of consistency for identifying the intrinsic group structure, lend significant weight to the proposed method. If the discussion of prior work is accurate, this work represents a potentially significant advance for fitting flexible and interpretable high-dimensional regression models.

The main weakness concerns the computational scalability of the proposed method. The exhaustive search algorithm is computationally prohibitive as the number of predictors grows. The backward stepwise algorithm offered as an alternative is a greedy heuristic that is not guaranteed to find the optimal structure and, as seen in the simulations (e.g., Model 4), can fail to recover the true model. This computational challenge may limit the practical utility of the method in high-dimensional settings, which is a primary motivation for this line of work.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   Novel and theoretically-grounded penalty for group structure identification based on RKHS covering numbers.
*   Comprehensive paper with a complete theoretical analysis, including consistency proofs.
*   Addresses the important problem of balancing flexibility and interpretability in nonparametric regression.
*   Solid experimental results on a variety of synthetic and real data settings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The proposed optimization algorithms have scalability issues, which may limit the method's applicability to problems with a large number of variables.
*   The greedy nature of the stepwise algorithm is a significant limitation.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is generally well-written, but would benefit from further clarification and polishing.

*   **Clarity:**
    *   For the reader's intuition, could the authors provide a simpler characterization of what it means for two variables to "interact" in this context, perhaps without direct reference to the formal definition in Equation (1)?
    *   In the discussion of amiable group structures (around Section 2.1), the use of "almost surely" in the phrase "fG∗ = fG almost surely (in population)" is slightly confusing. Does this refer to an asymptotic property as n → ∞, or with respect to the measure Px? Please clarify.
    *   In Equation (3), the summation over the data points `i` from 1 to `n` appears to be missing inside the empirical risk term.
    *   The term "convolution kernel" is used. While correct, "translation-invariant kernel" is perhaps more common in the machine learning literature.

*   **Typos and Minor Corrections:** The manuscript contains a number of typos and grammatical errors that should be corrected in a final version. Examples include:
    *   "on RHS" should be "on the RHS"
    *   "not only can the bias of f̂ reduce" should be "not only can the bias of f̂ be reduced"
    *   "G∗ exists and unique" should be "G∗ exists and is unique"
    *   "turning parameters" should be "tuning parameters"
    *   In Algorithm 2: "State with" should be "Start with"
    *   "by compare" should be "by comparing"
