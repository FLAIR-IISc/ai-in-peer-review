Summary:
This paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models, where predictor variables are partitioned into groups and the regression function is additive across groups. This formulation interpolates between fully nonparametric regression (often hard to fit) and classical additive models (potentially too restrictive). The group structure is assumed unknown and is learned from data. The authors propose a novel complexity penalty, based on covering numbers of RKHS balls for direct-sum spaces induced by a given group structure and kernel, and add it to a kernel ridge regression objective. They present two algorithms to approximately optimize over both the group structure and the functions within groups. Theoretical results include convergence rates for empirical-to-true risk and consistency of the recovered group structure as n → ∞. Experiments on synthetic and real data demonstrate the method’s utility.

Strengths:
- Novelty: The key insight is to quantify the complexity of a candidate group structure via covering numbers of the induced direct-sum RKHS. This is a principled way to regularize structure learning in nonparametric settings.
- Breadth: The paper provides a coherent pipeline: model, penalty, algorithms, and theory, along with empirical validation.
- Theory: The guarantees on excess risk and consistency of the estimated group structure are compelling and align with the learning objective.
- Clarity (mostly): The exposition is generally clear and well-motivated.

Concerns and suggestions:
- Optimization over group structures: The main practical limitation is the difficulty of optimizing the nonconvex, combinatorial objective when the number of variables is large. While the two proposed algorithms are reasonable heuristics, more discussion of their computational complexity, scalability, initialization strategies, and potential failure modes would strengthen the paper. Empirical sensitivity analyses (e.g., runtime and accuracy vs. p and group sizes) would also be helpful.
- Related work: I am not deeply familiar with all alternatives that interpolate between additive and fully nonparametric models. Assuming the coverage in Lines 31–50 is fairly complete, the contribution appears significant. Still, it may help readers to more explicitly position this approach relative to:
  - Sparse/additive models with interaction search (e.g., hierarchical group lasso for interactions).
  - Multiple-kernel learning approaches that combine kernels over subsets of variables.
  - Bayesian nonparametric methods that encourage sparse interaction structures.
- Practical guidance: Provide more actionable guidance on choosing tuning parameters (including the regularization parameter and any algorithmic hyperparameters), and discuss how to set or adapt the complexity penalty in practice.

Questions and requests for clarification:
- Interaction characterization: Is there a simple operational or diagnostic notion of “interaction” between two variables in your framework that does not require writing out the full model as in Equation (1)? For example, can interaction be characterized via conditional independence in the induced RKHS or via tests comparing restricted vs. unrestricted groupings?
- Line 169 (“almost surely”): Please clarify the probabilistic statement. Is the convergence almost sure with respect to the data distribution as n → ∞? If so, stating it explicitly would help.
- Equation (3): It appears to be missing a summation over i.
- Terminology (Line 205): “Translation-invariant kernel” is a more standard term than “convolutional kernel” in this context; consider adopting the former or clarifying the relationship.

Overall assessment:
This is a thoughtful and well-executed contribution. The main innovation—penalizing group-structured nonparametric models via covering-number complexity of direct-sum RKHSs—is elegant and well-justified. The theoretical guarantees are appropriate to the objectives, and the empirical results are encouraging. The primary weakness is practical scalability when p is large, stemming from the combinatorial nature of structure search. If the authors can (i) expand the discussion and experiments on computational aspects, (ii) tighten a few theoretical statements and notation (e.g., clarify probabilistic limits and fix minor errors), and (iii) clean up the typographical issues, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
