This paper investigates kernel regression in high-dimensional settings. The authors begin with the “additive model,” which assumes regressors of the form f = Σ<sub>p</sub> f<sub>p</sub>(X<sub>p</sub>), effectively ignoring correlations between dimensions. Recognizing the limitations of this assumption, they introduce the “group additive model,” allowing for the modeling of correlations within defined groups of dimensions. The paper offers a formal treatment of this setting and proposes algorithms – though somewhat ad hoc – for identifying optimal groupings. The efficacy of these approaches is demonstrated through experiments on small synthetic datasets and real-world cases.

My primary concern lies with the paper’s inadequate discussion of related work. As someone not specializing in this specific area, it is difficult to assess the true novelty of the contribution. The problem of finding optimal groupings is also central to fields such as structured sparsity and the learning of graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these areas receive no mention. Similarly, the problem connects to feature selection, which is also overlooked. The related work section largely focuses on a single paper from KDD-2015 [8], which is insufficient to establish the paper’s novelty convincingly.

Despite this, the theoretical treatment appears novel. The authors define a complexity measure for groupings – favoring many small groups – derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This derivation seems reasonable. However, the complexity measure is not easily optimized. Consequently, the authors resort to either an exhaustive search (which does not scale well) or a greedy method (reminiscent of forward feature selection). While acknowledging the inherent difficulty of optimizing discrete functions like those representing optimal groupings, I was disappointed that a more efficiently optimizable measure was not developed. The authors mention exploring alternative measures (line 174) but provide no further details, hindering an assessment of the chosen measure’s suitability. 

The experimental evaluation consists of a straightforward synthetic validation and an application to the Boston Housing dataset. Critically, no baselines are provided, and the results are not compared to existing methods. Without such comparisons, it’s challenging to determine the significance of the presented results.

== Post rebuttal ==

The authors addressed some of my concerns in their rebuttal. I remain unconvinced by the limited scope of the experiments and the insufficient contextualization of the work within the broader machine learning literature. Nevertheless, I acknowledge the paper’s valid contributions and have adjusted my score accordingly. I strongly recommend the authors significantly expand their discussion of related work prior to publication.
