This paper addresses kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, motivated by the limitations of traditional additive models which neglect interactions between predictor variables. The authors propose revisiting the “group additive model,” where predictors are grouped to allow for modeling correlations within groups, but not across them. They provide a formal definition of an “intrinsic group additive structure” and develop a structure-penalized kernel method for simultaneously identifying this structure and performing nonparametric function estimation. The method utilizes a novel complexity measure for group additive structures derived from covering number bounds. The authors demonstrate consistency in identifying the intrinsic structure through theoretical analysis and validate the approach with simulations and a real-world dataset.

The core contribution of this work lies in providing a formal treatment of group additive models and introducing a complexity measure for evaluating different group structures. While the additive model is well-established (Hastie & Tibshirani, 2009), extending it to group structures offers a flexible intermediate approach between purely additive models and more complex interaction models like functional ANOVA (Gu, 2002) or higher-order interaction models (Kandasamy & Yu, 2016). The use of covering numbers to define a complexity penalty for the group structure is a novel and interesting approach.

However, a significant weakness of the paper is its limited engagement with related work. The authors primarily cite a single KDD 2015 paper (Pan et al., 2015) and don’t adequately connect their work to broader research areas such as structured sparsity, graphical model learning (Koller & Friedman, 2009), or feature selection. The proposed problem shares similarities with these areas, and acknowledging this connection would help establish the novelty and significance of the work.  For example, identifying group structures could be viewed as learning a block structure in a sparse graphical model (Marlin & Murphy, 2009).

The optimization methods proposed for identifying the group structure also present a concern. While acknowledging the difficulty of optimizing discrete structures, the reliance on exhaustive search (which is computationally expensive) or a greedy approach feels somewhat limited. The authors mention exploring alternative complexity measures but do not elaborate, leaving the reader wondering if a more efficiently optimizable measure exists.

The experimental evaluation is relatively basic. The simulations are useful for demonstrating the method’s functionality, but lack comparisons to baseline methods. The application to the Boston Housing dataset is similarly presented without any benchmark results. It is difficult to assess the practical benefits of the proposed approach without such comparisons.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formalization of group additive models and the intrinsic group structure.
*   Interesting and potentially useful complexity measure based on covering numbers.
*   Theoretical guarantees of consistency for identifying the intrinsic structure.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient discussion of related work, hindering assessment of novelty.
*   Limited scalability of the optimization methods.
*   Weak experimental evaluation lacking baseline comparisons.

**Overall:** The paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution to the field of nonparametric regression. The theoretical results are promising, and the idea of leveraging complexity measures derived from covering numbers is intriguing. However, the limited discussion of related work and the lack of comprehensive experimental evaluation are significant weaknesses.
