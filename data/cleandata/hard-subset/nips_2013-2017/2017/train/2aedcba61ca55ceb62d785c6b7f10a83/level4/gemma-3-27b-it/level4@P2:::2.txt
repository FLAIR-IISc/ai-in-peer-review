This paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models, offering a compromise between fully nonparametric models (prone to the curse of dimensionality) and standard additive models (potentially overly restrictive). The core problem addressed is learning the group structure from data when it is not known *a priori*. The authors propose a novel penalty function, based on covering numbers of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHSs), incorporated into a kernel ridge regression objective. This leads to a combined optimization problem for both the group structure and the regression estimates within each group. Two algorithms are presented for approximate solution, accompanied by theoretical results demonstrating empirical risk convergence and consistency of the group structure estimate – meaning the probability of correctly identifying the true structure approaches 1 as the sample size grows. The method is evaluated on both synthetic and real datasets.

The key innovation lies in quantifying the complexity of a group structure using covering numbers of the corresponding direct sum RKHS. This is a comprehensive work, presenting a well-motivated and novel method supported by reasonably strong theoretical and empirical results. While I am not deeply familiar with alternative approaches for modeling between fully additive and nonparametric forms, the paper appears to be a potentially significant advancement, particularly given the discussion in Lines 31-50. A noted limitation, acknowledged in the Discussion, is the computational challenge of solving the optimization problem when the number of predictor variables is large. The paper is generally well-written, though contains numerous minor typographical errors.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The use of covering numbers to penalize group structure complexity is a creative and potentially impactful approach.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides theoretical guarantees for both the risk convergence and consistency of the proposed method.
*   **Comprehensive Approach:** The authors tackle both the statistical estimation and algorithmic aspects of the problem.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and motivates the development of the proposed method.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The method is evaluated on both simulated and real-world data.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Scalability:** The optimization problem becomes difficult to solve for a large number of predictors, limiting the practical applicability of the exhaustive search algorithm. The stepwise algorithm is presented as a solution, but its performance is not consistently strong in the simulations.
*   **Typos and Minor Clarity Issues:** The manuscript contains a noticeable number of typographical errors and occasional awkward phrasing that detract from readability.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While the paper references related work, a more detailed comparative analysis with existing approaches (especially those capable of handling larger datasets) would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Conceptual Clarity:** The motivation for using covering numbers specifically could be more intuitively explained, particularly for readers less familiar with that area of measure theory.



**Specific Questions/Comments:**

*   Is there a simpler characterization of "interaction" between two variables that doesn't require specifying the entire model in Equation (1)? A more intuitive description of the interaction captured by the group structure could be beneficial.
*   Line 169: The phrase "almost surely" is somewhat imprecise. Clarifying whether this refers to convergence as *n* approaches infinity would be helpful.
*   Equation (3) is missing a summation over *i*; it should be ∑<sub>i=1</sub><sup>n</sup>.
*   Line 205: "Convolutional kernel" might be more commonly referred to as a "translation invariant kernel."
*   Line 145: "on RHS" should be "on the RHS."
*   Line 152: "not only can the bias of \hat f{\lambda,G} reduces" should be "not only can the bias of \hat f{\lambda,G} reduce."
*   Line 160: "G^ exists and unique" should be "G^ exists and is unique."
*   Line 247: "turning parameters" should be "tuning parameters."
*   Algorithm 2, Line 1: "State with" should be "start with."
*   Algorithm 2, Line 251: "by compare" should be "by comparing."

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in nonparametric regression.
*   The use of covering numbers as a complexity measure is a significant contribution.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, at least for moderate-sized datasets.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The computational scalability issues limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The presence of numerous typos and minor clarity issues detracts from the overall quality of the presentation.
*   A more thorough comparative analysis with existing methods would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the computational limitations and minor presentation issues are concerns, the novelty and theoretical strength of the proposed method outweigh these drawbacks. I would lean towards **Acceptance**, contingent on the authors addressing the identified typos and clarity issues, and potentially providing some discussion of strategies for improving scalability in the final version.
