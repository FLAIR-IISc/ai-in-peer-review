

The paper examines kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, beginning with the additive model where the regression function is expressed as $f = \sum_p f_p(X_p)$, effectively ignoring correlations across dimensions. Recognizing this limitation, the authors propose a "group additive model" that partitions features into groups to capture interdimensional correlations. They provide a formal framework for this setting and introduce heuristic algorithms for group selection, with results on small synthetic and real-world datasets.  

The primary concern lies in the inadequate treatment of related work. The authors fail to contextualize their approach within established fields such as structured sparsity, graphical model learning, or feature selection—areas where optimal grouping is explicitly addressed. Instead, the related work discussion reduces to a single citation (KDD-2015 [8]), making it impossible to assess the paper’s true novelty. Without this context, the contribution appears incremental rather than substantive.  

The theoretical contribution—defining a complexity measure favoring finer groupings as an upper bound on the covering number—appears novel and well-motivated. However, this measure does not admit efficient optimization. The authors resort to either exhaustive search (computationally intractable for large $p$) or a greedy heuristic (reminiscent of forward feature selection). While discrete optimization is inherently challenging, the absence of a more efficient alternative—despite the authors’ mention of investigating "different measures" (line 174)—is disappointing. The paper provides no justification for why the chosen measure is preferable to cross-validation, leaving the practical viability of their approach unclear.  

Experimentally, the validation is limited to synthetic data and a single real-world example (Boston Housing) without comparative baselines or benchmarks against existing methods. Without such comparisons, the significance of the results remains unverified. The authors present no evidence that their approach outperforms simpler alternatives or standard kernel regression techniques, undermining the claimed utility.  

== Post-rebuttal ==  
The authors addressed some concerns in their rebuttal, particularly regarding the theoretical framework. However, the experimental section remains underdeveloped, lacking comparative baselines or rigorous validation. Crucially, the related work discussion remains insufficiently thorough; the authors still omit key connections to structured sparsity, graphical models, and feature selection literature. While the paper contains valid theoretical insights, the failure to properly position it within the broader ML landscape weakens its impact.  

I have modestly increased my score to acknowledge the theoretical novelty, but the paper requires substantial revisions before publication. I strongly recommend the authors expand the related work section to explicitly discuss connections with structured sparsity and feature selection, and to include comparative experiments with relevant baselines. Without these improvements, the work risks being perceived as a minor extension of existing ideas rather than a meaningful contribution.
