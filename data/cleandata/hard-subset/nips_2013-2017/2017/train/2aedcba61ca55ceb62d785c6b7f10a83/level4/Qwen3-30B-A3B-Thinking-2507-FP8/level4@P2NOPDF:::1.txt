

The paper investigates kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, proposing a "group additive model" as an extension of the standard additive model (which assumes no correlations between dimensions). While the group additive model addresses this limitation by grouping dimensions to capture correlations, the authors' treatment of related work is insufficient. The approach overlaps significantly with structured sparsity, graphical model learning, and feature selection—fields not acknowledged in the paper. Instead, the related work section relies almost exclusively on a single KDD-2015 reference [8], making it impossible to assess the novelty of the contribution. For instance, structured sparsity methods explicitly handle grouped features, and Bayesian network learning tackles optimal grouping of variables; omitting these comparisons undermines the paper's positioning.

The theoretical contribution centers on a complexity measure for groupings (favoring numerous small groups), derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This measure is theoretically sound, yet its practical utility is limited. The authors propose exhaustive search (computationally intractable for large groups) or a greedy heuristic (reminiscent of forward feature selection). While discrete optimization is inherently challenging, the authors’ failure to explore more efficient alternatives—despite mentioning "investigating different measures" (line 174)—is disappointing. Without a measure amenable to scalable optimization (e.g., convex relaxation), the framework’s applicability remains constrained. The paper offers no insights into why the chosen measure outperforms alternatives, leaving its suitability unverified.

Experimentally, the paper presents synthetic validation and a minimal Boston Housing case study. Crucially, it lacks baseline comparisons with established methods (e.g., structured sparsity techniques or standard kernel regression), rendering the results’ significance unclear. Without baselines, it is impossible to determine whether the proposed approach yields meaningful improvements. The synthetic experiments, while conceptually straightforward, do not address scalability or robustness to noise—key concerns in high-dimensional settings.

**Post-rebuttal considerations:**  
The authors partially addressed the related work critique, clarifying connections to structured sparsity. However, the discussion remains superficial, and the experimental section still lacks comparative baselines. While the theoretical framework is valid and the rebuttal improved the paper’s standing, the insufficient literature engagement and weak empirical validation persist as critical shortcomings. The authors should, at minimum, substantially expand the related work section to contextualize their contribution within established ML paradigms (e.g., structured sparsity, graphical models) and include baseline comparisons in future work.

**Recommendation:**  
The paper presents a technically plausible extension to additive models but fails to demonstrate clear novelty or practical utility due to inadequate literature review and experimental validation. The theoretical contribution is modest, and the proposed algorithms lack scalability. While the framework is sound, the current work does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for publication. I recommend rejection, but with the caveat that a revised version addressing these issues (particularly related work and baselines) could be reconsidered.
