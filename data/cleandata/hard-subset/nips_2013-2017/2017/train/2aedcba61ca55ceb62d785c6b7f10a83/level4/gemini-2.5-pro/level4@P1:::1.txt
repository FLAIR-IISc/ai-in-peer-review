This paper addresses the problem of high-dimensional nonparametric regression. The authors begin by noting the limitations of the standard additive model, `f(X) = sum(fi(Xi))`, which cannot capture interactions between predictor variables. To overcome this, they investigate the "group additive model," where variables are partitioned into groups, allowing for interactions within each group while maintaining an additive structure between groups. The paper's primary contributions are a formal framework for this setting, including the definition of a unique "intrinsic group additive structure," and a method for identifying this structure. This is achieved by penalizing model complexity using a novel measure derived from an upper bound on the covering number of the associated RKHS. The authors propose both an exhaustive search and a greedy backward stepwise algorithm for finding the optimal grouping. The approach is demonstrated on synthetic datasets and the Boston Housing dataset.

My primary concern with this paper is its insufficient engagement with related work, which makes it difficult to assess the novelty and significance of the contributions. The problem of identifying groups of interacting variables is central to several other areas of machine learning, yet these connections are not explored. For instance, the task is closely related to learning "structured sparsity" penalties, where priors encourage variables to be selected in groups. It also shares goals with methods for learning the structure of graphical models or Bayesian networks. Finally, at a high level, this can be viewed as a form of feature grouping. The paper's literature review appears to focus almost exclusively on a single prior work ([11] in the paper), which is not sufficient for a NIPS submission. Without placing the work in this broader context, its unique contributions are unclear.

While the theoretical development of the complexity penalty based on covering numbers is interesting and seems sound, its practical application is a significant weakness. The proposed penalty does not lend itself to efficient optimization. This forces the authors to resort to either an exhaustive search, which is computationally infeasible for even a moderate number of variables, or a greedy stepwise algorithm. While discrete optimization problems are inherently difficult, I was disappointed that the proposed framework did not lead to a more elegant or efficient optimization strategy. The authors mention they were "investigating and comparing different measures" but provide no details on this investigation. This makes it difficult to understand why this specific, difficult-to-optimize measure was chosen over other potential alternatives.

The experimental evaluation is another weak point. The authors present results on a few synthetic models and the Boston Housing dataset, but they provide no comparisons against any baseline or alternative methods. Without any point of comparison, it is impossible to judge whether the proposed method offers any advantage over existing techniques for high-dimensional regression or interaction detection. The experiments demonstrate that the method can work in idealized settings, but they do not establish its practical utility or superiority.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors' rebuttal addressed some of these concerns. However, I still find the experimental section to be too thin, and the discussion of related work remains a major weakness. The failure to properly contextualize the work within established machine learning fields is a significant omission. Despite these shortcomings, I acknowledge the paper's valid theoretical contributions, and I have raised my score accordingly. I strongly urge the authors to substantially revise the introduction and related work sections to properly situate their contribution within the broader literature should the paper be accepted.
