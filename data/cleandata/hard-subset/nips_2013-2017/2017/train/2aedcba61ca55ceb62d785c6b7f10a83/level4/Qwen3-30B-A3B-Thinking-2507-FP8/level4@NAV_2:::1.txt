

The paper investigates group additive structures for high-dimensional nonparametric regression, addressing a key limitation of standard additive models that ignore variable interactions. It rigorously defines the intrinsic group additive structure as the minimal group partition enabling accurate representation of the regression function, and proposes a structure-penalized kernel method for simultaneous structure identification and function estimation. The method leverages a novel complexity measure derived from RKHS covering numbers to penalize group structures, with theoretical consistency guarantees and two algorithmic approaches (exhaustive search and backward stepwise). Experimental validation includes synthetic data studies and a Boston Housing application.

**Quality**: The theoretical development is sound, with rigorous definitions of intrinsic group structures and consistency proofs for the proposed method. The complexity measure derived from covering numbers is well-motivated and appropriately applied. However, the experimental section lacks comparative baselines against established methods (e.g., functional ANOVA [4,13], group sparsity techniques [9,14]), making it difficult to assess performance gains. The absence of scalability analysis for the greedy algorithm beyond small-scale synthetic data is a notable limitation.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise mathematical formulations. The connection between group additive structures and graphical models (Section 1) is particularly insightful, though the distinction from conditional dependence structures could be emphasized more. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1–2) are sufficiently detailed for reproduction. Minor improvements could be made to clarify the tuning parameter selection strategy in Section 4.

**Originality**: The core contribution—defining the intrinsic group additive structure and using RKHS covering numbers for structure penalization—is novel. While the group additive framework builds on [11], the formalization of minimality (Theorem 1) and the complexity measure (Section 2.3.2) significantly advance prior work. The paper could strengthen novelty by more explicitly contrasting with related work on structured sparsity (e.g., group Lasso [14]) and graphical model-based group discovery [9], which are mentioned in the references but not discussed in the main text. The related work section remains underdeveloped, focusing narrowly on [11] instead of contextualizing contributions within NIPS proceedings (e.g., recent work on sparse additive models [6,15]).

**Significance**: The problem addressed—efficient high-dimensional nonparametric regression with interpretable interaction modeling—is significant. The method offers a middle ground between standard additive models and full functional ANOVA, potentially mitigating the curse of dimensionality. However, the limited experimental scope (only synthetic data and one real-world example) and lack of comparison to state-of-the-art baselines weaken claims about practical impact. The method’s applicability to large-scale problems remains uncertain due to computational constraints of the proposed algorithms.

**Strengths**:  
- Rigorous theoretical foundation for intrinsic group structures and consistency.  
- Novel complexity measure derived from RKHS capacity theory.  
- Clear exposition of the connection to graphical models and its distinction from conditional dependence structures.  
- Practical algorithms for structure identification with empirical validation.  

**Weaknesses**:  
- Underdeveloped related work section fails to position contributions against key ML literature (structured sparsity, graphical models).  
- Experimental section lacks baselines and scalability analysis, limiting assessment of practical utility.  
- Greedy algorithm’s potential suboptimality (Section 4) is acknowledged but not addressed with alternative strategies.  
- Theoretical guarantees assume known kernel properties, which may not hold in practice without tuning.  

**Recommendation**: The paper makes a technically valid contribution to high-dimensional nonparametric regression with sound theory, but the weak experimental evaluation and insufficient contextualization within the broader ML literature prevent it from meeting the significance threshold for NIPS. The authors should substantially strengthen the related work and experimental sections (including comparisons to methods like [9,14]) to justify acceptance. As submitted, the work is of moderate interest but not sufficiently impactful for the conference.
