This paper addresses the challenge of high-dimensional kernel regression. The authors begin by considering the standard "additive model," where the regression function is a sum of functions of individual features, `f(X) = sum_p f_p(X_p)`. Acknowledging the key limitation of this model—its inability to capture correlations between different dimensions—the authors propose a "group additive model." In this framework, dimensions are partitioned into groups, allowing the model to capture dependencies within each group while maintaining an additive structure across groups. The paper provides a formal theoretical treatment of this setting, proposes a complexity measure for evaluating groupings, and presents algorithms for discovering effective groups. The framework is evaluated on small-scale synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Detailed Comments**

A primary concern with the manuscript is its insufficient discussion of related work. This makes it difficult to assess the novelty and context of the contributions, particularly for a reader not intimately familiar with this specific subfield. The task of identifying influential groups of variables is a central theme in several areas of machine learning, yet these connections are not explored. For instance:

*   **Structured Sparsity:** This field develops methods for selecting groups of features (e.g., group LASSO) and more complex structures, which is highly relevant to the goal of finding optimal groupings.
*   **Graphical Model Learning:** Learning the structure of Bayesian networks or Markov random fields involves identifying conditional dependencies and variable relationships, a problem conceptually similar to the one addressed here.
*   **Feature Selection:** At its core, this work can be viewed as a sophisticated form of feature selection, where the goal is to select interacting groups of features rather than individual ones.

The related work section, as presented, focuses almost exclusively on a single KDD 2015 paper [8]. A comprehensive literature review that situates the work within these broader contexts is necessary to establish its novelty and significance.

On a positive note, the theoretical treatment appears to be a novel contribution. The authors define a complexity measure for a given grouping, which favors partitions with many small groups. This measure is sensibly derived as an upper bound on the covering number of the function class. However, a significant practical downside is that this measure does not lend itself to efficient optimization. The authors propose either an exhaustive search, which is computationally infeasible for even a moderate number of dimensions, or a greedy method analogous to forward selection. While optimizing discrete functions is inherently challenging, it is somewhat disappointing that the proposed measure does not admit a more efficient optimization strategy. The authors' brief mention of "investigating and comparing different measures" (line 174) without further detail leaves the reader unable to judge the rationale behind the chosen measure over other potential candidates.

The experimental validation is limited and does not sufficiently demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method. The experiments are confined to a simple synthetic example and the Boston Housing dataset. Crucially, the paper provides no baselines or comparisons against other relevant methods from the areas mentioned above. Without any points of comparison, it is impossible to determine whether the presented results are competitive or to gauge the practical significance of the proposed framework.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments**

The author rebuttal addressed some of the concerns raised in the initial review. However, major weaknesses remain. The experimental evaluation is still insufficient, and the paper's positioning relative to the broader machine learning literature is severely lacking. Nevertheless, I acknowledge the paper's valid theoretical contributions and have raised my score accordingly. I strongly urge the authors to substantially expand the discussion of related work in the final version of the paper to properly contextualize their contributions for the community.
