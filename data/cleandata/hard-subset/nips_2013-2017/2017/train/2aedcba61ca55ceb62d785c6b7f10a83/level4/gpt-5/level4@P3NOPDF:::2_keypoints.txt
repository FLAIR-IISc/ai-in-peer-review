- Paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression with unknown group structure learned from data via a novel covering-number-based complexity penalty added to kernel ridge regression.  
- Offers two algorithms to jointly optimize group structure and within-group functions, with theoretical convergence rates and group-consistency as n → ∞.  
- Key novelty: quantifying candidate group-structure complexity using covering numbers of the induced direct-sum RKHS.  
- Provides an end-to-end pipeline: model, penalty, algorithms, theory, and empirical validation.  
- Theoretical guarantees on excess risk and consistency are compelling and aligned with objectives.  
- Exposition is generally clear and well-motivated.  
- Main practical concern: optimizing the nonconvex combinatorial objective does not scale well with large p; more detail on complexity, scalability, initialization, and failure modes is needed.  
- Recommend empirical sensitivity analyses showing runtime and accuracy versus p and group sizes.  
- Suggest positioning relative to related methods: hierarchical group lasso for interactions, multiple-kernel learning over subsets, and Bayesian nonparametrics for sparse interactions.  
- Request more actionable guidance for choosing tuning parameters and adapting the complexity penalty in practice.  
- Ask for an operational diagnostic for interactions (e.g., conditional-independence tests or comparisons between restricted and unrestricted groupings).  
- Clarify probabilistic language: specify whether “almost surely” convergence in Line 169 is with respect to the data distribution as n → ∞.  
- Fix Equation (3): appears to be missing a summation over i.  
- Terminology: prefer “translation-invariant kernel” over “convolutional kernel” or clarify their relationship.  
- Minor typos to correct: Line 145, Line 152, Line 160, Line 247, Algorithm 2 Line 1, and Line 251 as noted.  
- Overall: elegant, well-justified main idea with appropriate theory and promising experiments; needs expanded discussion/experiments on computational aspects, tightened notation and probabilistic statements, and minor typographical fixes.
