## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

**Summary:**

This paper investigates group-additive nonparametric regression, a model class that offers a flexible compromise between fully nonparametric regression (which can be computationally challenging) and traditional additive models (which may be overly restrictive). The core problem addressed is learning both the regression function *and* the underlying group structure from data. The authors propose a novel penalty term, grounded in the covering numbers of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) balls, incorporated into a kernel ridge regression objective. This leads to a joint optimization problem over group structure – which, along with the kernel, defines a function space via direct sums of RKHSs – and the regression estimate within each group. Two algorithms are presented for approximate solution, accompanied by theoretical guarantees regarding (a) the rate of convergence of the empirical risk to the true risk, and (b) the consistency of the estimated group structure as the sample size grows. The method is demonstrated through experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.

**Main Comments:**

The key contribution of this work lies in recognizing that the complexity of a given group structure can be effectively quantified using covering numbers of the corresponding direct sum space. The paper is comprehensive, presenting a well-motivated and novel methodology supported by reasonably strong theoretical and empirical results. While I am not deeply familiar with the broader landscape of methods fitting models between purely additive and fully nonparametric forms, the discussion in Lines 31-50 appears to provide a fair and complete overview, suggesting this work represents a potentially significant advancement. The authors acknowledge in their Discussion section that a primary limitation of the method is the computational difficulty of optimizing over the group structure when dealing with a large number of predictor variables. Overall, the paper is clearly written, although it contains a number of typographical errors.

**Minor Comments/Questions:**

*   Is there a simpler, more intuitive characterization of “interaction” between two variables that doesn’t necessitate expressing the entire model as in Equation (1)? A more accessible definition could aid understanding.
*   Line 169: The use of “almost surely” is unclear. Is this intended to convey convergence as *n* approaches infinity? Clarification would be beneficial.
*   Equation (3) appears to be missing a summation (over *i*).
*   Line 205: The term “convolutional kernel” may be less common than “translation invariant kernel” in this context.
*   **Typos:**
    *   Line 145: “on RHS” should be “on the RHS.”
    *   Line 152: “not only can the bias of \hat{f}_{\lambda,G} reduces” should be “not only can the bias of \hat{f}_{\lambda,G} reduce.”
    *   Line 160: “G^ exists and unique” should be “G^ exists and is unique.”
    *   Line 247: “turning parameters” should be “tuning parameters.”
    *   Algorithm 2: Line 1: “State with” should be “start with.”
    *   Line 251: “by compare” should be “by comparing.”

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong submission with a novel and well-supported approach to an important problem. The theoretical results are compelling, and the empirical validation is encouraging. While the computational challenges associated with scaling to high-dimensional data are a limitation, they are acknowledged by the authors. The paper, after addressing the minor comments (particularly the typos), would be a valuable addition to the NIPS workshop. I recommend acceptance.
