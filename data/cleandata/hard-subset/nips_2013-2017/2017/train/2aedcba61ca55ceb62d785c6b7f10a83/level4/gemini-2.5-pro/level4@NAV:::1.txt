This work investigates kernel regression in a high-dimensional context. It builds upon the "additive model," where regressors are constructed as a sum of functions of individual dimensions (f = Σ fp(X_p)). A clear limitation of this approach is its inability to capture inter-dimensional correlations. To overcome this, the authors introduce a "group additive model," which partitions dimensions into groups to model correlations within each subset. The paper offers a formal analysis of this model, alongside several heuristic algorithms for identifying optimal groupings. The framework is demonstrated on small-scale synthetic and real-world datasets.

A primary weakness of the submission is its inadequate review of related literature. This makes it difficult for a non-expert reviewer to accurately gauge the work's novelty. The problem of identifying optimal variable groups is central to fields like "structured sparsity" and the learning of graphical models or Bayesian networks, yet these areas are not discussed. Furthermore, the task can be framed as a form of "feature selection," another relevant field that is absent from the discussion. As it stands, the related work section is almost entirely focused on a single paper (KDD 2015, [8]), which is insufficient for situating the contribution within the broader literature.

Nevertheless, the theoretical treatment appears to be a novel contribution. The authors define a straightforward complexity measure for groupings, which is derived as an upper bound on the covering number and favors configurations with numerous small groups. This formulation seems reasonable. A notable drawback, however, is that this measure is not amenable to efficient optimization. Consequently, the authors propose either an exhaustive search, which is not scalable, or a greedy algorithm analogous to forward feature selection. While optimizing discrete functions is inherently challenging, it is somewhat disappointing that the proposed measure does not permit a more efficient optimization strategy than cross-validation. The authors note they have been "investigating and comparing different measures" (line 174) but offer no further discussion, making it difficult to assess the appropriateness of their final choice.

The experimental validation is limited. The authors present a simple synthetic experiment and a single application to the Boston Housing dataset. Crucially, the results are presented without any baselines or comparisons to alternative methods. This omission makes it impossible to judge whether the presented performance is competitive.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors' rebuttal addressed some of my concerns. However, I maintain that the experiments are preliminary and that the paper's treatment of related work is critically insufficient for a publication at this venue. Nevertheless, I recognize the paper contains valid contributions and have adjusted my score upward accordingly. I strongly recommend that the authors substantially expand their discussion of related work for the camera-ready version.
