This paper studies kernel nonparametric regression in high dimensions. It starts from the additive model, f(X) = ∑p i=1 fi(Xi), which is computationally attractive and interpretable but ignores interactions. The authors instead consider a group additive model, where variables are partitioned into groups and f decomposes as a sum of multivariate functions over groups. They formalize this setting by defining a partial order over group structures, the set of “amiable” structures for a target function, and the unique “intrinsic” group additive structure (the minimal element that captures the true interactions). They then propose a structure-penalized kernel method in RKHSs that jointly estimates the function and identifies the group structure. The key idea is to define a complexity measure for a grouping via covering-number bounds for the induced direct-sum RKHS; the resulting penalty favors simpler (in practice, smaller) groups. They prove selection consistency for the group structure under suitable conditions and provide two search strategies: exhaustive search for small p and a greedy stepwise procedure for larger p. Simulations and a Boston Housing example illustrate the approach.

Strengths
- The formalization of group additive structures, including the partial order, the notion of amiable structures, and the existence/uniqueness of the intrinsic structure, is clear and useful. This framework helps distinguish identifiability issues from estimation issues.
- The penalty based on covering-number bounds for direct-sum RKHSs is principled and aligns with established capacity control in statistical learning theory.
- Theoretical results include nontrivial uniform risk bounds and consistency of structure recovery with appropriately chosen tuning parameters.
- The idea of targeting group structure directly, rather than inferring it from a full conditional dependence graph, is appealing and potentially more tractable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Related work is insufficiently discussed. Learning group structure connects to structured sparsity (e.g., overlapping/group lasso variants), graphical models and Bayesian networks (structure learning), and feature selection. The paper provides very limited context beyond an earlier KDD paper on OKGT, making it difficult to assess novelty for non-experts. A broader and more precise comparison is needed to clarify how the proposed framework differs from, or improves on, these lines of work.
- The proposed complexity measure, while sensible, leads to a difficult discrete optimization problem. The paper offers either exhaustive search (which scales poorly) or a greedy stepwise procedure. I was hoping for a surrogate or relaxation enabling more efficient global optimization than cross-validation over discrete structures. The paper mentions having compared different capacity measures but does not share insights that might guide the choice or lead to more tractable objectives.
- Experiments are thin. The synthetic studies are small-scale and the real-data demonstration (Boston Housing) is illustrative only. There are no baselines (e.g., additive models, functional ANOVA/SALSA, interaction-regularized kernel methods, or structure-learning methods) and no comparative metrics. Without such comparisons, it is hard to judge practical benefits, robustness, or scalability.
- Practical guidance on selecting λ, µ, and the α parameter is limited, and performance appears sensitive to these choices.

Quality
- The theoretical development is solid: the definition of the intrinsic structure, the covering-number–based complexity, and the selection consistency are technically sound. However, the optimization side is ad hoc, and the empirical evaluation does not convincingly demonstrate the method’s effectiveness relative to alternatives.

Clarity
- The exposition of the group-structure framework and the connection to RKHS capacity is clear. The paper is generally well organized. That said, the related-work section needs substantial expansion, and more discussion is needed to interpret the complexity penalty, its bias toward smaller groups, and its practical tuning.

Originality
- The formal identification of an intrinsic group additive structure and the use of a covering-number–based penalty for direct-sum RKHSs are, to my knowledge, novel. However, the paper does not adequately position itself with respect to structured sparsity, graphical model structure learning, or feature selection, making the extent of novelty difficult to evaluate.

Significance
- If reliably scalable and competitive, the ability to recover interacting groups could be impactful for high-dimensional nonparametric regression. At present, the lack of baselines and limited scale of experiments make it difficult to assess practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and rigorous formulation of group additive structure and identifiability.
- Principled complexity measure and nontrivial consistency theory.
- Potentially useful framework for modeling interactions while retaining interpretability.

Cons for acceptance
- Inadequate related-work discussion; novelty relative to established areas is unclear.
- Optimization relies on exhaustive or greedy search with cross-validation; scalability is uncertain.
- Empirical evaluation lacks baselines and comprehensive comparisons.

Post-rebuttal
The rebuttal clarified some points, and I acknowledge that the paper contains valid contributions. Nonetheless, the experiments remain sparse, and the discussion of related machine learning literature is still insufficient. I encourage the authors to substantially improve the related-work section and to include comparative experiments with strong baselines in a final version.
