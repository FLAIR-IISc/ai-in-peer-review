Summary:
- The paper studies group-additive nonparametric regression models, in which, given a partition of the predictor variables, the regression function is additive across groups. This framework interpolates between fully nonparametric models, which are difficult to fit, and additive models, which can be overly restrictive.
- The group structure is unknown and must be learned from data. To address this, the paper introduces a novel penalty based on covering numbers of RKHS balls, incorporated into a kernel ridge regression objective.
- The resulting objective is optimized jointly over the group structure (which, together with the kernel, induces a function space via a direct sum of group-specific RKHSs) and the regression estimate within each group.
- Two algorithms are proposed to approximately solve this joint optimization. Theoretical results establish (a) the rate at which the empirical risk of the estimator approaches the true risk of the population optimum, and (b) consistency of the estimated group structure: the probability that it matches the true partition tends to 1 as n → ∞.
- Experiments on both synthetic and real data are reported.

Main Comments:
- The key innovation is to quantify the complexity of a given group structure via covering numbers of the associated direct-sum space.
- The paper is comprehensive: the method is well motivated and novel, and the theoretical and empirical results are reasonably solid.
- I am not very familiar with alternative approaches that interpolate between additive and fully nonparametric models; if the overview in Lines 31–50 is reasonably complete, this work appears to be a meaningful advance.
- As noted in the Discussion, the primary limitation is the difficulty of optimizing over group structures when the number of variables is large.
- The writing is generally clear, aside from numerous typos.
