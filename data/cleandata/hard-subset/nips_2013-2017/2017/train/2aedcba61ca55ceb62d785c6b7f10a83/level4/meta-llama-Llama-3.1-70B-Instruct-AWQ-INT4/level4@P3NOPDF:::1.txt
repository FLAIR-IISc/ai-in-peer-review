The paper explores kernel regression in high-dimensional settings, focusing on the "additive model" as its starting point. This model considers regressors of the form f = ∑ f_p(X_p), effectively ignoring correlations between different dimensions. Recognizing the limitations of this approach, the authors propose the "group additive model," which groups dimensions to capture correlations between some of them.

The authors provide a formal treatment of this setting and introduce algorithms for finding optimal groupings, albeit somewhat ad hoc. They present results for small synthetic and real-world cases. However, I found the paper's discussion of related work to be lacking, making it challenging for a non-expert like myself to assess the true novelty of the work.

The task of finding optimal groups in data is also explored in fields like "structured sparsity" and learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these are not mentioned in the paper. Furthermore, the problem can be seen as an instance of "feature selection," which is also not discussed. As a result, the related work described in the paper is limited to a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient for me to determine the actual novelty of the paper.

That being said, the theoretical treatment appears to be novel. The authors define a simple complexity measure on a grouping, which favors many small groups. This measure is derived as an upper bound on the covering number, which seems sensible. However, this measure does not lend itself to easy optimization, leading the authors to propose either an exhaustive search (which scales poorly) or a greedy method (akin to forward feature selection).

While I acknowledge that discrete functions, such as those describing optimal groupings, are generally difficult to optimize, I was somewhat disappointed that the authors did not arrive at a measure that could be optimized more efficiently than just using cross-validation. The authors mention that they have been "investigating and comparing different measures" (line 174), but they provide no further insights, making it hard for me to determine the suitability of the chosen measure.

Experimentally, the authors provide simple synthetic validation of their framework along with a simple example on the Boston Housing data. However, they do not provide a baseline or compare their method with other approaches, making it difficult for me to assess the quality of the presented results.

In response to my concerns, the authors provided clarifications in their rebuttal. While I still believe the experiments are thin and the authors did an unacceptably poor job of relating their work to ideas in machine learning, I acknowledge that the paper has valid contributions and have improved my score accordingly. I strongly encourage the authors to improve their discussion of related work when the paper is published.
