Review- The paper studies kernel regression in a high-dimensional regime. Its point of departure is the additive model, which assumes predictors of the form
 f = ∑_p f_p(X_p)
that is, it ignores correlations across dimensions. Recognizing the limitations of discarding such correlations, the authors move to a group additive model, in which dimensions are partitioned into groups so that within-group correlations can be captured. The paper offers a formal treatment of this setup and introduces somewhat ad hoc procedures to identify good groupings. Results are shown on small synthetic datasets and a modest real-world example.

My primary concern is that the treatment of related work is weak, making it hard for a non-specialist like me to assess the true novelty. The problem of discovering optimal groups is also addressed in areas such as structured sparsity and in learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these literatures are not cited. Similarly, the problem can be framed as feature selection, which is likewise omitted. In effect, the related work section essentially reduces to a single KDD-2015 paper [8], which is insufficient for me to judge the contribution’s originality.

That said, the theoretical development appears novel. The authors define a simple complexity metric over groupings (which favors many small groups), derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This seems reasonable. However, the metric is not easily optimized: the proposed strategies are either exhaustive search (poor scalability) or a greedy approach akin to forward feature selection. While discrete objectives (like those defining optimal groupings) are often hard to optimize, I was disappointed that the proposed criterion cannot be optimized more efficiently than via cross-validation. The authors note they have been “investigating and comparing different measures” (line 174), but give no further detail, leaving me unable to judge the suitability of the chosen metric.

On the empirical side, the paper includes simple synthetic validations and a small example on the Boston Housing dataset. There are no baselines or comparisons against alternative methods. Consequently, it is difficult to assess the quality of the results in the absence of benchmarks.

== Post rebuttal ==
The authors addressed some of my questions in the rebuttal. I still find the experiments underpowered and the discussion of connections to machine learning literature unacceptably thin. Nevertheless, I acknowledge that the paper contains valid contributions and have modestly raised my score. I strongly urge the authors to improve the related work discussion at publication time.
