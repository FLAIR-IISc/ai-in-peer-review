The paper proposes a kernel regression method in the high dimensional setting, extending the traditional additive model by considering a "group additive model". This approach allows for correlations between certain dimensions to be modeled by grouping them together. The authors provide a formal treatment of this setting and propose algorithms for finding optimal groupings. The results are demonstrated on small synthetic and real-world cases.

However, the paper's discussion of related work is limited, making it difficult to assess the novelty of the work. The task of finding optimal groups in data is also considered in other fields, such as structured sparsity and learning graphical models/Bayesian networks, but these are not mentioned. Additionally, the problem can be seen as an instance of feature selection, but this connection is not explored. The only related work mentioned is a single KDD-2015 paper, which is insufficient to determine the actual novelty of the paper.

The theoretical treatment, on the other hand, appears to be novel. The authors define a simple complexity measure on a grouping, which favors many small groups, derived as an upper bound on the covering number. While this measure is sensible, it does not lend itself to easy optimization, leading the authors to propose either an exhaustive search or a greedy method. It is disappointing that a more efficient optimization method was not developed, especially given the authors' mention of investigating and comparing different measures without providing further insights.

The experimental validation is limited, with simple synthetic examples and a single real-world case on the Boston Housing data. The lack of baseline comparisons and evaluations against other methods makes it difficult to assess the quality of the results. Overall, while the paper has some valid contributions, the poor discussion of related work and limited experimental evaluation are notable weaknesses.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

1. The paper proposes a novel extension of the traditional additive model, allowing for correlations between certain dimensions to be modeled.
2. The theoretical treatment, including the definition of a complexity measure on groupings, appears to be novel and sensible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. The discussion of related work is limited, making it difficult to assess the novelty of the work.
2. The experimental validation is limited, with no baseline comparisons and evaluations against other methods.
3. The optimization method proposed is not efficient, relying on either exhaustive search or a greedy approach.
