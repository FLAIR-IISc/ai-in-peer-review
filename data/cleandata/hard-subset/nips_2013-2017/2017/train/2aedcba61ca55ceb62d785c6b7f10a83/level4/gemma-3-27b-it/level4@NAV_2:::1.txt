The paper considers kernel regression in the high dimensional setting. The starting point of the paper is the additive model, which assumes that the relationship between the response and predictors can be modeled as a sum of univariate functions, effectively ignoring correlations between dimensions. Recognizing the limitations of this approach, the authors instead investigate the group additive model, which allows for correlations within groups of dimensions. They provide a formal treatment of this setting and propose algorithms for identifying optimal groupings. Results are presented for small synthetic and real-world cases.

A key concern is the paper’s limited discussion of related work, making it difficult to assess the novelty of the contribution. The problem of finding optimal groupings in data is also addressed in fields like structured sparsity and the learning of graphical models/Bayesian networks, yet these areas are not mentioned. Similarly, the problem can be viewed as a feature selection task, which is also absent from the related work discussion. The cited related work primarily consists of a single KDD-2015 paper [11], which is insufficient to gauge the paper’s originality. 

Despite this, the theoretical treatment appears novel. The authors define a complexity measure for a grouping – favoring many small groups – derived as an upper bound on the covering number. This seems reasonable. However, this measure doesn’t lend itself to easy optimization, leading the authors to propose either an exhaustive search (which doesn’t scale well) or a greedy method (similar to forward feature selection). While acknowledging the difficulty of optimizing discrete functions, I was somewhat disappointed that a more efficiently optimizable measure wasn’t developed. The authors mention investigating different measures (line 174) but offer no further insights into their suitability.

Experimentally, the authors present simple synthetic validation and a demonstration on the Boston Housing data. Importantly, no baselines are included, and there is no comparison to other methods. This makes it challenging to assess the quality of the presented results and their significance.

In summary, the paper presents a novel theoretical contribution regarding the complexity measure for group additive structures. However, the limited related work discussion and lack of comparative experimental results hinder a strong evaluation of the paper’s overall impact.
